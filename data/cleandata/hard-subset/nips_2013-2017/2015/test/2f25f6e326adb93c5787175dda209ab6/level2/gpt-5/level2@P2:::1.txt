Summary
The paper proposes nonspectral upper and lower bounds on the DPP likelihood normalization term for both finite and continuous domains, enabling inference without computing eigenvalues of the L-ensemble kernel. For finite domains, given inducing (pseudo-)inputs Z and the Nyström-style low-rank approximation Q = LYZ LZ^{-1} LZY, the authors derive bounds of the form det(Q+I) exp(−tr(L−Q)) ≤ 1/det(L+I) ≤ 1/det(Q+I). For continuous domains (with base measure µ and trace-class kernel operator L), they obtain analogous bounds on the Fredholm determinant det(I+L) involving det(LZ), det(LZ+Ψ), and an exponential of trace terms, where Ψij = ∫ L(zi,x)L(x,zj)dµ(x). These bounds are inexpensive to evaluate (O(n m^2) for finite case) and do not require any spectral approximation.

Building on these bounds, the paper develops two inference strategies: (i) a variational approach maximizing a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood over kernel parameters and pseudo-inputs; and (ii) a retrospective Metropolis–Hastings sampler that adaptively refines the bounds by increasing m and re-optimizing Z until the accept/reject decision can be made. The approach is related to sparse GP variational bounds (Titsias, 2009) and contrasts with previous DPP inference methods that rely on spectral truncation (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML’13; Bornemann’10 for Fredholm determinants). Experiments on a Gaussian–Gaussian 1D DPP (where the spectrum is known) validate the MCMC against the exact likelihood; on a diabetic neuropathy dataset, the variational method yields coherent over-dispersion estimates that separate classes, with tightness improving as m increases.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The finite-domain inequality matches the Seiler–Simon determinant inequality specialized to Q ≤ L (which holds by Schur complement for the joint Gram matrix on Y∪Z). The upper bound (monotonicity of det(I+·)) and exponential lower correction appear correct. The continuous counterpart is consistent with the finite case via det(I+Q) = det(LZ+Ψ)/det(LZ), and the exponential correction uses ∫ L(x,x)dµ and tr(LZ^{-1}Ψ). Proof details are deferred, but the constructions are plausible.
- Algorithms: The variational objective F(θ,Z) is consistent and cheap to evaluate when |Yt| are modest; the MCMC with retrospective acceptance is standard and correct as long as bounds are valid for any Z (optimization only tightens them, so local optima do not bias acceptance).
- Empirics: The 1D validation is convincing for the sampler. The real-data study reproduces earlier findings and shows monotone tightening with m. However, the empirical evaluation is limited: no large-scale finite-domain results; no timing/accuracy comparison against spectral methods; and limited sensitivity analysis on m and Z optimization.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear motivation for avoiding spectral methods. The connection to sparse GP bounds is helpful. Some notational glitches and minor typos (e.g., K(xi,xi)K(yi,yi) likely K(xi,xi)K(xj,xj); occasional equation number mismatches) should be fixed. It would help to explicitly state the PSD ordering Q ≤ L and the block matrix argument that guarantees it when Z ∉ Y.

Originality
The main contribution—nonspectral determinant bounds tailored to DPP likelihoods and their extension to continuous domains—is novel and nontrivial. The idea to reuse inducing-point GP techniques for DPP determinants is elegant and, to my knowledge, not previously instantiated in this way. Prior DPP inference has relied on spectral truncations or fixed-point/EM updates over L; this work provides a complementary route.

Significance
The bounds are practical and widely applicable: they sidestep eigen-computations in both finite and continuous settings and enable both variational and exact (retrospective) MCMC inference. This could broaden the usability of DPPs, especially for continuous domains where spectra are unavailable. The significance would be strengthened by more extensive empirical comparisons and scalability demonstrations.

Pros
- Conceptually clean, general nonspectral bounds with provable ordering and simple forms.
- Works for continuous DPPs with Fredholm determinants; leverages Ψ integrals available in common Gaussian–Gaussian settings.
- Enables both fast variational training and exact retrospective MCMC without spectra.
- Solid theoretical grounding; finite and continuous cases align nicely.
- Empirical validations that check MCMC correctness against ground truth.

Cons
- Limited experimental scope: no large finite-domain benchmarks, no runtime/accuracy comparisons vs spectral bounds or fixed-point/EM baselines.
- No formal convergence/tightness guarantees as m→∞ beyond intuition; a short theorem (conditions under which bounds converge) would be valuable.
- Dependence on optimizing Z may introduce variability; practical guidance on initialization/optimization would help.
- Identifiability concerns when parameterizing L directly (e.g., κ) reduce interpretability; discussion is good but empirical mitigation strategies (priors, reparameterizations) would help.

Questions and suggestions
1) Can you provide conditions ensuring limm→∞ det(Q+I)→det(L+I) and the exponential correction term vanishes, in both finite and continuous cases? A short theorem would strengthen the contribution.
2) For the continuous case, please detail assumptions ensuring Q ≤ L as operators and the applicability of Seiler–Simon in the trace-class setting.
3) How sensitive are results to Z initialization? Have you tried joint optimization of θ and Z with gradients (when available) versus CMA-ES? Providing practical recommendations would aid adoption.
4) Please compare against spectral methods (e.g., Affandi et al.’s adaptive truncation) on at least one finite-domain dataset in terms of wall-clock, accuracy, and memory, and include larger n.
5) For the diabetic dataset, could you report uncertainty (e.g., via MCMC) on γd and discuss κ identifiability (priors, constraints)?
6) Clarify computational complexity: computing tr(L−Q) in O(n m^2) relies on diagonal L being cheap; state this assumption explicitly.

Minor comments
- Fix typos and index inconsistencies; ensure equation numbering is consistent.
- Briefly justify that Z outside Y still yields a valid PSD Q via the Schur complement of the joint Gram matrix on Y∪Z.
- State explicitly that the bounds hold for any Z; optimization only tightens them.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated, technically sound, and novel contribution that removes a major bottleneck in DPP inference by avoiding spectral computations, with a clean extension to continuous domains. While the empirical evaluation could be stronger, the methodological advance is significant and broadly useful. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying theoretical conditions and adding at least one comparative experiment.
