This paper addresses the problem of likelihood-based parameter inference for determinantal point processes (DPPs), a class of models that encode diversity. The main challenge in this setting is the computation of the likelihood's normalizing constant, which involves evaluating `det(L + I)`, a large matrix determinant for finite DPPs or an intractable Fredholm determinant for continuous DPPs. The authors note that prior work, such as Affandi et al. (2014), has tackled this by approximating the spectrum of the kernel matrix L, which can still be computationally demanding.

The main contribution of this work is the derivation of novel, non-spectral upper and lower bounds on this normalizing constant. Drawing inspiration from variational sparse approximations in Gaussian processes (Titsias, 2009), the authors introduce a set of `m` "pseudo-inputs" or "inducing points" `Z` to construct a low-rank approximation to the kernel matrix. This leads to bounds that can be computed much more efficiently (e.g., in O(nm^2) time for the finite case) than spectral methods, without requiring any eigendecompositions. The authors present these bounds for both DPPs on finite sets and on continuous domains.

Based on these bounds, the paper proposes two inference schemes. The first is a fast, approximate variational inference (VI) method that maximizes the lower bound on the log-likelihood with respect to both the model parameters and the locations of the inducing points. The second is an asymptotically exact MCMC method using Metropolis-Hastings with retrospective sampling, where the bounds are adaptively tightened by increasing the number of inducing points until an acceptance decision can be made. The utility of these methods is demonstrated on a synthetic 1D continuous DPP and a real-world 2D dataset of nerve fiber patterns in diabetic patients.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the bounds is based on a known determinant inequality from Seiler & Simon (1975), and its application in this context is novel and well-motivated. The extension of the core idea from the finite to the continuous setting is a valuable contribution and is handled with appropriate mathematical care. The experimental validation is solid: the MCMC method is shown to match an ideal sampler on a synthetic problem, and the VI method yields coherent and useful results on a real-world scientific dataset, reproducing findings from previous work.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, and the structure of the paper, which first introduces the simpler finite case before moving to the continuous case, is very effective. The connection to sparse Gaussian processes is explained well, making the approach intuitive for readers familiar with that literature. The notation is consistent and the mathematical developments are clear.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While DPPs and sparse GPs are both established topics, the idea of using inducing-point methods to derive non-spectral bounds for the DPP likelihood is novel. This provides a new and powerful alternative to the existing spectral approximation methods. The paper is clear about its relationship to prior work, particularly [7], and effectively differentiates its contribution as a fundamentally different approach to the same problem.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be impactful. DPPs are an increasingly important tool in machine learning for modeling diversity, but their application has been hampered by computational bottlenecks. By providing a cheaper way to perform both approximate and exact Bayesian inference, this work could enable the application of DPPs to larger-scale problems than were previously feasible. The methods are general and should be broadly applicable. Researchers and practitioners working with DPPs are likely to find these techniques very useful.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty:** The core contribution of introducing non-spectral, inducing-point-based bounds for the DPP likelihood is a significant and original idea.
*   **Generality:** The framework is developed for both finite and continuous DPPs, broadening its applicability.
*   **Practicality:** The paper provides two concrete inference algorithms (VI for speed, MCMC for accuracy) and demonstrates their effectiveness. The computational savings over spectral methods are a major practical advantage.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making a sophisticated technical contribution accessible.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Direct Experimental Comparison:** The main competitor to this approach is the spectral method of Affandi et al. [7]. While the paper argues for its method's superior efficiency, the experimental section lacks a direct empirical comparison. A comparison of runtime and accuracy (e.g., quality of the likelihood approximation) against the spectral method on the diabetic neuropathy dataset would have made the claims of computational advantage more concrete and compelling.
*   **Details on Inducing Point Optimization:** The performance of the method depends on the optimization of the inducing points `Z`. The paper could be strengthened by including more practical details on this process. For instance, a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the initialization of `Z`, the choice of optimizer, and practical heuristics for choosing the number of inducing points `m` (beyond increasing it until results stabilize) would be valuable for others seeking to implement this method.
*   **The L-Kernel Parameterization Issue:** The authors are commendably transparent about the interpretability trade-offs of parameterizing the L-kernel directly, and the toy experiment clearly demonstrates an identifiability issue. While this is a known problem with this parameterization rather than a flaw in the proposed inference method, it might be helpful to briefly discuss if the proposed framework could offer any ways to mitigate this, for instance through the choice of priors in the MCMC scheme.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, well-executed, and significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling. It elegantly connects ideas from Gaussian processes to solve a key problem in inference for determinantal point processes. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate mostly to the experimental comparison, but they do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
