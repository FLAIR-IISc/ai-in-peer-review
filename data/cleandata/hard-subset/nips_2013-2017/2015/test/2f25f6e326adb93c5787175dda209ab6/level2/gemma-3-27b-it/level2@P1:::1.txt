This paper addresses the problem of inference for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), focusing on avoiding the computationally expensive spectral decomposition required for likelihood evaluation. The authors derive bounds on the DPP likelihood, for both finite and continuous domains, that do not rely on spectral knowledge. They then demonstrate how these bounds can be used within variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) frameworks.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the challenges associated with DPP inference and the limitations of existing approaches. The introduction provides a good overview of DPPs and their applications, referencing key works in the field ([1-7]). The problem statement is clearly articulated: the need for efficient inference methods that circumvent the spectral decomposition step. The proposed bounds, based on ideas from variational GPs, appear to be a valuable contribution, addressing a significant bottleneck in DPP inference. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivations of the bounds (Propositions 1 and 2) seem rigorous, building on existing inequalities for determinants. The connection to variational GP bounds provides a strong foundation.
*   **Novelty:** Avoiding spectral decomposition for DPP likelihood evaluation is a non-trivial contribution. The proposed bounds offer a practical alternative to existing methods, particularly for large-scale problems.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the technical details and connecting them to the broader context of DPP inference. The separation of the finite and continuous cases is logical.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experiments, both a synthetic Gaussian example and a real-world dataset (diabetic neuropathy), is a significant strength. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the MCMC approach and the convergence of variational inference with increasing inducing points.
*   **Connections to Related Work:** The authors appropriately cite relevant literature, including work on GPs, DPPs, and spectral methods. They clearly position their work within the existing body of knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost of Pseudo-input Optimization:** While avoiding the spectral decomposition is a major win, the need to optimize the pseudo-inputs (Z) within the MCMC and variational inference frameworks introduces a new computational cost. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between spectral decomposition and pseudo-input optimization would be beneficial. Specifically, how does the cost of optimizing Z scale with problem size?
*   **Interpretability Concerns:** The paper reiterates the loss of interpretability when directly parameterizing L instead of K. This is a valid concern, and while the authors acknowledge it, exploring potential strategies to recover some interpretability (e.g., through post-hoc analysis) could strengthen the work.
*   **Limited Discussion of Prior Selection:** The choice of prior distributions (e.g., in the MCMC experiment) can significantly influence the results. The paper provides limited justification for the specific priors used.
*   **Continuous Case – Assumptions:** The continuous case relies on assumptions regarding the trace-class property of the kernel operator L and Mercer’s theorem. While acknowledged, a more accessible explanation of these assumptions and their implications for practical applications would be helpful.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Existing Alternatives:** While the authors compare against their own "ideal MH" in the synthetic example, the experimental section lacks a direct comparison with other state-of-the-art DPP inference methods that *do* rely on spectral techniques (where feasible), even if limited to smaller problem sizes. This would provide a stronger benchmark for assessing the performance of the proposed approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in DPP inference – avoiding the computationally expensive spectral decomposition.
*   The proposed bounds are novel and theoretically sound.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The potential impact of this work on applications of DPPs is significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The optimization of pseudo-inputs introduces a new computational cost that is not fully analyzed.
*   The loss of interpretability when parameterizing L is a concern.
*   The lack of a direct comparison to existing spectral methods limits the assessment of the proposed approach's advantages.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of DPP inference. The proposed bounds and inference methods offer a valuable alternative to existing approaches, particularly for large-scale problems where spectral decomposition is intractable. While there are some weaknesses, they are outweighed by the strengths of the work. I recommend **acceptance**, with the suggestion that the authors address the computational cost of pseudo-input optimization and provide a more thorough comparison with existing methods in a revised version. Addressing the points raised regarding prior selection and clarifying the assumptions in the continuous case would also be beneficial.
