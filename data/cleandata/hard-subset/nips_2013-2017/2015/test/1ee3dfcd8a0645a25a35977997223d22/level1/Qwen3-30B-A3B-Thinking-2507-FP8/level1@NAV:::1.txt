

This paper addresses graph transduction by proposing Spectral Norm Regularized Orthonormal Embedding (SPORE), a novel approach for learning on graph-structured data. The authors build on prior NIPS work [1, 16] that established orthonormal representations as effective for graph transduction but left open the question of optimal embedding selection. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) it establishes PAC learnability of orthonormal representations for graph transduction, (2) it proposes SPORE as a spectral norm regularized optimization problem over an elliptope, and (3) it introduces the Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method for efficient computation that scales to thousands of vertices.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions with a PAC analysis that improves upon previous sample complexity estimates [16], showing a significant improvement from Ω(ϑ/n)^1/3 to Ω(ϑ/n)^1/2. The IIP algorithm is theoretically justified with O(1/√T) convergence and provides a practical solution to the computational bottleneck of standard SDP approaches. The experimental results on both real-world (UCI datasets) and synthetic graphs demonstrate SPORE's superiority over existing methods, with particularly impressive scalability on large graphs (2000+ vertices). The extension to multiple graph transduction (MKL-SPORE) further enhances the practical utility of the approach.

Strengths include the clear theoretical foundation, the practical algorithmic contribution with strong scalability, and the comprehensive experimental validation. The paper effectively relates its work to prior NIPS contributions [1, 16] while making meaningful improvements. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, though some technical details in the supplementary material could be made more accessible.

Weaknesses include a somewhat limited discussion of the practical implementation details of IIP and the potential limitations of the spectral norm regularization approach. The theoretical analysis of the sample complexity improvement could be more detailed, and the connection between the Lovász θ function and practical graph properties could be strengthened.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution with meaningful PAC analysis, significant scalability improvements over SDP approaches, comprehensive experimental validation on both small and large graphs.

Con: Some technical details could be better explained, limited discussion of potential limitations of the spectral norm regularization approach.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of graph learning with both theoretical and practical significance. The proposed methods are well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically effective, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
