This paper proposes SPORE, a spectral norm regularized orthonormal embedding for graph transduction. The core idea is to leverage recent results showing that embeddings on the unit sphere lead to better generalization, and to formulate a tractable optimization problem based on a PAC bound that doesn't rely on VC dimension but utilizes the Lovász θ function. The authors propose an Infeasible Inexact proximal (IIP) method to efficiently solve the resulting non-smooth convex problem, and demonstrate scalability and improved performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Summary in relation to prior work:** The work builds on [1, 16, 20] which suggest and explore unit-sphere embeddings for graph transduction. It addresses the open problem of finding optimal embeddings and efficient algorithms, a gap highlighted in [16]. The approach differs from [1] by not being restricted to Laplacian matrices and offers insights beyond simply normalizing the kernel. The IIP method is a novel contribution to solving the resulting optimization problem, drawing on proximal algorithms [14, 15, 18] but adapted for the specific context of inexact projections.  The extension to multiple graphs is also a contribution, building on multi-kernel learning [17, 21].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the PAC bound (Theorem 1) appears rigorous, and the formulation of SPORE based on this bound is logical. The IIP algorithm is well-motivated and the convergence analysis (Theorem 5 & 6) seems correct, though relies on supplementary material. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of SPORE over existing methods, particularly in terms of accuracy and scalability.  The extensions to multiple graphs are also well-reasoned and supported by experiments. The authors are careful to evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, acknowledging the dependence on approximating the projection. The analysis, especially the connection between graph structure and sample complexity, is a significant contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions.  The notation is standard and well-defined.  However, some sections (e.g., the convergence analysis of IIP) rely heavily on the supplementary material, which could be less accessible to readers. Providing more details within the main body would enhance clarity. The discussion of related work is comprehensive but could be more concise.  The organization of the experimental section is clear.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel combination of ideas:  a PAC-based analysis for orthonormal embeddings, spectral norm regularization, and an inexact proximal method tailored for the resulting optimization problem.  The IIP algorithm itself is a novel contribution, and the application of this method to SPORE is original. The extension to multiple graphs is a reasonable and valuable extension of the core idea.  While building on prior work, the paper offers significant new insights and algorithms.

**Significance:** The results are important. Improved generalization bounds and a scalable algorithm for graph transduction are valuable contributions. The analysis linking graph structure (via the Lovász θ function) to sample complexity is a significant theoretical result.  The practical demonstration of SPORE's superior performance on real-world datasets highlights its potential impact. The extension to multiple graphs further broadens the applicability of the work. The IIP method is generally applicable and could be useful beyond this specific application.  The work advances the state-of-the-art in graph transduction by offering a more principled and scalable approach.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical foundations:** The PAC-based analysis and connection to the Lovász θ function provide a solid theoretical basis for the proposed method.
*   **Scalability:** The IIP algorithm demonstrates excellent scalability compared to SDP-based approaches.
*   **Empirical performance:**  SPORE consistently outperforms existing methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of ideas and the IIP algorithm are original contributions.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a challenging problem with potentially broad impact.
*   **Multiple graph extension:** Makes the approach applicable to a wider range of scenarios.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   **Reliance on Supplementary Material:** Key details of the proofs and convergence analysis are relegated to the supplementary material, potentially hindering understanding.
*   **Approximation in IIP:** The inexact projection introduces an approximation error, though the authors address this in their analysis.  The sensitivity of performance to the approximation quality could be further investigated.
*   **Parameter tuning:** The paper doesn't provide much guidance on choosing the parameters (e.g., C, β) in practice, relying on cross-validation.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of graph transduction. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the algorithm is efficient and scalable, and the experimental results are compelling. While the reliance on supplementary material and the approximation in the IIP method are minor concerns, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I **strongly recommend acceptance**.
