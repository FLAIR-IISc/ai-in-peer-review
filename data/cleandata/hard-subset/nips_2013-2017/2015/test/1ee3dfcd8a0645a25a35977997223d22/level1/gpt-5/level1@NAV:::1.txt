This paper studies graph transduction via unit-sphere embeddings, focusing on orthonormal representations, and proposes a spectral-norm–regularized formulation (SPORE) with both PAC-style generalization bounds and a scalable first-order solver (IIP). Building on earlier generalization results for Laplacian-based kernels [1] and consistency results for orthonormal representations [16], the authors derive a high-probability bound (Theorem 1) in which the excess term depends on the spectral norm λ1(K), thereby motivating spectral regularization. They connect the feasible elliptope to the Lovász θ function by showing maxK∈K(G) λ1(K) = θ(Ḡ), yielding graph-dependent rates and an improved labeled sample complexity that scales with √(θ/n), improving on the θ/n)^{1/3} rate in [16]. They instantiate these insights in the convex SPORE objective, and avoid SDPs via an infeasible inexact proximal method using proximal operators of support functions and FISTA for composite projections; they prove O(1/√T) convergence for non-smooth objectives. Experiments on UCI/MNIST and synthetic multi-graph settings show gains over Laplacian kernels and K-scaling, with an MKL extension improving robustness and rates when at least one graph is “good,” echoing multi-view results (cf. [18], NIPS’ spectral SSL).

Quality: The theoretical development is careful, with a clear chain from generalization to algorithm design. The IIP analysis for non-smooth objectives with approximate projections contributes beyond standard inexact proximal-gradient results (e.g., Schmidt et al., NIPS). However, some dependencies (choice of C, β) rely on θ(G) and θ(Ḡ), which are expensive to compute; practical selection via CV breaks the clean PAC prescription. Empirical validation is positive but limited in scale and baselines.

Clarity: Generally well-organized but dense; several typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., SDP complexity is cited as O(n) in abstract vs O(n^6) later). Algorithm 1 has variable shadowing and could be clarified.

Originality: The spectral-norm lens on orthonormal embeddings and the θ-link for bounds are interesting; the IIP framework for infeasible approximate projections on intersections seems novel.

Significance: Potentially impactful for scalable graph SSL beyond SDPs, with extensions to multi-graph settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled bound motivating a concrete, convex algorithm tied to θ(G).
- Novel inexact proximal methodology for non-smooth objectives with approximate projections.
- Improved sample complexity over prior orthonormal-embedding analyses.
- Empirical gains and multi-graph robustness.

Cons for acceptance:
- Parameter choices in theory require θ; practical guidance unclear.
- Experimental scale modest; lacks comparisons to strong modern baselines (label propagation, graph neural methods).
- Clarity issues and minor inconsistencies.

Suggestions: Report wall-clock vs SDP on larger graphs (≥10k nodes); ablate spectral term (β) and projection accuracy; compare to label propagation and recent GNN-based transduction; clarify Algorithm 1 and complexity statements.
