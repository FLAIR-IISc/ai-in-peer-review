This paper addresses the open problem of selecting an optimal unit-sphere graph embedding for graph transduction, an issue raised by prior work [1]. The authors focus on the class of orthonormal representations and make three primary contributions. First, they derive a novel PAC-based generalization bound that, unlike previous analyses, does not rely on VC dimension but instead depends on the spectral norm of the embedding's kernel matrix. Second, this theoretical result directly motivates a new formulation, SPectral regularized ORthonormal Embedding (SPORE), which minimizes a convex objective composed of the empirical loss and a spectral norm regularizer over the elliptope. Third, to overcome the poor scalability of standard semi-definite programming (SDP) solvers for this problem, the authors develop a novel, efficient first-order algorithm called the Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method, providing an O(1/√T) convergence guarantee.

**Quality and Originality:** The paper is of high quality, presenting a technically sound and complete piece of research. The main strength lies in its elegant and principled connection between a new theoretical analysis and a practical algorithm. The PAC bound is an original contribution that provides fresh insight into the problem. The resulting SPORE formulation is novel, and the IIP algorithm is a valuable contribution for optimizing over elliptopes, complementing existing work on inexact proximal methods by handling non-smooth objectives. Furthermore, the paper improves upon the sample complexity bounds from recent NIPS papers [16].

**Significance:** The contributions are significant and likely to influence future work. The SPORE method advances the state of the art in graph transduction, as demonstrated by strong empirical results on several UCI and MNIST datasets where it outperforms established Laplacian-based baselines. The development of a scalable solver makes the approach practical for graphs with thousands of nodes, a clear advantage over SDP-based methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. However, one point could be clarified: the theoretical choice of hyperparameters C and β depends on the Lovász θ function, which is itself hard to compute. The paper should briefly discuss this disconnect with the practical approach of using cross-validation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and well-motivated PAC bound that directly informs the proposed method.
*   Introduces SPORE, a principled and effective formulation for graph transduction.
*   Develops a scalable first-order algorithm (IIP) with theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrates superior performance over strong baselines in comprehensive experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical selection of hyperparameters could be discussed in more detail in light of the theoretical analysis.

This is an excellent paper with strong theoretical and practical contributions. I recommend acceptance.
