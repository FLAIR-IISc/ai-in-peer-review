- Addresses graph transduction by learning orthonormal-representation kernels (PSD with unit diagonal and zeros for non-edges) for semi-supervised learning on graphs.

- Contribution 1: Derives a new PAC-style generalization bound that depends on the kernel spectral norm (largest eigenvalue λ1(K)) rather than trace.

- Contribution 2: Proposes SPORE, a convex kernel-learning objective combining SVM hinge loss with a spectral-norm regularizer over the elliptope, and provides PAC analysis linking error to the Lovász ϑ function.

- Contribution 3: Introduces IIP, a scalable first-order projected subgradient method with inexact projections (implemented via FISTA) and O(1/√T) convergence guarantees for the non-smooth SPORE problem.

- Contribution 4: Extends the SPORE framework naturally to multi-graph (MKL-SPORE) settings with corresponding theoretical and algorithmic generalizations.

- Quality: Theoretical claims are well-supported with proofs; the IIP algorithm and its convergence analysis are substantial technical contributions; experiments show SPORE outperforms standard baselines including on large datasets.

- Clarity: Paper is well-structured but dense, assuming strong background in convex optimization and spectral graph theory; key algorithmic details and constants are deferred to the supplement, affecting immediate reproducibility.

- Originality: Novel spectral-norm-based generalization bound, principled SPORE formulation, a new IIP optimization method with dual approximate projection criteria, and a novel connection to the Lovász ϑ function.

- Significance: Combines PAC-justified kernel learning with a scalable, provably convergent algorithm, making learned graph kernels practical and broadly impactful; IIP may apply beyond this problem.

- Arguments for acceptance: Provides a principled theoretical motivation, introduces an effective kernel-learning model, contributes a novel scalable algorithm with guarantees, achieves strong empirical results, and offers new insights linking graph structure to learnability.

- Arguments against acceptance: Paper density limits accessibility; important algorithmic proofs/details are in the supplement; lacks a runtime comparison versus SDP solvers on small graphs to empirically quantify scalability gains.

- Overall recommendation: Strong, original work with minor weaknesses; recommend acceptance.
