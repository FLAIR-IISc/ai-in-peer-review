- Addresses graph transduction with focus on orthonormal (unit-sphere) graph embeddings and efficient optimal embedding selection.
- Introduces a novel PAC learning analysis for orthonormal embeddings using Rademacher averages because VC dimension is infinite for this class.
- Proposes SPORE: a spectral-norm-regularized formulation motivated by the theoretical analysis linking spectral norm to generalization.
- Theorem 1: provides a high-probability generalization bound explicitly connecting the kernel spectral norm to error, implying low spectral-norm embeddings generalize better.
- Theorem 4: derives a refined sample complexity Ω(√(ϑ/n)) (improving on prior Ω((ϑ/n)^(1/3))), linking convergence to the Lovász θ function.
- Interprets the Lovász θ connection: denser graphs (small θ) need fewer labeled vertices than sparser graphs (large θ).
- Presents IIP (Infeasible Inexact Proximal) algorithm that achieves O(1/√T) convergence for non-smooth convex optimization over the elliptope while avoiding costly exact projections.
- IIP scales to thousands of vertices (experiments up to 2000) versus SDPs limited to hundreds, showing significant practical scalability.
- Empirical results: SPORE and MKL-SPORE significantly outperform existing methods and show robustness to noisy multiple graphs.
- Extension to multiple graphs (MKL-SPORE) is well-motivated and empirically effective.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility and relation to prior work.
- Strengths: novel theory resolving prior limitations, well-motivated SPORE, scalable IIP algorithm, strong empirical gains, and insightful graph-structure/error rate link.
- Weaknesses: needs clearer justification for using Lovász θ over other graph properties, more detailed IIP complexity/constant-factor comparison with SDP solvers, tighter link between Theorem 4 and experimental results, and lacks comparison to newer graph transduction methods (e.g., GNNs).
- Overall assessment: technically strong, significant theoretical and algorithmic contributions, convincing experiments — recommendation: accept.
