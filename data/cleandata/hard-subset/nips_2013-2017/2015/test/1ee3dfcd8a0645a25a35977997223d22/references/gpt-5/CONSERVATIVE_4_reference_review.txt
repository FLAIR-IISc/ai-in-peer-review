Summary
The paper studies graph transduction via orthonormal representations (unit-sphere embeddings with zero inner products on non-edges) and proposes a spectral-norm-regularized kernel learning formulation (SPORE). Starting from a dual SVM-like objective over graph kernels constrained to the “elliptope” K(G), the authors prove a high-probability generalization bound where the excess term depends on the spectral norm λ1(K). This motivates adding a βλ1(K) penalty and optimizing over K(G). They then provide PAC-style labeled sample complexity bounds expressed via the Lovász theta function θ(G), showing improved dependence over prior work and arguing that dense graphs need fewer labels. To solve the convex problem at scale, they introduce an “infeasible inexact proximal” (IIP) projected subgradient method that approximates projections onto the intersection S+ ∩ P(G) using proximal operators of the associated support functions and FISTA. They extend SPORE to multiple graphs via a MKL-style combination and present empirical results on UCI/MNIST and synthetic multi-graph data.

Strengths
- Clear connection between generalization and spectral norm that motivates a convex spectral penalty; the high-probability bound is intuitively appealing and improves on earlier expected-risk analyses.
- Leveraging orthonormal representations and θ(G) to obtain graph-structure-dependent PAC rates is interesting; the dense-vs-sparse interpretation is useful.
- Algorithmic effort to avoid SDP: the inexact proximal approach and decomposition over intersection sets are reasonable and potentially more scalable than interior-point SDPs.
- Multi-graph extension is natural; the theory suggests benefits with at least one “good” graph, and synthetic experiments reflect robustness.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Parameter dependence on θ(G), θ(Ḡ): The labeled sample complexity and the choice of C, β in Theorem 4 depend on θ(G) and θ(Ḡ), which require solving SDPs and are unavailable in practice on large graphs. In experiments, parameters are chosen by cross-validation, so the theory does not inform practice. Please discuss practical proxies or bounds for θ that can guide parameter selection.

- Sample complexity derivation: The bound er ≤ O((1/m(√(nθ)+log 1/δ))1/2) implies m = Ω((√(nθ)+log 1/δ)/ε2) to achieve error ≤ ε. The stated m̃ seems to drop the ε dependence and constants. Please correct the statement and compare to [16] under the same ε, δ.

- Novelty of the optimization method: The IIP scheme, approximate projections via proximal of support functions, and use of FISTA align closely with established inexact proximal-gradient literature (e.g., Schmidt et al. 2011; Villa et al. 2013; Parikh & Boyd). The “novel approximation criterion” appears to be a standard dual gap criterion for proximal accuracy. The paper would benefit from a more precise positioning, explicit differences, and ablations versus simpler alternatives (Dykstra with finite-iteration truncation, ADMM on the intersection, conditional gradient on the elliptope, spectral projected gradient). As presented, the algorithmic novelty is modest.

- Technical clarity and completeness: Many essential details are deferred to the supplement, including proofs of Lemmas 2–3 and the explicit proximal operators for σA (PSD cone) and σB (affine polytope). The feasibility step (line 9) is vague: “Kt needs to be psd for the next SVM call. Use (14).” How is PSD enforced when the approximation is infeasible? Are there guarantees that the inexact projection yields K ∈ S+ ∩ P(G) within a tolerance? The constants used in step-size selection (L = nC2, R = n, R̂ = n2.5) look very loose; please justify and provide Lipschitz constants for subgradients of ωC and λ1.

- Potential degeneracy: With K constrained by Kii=1 and Kij=0 for non-edges, minimizing λ1(K) encourages K close to I. Without careful β tuning, the method could suppress transduction (K near I yields no label propagation). Some empirical or theoretical analysis of this trade-off (e.g., ablation on β; effect on off-diagonal entries for edges) is needed.

- Experimental validation is limited:
  - Baselines: Only Laplacian kernels and K-Scaling are included. Missing strong SSL/transductive baselines: label propagation/harmonic functions (Zhu & Ghahramani), manifold regularization/LLGC (Zhou et al.), recent scalable graph SSL, and orthonormal representation methods (e.g., Shivanna & Bhattacharyya). On MNIST, modern GNNs (GCN/SGC/label propagation variants) are relevant, even in transductive settings. Without these, the empirical superiority is not compelling.
  - Datasets/scale: Graphs are thresholded similarities on small subsets (500–2000 nodes). Claims of scalability beyond “few hundred” are weak by current standards; comparisons of wall-clock/runtime and convergence vs SDP, ADMM, or Dykstra are absent.
  - Hyperparameter selection: With only 5–10% labels, cross-validating C and β is prone to overfitting/noise; describe the protocol and variance. Include sensitivity analyses.
  - Multi-graph: Only synthetic mixtures are shown; real multi-view graphs (e.g., bioinformatics or web graphs) would substantiate claims.

- Presentation issues: Several references are incomplete (missing years, venues). Notation is sometimes heavy and inconsistent. The main text relies heavily on the supplement for key definitions and algorithmic steps, hindering reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. How are proxσS+ and proxσP(G) computed in closed form? Please provide explicit formulas and complexity. How is PSD enforced after inexact steps?
2. Can you provide practical θ proxies (e.g., spectral bounds, degree-based surrogates) to set C, β without cross-validation?
3. How does IIP compare empirically to ADMM/Dykstra/Frank–Wolfe in accuracy, runtime, and memory on the same problems?
4. Can you include ablations on β, and report the fraction of nonzero edge entries in K* to show non-degenerate embeddings?
5. Please revisit the labeled sample complexity derivation and include ε, δ dependencies.

Pros
- Conceptually clean bound linking spectral norm to generalization.
- PAC analysis tied to θ(G) offers structural insight.
- Reasonable first-order approach to avoid SDP; convex formulation.

Cons
- Practical impact limited by dependence on θ; missing strong baselines and large-scale validation.
- Algorithmic novelty over established inexact proximal methods is unclear.
- Some theoretical statements appear imprecise; essential details deferred.
- Empirical evaluation lacks breadth, ablations, and runtime comparisons.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The theoretical direction is interesting and the convex formulation is sensible, but the current submission falls short on methodological clarity, empirical validation against established baselines, scale, and careful positioning relative to existing optimization methods. Strengthening the theory (correct sample complexity), clarifying and justifying the optimization details, and substantially expanding experiments would make this work much more compelling.

Confidence: High.
