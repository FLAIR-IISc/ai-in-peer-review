- Paper proposes SPORE: a convex program that learns a kernel K in the elliptope by minimizing the SVM dual plus a spectral-norm penalty, with an IIP algorithm and MKL extension; experiments up to 2000 nodes.  
- Theorem 1: 0-1 error is bounded by empirical hinge loss plus a term scaling with C√λ1(K), motivating spectral-norm regularization; convexity and elliptope constraints are correct.  
- Use of Lovász theta (θ(G), θ(Ḡ)) to bound λ1(K) and relate sample complexity is plausible and aligns with SDP characterizations, but some proofs are deferred.  
- Critical concern: the paper claims θ(G)·θ(Ḡ)=n, but generally only θ(G)·θ(Ḡ) ≥ n holds; any reliance on equality in Theorem 4 must be corrected or justified.  
- Practicality of PAC bound is limited because Theorem 4 selects C and β based on θ, which is expensive to compute; this weakens practical parameter guidance.  
- Algorithm (IIP) is reasonable and achieves O(1/√T), but novelty is modest as inexact proximal/splitting ideas are known; the elliptope instantiation is useful.  
- Some algorithmic constants (e.g., L=nC^2, R=n, R̂=n^2.5) appear loose and may affect step-size choices in practice.  
- Proximal operators for S+ and P(G) are relegated to the supplement; these should be clarified in the main text and Algorithm 1 notation fixed (inconsistent use of Kt).  
- Experiments show gains over Laplacian kernels and K-scaling but baselines are limited; missing classical graph SSL (label propagation, harmonic functions) and modern GNN transductive baselines.  
- No ablation on spectral-norm regularization (β), no sensitivity analysis, and no convergence/runtime comparisons with SDP or other first-order methods.  
- Multi-graph experiments are synthetic only; a real multi-view benchmark would strengthen empirical claims.  
- Paper is dense and relies heavily on the supplement for proofs and key proximal expressions, making it less self-contained and harder to follow.  
- Notation and algorithmic steps (e.g., projection lines in Algorithm 1) are confusing; references sometimes lack years and assumptions for “similarity graphs” are not formalized.  
- Originality: combining orthonormal representations, spectral-norm regularization, and linking generalization to Lovász theta is novel and insightful.  
- Optimization contribution is incremental (adapting inexact proximal methods to the elliptope) but practically relevant for scalability of such formulations.  
- MKL extension is natural; the theoretical bound that benefits from the best graph is expected but still useful.  
- Significance: theoretical link between spectral norm, θ, and transductive generalization is valuable; optimization approach could enable more scalable elliptope-regularized models but demonstrated scale is modest.  
- Pros: clear convex formulation, principled regularization, generalization bound depending on λ1(K), first-order algorithm with guarantees, and empirical gains on several datasets.  
- Cons: incorrect/unqualified θ(G)·θ(Ḡ) claim, theory depends on hard-to-compute θ, limited experimental baselines/ablations/scalability, and clarity issues with heavy supplement reliance.  
- Suggestions: correct/justify the θ product statement; provide practical approximations or robust schedules for θ-based parameter choices; add baselines (label propagation, manifold regularization, GNNs); include β ablation, sensitivity and runtime/SDF comparisons; formalize similarity-graph assumptions and expose proximal formulas in the main text.  
- Overall: solid theoretical ideas and a reasonable algorithm with promising but limited experiments; with corrections to the Lovász theta relation, stronger empirical evaluation, and improved clarity, the work would be a stronger contribution.
