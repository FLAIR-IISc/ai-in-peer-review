Summary
This paper studies graph transduction via orthonormal representations and introduces SPORE: a convex kernel-learning formulation over the elliptope that regularizes by the spectral norm (largest eigenvalue) of the kernel. The authors first derive a high-probability generalization bound for the SVM-like dual ωC(K, yS) that depends on the empirical hinge loss on labeled nodes and √λ1(K), suggesting spectral norm control is beneficial. They then analyze SPORE and connect its optimal value to Lovász theta numbers, proving PAC labeled sample complexity bounds with explicit graph dependence via ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ḡ), using the relation ϑ(G)ϑ(Ḡ) = n. Algorithmically, they propose an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method: a projected subgradient scheme where the projection onto the feasible set K(G) = S+ ∩ P(G) is approximated via the proximal operator of the support function, solved with FISTA on the split formulation. They prove O(1/√T) convergence despite inexact, potentially infeasible projections under a dual optimality criterion. They extend to multiple graphs (MKL-SPORE) with a max spectral-norm regularizer and show a bound governed by the best individual graph’s theta. Experiments on UCI/MNIST graphs and synthetic multi-graph settings show consistent gains over Laplacian baselines and K-scaling.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Clear, elegant link between generalization for graph transduction and spectral norm of orthonormal embeddings, leading to an explicit, convex spectral-norm-regularized kernel learning formulation over the elliptope.
- Theoretical contributions: High-probability bound depending on λ1(K); PAC analysis for SPORE via Lovász theta that ties error rates to graph structure; MKL extension with a bound depending on the best graph. The use of ϑ(Ḡ) = maxK∈K(G) λ1(K) is insightful.
- Algorithmic contribution: IIP method for optimizing nonsmooth convex objectives with approximate projections onto an intersection of convex sets, with convergence guarantees based on a dual proximal approximation criterion. The FISTA-based construction for prox of support functions for intersections is neat and potentially reusable.
- Empirical results: SPORE outperforms several Laplacian kernel baselines across datasets. The multi-graph setting shows robustness to noisy graphs, consistent with theory.

Weaknesses
- Sample complexity derivation appears inconsistent. From (6), er = O( sqrt((√n ϑ(G) + log)/m) ). Solving for m to achieve error ≤ ε suggests m ≳ √n ϑ(G)/ε^2, implying m/n ≳ ϑ(G)/(ε^2 √n), not Ω((ϑ/n)1/2) as claimed. For θ ≈ √n (random graphs), this yields a constant fraction of labels, contrary to the claimed n−1/4 fraction. This discrepancy needs clarification or correction.
- Practical parameter selection: The bound-driven choices C, β depend on ϑ(G), ϑ(Ḡ), which are SDP-hard to compute; experiments instead use cross-validation. Please discuss principled estimators or surrogates, and the sensitivity of accuracy to β, C.
- Experimental baselines and analysis: Comparisons are primarily to Laplacian kernels and K-scaling. Missing are strong graph SSL baselines (e.g., label propagation, harmonic functions), and comparisons to direct SDP relaxations (even on small graphs) or to other first-order methods (Dykstra/ADMM/DR splitting) to isolate the optimization contribution. Ablations on the effect of the spectral-norm term, and of inexact vs exact projections, would strengthen claims.
- Scalability: The largest experiment is n=2000, with few outer/inner iterations. Some indication of scaling behavior (wall-clock vs n, effect of T and inner S) and memory profile would be helpful, as well as comparisons to simple projected methods using standard PSD and affine projections.
- Clarity/Presentation: The paper is dense; several key steps are deferred to the supplement. Some notation and footnotes are a bit terse; reference years are missing. A clearer statement of assumptions (e.g., “similarity graph”) would help.

Quality
Technically solid overall: the convexity of SPORE, subgradient characterization, and convergence of IIP are sound. The θ-based bounds are interesting. The main concern is the sample complexity scaling; if corrected, the qualitative message (denser graphs need fewer labels) likely survives.

Clarity
Generally well organized, but heavy. The main ideas are understandable; however, more intuition around the spectral norm penalty’s effect on embeddings and a short algorithm box in the main text would help.

Originality
High conceptually (spectral norm regularization for orthonormal graph embeddings and θ-based generalization analysis). The optimization component is incremental but thoughtfully adapted (dual prox of support functions, infeasible inexact projections for nonsmooth f with guarantees).

Significance
Moderate to high. The connection between graph structure (via θ), generalization, and a tractable convex formulation is valuable, and the IIP approach may apply beyond this problem. The empirical improvements are consistent though not uniformly large; the multi-graph robustness is appealing.

Questions for authors
- Please reconcile the labeled sample complexity derivation with Eq. (6). If our algebra is off, provide a clear derivation in the main text.
- How should practitioners set β and C without access to ϑ(G), ϑ(Ḡ)? Are there computable upper bounds or data-driven proxies?
- What are the explicit forms of prox for σS+ and σP(G)? A brief summary in the main text would aid reproducibility.
- Can you compare IIP to Dykstra/DR/ADMM and to an SDP solver on small graphs, and provide ablations on the spectral-norm term?
- How sensitive are results to graph construction (thresholds, weighted vs unweighted)?

Pros
- Novel, principled formulation with insightful theory (θ-based).
- General, reusable optimization idea with guarantees for inexact projections.
- Robust MKL extension with theory and experiments.
- Consistent empirical gains.

Cons
- Apparent mistake/inconsistency in sample complexity scaling.
- Limited baselines and ablations; parameter setting practicality under-discussed.
- Scalability evidence modest; clarity could improve.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). Despite some weaknesses, the paper advances a fresh and promising direction (spectral-norm-regularized orthonormal embeddings for graph SSL) with both theory and an implementable algorithm. If the sample complexity issue is corrected/clarified and empirical comparisons broadened, this could be a strong contribution.

Confidence
3.5/5 (moderate).
