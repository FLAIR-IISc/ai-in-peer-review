- Paper proposes SPORE: optimize unit-norm orthonormal node embeddings (zeros on non-edges) by minimizing a convex SVM-dual loss plus spectral-norm penalty over the elliptope K(G).
- Theorem 1 gives a high-probability generalization bound where the leading complexity term depends on the spectral norm λ1(K), motivating spectral-norm regularization.
- Authors relate extremal λ1(K) over K(G) to the Lovász θ function and derive PAC-style labeled sample complexity bounds (Theorem 4) claiming improvement over prior work.
- Because the SDP is costly, they introduce an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) projected subgradient method approximating projections via proximal operators of support functions, with O(1/√T) convergence.
- Method is extended to multiple graphs via an MKL-style convex combination and shows empirical gains on UCI, MNIST subsets, and synthetic multi-graph data.
- Strength: clear conceptual chain from λ1(K)-dependent bound to SPORE and θ(G)-based PAC rates.
- Strength: novel use of θ(G)/θ(Ḡ) to tie orthonormal representation geometry to sample complexity.
- Strength: convex formulation over the elliptope that is in principle scalable with first-order methods.
- Strength: IIP proximal-support approach is a generic tool potentially useful beyond this application.
- Strength: empirical results consistently outperform Laplacian baselines and K-scaling; multi-graph extension appears robust to noise.
- Weakness: Theorem 4’s use of ϑ(G)ϑ(Ḡ)=n is generally false; equality holds only for special graphs, so scope or bounds need clarification or correction.
- Weakness: Lemma 2’s claim max_{K∈K(G)} λ1(K)=ϑ(Ḡ) depends on zero-pattern conventions (edges vs non-edges) and needs explicit mapping to standard θ formulations to avoid complement confusion.
- Weakness: Main bound’s dependence on m is odd—C must scale with m (e.g., C∝m^{-1/2}) to avoid vacuity; the choice and empirical compatibility of C need justification.
- Weakness: Inconsistent loss definitions—squared hinge is used but duals appear SVM-like unsquared—requiring unification to avoid incorrect constants/rates.
- Weakness: Theorem 1’s transductive Rademacher-style analysis is central but relegated to supplement; a self-contained proof outline in the main text is needed.
- Weakness: Practical prox implementation depends on closed-form prox for support functions of S+ and affine sets; well-posedness and formulas should be made explicit in the main text.
- Weakness: Overlap with known splitting methods (Douglas–Rachford, ADMM); novelty over these methods is not empirically demonstrated or discussed sufficiently.
- Weakness: Per-iteration cost (SVM solve + dominant eigenvector) may be expensive; abstract cost notation (cp,c0) insufficient—need profiling and scalability analysis.
- Weakness: Experimental issues—graph binarization (thresholding) may disadvantage Laplacian baselines; weighted heat-kernel graphs should be reported.
- Weakness: Hyperparameter tuning via CV on few transductive labels can be unstable; clarify CV protocol, report variance/error bars over more runs.
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to strong transductive/SSL baselines (label propagation, graph trend filtering, local/global consistency) and to established multi-graph/MKL SSL methods.
- Weakness: Key lemmas, prox formulas, and important claims are deferred to supplement; more of these should appear in the main paper.
- Recommendation: Clarify/correct θ identities and state bounds under ϑ(G)ϑ(Ḡ)≥n, unify loss definitions, provide clearer proof outlines, detail prox computations, discuss alternative splitting baselines, and strengthen experiments (weighted graphs, more runs, stronger baselines).
- Overall: Interesting and principled contribution linking spectral-norm regularization, orthonormal representations, and Lovász θ with promising empirical results, but theoretical misuses of θ identities, optimization-context gaps, and experimental weaknesses make the paper borderline without revisions.
