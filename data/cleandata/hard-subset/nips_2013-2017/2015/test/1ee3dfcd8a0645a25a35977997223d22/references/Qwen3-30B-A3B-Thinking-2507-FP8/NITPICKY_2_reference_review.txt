

# Review of "Spectral Norm Regularization of Orthonormal Representations for Graph Transduction"

This paper proposes a novel approach to graph transduction called SPORE (SPectral norm regularized ORthonormal Embedding), which regularizes the spectral norm of orthonormal representations. The authors claim to show that orthonormal representations are efficiently PAC learnable and propose a novel inexact proximal method (IIP) to solve the resulting optimization problem. The paper also extends the approach to multiple graphs using an MKL-style formulation.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by connecting orthonormal representations to graph transduction and proposing a spectral norm regularization approach. The PAC analysis in Theorem 4 provides a meaningful improvement over previous work, particularly in the sample complexity estimates. The experimental results on both small and large graphs are promising, showing consistent improvements over baseline methods. The IIP algorithm is novel and the convergence analysis is technically sound.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inconsistencies and Errors
1. The paper claims "K(G) is an Elliptope [5], a convex body which can be described by the intersection of a positive semi-definite and affine constraints." This is factually incorrect. An elliptope is specifically defined as the set of positive semidefinite matrices with ones on the diagonal (not just any intersection of PSD and affine constraints). The elliptope is a specific convex body, not a general description of such intersections.

2. Theorem 1 contains a dimensionally inconsistent term: "2C √2λ1(K)". The term λ1(K) is a scalar (eigenvalue), but its units don't match the other terms in the bound, suggesting a mathematical error.

3. Lemma 2 states "maxK∈K(G) λ1(K) = ϑ(Ḡ)" without explaining the relationship between the Lovász ϑ function of a graph and its complement. The paper cites [13] as the source for this identity, but this is not a standard result (the Lovász ϑ function is not directly related to the complement graph in this manner).

4. The paper claims "ϑ(G)ϑ(Ḡ) = n [13]" but this is not true for all graphs. This identity holds for certain specific graphs (like complete graphs), but not generally. The paper should clarify the specific graph families for which this holds.

5. The proof sketch of Theorem 4 contains an inconsistency: "C ∑ i∈S `hng(yi, ŷi) ≤ ωC(K∗,yS)" where the left side is a sum over S and the right side is a single value. This is mathematically incorrect.

### Notational and Formatting Issues
1. The paper uses inconsistent notation for the complement set (S̄, S̄, S̄, etc.), which appears to be a typesetting error rather than a consistent choice.

2. The paper uses "trp(K)" without clearly defining the parameter p, which is confusing as p appears to be an arbitrary parameter.

3. The paper uses "λ1(K)" to denote the spectral norm, which is technically incorrect. For symmetric matrices, the spectral norm is |λ_max|, but for positive semidefinite matrices, λ1(K) (the largest eigenvalue) is equal to the spectral norm. However, the notation is ambiguous and should be clarified.

4. The paper states "the final predictions are given by ŷi = ∑ j∈S Kijα ∗ jyj ∀i ∈ [n]" but doesn't clarify what K is here (the kernel matrix or the embedding matrix).

### Experimental and Methodological Concerns
1. The paper claims to use "RBF kernel" for UCI datasets but doesn't explain how the RBF kernel is constructed from the graph structure, which is confusing since graph transduction typically uses graph-based kernels rather than feature-based kernels.

2. The experimental section refers to "KS" (K-Scaling) without defining it, making it difficult for readers to understand what method is being compared.

3. The paper claims to "use a similarity matrix using cosine distance for a random sample of 500 nodes" for MNIST, but doesn't explain why a random sample is used rather than the full graph.

4. The paper states "With 10% labelled nodes" but doesn't specify whether this is 10% of the total nodes or 10% of the unlabeled nodes, which is critical information for reproducibility.

### Theoretical Gaps
1. The paper claims to "show the class of orthonormal representations are efficiently PAC learnable" but refers to supplementary material for the proof. The main paper should include the key steps of this proof.

2. The paper cites [1] as the source for a generalization bound but the reference doesn't match the content described. The cited reference "Learning on graph with Laplacian regularization, R.K. Ando, T. Zhang, NIPS" is not a match for the bound described in the paper.

3. The paper states "the IIP algorithm is then applied to the case when the set of interest is composed of the intersection of two convex sets" but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how the intersection is handled in practice.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting approach to graph transduction with potentially valuable theoretical insights, the numerous technical inconsistencies, notational errors, and unclear methodological descriptions significantly undermine the paper's technical soundness. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too numerous to overlook, especially given the paper's claim of providing a rigorous theoretical analysis.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these technical inconsistencies, clarify the notation, and provide sufficient detail about the methods and proofs. The current version contains too many mathematical errors and ambiguities to be considered a complete scientific contribution.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial technical flaws that prevent the paper from being evaluated on its merits. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
