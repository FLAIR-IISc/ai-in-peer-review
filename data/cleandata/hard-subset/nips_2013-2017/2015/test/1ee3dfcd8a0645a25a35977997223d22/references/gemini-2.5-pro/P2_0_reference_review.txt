This paper addresses the problem of graph transduction by learning an optimal kernel from the class of orthonormal representations. The authors' central idea is that while prior work established the statistical consistency of this class and the benefit of unit-norm embeddings, it did not provide a principle for selecting the *best* embedding. The paper makes several contributions to address this: 1) It derives a new high-probability generalization bound for graph transduction that explicitly depends on the spectral norm of the kernel matrix. 2) Motivated by this bound, it proposes a new formulation, SPORE (SPectral norm regularized ORthonormal Embedding), which minimizes a combination of the transductive SVM objective and the kernel's spectral norm over the elliptope. 3) To solve this challenging optimization problem, it develops a novel first-order method called the Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm, providing a convergence analysis. 4) It extends the framework to a multiple-graph (MKL) setting and provides experimental validation for both the single and multiple graph cases.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated. The derivation of a new generalization bound (Theorem 1) that highlights the importance of the spectral norm is a key step that provides a solid foundation for the rest of the work. The subsequent PAC analysis (Theorem 4), which yields an improved sample complexity estimate of O((ϑ/n)^(1/2)) compared to the O((ϑ/n)^(1/3)) from prior work, is a strong theoretical result. This analysis correctly links the difficulty of the learning problem to graph-structural properties (via the Lovász ϑ number), which is an important insight.

The algorithmic contribution is also strong. Recognizing that the SPORE formulation is a non-smooth convex optimization problem over an elliptope (a difficult constraint set), the authors rightly avoid standard SDP solvers that do not scale. The proposed IIP algorithm, which uses an approximate projection sub-routine, is a practical and well-justified approach. The provided O(1/√T) convergence guarantee (Theorem 5) for a non-smooth objective with inexact projections is a valuable result. The experiments, while not exhaustive, are carefully designed and convincingly demonstrate that the proposed SPORE method outperforms several standard graph kernel baselines across a range of datasets.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, but extremely dense. It covers a significant amount of ground—generalization theory, PAC analysis, a novel optimization algorithm, and an MKL extension—in a very limited space. This density sometimes sacrifices clarity for brevity. For instance, the proof sketch for Theorem 4 is very high-level, and the reader must heavily rely on the supplementary material to understand the derivation. Similarly, the description of the IIP algorithm in Section 4 is highly compressed. Algorithm 1 is presented at a high level, with the core projection mechanism ("IIP FISTA") deferred to the supplement, making it difficult for a reader to fully grasp the mechanics of the proposed solver from the main text alone. While this is often necessary for conference papers, expanding slightly on the intuition behind the IIP FISTA steps in the main text would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While graph transduction and orthonormal representations are established topics, the contributions here are novel and significant.
1.  **Formulation:** The SPORE formulation (Eq. 5) is, to my knowledge, novel. It is directly and elegantly motivated by a new theoretical bound, which represents a principled approach to kernel selection in this space, moving beyond heuristics or standard Laplacian constructions.
2.  **Algorithm:** The IIP algorithm, particularly its convergence analysis for non-smooth functions with the specific dual approximation criterion (Eq. 11), appears to be a novel contribution to the literature on inexact proximal methods. While related work exists, the authors correctly point out that their analysis extends to the non-smooth case, which is crucial for their objective function containing the max-margin term and the spectral norm.
3.  **Analysis:** The improved sample complexity bound is a concrete and original improvement over existing results for this problem class.

The paper does a good job of situating its contributions with respect to prior work, clearly articulating how it improves upon the analysis of Ando & Zhang [1] and the consistency results of Shivanna & Bhattacharyya [16].

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners.
*   It provides a practical and theoretically-grounded method for graph transduction that demonstrably outperforms standard baselines. The connection between the generalization error and the Lovász ϑ number provides valuable intuition about which types of graphs are easier to learn on.
*   The IIP algorithm is a general-purpose tool that could be applied to other optimization problems involving difficult projection steps, particularly those involving intersections of convex sets like the elliptope. This algorithmic contribution is of independent interest to the optimization community.
*   The extension to the multi-graph setting (MKL-SPORE) is timely and addresses a growing need in applications like bioinformatics and social network analysis where multiple views of data are common. The theoretical result showing that the performance depends on the *best* graph in the set is a powerful motivation for this approach.

**Strengths:**
*   Strong, clear connection between theory (generalization bound) and a practical formulation (SPORE).
*   Novel and efficient first-order optimization algorithm (IIP) with convergence guarantees for a difficult non-smooth problem.
*   Improved and insightful PAC sample complexity analysis that connects learnability to graph structure.
*   Comprehensive work, spanning theory, algorithm design, and empirical validation.
*   Consistently strong experimental results against relevant baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper is very dense, which can make it difficult to follow. Key components, especially the details of the IIP algorithm, are largely deferred to the supplement.
*   The experimental evaluation, while positive, could be strengthened. The "large scale" experiments are on 2000 nodes, which is a good demonstration of scalability beyond SDPs, but the term "large scale" may be an overstatement in the context of modern graph learning. A comparison of wall-clock time against the baselines would have been a valuable addition to substantiate the efficiency claims.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a complete and compelling story. It identifies a clear gap in the literature, provides a principled solution based on sound theoretical analysis, develops a novel algorithm to make the solution practical, and validates its approach with solid experiments. Despite some minor issues with clarity due to its density, the quality, originality, and significance of the contributions are very high. I strongly recommend acceptance.
