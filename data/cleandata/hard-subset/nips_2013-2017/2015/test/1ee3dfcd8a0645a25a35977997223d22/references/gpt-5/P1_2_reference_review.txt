Summary
The paper studies graph transduction via orthonormal representations (unit-sphere embeddings respecting graph sparsity) and proposes SPORE, a spectral-norm-regularized kernel learning formulation over the elliptope K(G). The authors first derive a high-probability generalization bound (Theorem 1) for SVM-like transduction that highlights dependence on the spectral norm λ1(K), motivating explicit spectral norm regularization. They then analyze PAC learnability of orthonormal embeddings, connecting the feasible set’s extremal spectral norm to the Lovász theta function: maxK∈K(G) λ1(K) = θ(Ḡ) and minK∈K(G) ωC(·) ≤ θ(G)/2. Choosing C and β in terms of θ(G), θ(Ḡ), they obtain an error bound er ≤ O((1/m (√(n θ(G)) + log(1/δ)))1/2) and a labeled sample complexity scaling m̃/n = Ω((θ/n)1/2), improving over prior Ω((θ/n)1/3).

They formulate SPORE as minK∈K(G) ωC(K,yS) + β λ1(K), a convex problem over an intersection of the PSD cone and affine constraints. To scale beyond SDP solvers, they propose an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) projected subgradient method that approximates projections via proximal operators of support functions using FISTA. They prove O(1/√T) convergence under an inexact proximal accuracy criterion and show how to compose proximal operators over intersections. They extend SPORE to multiple graphs via an MKL-style combination with a max spectral penalty and show that the bound depends on mink θ(G(k)). Experiments on UCI and MNIST (500–2000 nodes) show gains over Laplacian baselines and K-scaling; synthetic multi-graph experiments demonstrate robustness with noisy graphs.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Ando & Zhang-style graph transduction, consistency of orthonormal embeddings, and Lovász theta connections, and addresses limitations in choosing normalized kernels. It improves prior PAC rates claimed for orthonormal representations by leveraging Rademacher complexity and a spectral penalty. Algorithmically, it departs from SDP reliance by using inexact proximal methods tailored to the elliptope.

Strengths
- Conceptual: Clear insight that generalization depends on λ1(K), leading naturally to spectral norm regularization within orthonormal embeddings.
- Theory: High-probability generalization bound; tight connection to θ(G), θ(Ḡ); bound that improves labeled sample complexity from Ω((θ/n)1/3) to Ω((θ/n)1/2) under stated assumptions; extension to multiple graphs with bound depending on mink θ(G(k)).
- Optimization: An inexact proximal framework for projections onto intersections via proximal support functions, with an explicit O(1/√T) rate for a nonsmooth convex objective; practical decomposition using FISTA and closed-form proxes for the PSD cone and the affine set.
- Empirics: Consistent improvements over Laplacian-kernel baselines and K-scaling on several datasets; shows scalability to a few thousand nodes and gains in multi-graph settings, including noisy cases.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicality of parameter choices in bounds: The PAC choices for C and β depend on θ(G) and θ(Ḡ), which are generally hard to compute (require SDPs). While the algorithm cross-validates β in practice, the learnability claim is more “existence” than constructive; discuss data-driven surrogates for θ or adaptive schedules.
- Bound tightness and assumptions: Theorem 4 depends on similarity graph assumptions but these are only informally stated. Please state conditions (e.g., stochastic block or homophily assumptions) precisely and clarify where they are used. The dependence er = O((1/m (√(n θ(G)) + log(1/δ)))1/2) is unusual; a brief intuitive derivation in the main text would help.
- Lemma 3 generality: The claim minK ωC(K,yS) ≤ θ(G)/2 “for any S,y” is strong; please clarify conditions (e.g., dependence on m, C) and provide more intuition. Is the bound uniform over labels or worst-case over y?
- Proximal operators: The feasibility of prox for σS+ and σP(G) is central. The support function σS+ is infinite unless the argument is negative semidefinite; the prox thus reduces to projection onto the negative semidefinite cone. Please move the closed-form expressions and derivations from the supplement into the main paper (even briefly) to avoid ambiguity.
- Computational evaluation: The experiments would benefit from wall-clock times, number of eigendecompositions per iteration, and a comparison to a fast SDP baseline (on smaller graphs) to demonstrate speedups. Also include an ablation showing the effect of the spectral regularizer (β=0 vs β>0) on both accuracy and λ1(K).
- Baselines: Only Laplacian kernels and K-scaling are included. Please add standard graph SSL baselines such as label propagation/local-global consistency and, if feasible, modern GNN-based transductive baselines on the MNIST graphs (even if small-scale), to better situate improvements.
- Sensitivity: Graphs are built by thresholding similarities; performance can be sensitive to thresholds. Please include sensitivity to graph construction and to β,C. For multiple graphs, compare to alternative combinations (learned weighted sum without spectral penalty) beyond union/intersection/majority.
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is dense and relies heavily on the supplement (e.g., algorithm details, prox forms, constants L,R,R̂). Some references are incomplete (missing years/pages), and there are minor typos. Tightening notation and summarizing key proof ideas in the main text would improve readability.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled regularization for orthonormal graph embeddings with clear theoretical motivation.
- Improved PAC bounds leveraging Lovász theta, with an interpretable dependence on graph structure.
- A practically motivated first-order method with inexact projection guarantees and applicability beyond this setting.
- Positive empirical results and a useful extension to multiple graphs.

Cons for acceptance
- Parameterization of theoretical results depends on intractable quantities; limited guidance for practical tuning.
- Empirical evaluation is somewhat narrow in baselines and lacks runtime analysis and sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Some technical details critical to feasibility are deferred to the supplement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide practical heuristics or bounds for θ(G) to guide β without cross-validation; e.g., spectral surrogates based on degrees or eigenvalues of A.
- Add runtime and scalability plots; report eigendecomposition costs and memory footprint.
- Include ablations on β and graph construction thresholds; visualize learned spectra and embeddings.
- Expand baselines to include label propagation (Zhou et al.) and, if possible, a GNN transductive method on the constructed graphs.
- Bring key proximal derivations into the main paper and tighten statements/assumptions of theorems.

Overall recommendation
This is a solid contribution at the intersection of graph SSL, convex geometry, and first-order optimization. Despite some gaps in practical guidance and empirical breadth, the core ideas are novel and well-motivated, and the theory is interesting. I lean toward weak accept, contingent on strengthening experimental comparisons and clarifying key technical details.
