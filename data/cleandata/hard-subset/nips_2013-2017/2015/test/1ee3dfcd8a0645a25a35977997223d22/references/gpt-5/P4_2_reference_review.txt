Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses transductive learning on graphs using orthonormal representations. It starts from the dual kernelized formulation of graph transduction (SVM-like objective restricted to labeled nodes) and derives a new high-probability generalization bound (Theorem 1) in which the generalization term depends on the spectral norm λ1(K) of the graph kernel matrix K. This motivates a new convex formulation, SPORE, that learns an orthonormal embedding (equivalently, a kernel K in the elliptope K(G)) by minimizing the transduction objective plus a spectral norm penalty, i.e., minK∈K(G) ωC(K,yS) + β λ1(K). Using links between orthonormal representations and the Lovász theta function ϑ(·), the paper shows that maxK∈K(G) λ1(K) = ϑ(Ḡ) and minK∈K(G) ωC(K,yS) ≤ ϑ(G)/2, which, combined with Theorem 1, yields a PAC-style error bound with labeled sample complexity scaling as m̃/n = Ω((ϑ/n)1/2), improving over a prior Ω((ϑ/n)1/3) estimate for orthonormal representations. The analysis also extends to multiple graphs with an MKL-style combination, leading to bounds driven by mink ϑ(G(k)).

Algorithmically, the paper proposes an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) projected subgradient method to solve SPORE at scale. The key idea is to approximate the projection onto the elliptope via the proximal operator of the support function of an intersection X = S+ ∩ P(G), handled with a FISTA routine that uses proximal operators for σS+ and σP(G). The method achieves O(1/√T) convergence for nonsmooth convex objectives with inexact projections under a dual approximate optimality criterion. Experiments on UCI/MNIST graphs and synthetic multi-graph settings show accuracy improvements over Laplacian-based kernels and a k-scaling baseline, with additional benefits from combining multiple graphs.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The convexity of SPORE and the subgradient structure are correct. The generalization bound (Theorem 1) that isolates λ1(K) is plausible and aligns with the transductive learning literature that leverages graph-dependent norms, though the proof is deferred. The use of θ-function connections to bound λ1(K) over K(G) is well-motivated; however, two technical steps would benefit from more explicit derivations/references:
  - Lemma 2 (maxK∈K(G) λ1(K) = ϑ(Ḡ)) and Lemma 3 (minK ωC ≤ ϑ(G)/2) should be accompanied by precise formulations/proofs or citations to canonical θ(G) SDP characterizations that make these identities exact.
  - The proximal framework relies on prox of the support function for unbounded sets (PSD cone and an affine subspace). For cones/affine sets, σX is often +∞ in many directions, and proxσX reduces to projections onto polar/dual sets via Moreau identities. The paper should explicitly provide the closed-form proximal mappings (e.g., proxσS+ as projection onto the negative semidefinite cone, and proxσP(G) as projection onto the orthogonal complement of the affine subspace), and justify the FISTA application to the sum σA+σB with the smooth coupling term. Without these details in the main text, it is difficult to fully verify the algorithmic claims.
- Experiments: Empirically, SPORE outperforms baselines on several datasets and scales to n≈2000 with a small number of iterations. However, important comparisons are missing: classical label propagation/LLGC and other recent graph SSL methods; projection-based methods like ADMM/Douglas–Rachford/Dykstra; and an ablation that optimizes ωC(K,yS) over K(G) without spectral norm regularization to isolate the contribution of the λ1(K) penalty. Runtime and scaling plots and sensitivity to C, β would strengthen the claims. Details on cross-validation with very few labels (to prevent leakage) are also needed.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized, with a coherent progression from theory to algorithm and experiments. Notation is heavy but standard. Several definitions (proximal operators for σS+ and σP(G), explicit steps of IIP-FISTA, and the exact subgradient norms leading to L, R, R̂) are relegated to the supplement; brief summaries in the main text would improve readability. References are sometimes incomplete; ensure precise citations for the θ-function formulations and transductive generalization tools used.

Originality:
- The spectral-norm-based generalization bound for transduction over orthonormal representations, and the subsequent SPORE formulation, provide a fresh perspective compared to diagonal normalization or Laplacian normalization heuristics. The improved sample complexity scaling with √(ϑ/n) is a meaningful theoretical refinement over prior consistency and VC-based analyses.
- The IIP method applies inexact proximal ideas to nonsmooth projected subgradient descent over elliptopes by leveraging the proximal of support functions; while inexact proximal-gradient methods are established, the specific use of dual prox for support functions of an intersection pertinent to elliptope constraints appears novel in this context.

Significance:
The connection to ϑ(·) yields interpretable, graph-structure-dependent guarantees and informs the role of spectral regularization. If validated and broadened experimentally, the method could impact graph SSL where orthonormal embeddings are relevant. The multi-graph extension and its bound are also useful for multi-view/noisy-graph scenarios.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical motivation for spectral norm regularization and its link to graph structure via θ(G).
- PAC bound and improved labeled sample complexity relative to prior work on orthonormal representations.
- A scalable first-order algorithm with convergence guarantees tailored to elliptope constraints.
- Empirical gains over common Laplacian-based baselines and robustness in multi-graph settings.

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficient detail in the main text on key proximal operators for unbounded sets; potential ambiguity about well-posedness without norms/bounds.
- Limited experimental baselines (no LLGC/label propagation/ADMM) and scarce runtime/sensitivity analyses.
- Some theoretical steps (Lemma 2–3, Theorem 1 proof sketch) would benefit from stronger justification/citations to standard formulations.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide explicit formulas and short derivations for proxσS+ and proxσP(G), and clarify how FISTA is applied to their sum with the smooth coupling term; discuss numerical stability.
- Add comparisons to LLGC/label propagation, graph SVM variants, and operator-splitting methods (ADMM/Douglas–Rachford/Dykstra) for projecting onto intersections; include runtime and scaling plots.
- Include an ablation where β=0 to quantify the effect of spectral norm regularization.
- Clarify cross-validation protocol with few labels to avoid leakage; report sensitivity to C and β.
- Strengthen citations and proofs for the θ-function connections used in Lemmas 2–3 and the derivation of Theorem 1.

Scope:
The paper is in scope for NeurIPS (graph-based semi-supervised/transductive learning, convex optimization, generalization theory).

Overall, the work offers a theoretically informed approach with a scalable algorithm and promising results, but requires additional technical clarity on the proximal mappings and broader empirical validation.
