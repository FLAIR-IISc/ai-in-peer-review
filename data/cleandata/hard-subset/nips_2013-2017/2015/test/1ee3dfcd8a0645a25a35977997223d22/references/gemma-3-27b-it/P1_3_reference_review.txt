## Peer Review: Spectral Norm Regularization of Orthonormal Representations for Graph Transduction

**Summary:**

This paper investigates spectral norm regularization within the framework of orthonormal representations for graph transduction. The authors build upon prior work establishing the statistical consistency of orthonormal embeddings and address the challenge of choosing the optimal embedding. They propose a novel formulation, SPORE, which minimizes a convex function incorporating spectral norm regularization, and develop an efficient Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm for its solution.  Furthermore, they extend the work to a multiple graph transduction setting. The paper provides both theoretical analysis, including generalization bounds and PAC learnability results, and empirical validation.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work is well-situated within the existing literature on graph transduction, referencing key papers from Ando & Zhang, Blum et al., and Zhou et al. It builds directly on the recent consistency results for orthonormal embeddings from Shivanna & Bhattacharyya (2023) and aims to address the open question of optimal embedding selection. The introduction of spectral norm regularization is a novel contribution, drawing inspiration from existing regularization techniques but applying them specifically to the orthonormal representation context. The IIP algorithm is also a significant contribution, offering a practical solution to a problem often requiring computationally expensive SDP solvers.  The extension to multi-graph transduction is a logical and valuable extension, connecting to areas like bioinformatics and spam detection.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including a generalization bound (Theorem 1) and PAC learnability results (Theorems 4 and 6). The derivation of sample complexity estimates is a significant contribution.
* **Novel Formulation (SPORE):** The SPORE formulation elegantly combines orthonormal embeddings with spectral norm regularization, offering a principled approach to embedding selection.
* **Practical Algorithm (IIP):** The proposed IIP algorithm provides a computationally efficient alternative to SDP solvers, enabling application to graphs with a larger number of vertices. The approximate proximal method is a clever way to deal with the projection step.
* **Extension to Multiple Graphs:**  The extension to the multiple graph transduction setting demonstrates the versatility of the approach and its potential for real-world applications.  The analysis of the multi-graph case provides insights into the benefits of combining different views.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both UCI and MNIST datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of SPORE compared to existing methods. The large-scale experiments showing good performance with the IIP algorithm are encouraging.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and explanations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Algorithm:** While the IIP algorithm is an improvement over SDPs, it still involves multiple steps and parameters (e.g., choice of α, the approximation parameter). The practical sensitivity to these parameters isn’t thoroughly discussed.
* **Parameter Tuning:**  The paper mentions cross-validation for parameter selection, but doesn't provide details on the specific cross-validation strategy used.  More details on the parameter tuning process would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with existing methods could be more comprehensive.  For example, comparing against more recent graph neural network approaches (despite differing frameworks) would provide a stronger benchmark.
* **Scalability Discussion:** While the large-scale experiments are promising, a more detailed discussion of the scalability limitations of the IIP algorithm would be valuable. What are the practical bottlenecks as graph size increases?
* **Connection to Lovasz Theta:** The paper mentions the connection to the Lovasz Theta function but doesn't fully exploit it.  While computing Theta is intractable, discussing strategies to approximate it or use it for initialization could be beneficial.
* **Synthetic Data:** The multi-graph transduction experiments use synthetic data. While useful for demonstrating the theoretical benefits, evaluating on real-world multi-graph datasets would further strengthen the results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to graph transduction.
* The IIP algorithm is a significant practical contribution, enabling application to larger graphs.
* The extension to multiple graphs demonstrates the versatility of the method.
* The empirical results are convincing, showing superior performance compared to existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The IIP algorithm is complex, and its practical sensitivity to parameter tuning isn't fully explored.
* The comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited.
* The scalability of the algorithm could be a concern for very large graphs.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the field of graph transduction. The theoretical foundations are solid, the proposed approach is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results are promising. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper, contingent upon the authors addressing the following points:

1.  Provide more details on the parameter tuning process, including the cross-validation strategy used.
2.  Discuss the practical sensitivity of the IIP algorithm to its parameters.
3.  Expand the comparison to include more recent graph transduction methods, if feasible.
4.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the scalability limitations of the IIP algorithm.
5.  Consider exploring methods for approximating the Lovasz Theta function or using it for algorithm initialization.
6.   If possible, include results on real-world multi-graph datasets.



**Confidence:** 8/10
