Summary
The paper studies graph transduction via orthonormal representations (unit-sphere embeddings with non-edges orthogonal) and proposes SPORE: learning a kernel K in the elliptope K(G) with an explicit spectral-norm penalty. Theoretical contributions include: (i) a high-probability generalization bound that cleanly highlights λ1(K), (ii) PAC analysis for orthonormal embeddings yielding labeled sample complexity m/n = Ω((ϑ(G)/n)1/2), improving on prior Ω((ϑ/n)1/3), and (iii) connections between λ1(K) over K(G) and Lovász ϑ of the complement, enabling graph-structure dependent rates. Algorithmically, the paper proposes an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method: a projected subgradient scheme where projections onto the intersection K(G)=S+ ∩ P(G) are approximated via an inexact proximal operator of the support function, implemented with FISTA over the sum σS+ + σP(G). The method achieves O(1/√T) convergence even with infeasible intermediate points, with a concrete inner/outer complexity tradeoff. The paper extends SPORE to multiple graphs via an MKL-style combination and provides a matching bound that depends on the best graph’s ϑ. Experiments on UCI/MNIST graphs and synthetic multi-graph data show noticeable accuracy gains over Laplacian baselines and suggest practical scalability to a few thousand nodes.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on earlier transductive SVM-style formulations and graph Laplacian regularization (e.g., Ando & Zhang; Zhou et al.) and the orthonormal-representation viewpoint linking to Lovász ϑ (Lovász; Goemans; Jethava et al.; Shivanna & Bhattacharyya). It moves beyond diagonal normalization heuristics and Laplacian-specific analyses by proposing spectral norm regularization as a principled choice supported by generalization theory. On the optimization side, it complements inexact proximal-gradient literature by handling a nonsmooth objective with an approximate projection onto an intersection via the support-function proximal route.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The move to spectral-norm regularization within orthonormal embeddings, tied explicitly to λ1(K) and ϑ, is a clean and compelling idea that unifies generalization and combinatorial structure. The MKL extension with theory that depends on the best graph is also elegant and useful.
- Theory with graph-structural dependence: The theorems relate error/sample complexity to ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ĝ), yielding interpretable predictions (denser graphs easier; sparse graphs need more labels) and an improved labeled sample complexity rate over prior work.
- Optimization contribution: The IIP framework for approximate projections via prox of support functions on intersecting sets is well-motivated and, to my knowledge, novel in this particular nonsmooth setting. The convergence result with a practical inner/outer loop prescription is useful beyond this application.
- Empirical signals: Consistent gains over Laplacian and K-scaling baselines across several datasets; multi-graph setting shows robustness in the presence of a noisy view.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicality of parameter selection: The bound-driven choices for C and β depend on ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ĝ), which are generally hard to compute. In practice the paper uses cross-validation; please discuss principled proxies (e.g., spectral surrogates, scalable ϑ estimators) and sensitivity to β.
- Scope of baselines: The empirical comparison is limited to Laplacian kernels and K-scaling. Stronger transductive baselines (e.g., label propagation/harmonic functions, manifold regularization variants, game-theoretic transduction) and, if applicable, recent graph SSL methods would strengthen the case. A runtime comparison against an SDP baseline (on small n) would concretely support the scalability claims.
- Clarity/detail gaps: Many crucial derivations are deferred to the supplement. The main text would benefit from:
  - A brief derivation or citation clarifying Lemma 2’s equality maxK∈K(G) λ1(K) = ϑ(Ĝ) and assumptions.
  - Explicit formulas for prox of σS+ and σP(G), and a short discussion of numerical stability and the cost of the eigensteps.
  - Clearer statement of Theorem 4’s rate (the nested square-roots make scaling a bit opaque at first read).
- Experimental scale and diversity: Graphs up to ~2000 nodes are helpful but still modest by today’s standards. Including at least one larger real graph (e.g., citation/coauthorship) or reporting wall-clock vs. accuracy tradeoffs would help establish practical impact.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameter selection, stopping criteria for inner FISTA and outer loops, and SVM solver details should be specified more precisely; code availability would be valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a new, principled regularization (spectral norm) for orthonormal graph embeddings tied to Lovász ϑ, with improved PAC/sample complexity results.
- Presents a broadly applicable optimization technique (IIP) for approximate projections onto intersections, with theory and demonstrated utility.
- Multi-graph extension with theory that naturally captures “at least one good view” behavior.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in baselines and scale; no comparison to SDP-based optimum on small graphs or to stronger SSL baselines.
- Some theoretical dependencies (ϑ) are not directly usable in practice; guidance on proxies is limited.
- The main text relies heavily on the supplement for key technical details.

Requested clarifications/suggestions
- Provide practical heuristics or bounds to set β without computing ϑ, and include a sensitivity plot.
- Add at least one stronger transductive baseline and a small-n SDP comparison for accuracy/runtime.
- Expand the main text with the prox expressions and a short proof sketch for Lemma 2.
- Report wall-clock times and convergence diagnostics (outer iterations vs. accuracy).

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance for its conceptual novelty and the promising optimization framework, despite some experimental and clarity shortcomings. The paper opens a meaningful direction by tying generalization to spectral properties within orthonormal embeddings and providing an implementable algorithm.
