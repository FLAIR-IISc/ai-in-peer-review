## Review of "Spectral Norm Regularization of Orthonormal Representations for Graph Transduction"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically strong and technically sound contribution to graph transduction. It builds upon recent work on orthonormal representations and proposes a novel regularization technique (SPORE) that addresses a key challenge – choosing the optimal embedding. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, including PAC learnability bounds and convergence guarantees for a proposed algorithm (IIP). The experimental results are promising. While the paper is dense and could benefit from some clarity improvements, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of graph transduction, specifically learning labels on vertices of a graph given a small labeled subset.  It focuses on orthonormal representations of the graph, a relatively recent and statistically consistent approach. The authors identify that while normalizing the kernel (enforcing orthonormal embeddings) is beneficial, there's little guidance on *which* orthonormal embedding to choose. They propose regularizing the spectral norm of the kernel as a means to achieve this, leading to the SPORE formulation.

The paper is well-situated within the existing literature. It correctly points to prior work by Blum & Alon [1], and Peyré et al. [16], and acknowledges the limitations of [1]'s analysis.  The connection to Lovász theta function and its computational complexity is well highlighted.  The references to proximal algorithms and related work in optimization are also appropriate. The discussion of MKL-SPORE places the work within the broader context of multi-view learning.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The PAC learnability analysis is a significant contribution, providing theoretical guarantees for the proposed SPORE method. The improved sample complexity estimate compared to [16] is noteworthy.  The convergence analysis of the IIP algorithm is also a solid result.
* **Novel Formulation (SPORE):**  The spectral norm regularization is a clever idea that addresses a fundamental issue in working with orthonormal embeddings. It provides a principled way to select a suitable embedding.
* **Algorithm Development (IIP):** The proposed IIP algorithm is a practical contribution. Recognizing the scalability limitations of SDP solvers, the authors develop a novel and efficient first-order method. The use of inexact proximal computation is a sensible approach to deal with the complexity of the projection step.
* **Multi-Graph Extension:**  The extension to multiple graphs (MKL-SPORE) is a logical and potentially important step, opening up applications to problems where data is available in multiple related graph structures.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of SPORE compared to baseline methods on both benchmark and synthetic datasets.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is quite dense and mathematically involved.  Some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations and a clearer flow of ideas. For example, the motivation for the IIP algorithm and its approximation criteria could be more clearly articulated.
* **Experimental Details:** While the experimental results are encouraging, the description of the experimental setup could be more detailed.  Specifically, the choice of parameters for the baselines and the cross-validation procedure used to select hyperparameters for SPORE should be clarified.
* **Scalability Concerns:** While the IIP algorithm improves scalability compared to SDP solvers, the computational cost remains a potential concern for extremely large graphs. A more detailed discussion of the algorithm's runtime complexity and its limitations would be valuable.
* **Assumptions on Graph Structure:** The theoretical analysis relies on certain assumptions about the graph structure (e.g., power-law, random graphs). The authors should acknowledge the limitations of their results when applied to graphs with different properties.




**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2 (Generalization Bound):** How sensitive is the generalization bound (Theorem 1) to the choice of the loss function `?`  Could the authors comment on the implications of using different loss functions?
* **Section 3 (SPORE Formulation):** The connection between the spectral norm and the Lovász theta function is mentioned but could be explored further. Does minimizing the spectral norm indirectly provide some approximation to the Lovász theta function?
* **Section 4 (IIP Algorithm):** The claim of O(1/√T) convergence for IIP is good. How does this compare to the convergence rate of standard proximal gradient methods with exact projections? What is the impact of the approximation parameter epsilon on the overall performance?
* **Section 5 (Multiple Graph Transduction):** The experimental setup for MKL-SPORE needs to be detailed. What method was used to combine the predictions from different graphs? Was there any parameter tuning involved in the combination process?
* **Experiments:** More details on the kernel parameters for the baseline methods (Un-Lap, N-Lap, KS) and how these were optimized would be valuable.  Provide confidence intervals (e.g., standard deviations) for the accuracy measurements.

**5. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical guarantees and novel formulation.
* Well-motivated algorithm with convergence analysis.
* Promising experimental results demonstrating superiority over baselines.
* Addresses a key open problem in graph transduction.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Paper is dense and requires significant effort to understand.
*  Experimental details are somewhat lacking.
*  Scalability concerns for very large graphs are not fully addressed.



**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  The authors should focus on improving the clarity and readability of the paper, providing more detailed experimental descriptions, and addressing the questions raised in the detailed comments section. Addressing these points will significantly enhance the impact of this valuable contribution to the field.
