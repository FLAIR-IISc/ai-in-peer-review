Summary
The paper studies graph transduction with orthonormal representations (ORs) and proposes a new formulation, SPORE, that regularizes the spectral norm of the kernel K constrained to the elliptope K(G). Theoretical contributions include: (i) a high-probability generalization bound (Theorem 1) that depends on the empirical hinge loss on labeled nodes and the spectral norm λ1(K); (ii) PAC analysis arguing improved labeled sample complexity that scales with Lovász theta ϑ(G); (iii) an inexact proximal algorithm (IIP) for minimizing a nonsmooth convex objective over an intersection (PSD cone ∩ affine constraints), with O(1/√T) convergence, using proximal operators of support functions and an inner FISTA scheme; and (iv) an MKL-style extension to multiple graphs with a corresponding bound. Experiments on UCI and MNIST (single-graph) and synthetic multi-graph data show accuracy improvements over Laplacian-based kernels and K-scaling.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for spectral norm regularization via a bound that explicitly depends on λ1(K), complementing prior advice to normalize diagonals.
- Interesting use of Lovász theta to relate graph structure to learnability and to derive labeled sample complexity scaling with ϑ(G). The connection that dense graphs (small ϑ) need fewer labels is intuitively appealing.
- Convex formulation over the elliptope and an attempt to address scalability beyond generic SDPs.
- The IIP framework is principled and connects inexact projections to proximal operators of support functions; the analysis yields standard O(1/√T) rates.
- Extension to multiple graphs with a bound that depends on the best view is reasonable and the synthetic experiments support robustness.

Weaknesses and concerns
Theoretical aspects
- Parameter selection depends on unknown graph quantities. The central bound (Theorem 4) chooses C and β in terms of ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ḡ), which themselves require solving SDPs and are unavailable in practice. The paper does not provide a data-driven procedure with guarantees nor demonstrates empirically that tuning proxies correlate with the theory.
- Lemma 3 (minK ωC(K,yS) ≤ ϑ(G)/2 for any y,S,C) is surprisingly label- and C-independent; without proof in the main text this is hard to assess and suggests a loose bound. If true, it may be too weak to meaningfully guide learning; if not, conclusions following from it may not hold.
- Theorem 4’s bound is hard to parse: er0-1 = O((1/m (√(nϑ(G)) + log(1/δ)))1/2). As written, there is a nested square root and scaling that seems dimensionally inconsistent. Please clarify the exact rate and constants. As is, it is difficult to compare to prior results.
- The “improved” sample complexity comparison (ϑ/n)1/2 vs (ϑ/n)1/3 relies on strong assumptions (similarity graphs) and on the SPORE-specific parameterization. It is unclear whether the improvement is inherent or an artifact of different proof techniques (Rademacher vs VC) and assumptions. A careful apples-to-apples comparison is needed.
- Novelty of the optimization contribution is incremental. O(1/√T) for nonsmooth convex objectives with inexact projections is standard; there is a substantial literature on projected subgradient with errors, mirror descent, inexact proximal-point/forward–backward, ADMM/Dykstra variants, and constraint splitting. The paper does not convincingly position its criteria relative to classical inexact projection subgradient methods (e.g., Bertsekas and subsequent work) or to projection-free alternatives (Frank–Wolfe on the elliptope, Burer–Monteiro factorization). The claimed novelty (prox of support functions for nonsmooth case) needs stronger differentiation and citations.

Algorithmic/clarity issues
- Feasibility and projections. The algorithm maintains feasibility by an “infeasible inexact proximal” followed by an explicit PSD projection (Step 8), but then feasibility w.r.t. P(G) is not guaranteed. How is feasibility preserved across iterations for both constraints before each SVM call? The description suggests potential oscillation between constraints; convergence under such alternating approximate projections should be clarified.
- Prox operators. The text asserts simple forms for ProxσA and ProxσB but does not provide them; for the PSD cone and the polytope P(G), these support functions are unbounded unless restricted appropriately. Without explicit forms, it is difficult to assess correctness and implementability.
- Complexity. Each outer iteration solves an SVM-like dual over labeled nodes and an eigenvector computation for λ1(K). The overall complexity and sensitivity to m and n are not quantified rigorously; the claim of scalability to “1000’s of vertices” is modest relative to existing graph SSL methods (e.g., label propagation) and modern first-order SDP surrogates. No comparison to factorized (Burer–Monteiro) approaches that often scale better for elliptope-like constraints.
- Notation and exposition need work: multiple typos, inconsistent use of subscripts/superscripts, missing years in references, and several key derivations deferred to the supplement. The main text should contain precise statements and at least sketches of the most critical proofs.

Experimental evaluation
- Baselines are limited. Strong and widely used baselines (Zhu–GhAh harmonic functions, Zhou et al. label propagation/LGC, LapRLS/manifold regularization, graph-regularized SVMs, game-theoretic transduction) are absent or only partially represented via simple Laplacian kernels. Comparison to more recent scalable SSL on graphs is needed.
- Graph construction is ad hoc (thresholded RBF/cosine). Results are sensitive to similarity graph construction; there is no sensitivity analysis, no robustness to threshold/σ, and no study of sparsity/density tradeoffs—particularly relevant given the theoretical dependence on ϑ and density.
- Ablations are missing. There is no comparison to the SPORE objective without the spectral norm penalty (β=0) or to alternative regularizers (trace norm, Frobenius, max-norm). Since the key claim is that spectral norm regularization helps, an ablation is essential.
- Scale and reporting. Experiments use small subsets (500–2000 nodes), only 5–10 splits, no standard deviations/confidence intervals, and limited details on hyperparameter tuning. Runtime vs. accuracy plots and convergence curves would strengthen the case.
- Multi-graph results are synthetic only; no real multi-view datasets are used, and no comparison to multi-graph SSL baselines (e.g., Zhou & Burges, multi-view label propagation). The gains over simple unions/majority voting could be problem-specific.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptual connection between spectral norm and generalization in graph SSL is interesting and may inspire further work.
- Use of Lovász theta to tie graph structure to sample complexity is novel within this context.
- Attempt to scale elliptope-constrained kernel learning beyond generic SDPs.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical results depend on unknown quantities and contain unclear statements; key lemmas and parameter choices are not actionable in practice.
- Optimization novelty is limited and not convincingly compared to established alternatives; feasibility and proximal details are underspecified.
- Empirical evaluation is not rigorous enough: weak baselines, small scale, no ablations, limited reporting, and no real multi-graph benchmarks.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you choose C and β in practice without access to ϑ(G), ϑ(Ḡ)? Can you provide a data-driven tuning rule with theoretical backing?
2. Please include the explicit forms for ProxσS+ and ProxσP(G) and a proof that the composite inexact projection preserves feasibility needed by the SVM step.
3. Can you add ablations isolating the effect of the spectral norm penalty and sensitivity analyses over graph construction?
4. How does SPORE compare to label propagation/LGC, LapRLS, and Burer–Monteiro factorization-based solvers on larger graphs (e.g., n≥50k)?

Recommendation: Weak reject
The ideas are promising, but the current paper overstates theoretical implications given the dependence on unknown quantities and lacks sufficient empirical validation and algorithmic clarity relative to established baselines and methods. With strengthened proofs, clearer parameterization, and a more thorough experimental suite, this work could become compelling.
