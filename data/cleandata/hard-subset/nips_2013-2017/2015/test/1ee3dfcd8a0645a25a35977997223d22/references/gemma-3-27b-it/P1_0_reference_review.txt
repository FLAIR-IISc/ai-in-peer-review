## Peer Review: Spectral Norm Regularization of Orthonormal Representations for Graph Transduction

**Summary:**

This paper investigates spectral norm regularization within the framework of orthonormal graph embeddings for graph transduction. The authors build upon recent work demonstrating the statistical consistency of orthonormal representations and address the open problem of choosing the optimal embedding. They propose a novel formulation, SPORE, that explicitly incorporates spectral norm regularization, and develop an efficient Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method to solve the resulting optimization problem. The paper further extends this to a multiple graph transduction scenario and provides both theoretical generalization bounds and empirical validation.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper firmly grounds itself in existing literature on graph transduction (Ando & Zhang, 2007), PAC learning (Balcan et al., 2006), and orthonormal embeddings (Shivanna & Bhattacharyya, 2023; Jethava et al., 2017). It effectively acknowledges the limitations of previous approaches, specifically highlighting the lack of guidance in kernel selection from (Ando & Zhang, 2007) and the suboptimal sample complexity estimates of (Shivanna & Bhattacharyya, 2023). The IIP method draws upon the broader research area of proximal algorithms (Parikh & Boyd, 2014; Schmidt et al., 2013) and adapts them to the specific challenges of this problem. The multiple graph transduction extension connects to the area of multi-view learning (Zhou & Burges, 2007).

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The SPORE formulation offers a principled way to incorporate spectral norm regularization into orthonormal graph embeddings, providing a theoretically grounded approach to kernel selection.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The derivation of a generalization bound (Theorem 1) and PAC learnability analysis (Theorem 4) are significant contributions, providing insights into the sample complexity and performance guarantees of the proposed method.  The improved sample complexity compared to (Shivanna & Bhattacharyya, 2023) is particularly noteworthy.
* **Algorithmic Innovation:** The IIP algorithm provides an efficient solution for a problem that would otherwise require computationally expensive SDP solvers. The inexact proximal approach is well-motivated and the convergence analysis (Theorem 5 & 6) is rigorous.
* **Multiple Graph Transduction:** The extension to the multiple graph setting is a valuable contribution, potentially applicable to real-world scenarios with heterogeneous data sources.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both UCI and MNIST datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to established baselines.  Scaling the algorithm to 2000 nodes showcases its practical applicability.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the IIP Algorithm:** While the IIP algorithm is presented clearly, its implementation and tuning likely require significant expertise. The details regarding the inner FISTA iterations and parameter choices could be more thoroughly explained.  
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of SPORE relies on carefully choosing parameters like β and C. The paper mentions cross-validation, but further discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions for PAC Analysis:** The PAC analysis relies on assumptions about the graph structure (e.g., similarity graphs). It would be valuable to discuss the robustness of the results to deviations from these assumptions.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons are made to established baselines, the paper doesn't extensively compare to more recent advancements in graph neural networks (GNNs) which are currently dominant in graph learning.  A discussion of the potential advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach compared to GNNs would be insightful.
* **Theoretical Gaps**: The derivation of the PAC bounds (Theorem 4) relies on Lemma 2 & 3. While the proof sketch is provided, a more detailed and complete derivation would bolster confidence in the results.




**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and novel contributions.
*  Efficient algorithm for a challenging problem.
*  Solid empirical validation.
*  Clear and well-written presentation.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Complexity of the proposed algorithm may limit its practical adoption.
*  Limited comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods.
*  Some theoretical gaps in the PAC analysis.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and algorithmic contributions to the field of graph transduction. The SPORE formulation, coupled with the efficient IIP algorithm, offers a promising approach for learning on graphs. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality and originality of the work. The paper addresses an important open problem and provides valuable insights into the interplay between spectral norm regularization and orthonormal graph embeddings.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  Provide more detailed explanations regarding the implementation and parameter tuning of the IIP algorithm.
2.  Discuss the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices (β, C).
3.  Include a discussion of the potential advantages and disadvantages of SPORE compared to recent advances in graph neural networks.
4.  Provide a complete and detailed derivation of the PAC bounds and supporting lemmas in the supplementary material.




**Confidence:** 8/10
