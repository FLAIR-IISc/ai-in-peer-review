

# Review of "Spectral Norm Regularization of Orthonormal Representations for Graph Transduction"

This paper addresses graph transduction by proposing a novel spectral norm regularized orthonormal embedding (SPORE) approach. The authors build upon [16]'s work on orthonormal representations for graph transduction, arguing that previous work lacks guidance on selecting optimal embeddings. They provide a PAC learning analysis showing that orthonormal embeddings are efficiently learnable, and propose SPORE to regularize the spectral norm of the kernel matrix. The paper develops an Inexact Infeasible Proximal (IIP) algorithm for efficient computation, and extends the approach to multiple graph transduction via an MKL-style formulation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified:** The paper provides a significant improvement over [1]'s expected error bound with a high-probability bound (Theorem 1) that explicitly connects spectral norm to generalization. The PAC analysis (Theorem 4) provides a meaningful sample complexity estimate that relates to graph structure (ϑ function), showing improved rates for dense graphs compared to sparse ones.

2. **Novel algorithmic contribution:** The IIP method is a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature for constrained convex problems. The authors provide a clear theoretical justification for their approximation criterion, which is superior to naive distance-based approximations (Theorem 5-6).

3. **Strong empirical validation:** The experiments consistently show significant improvements over baselines (Table 1-2), including on large-scale problems (2000 nodes) with only 5 outer iterations. The multiple graph experiments (Table 4) convincingly demonstrate the robustness of the MKL-SPORE approach.

4. **Clear connections to graph theory:** The paper makes valuable connections to the Lovász ϑ function, which provides a meaningful measure of graph structure relevant to the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited connection to recent graph learning literature:** The paper does not adequately discuss how its approach relates to the rapidly growing field of graph neural networks (GNNs) and recent advances in graph representation learning. This is particularly notable given that GNNs have become the dominant paradigm for graph learning tasks. The comparison with graph neural network approaches would strengthen the paper's significance.

2. **Supplementary material dependency:** Several key proofs (e.g., Theorem 4) are relegated to supplementary material, which is unusual for a NeurIPS paper. The paper could benefit from more self-contained presentation of the main theoretical contributions.

3. **Limited discussion of limitations:** The paper does not discuss the limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the method perform on extremely sparse graphs (where ϑ(G) is large) or on graphs with community structures? The paper claims "significant improvement" over [16], but the exact magnitude of this improvement in sample complexity is not clearly quantified.

4. **Experimental details could be improved:** The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about hyperparameter tuning (e.g., how C and β were selected) or computational resources used for the large-scale experiments.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Strong theoretical contribution with meaningful improvements to existing PAC analysis
- Novel algorithmic approach (IIP) with provable convergence guarantees
- Consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets
- Clear connection between graph structure and sample complexity

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited connection to recent graph learning literature (GNNs)
- Over-reliance on supplementary material for key proofs
- Insufficient discussion of limitations and edge cases
- Lack of comparison with modern graph learning approaches

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. Add a discussion comparing the approach to recent GNN-based methods for graph transduction, highlighting both complementary strengths and potential limitations.
2. Move key proofs from supplementary material to main text to improve accessibility.
3. Include a more detailed analysis of the method's limitations, particularly for extreme graph structures.
4. Provide more details about hyperparameter selection and computational resources in the experimental section.

**Overall assessment:**
This is a technically strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to graph transduction. The SPORE formulation and IIP algorithm are novel and well-justified, with clear empirical validation. However, the paper would benefit from better integration with the broader graph learning literature and more thorough discussion of limitations. The theoretical advances are meaningful, but the connection to current graph learning paradigms is weak.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of graph transduction and provides an efficient algorithm for practical implementation. While not the most significant advance in the field, it is a well-executed piece of work that advances the state of the art in a specific direction. The quality of the theoretical analysis is high, and the empirical results are convincing. Given the strong theoretical foundation and practical utility, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations in connection to recent literature and to improve the presentation of key proofs.
