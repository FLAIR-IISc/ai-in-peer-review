

The paper tackles the problem of vertex label prediction in graphs, extending prior work by Ando and Zhang that proposed embedding graphs on a unit sphere using orthonormal representations to improve generalization. While Ando and Zhang established the theoretical benefit of this embedding approach, they did not provide a concrete method for selecting the optimal embedding. This paper addresses this gap by introducing a novel optimization framework that jointly optimizes the embedding and label predictions through a spectral norm regularization term. This regularization term is motivated by Theorem 1, which establishes an upper bound on the generalization error. The paper further derives asymptotic error bounds that demonstrate favorable scaling properties compared to previous results, and translates these bounds into labeled sample complexity estimates. Although the proposed optimization can be formulated as a semidefinite program (SDP), the authors recognize the scalability limitations of SDP solvers and instead propose an inexact proximal method that achieves O(1/√T) convergence and scales to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing superior performance compared to existing methods.

The reviewer encountered significant difficulty understanding the main result of the paper, specifically Theorem 4. The paragraph preceding Theorem 4 states that "in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances." However, the paper provides no definition of what "similar" means, and this assumption is not referenced or utilized in the theorem's proof. Despite this, the reviewer interprets the theorem's claim for random graphs as er^{0-1}[ŷ] = O(1/m (n^{3/4} + log(1/δ))^{1/2}). This would imply that for large random graphs with randomly assigned {0,1} labels, observing a diminishing fraction of labels (e.g., ω(n^{3/4})) would guarantee high-probability recovery of the remaining labels with small error. Due to the lack of clarity on Theorem 4 and the undefined assumption about "similar" instances, the reviewer is unable to assess the paper's main contribution and cannot make a recommendation for acceptance.
