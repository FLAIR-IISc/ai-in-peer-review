The paper proposes a new framework, SPORE, for spectral norm regularization of orthonormal embeddings applied to graph transduction. While the benefits of orthonormal embeddings could be more thoroughly motivated, the proposed framework is a valuable contribution. The authors then present an interesting and elegant algorithm, an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method, to approximately solve the resulting optimization problem. This is an original contribution and appears to offer a scalable alternative to existing approaches. Experimental results demonstrate the benefits of the proposed solution.

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical grounding and algorithmic innovation. The derivation of a PAC-learnability bound for orthonormal representations is a significant result. The IIP algorithm offers a practical solution to a computationally challenging problem, enabling scaling to larger graphs than standard SDP-based methods. The extension to multiple graph transduction is also a promising direction.

However, the presentation could be improved for clarity and impact. While the experimental results are positive, they don’t fully isolate the benefit of the SPORE framework or the orthonormal representation itself (defined by equation (3)). Demonstrating performance gains specifically attributable to these aspects, before presenting the IIP results, would strengthen the paper’s narrative.  Additionally, the approximation accuracy of the IIP solution relative to the true SPORE solution would be valuable to quantify.

The discussion of multiple graph transduction is currently too high-level. More detail regarding the motivation, intuition, and implementation within this setting is needed. It is difficult to fully grasp the contributions in this case, and appreciating the experimental results requires a clearer understanding of the approach. Specifically, the result in the "noisy case" (last column of Table 4) is counterintuitive – the performance is lower with multiple graphs compared to a single graph, which seems to contradict the motivation for using a multi-graph approach. A clearer explanation is needed. 

Finally, the paper is dense and occasionally challenging to read. The frequent references to supplementary material suggest an attempt to include too much detail within a single paper, which can hinder accessibility and potentially reduce the visibility of the key contributions. Streamlining the presentation and incorporating more critical details into the main text would improve the overall impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framework (SPORE) for spectral regularization of orthonormal embeddings.
*   Original and scalable IIP algorithm for solving the associated optimization problem.
*   Strong theoretical foundation, including PAC-learnability analysis.
*   Promising extension to the multiple graph transduction setting.
*   Empirically validated performance gains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Motivation for orthonormal embeddings could be stronger.
*   Experimental results could be better structured to isolate the contributions of the SPORE framework and the IIP algorithm.
*   Discussion of multiple graph transduction lacks sufficient detail and intuition.
*   Paper is dense and challenging to read in places.
