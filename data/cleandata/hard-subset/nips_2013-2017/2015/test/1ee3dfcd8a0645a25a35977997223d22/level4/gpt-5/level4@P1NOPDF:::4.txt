The paper introduces a new framework for spectral regularized embedding (SEER) applied to graph transduction. While the framework is promising, the motivation for enforcing an orthonormal embedding and its concrete benefits could be articulated more convincingly; this is not a critical flaw.

The authors then present an interesting and elegant algorithm to approximately solve the SEER problem using IIP. This appears to be an original and useful contribution that offers a practical alternative for an otherwise challenging learning task.

The experimental results generally support the proposed approach. However, it would be valuable to include experiments that (i) isolate and demonstrate the benefits of the SEER framework itself, in particular the orthonormal representation defined in Eq. (3), and (ii) quantify the approximation accuracy of the IIP solution relative to the exact SEER objective. These analyses are missing from the current results and would help situate the contribution in a broader context.

The multiple-graph transduction extension is presented at a very high level. Although it is an appealing direction, more detail, motivation, and intuition are needed to clarify how and why the method extends to multiple graphs and, in particular, how the algorithm is implemented in this setting. As written, it is difficult to grasp the main ideas and to fully interpret the experimental results. In that regard, the last column of Table 4 (noisy case) is not intuitive: it suggests that a single graph outperforms multiple graphs, which appears to contradict the motivation for using multiple graphs.

The paper is at times difficult to read and quite dense, with frequent references to the supplementary material. This suggests that too much has been packed into a single paper, which hurts accessibility and likely the visibility of the core contributions.

Overall, the paper proposes the SEER framework for orthonormal embeddings in graph transduction, introduces a promising IIP-based algorithm to solve the associated learning problem, and presents reasonably convincing empirical results.
