This paper introduces a novel framework for spectral regularized embedding (SEER) and applies it to graph transduction. The motivation for the framework, and specifically the advantages of the orthonormal embedding, could be articulated more persuasively, but this is a minor point.

The authors put forward an interesting and elegant iterative algorithm as an approximate solution to the SEER optimization problem. This stands as an original contribution and appears to be a promising alternative for addressing the complex learning challenge.

The experimental evaluation provides evidence for the proposed solution's effectiveness. The paper would be strengthened by including results that specifically isolate the benefits of the SEER framework and the orthonormal representation (defined in equation 3). Additionally, an analysis of the IIP solution's approximation accuracy relative to the SEER objective would be highly beneficial. These elements are not currently present but would help to better contextualize the contribution within the broader field.

Unfortunately, the extension to multiple graph transduction is described at a very high level. While this is an interesting direction, the manuscript requires more detail, motivation, and intuition to clarify how and why the method is effective in a multi-graph context, particularly concerning its implementation. In its current form, the central ideas for the multi-graph scenario are difficult to grasp, which in turn makes it hard to fully appreciate the corresponding experimental results. In this regard, the final column of Table 4 (the noisy case) is counterintuitive, as it seems to suggest that a single graph yields better results than multiple graphs, contradicting the stated motivation for this extension.

Finally, the manuscript's presentation is dense in places, making it difficult to read. It also relies too heavily on the supplementary material for important details. This may indicate an attempt to include too much information in a single paper, which unfortunately compromises the work's accessibility and potential impact.
