This paper investigates spectral norm regularization for graph transduction using orthonormal representations. It builds upon recent work demonstrating the benefits of unit-sphere graph embeddings and aims to address the challenge of finding optimal embeddings and efficient algorithms for their computation. The authors demonstrate PAC learnability of orthonormal representations, derive a generalization bound suggesting the benefit of spectral norm regularization, and propose a novel algorithm, SPORE, for computing this regularized embedding. A key contribution is an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method, designed to scale to larger graphs than standard semi-definite programming (SDP) approaches. The work extends to the multiple graph setting with an MKL-style approach, supported by both algorithmic and generalization results. Experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The paper is well-motivated and presents a substantial amount of material, offering several significant contributions to the field of graph transduction. The connection established between the topological properties of the data graph (via the Lovász number) and the convergence of the embedding estimation is particularly insightful, bridging concepts from multiple areas. The proposed IIP method represents a practical advancement for scaling these techniques to larger datasets.

There are a few points that could be clarified for improved readability and impact. In Section 1, after equation (3), explicitly stating that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) transforms a semi-supervised learning problem into a supervised learning problem would strengthen the explanation. Currently, the indices over *S* only in equation (3) might be confusing, as equation (1) includes a regularizer over unlabeled samples alongside a loss on labeled data. 

In Section 2, the notation in equation (5) is slightly unconventional; the placement of the equal sign immediately before ω<sub>C</sub> is unclear. 

In Section 3, around line 162, adding a brief discussion of how the embedding *U* is recovered from the optimized kernel *K* would be helpful. Mentioning potential factorization approaches and their practical implications would add to the completeness of the explanation.

Regarding Section 4, Algorithm 1’s steps 8-9, ensuring the positive semi-definiteness (PSD) of K<sub>t</sub> can be computationally expensive due to the eigendecomposition. It is worth discussing whether this step represents a significant bottleneck. An alternative approach, such as directly optimizing on a factorization of *K* (e.g., Cholesky decomposition with K=LL<sup>T</sup>), where the projection enforces a zero upper triangular matrix (ensuring PSD), could be explored.

Finally, the citation for Zhang and Ando is from NIPS 2005, not 2006.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents significant theoretical insights and compelling experimental results. It tackles an important problem in graph transduction and proposes a novel and effective solution. The thorough analysis and well-designed experiments bolster the claims made.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of spectral norm regularization for orthonormal embeddings is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The PAC learnability analysis and generalization bounds provide a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
*   **Practical Significance:** The IIP algorithm addresses the scalability limitations of existing SDP-based approaches.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The results on both real-world and synthetic datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of SPORE.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the PSD check in Algorithm 1 could be a limiting factor. This could be mitigated with alternative formulations.
*   The paper is dense, and some explanations could benefit from additional clarity (as noted above).



**Recommendation:**

Strongly Accept. The contributions of this paper are significant and the work is of high quality, making it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. Addressing the minor points raised above would further enhance the clarity and impact of the work.
