This paper studies graph transduction with orthonormal representations (unit-sphere embeddings) and develops both generalization bounds and scalable algorithms. Building on the reduction of semi-supervised learning to an equivalent kernel-based supervised formulation (following Zhang and Ando), the authors derive a high-probability error bound showing that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix can improve generalization. Motivated by this, they introduce SPORE, which augments the kernel learning objective with a spectral norm regularizer and connects its convergence behavior to the Lovász θ function of the graph. They then propose an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) method to solve the resulting nonsmooth convex optimization over the elliptope using approximate projections computed by FISTA, with an O(1/√T) convergence rate. The approach extends naturally to multiple graphs via an MKL-style combination with improved bounds. Experiments on UCI, MNIST, and synthetic multi-graph settings show accuracy gains and scalability beyond standard SDP solvers.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: a high-probability bound (Theorem 1) that directly motivates spectral norm regularization; connections to ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ḡ) that yield interpretable, graph-dependent rates and improved labeled sample complexity (tightening over prior work that used VC dimension or less specialized analyses).
- Algorithmic novelty: the IIP framework with approximate projections via proximal operators of support functions, combined with FISTA, achieves O(1/√T) rates while avoiding expensive exact projections onto the elliptope.
- Practical impact: the solver scales to graphs with thousands of vertices, where SDPs do not; empirical results are consistently strong across benchmarks.
- Extension to multiple graphs: the MKL-SPORE formulation is theoretically supported and empirically robust, especially in the presence of noisy graphs.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarify the reduction from semi-supervised to supervised: After equation (3), please make explicit that the optimization involves only labeled indices S (αj = 0 for j ∉ S), whereas (1) includes a regularization over all vertices. Although standard in this line of work, stating that (3) is the supervised kernel learning reformulation of (1) would prevent confusion about why sums and constraints are over S in (3).
- Notation in equation (5): The notation around g(K) and ωC is confusing—there appears to be an extraneous equals sign before ωC. Please clarify the definition g(K) = ωC(K, yS) + β λ1(K) and its role in ΨC,β(G, yS) = minK∈K(G) g(K).
- Recovering U from K: Around line 162, it would help to state how an orthonormal embedding U is recovered from K (e.g., via eigendecomposition, Cholesky on a rank-truncated K, or other practical factorization choices), and to discuss any implications for dimensionality and numerical stability.
- Cost of ensuring PSD in Algorithm 1 (steps 8–9): Making Kt PSD at each iteration using an eigendecomposition can be expensive for dense matrices (as noted in supplementary D.3.2). Is this the main runtime bottleneck? Would it be feasible to optimize over a factorization K = LLᵀ directly (e.g., with L unconstrained and projections applied to enforce the zero pattern and unit diagonal), thereby guaranteeing PSD by construction? A brief discussion of trade-offs, including how equality constraints Mij = 0 for (i, j) ∉ E would be enforced under a factorized parameterization, would be valuable.
- Density of presentation: The paper contains many ideas and technical results; a slightly more guided narrative (e.g., a figure or pseudocode summarizing the IIP + FISTA interaction and where the approximate projection enters) could help readability.
- Citation correction: Zhang and Ando is NIPS 2005, not 2006.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and ambitious paper that advances the theory and practice of graph transduction using orthonormal embeddings. The link between spectral norm regularization, generalization bounds, and the Lovász number provides useful insight into how graph structure influences learnability (e.g., faster convergence for denser graphs). The IIP algorithm is well-motivated and scalable, and the multi-graph extension is natural and robust. The paper would benefit from clarifying the equivalence to supervised learning, fixing minor notation issues, discussing practical recovery of U from K, and addressing the computational cost of PSD enforcement. With these clarifications, the contribution is strong both theoretically and empirically.
