

The paper tackles learning labels on graph vertices by extending Ando and Zhang's approach, which demonstrated that embedding graphs on a unit sphere via orthonormal representations improves generalization error. However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a concrete method for selecting an optimal embedding. The current work proposes a novel optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and learned labels through a regularization term—the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix—derived from Theorem 1, which establishes an upper bound on generalization error. Additional results provide an asymptotic upper bound on the generalization error of the proposed method, exhibiting favorable scaling over prior work, and translate this bound into an upper bound for labeled sample complexity. Although the optimization can be formulated as an SDP, the paper addresses scalability by suggesting inexact proximal methods, enabling application to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results demonstrate the method's superiority over existing approaches.  

The reviewer could not grasp the high-level significance of the main result (Theorem 4). The paragraph preceding Theorem 4 states: "in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances." However, the term *similar* remains undefined, is absent from the theorem's assumptions, and is not referenced or utilized in the theorem's proof. Despite this, the theorem's claim (for random graphs) can be roughly summarized as:  
$$\text{er}^{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O\left( \frac{1}{m} \left( n^{3/4} + \log \frac{1}{\delta} \right)^{1/2} \right).$$  
This suggests that for large random graphs with {0,1} vertex labels assigned randomly, observing a diminishing fraction of labels (e.g., $\omega(n^{3/4})$) guarantees high-probability recovery of the remaining labels with small error. Due to the unresolved ambiguity in the theorem's assumptions and proof, the reviewer could not comprehend the main result and is unable to render a judgment.
