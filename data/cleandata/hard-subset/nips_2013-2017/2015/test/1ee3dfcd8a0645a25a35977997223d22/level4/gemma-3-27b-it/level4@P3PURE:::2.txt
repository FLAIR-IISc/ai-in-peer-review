The paper addresses the problem of learning labels on the vertices of a graph. It builds upon the work of Ando and Zhang, who observed that embedding the graph onto a unit sphere using an orthonormal representation leads to improved generalization error – that is, better performance on the test set. However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a concrete method for selecting an optimal embedding. The core contribution of this paper is a novel optimization formulation that jointly learns the embedding and the vertex labels, incorporating a regularization term based on insights from Theorem 1, which establishes an upper bound on the generalization error. The paper further presents asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, demonstrating favorable scaling compared to existing results. This upper bound also translates to an upper bound on the labeled sample complexity required for learning. While the optimization problem can be formulated as a semidefinite program (SDP), the computational cost of solving SDPs is prohibitive. Therefore, the authors propose utilizing an inexact proximal method to tackle the problem, enabling scalability to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results suggest that the proposed method outperforms previous approaches.

The reviewer found it difficult to fully grasp the main result of the paper, Theorem 4, at a high level. The paragraph preceding Theorem 4 states that “in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels.” It then references existing literature [1, 12] and asserts that “we assume an edge links similar instances.” However, the notion of “similar” is not defined, nor is it explicitly stated as an assumption within the theorem itself. Furthermore, the reviewer could not identify any mention or use of this assumed similarity in the proof of the theorem. Despite this ambiguity, the theorem can be roughly interpreted, in the context of random graphs, as stating that 
er<sub>0-1</sub>[ŷ] = O( 1/m (n<sup>3/4</sup> + log(1/δ))<sup>1/2</sup> ). 

As the reviewer understands it, this implies that if vertices of a large random graph are randomly assigned {0,1} labels, and then a diminishing fraction – for example, ω(n<sup>3/4</sup>) – of these labels are observed, the algorithm is guaranteed, with high probability, to accurately infer the remaining labels with small error. However, due to the difficulty in understanding the main result, the reviewer is unable to provide a complete judgment on the paper.
