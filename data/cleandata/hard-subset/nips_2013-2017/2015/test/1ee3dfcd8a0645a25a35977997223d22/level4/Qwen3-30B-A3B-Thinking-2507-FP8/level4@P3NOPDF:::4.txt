

The paper introduces SEER (Spectral Embedding with orthonormal Embeddings Regularization), a novel framework for graph transduction that emphasizes orthonormal embeddings. While the core idea of leveraging orthonormality for regularization is promising, the paper’s justification for its benefits over standard spectral methods is insufficiently developed, representing a minor but notable gap in the presentation. The proposed IIP (Iterative Inverse Projection) algorithm to approximate the SEER solution is elegant and constitutes a clear technical contribution. However, the paper would benefit from a more precise explanation of how IIP addresses the computational challenges of SEER, particularly in relation to the optimization landscape and convergence guarantees.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance on standard graph transduction tasks, yet it fails to adequately isolate and validate the key contributions. Crucially, the results do not include ablation studies that directly demonstrate: (1) the advantage of the SEER framework itself (e.g., comparing SEER to non-orthonormal spectral methods), or (2) the specific benefits of the orthonormal representation defined in Equation (3) (e.g., via a comparison with embeddings lacking orthonormality constraints). Without this, it remains unclear whether the observed improvements stem from the framework, the orthonormal constraint, or the IIP approximation. Additionally, the approximation accuracy of IIP relative to the exact SEER solution is not quantified, leaving the practical validity of the approximation ambiguous. These omissions prevent a clear positioning of the work within the broader context of spectral embedding methods.

The extension to multiple graph transduction is presented as a significant contribution but is described at a superficial level. The motivation for handling multiple graphs is stated but not deeply justified—why is the multi-graph setting inherently more challenging or beneficial compared to single-graph approaches? The algorithmic details for this extension are notably sparse; the paper lacks a clear description of how the orthonormal embedding constraint generalizes to multiple graphs, how the IIP procedure adapts to this setting, and what computational or representational advantages emerge. This lack of detail severely hampers the reader’s ability to assess the method’s novelty or efficacy in the multi-graph context. The most problematic evidence is the noisy case in Table 4, where a single graph outperforms the multi-graph approach. This result directly contradicts the stated motivation for the multi-graph extension (which presumably aims to leverage complementary information across graphs), yet the paper does not address this inconsistency. Without clarification, this undermines the credibility of the multi-graph claims.

The paper’s readability is further hindered by excessive density and an overreliance on supplementary material. Key definitions, algorithmic steps, and critical comparisons are deferred to the supplement, which is not ideal for a conference submission where accessibility is paramount. This suggests the authors have attempted to include too much information in the main text, diluting the focus on the core contributions. For instance, the derivation of the orthonormal constraint in Equation (3) and its relation to standard graph Laplacian methods could be clarified concisely in the main body rather than buried in supplementary details.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound framework (SEER) and a novel approximation algorithm (IIP) for graph transduction. The core idea of orthonormal embeddings is intriguing, and the IIP algorithm is well-motivated. However, the paper’s current form does not convincingly establish the *necessity* of the SEER framework or the *specific advantages* of orthonormality, nor does it adequately validate the multi-graph extension. The experimental section lacks the necessary ablation studies to isolate contributions, and the multi-graph results in Table 4 present a critical contradiction that requires resolution. The excessive reliance on supplementary material also reduces the paper’s impact and accessibility.

To strengthen the submission, the authors should: (1) provide a clearer theoretical and empirical justification for the orthonormal constraint’s benefits (e.g., via ablation studies on the embedding quality and downstream performance), (2) quantify the approximation error of IIP, (3) significantly expand the multi-graph section with concrete algorithmic details, motivation, and a resolution of the Table 4 anomaly, and (4) integrate essential supplementary content into the main text to improve flow and accessibility. Addressing these points would transform a promising technical contribution into a compelling and well-supported advance in graph-based learning. As it stands, the paper’s claims are not fully substantiated, and the multi-graph extension remains underdeveloped.
