This paper proposes a new framework for spectral-controlled embedding in graph transduction. The framework itself, and especially the advantages of having orthonormal embedding, can be described more thoroughly, but it's not a very important point.

The authors then propose an interesting and elegant algorithm for solving the SEER problem. It seems to offer a fundamental contribution and a good alternative for solving complex learning problems.

The experimental results demonstrate the advantages of the proposed solution. It would be great to show results that demonstrate the advantages of the SEER framework or the orthonormal representation defined by (3), and also demonstrate the approximate value accuracy of the IIP solution to the SEER problem. This is not visible in the current results, and it would be very interesting to establish a real contribution in a broader context.

Many graph transductions are unfortunately described at a very high level. It is certainly an interesting extension, but more detailed, motivation, and insight are needed as to how and why the proposed method is interesting for many graphs, and especially how the algorithm is applied in such settings. In its current form, it is difficult to understand the core concepts in the case of many graphs, and especially in this case, the experimental results are really difficult to understand. For example, the last column of Table 4 (noisy cases) is not very intuitive - it seems that a graph is better than multiple graphs, which would contradict the motivation for working with multiple graphs.

Finally, the paper is not always easy to read and is quite complex in some places. It mentions additional supporting material in many places. Perhaps it is a sign that the authors want to give too much information in a single paper, which actually damages accessibility and possibly the visibility of the work. The paper proposes a new framework called SEER for orthonormal embedding, which is applied in graph transduction. An interesting algorithm based on IIP for solving learning problems is proposed in SEER, and relatively reliable results are proposed.
