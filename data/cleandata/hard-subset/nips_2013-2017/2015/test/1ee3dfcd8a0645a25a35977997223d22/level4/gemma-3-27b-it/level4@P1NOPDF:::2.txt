The paper addresses the problem of learning labels on the vertices of a graph. It builds upon the work of Ando and Zhang, who observed that embedding the graph onto a unit sphere using an orthonormal representation leads to improved generalization performance – specifically, lower error on the test set. However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a concrete method for choosing an optimal embedding. The main contribution of this paper is a novel optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and the learned labels, incorporating a regularization term. This regularization term is the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix, and its selection is motivated by Theorem 1, which provides an upper bound on the generalization error. The paper further establishes asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, demonstrating favorable scaling compared to existing results. These bounds can also be translated into an upper bound for the labeled sample complexity. While the proposed optimization problem can be formulated as a semidefinite program (SDP), solving SDPs is computationally expensive. Therefore, the authors propose utilizing an inexact proximal method to optimize the problem, enabling scalability to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results suggest the proposed method outperforms previous approaches.

The reviewer found it difficult to fully grasp the main result of the paper, Theorem 4, at a high level. The paragraph preceding Theorem 4 states that “in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels.” The authors then follow existing literature by assuming an edge links similar instances. However, the definition of “similar” is not provided, nor is it formally included in the theorem’s assumptions. The reviewer could not identify any mention or use of this assumption within the theorem’s proof either. Despite this ambiguity, the theorem's claim can be roughly interpreted as follows: for a large random graph with vertices labeled randomly as {0, 1}, if a diminishing fraction of these labels – for example, ω(n<sup>3/4</sup>) – is observed, the algorithm is guaranteed to recover the remaining labels with small error, with high probability. Due to the reviewer’s inability to fully understand the core result, a comprehensive judgment cannot be made.
