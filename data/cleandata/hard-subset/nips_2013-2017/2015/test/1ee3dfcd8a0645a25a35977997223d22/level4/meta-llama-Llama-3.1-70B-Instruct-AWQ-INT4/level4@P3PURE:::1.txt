The paper begins by introducing the background and highlighting the contributions of the authors. They then utilize the Zhang and Ando result, which demonstrates that semi-supervised learning (SSL) can be reduced to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem, as the foundation for the remainder of the paper. In Section 2 and the appendix, the authors derive an error bound, illustrating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix is an effective approach to improving generalization. In Section 3, they incorporate a spectral norm regularization term into the Zhang and Ando formulation and establish a connection between the error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. Section 4 proposes a proximal solver, notable for its approximation of the projection step to accommodate the constraint of a cone-polytope intersection. Section 5 presents a multiple kernel learning (MKL) extension, and Section 6 concludes with experimental results on several datasets.

Overall, the paper is interesting and rich in content, perhaps even a bit dense for a single paper, with several significant contributions. The link between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (Lovasz number), which reflect its organization in some space or manifold, and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5) is particularly enlightening, bridging several fields.

In Section 1, after equation 3, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) implies that a semi-supervised learning problem becomes a supervised learning problem, where learning is based on labeled samples. Otherwise, it may be surprising to see indices over S only (with no \bar{S}) in the sums in equation (3), whereas in equation 1, the regularizer is also applied to non-labeled samples, while the loss is computed on labeled data.

In Section 2, the notation in equation 5 is confusing, particularly the equal sign preceding \omega_C.

In Section 3, around line 162, it would be helpful to mention how the embedding (U) is recovered from K, as there are several practical choices for factorization.

In Section 4, Algorithm 1, steps 8-9, ensuring that K_r is positive semi-definite (PSD) can be quite expensive for general dense square symmetric matrices, and an eigendecomposition is performed every time (supplementary D.3.2). Is this where the algorithm incurs most of its computational time? Would it be feasible to optimize over a factorization of K (e.g., Cholesky with K=LL^T), where projection to the feasible set would result in a matrix with all zeros in the upper triangular part, thus ensuring that K is PSD by construction?

Additionally, the Zhang and Ando reference should be corrected to NIPS 2005, rather than 2006.

This paper provides an interesting exploration of how spectral regularization impacts error bounds on graph transduction using orthonormal embedding, offering new theoretical insights and convincing experimental results.
