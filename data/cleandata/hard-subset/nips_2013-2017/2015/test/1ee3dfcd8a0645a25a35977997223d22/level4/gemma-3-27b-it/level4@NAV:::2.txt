The paper tackles the problem of graph vertex label learning, building upon the work of Ando and Zhang, who demonstrated that embedding the graph onto a unit sphere via an orthonormal representation improves generalization performance. While Ando and Zhang highlighted the benefit of such embeddings, they did not provide a method for selecting an optimal embedding. This paper contributes a novel optimization formulation that simultaneously learns the embedding and vertex labels, incorporating a regularization term derived from insights in Theorem 1—which establishes an upper bound on generalization error. The paper further presents asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, exhibiting favorable scaling properties compared to existing bounds, and translates this to an upper bound on labeled sample complexity. Recognizing the computational challenges of solving the resulting Semi-Definite Program (SDP), the authors propose an inexact proximal method for optimization, allowing the method to scale to graphs containing thousands of vertices. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method relative to prior approaches.

However, the reviewer found the main result, Theorem 4, difficult to fully comprehend. The text preceding Theorem 4 states that learning labels is impossible without assumptions, and invokes the notion of edges connecting “similar” instances, citing existing literature. Critically, the paper does not define what constitutes “similarity” nor explicitly state this as an assumption within the theorem itself. Furthermore, this notion of similarity appears absent from the theorem’s proof. Despite this ambiguity, the theorem’s claim, specifically in the context of random graphs, appears to state that, given a large randomly labeled graph with a fraction of labels observed—specifically on the order of  ω(n<sup>3/4</sup>)—the algorithm can reliably recover the remaining labels with low error.  Due to the reviewer’s inability to fully grasp the core result, a definitive assessment of the paper’s contribution is not possible.
