This paper introduces SEER, a spectral regularized embedding framework for graph transduction that enforces orthonormal embeddings. While the idea is promising, the motivation and benefits of using an orthonormal embedding are not articulated strongly enough. Additional intuition, theoretical insight, or ablations contrasting orthonormal versus non-orthonormal embeddings would make the case more compelling. This is not a fatal weakness, but it does limit the clarity of the contribution.

The authors present an elegant and original approximate solver for SEER based on IIP. This is a notable contribution and appears to be a practical alternative for tackling the underlying nontrivial optimization problem. However, the paper would benefit from a more direct assessment of how well the IIP solution approximates the true SEER objective. For example, reporting optimality gaps, constraint satisfaction, convergence behavior, and comparisons to exact or strong baseline solvers (on small instances) would help quantify the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency.

The experimental results suggest the proposed approach is effective, but they do not clearly isolate the contribution of the SEER framework itself or of the orthonormal constraint (Equation 3). Ablation studies and controlled comparisons would help position the work in a broader context and clarify which design choices drive the observed gains.

The multiple-graph transduction extension is interesting but described at too high a level. More detail, motivation, and intuition are needed to understand how and why the method extends to multiple graphs, how the graphs are combined or weighted, and how the algorithm is implemented in this setting (including complexity and convergence). As presented, it is hard to follow the main ideas and to fully interpret the corresponding experiments. In particular, the last column of Table 4 (noisy case) is puzzling: it appears a single graph can outperform multiple graphs, which seems to run counter to the motivation for the multi-graph setting. Clarification, diagnostic analyses, and sensitivity studies to noise and graph quality would be valuable.

The paper is dense and sometimes difficult to read, with frequent referrals to the supplementary material. This suggests the authors may be trying to cover too much in one paper, which hurts accessibility. Consider moving key definitions, algorithmic details, and essential proofs or proof sketches into the main text, and streamline notation where possible.

Overall, the paper proposes a meaningful framework (SEER) for orthonormal embeddings in graph transduction and introduces a well-designed IIP-based algorithm with promising empirical results. With clearer motivation for orthonormality, explicit evaluation of the IIP approximation quality, more thorough ablations, and a fuller treatment of the multi-graph extension, the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
