This paper investigates graph transduction through the lens of orthonormal representations. Building on prior work that connects semi-supervised learning on graphs to an equivalent kernel learning problem, the authors first derive a PAC-style generalization bound for graph transduction using orthonormal embeddings. This theoretical result, presented in Section 2, suggests that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix can improve generalization. This insight motivates the paper's main contribution: SPORE, a formulation that regularizes the spectral norm of the kernel, presented in Section 3. The authors establish a compelling link between the error convergence rate of their method and the Lovász θ number of the graph. To solve the resulting non-smooth convex optimization problem over an elliptope, Section 4 introduces a novel infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) method, which is more scalable than standard SDP solvers by using an approximate projection. The framework is then extended to a multiple kernel learning setting for multi-graph transduction in Section 5. The paper concludes with experiments on several datasets demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
This is a substantial and interesting paper with several significant contributions. The work is theoretically dense and well-motivated. A particular strength is the elegant connection established between the generalization error and a fundamental topological property of the graph, the Lovász θ number. This insight bridges graph theory and machine learning, providing a deeper understanding of why certain graph structures are more amenable to transduction. The development of the IIP algorithm to efficiently solve the proposed SPORE formulation is another strong contribution, addressing the practical scalability limitations of traditional SDP solvers for this class of problems. The experimental results are convincing and effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach over existing methods.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is rich in content, and a few points could be clarified to improve its accessibility and completeness.

*   In Section 1, after introducing Equation (3), it would be helpful to explicitly elaborate on the transition from the semi-supervised formulation (1) to the supervised kernel learning formulation (3). Specifically, a brief explanation of why the objective function in (3) only involves sums over the labeled set S would be beneficial for readers less familiar with this equivalence, as Equation (1) contains a regularization term over all nodes.

*   In the discussion of the SPORE formulation in Section 3, while the optimal kernel K* is computed, the paper is ultimately about orthonormal embeddings (U). It would be valuable to add a brief note on how the embedding U is practically recovered from K*. Since the matrix factorization is not unique, mentioning the standard choice (e.g., via eigendecomposition) would be a useful detail.

*   Regarding the IIP FISTA algorithm in Section 4, the projection step to ensure K is positive semi-definite (Algorithm 1, step 8) is a critical component. The supplement indicates this is done via eigendecomposition. Could the authors comment on the computational cost of this step, as it is performed in each iteration? It would be interesting to know if alternative approaches were considered, such as optimizing directly over a factorization of K (e.g., Cholesky) to enforce the PSD constraint by construction and potentially avoid repeated decompositions.

*   A minor point: The citation for Zhang and Ando [19] should be NIPS 2005.

**Conclusion:**
This is a strong paper that provides novel theoretical insights into graph transduction using orthonormal embeddings, supported by a principled algorithm and solid experimental validation. The connection to the Lovász θ number is particularly enlightening. With minor clarifications, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
