This paper studies learning labels on the vertices of a graph. It builds on Ando and Zhang, who suggested that embedding the graph on the unit sphere via an orthonormal presentation can improve generalization error (i.e., test error). However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a concrete method for choosing an optimal embedding. The current paper proposes a new optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and the labels by introducing a regularization term. This regularizer is the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix, motivated by Theorem 1, which provides an upper bound on the generalization error.

The paper further presents asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, with scaling that is favorable compared to prior results. This bound can also be translated into an upper bound on the labeled sample complexity. While the optimization can be cast as an SDP, solving SDPs is computationally expensive. The paper therefore suggests solving the problem with an inexact proximal method, which is claimed to scale to graphs with thousands of vertices. Some experimental results indicate that the proposed method outperforms previous approaches.

However, I was not able to understand the main result (Theorem 4) at a high level. In the paragraph preceding Theorem 4, the paper states: “in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances.” There is no definition of what “similar” means, and this assumption does not appear in the theorem’s stated assumptions. I could not find any mention or use of such an assumption in the proof of the theorem either.

Despite this, the theorem’s claim can be roughly summarized, in the case of random graphs, as
er^{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O( 1/m (n^{3/4} + \log(1/\delta) )^{1/2} ).
As I understand it, this implies that if the vertices of a large random graph are labeled in {0,1} at random, and then a diminishing fraction of these labels is observed—e.g., m = ω(n^{3/4})—the algorithm is guaranteed, with high probability, to predict the remaining labels with small error. Because I do not understand the main result (see above), I cannot make a judgment on the paper’s primary theoretical contribution.
