The paper addresses the problem of learning labels on the vertices of a graph. Building on the work of Ando and Zhang, the authors propose a new approach that embeds the graph on a unit sphere using an orthonormal representation, which has been shown to result in better generalization error. However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a concrete solution for choosing an optimal embedding. The current paper's main contribution is a new optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and the learned labels by introducing a regularization term.

The regularization term, the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix, is chosen based on the insight obtained from Theorem 1 in the paper, which states an upper bound on the generalization error. Furthermore, the paper presents asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, which have favorable scaling compared to previous results. This upper bound can also be translated to an upper bound for labeled sample complexity. While the proposed optimization can be cast as a semi-definite program (SDP), the complexity of solving SDPs is undesirable. Hence, the paper suggests optimizing this problem using an inexact proximal method, which can scale to graphs with thousands of vertices. Some experimental results indicate the superiority of the proposed method compared to previous methods.

The reviewer had difficulty understanding the main result of the paper, specifically Theorem 4, at a high level. In the paragraph preceding Theorem 4, the paper states that "in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances." However, the definition of "similar" is not provided, and there is no mention of it in the assumptions of the theorem. The reviewer could not find any mention or use of such an assumption in the proof of the theorem either. Despite this, the claim of the theorem can be roughly stated, in the case of random graphs, as er^{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O(1/m (n^{3/4} + log{1/δ})^{1/2}). This implies that if vertices of a large random graph are {0,1} labeled at random and then a diminishing fraction, e.g., ω(n^{3/4}), of these labels are observed, the algorithm is guaranteed with high probability to find the remaining labels with small error. However, the reviewer was unable to fully understand the main result and therefore cannot make a judgment.
