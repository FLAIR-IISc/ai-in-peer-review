The paper begins by establishing background context and outlining its contributions. The authors then leverage the result from Zhang and Ando (2005) – demonstrating the equivalence of semi-supervised learning (SSL) to a kernel-based supervised learning problem – as the foundation for their subsequent analysis. Sections 2 and the appendix present an error bound demonstrating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix improves generalization performance. In section 3, a spectral norm regularization term is added to the Zhang and Ando formulation, and connections are established between the error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. Section 4 introduces a proximal solver, notable for its approximate treatment of the projection step to handle constraints involving a cone-polytope intersection. An MKL extension is proposed in section 5, and section 6 concludes with experimental results on several datasets.

Overall, this is an interesting and substantial paper – potentially dense for a single publication – with several important contributions. The link between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (as captured by the Lovasz number), reflecting the data’s organization within a space or manifold, and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5) is particularly insightful and bridges multiple research areas. 

In section 1, following equation 3, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) transforms the semi-supervised learning problem into a supervised learning problem, focusing learning on labeled samples. Currently, the indices over *S* only (without *S̄*) in the sums of equation (3) are somewhat surprising, given that the regularizer in equation (1) includes unlabeled samples while the loss function is applied to labeled data.

In section 2, the notation in equation 5 is unclear – the presence of an equal sign immediately preceding *ω<sub>C</sub>* is confusing.

In section 3, around line 162, clarifying how the embedding (*U*) is recovered from *K* would be helpful. Several practical factorization choices exist, and mentioning them would add value.

Algorithm 1, steps 8-9: ensuring that *K<sub>r</sub>* is positive semi-definite (PSD) is typically computationally expensive, particularly for general dense symmetric matrices, and the current implementation relies on an eigendecomposition at each iteration (supplementary D.3.2). Is this the primary computational bottleneck of the algorithm? Exploring alternative approaches, such as optimizing directly over a factorization of *K* (e.g., Cholesky decomposition with *K = LL<sup>T</sup>*), could be advantageous. Projection to the feasible set would then involve setting the upper triangular elements to zero, which would guarantee PSD by construction.

Please note that Zhang and Ando’s work was published in NIPS 2005, not 2006.

This is a compelling paper demonstrating how spectral regularization impacts error bounds on graph transduction using orthonormal embeddings, offering novel theoretical insights and supported by convincing experimental results.
