The paper addresses the problem of learning labels on the vertices of a graph. It builds on Ando and Zhang’s observation that embedding a graph on the unit sphere via an orthonormal representation can improve generalization (test) error. However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a concrete method for selecting an optimal embedding. The main contribution here is a new optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and the labels by introducing a regularization term. This term—the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix—is motivated by Theorem 1, which provides an upper bound on the generalization error. The paper further presents asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, with more favorable scaling than prior results; these bounds can also be translated into bounds on the labeled sample complexity. While the optimization can be cast as a semidefinite program (SDP), solving SDPs is computationally expensive. The authors therefore propose an inexact proximal method that scales to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experiments indicate that the proposed method outperforms previous approaches.

However, I could not understand the main result, Theorem 4, at a high level. In the paragraph preceding Theorem 4, the paper states: “in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances.” There is no definition of what “similar” means, nor is this assumption stated among the theorem’s formal assumptions. I also could not find any mention or use of this assumption in the proof.

Despite this, the theorem’s claim can be roughly summarized, in the case of random graphs, as:
er^{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O( (n^{3/4} + log(1/δ))^{1/2} / m ).
As far as I understand, this means that if the vertices of a large random graph are labeled {0,1} at random, then observing a vanishing fraction of these labels—e.g., ω(n^{3/4}) labeled nodes—suffices for the algorithm to recover the remaining labels with small error, with high probability.

Because I could not understand the main result and its assumptions, I am unable to assess its correctness or significance.
