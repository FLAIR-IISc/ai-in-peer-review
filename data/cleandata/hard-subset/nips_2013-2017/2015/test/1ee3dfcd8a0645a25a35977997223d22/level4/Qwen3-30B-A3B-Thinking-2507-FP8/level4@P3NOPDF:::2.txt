

The paper proposes a novel approach for vertex label learning on graphs, building on Ando and Zhang's insight that spherical embeddings improve generalization. The key contribution is a joint optimization framework for embedding and labels, incorporating a spectral norm regularization term on the graph kernel matrix. This term is derived from Theorem 1, which establishes a generalization error upper bound. The paper further provides asymptotic bounds showing favorable scaling over prior work and translates these to labeled sample complexity guarantees. While the optimization is cast as a semidefinite program (SDP), the authors propose an inexact proximal method for scalability to graphs with thousands of vertices, supported by empirical results demonstrating superiority over baselines.

The theoretical contribution hinges on Theorem 4, which claims that for graphs with unlabeled nodes, a generalization error bound of \( \mathcal{O}\left( \frac{1}{m} \left( n^{3/4} + \log \frac{1}{\delta} \right)^{1/2} \right) \) holds under an assumption of edge similarity. However, this assumption is critically underdeveloped. The paper states: "Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances," yet provides no formal definition of "similar" and fails to reference this concept in the theorem's assumptions or proof. The term "similar" appears nowhere in the mathematical formulation of Theorem 4 or its supporting lemmas. Without a precise definition or justification for this assumption—especially given the theorem's claim about label recovery in the absence of assumptions—the bound's foundation is unverifiable. The reviewer could not reconcile the stated assumption with the theorem's proof or its applicability to random graphs, where the bound suggests that observing \( \omega(n^{3/4}) \) labels suffices for low error with high probability. This disconnect prevents assessment of the theorem's validity.

The paper's strengths lie in its practical optimization approach and empirical validation. The spectral regularization term is well-motivated by the generalization error analysis, and the proximal method effectively addresses SDP scalability. Experiments on benchmark datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over competitive baselines, lending credibility to the proposed method. However, the theoretical core is undermined by the ambiguity in Theorem 4. The absence of a rigorous definition for "similar" and its non-usage in the proof constitute a significant gap, as the theorem's claim is central to the paper's contribution. If "similar" is intended as a standard smoothness assumption (e.g., labels vary smoothly across edges), this should be explicitly stated and formalized. Without it, the bound's conditions remain opaque, and the claimed asymptotic guarantees lack justification.

Minor issues include inconsistent notation (e.g., \( \omega{n^{3/4}} \) should be \( \omega(n^{3/4}) \)) and a lack of clarity on how the spectral norm regularization connects to the graph kernel matrix's structure. The proof sketch for Theorem 1 is also insufficiently detailed, making it difficult to assess the tightness of the bound.

In summary, the paper presents a practical and empirically sound method for graph label learning with a theoretically grounded optimization approach. The scalability via proximal methods is a notable practical contribution. However, the core theoretical claim in Theorem 4 is not sufficiently supported due to the undefined "similar" assumption and its absence in the theorem's formalism. This prevents the reviewer from endorsing the paper's main theoretical contribution. The authors should: (1) define "similar" rigorously and incorporate it into the theorem's assumptions; (2) explicitly reference this definition in the proof; and (3) clarify the role of unlabeled nodes in the bound. Without these revisions, the theoretical contribution remains unconvincing, despite the method's practical merits. The paper is promising but requires significant theoretical clarification to be considered for publication.
