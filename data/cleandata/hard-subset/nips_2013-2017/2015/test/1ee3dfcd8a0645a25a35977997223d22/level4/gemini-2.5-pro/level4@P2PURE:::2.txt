This paper addresses the problem of learning labels for vertices in a graph, building on the work of Ando and Zhang. That prior work suggested that embedding a graph onto a unit sphere using an orthonormal representation improves generalization error. However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a concrete method for choosing an optimal embedding. The current paper's main contribution is a novel optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and the labels by introducing a regularization term. This regularizer—the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix—is motivated by the paper's Theorem 1, which provides an upper bound on the generalization error.

The paper further presents an asymptotic upper bound on the generalization error of the proposed method, which demonstrates more favorable scaling compared to previous results. This result also translates into an upper bound on the labeled sample complexity. While the proposed optimization problem can be cast as a Semidefinite Program (SDP), the authors acknowledge that solving SDPs is often computationally prohibitive. Consequently, they propose an inexact proximal method that can scale to graphs with thousands of vertices. The experimental results indicate that the proposed method outperforms previous approaches.

I was unable to fully understand the paper's main theoretical result, Theorem 4. The paragraph preceding the theorem states: "in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances." However, the term \emph{similar} is not formally defined, nor is this assumption explicitly stated in the theorem or, to my knowledge, used in its proof.

Despite this ambiguity, the claim of the theorem for random graphs can be roughly stated as:
er^{0-1}[\hat{y}]= O( 1/m (n^{3/4} + \log{1/\delta} )^{1/2} ).

As I understand it, this implies that if the vertices of a large random graph are assigned {0,1} labels at random, the algorithm is guaranteed with high probability to recover the unobserved labels with small error, even when only a diminishing fraction (e.g., ω(n^{3/4})) of labels are provided. This result seems counter-intuitive without a formal and strong assumption connecting the graph structure to the labels. My inability to understand the basis for this main result (as detailed above) makes it difficult to judge the significance of the paper's theoretical contributions.
