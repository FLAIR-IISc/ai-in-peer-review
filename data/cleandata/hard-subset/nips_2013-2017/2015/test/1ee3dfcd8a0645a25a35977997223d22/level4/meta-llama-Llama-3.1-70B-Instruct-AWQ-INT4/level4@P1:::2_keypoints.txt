- Paper addresses vertex-label learning on graphs and builds on Ando & Zhang’s unit-sphere embedding idea but adds a concrete optimization for choosing the embedding.
- Main contribution: joint optimization of graph embedding and labels with a spectral-norm regularizer motivated by a generalization-error bound (Theorem 1).
- Provides asymptotic upper bounds on generalization error (and labeled sample complexity) that scale favorably versus prior work.
- Optimization can be cast as an SDP, but due to SDP cost the paper proposes an inexact proximal method that scales to graphs with thousands of nodes.
- Empirical results are reported and indicate the proposed method can outperform previous methods.
- Reviewer could not understand the high-level meaning of Theorem 4 and therefore could not judge the main result.
- The paper asserts an assumption that “an edge links similar instances” but never defines “similar” nor clarifies its presence in the theorem assumptions or proof.
- Theorem 4’s claim (roughly: er^{0-1} = O(1/m * (sqrt(n θ(G)) + log(1/δ))^{1/2}) in random graphs) is not explained at a high level and the reviewer found the proof opaque.
- Strengths: new formulation, joint optimization with regularization, favorable asymptotic bounds, scalable optimization approach, some supportive experiments.
- Weaknesses: unclear explanation of Theorem 4, undefined “similar instances” assumption, inability of reviewer to assess the main result.
- Originality: novel optimization formulation and regularizer choice motivated by generalization bounds.
- Significance: addresses an important graph-labeling problem with potential applications (e.g., vision, NLP, bioinformatics) and provides improved scaling guarantees.
- Clarity: paper is generally well-written and notationally clear, but lacks a clear high-level exposition of the main theorem.
- Quality: solid formulation and results, but main theoretical proof (Theorem 4) is insufficiently explained.
- Recommendation: revise to clearly explain Theorem 4, formalize the “similar instances” assumption and its role, and add more experiments to support empirical claims.
