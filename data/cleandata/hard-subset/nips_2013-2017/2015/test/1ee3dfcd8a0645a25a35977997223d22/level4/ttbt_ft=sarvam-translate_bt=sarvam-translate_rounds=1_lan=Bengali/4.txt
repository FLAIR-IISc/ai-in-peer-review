This paper proposes a new framework for spectral-regulated embedding in graph transduction. The framework itself, and especially the advantages of having orthonormal embedding, could be better described, but it's not a very important issue.

The authors then propose an interesting and elegant algorithm to roughly solve the SEER problem. This is a fundamental contribution and seems to offer a good alternative for solving complex learning problems.

The experimental results demonstrate the advantages of the proposed solution. It would be great to show results that demonstrate the advantages of the SEER framework or the orthonormal representation defined by (3), and also show the approximate value accuracy of the SEER problem's IIP solution. This is not visible in the current results, and it would be very interesting to place the actual contribution in a broader context.

Many graph transductions are unfortunately described at a very high level. This is certainly an interesting extension, but more detail, motivation, and insight are needed to clearly understand how and why the proposed method is interesting for many graphs, and especially how the algorithm is applied in such settings. In its current form, it is difficult to understand the core concepts in the case of many graphs, and especially in this field, the experimental results are really difficult to grasp. As such, the last column of Table 4 (the noisy case) is not very intuitive - it seems that a graph is better than multiple graphs, which would contradict the motivation to work with multiple graphs.

Finally, the paper is not always easy to read and is quite complex in some places. It mentions additional helpful elements in many places. Perhaps this is a sign that the authors want to give too much information in a single paper, which actually damages the accessibility and perhaps visibility of the work. The paper proposes a new framework called SEER for orthonormal embedding, which is applied in graph transduction. An interesting algorithm based on IIP for solving learning problems is proposed in SEER, and relatively reliable results are proposed.
