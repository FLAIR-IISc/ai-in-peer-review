Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies graph transduction via unit-sphere embeddings and focuses on orthonormal representations. Building on prior work that advocated unit-sphere embeddings for better generalization, the authors derive a PAC-style generalization bound that avoids VC-dimension arguments by relating error to the spectral norm of the kernel and the Lovász θ function. Motivated by this analysis, they propose SPORE, a spectral norm-regularized orthonormal embedding learned over the elliptope. While such problems are typically solved by SDPs (which do not scale well), the authors develop an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method that replaces exact projections with approximate proximal steps on the support function, computed via FISTA, and prove an O(1/√T) convergence rate. They also extend the analysis and algorithm to multiple graphs in an MKL-style framework. Empirical results on UCI and MNIST datasets, and on synthetic multi-graph settings, suggest improved performance over Laplacian baselines and a k-scaling variant.

Strengths
- Originality: The paper presents a clear conceptual step: using a PAC analysis tied to spectral norm and the Lovász θ function to motivate spectral norm regularization for orthonormal embeddings. The IIP algorithm that uses approximate projections via proximal operators of support functions is elegant and, to my knowledge, novel in this setting.
- Technical quality: The learning-theoretic argument is nontrivial and interesting, especially the bound that motivates spectral norm control and the connection maxK∈K(G) λ1(K) = θ(Ḡ). The IIP framework and its O(1/√T) rate are technically sound and relevant for scalable optimization over the elliptope.
- Practical significance: Replacing SDPs with a first-order method that scales to thousands of nodes is valuable. The empirical results demonstrate competitive to superior performance over standard Laplacian-based baselines and k-scaling, with reasonable computational budgets.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Motivation and framing: While the motivation for unit-sphere embeddings and orthonormal representations is known, the paper could articulate more convincingly why the specific SPORE formulation is the right choice beyond the bound—e.g., by isolating and empirically demonstrating the contribution of spectral norm regularization relative to alternative constraints within the same embedding class. The current results show end-to-end benefits, but do not explicitly demonstrate the gains attributable to the SPORE framework itself or to the orthonormal representation induced by (3).
- Approximation accuracy of IIP: The algorithmic section would be stronger with an empirical assessment of the approximation error induced by IIP relative to an SDP or a high-accuracy solver on smaller graphs. For example: objective suboptimality versus runtime, and the impact of projection accuracy (number of FISTA steps) on test performance. This would help position IIP not only as scalable but also as a faithful solver for SPORE.
- Multiple graph transduction: This extension is interesting but is presented at a high level. The paper needs more intuition, algorithmic detail, and implementation specifics to make the approach and its advantages clear. In particular, a clearer description of how the η-weights are optimized jointly with the individual K(k), how the spectral norm penalties are handled across graphs, and any additional computational overhead would make the contribution more accessible. The noisy case in Table 4 is also not intuitive: the best single graph (G(1), 84.4%) outperforms Multiple Graphs (81.9%), which appears to contradict the claimed robustness and the bound suggesting benefits when at least one “good” graph exists. Please clarify whether this is due to optimization, model selection, or data generation specifics.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is dense and relies heavily on the supplementary material for key arguments and algorithmic details. Consider moving more intuition, the statement of core theorems, and a concise pseudocode of IIP/FISTA steps into the main text. Streamlining the exposition would improve readability and visibility of the main contributions.

Quality
- The theoretical claims are plausible and well-motivated, but the paper would benefit from a small-scale validation of IIP’s solution quality and a more direct empirical ablation isolating the effect of spectral norm regularization.
- The experimental comparisons are promising. Adding convergence plots and a runtime vs. accuracy trade-off would strengthen the experimental validation of the optimizer.

Clarity
- Generally clear but compact. Improve the narrative around the role of θ(G) and θ(Ḡ) in the bounds, the choice of parameters C and β, and the practical interpretation of these constants.
- Reduce cross-references to the supplement where possible by including more intuition and key steps in the main paper.

Originality
- The PAC analysis and its use to motivate spectral norm regularization for orthonormal embeddings, combined with the IIP approach, are original contributions.

Significance
- The work advances scalable graph transduction with theoretically grounded regularization and could influence subsequent methods for learning over the elliptope and related feasible sets. The multiple-graph extension has potential impact, provided the clarity and empirical evidence are strengthened.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and elegant optimization framework (IIP) with proven convergence and practical scalability.
- Interesting PAC analysis connecting spectral norm and Lovász θ to generalization, leading to a principled objective.
- Empirical results that are generally favorable compared to strong baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical analysis of IIP’s approximation quality and its impact on downstream accuracy.
- Sparse details and somewhat unclear findings in the multiple-graph setting, including an unintuitive noisy case result.
- Dense presentation with heavy reliance on the supplement, which hinders accessibility.

Overall assessment
The paper proposes a principled framework (SPORE) with a thoughtful theoretical underpinning and an appealing scalable optimizer (IIP). The contributions are original and potentially impactful. To strengthen the paper, I recommend clarifying the multiple-graph methodology and results, adding empirical evidence of IIP’s approximation fidelity, and improving the readability by moving more intuition and key details into the main text.
