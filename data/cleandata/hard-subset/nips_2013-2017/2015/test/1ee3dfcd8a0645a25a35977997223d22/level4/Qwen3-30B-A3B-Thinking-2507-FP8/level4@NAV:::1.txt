

The paper begins by establishing background and outlining contributions. The authors subsequently leverage the Zhang and Ando result, demonstrating that semi-supervised learning reduces to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem for the remainder of the work. Sections 2 and the appendix present an error bound proving that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix enhances generalization. Section 3 introduces a spectral norm regularization term into the Zhang and Ando framework, establishing connections between error convergence and the Lovász number of the data graph. Section 4 proposes a proximal solver, specifically addressing the approximate handling of the projection step for the cone-polytope intersection constraint. Section 5 extends the approach to multiple kernel learning (MKL), and Section 6 concludes with experimental validation across multiple datasets.

Overall, the paper is engaging and contains substantial material (perhaps somewhat dense for a single paper), featuring several significant contributions. The linkage between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (via the Lovász number) and embedding estimation convergence (Equation 5) is particularly insightful, bridging multiple fields.

In Section 1 following Equation 3, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the correspondence between Equations (3) and (1) implies the semi-supervised learning problem reduces to a supervised one, where learning occurs solely on labeled samples. This clarifies the apparent discrepancy in Equation 3 (sums over indices in S only, without \bar{S}) compared to Equation 1 (regularizer over non-labeled samples while loss is on labeled data).

In Section 2, the notation in Equation 5 is confusing—the equal sign preceding \omega_C requires clarification.

In Section 3 around line 162, it would be helpful to specify how the embedding (U) is recovered from K, as several practical factorization choices exist.

In Section 4, Algorithm 1 steps 8–9: ensuring K_r is positive semi-definite typically incurs high computational cost for general dense symmetric matrices, and the current approach employs an eigendecomposition at each iteration (Supplementary D.3.2). Is this the primary computational bottleneck? Could optimization via Cholesky factorization (K = LL^T) be feasible, where projection to the feasible set (zeroing the upper triangular part) inherently maintains positive semi-definiteness?

Zhang and Ando is cited as NIPS 2005, not 2006.

This is an interesting paper demonstrating how spectral regularization influences error bounds in graph transduction via orthonormal embedding, offering novel theoretical insights and compelling experimental results.
