The paper introduces the background and contributions, then builds on the Zhang and Ando result that reduces semi-supervised learning (SSL) to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem. In Section 2 and the appendix, the authors derive an error bound showing that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix can improve generalization. Section 3 augments the Zhang and Ando formulation with a spectral-norm regularizer and establishes connections between error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. Section 4 proposes a proximal solver that approximates the projection step to handle the cone–polytope intersection constraint. Section 5 extends the approach to multiple kernel learning (MKL), and Section 6 presents experiments on several datasets.

Overall, the paper is interesting and somewhat dense, with several notable contributions. The link between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (via the Lovasz number), which reflect its organization in some space or manifold, and the convergence of the embedding estimate (Eq. 5) is particularly illuminating and bridges ideas across communities.

Comments and questions by section:

- Section 1 (after Eq. 3): It would help to make explicit that the correspondence between Eqs. (3) and (1) implies the SSL problem becomes a supervised learning problem, i.e., the optimization is over labeled samples only. Otherwise, it is surprising to see sums indexed over S only in Eq. (3), whereas in Eq. (1) the regularizer involves unlabeled samples while the loss is on labeled data. Stating this reduction clearly would avoid confusion about why indices over S (and not S̄) appear in Eq. (3).

- Section 2: The notation in Eq. (5) is confusing—why is there an equals sign immediately before ω_C? If ω_C is being defined, a clearer definition or separation of terms would help.

- Section 3 (around line 162): Please clarify how the embedding U is recovered from K in practice. There are several reasonable factorization choices (e.g., eigen/SVD, Cholesky for PSD matrices, possibly with rank truncation), each with different numerical and statistical implications. A brief discussion of the chosen approach would improve reproducibility.

- Section 4 (Algorithm 1, steps 8–9): Ensuring K_r is PSD is typically expensive for general dense symmetric matrices; according to Supplement D.3.2, an eigendecomposition is performed at each iteration. Is this the computational bottleneck in practice? Would it be feasible to optimize directly over a factorization of K (e.g., K = LL^T via a Cholesky-like parameterization), so that projection onto the feasible set reduces to enforcing a triangular structure (zeros in the upper triangular part), guaranteeing PSD by construction? Even if additional constraints couple entries, discussing this alternative parameterization and its trade-offs would be useful.

- Minor: The correct citation is Zhang and Ando, NIPS 2005 (not 2006).

In sum, this is an interesting paper that clarifies how spectral regularization affects error bounds in graph transduction with orthonormal embeddings. It offers new theoretical insights, practical algorithms, and convincing empirical results. Addressing the notational issues and clarifying the recovery of U and the computational cost of PSD projection would further strengthen the presentation.
