This paper presents a comprehensive treatment of semi-supervised learning (SSL) by leveraging the Zhang and Ando result, which demonstrates that SSL can be reduced to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem. The authors begin by introducing the background and highlighting their contributions. They then delve into the theoretical foundations of their approach, providing an error bound in Section 2 and the appendix, which shows that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix is a viable strategy for improving generalization.

The theoretical framework is further developed in Section 3, where the authors introduce a spectral norm regularization term to the Zhang and Ando formulation. This leads to an insightful connection between the error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. The Lovasz number, which reflects the large-scale topological properties of the data graph, plays a crucial role in bridging the gap between the convergence of the embedding estimation and the organization of the data in some space or manifold.

The paper also presents a proximal solver in Section 4, which is specifically designed to handle the projection step approximately, thereby addressing the constraint of a cone-polytope intersection. Furthermore, the authors propose a Multiple Kernel Learning (MKL) extension in Section 5, demonstrating the versatility of their approach. The experimental results in Section 6 provide convincing evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed method on several datasets.

Overall, the paper is interesting and contains a wealth of material, with several important contributions. However, the density of the content may make it challenging for readers to fully appreciate the authors' efforts. One of the most significant contributions is the link between the large-scale topological properties of the data graph and the convergence of the embedding estimation, which bridges several fields and provides new theoretical insights.

To enhance the clarity of the paper, a few suggestions are in order. In Section 1, after equation 3, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) implies that a semi-supervised learning problem becomes a supervised learning problem. This would help readers understand why the sums in equation (3) only involve indices over S, whereas equation (1) includes a regularizer on non-labeled samples and a loss on labeled data.

In Section 2, the notation in equation 5 is confusing, and the equal sign before ωC warrants clarification. In Section 3, around line 162, the authors should mention how the embedding (U) is recovered from K, as there are several practical choices for factorization.

Regarding the proximal solver in Section 4, ensuring that K_r is Positive Semi-Definite (PSD) can be computationally expensive, especially for general dense square symmetric matrices. The current implementation involves an eigendecomposition every time, which may be the primary bottleneck of the algorithm. A potential optimization could be to optimize over a factorization of K, such as a Cholesky decomposition (K=LL^T), where the projection to the feasible set would result in a matrix with all-zeros in the upper triangular part, thereby ensuring that K is PSD.

Finally, the authors should correct the reference to Zhang and Ando, which was published in NIPS 2005, not 2006.

In conclusion, this paper provides a comprehensive treatment of semi-supervised learning, offering new theoretical insights and convincing experimental results. The authors' efforts to bridge the gap between the convergence of the embedding estimation and the organization of the data in some space or manifold are particularly noteworthy.
