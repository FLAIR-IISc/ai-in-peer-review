The paper tackles the problem of learning node labels in a graph. It extends the work of Ando and Zhang, who observed that embedding the graph onto a unit sphere using an orthonormal representation improves generalization performance. However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a method for determining the optimal embedding. This paper's primary contribution is a novel optimization formulation that jointly learns the embedding and node labels, incorporating a regularization term based on the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix. This regularization is motivated by Theorem 1, which establishes an upper bound on the generalization error. The paper further derives asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, demonstrating favorable scaling properties compared to existing results; these bounds also translate to an upper bound on the labeled sample complexity. While the optimization problem can be expressed as a semidefinite program (SDP), the computational cost of solving SDPs is prohibitive. To address this, the authors propose an inexact proximal method that scales to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results suggest that the proposed method outperforms existing approaches.

The reviewer found the main result of the paper, Theorem 4, difficult to interpret at a high level. The text preceding Theorem 4 states that learning labels is impossible in the presence of unlabeled nodes without assumptions about the data, and, following prior work, assumes that edges connect “similar” instances. However, the paper does not define what constitutes “similarity,” nor is this notion included in the theorem’s assumptions. Furthermore, the reviewer could not identify any use of this assumption within the theorem’s proof. Despite this ambiguity, the theorem’s claim—which, in the case of random graphs, can be roughly stated as er^{0-1}[\hat{y}]= O( 1/m (n^{3/4} + \log{1/\delta} )^{1/2} )—appears to indicate that if the vertices of a large random graph are randomly labeled with {0,1} labels, and only a diminishing fraction (e.g., \omega{n^{3/4}}) of these labels are observed, the algorithm can reliably recover the remaining labels with small error. Due to difficulties understanding the main result, the reviewer is unable to form a judgment regarding its validity.
