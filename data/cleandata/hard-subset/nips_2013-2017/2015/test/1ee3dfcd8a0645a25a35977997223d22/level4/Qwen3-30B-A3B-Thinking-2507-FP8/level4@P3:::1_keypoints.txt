- Studies graph transduction using orthonormal representations with spectral-norm regularization to improve generalization.
- Builds on Zhang & Ando (NIPS 2005) showing graph transduction reduces to a kernel-based supervised learning problem.
- Proves orthonormal representations are PAC learnable and gives a novel generalization bound (Theorem 1) linking spectral-norm minimization to better generalization.
- Proposes SPORE: a convex spectral-norm-regularized optimization formulation over the elliptope.
- Shows the optimal kernel equals the Lovász θ function of the complement graph and derives an improved labeled sample complexity bound.
- Introduces the IIP (Infeasible Inexact Proximal) algorithm that scales to thousands of vertices and achieves O(1/√T) convergence.
- Extends the framework to multiple graphs with MKL-SPORE, providing theoretical guarantees and robustness to noisy graphs.
- Theoretical quality is strong: Rademacher-based analysis yields tighter sample complexity than prior work (Shivanna & Bhattacharyya 2013).
- IIP algorithm is a novel, theoretically sound method for approximate projection over elliptopes with optimal convergence rates.
- Empirical results show SPORE consistently outperforms existing methods on small and large graphs, with strong MNIST performance.
- Paper is well-written and organized, though some sections could be clearer (notably the K–U connection in Section 3).
- Notation issues: Equation (5) is confusing and the Zhang & Ando citation year should be corrected to NIPS 2005.
- Originality is high: new connection between Lovász θ and graph transduction, and novel inexact-proximal approach for elliptope optimization.
- Significance is substantial: advances theoretical guarantees and provides scalable algorithms for practical graph transduction and multi-view learning.
- Weaknesses: dense presentation, some unclear notation, and practical impact could be further emphasized with more real-world applications.
- Overall recommendation: accept — strong theoretical contributions, novel algorithms, and compelling experimental validation.
