This paper proposes SEER, a new framework for regularized spectral embedding, and applies it to the problem of graph transduction. The motivation for the framework, particularly the benefits of enforcing an orthonormal embedding, could be more convincingly described, but this is a minor point. The authors then introduce an interesting and elegant algorithm to solve the SEER optimization problem approximately. This algorithm is an original contribution and provides a promising alternative for tackling this complex learning problem.

The experimental results are encouraging and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed solution. However, the evaluation could be significantly strengthened with additional analysis. It would be valuable to see results that specifically isolate and demonstrate the advantages of the SEER framework itself, particularly the orthonormal representation defined in equation (3). Furthermore, an analysis quantifying the approximation accuracy of the proposed IIP solution with respect to the true SEER objective would be highly insightful. These results are currently absent but would be of great interest for positioning the paper's contributions in a broader context.

The extension to multiple graph transduction is unfortunately described at a very high level. While this is an interesting direction, the paper needs to provide more detail, motivation, and intuition. As written, it is difficult to understand precisely how and why the proposed method is beneficial for the multi-graph case, and crucial implementation details for this setting are omitted. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to grasp the core ideas of the multi-graph extension and, in turn, to fully appreciate the corresponding experimental results. For instance, the last column of Table 4 (the noisy case) presents a counter-intuitive result: it appears that using a single graph is better than using multiple graphs, which would contradict the primary motivation for this part of the work.

Finally, the paper's presentation could be improved. The writing is quite dense in places, making it difficult to read. The paper also refers too frequently to the supplementary material for key details. This may be a sign that the authors have tried to include too much material in a single paper, which unfortunately detracts from its clarity, accessibility, and potential impact.

In summary, the paper introduces a valuable new framework and a compelling algorithm. However, its contributions would be more convincing with a more thorough experimental validation and a much clearer exposition of the multi-graph extension.
