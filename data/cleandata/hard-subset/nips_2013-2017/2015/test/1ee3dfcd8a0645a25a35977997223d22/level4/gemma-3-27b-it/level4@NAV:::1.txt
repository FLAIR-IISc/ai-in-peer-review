The paper begins with a clear introduction of the problem and outlines its contributions. The authors then leverage the Zhang and Ando result to frame self-supervised learning as an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem, a perspective maintained throughout the remainder of the work. Sections 2 and the appendix present an error bound demonstrating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix leads to improved generalization performance. In section 3, a spectral norm regularization term is incorporated into the Zhang and Ando formulation, with connections established between error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. A proximal solver is proposed in section 4, notable for its approximate handling of the projection step required to satisfy a cone-polytope intersection constraint. Section 5 extends the approach to a multiple kernel learning (MKL) setting, and section 6 concludes with experimental validation across multiple datasets.

Overall, this is a compelling paper that presents a substantial body of work – potentially quite dense for a single publication – and includes several significant contributions. The relationship demonstrated between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (captured by the Lovasz number) and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5) is particularly insightful, creating a bridge between multiple research areas. 

Specifically, in section 1 following equation 3, clarifying that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) transforms a semi-supervised learning problem into a supervised learning framework would be beneficial. Currently, the summation indices over *S* only (excluding *¬S*) in equation (3) are somewhat surprising, given that the regularizer in equation (1) considers both labelled and unlabelled samples while the loss is defined on labelled data.

In section 2, the notation in equation 5 is unclear – the presence of an equal sign immediately preceding *ω<sub>C</sub>* is puzzling. 

In section 3, around line 162, a discussion of how the embedding (*U*) is recovered from the kernel matrix (*K*) would be helpful, as several practical factorization choices exist.

Regarding section 4, the algorithm’s step 8-9 involves ensuring *K<sub>r</sub>* is positive semi-definite (PSD), a computationally expensive operation, particularly given the repeated eigendecomposition detailed in supplementary D.3.2. This step may represent a performance bottleneck. Exploring alternative optimization strategies, such as optimizing directly on a factorization of *K* (e.g., Cholesky decomposition with *K = LL<sup>T</sup>*), could be worthwhile; projection to the feasible set would then involve setting upper triangular elements to zero, inherently guaranteeing PSD.

It should be noted that the Zhang and Ando paper was published in NIPS 2005, not 2006. 

In conclusion, this is a noteworthy paper that elucidates the impact of spectral regularization on error bounds for graph transduction via orthonormal embedding, providing novel theoretical insights and supported by persuasive experimental results.
