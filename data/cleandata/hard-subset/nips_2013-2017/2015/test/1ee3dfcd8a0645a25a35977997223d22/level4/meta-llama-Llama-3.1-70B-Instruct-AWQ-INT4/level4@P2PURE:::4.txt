The paper introduces a novel framework for spectral regularized embedding, applied to graph transduction, which could be more persuasively presented, particularly in highlighting the benefits of an orthonormal embedding. However, this is not a major concern.

The authors propose an interesting and elegant algorithm to solve the SEER problem approximately, which is an original contribution offering a viable alternative for tackling the complex learning problem.

The experimental results demonstrate the benefits of the proposed solution. To provide a more comprehensive understanding, it would be beneficial to present results that showcase the advantages of the SEER framework or the orthonormal representation defined by equation (3) initially. Additionally, it would be valuable to assess the approximation accuracy of the IIP solution to the SEER problem, which is not currently visible in the results. This would help position the actual contribution within a broader context.

Unfortunately, the extension to multiple graph transduction is described at a high level, lacking sufficient details, motivation, and intuition to clearly comprehend how and why the proposed method is also relevant for multiple graphs. Specifically, it is unclear how the algorithm is implemented in such settings. As a result, it is challenging to grasp the main ideas in the multiple graph case and appreciate the experimental results. For instance, the last column of Table 4 (noisy case) is not intuitive, suggesting that one graph performs better than multiple graphs, which contradicts the motivation for dealing with multiple graphs.

The paper is not always easy to read and is dense in certain sections. Furthermore, it frequently refers to the supplementary material, indicating that the authors may be attempting to convey too much information in a single paper, which compromises the accessibility and potentially the visibility of the work. Nevertheless, the paper proposes a new framework, SEER, for orthonormal embeddings applied to graph transduction, an interesting algorithm based on IIP to solve the learning problem in SEER, and presents relatively convincing results.
