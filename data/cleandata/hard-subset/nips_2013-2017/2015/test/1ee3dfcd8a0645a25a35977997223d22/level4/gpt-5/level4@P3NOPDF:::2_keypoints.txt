- Paper studies semi-supervised label learning on graphs and proposes jointly learning embeddings and labels via a spectral-norm regularizer motivated by a generalization bound, with an inexact proximal algorithm and empirical gains.  
- Strength: Clear motivation for coupling embedding and label learning grounded in generalization analysis.  
- Strength: Use of the spectral norm as a principled regularizer aligned with Theorem 1.  
- Strength: Proposed inexact proximal algorithm addresses SDP scalability and appears practical for thousands of vertices.  
- Strength: Experiments indicate improved performance over presented baselines.  
- Main concern: Theorem 4 is hard to interpret because key assumptions are missing or implicit, so its validity is unclear.  
- Main concern: The informal premise “edges link similar instances” is never defined (no formal notion of similarity) and is not stated in the theorem or located in the proof.  
- Main concern: The theorem’s claimed 0–1 error scaling suggests implausible recovery under random labels unless the graph/label model imposes strong structure, which is not specified.  
- Main concern: Theorem 4 omits critical model details—precise graph model, label-generation process, source of randomness, transductive vs inductive setting, and constant/regime scaling—so significance is unclear.  
- Request: Make explicit how the spectral-norm regularizer controls the complexity term in Theorem 1 and provide a sketch linking the bound to the optimization design.  
- Request: Clearly compare and contrast bounds and algorithmic differences with Ando and Zhang (assumptions, rates, practical performance, when improvements occur).  
- Request: Elaborate how joint optimization of embedding and labels prevents overfitting and whether the regularizer gives a provable bias–variance trade-off.  
- Request: Provide convergence guarantees for the inexact proximal method (rates, stopping criteria, dependence on problem constants) and detail memory/time complexity as functions of n, m, and sparsity.  
- Request: Explain how scalability is maintained if the induced kernel matrix is dense.  
- Request: Clarify hyperparameter selection and include sensitivity analyses or ablations to show robustness across datasets and graph types.  
- Request: Strengthen experiments with standard baselines (label propagation, Laplacian regularization, graph SVMs), report statistical significance, runtime, memory, and scalability sweeps over n and sparsity.  
- Request: If a sample-complexity guarantee follows, present it as a corollary with explicit assumptions and clean dependence on n, graph parameters, and δ, and compare to prior results.  
- Minor: Define “similar” precisely and include it in theorem assumptions and proofs.  
- Minor: Clearly define all symbols in Theorem 4 (m, n, δ) and the probability space for high-probability statements.  
- Minor: State the exact random graph/label model and any regularity conditions (minimum degree, spectral gap).  
- Minor: Clarify whether edges are weighted and how weights affect the kernel and regularizer.  
- Minor: Rephrase “orthonormal presentation” as needed and explicitly relate the embedding to the kernel construction.  
- Minor: Provide a brief, self-contained description of baselines and their hyperparameter tuning.  
- Minor: Fix grammatical issues and unify notation for norms (consistently denote the spectral norm).  
- Overall: The approach is promising with encouraging empirical results, but Theorem 4 is currently too opaque—clarify assumptions and provide a self-contained theorem statement before the core theoretical advance can be judged.
