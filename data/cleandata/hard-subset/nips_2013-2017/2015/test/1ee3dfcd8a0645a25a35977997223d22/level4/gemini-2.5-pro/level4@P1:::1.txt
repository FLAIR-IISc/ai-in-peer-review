This paper addresses the problem of graph transduction by learning an optimal orthonormal representation of the graph. The authors build upon prior work that recasts the semi-supervised problem as an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning task. The main contributions are both theoretical and algorithmic. First, the paper derives a PAC-based generalization bound which reveals that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix is beneficial for generalization. This insight motivates the core proposal: SPORE (SPectral regularized ORthonormal Embedding), a formulation for graph transduction that includes a spectral norm regularizer. A key theoretical result connects the error convergence rate to the Lovász ϑ number of the graph, establishing a novel link between topological graph properties and learning guarantees. To solve the resulting non-smooth convex optimization problem, the authors develop a scalable Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm, which is notable for its use of an approximate projection to handle the constraints of an elliptope. The framework is also extended to multiple graph learning, and the paper concludes with experiments demonstrating the method's effectiveness on several datasets.

**Strengths**

This is a strong and interesting paper containing a significant amount of material and several important contributions.
*   **Originality & Significance:** The main strength lies in its novel theoretical analysis. The connection established between the large-scale topological properties of the data graph (via the Lovász ϑ number) and the convergence of the embedding estimation is particularly enlightening. This insight bridges graph theory, optimization, and learning theory in a compelling way and provides a clear, principled motivation for the proposed spectral norm regularizer.
*   **Quality:** The development of the IIP algorithm to efficiently solve the SPORE formulation is another key contribution, making the approach practical for large-scale problems where standard SDP solvers would fail. The theoretical analysis of the algorithm appears sound.
*   **Significance:** The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating superior performance over existing methods and validating the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, there are several areas where its clarity and presentation could be improved. The paper is quite dense, and addressing the following points would make it more accessible.

1.  **Clarity of Formulation:** In Section 1, after Equation (3), it would be helpful to explicitly state that the correspondence with Equation (1) effectively transforms the semi-supervised problem into a supervised one that is learned only on the labeled samples. This would clarify why the sums in Equation (3) are only over the labeled set S, which can otherwise be confusing given that the regularizer in Equation (1) involves all samples.

2.  **Recovering the Embedding:** In Section 3, after defining the optimal kernel K*, it would be useful to discuss how the corresponding embedding (U) is recovered from K. Since the factorization is not unique, briefly mentioning the practical choices would be beneficial for reproducibility.

3.  **Algorithmic Efficiency:** Regarding the proposed IIP algorithm in Section 4, steps 8-9 of Algorithm 1 require projecting K onto the PSD cone, which is handled via an eigendecomposition (as detailed in the supplement). This step can be computationally expensive for large, dense matrices. Is this the primary computational bottleneck of the algorithm? As a potential improvement, have the authors considered optimizing directly over a factorization of K (e.g., a Cholesky factorization K=LL^T)? This would enforce the PSD constraint by construction, and the projection onto the feasible set of equality constraints might be simpler.

4.  **Minor Corrections:**
    *   The notation in Equation (5) appears to contain a typographical error. Please double-check the formulation for clarity.
    *   The reference to Zhang and Ando [19] should cite NIPS 2005.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a well-motivated paper that provides new theoretical insights into graph transduction, supported by a practical and scalable algorithm with convincing experimental results. I recommend acceptance.
