The paper starts by introducing the background and showing the contributions. The authors then use the Zhang and Ando result showing that semi-supervised learning (SSL) reduces to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem for the rest of the paper. In section 2 and appendix, they provide an error bound showing that minimising the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix is a good way to improve generalisation. In section 3, they add a spectral norm regularization term to the Zhang and Ando formulation, and provide links between the error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. Section 4 proposes a proximal solver, where the specificity is that it deals with the projection step approximately to handle the constraint of a cone-polytope intersection. Section 5 proposes an MKL extension, and section 6 concludes with experimental results on several datasets.

Overall, the paper is interesting and contains a lot of material (maybe even a bit dense for a single paper), with several important contributions. The link between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (Lovasz number), which reflect its organisation in some space or manifold, and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5) is particularly enlightening and bridges several fields.

Some minor suggestions for improvement are as follows:

* In section 1 after equation 3, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) means that a semi-supervised learning problem becomes a supervised learning problem, so that the learning is on labelled samples. This would clarify why the sums in equation (3) only have indices over S, whereas in equation (1) the regulariser is also on non-labeled samples while the loss is on labelled data.
* In section 2, the notation of equation 5 is slightly confusing, as the equal sign before ω_C is not necessary.
* In section 3, around line 162, it would be useful to mention how the embedding (U) is recovered from K. There are several practical choices for the factorisation, and a brief discussion of these would be helpful.
* In section 4, Algorithm 1 steps 8-9 involve ensuring that K_r is PSD, which can be quite expensive for general dense square symmetric matrices. An eigendecomposition is done every time, which may be where the algorithm takes most of its time. It would be worth considering whether it would be feasible to optimise on a factorisation of K (say, Cholesky with K=LL^T), where projection to the feasible set would be to a matrix with all-zeros in the upper triangular, which would by construction ensure that K is PSD.
* The Zhang and Ando reference is listed as being from NIPS 2006, but it actually appeared in NIPS 2005.

This is an interesting paper showing how spectral regularization impacts error bounds on graph transduction using orthonormal embedding, which provides new theoretical insights and convincing experimental results.

Strengths:

* The paper provides a clear and well-motivated introduction to the problem of graph transduction using orthonormal embedding.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the error bounds for the proposed method, including a discussion of the Lovasz number and its relationship to the convergence of the embedding estimation.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Weaknesses:

* The paper is a bit dense and could be improved with some minor reorganisation and clarification of the notation.
* Some of the proofs and derivations are relegated to the appendix, which may make it difficult for some readers to follow the paper.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper for publication, as it provides a significant contribution to the field of graph transduction and semi-supervised learning.
