

The paper introduces SPORE (SPectral regularized ORthonormal Embedding), a novel framework for graph transduction that leverages orthonormal representations with spectral norm regularization. The authors establish PAC learnability for orthonormal embeddings, which they show are equivalent to the set of graph kernel matrices with normalized diagonals. Building on this, they derive a generalization bound (Theorem 1) that suggests spectral norm regularization can improve generalization performance, leading to their SPORE formulation as a convex optimization problem over an elliptope. The key technical contribution is the Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm for efficiently solving SPORE, which avoids the scalability limitations of standard SDP approaches. The paper also extends their framework to multiple graph transduction using an MKL-style approach.

The work relates well to previous NIPS contributions. While [1] established the importance of unit-sphere embeddings for graph transduction, their analysis did not provide guidance for choosing optimal embeddings. [16] demonstrated statistical consistency of orthonormal embeddings but left open the question of optimal embedding selection and efficient computation. The authors address these gaps by showing how spectral norm regularization leads to better generalization (Theorem 1) and providing an efficient algorithm (IIP) to compute the optimal embedding.

**Strengths:** The paper makes several strong contributions. First, the PAC analysis (Theorem 1) is technically sound and provides a theoretically grounded approach to choosing optimal embeddings, improving upon the sample complexity estimates in [1, 16]. The authors correctly identify that the VC dimension-based analysis in [2] does not apply to orthonormal embeddings, and their alternative approach using Rademacher averages is appropriate and well-executed. Second, the IIP algorithm is novel and well-motivated, providing a scalable alternative to SDP solvers with a rigorous O(1/√T) convergence rate (Theorem 6). The theoretical guarantees for the IIP algorithm (Theorems 5 and 6) are particularly strong, addressing a key computational bottleneck in the field. Third, the extension to multiple graph transduction (MKL-SPORE) is well-justified and provides a principled way to combine multiple graph views.

**Weaknesses:** The paper would benefit from greater clarity in several areas. The connection between orthonormal representations and the kernel learning formulation (Equation 3) could be better motivated, as it's currently presented as a given. The experimental section lacks a clear demonstration of the benefits of the SPORE framework itself versus existing methods. Table 1 shows improved accuracy over baselines, but without comparing to the unregularized version of the same kernel learning approach, it's difficult to isolate the contribution of the spectral regularization. The authors should have included a comparison showing how SPORE improves upon the baseline kernel methods without spectral regularization.

The multiple graph transduction section (Section 5) is too brief and lacks sufficient motivation. While the extension to multiple graphs is theoretically sound, the paper doesn't clearly explain why the proposed MKL-SPORE formulation (Equation 17) is preferable to simpler approaches like weighted combinations of individual graph kernels. The experimental results in Table 4 for the "noisy" case (where one graph is worse than the others) are confusing and seem to contradict the paper's own motivation for combining multiple graphs. The authors should clarify why the noisy case shows improved performance with multiple graphs despite one graph performing worse than the others.

The paper is well-written technically, but some sections are dense and rely too heavily on the supplementary material. The authors could improve accessibility by moving key proofs and derivations to the main text. The notation is generally consistent, though the use of "SEER" instead of "SPORE" in the draft review (and in parts of the paper) is confusing and should be corrected.

**Quality:** The theoretical contributions are strong and well-supported. The PAC analysis, SPORE formulation, and IIP algorithm are technically sound with rigorous convergence guarantees. The experiments validate the main claims but could better isolate the contribution of spectral regularization.

**Clarity:** The paper would benefit from a clearer presentation of the relationship between orthonormal representations and the kernel learning formulation (Equation 3). The multiple graph section needs more detailed explanation to help readers understand the novelty of the MKL-SPORE approach.

**Originality:** The spectral norm regularization approach for orthonormal embeddings is novel, as the authors correctly note that existing work [1, 16] did not address optimal embedding selection. The IIP algorithm for solving the elliptope-constrained optimization problem is also novel and addresses a significant scalability limitation.

**Significance:** The work has clear practical significance for graph transduction tasks, especially for large graphs where SDP solvers are infeasible. The theoretical improvements to sample complexity estimates (Theorem 4) advance our understanding of the relationship between graph structure and learning performance. The extension to multiple graphs provides a useful framework for multi-view learning problems.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution with practical implications, addressing important gaps in the literature on graph transduction. While the experimental section could better isolate the contribution of the spectral regularization component, the theoretical advances are substantial and the IIP algorithm is a valuable contribution to the field. The authors should revise the multiple graph section to better explain the motivation and clarify the results in the noisy case. With these minor revisions, the paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
