This paper introduces SEER, a new framework for spectral regularized orthonormal embedding, and applies it to graph transduction. The authors propose an interesting and elegant iterative algorithm to approximately solve the SEER optimization problem. This algorithm is a novel contribution and appears to be a promising alternative for this complex learning task.

While the framework is compelling, its motivation could be strengthened. In particular, the benefits of enforcing an orthonormal embedding are not described convincingly enough to highlight the framework's advantages.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed solution, but they could be significantly improved by providing more targeted analysis. The current results do not isolate the benefits of the core contributions. It would be valuable to include experiments that: 1) first demonstrate the advantages of the SEER framework itself and the orthonormal representation from Eq. (3), separate from the proposed solver, and 2) then evaluate the approximation accuracy of the IIP solution relative to the true SEER problem. Such results would help to better contextualize the paper's specific contributions.

The extension to multiple graph transduction is described at a very high level and lacks sufficient detail. To be comprehensible, this section requires more motivation, intuition, and a clearer explanation of how the algorithm is implemented in the multi-graph setting. As it stands, it is difficult to grasp the core ideas and fully appreciate the corresponding experimental results. For instance, the result in the last column of Table 4 (the noisy case) is counter-intuitive, as it suggests that a single graph outperforms multiple graphs, which contradicts the primary motivation for the multi-graph approach.

Finally, the paper is dense and not always easy to read. The frequent references to the supplementary material for key details detract from the main text. This suggests the paper may be attempting to convey too much information, which ultimately hinders the accessibility and impact of the work.

In summary, the paper introduces the promising SEER framework and a novel algorithm to solve its learning problem. The work would be substantially strengthened by a clearer motivation for the framework, more insightful experiments that isolate the individual contributions, and a much more detailed treatment of the multi-graph extension. Improving the clarity and focus of the presentation would also significantly increase the paper's impact.
