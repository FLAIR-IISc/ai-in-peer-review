The paper begins by introducing the necessary background and outlining its contributions. The authors then leverage the result from Zhang and Ando (2005) demonstrating that semi-supervised learning (SSL) can be reduced to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem, framing the remainder of their work within this context. In Section 2 and the appendix, they present an error bound demonstrating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix is an effective approach to improving generalization. Section 3 adds a spectral norm regularization term to the Zhang and Ando formulation and establishes connections between the error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. Section 4 proposes a proximal solver, notable for its approximate handling of the projection step required for a cone-polytope intersection constraint. An MKL extension is proposed in Section 5, and Section 6 concludes with experimental results on several datasets. 

Overall, this is an interesting and substantial paper—perhaps even somewhat dense for a single publication—with several important contributions. The connection established between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (specifically, the Lovasz number), which reflect its organization within a space or manifold, and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5) is particularly insightful and bridges multiple fields.

In Section 1, following equation (3), clarifying that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) signifies a reduction of the SSL problem to a supervised learning problem would be beneficial. Currently, the indices over *S* only (without *¬S*) in the sums of equation (3) are somewhat surprising, given that the regularizer in equation (1) applies to unlabeled samples while the loss is computed on labeled data.

In Section 2, the notation in equation (5) is confusing—the presence of an equal sign immediately preceding *ω<sub>C</sub>* is unclear.

In Section 3, around line 162, it would be helpful to mention how the embedding (*U*) is recovered from *K*. Several practical choices exist for this factorization.

In Section 4, Algorithm 1, steps 8-9 involve ensuring that *K<sub>r</sub>* is positive semi-definite (PSD), a typically computationally expensive operation, particularly for general dense, symmetric matrices. The supplementary material (D.3.2) indicates an eigendecomposition is performed at each iteration. Is this the primary bottleneck of the algorithm? Would it be feasible to optimize directly over a factorization of *K* (e.g., Cholesky decomposition with *K = LL<sup>T</sup>*)? Projection to the feasible set could then be achieved by zeroing the upper triangular entries of the matrix, which would by construction guarantee that *K* remains PSD.

Finally, please note that Zhang and Ando’s work was published in NIPS 2005, not 2006.

This is an interesting paper demonstrating how spectral regularization impacts error bounds on graph transduction using orthonormal embedding, providing new theoretical insights and supported by convincing experimental results.
