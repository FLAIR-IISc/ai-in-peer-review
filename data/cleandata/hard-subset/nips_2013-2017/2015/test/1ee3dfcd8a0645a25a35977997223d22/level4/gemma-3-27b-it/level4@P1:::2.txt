The paper addresses the problem of learning labels on the vertices of a graph. It builds on the work of Ando and Zhang, which suggested that embedding the graph on a unit sphere, using an orthonormal representation, leads to better generalization performance. Ando and Zhang, however, did not present a concrete method for choosing an optimal embedding. This paper's contribution is a novel optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and the learned labels, introducing a regularization term based on the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix. This regularization is motivated by Theorem 1, which provides an upper bound on the generalization error. Further results in the paper establish asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, exhibiting favorable scaling compared to previous results, and translate this to an upper bound for labeled sample complexity. While the proposed optimization problem can be formulated as an SDP, the computational cost is prohibitive for large graphs. Therefore, the paper proposes solving it using an inexact proximal method, which scales to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method compared to existing approaches.

The reviewer found it difficult to fully grasp the main result of the paper, Theorem 4, at a high level. The paragraph preceding Theorem 4 states that learning labels requires an assumption of similarity between connected nodes, but lacks a clear definition of “similar” and doesn’t explicitly state this assumption within the theorem itself. The reviewer could not find any explicit use of this assumption in the theorem's proof. Despite this, the theorem's claim, particularly in the case of random graphs, can be roughly stated as: 
er<sup>0-1</sup>[ŷ] = O( 1/m (√n + log(1/δ))<sup>1/2</sup> ). 
This suggests that given a large random graph with randomly assigned {0,1} labels, and observing a diminishing fraction of these labels (e.g., proportional to √n), the algorithm is likely to accurately predict the remaining labels with high probability. The reviewer’s difficulty in fully understanding the main result hinders a complete evaluation of the work.

Here’s a breakdown of strengths and weaknesses:

**Strengths:**

*   **Technically Sound:** The paper appears technically rigorous, building on established concepts in graph learning and regularization. The derivation of the generalization bound (Theorem 1) and its application to the proposed SPORE formulation seem well-motivated.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of spectral norm regularization with orthonormal embeddings for graph transduction is a novel approach. The use of an inexact proximal method for scalable optimization is also a valuable contribution.
*   **Scalability:** Addressing the scalability issue of SDP solvers with the proposed IIP method is a significant practical improvement.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, including generalization bounds and sample complexity estimates. The analysis extends to multiple graphs, adding further value.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Assumptions:** The assumption about the similarity of connected nodes needs to be explicitly stated and justified within the context of Theorem 4. The current wording is vague and hinders understanding.
*   **Accessibility:** While technically sound, some parts of the paper are dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with the specific technical details of PAC learning and spectral graph theory to follow.
*   **Limited Explanation of IIP:** The explanation of the IIP method could be more detailed, particularly the justification for the approximation criteria and its impact on convergence.
*   **Experiments scope:** Experiments are limited and comparison with more recent methods would benefit the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to graph transduction.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the generalization properties of the proposed method.
*   The proposed IIP method addresses the scalability issue of existing SDP-based approaches.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity surrounding the assumptions in Theorem 4 is a significant concern.
*   The technical density of the paper may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of graph transduction. While the clarity of the assumptions underlying Theorem 4 is a concern, the technical soundness, novelty, and scalability of the proposed approach outweigh this weakness. The paper addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and thoroughly analyzed solution.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should clarify the assumption about node similarity in the context of Theorem 4 and consider adding more detailed explanations of the IIP method and related theoretical concepts. Improving the accessibility of the paper would also be beneficial.
