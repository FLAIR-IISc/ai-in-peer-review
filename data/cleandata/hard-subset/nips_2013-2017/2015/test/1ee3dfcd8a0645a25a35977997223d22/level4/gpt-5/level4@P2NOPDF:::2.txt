This paper studies learning binary labels on the vertices of a graph. It builds on Ando and Zhang’s observation that embedding the graph on the unit sphere via an orthonormal representation can improve generalization (test) error, but that prior work did not specify how to choose an optimal embedding. The main contribution here is an optimization framework that jointly learns the embedding and the labels by adding a regularization term. Motivated by Theorem 1’s generalization bound, the authors select the spectral norm of the associated graph kernel matrix as the regularizer. The paper further derives asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method with more favorable scaling than previous results; this also yields a bound on the number of labeled samples required. Although the joint optimization can be written as a semidefinite program (SDP), solving SDPs at scale is impractical; instead, the paper proposes an inexact proximal method, which the authors claim scales to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experiments suggest performance improvements over prior methods.

However, I could not understand the paper’s main theoretical result (Theorem 4) at a high level. Immediately before Theorem 4, the paper states that “in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances.” The paper does not define “similar,” and this assumption does not appear among the theorem’s stated assumptions. I also could not find where or how such an assumption is used in the proof of Theorem 4. As a result, it is unclear what data-generating conditions are required for the bound to hold or how the graph construction must reflect “similarity.”

As I understand it, the theorem’s claim in the random-graph setting can be summarized as:
er^{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O((1/m) · (n^{3/4} + log(1/δ))^{1/2}),
where n is the number of nodes, m is the number of labeled nodes, and δ is a confidence parameter. Interpreting this, for large random graphs with labels assigned uniformly at random, if we observe m = ω(n^{3/4}) labeled nodes—i.e., a vanishing fraction of the total—the algorithm recovers the remaining labels with small 0–1 error with high probability. Because I do not see the role of the “edges link similar instances” assumption in the theorem statement or proof, I am unable to assess whether this interpretation is correct or whether the result applies beyond this specific random-label scenario.

In summary, the paper proposes a well-motivated joint embedding-and-labeling objective with a spectral-norm regularizer, offers an algorithmic approach that avoids solving full SDPs, and presents encouraging empirical results. However, without a clear, explicit statement of the similarity assumption (its definition, where it enters the model, and how it is used in Theorem 4 and its proof), I am unable to evaluate the paper’s main theoretical contribution. I encourage the authors to precisely define “similar,” incorporate it into the theorem’s assumptions, and make its use explicit in the proof and in the discussion preceding Theorem 4.
