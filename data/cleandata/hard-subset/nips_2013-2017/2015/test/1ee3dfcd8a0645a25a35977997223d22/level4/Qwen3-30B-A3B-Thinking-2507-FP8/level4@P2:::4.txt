

This paper proposes SPORE (SPectral norm regularized ORthonormal Embedding), a novel framework for graph transduction that leverages orthonormal representations with spectral norm regularization. The authors demonstrate that orthonormal representations are efficiently PAC learnable and derive a generalization bound connecting spectral norm to performance. They then develop an efficient Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm for solving the resulting optimization problem, which scales to graphs with thousands of vertices compared to the hundreds typical for standard SDP approaches.

The paper makes three significant contributions. First, it provides a PAC analysis of orthonormal embeddings for graph transduction, addressing a key limitation in prior work by [1] (which offers no guidance on embedding choice) and [16] (which establishes statistical consistency but leaves optimal embedding unspecified). Theorem 1 offers a refined generalization bound that explicitly connects spectral norm to performance, a notable theoretical advance.

Second, the authors develop a novel IIP algorithm for approximating projections onto the elliptope, enabling efficient solution of SPORE. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 5 and 6) is sound, demonstrating O(1/√T) convergence, and the application to SPORE (Section 4.2) is well-motivated and technically rigorous.

Third, the paper extends the approach to multiple graph transduction (MKL-SPORE), with theoretical analysis (Theorem 8) and experimental results (Table 4) demonstrating benefits of combining multiple graphs, especially in noisy settings.

The paper's quality is strong: Theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs in supplementary material, and experimental results consistently outperform baselines (Tables 1 and 2). The authors provide clear comparisons against Unnormalized Laplacian, Normalized Laplacian, and K-Scaling methods.

The originality is high. While orthonormal embeddings have been studied in [16], the authors' PAC analysis and spectral norm regularization are novel contributions. The IIP algorithm also advances the optimization literature for constrained convex problems.

The significance of this work is substantial. Graph transduction is a fundamental problem with broad applications, and SPORE's scalability could enable practical application to larger real-world graphs. The multiple graph extension is particularly relevant for multi-view learning scenarios.

However, the paper would benefit from several improvements. First, the paper would be strengthened by a clearer explanation of why orthonormal representations are beneficial for graph transduction. The connection to the Lovász θ function is mentioned but not sufficiently developed.

Second, the experimental section would be more compelling with results specifically demonstrating the benefits of the SPORE framework over existing kernel-based approaches. The current results (Table 1) show SPORE outperforms baselines but don't isolate whether the improvement comes from the embedding choice or the regularization. A controlled experiment comparing orthonormal embeddings with and without spectral regularization would be valuable.

Third, the multiple graph transduction section (Section 5) is described at a high level. The paper states that "combining multiple graphs improves classification accuracy" but provides insufficient intuition about why this is the case. The noisy graph results in Table 4 are particularly confusing, as the multiple graph approach appears to underperform compared to single graphs in the noisy case. The authors should clarify this apparent contradiction.

Finally, while technically strong, the paper is dense and relies heavily on supplementary material. The authors should incorporate more key details into the main text to improve accessibility and clarity.

In conclusion, this is a solid contribution with strong theoretical foundations and practical scalability improvements. The proposed SPORE framework and IIP algorithm are novel and well-motivated. With improved clarity and more specific experimental comparisons, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
