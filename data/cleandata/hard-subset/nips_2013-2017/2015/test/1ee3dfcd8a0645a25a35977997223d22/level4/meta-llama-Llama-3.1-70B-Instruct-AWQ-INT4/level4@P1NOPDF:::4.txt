The paper proposes a new framework for spectral regularized embedding, applied to graph transduction. While the framework itself is intriguing, the authors could further elaborate on its benefits, particularly in having an orthonormal embedding. However, this is not a major concern.

The authors then propose an interesting and elegant algorithm to solve the SEER problem approximately. This is an original contribution, providing a viable alternative to tackling the complex learning problem. The algorithm's development is a notable strength of the paper.

Experimental results tend to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed solution. However, it would be beneficial to provide additional results that highlight the advantages of the SEER framework and the orthonormal representation defined by equation (3). Furthermore, assessing the approximation accuracy of the IIP solution to the SEER problem would be valuable. Currently, these aspects are not evident in the results, and presenting them in a broader context would be highly informative.

The extension to multiple graph transduction is described at a high level, but it lacks sufficient details, motivation, and intuition. This makes it challenging to grasp the main ideas and appreciate the experimental results in this context. Specifically, the last column of Table 4 (noisy case) is counterintuitive, suggesting that one graph may be more effective than multiple graphs, which contradicts the motivation for addressing multiple graphs.

The paper's readability is sometimes compromised by its density and the numerous references to the supplementary material. This may indicate that the authors are attempting to convey too much information in a single paper, which ultimately hinders the work's accessibility and visibility. Nevertheless, the paper proposes a novel framework, SEER, for orthonormal embeddings applied to graph transduction, and presents an interesting algorithm based on IIP to solve the learning problem in SEER, along with relatively convincing results.
