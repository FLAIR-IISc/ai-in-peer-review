The paper begins with a clear introduction of the problem and a statement of its contributions. The authors then leverage the Zhang and Ando result to frame self-supervised learning as an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem, a perspective maintained throughout the remainder of the work. Sections 2 and the appendix detail an error bound demonstrating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix promotes improved generalization. Building on this, Section 3 incorporates a spectral norm regularization term into the Zhang and Ando formulation, establishing connections between error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. A proximal solver is proposed in Section 4, with a notable focus on approximating the projection step to efficiently handle the constraint of a cone-polytope intersection. Section 5 extends the approach to a Multiple Kernel Learning (MKL) setting, and Section 6 presents experimental validation on multiple datasets.

Overall, this is a compelling paper with substantial content – perhaps even ambitious in scope for a single publication – and several noteworthy contributions. The connection established between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (specifically, the Lovasz number) and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5) is particularly insightful, effectively bridging multiple research areas. 

Regarding Section 1, following equation 3, clarifying that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) signifies a reduction of the semi-supervised learning problem to a supervised learning task would be beneficial. Currently, the indices over *S* only in equation (3), without inclusion of \bar{S}, are somewhat surprising given the regularizer in equation (1) operates on both labeled and unlabeled samples while the loss is computed on labeled data.

In Section 2, the notation in equation 5 is unclear – the presence of an equal sign immediately preceding \omega_C is confusing.

Around line 162 in Section 3, a brief discussion of how the embedding (*U*) is recovered from *K* would be helpful, as there are several practical factorization choices available.

Concerning Section 4, the algorithm’s step 8-9 involves ensuring *K<sub>r</sub>* is positive semi-definite (PSD), a typically computationally expensive operation, particularly given the repeated eigendecomposition detailed in supplementary material D.3.2. This step appears to be a potential bottleneck for the algorithm’s performance. Exploring alternative optimization strategies, such as optimizing directly on a factorization of *K* (e.g., Cholesky decomposition with *K=LL<sup>T</sup>*), and projecting to the feasible set by enforcing zeros in the upper triangular matrix, could offer a more efficient approach to guaranteeing PSD while potentially reducing computational cost.

It should be noted that the Zhang and Ando paper was published in NIPS 2005, not 2006.

In conclusion, this is a valuable paper that demonstrates the impact of spectral regularization on error bounds for graph transduction using orthonormal embeddings, offering new theoretical insights and supported by convincing experimental results.
