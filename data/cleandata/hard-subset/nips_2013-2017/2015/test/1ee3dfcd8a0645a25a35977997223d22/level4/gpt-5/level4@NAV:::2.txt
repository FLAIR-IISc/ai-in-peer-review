The paper studies the problem of learning labels on graph vertices. It builds on Ando and Zhang, who argued that embedding the graph on the unit sphere via an orthonormal representation improves generalization (test) error, but did not provide a concrete method for selecting an optimal embedding. This work introduces a new optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and the labels by adding a regularization term. The regularizer—the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix—is motivated by Theorem 1, which provides an upper bound on generalization error. The paper further presents asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method with favorable scaling relative to prior results, and translates this into a bound on labeled sample complexity. Although the problem can be cast as an SDP, solving SDPs is computationally impractical; therefore, the paper advocates an inexact proximal method that scales to graphs with thousands of vertices. Empirical results indicate the proposed approach outperforms previous methods.

I could not understand the paper’s main result (Theorem 4) at a high level. In the paragraph preceding Theorem 4, the authors state that with unlabeled nodes, learning is impossible without assumptions, and, following prior work [1, 12], assume that an edge connects similar instances. However, “similar” is never defined, it does not appear in the theorem’s stated assumptions, and I could not find where this assumption is used in the proof. Despite this, the theorem’s claim can be summarized, in the random-graph setting, as er^{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O((1/m) (n^{3/4} + log(1/delta))^{1/2}). As I understand it, this implies that if vertices of a large random graph receive {0,1} labels at random and then a vanishing fraction of labels—for example, ω(n^{3/4}) out of n—are observed, the algorithm is guaranteed with high probability to recover the remaining labels with small error.
