This paper presents a compelling investigation into the impact of spectral regularization on error bounds for graph transduction, leveraging orthonormal embeddings and establishing new theoretical insights supported by convincing experimental results. The work is interesting and ambitious, covering a substantial amount of material – perhaps even a bit dense for a single publication – and offers several important contributions. Notably, the connection drawn between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (specifically, the Lovasz number) and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5) is particularly enlightening, bridging concepts from several fields.

The paper begins by introducing the necessary background and clearly outlining its contributions. The authors build upon the foundational result by Zhang and Ando (2005 – note the correct year) to frame semi-supervised learning (SSL) as an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem, a perspective maintained throughout the remainder of the paper. Sections 2 and the appendix present an error bound demonstrating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix promotes improved generalization. In Section 3, a spectral norm regularization term is added to the Zhang and Ando formulation, and links are established between error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. A proximal solver is proposed in Section 4, with a key feature being its approximate handling of the projection step to enforce constraints involving the intersection of a cone and a polytope. Section 5 extends the framework to Multiple Kernel Learning (MKL), and Section 6 concludes with experimental validation on several datasets.

I have a few specific suggestions for improvement. In Section 1, following equation (3), it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) signifies a reduction of the SSL problem to a supervised learning problem, effectively focusing learning on the labeled samples. Currently, the summation indices over *S* only (without *Ŝ*, representing the unlabeled set) in equation (3) are somewhat surprising, given that the regularizer in equation (1) operates on both labeled and unlabeled samples, while the loss is defined only on labeled data. Clarifying this connection would enhance understanding.

In Section 2, the notation in equation (5) is slightly confusing. The presence of an equal sign immediately before *ω<sub>C</sub>* is unclear and should be revisited. 

Around line 162 in Section 3, a brief discussion of how the embedding *U* is recovered from the kernel matrix *K* would be helpful. Several practical factorization choices exist, and mentioning them would offer valuable context.

Section 4 describes a proximal solver where Algorithm 1, steps 8-9, ensures that *K<sub>r</sub>* remains positive semi-definite (PSD). This PSD check, typically implemented via eigendecomposition (as detailed in Supplementary D.3.2), can be computationally expensive for general dense, symmetric matrices. Is this step a major bottleneck in the algorithm's runtime? Exploring alternative approaches might be worthwhile. For example, optimization could be performed directly on a factorization of *K* (e.g., Cholesky decomposition, *K = LL<sup>T</sup>*). Projection to the feasible set would then involve setting the upper triangular elements of *L* to zero, guaranteeing that *K* remains PSD by construction.

Overall, this paper presents a strong contribution to the field of semi-supervised learning, offering both theoretical advancements and practical algorithmic developments. The clarity of a few points, as suggested above, would further strengthen its impact.
