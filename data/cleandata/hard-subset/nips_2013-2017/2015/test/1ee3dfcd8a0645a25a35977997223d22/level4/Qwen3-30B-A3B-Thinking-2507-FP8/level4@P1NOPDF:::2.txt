

The paper addresses vertex labeling on graphs, extending Ando and Zhang's work which showed that embedding graphs on a unit sphere via orthonormal representations improves generalization error. However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a practical method for selecting an optimal embedding. The authors propose a novel optimization framework that jointly learns the embedding and labels by introducing a regularization term—the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix. This choice is motivated by Theorem 1, which establishes an upper bound on generalization error. Subsequent results derive an asymptotic bound for the proposed method, demonstrating favorable scaling compared to prior work. This bound also yields a labeled sample complexity upper bound. While the optimization can be formulated as a semidefinite program (SDP), the authors note SDP solvers are computationally expensive for large graphs. They instead propose an inexact proximal method, enabling scalability to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results suggest the method outperforms existing approaches.

The reviewer could not grasp the high-level intuition of Theorem 4. The paper states that "in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances." However, "similar" is never defined, and no such assumption appears in the theorem's statement or proof. This omission impedes understanding. The theorem's claim (for random graphs) roughly states that the 0-1 loss error satisfies  
$$\text{error}[\hat{y}] = O\left( \frac{(n^{3/4} + \log(1/\delta))^{1/2}}{m} \right),$$  
where $n$ is the number of vertices, $m$ is the number of labeled examples, and $\delta$ is the failure probability. Interpreting this: if vertices in a large random graph are randomly assigned binary labels, and a vanishing fraction $\omega(n^{3/4})$ of these labels are observed, the algorithm is guaranteed to recover the remaining labels with small error with high probability. Yet without a clear definition of "similar" or its role in the proof, the theorem's validity and scope remain unclear. The reviewer cannot assess this result or its implications.
