This paper presents a compelling analysis of spectral regularization techniques for semi-supervised learning (SSL), demonstrating their impact on error bounds for graph transduction via orthonormal embeddings. The work offers novel theoretical insights and is supported by convincing experimental results. The paper is rich in content, perhaps to a degree that makes it quite dense, but ultimately delivers several important contributions. A particularly insightful aspect is the connection established between large-scale topological properties of the data graph – specifically, the Lovasz number – and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5). This bridge between graph theory, topology, and learning is a significant strength.

The paper begins by outlining the necessary background and clearly articulating its contributions. It then leverages the foundational result of Zhang and Ando (NIPS 2005) to frame SSL as an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem, a perspective maintained throughout the remainder of the work. In section 2 and the accompanying appendix, the authors derive an error bound demonstrating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix effectively improves generalization performance. Section 3 extends this by incorporating a spectral norm regularization term into the Zhang and Ando formulation, and importantly, establishes links between the error convergence rate and the Lovasz number of the data graph. A proximal solver is proposed in section 4, notable for its approximate projection step designed to handle the constraint of intersection within a cone-polytope. Section 5 explores a multi-kernel learning (MKL) extension, and the paper concludes with experimental validation on a variety of datasets (section 6).

I have a few suggestions that could enhance the paper’s clarity. In section 1, following equation (3), explicitly stating that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) transforms the SSL problem into a supervised learning problem would be beneficial. Currently, the sums in equation (3) only index over labelled samples (S), which is somewhat surprising given that equation (1) includes a regularizer applied to both labelled and unlabelled samples, while the loss is computed only on labelled data. 

Regarding section 2, the notation in equation (5) is slightly confusing; the equal sign immediately preceding ω<sub>C</sub> warrants clarification. 

In section 3, around line 162, it would be helpful to briefly mention how the embedding (U) is recovered from the kernel matrix (K). There are several practical choices for this factorization, and acknowledging this would be valuable.

My primary concern lies within section 4, specifically with Algorithm 1, steps 8-9. Ensuring that K<sub>r</sub> is positive semi-definite (PSD) is generally computationally expensive, particularly for general dense square symmetric matrices, and the current implementation relies on an eigendecomposition at each iteration (supplementary D.3.2). This appears to be the most time-consuming step in the algorithm. Exploring alternatives, such as optimizing directly on a factorization of K (e.g., Cholesky decomposition with K=LL<sup>T</sup>), could offer performance improvements. Projection to the feasible set in this case would involve zeroing the upper triangular elements of the matrix, guaranteeing PSD by construction.
