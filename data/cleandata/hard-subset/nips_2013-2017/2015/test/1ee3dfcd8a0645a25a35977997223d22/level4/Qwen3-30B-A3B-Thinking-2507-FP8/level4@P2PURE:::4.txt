

The paper proposes a new framework, SEER, for spectral regularized embedding applied to graph transduction. While the framework's core idea and the benefits of orthonormal embeddings could be articulated more persuasively, this does not constitute a major flaw.  

The authors present an original and elegant algorithm based on IIP to approximately solve the SEER problem, offering a compelling alternative for this complex learning task. However, the experimental results fail to adequately demonstrate the benefits of the SEER framework itself or of the orthonormal representation defined in Equation (3). Additionally, the approximation accuracy of the IIP solution relative to the exact SEER problem is not evaluated. These missing elements are critical to establishing the contribution's significance within the broader context of graph transduction.  

The extension to multiple graph transduction is described at a high level, despite being an interesting direction. The paper lacks sufficient detail, motivation, and intuitive explanation for how and why the method applies to multiple graphs, particularly regarding implementation in such settings. Consequently, the main ideas for the multiple-graph case remain unclear, and the experimental results (e.g., the last column of Table 4, where a single graph outperforms multiple graphs in the noisy case) are difficult to interpret and appear to contradict the stated motivation for handling multiple graphs.  

The paper is occasionally dense and challenging to follow. Its excessive reliance on supplementary material suggests an overambitious scope that undermines accessibility and visibility. The core contribution—SEER for orthonormal embeddings, the IIP-based algorithm, and the experimental results—remains valid but requires clearer presentation and more targeted evaluation.
