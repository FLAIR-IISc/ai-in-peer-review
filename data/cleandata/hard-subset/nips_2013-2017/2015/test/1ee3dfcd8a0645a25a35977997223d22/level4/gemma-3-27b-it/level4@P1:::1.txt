The paper introduces a novel approach to graph transduction by focusing on spectral norm regularization of orthonormal representations. It builds upon recent work demonstrating the benefits of embedding graphs on a unit sphere, addressing the challenge of finding optimal embeddings and efficient algorithms for computation. The authors demonstrate that orthonormal representations are PAC learnable and propose SPORE, a spectral regularized orthonormal embedding, derived from a novel PAC bound. A key contribution is the development of an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method, a scalable algorithm for solving the resulting non-smooth convex optimization problem, and its application to SPORE. The work extends to the multiple graph setting, offering potential benefits in scenarios with heterogeneous data.

The paper begins by outlining the background and establishing its contributions. The authors leverage the Zhang and Ando result, framing the problem as an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning task. Sections 2 and the appendix provide an error bound demonstrating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix improves generalization. Section 3 introduces spectral norm regularization to the Zhang and Ando formulation, linking error convergence to the Lovász number of the data graph. Section 4 proposes the IIP method to address the computational challenges of the constrained optimization problem, and Section 5 presents an MKL extension. Finally, Section 6 validates the approach with experiments on several datasets.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a comprehensive and theoretically grounded contribution to graph transduction. The connection between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (the Lovász number) and the convergence of the embedding estimation (equation 5) is particularly insightful, bridging several fields. The proposed algorithm offers a practical solution for scaling to larger graphs, addressing a limitation of existing methods.

There are a few areas where clarity could be improved. After equation (3), it would be helpful to explicitly state that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) transforms a semi-supervised learning problem into a supervised learning problem focused on labeled samples. This would clarify the indices used in the sums, particularly the absence of \bar{S} in equation (3), given that equation (1) includes a regularizer over unlabeled samples.

In section 2, the notation in equation (5) is slightly ambiguous – the equal sign preceding \omega_C is unconventional and could be clarified.

In section 3, around line 162, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss how the embedding (U) is recovered from the optimal K*. There are various practical factorization choices, and acknowledging this would strengthen the discussion.

In section 4, Algorithm 1, steps 8-9 involve ensuring that K_t is positive semi-definite (PSD), which can be computationally expensive, especially for dense matrices, as it necessitates an eigendecomposition. Investigating alternative strategies, such as optimizing directly on a factorization of K (e.g., Cholesky decomposition with K=LL^T), where projection to the feasible set would involve setting upper triangular elements to zero—guaranteeing PSD by construction—could potentially improve efficiency.

A minor correction: Zhang and Ando's work was published in NIPS 2005, not 2006.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including PAC learnability bounds and connections to graph topological properties (Lovász number).
*   **Novel Approach:** The spectral norm regularization strategy is a novel and insightful approach to graph transduction.
*   **Scalable Algorithm:** The IIP method offers a practical and scalable solution for solving the optimization problem, addressing a limitation of existing SDP-based methods.
*   **Strong Experimental Results:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   **Generalizability:** The extension to multiple graphs further enhances the applicability and significance of the work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The paper is dense and covers a significant amount of material, which might make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with all the underlying concepts.
*   **Computational Cost (Potential):** The PSD projection step in Algorithm 1 could be a bottleneck for very large graphs, although the authors acknowledge this and suggest potential improvements.




This is an interesting and valuable paper demonstrating the impact of spectral regularization on error bounds for graph transduction using orthonormal embeddings. It provides new theoretical insights and convincing experimental results, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
