This paper studies semi-supervised learning (SSL) via the reduction of Zhang and Ando, recasting SSL as a kernel-based supervised problem on the labeled set. Building on this formulation, the authors propose to minimize the spectral norm of the graph kernel to improve generalization, establish error bounds linking convergence to the Lovász number of the data graph, develop a proximal solver that approximately projects onto a cone–polytope intersection, and extend the approach to multiple kernel learning (MKL). Experiments on several datasets demonstrate competitive performance.

Strengths
- The reduction from SSL to a supervised kernel problem is leveraged thoughtfully to introduce spectral-norm regularization on the graph kernel.
- Theoretical contributions are substantial: the error bound in Section 2 and the connection to the Lovász number provide valuable intuition about how large-scale graph structure affects generalization and convergence.
- The proximal algorithm for handling the nontrivial feasible set is carefully designed, and the MKL extension broadens the applicability.
- Empirical results are convincing and align with the theory.

Major comments and questions
1) Clarify the supervised reduction after Eq. (3). Right after Eq. (3), it would help to state explicitly that the SSL objective becomes a supervised learning problem defined solely over labeled samples S, with the unlabeled nodes influencing the solution only through the kernel/regularizer. This would resolve the surprise of seeing sums over S only in Eq. (3), whereas Eq. (1) involves unlabeled samples through the regularizer. A brief remark about how the equivalence handles unlabeled data would improve readability.

2) Notation in Eq. (5). The notation is currently confusing; specifically, the equality sign immediately preceding ω_C is unclear. If the intent is to define ω_C, please use “:=” or reformat the expression to avoid the impression that an equality is being asserted at that point. A short line explaining each symbol in Eq. (5) (especially any dependence on the graph and sample size) would also help.

3) Assumptions behind the generalization bound. In Section 2 (and the appendix), it would be useful to make explicit the assumptions under which minimizing the spectral norm of the kernel matrix leads to improved generalization: e.g., distributional assumptions, stability or Lipschitz properties, and how the graph construction enters the bound. A brief comparison to bounds based on the trace norm or Frobenius norm would also situate the contribution.

4) Recovering U from K (Section 3, around line 162). Please describe how the embedding U is obtained from K in practice. Since multiple factorizations are possible (eigen-decomposition with a rank constraint, Cholesky, or other low-rank factorizations), guidance on the chosen approach, rank selection, and its impact on performance would be helpful. If the bound or the connection to the Lovász number depends on a particular factorization (e.g., orthonormal representations), note this explicitly.

5) Complexity of PSD projection in the proximal solver (Section 4). Algorithm 1 (steps 8–9) involves ensuring K_r is PSD, which requires an eigen-decomposition of a dense symmetric matrix (as per Supplementary D.3.2). This can dominate the runtime, especially at scale.
- Is this indeed the main computational bottleneck? Please quantify the per-iteration cost and wall-clock breakdown.
- Would a factorized parameterization, e.g., K = LL^T or a low-rank U U^T, be feasible? This would enforce PSD by construction and could avoid repeated full eigen-decompositions, at the cost of introducing nonconvexity or complicating the projection onto the cone–polytope intersection. A discussion of this trade-off (including whether truncated eigensolvers or Lanczos methods are used) would be valuable.
- Since the projection onto the feasible set is approximate, please clarify what convergence guarantees hold: does the method ensure feasibility in the limit? Is the objective monotonically decreasing? Under what accuracy of the projection do the theoretical guarantees remain valid?

6) Connection to the Lovász number. The linkage between error convergence and the Lovász number is insightful. A brief, self-contained explanation of the mechanism would help readers from ML who may not be familiar with graph-theoretic parameters: for example, how orthonormal graph representations enter the analysis, why the Lovász number (rather than, say, chromatic number or spectral gap) is the natural quantity here, and whether the bound is tight for classes of graphs encountered in practice.

7) MKL extension (Section 5). The extension is appealing but somewhat terse. Please clarify:
- Whether spectral-norm regularization applies to each base kernel, the combined kernel, or both.
- How kernel weights are regularized (e.g., l1 vs. l2) and whether sparsity in kernel selection emerges.
- Any additional computational cost relative to the single-kernel solver and whether the same convergence properties hold.

8) Experiments. The results are compelling; a few additions would strengthen the empirical section:
- Report runtime and iteration counts, and indicate how much time is spent in PSD projection.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for the spectral-norm regularization weight and the accuracy of the approximate projection.
- If possible, include an ablation comparing full eigen-decomposition versus truncated methods or a factorized parameterization.
- Discuss scalability with n and graph sparsity, and whether performance depends strongly on the graph construction method.

Minor comments
- Section 1, after Eq. (3): add a sentence explicitly stating the reduction to a supervised problem over labeled samples.
- Section 2, Eq. (5): fix the notation near ω_C (replace “=” with “:=” or restructure the expression).
- Section 3 (around line 162): briefly describe the practical recovery of U from K and the choice of factorization.
- Section 4, Algorithm 1 steps 8–9 and Supplement D.3.2: note the computational cost of PSD enforcement and, if applicable, mention any use of truncated eigendecompositions.
- Citation: Zhang and Ando is NIPS 2005, not 2006.
- Proof readability: adding a short proof sketch in the main text for the key bound would aid accessibility.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and ambitious paper that advances our theoretical and algorithmic understanding of spectral regularization for graph-based SSL. The connection to the Lovász number is particularly illuminating, and the proposed solver and empirical validation are solid. With clarifications on the supervised reduction, notation, recovery of embeddings, and the computational aspects of PSD projection—along with minor corrections—the paper would be significantly strengthened. I recommend acceptance after addressing the above points.
