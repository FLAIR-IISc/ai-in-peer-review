

The paper addresses the problem of learning labels on the vertices of a graph. It builds on Ando and Zhang's work, which demonstrated that embedding a graph on a unit sphere via an orthonormal representation improves generalization error (i.e., test set error). However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a concrete method for selecting an optimal embedding. The paper's contribution is a novel optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and learned labels by introducing a regularization term. This term, the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix, is motivated by Theorem 1, which establishes an upper bound on generalization error. Subsequent results provide an asymptotic upper bound on the generalization error of the proposed method, exhibiting favorable scaling compared to prior work. This bound also translates to an upper bound on labeled sample complexity. While the proposed optimization can be formulated as a semidefinite program (SDP), the computational cost of solving SDPs is undesirable. The paper therefore proposes optimizing the problem using an inexact proximal method, which scales to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results indicate the proposed method's superiority over existing approaches.

The reviewer was unable to grasp the high-level meaning of the main result, Theorem 4. The paragraph preceding Theorem 4 states: "In the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances." However, the term *similar* is never defined, and no such assumption is stated in the theorem's assumptions. The reviewer also found no mention or utilization of this assumption in the theorem's proof. Despite this, the theorem's claim can be roughly summarized (for random graphs) as  
\[
\mathcal{E}^{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O\left( \frac{1}{m} \left( n^{3/4} + \log \frac{1}{\delta} \right)^{1/2} \right),
\]  
which, as the reviewer understands, implies that if vertices of a large random graph are \{0,1\}-labeled uniformly at random and a diminishing fraction (e.g., \(\omega(n^{3/4})\)) of these labels are observed, the algorithm is guaranteed with high probability to recover the remaining labels with small error. The reviewer could not understand the main result (as detailed above) and therefore cannot make a judgment on its validity.
