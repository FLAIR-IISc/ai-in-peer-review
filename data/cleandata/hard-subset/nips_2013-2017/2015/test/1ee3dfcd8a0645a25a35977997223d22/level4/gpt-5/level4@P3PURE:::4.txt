This paper introduces a new framework for spectral regularized embedding (SEER) applied to graph transduction, with an emphasis on orthonormal embeddings. While the overall framework is interesting, the motivation for and benefits of enforcing an orthonormal embedding could be articulated more convincingly; however, this is not a major concern.

A key contribution is an elegant, original algorithm that approximately solves the SEER problem using IIP. This appears to be a solid alternative for tackling an otherwise complex learning problem.

The experimental results generally support the proposed approach. That said, it would be important to include results that (i) explicitly demonstrate the benefits of the SEER framework itself—or, more specifically, of the orthonormal representation defined by equation (3)—and (ii) quantify how well the IIP-based solution approximates the SEER objective. These aspects are not visible in the current experiments and are crucial to positioning the contribution in a broader context and clarifying what, precisely, drives the observed gains.

The multiple-graph transduction extension is potentially valuable but is presented at too high a level. More detail, motivation, and intuition are needed to understand how and why the method remains compelling in the multiple-graph setting, and how the algorithm is implemented in practice in this case. As written, it is difficult to grasp the main ideas and to fully appreciate the corresponding experimental results. In particular, the last column of Table 4 (noisy case) is not intuitive: it appears that a single graph outperforms multiple graphs, which seems to contradict the motivation for modeling multiple graphs.

The paper is occasionally dense and not always easy to follow, with frequent references to the supplementary material. This gives the impression that too much is being packed into a single paper, which ultimately hurts accessibility and, potentially, the visibility of the work.

In summary, the paper proposes SEER for orthonormal embeddings in graph transduction and an interesting IIP-based algorithm to solve the associated learning problem, with generally convincing empirical results. The work would be significantly strengthened by: clearer justification for orthonormal embeddings within SEER; ablations or analyses isolating the benefits of the SEER/orthonormal formulation (e.g., equation (3)); explicit evaluation of the approximation accuracy of the IIP solution to SEER; and a more detailed, better motivated, and more interpretable treatment of the multiple-graph extension and its empirical behavior (especially in the noisy scenario of Table 4).
