The paper addresses the problem of learning labels on the vertices of a graph, building on the work of Ando and Zhang. They suggested that embedding the graph on a unit sphere via an orthonormal representation results in better generalization error. However, Ando and Zhang did not provide a concrete solution for choosing an optimal embedding. The current paper's primary contribution is proposing a new optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and the learned labels by introducing a regularization term. This term, the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix, is chosen based on the insight gained from Theorem 1, which states an upper bound on the generalization error.

The paper further presents asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, which have favorable scaling compared to previous results. These upper bounds can also be translated to an upper bound for labeled sample complexity. Although the proposed optimization can be cast as a semi-definite program (SDP), the complexity of solving SDPs is not desirable. Therefore, the paper suggests optimizing this problem using an inexact proximal method, which can scale to graphs with thousands of vertices. Some experimental results indicate the superiority of the proposed method compared to previous methods.

However, the reviewer was unable to understand the main result of the paper, specifically Theorem 4, at a high level. The paragraph preceding Theorem 4 states that "in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels." The paper then assumes that an edge links similar instances, following existing literature [1, 12]. However, there is no definition of what "similar" means, and it is not mentioned in the assumptions of the theorem. The reviewer could not find any mention or use of this assumption in the proof of the theorem either.

Despite this, the claim of the theorem can be roughly stated, in the case of random graphs, as er^{0-1}[\hat{y}]= O( 1/m (n^{3/4} + \log{1/\delta} )^{1/2} ). This means that if vertices of a large random graph are {0,1} labeled at random, and then a diminishing fraction, e.g., \omega{n^{3/4}}, of these labels are observed, the algorithm is guaranteed with high probability to find the remaining labels with small error. Unfortunately, the reviewer was unable to understand the main result and cannot make any judgment on it.
