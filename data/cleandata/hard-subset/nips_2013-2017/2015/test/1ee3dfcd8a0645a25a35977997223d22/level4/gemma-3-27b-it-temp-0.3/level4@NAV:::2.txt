The paper investigates the problem of graph vertex labeling. It extends the work of Ando and Zhang, who demonstrated that embedding the graph onto a unit sphere using an orthonormal representation improves generalization performance. While Ando and Zhang highlighted the benefit of such embeddings, they did not provide a method for optimal embedding selection. This paper contributes a novel optimization framework that simultaneously learns the embedding and vertex labels, incorporating a regularization term derived from theoretical insights. Specifically, the regularization term is based on the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix, motivated by Theorem 1, which establishes an upper bound on generalization error. The paper further presents asymptotic upper bounds on the proposed method’s generalization error, exhibiting favorable scaling properties compared to existing approaches. This bound also translates to an upper bound on the required labeled sample complexity. Recognizing the computational challenges of solving the resulting semidefinite program (SDP), the authors propose an inexact proximal method for optimization, enabling scalability to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results demonstrate the proposed method’s performance advantages over prior techniques.

However, the reviewer encountered difficulty fully understanding the core result, Theorem 4. The text preceding Theorem 4 states that learning labels is impossible in the presence of unlabeled nodes without assumptions, and then invokes an assumption of similarity between connected nodes, citing existing literature. Critically, the definition of “similar” is absent, both in the statement of the theorem’s assumptions and within the theorem’s proof. Despite this ambiguity, the theorem’s claim, in the context of random graphs, can be interpreted as: 
er^{0-1}[\hat{y}]= O( 1/m (n^{3/4} + \log{1/\delta} )^{1/2} ).
This suggests that, given a large randomly labeled graph, observing a fraction of labels proportional to ω(n^{3/4}) guarantees accurate recovery of the remaining labels with high probability.
