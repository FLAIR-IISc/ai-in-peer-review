

The paper addresses vertex label learning on graphs. It extends Ando and Zhang's work, which demonstrated that embedding a graph on a unit sphere via orthonormal representation improves generalization error (test error), but left open the question of optimal embedding selection. The paper's contribution is a novel optimization formulation that jointly learns the embedding and vertex labels through a regularization term. This term, the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix, is motivated by Theorem 1, which provides an upper bound on generalization error. Subsequent results establish an asymptotic upper bound on the generalization error of the proposed method, exhibiting favorable scaling relative to prior work. This bound also yields an upper bound on labeled sample complexity. Although the optimization can be formulated as a semidefinite program (SDP), the paper proposes an inexact proximal method to avoid the high cost of SDP solvers, enabling scalability to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results demonstrate the proposed method's superiority over existing approaches.

However, the reviewer could not grasp the high-level meaning of Theorem 4. The paragraph preceding it states: "With unlabeled nodes and no assumptions on the data, learning labels is impossible. Following [1, 12], we assume edges link similar instances." The term "similar" is undefined, and this assumption is neither formally stated in the theorem's conditions nor referenced in its proof. Despite this, the theorem's claim for random graphs can be roughly interpreted as: the 0-1 error satisfies \( \mathcal{E}_{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O\left( \frac{1}{m} (n^{3/4} + \log \frac{1}{\delta})^{1/2} \right) \). This implies that for a large random graph with {0,1}-labeled vertices, observing a diminishing fraction \( \omega(n^{3/4}) \) of labels guarantees high-probability recovery of the remaining labels with small error. Due to the unresolved ambiguity in the theorem's assumptions and proof, the reviewer cannot comprehend the main result and cannot provide a substantive evaluation.
