

The paper introduces SEER, a novel framework for spectral regularized embedding in graph transduction, centered on orthonormal embeddings. While the core idea of orthonormal embeddings is promising, the paper’s justification for their benefits lacks sufficient depth, though this constitutes a minor concern rather than a fundamental flaw. The proposed IIP algorithm for approximately solving the SEER problem represents a clear original contribution and offers a compelling alternative to existing methods for this complex learning task.  

The experimental evaluation, however, requires significant refinement to fully validate the paper’s claims. Crucially, the results fail to isolate and demonstrate the specific advantages of the SEER framework itself or the orthonormal representation defined in Equation (3). The current experiments primarily compare the full SEER-IIP pipeline against baselines, obscuring whether the framework’s core innovations drive the performance gains. Additionally, the paper omits quantitative analysis of the IIP algorithm’s approximation accuracy relative to the exact SEER solution, which would be essential for positioning the method’s theoretical and practical value. Without this, the contribution risks appearing as merely an effective heuristic rather than a principled advancement.  

The extension to multiple graph transduction is introduced but lacks methodological clarity. The description remains superficial, offering insufficient motivation for why the approach generalizes meaningfully to multiple graphs and how the algorithm operates in this setting. This ambiguity severely hinders the reader’s ability to assess the proposed method’s novelty or utility in multi-graph scenarios. Specifically, the results in Table 4 (noisy case) present a counterintuitive finding: using a single graph outperforms multiple graphs, directly contradicting the paper’s stated motivation for handling multiple graphs. This undermines the proposed extension and necessitates a thorough explanation—e.g., whether the "multiple graph" setting was misconfigured or if the method’s design inherently struggles with noisy graph combinations.  

The manuscript’s readability is also hampered by excessive density and over-reliance on the supplementary material. Key technical details, such as the derivation of the orthonormal constraint or the implementation of the multi-graph extension, are deferred to supplements, weakening the main argument and reducing accessibility. This suggests the authors have attempted to pack too many ideas into a single paper, ultimately diminishing the work’s impact and clarity. A more focused presentation—moving non-essential details to supplements—would significantly improve the paper’s accessibility and impact.  

In summary, the SEER framework and IIP algorithm present a promising direction for graph transduction. However, the current presentation requires substantial revision to: (1) explicitly demonstrate the value of the SEER framework and orthonormal representation beyond the full pipeline; (2) provide clear justification for the multi-graph extension and resolve the contradictory Table 4 results; and (3) streamline the main text to prioritize core contributions over supplementary details. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper’s contribution and clarity.
