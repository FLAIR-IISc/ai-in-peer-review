The paper studies the task of predicting labels on the vertices of a graph. It builds on prior work by Ando and Zhang, which argued that embedding a graph on the unit sphere using an orthonormal representation can improve generalization error, but did not provide a principled method for selecting an optimal embedding. The present work proposes a new optimization formulation that effectively selects the embedding while learning by introducing a regularization term. The regularizer—the spectral norm of the resulting graph kernel matrix—is motivated by Theorem 1, which provides an upper bound on the generalization error. The paper further derives asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed approach with scaling that compares favorably to earlier results, and translates these into labeled sample complexity bounds. Although the formulation can be expressed as an SDP, whose computational cost is undesirable, the paper recommends an inexact proximal method that scales to graphs with thousands of nodes. Empirical results suggest the proposed method outperforms prior techniques.

I was not able to understand the main result, Theorem 4, at a high level. In the paragraph preceding Theorem 4, the paper states that “in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances.” However, “similar” is never defined, and this assumption does not appear among the theorem’s stated hypotheses. I could not find where (or whether) such an assumption is used in the proof either. Despite this, the theorem’s claim can be roughly interpreted, in the case of random graphs, as
er_{0-1}[\hat y] = O( (1/m) (n^{3/4} + log(1/δ))^{1/2} ).
As I understand it, this would imply that if the vertices of a large random graph are labeled in {0,1} at random and then only a vanishing fraction, e.g., ω(n^{3/4}), of these labels are observed, the algorithm is guaranteed with high probability to recover the remaining labels with small error. Because I could not fully grasp the main result (see above), I am unable to make a definitive judgment.
