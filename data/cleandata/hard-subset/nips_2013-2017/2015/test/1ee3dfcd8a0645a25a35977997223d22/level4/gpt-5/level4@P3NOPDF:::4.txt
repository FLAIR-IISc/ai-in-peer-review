Summary
The paper introduces SEER, a spectral regularization framework that learns orthonormal embeddings for graph transduction, and proposes an iterative IIP-based algorithm to approximately solve the resulting optimization. The algorithm is elegant and appears to address a challenging learning problem effectively. Empirical results are generally positive.

Strengths
- Novelty and elegance: The SEER formulation and the IIP-based solver are original and well-motivated from an algorithmic standpoint. The proposed approach seems to offer a practical alternative to more complex exact solvers.
- Empirical promise: The experiments suggest that the method is competitive on standard graph transduction tasks.

Main concerns and suggestions
1) Motivation and benefits of orthonormal embeddings:
- The paper does not convincingly articulate why the orthonormal constraint (e.g., as in Eq. (3)) is crucial in this setting or how it improves transductive performance relative to unconstrained or differently regularized embeddings.
- Suggestion: Provide ablations that isolate the effect of orthonormality (e.g., compare SEER with and without the constraint, or with alternative normalization). Include qualitative or theoretical intuition (stability, de-correlation, avoidance of trivial solutions, improved label propagation) and, if possible, a small synthetic example illustrating the advantage.

2) Approximation quality of the IIP solver:
- While the IIP approach is appealing, the paper does not quantify how closely it approximates the SEER solution or characterize its convergence behavior.
- Suggestions:
  - Report optimality gaps or duality gaps on small instances where an exact or tighter baseline is tractable.
  - Plot objective value vs. iterations and time, and compare to alternative solvers or simplified baselines.
  - Provide sensitivity to initialization and hyperparameters, and discuss convergence guarantees or empirical stability.

3) Multiple-graph transduction section is underdeveloped:
- The formulation and intuition for extending SEER to multiple graphs are presented at a high level, making it hard to assess the contribution and interpret the results.
- Clarifications needed:
  - How are multiple graphs combined (e.g., weighted sum of Laplacians, block structure, or co-regularization)? How are weights learned or set? What is the complexity impact?
  - What inductive biases make SEER suitable for multiple graphs, and under what conditions should multiple graphs help?
  - In Table 4 (noisy case), the last column suggests a single graph may outperform multiple graphs, seemingly contradicting the motivation. Provide analysis: Are the additional graphs too noisy? Does the weighting/regularization fail to downweight them? Show learned weights, sensitivity to noise levels, and statistical significance.
  - Include implementation details so readers can reproduce the multi-graph setting.

4) Positioning and broader context:
- The paper would benefit from a clearer comparison to established graph transduction and spectral embedding methods (e.g., Laplacian eigenmaps-based approaches, label propagation variants, multi-view graph methods). Emphasize when and why SEER is expected to outperform, and provide complexity/runtime comparisons.

5) Clarity and organization:
- The manuscript is dense, with many essential details deferred to the supplement, which hampers accessibility.
- Suggestions:
  - Move core elements into the main text: the SEER objective with clear notation, the rationale for orthonormality, algorithm pseudocode and key steps, complexity analysis, and at least proof sketches of central claims.
  - Add a concise notation table, improve figure/table captions, and include a short “takeaways” paragraph per experiment to guide interpretation.

Experimental recommendations
- Provide ablations isolating the contributions: (i) SEER vs. non-orthonormal variants; (ii) IIP solver vs. alternatives; (iii) single- vs. multi-graph with diagnostics on learned weights/robustness to noise.
- Report statistical significance (mean ± std, paired tests) and convergence curves.
- Include at least one small-scale case with near-exact solutions to benchmark approximation quality.

Overall assessment
This is a promising paper with an original framework and a neat algorithmic solution. The empirical results are encouraging, but the contribution would be significantly strengthened by (i) a clearer justification and empirical validation of the orthonormal embedding, (ii) quantitative evidence for the IIP solver’s approximation quality, and (iii) a more thorough and transparent treatment of the multiple-graph extension. The presentation should also be streamlined to reduce reliance on the supplement.

With these refinements, the paper could make a solid contribution to spectral methods for graph transduction. As it stands, I view it as borderline: technically interesting and potentially impactful, but in need of clearer motivation and more diagnostic evaluation to fully support the claims.
