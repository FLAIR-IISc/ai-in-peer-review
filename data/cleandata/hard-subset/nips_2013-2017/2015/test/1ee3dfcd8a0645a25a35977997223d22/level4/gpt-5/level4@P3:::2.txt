Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies graph transduction via unit-sphere embeddings, focusing on orthonormal representations. Building on prior work that normalizing kernels (or unit-sphere embeddings) improves generalization (e.g., Ando & Zhang; Laplacian-based transduction), the paper presents two main theoretical ingredients: (i) a high-probability generalization bound (Theorem 1) for SVM-based transduction that depends on the spectral norm λ1(K), and (ii) a PAC analysis showing that orthonormal embeddings are learnable with a labeled sample complexity that improves over previous results (e.g., Shivanna & Bhattacharyya). From these insights, the authors propose SPORE, which learns an orthonormal embedding K ∈ K(G) by minimizing the SVM dual objective plus a spectral-norm regularizer βλ1(K) over the elliptope. They connect the spectral norm to the Lovász theta function via lemmas that upper bound λ1(K) by ϑ(Ḡ) and upper bound the SVM objective by ϑ(G), yielding a rate that depends on ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ḡ). The paper also introduces an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm to optimize SPORE without a full SDP, using an approximate projection derived from proximal operators of support functions and FISTA; it achieves O(1/√T) convergence. An MKL-style extension to multiple graphs is proposed, with a bound indicating benefits when at least one view is “good” (small ϑ). Empirical comparisons on UCI and MNIST, and synthetic multi-graph data, show improvements over Laplacian baselines and K-scaling, and scaling to a few thousand nodes.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual link between generalization and spectral norm, tied to ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ḡ).
- Convex formulation (SPORE) that directly regularizes λ1(K) over the elliptope, rather than heuristically normalizing.
- IIP algorithm is practical and scalable; the inexact projection via proximal support functions and FISTA is interesting and broadly applicable to intersections (PSD ∩ affine).
- PAC analysis and labeled sample complexity improve on prior results, with interpretable dependence on graph structure (dense vs. sparse).
- Multiple-graph extension is natural and theoretically supported; experiments corroborate robustness to noisy views.

Weaknesses and key concerns:
- Theorem 4’s assumptions are not stated with sufficient precision. The text says “edges link similar instances,” but “similar” is neither defined nor invoked in the theorem statement or proof sketch. It is unclear whether a stochastic block/similarity graph model is assumed, whether labels are consistent with graph similarity, and whether randomness is over S only or over the graph as well.
- The choice of C and β in Theorem 4 depends on ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ḡ), which are typically unknown and expensive to compute. Practical guidance for setting these parameters without access to θ (or with efficiently computable surrogates) is missing.
- The algorithmic novelty relative to existing inexact proximal/splitting methods (e.g., ADMM, Douglas–Rachford/Dykstra variants with finite-accuracy stopping, primal–dual methods) is not fully positioned. The paper would benefit from a more careful comparison and, if possible, runtime/accuracy comparisons against established first-order methods for SDPs/elliptope problems.
- Some technical details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., proximal operators for the support functions of S+ and the affine set, and the justification of the FISTA scheme on their sum). Clear closed forms or references would improve readability.
- Experimental evaluation lacks runtime plots and memory use; more systematic scaling experiments (n vs time/accuracy) and ablations (effect of β, effect on λ1(K)) would strengthen the empirical case.

Quality:
- The theoretical direction is appealing and the bound-driven design is sensible. However, the main PAC result (Theorem 4) needs a precise statement of the data/graph/label model and randomness. The dependence on ϑ is elegant, and the improvement in labeled sample complexity over prior work is notable, but the practical instantiation of hyperparameters that depend on ϑ requires attention.
- The IIP convergence analysis is plausible and aligns with known O(1/√T) rates for nonsmooth problems with inexact projections; the proposed dual (support-function) approximation criterion is reasonable. More detail on constants and error propagation from inner to outer loops would help practitioners.

Clarity:
- Overall readable, but notation-heavy. Several definitions (e.g., “similarity”) and assumptions should be made explicit in the main text. Tighten cross-references to the supplement and provide brief summaries of key proximal steps. There are minor typos and inconsistent reference formatting.

Originality:
- The spectral-norm-regularized orthonormal embedding for transduction and its PAC analysis appear novel. The IIP method is a thoughtful adaptation of inexact proximal ideas to elliptope constraints; while related to existing splitting methods, the specific approximation criterion and application are interesting.

Significance:
- If the assumptions and parameterization issues are clarified, SPORE could influence how unit-sphere graph embeddings are chosen for transduction. The structural dependence on ϑ is insightful, and the multiple-graph extension is timely.

Questions for the authors:
- Precisely specify the “similarity”/graph-label model in Theorem 4. What is random (S, graph, labels)? Under what conditions on label–edge consistency does the bound hold?
- How should C and β be chosen in practice without ϑ(G), ϑ(Ḡ)? Are there computable surrogates or data-driven heuristics with guarantees?
- Provide the explicit forms (or citations) for prox of the support functions for S+ and the affine set, and for their sum in the FISTA routine. How do you ensure feasibility for the SVM step when using infeasible projections?
- Include runtime/memory comparisons with (a) interior-point SDP, (b) ADMM/Douglas–Rachford/Dykstra, and (c) projected subgradient with standard PSD+masking projections.
- How sensitive is SPORE to β? Report ablations showing λ1(K) vs accuracy.

Pros:
- Theory-guided design; clean link to ϑ.
- Convex, scalable method; practical improvement over Laplacian baselines.
- Multiple-graph extension with supporting theory and experiments.

Cons:
- Ambiguity in key theorem assumptions and parameter selection.
- Limited empirical analysis of computational trade-offs and baselines.
- Some technical steps are opaque without the supplement.

Recommendation:
Borderline accept. The approach is promising and the contributions are potentially impactful, but acceptance should be contingent on clarifying the assumptions and practical parameterization in Theorem 4, and strengthening the algorithmic comparisons.
