The paper addresses the problem of learning labels on the vertices of a graph, building on the work of Ando and Zhang. They suggested that embedding the graph on a unit sphere, using an orthonormal representation, results in better generalization error, i.e., error on the test set. However, Ando and Zhang did not present a concrete solution for choosing an optimal embedding. The current paper's contribution is the proposal of a new optimization formulation that jointly optimizes the embedding and the learned labels by introducing a regularization term. This regularization term, the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix, is chosen based on the insight obtained from Theorem 1 in the paper, which states an upper bound on the generalization error.

The paper further presents asymptotic upper bounds on the generalization error of the proposed method, which have favorable scaling compared to previous results. This upper bound can also be translated into an upper bound for labeled sample complexity. Although the proposed optimization can be cast as a semi-definite program (SDP), the complexity of solving SDPs is not desirable. Therefore, the paper suggests optimizing this problem using an inexact proximal method, which can scale to graphs with thousands of vertices. Some experimental results indicate the superiority of the proposed method compared to previous methods.

However, I had difficulty understanding the main result of the paper, specifically Theorem 4, at a high level. The paragraph preceding Theorem 4 states that "in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances." However, there is no clear definition of what "similar" means, and it is not mentioned in the assumptions of the Theorem. I could not find any mention or use of such an assumption in the proof of the Theorem either.

Despite this, the claim of the theorem can be roughly stated, in the case of random graphs, as er^{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O(1/m (n^{3/4} + log{1/δ})^{1/2}). As far as I understand, this means that if vertices of a large random graph are {0,1} labeled at random and then a diminishing fraction, e.g., ω(n^{3/4}), of these labels are observed, the algorithm is guaranteed with high probability to find the remaining labels with small error. Unfortunately, I was unable to fully understand the main result and therefore cannot make a judgment about its significance or correctness.

To improve the clarity and impact of the paper, I suggest providing a clearer explanation of the assumptions and proof of Theorem 4. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the experimental results, including a comparison with other state-of-the-art methods and an analysis of the proposed method's strengths and limitations.
