

The paper tackles vertex label learning on graphs, extending Ando and Zhang's insight that spherical embeddings via orthonormal representations improve generalization. While Ando and Zhang established this theoretical benefit, they did not provide a concrete method for optimal embedding. This work addresses that gap by proposing a novel joint optimization framework that simultaneously learns graph embeddings and vertex labels, incorporating a spectral norm regularization term on the graph kernel matrix. This term is derived from Theorem 1, which provides an upper bound on generalization error. The paper further establishes asymptotic error bounds for the proposed method, demonstrating favorable scaling compared to prior work, and translates these bounds into label complexity guarantees. Although the optimization can be formulated as a semidefinite program (SDP), the authors note SDPs become computationally intractable for large graphs. To address this, they propose an inexact proximal method, enabling scalability to graphs with thousands of vertices. Preliminary experiments suggest the method outperforms existing approaches.

However, the reviewer finds the core contribution of Theorem 4 fundamentally unclear due to a critical omission in the paper's assumptions. The paragraph preceding Theorem 4 states: "in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances." This introduces the term "similar" without definition, and the paper provides no formal assumption specifying what constitutes "similarity" (e.g., metric, probabilistic, or structural). Crucially, this assumption is absent from Theorem 4's stated conditions, and the reviewer could not identify any use of such an assumption in the theorem's proof. The theorem claims, for random graphs, that the 0-1 error satisfies:
\[
\text{er}^{0-1}[\hat{y}] = O\left( \frac{(n^{3/4} + \log(1/\delta))^{1/2}}{m} \right),
\]
where \(n\) is the number of vertices, \(m\) is the number of observed labels, and \(\delta\) is the confidence parameter. Interpreting this, the theorem asserts that if a large random graph with binary labels is generated uniformly at random, and a diminishing fraction \(\omega(n^{3/4})\) of labels is observed (i.e., \(m = \omega(n^{3/4})\)), the algorithm recovers the remaining labels with small error with high probability. 

The reviewer cannot verify this claim due to the undefined "similarity" assumption. The theorem's conclusion relies on an unverified premise about graph structure (edges connecting "similar" nodes), yet the paper neither defines "similarity" nor incorporates this condition into the theorem's formal statement or proof. Without this, the bound's validity is unclear. For instance, if "similarity" is undefined, the assumption could be vacuous (e.g., trivially satisfied for any graph), rendering the bound meaningless. Alternatively, if it implies a specific data-generating process (e.g., a smooth label function), the paper must state this explicitly to justify the bound. The absence of such specification prevents the reviewer from assessing whether the theorem correctly derives its error guarantee or whether the bound is merely a restatement of the unverified assumption.

The paper's other contributions—such as the spectral norm regularization, the asymptotic error analysis, and the scalable optimization method—appear technically sound and represent a reasonable extension of prior work. The experimental results, though limited, align with the theoretical claims. However, the unresolved ambiguity in Theorem 4 undermines confidence in the primary theoretical advance. A rigorous proof of the error bound requires a precise, stated assumption about graph structure or label distribution that the theorem's claim depends on, which is currently missing. Without this, the reviewer cannot evaluate the significance of the main result, as the stated guarantee may not hold under the paper's implicit assumptions.

In summary, the paper makes a plausible technical contribution to graph label learning with a scalable optimization method and useful error bounds. However, the critical flaw in Theorem 4—relying on an undefined "similarity" assumption without formal integration into the theorem—prevents the reviewer from endorsing the main theoretical claim. The authors must clarify this assumption, define "similar," and demonstrate its role in the theorem's proof to validate the error bound. Until resolved, the core contribution remains unverified.
