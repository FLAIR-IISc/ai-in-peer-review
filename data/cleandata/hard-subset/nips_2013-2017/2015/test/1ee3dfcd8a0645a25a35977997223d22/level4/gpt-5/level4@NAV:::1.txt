The paper opens by presenting the background and listing its contributions. The authors then rely on the Zhang and Ando result to reduce SSL to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning formulation, which underpins the remainder of the work. In Section 2 and the appendix, they derive an error bound indicating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix is a sound strategy for improving generalization. In Section 3, they add a spectral-norm regularizer to the Zhang and Ando setup and establish connections between error convergence and the Lovász number of the data graph. Section 4 introduces a proximal solver whose distinguishing feature is an approximate projection step to handle a cone–polytope intersection constraint. Section 5 presents an MKL extension, and Section 6 reports experiments on several datasets.

Overall, the paper is engaging and rich in content (arguably a bit dense for a single submission), with multiple significant contributions. The link between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (the Lovász number), which reflect its structure in an ambient space or manifold, and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5) is particularly insightful and bridges multiple areas.

In Section 1, after equation (3), I would make explicit that the correspondence between eqs. (3) and (1) implies that a semi-supervised problem becomes a supervised one—i.e., learning proceeds on labeled samples. Otherwise, it can be surprising to see sums indexed only over S in eq. (3), whereas in eq. (1) the regularizer also involves unlabeled points while the loss is defined on labeled data.

In Section 2, the notation in equation (5) is confusing—why is there an equals sign immediately before ω_C?

In Section 3 around line 162, it would help to clarify how the embedding U is recovered from K; there are multiple practical factorization choices.

In Section 4,
Algorithm 1, steps 8–9: enforcing K_r to be PSD is typically quite expensive for general dense symmetric matrices, and here an eigendecomposition is performed at each iteration (Supplementary D.3.2). Is this the main computational bottleneck? Would it be feasible to optimize directly over a factorization of K (e.g., Cholesky with K = LL^T), where projection to the feasible set would amount to zeroing the upper-triangular part, thereby ensuring PSD by construction?

Zhang and Ando is NIPS 2005, not 2006.

This is an interesting paper demonstrating how spectral regularization affects error bounds for graph transduction using orthonormal embeddings, offering new theoretical insights and convincing experimental results.
