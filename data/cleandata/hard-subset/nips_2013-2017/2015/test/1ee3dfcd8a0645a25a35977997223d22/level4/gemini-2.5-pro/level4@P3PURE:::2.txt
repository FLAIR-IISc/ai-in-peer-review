This paper addresses the problem of learning labels for the vertices of a graph, building on the work of Ando and Zhang. Their prior work suggested that embedding a graph on the unit sphere via an orthonormal representation leads to improved generalization error, but it did not provide a concrete method for choosing an optimal embedding. The primary contribution of this work is a novel optimization formulation that jointly learns the embedding and the vertex labels by introducing a regularization term. The choice of regularizer—the spectral norm of the corresponding graph kernel matrix—is motivated by an upper bound on the generalization error stated in Theorem 1.

The paper further establishes an asymptotic upper bound on the generalization error of the proposed method, which scales more favorably than previous results and translates into an upper bound on the labeled sample complexity. While this formulation can be cast as a semidefinite program (SDP), the authors note that the complexity of solving SDPs can be prohibitive. They therefore propose an inexact proximal method for the optimization, which is shown to scale to graphs with thousands of vertices. Experimental results are presented that indicate the superiority of the proposed method over previous approaches.

However, this reviewer was unable to grasp the high-level justification for the paper's main result, Theorem 4. The paragraph preceding the theorem states, "in the presence of unlabeled nodes, without any assumption on the data, it is impossible to learn labels. Following existing literature [1, 12], we assume an edge links similar instances." Yet, the term *similar* is not formally defined, nor does this critical assumption appear to be mentioned in the theorem's formal statement or used in its proof.

Despite this disconnect, the claim of the theorem for random graphs can be summarized as:
er^{0-1}[\hat{y}]= O( 1/m (n^{3/4} + \log{1/\delta} )^{1/2} ).

As this reviewer understands it, this implies that for a large random graph with random {0,1} labels, if a sufficient number of labels (e.g., m = ω(n^{3/4})) are observed, the algorithm is guaranteed with high probability to recover the remaining labels with low error. Given the lack of clarity regarding the assumptions underpinning this main theoretical result, this reviewer cannot confidently assess its validity or significance.
