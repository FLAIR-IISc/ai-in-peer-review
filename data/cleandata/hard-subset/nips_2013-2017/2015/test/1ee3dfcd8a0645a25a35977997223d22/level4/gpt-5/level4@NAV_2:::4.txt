The paper introduces a new framework for spectrally regularized embeddings applied to graph transduction. While the rationale for using orthonormal embeddings and their advantages could be argued more persuasively, this is a minor concern.

The authors then present an appealing and elegant algorithm to approximately solve the SEER objective. This is an original contribution and appears to offer a solid alternative for tackling the underlying complex learning task.

The empirical results generally support the proposed approach. It would strengthen the paper to include experiments that explicitly demonstrate the value of the SEER framework itself, or of the orthonormal representation in (3) in isolation, and to quantify the approximation accuracy of the IIP solver for the SEER problem. These aspects are not evident in the current experiments, and clarifying them would better position the contribution in a broader context.

The multiple-graph transduction extension is described at a fairly high level. Although it is a promising direction, more details, motivation, and intuition are needed to make clear how and why the method is useful for multiple graphs, and how the algorithm is instantiated in that setting. As it stands, the main ideas for the multi-graph case and the interpretation of the corresponding experiments are hard to follow. In particular, the last column of Table 4 (noisy case) is not very intuitive—one single graph seems to outperform the multiple-graph model—which appears to contradict the stated motivation for combining graphs.

Finally, the paper is sometimes difficult to read and dense in places, with too many pointers to the supplementary material. This suggests the manuscript attempts to cover too much in a single paper, which reduces accessibility and likely visibility. Overall, the submission presents a new framework, SEER, for orthonormal embeddings in graph transduction, proposes an IIP-based algorithm to solve the SEER learning problem, and provides reasonably convincing empirical results.
