The paper begins by introducing the background and highlighting the authors' contributions. The authors then leverage the Zhang and Ando result, which demonstrates that semi-supervised learning (SSL) can be reduced to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem, and use this framework for the remainder of the paper. In Section 2 and the appendix, they derive an error bound, showing that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix is an effective approach to improving generalization. In Section 3, they introduce a spectral norm regularization term into the Zhang and Ando formulation and establish connections between error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. Section 4 proposes a proximal solver, notable for its approximation of the projection step to handle the constraint of a cone-polytope intersection. Section 5 extends the work to Multiple Kernel Learning (MKL), and Section 6 concludes with experimental results on several datasets.

Overall, the paper is interesting and contains a wealth of material, with several significant contributions. The link between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (Lovasz number), reflecting its organization in some space or manifold, and the convergence of the embedding estimation (eq. 5) is particularly enlightening, bridging several fields.

In Section 1, after equation 3, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) implies that a semi-supervised learning problem is effectively reduced to a supervised learning problem. This clarification would help to avoid confusion, as the sums in equation (3) only include indices over S, whereas equation (1) includes a regularizer over non-labeled samples in addition to the loss over labeled data.

In Section 2, the notation in equation 5 is confusing, specifically the equal sign preceding \omega_C. A clearer explanation or notation would be helpful.

In Section 3, around line 162, it would be useful to mention how the embedding (U) is recovered from K. There are several practical choices for factorization, and a brief discussion of these options would provide additional clarity.

In Section 4, Algorithm 1, steps 8-9, ensuring that K_r is positive semi-definite (PSD) can be computationally expensive, particularly for general dense square symmetric matrices. The current approach involves an eigendecomposition at each iteration (supplementary D.3.2). It is likely that this step dominates the computational cost of the algorithm. A potential optimization would be to work directly with a factorization of K, such as the Cholesky decomposition (K=LL^T), and project the resulting matrix to the feasible set, which would have all zeros in the upper triangular part, ensuring that K is PSD by construction.

Additionally, the reference to Zhang and Ando should be corrected to NIPS 2005, rather than 2006.

This paper provides an interesting exploration of how spectral regularization impacts error bounds on graph transduction using orthonormal embedding, offering new theoretical insights and convincing experimental results.
