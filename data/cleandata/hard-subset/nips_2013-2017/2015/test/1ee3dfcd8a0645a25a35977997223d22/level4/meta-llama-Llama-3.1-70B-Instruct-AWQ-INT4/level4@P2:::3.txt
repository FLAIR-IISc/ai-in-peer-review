The authors investigate the problem of finding an optimal embedding for graph transduction. They derive a novel method, SPORE (SPectral regularized ORthonormal Embedding), and demonstrate its effectiveness in practice.

The paper begins by reviewing prior work on graph transduction, specifically the use of Laplacian regularization and the corresponding generalization bound (eqn. 2). The authors then discuss their own bound, which is based on the maximum eigenvalue of the kernel matrix (eqn. 4). This bound leads to an alternate optimization criterion, equation (5), which involves minimizing a convex function over an elliptope. Notably, the spectral norm of the kernel matrix is not differentiable, but the optimization criterion is convex, being the sum of convex functions.

The authors propose an inexact proximal method, IIP (Infeasible Inexact Proximal), to solve the SPORE problem. This method is based on approximating the proximal operator for the support function of the feasible set within a given precision. The authors prove that IIP converges to the optimal minimum of a non-smooth convex function with a rate of O(1/√T) in T iterations.

The paper also explores the application of SPORE to multiple graph transduction, where individual views are expressed by a graph. The authors propose an MKL-SPORE formulation, which combines multiple graphs using a convex combination of graph kernels. They demonstrate the effectiveness of MKL-SPORE in improving classification accuracy and robustness to noise.

The experiments section presents results on both real-world and synthetic graphs, illustrating the theoretical observations. The authors compare SPORE with existing methods, such as unnormalized and normalized Laplacian, and K-Scaling, and show significant performance improvements. They also tackle large-scale graph transduction problems and demonstrate the scalability of their algorithm.

Overall, this paper is a solid contribution to the field of graph transduction. The authors provide a novel method, SPORE, which is theoretically justified and demonstrated to be effective in practice. The paper is well-explained, and the authors have made a clear effort to provide intuitive leaps and clever insights throughout the paper. The application of SPORE to multiple graph transduction is also a nice advancement over prior approaches.

Strengths:

* Novel method, SPORE, which is theoretically justified and demonstrated to be effective in practice.
* Inexact proximal method, IIP, which is proven to converge to the optimal minimum of a non-smooth convex function.
* Application of SPORE to multiple graph transduction, which improves classification accuracy and robustness to noise.
* Well-explained paper with intuitive leaps and clever insights.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes a basic understanding of graph transduction and semi-supervised learning, which may make it difficult for non-experts to follow.
* Some of the theoretical results, such as the convergence rate of IIP, may be difficult to understand without a strong background in convex optimization.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel method, SPORE, which is theoretically justified and demonstrated to be effective in practice.

Arguments for rejection:
