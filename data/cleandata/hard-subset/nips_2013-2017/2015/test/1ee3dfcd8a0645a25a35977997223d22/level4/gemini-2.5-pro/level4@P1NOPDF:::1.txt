This is an interesting paper that demonstrates how spectral regularization impacts error bounds in graph transduction using orthonormal embeddings. The work provides new theoretical insights and is supported by convincing experimental results.

The paper begins by outlining the background and its main contributions. The authors then leverage the result from Zhang and Ando, which reduces the semi-supervised learning (SSL) problem to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem. This reduction forms the basis for the remainder of the paper. In Section 2 and the appendix, the authors derive an error bound demonstrating that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix improves generalization. In Section 3, they augment the Zhang and Ando formulation with a spectral norm regularization term and establish a connection between the error convergence and the Lovasz number of the data graph. Section 4 proposes a proximal solver; a key feature of this solver is its ability to handle the projection step approximately, which is necessary to manage the constraint of a cone-polytope intersection. Section 5 extends the approach to Multiple Kernel Learning (MKL), and Section 6 concludes with experimental validation on several datasets.

Overall, the paper is comprehensive, perhaps even dense for a single paper, but contains several important contributions. The connection established between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (specifically, the Lovasz number), which reflect the data's underlying spatial or manifold structure, and the convergence of the embedding estimation (Eq. 5) is particularly insightful. This contribution effectively bridges several distinct fields.

**Specific Comments and Questions:**

1.  In Section 1, following Equation (3), it would be helpful to explicitly state that the correspondence between Eqs. (1) and (3) signifies the transformation of the semi-supervised problem into a purely supervised one, where learning occurs only on the labeled samples. Without this clarification, it is initially surprising to see the sums in Eq. (3) indexed only over the labeled set S (with no mention of the unlabeled set $\bar{S}$), especially since the regularizer in Eq. (1) operates on both labeled and unlabeled samples.

2.  The notation in Equation (5) is confusing. What is the purpose of the equals sign immediately preceding $\omega_C$?

3.  In Section 3 (around line 162), it would be beneficial to mention how the embedding U is recovered from the learned kernel K. Since there are several practical choices for this factorization, clarifying the specific method used would be helpful.

4.  Regarding Algorithm 1 (steps 8-9) in Section 4: Ensuring that $K_r$ is positive semi-definite (PSD) is typically computationally expensive for general dense symmetric matrices. The proposed method performs an eigendecomposition at each iteration (as noted in supplementary D.3.2). Is this step the primary computational bottleneck of the algorithm? As an alternative, would it be feasible to optimize over a factorization of K, such as a Cholesky decomposition ($K=LL^T$)? Projecting onto the feasible set would then involve enforcing a lower triangular structure on L, which would guarantee by construction that K is PSD and could potentially be more efficient.

5.  A minor correction: The Zhang and Ando paper was published in NIPS 2005, not 2006.
