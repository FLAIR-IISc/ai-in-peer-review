The paper begins by outlining the background and contributions, then builds on the Zhang and Ando result to reduce SSL to an equivalent kernel-based supervised learning problem for the remainder of the work. In section 2 and the appendix, the authors derive an error bound showing that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel matrix is an effective way to improve generalization. In section 3, they add a spectral-norm regularization term to the Zhang–Ando formulation and connect error convergence to the Lovász number of the data graph. Section 4 introduces a proximal solver that approximates the projection step to handle the constraint given by the intersection of a cone and a polytope. Section 5 presents an MKL extension, and section 6 reports experimental results on several datasets.

Overall, the paper is interesting, fairly dense for a single submission, and offers several notable contributions. The connection between large-scale topological properties of the data graph (the Lovász number), which reflect its organization in an underlying space or manifold, and the convergence of the embedding estimate (eq. 5) is particularly illuminating and bridges multiple areas.

In section 1, after equation (3), it would help to state explicitly that the correspondence between eqs. (3) and (1) implies that the semi-supervised learning problem becomes a supervised one, so learning is carried out on labeled samples. Otherwise, it is a bit surprising to see sums indexed only over S (with no \bar{S}) in eq. (3), whereas in eq. (1) the regularizer also involves the unlabeled samples while the loss is on labeled data.

In section 2, the notation in equation (5) is confusing—why is there an equals sign immediately before omega_C?

In section 3, around line 162, please mention how the embedding U is recovered from K. There are several practical factorization choices.

In section 4, Algorithm 1 steps 8–9: ensuring that K_r is PSD is typically quite expensive for general dense symmetric matrices, and here an eigendecomposition is performed at every iteration (supplementary D.3.2). Is this where the algorithm spends most of its time? Would it be feasible to optimize directly over a factorization of K (e.g., a Cholesky factorization K = L L^T), so that projection onto the feasible set amounts to zeroing the upper-triangular part, thereby ensuring K remains PSD by construction?

Zhang and Ando is NIPS 2005, not 2006.

This is an interesting paper that demonstrates how spectral regularization influences error bounds for graph transduction using orthonormal embedding, providing new theoretical insights alongside convincing experimental results.
