- Addresses graph transduction using orthonormal representations and proposes SPORE, a spectral-norm regularization technique.
- Builds on unit-sphere graph embeddings and aims to provide theoretical guarantees plus an efficient embedding algorithm.
- Key contributions: PAC-learnability analysis for orthonormal representations, the SPORE formulation, the IIP algorithm for non-smooth convex optimization, and extensions to multiple graphs.
- Theoretical contributions are substantial: PAC bound and convergence analysis for IIP are strong results.
- Connection to the Lovász θ function provides a useful theoretical framework.
- IIP algorithm appears significantly more scalable than SDP-based approaches.
- Extension to multiple graphs broadens applicability to more complex datasets.
- Experiments on UCI and MNIST show SPORE outperforms existing methods and include analysis of graph density vs sample complexity.
- Presentation is dense and would benefit from more intuitive explanations, especially in the PAC-learnability section.
- The practical conditions under which the PAC bound holds are not clearly elucidated.
- The intuition for why spectral-norm regularization improves generalization is underexplained.
- Experiments should be expanded to include comparisons with recent GNN methods and larger graphs beyond 2000 nodes.
- Hyperparameter tuning (choices and effects of β and C) is under-detailed and needs further study.
- Notation is overwhelming; a comprehensive notation table and simpler sentences would improve readability.
- Section flow could be improved by explicitly linking theoretical results (e.g., Theorem 1) to the SPORE formulation.
- Originality: novel combination of spectral-norm regularization, orthonormal embeddings, and a new algorithm (IIP); PAC result is notable.
- Significance: offers a scalable, theoretically grounded approach to graph transduction with practical impact.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, scalable algorithm, promising empirical performance, and potential impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: dense presentation limits accessibility, limited comparison to state-of-the-art GNNs, missing hyperparameter details, and clarity issues.
- Recommendation: accept pending minor revisions to improve clarity and expand experimental evaluation.
