Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies graph transduction via unit-sphere embeddings, focusing on orthonormal representations. It introduces a PAC-style generalization bound that avoids VC-dimension (infinite here) by leveraging Rademacher analysis and a spectral-norm term, connecting the complexity to the Lovász θ function. This motivates SPORE, a spectral-norm regularized optimization over the elliptope. To scale beyond SDP solvers, the authors propose an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) method that uses approximate projections via proximal operators of support functions, implementable with FISTA, with O(1/√T) convergence. Extensions to multiple graphs (MKL-SPORE) are given. Empirically, SPORE outperforms Laplacian baselines and a K-scaling method on UCI/MNIST graphs, and MKL-SPORE improves robustness on synthetic multi-graph data. The work builds on NIPS results on Laplacian regularization, orthonormal representations, Lovász θ, and inexact proximal algorithms.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically interesting: the high-probability bound (Theorem 1), θ-based lemmas, and sample complexity scaling are coherent, and the IIP analysis for nonsmooth objectives is a useful contribution. However, several proofs are deferred to the supplement; crucially, the parameter choices in Theorem 4 depend on θ(G) and θ(Ḡ), which are SDP-hard to compute, raising practicality concerns. The optimization method is plausible, but empirical support focuses on accuracy; no wall-time/memory curves, ablations (e.g., IIP vs. Dykstra or vs. exact projection on small graphs), or sensitivity to β, C are reported.

Clarity: The paper is dense but generally readable. Some notational choices and algorithmic details (stopping rules for inner FISTA, feasibility guarantees when only PSD is enforced at the end of each outer step, and how L, R, R̂ are estimated in practice) should be clarified. References lack years and some citations are incomplete.

Originality: The link from orthonormal embeddings to a spectral-norm bound tied to Lovász θ, and the inexact-prox approach for projection onto the elliptope via support-function proximals are novel and of independent interest. The MKL extension is natural but useful.

Significance: The theory meaningfully advances understanding of sample complexity for graph SSL and suggests a principled regularizer. The optimization approach could impact a broader class of elliptope-constrained problems. The empirical section, while promising, is not yet commensurate with the theoretical ambition.

Pros for acceptance:
- New PAC bound and improved sample complexity tied to θ; clear conceptual advance.
- Convex SPORE formulation with spectral-norm regularization is well-motivated.
- IIP framework for approximate projections with convergence guarantees.
- Empirical gains over common Laplacian baselines; scalable to thousands of nodes.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical use of θ-dependent parameters is unclear; no surrogate or data-driven estimator proposed.
- Limited experimental scope: few baselines, no runtime/memory analyses, no ablations, and no real multi-graph datasets.
- Some algorithmic details and proofs are only in the supplement; clarity issues and incomplete references.

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide a θ-free parameter selection strategy (e.g., bounds via degree/expander estimates) or a data-driven proxy.
- Add runtime and scaling plots, compare to SDP on small graphs, and include alternative projection methods as baselines.
- Expand baselines (label propagation, harmonic functions, recent SSL/graph kernels), and test on real multi-graph tasks.
- Clarify feasibility in Algorithm 1, inner-loop stopping criteria, and sensitivity to hyperparameters; include code or pseudocode details.
- Improve references and bring key proof ideas into the main text.

Overall, a solid theoretical and algorithmic contribution with promising empirical signals; acceptance would be strengthened by broader and deeper experimental validation.
