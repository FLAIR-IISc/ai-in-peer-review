Summary
The paper studies graph transduction via orthonormal representations (unit-sphere embeddings constrained by graph non-edges) and argues for spectral norm regularization to improve generalization. The main theoretical contribution is a high-probability PAC-style bound for the transductive SVM objective under orthonormal embeddings that avoids VC-dimension and depends on the spectral norm of the kernel K. The authors connect the maximal spectral norm over feasible K ∈ K(G) to the Lovász theta of the complement graph, and upper-bound the empirical objective by a term involving θ(G), leading to a data-dependent bound with error O((1/m (√(n θ(G)) + log(1/δ)))1/2) and labeled sample complexity m/n = Ω((θ(G)/n)1/2), improving on prior Ω((θ/n)1/3). Motivated by the bound, they propose SPORE: minimizing the transductive SVM dual plus βλ1(K) over the elliptope K(G). As scalable optimization, they introduce an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) method that uses an approximate projection obtained from the proximal operator of the support function and FISTA over the intersection S+ ∩ P(G), with O(1/√T) convergence. They extend the formulation to multiple graphs via an MKL-style convex combination and a max spectral penalty, maintaining similar bounds (with θ reduced to the best graph). Experiments on UCI and MNIST show consistent gains over several Laplacian-based baselines and synthetic multi-graph experiments illustrate robustness.

Strengths
- Conceptual: Clear and interesting connection between generalization in graph transduction and Lovász θ via spectral norm control on unit-sphere embeddings. The move from VC to Rademacher-style analysis is appropriate and yields sharper, graph-structural sample complexity.
- Formulation: SPORE is a convex, interpretable objective tying empirical fit to a spectral capacity term.
- Optimization: IIP is a generally applicable approach when projections are hard but prox of support functions is accessible; the dual-criteria approximation and use over intersections via FISTA are neat and practically relevant beyond this problem.
- Extensions: Multiple-graph setting is addressed with a principled objective and bound; the “one good view suffices” insight is valuable.
- Empirics: Consistent accuracy gains over Laplacian kernels and K-Scaling on several datasets; evidence of scalability to a few thousand nodes.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Bound usage vs practice: The bound requires specific choices C = (θ(G)/(m√θ(Ḡ)))1/2 and β = θ(G)/θ(Ḡ), but θ(G) and θ(Ḡ) are typically unavailable at scale. In practice you cross-validate; please discuss how data-driven tuning interacts with the theory, or provide upper bounds (e.g., via fast approximations) that preserve guarantees.
- Clarity/rigor of lemmas: Lemma 2 (maxK λ1(K) = θ(Ḡ)) and Lemma 3 (minK ωC ≤ θ(G)/2) are central. They are plausible given the SDP characterizations of θ and the equivalence K=UᵀU, but please provide precise references or a more detailed proof sketch in the main text. Is Lemma 2 new or a corollary of known θ formulations?
- Optimization details: IIP uses infeasible iterates; Algorithm 1 then projects onto S+ before the SVM step. Please clarify:
  - Whether the SVM subproblem is ever solved with indefinite K; if so, how is it handled?
  - The complexity of each outer iteration (dominant eigenvector for λ1, SVM solve over m labels, and √T FISTA inner steps). A practical runtime breakdown and memory footprint would be useful.
  - Sensitivity to the chosen inner-outer iteration schedule (S = √T). Are there adaptive schemes?
- Empirical evaluation:
  - Baselines: Important transductive baselines (e.g., label propagation and harmonic functions, Zhou et al. 2004; Zhu et al. 2003) are not reported. On small graphs, an SDP-based θ-style baseline (or an orthonormal-embedding variant without spectral penalty) would help isolate the effect of spectral regularization.
  - Graph construction: Results hinge on the constructed similarity graphs (RBF thresholded by mean; cosine threshold 0.4). Please include sensitivity to the threshold and σ, and a stronger graph construction (kNN) commonly used in SSL.
  - Scale: Claims of scalability are promising; however, experiments cap at n=2000. Please add larger runs (e.g., n=10k–50k), with wall-clock times and convergence plots, and compare to projected subgradient with exact projections when feasible.
  - Ablations: Report the effect of removing the λ1 penalty (β=0) and of replacing λ1 with other spectral surrogates (e.g., trace/nuclear norm) to demonstrate the specific role of spectral norm.
- Minor notation and consistency issues: A few formulas have spacing/parentheses that make exponents ambiguous (e.g., sample complexity line for m̃). Please proof-read the references (many entries lack years/pages) and unify notation (e.g., use consistent symbols for sets and projections).

Clarity
- Overall organization is reasonable, but Section 4 mixes general IIP development with SPORE-specific steps; adding a short roadmap and a concise list of ingredients needed to apply IIP (what proxes are needed for S+ and P(G), their closed forms) would help reproducibility.
- Please explicitly state the proximal steps for σS+ and σP(G) used in FISTA, and the Lipschitz constant of the smooth term in (16).
- Briefly justify the step-size choices and constants L, R, R̂ for SPORE in the main text.

Originality
- The combination of: (i) a θ-based transductive bound for orthonormal embeddings, (ii) a spectral-norm-regularized elliptope optimization for graph transduction, and (iii) an inexact-projection method tailored via prox of support functions for an intersection, appears novel and appropriate for NeurIPS. The analysis for nonsmooth f in the inexact proximal setting is a worthwhile addition to the literature.

Significance
- If strengthened empirically and clarified theoretically, SPORE could influence graph SSL by providing a principled alternative to Laplacian regularization and a scalable route to elliptope problems. The multi-graph extension is timely. The IIP framework may be reused in other constrained kernel learning or SDP-like problems.

Questions for the authors
- Can θ(G) or θ(Ḡ) be efficiently upper-bounded to set C, β without SDP? Any empirical proxy correlating with θ that works well?
- How does SPORE perform with kNN graphs vs global thresholding? Sensitivity to graph sparsity?
- What is the computational bottleneck (top eigenvector vs SVM vs inner FISTA)? Can warm-starts be exploited across iterations?
- Is Lemma 3 tight? Any families where ωC ≈ θ(G)/2 is achieved?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and interesting theory linking transductive generalization to Lovász θ and spectral norm.
- Convex formulation with a generally useful optimization approach (IIP).
- Extension to multiple graphs with a clear bound and demonstrated robustness.
- Promising empirical gains.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing comparisons to standard graph SSL baselines and limited scale/sensitivity analysis.
- Practical use of theory requires θ-based parameter choices; guidance for practitioners is limited.
- Some clarity and referencing issues in the presentation.

Overall, I find the theoretical ideas compelling and the optimization contribution potentially impactful; with strengthened experiments and clarified proofs/algorithms, this would be a strong contribution.
