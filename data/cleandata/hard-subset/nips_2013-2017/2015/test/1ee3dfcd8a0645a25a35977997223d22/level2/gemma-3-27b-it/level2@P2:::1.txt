This paper addresses the problem of graph transduction, focusing on orthonormal representations of graphs and proposing a novel algorithm, SPORE, for computing these embeddings. The authors make a significant contribution by providing a PAC learning bound for orthonormal representations and developing an efficient, scalable algorithm (IIP) based on inexact proximal methods to solve the resulting optimization problem. The extension to multiple graphs is also a valuable addition.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on graph transduction, citing relevant works on Laplacian regularization [1, 11, 17, 20], PAC learning [1, 2], and orthonormal representations [13, 16]. The authors acknowledge the limitations of previous approaches and clearly articulate how their work advances the state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The PAC learning bound for orthonormal representations (Theorem 1) is a notable result. It provides a theoretical foundation for using these embeddings and connects them to the Lovász θ function, offering insights into their generalization properties.
* **Algorithmic Innovation:** The IIP algorithm is a clever approach to tackle the computational challenges associated with SPORE. By using an inexact proximal method, the authors bypass the need for solving computationally expensive SDPs, enabling scalability to larger graphs. The convergence rate analysis (O(1/√T)) is also a strength.
* **Scalability:** Demonstrating that the algorithm scales to 1000s of vertices is a significant practical advantage. This is supported by the experimental results on larger datasets.
* **Multiple Graph Extension:** Extending the framework to multiple graphs (Section 5) is a logical and valuable extension, opening up new applications in domains with multi-view data.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results (Section 6) on both synthetic and real-world datasets show that SPORE outperforms existing methods, validating the theoretical and algorithmic contributions. The ablation studies, showing the improvement with multiple graphs, are also compelling.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear presentation of the theoretical results and algorithmic details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of parameters (C, β, and parameters for IIP like R and R̂) can significantly impact performance. While the authors mention cross-validation, a more detailed discussion on parameter tuning and sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. The derivation of R and R̂ seems a bit hand-wavy.
* **Assumptions:** The PAC analysis relies on certain assumptions about the graph structure.  It would be helpful to discuss the limitations of these assumptions and how the results might generalize to other graph families. For instance, are the theoretical guarantees weakened significantly for graphs with very high degrees?
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to Un-Lap, N-Lap, and KS is useful, a comparison to more recent and sophisticated graph embedding techniques (e.g., Graph Convolutional Networks or other spectral methods) would strengthen the empirical evaluation.  It’s unclear how competitive SPORE is with these newer methods.
* **Technical Details in Supplementary Material:** Some critical details of the IIP algorithm and proofs are relegated to the supplementary material.  While understandable, this makes it harder for readers to fully grasp the technical intricacies. Important theorems like 6 should be fully presented.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** While the algorithm scales well, a more detailed computational cost analysis comparing IIP to SDP solvers beyond the vertex count would be helpful, specifically addressing the dependence on edge count and other graph properties.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical results appear well-justified, and the algorithm is carefully designed and analyzed. The experimental evaluation provides convincing evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written, although some sections (particularly those involving the IIP algorithm and its convergence analysis) require careful reading.

**Originality:** The work is original in several respects: the PAC learning bound for orthonormal representations, the IIP algorithm for solving SPORE, and the extension to multiple graphs. The combination of theoretical and algorithmic contributions is particularly noteworthy.

**Significance:** The results are significant because they provide a solid theoretical foundation for using orthonormal representations in graph transduction, and they offer a practical and scalable algorithm for computing these embeddings. The potential applications in various domains, including web page classification and bioinformatics, make this work highly relevant. The extension to multiple graphs further enhances the significance of the contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical contributions (PAC bound).
* Novel and scalable algorithm (IIP).
* Promising experimental results demonstrating superior performance.
* Clear and well-organized presentation.
* Extension to multiple graphs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter sensitivity could be a practical concern.
* Limited comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods.
* Some technical details buried in the supplementary material.
* Assumption regarding the type of graphs could limit the generality of results.

**Overall Assessment:**  This is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the field of graph transduction. The theoretical and algorithmic innovations are well-justified and supported by compelling experimental results. Despite some minor weaknesses, the overall quality of the work is excellent.

**Recommendation:** **Strong Accept.**  I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2015. The authors have addressed an important problem with a novel and effective approach. Addressing the minor concerns raised above (particularly regarding parameter sensitivity and comparison to more recent methods) could further strengthen the paper.
