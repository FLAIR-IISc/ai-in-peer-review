

This paper proposes SPORE (SPectral norm regularized ORthonormal Embedding), a novel approach for graph transduction that addresses the problem of choosing optimal orthonormal graph embeddings. The authors show that orthonormal representations are PAC learnable and derive a generalization bound (Theorem 1) that suggests spectral norm regularization could improve generalization. They then propose SPORE as a convex optimization problem over an elliptope, and develop an efficient Inexact Infeasible Proximal (IIP) method to solve it, which scales to thousands of vertices compared to standard SDP approaches.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical contribution is significant, particularly the improved sample complexity estimate (Theorem 4) that improves over [16] from Ω((ϑ/n)^1/3) to Ω((ϑ/n)^1/2). This is a meaningful theoretical advance in understanding graph transduction.

2. The IIP algorithm is well-motivated and provides a practical solution to the scalability problem of SDP-based approaches. The convergence analysis (Theorem 5-6) is rigorous and the connection to the proximal operator for the support function is novel.

3. The experimental results (Tables 1-4) consistently demonstrate SPORE's superiority over existing methods across multiple datasets, with particularly impressive results on large-scale problems (Table 2).

4. The extension to multiple graph transduction (MKL-SPORE) is well-motivated and provides practical value for multi-view learning scenarios.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper claims to improve over [16] in sample complexity, but the proof of Theorem 4 relies on ϑ(G)ϑ(Ḡ) = n, which is only true for perfect graphs according to Lovász's work. The paper doesn't clarify whether this assumption holds for the graph families they consider (e.g., power-law graphs), which weakens the theoretical claim.

2. While the paper states that "the set of normalized kernels is quite large," it doesn't provide guidance on how to choose the optimal kernel within this set. The SPORE approach appears to automatically select a kernel via spectral norm regularization, but the paper doesn't explain why this particular regularization leads to better embeddings.

3. The experimental section lacks deeper analysis of why SPORE outperforms baselines. For example, visualizations of the embeddings or ablation studies showing the contribution of different components would strengthen the empirical claims.

4. The paper cites [1] as arguing for unit sphere embeddings, but [1] actually suggests normalizing the diagonal entries of the kernel to 1, which is equivalent to the unit sphere embedding. This is a minor point, but the paper should clarify the relationship between [1] and their work.

5. The computational complexity claim (IIP scales to 1000s of vertices) is supported by Table 2, but the paper doesn't provide precise runtime comparisons with SDP solvers for graphs of similar size.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with strong theoretical contributions and practical significance. The theoretical improvements over [16] are meaningful, and the IIP algorithm provides a valuable solution to the scalability problem in graph transduction. The main weakness is the unverified assumption about the Lovász θ function for non-perfect graphs, which should be clarified. The paper would benefit from more detailed experimental analysis and a clearer explanation of how SPORE selects optimal embeddings.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear notation and logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to algorithm design. The supplementary material appears to contain rigorous proofs, which is a strength.

This work makes a significant contribution to the field of graph-based machine learning. The theoretical analysis is sound, the algorithmic approach is novel and practical, and the experimental results consistently demonstrate the method's superiority. While the claim about the Lovász θ function needs clarification, the paper is strong overall and would be a valuable contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
