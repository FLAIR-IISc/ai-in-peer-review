

This paper addresses graph transduction by proposing SPORE (SPectral regularized ORthonormal Embedding), a novel approach that leverages orthonormal representations for graph embeddings. The authors make several key contributions: (1) they establish that orthonormal representations are PAC learnable, deriving a generalization bound that relates to the Lovász θ function rather than VC dimension; (2) they formulate SPORE as a spectral norm regularized optimization problem over an elliptope; and (3) they propose the Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm for efficient computation, which scales to thousands of vertices compared to standard SDP methods.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with well-structured theoretical analysis. Theorem 1 provides a high-probability generalization bound, and Theorem 4 derives a sample complexity estimate that improves upon prior work [16] for graphs with ϑ(G) = o(n). The IIP algorithm (Theorem 6) is theoretically sound, with a convergence rate of O(1/√T), and the authors provide a detailed proof of its application to SPORE. The experimental results (Tables 1-4) convincingly show SPORE outperforms existing methods on both small and large graphs, with the IIP algorithm scaling to 2000+ vertices where SDPs fail.

The paper's novelty lies in connecting PAC learning theory to orthonormal embeddings and introducing the IIP method for non-smooth convex optimization over elliptopes. This extends prior work [16] by providing a theoretically grounded approach to choosing optimal embeddings, rather than just demonstrating consistency. The multiple graph extension (MKL-SPORE) further demonstrates practical significance.

The paper is well-written with clear organization, though the supplementary material could provide more detail on the implementation of IIP. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that their approach is limited to unit-sphere embeddings and that the multiple graph extension assumes convex combinations of kernels.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution with strong theoretical foundations, practical scalability, and significant improvements over existing methods. The paper advances the state of the art in graph transduction by bridging PAC learning theory with efficient optimization algorithms. It is well-suited for NIPS, offering both theoretical insights and practical solutions to an important problem in graph-based machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
