This paper addresses the problem of graph transduction, focusing on embeddings on the unit sphere, specifically orthonormal representations. The authors aim to provide theoretical guarantees and a scalable algorithm for computing these embeddings. They derive a PAC-based generalization bound that doesn't rely on VC dimension, instead leveraging the Lovász θ function. This leads to a spectral norm regularized formulation (SPORE) and an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method for solving it, which they demonstrate scales better than standard SDP approaches. The work also extends to the multiple graph setting.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing an important problem in graph learning. The connection between orthonormal representations and the Lovász θ function is a valuable contribution, providing a new theoretical lens for understanding graph embeddings. The derivation of the PAC bound and the resulting SPORE formulation are logically sound. The proposed IIP algorithm is a clever approach to tackling the computational challenges of solving the SPORE optimization problem, particularly the avoidance of full SDP solvers. The extension to multiple graphs is a natural and useful generalization.

Strengths:
* **Theoretical Novelty:** The PAC bound based on the Lovász θ function is a significant theoretical contribution.
* **Algorithmic Innovation:** The IIP algorithm offers a practical solution for computing SPORE that scales to larger graphs.
* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The problem is well-defined, and the motivation is clear and related to existing work.
* **Extension to Multiple Graphs:**  The extension to the multi-graph setting increases the applicability of the work.
* **Empirical Validation:** Experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of SPORE.

Weaknesses:
* **Clarity of IIP Convergence:** While the authors state that IIP converges at a rate of O(1/√T), the explanation of *why* it works with an inexact projection is somewhat hand-wavy. More intuition regarding the convergence guarantee, especially given the inherent difficulty of dealing with infeasible projections, would be helpful. The conditions for the approximation criteria (11) and its impact on convergence could be clarified.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments section would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of parameter selection (C, β). How sensitive are the results to changes in these parameters? A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Comparison Depth:** The comparisons to other methods (Un-Lap, N-Lap, KS) are somewhat limited.  More detailed comparison including runtimes (even just qualitatively) would be beneficial. How does the IIP method compare to other first-order methods in terms of runtime on these datasets?
* **Connection to Existing Work on Proximal Methods:**  The discussion of related work on inexact proximal methods feels a little cursory.  While the authors cite relevant papers, they don't fully contextualize their contribution within the broader landscape of proximal algorithms.  Specifically highlighting the novel aspects of *their* approach relative to existing methods would be beneficial.
* **Notational Density:** The paper utilizes a lot of notation, which can make it challenging to follow for readers not deeply familiar with the field. While the notation is defined, breaking down complex equations into smaller steps or providing more intuitive explanations could improve readability.

Arguments for acceptance:
* Novel theoretical contribution regarding PAC learnability of orthonormal representations.
* A practical and scalable algorithm (IIP) for computing SPORE.
* Strong empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* Significant improvement over existing methods on benchmark datasets.
* Extension to a relevant and challenging problem – multiple graph transduction.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Clarity of the IIP convergence analysis could be improved.
* Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity and runtime comparisons.
* Notational density may hinder readability for some readers.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of graph transduction. The theoretical results are novel, the proposed algorithm is practical, and the empirical evaluation is convincing. While there are a few areas where the presentation could be improved, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues related to the IIP convergence and expand the empirical analysis with runtime comparisons and a parameter sensitivity study.
