This paper tackles graph transduction by jointly learning vertex labels and a unit-sphere orthonormal embedding. It builds on Ando and Zhang’s insight that normalizing to the unit sphere aids generalization but lacked a principled way to select the embedding. The authors propose SPORE: a convex program over the elliptope that minimizes a margin-based dual objective plus a spectral-norm regularizer on the graph kernel. The choice of spectral norm is motivated by a high-probability bound (Theorem 1) that places λ1(K) in the generalization term. They further derive asymptotic upper bounds on transductive error (Theorem 4), translating into labeled sample complexity guarantees with favorable scaling in graph families where Lovász theta is o(n). Optimization-wise, while SPORE can be an SDP, the authors introduce an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) method with O(1/√T) guarantees that scales to graphs with thousands of nodes. Experiments on UCI, MNIST, and synthetic multi-graph settings show consistent gains over Laplacian baselines and K-scaling.

Quality: The optimization contribution is sound and practically useful; the inexact proximal analysis complements existing inexact proximal-gradient literature and is well-motivated for elliptopes. Empirically, the comparisons are favorable and the method appears scalable. However, I could not understand the main theory (Theorem 4) at a high level. The paper states learning is impossible without assumptions and informally appeals to “edges link similar instances,” yet “similar” is not defined nor used in the theorem’s assumptions or proof. The proof sketch does not indicate where this assumption enters. As stated, the bound er^{0-1} = O(((√(nθ(G)) + log(1/δ))/m)^{1/2}) implies, e.g., for Erdős–Rényi graphs where θ(G)=Θ(√n), one needs roughly m = Ω(n^{3/4}) labels to drive the error below a constant—this needs to be clearly articulated, contrasted with prior bounds, and aligned with the stated sample-complexity corollaries.

Clarity: The manuscript is generally well organized, but crucial assumptions and intuitions behind Theorem 4 are missing. Some algorithmic details (e.g., projection step notation) are confusing.

Originality: The coupling of orthonormal embeddings with spectral-norm regularization and the IIP solver is novel relative to prior graph SSL work.

Significance: Potentially impactful if the theoretical claims are clarified and validated; optimization and empirical results are solid.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled formulation connecting generalization to spectral norm
- Scalable first-order solver with analysis
- Strong empirical performance and multi-graph extension

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing and unused “similarity” assumption in main theorem
- Insufficient high-level intuition and clarity for Theorem 4
- Minor clarity issues in algorithm description and parameter choices

Verdict: I am unable to judge the main theoretical claim due to the above gaps; leaning reject, with the suggestion to precisely formalize and integrate the similarity assumption and expand the proof-level intuition for Theorem 4.
