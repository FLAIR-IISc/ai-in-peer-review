This paper studies graph transduction through orthonormal representations (unit-sphere embeddings) and proposes a spectral-norm-regularized formulation, SPORE, motivated by a new PAC-style generalization bound. Building on the kernel-based reduction of graph transduction (e.g., Ando & Zhang) to a supervised problem on labeled nodes only, the authors derive a high-probability error bound showing that small empirical hinge loss plus small spectral norm of the kernel K improves generalization. They connect the maximal spectral norm over the feasible set K(G) to the Lovász theta of the complement graph, relate the optimal embedding choice to θ, and show improved labeled sample complexity O((θ/n)1/2) compared to previous work on orthonormal embeddings. Algorithmically, they introduce an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) method that allows projected subgradient on the elliptope via an approximate projection defined through the proximal operator of the support function; an inner FISTA loop yields an O(1/√T) rate overall. They extend the formulation to multiple graphs (MKL-SPORE) with an accompanying bound showing improvement when at least one graph is “good.” Experiments on UCI/MNIST and synthetic multi-graph settings demonstrate consistent gains over Laplacian baselines and K-scaling, and scalability to a few thousand nodes.

Relation to prior work: The reduction to a kernelized supervised objective (Ando & Zhang, NIPS 2005) is used in a new way here to justify spectral norm regularization. The analysis explicitly avoids VC dimension (infinite here) and instead leverages Rademacher complexity and spectral properties, connecting orthonormal representations to Lovász θ (Lovász’ classic results; SDP connections via Goemans and subsequent work). Compared to Shivanna & Bhattacharyya (NIPS) on the consistency of orthonormal embeddings, the present bounds are sharper and provide labeled sample complexity. On optimization, the inexact proximal perspective complements Schmidt et al. (NIPS) and Villa et al. (SIAM J Opt) by handling a non-smooth objective and a projection that is only approximately available. The multiple graphs extension follows the MKL spirit common in semi-supervised and multi-view graph learning.

Quality: The theoretical development is interesting and largely technically sound. Theorem 1 provides a clear spectral-norm–based bound; Lemma 2 (maxK∈K(G) λ1(K) = θ(Ḡ)) and Lemma 3 link the optimization to θ nicely; Theorem 4 yields a graph-dependent rate and improved labeled sample complexity. One caveat is that setting C and β in Theorem 4 requires θ(G) and θ(Ḡ), which themselves require SDPs. In practice the authors cross-validate, but it would be useful to discuss practical proxies (e.g., spectral bounds) or sensitivity. The IIP analysis hinges on an approximate proximal condition for σX; the convergence result is clearly stated and the interplay between outer steps and inner FISTA iterations is well argued. Empirically, SPORE shows solid accuracy gains; additional timing breakdowns (e.g., cost per eigendecomposition and SVM call) would strengthen the scalability claims.

Clarity: The paper is well organized but dense. A few improvements would help:
- Explicitly emphasize early that, via (3), the learning is supervised on the labeled set S, with unlabeled nodes only affecting the feasible set K ∈ K(G). This will prevent confusion about sums over S vs V.
- Section 2/3 notation: equation (5) mixes ΨC,β and g(K) with an inline equals sign before ωC; please tidy the display to avoid ambiguity.
- Clarify how one recovers an embedding U from K in practice (eigendecomposition vs low-rank factorization), and whether the downstream use requires explicit U or only K.
- Algorithm 1, steps 8–9: ensuring Kt is PSD typically entails an eigendecomposition; please discuss whether this dominates runtime, and whether optimizing over a factorization K = LL^T (with appropriate handling of the zero constraints) or using low-rank updates/Nyström approximations could reduce cost.

Originality: High. The spectral-norm regularization for orthonormal representations, the θ-based generalization and sample complexity analysis, and the IIP framework that composes proximal operators of support functions over cone–polytope intersections are novel in combination.

Significance: Strong. The θ connection gives structural insight into when graph transduction is easier (denser graphs/lower θ). The algorithm provides a viable alternative to SDPs, scaling to thousands of nodes, and the MKL extension with a bound is practically relevant for multi-view/noisy graphs.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical motivation for spectral norm regularization tied to Lovász θ; improved labeled sample complexity over prior orthonormal embedding analyses.
- Convex formulation (SPORE) with a principled, scalable first-order solver and convergence guarantees.
- MKL extension with corresponding generalization bound; robustness to noisy graphs demonstrated.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over strong Laplacian baselines and K-scaling.

Cons:
- Practical selection of C and β relies on θ, which is not available at scale; guidance on proxies is missing.
- PSD enforcement likely requires repeated eigendecompositions; computational trade-offs and bottlenecks are not quantified.
- The paper packs many contributions; some proofs relegated to the supplement could benefit from more intuition in the main text.
- Comparisons could include additional graph SSL baselines (e.g., label propagation and local/global consistency) and, on small graphs, an SDP-based baseline to assess optimality gaps.

Specific questions/suggestions:
- Hyperparameters: Can you provide heuristics linking β to observable spectral quantities (e.g., bounds using degrees or λmax of A or L) that approximate θ(Ḡ)?
- Optimization: Would a low-rank parameterization (K = LL^T with diagonal and sparsity constraints enforced via penalties or augmented Lagrangian) avoid full EVD while maintaining convergence properties?
- Projection details: ProjA(K) = K − proxσA(K) still seems to require spectral operations. Is there a way to reuse eigenvectors across iterations or to employ partial EVD to cut cost?
- Constants: The bounds use L = nC^2, R = n, R̂ = n^2.5 for step sizes; these appear loose. Can data-dependent or adaptive step sizes improve convergence practically?
- Multiple graphs: For MKL-SPORE, do you learn η jointly with K(k)? Any sparsity on η to select a small subset of graphs, and how sensitive is performance to M?
- Experiments: Please report wall-clock times, number of outer/inner iterations, and eigendecomposition cost. On small graphs, comparing against SDP would help validate the optimizer’s quality.
- Minor corrections: Ando & Zhang is NIPS 2005, not 2006. Fix the equation display around (5) and minor typos in the text.

Overall evaluation: This is an interesting, substantive paper that advances both theory and algorithms for graph transduction via orthonormal embeddings and spectral regularization. With clarifications on hyperparameter selection and computational bottlenecks, it would make a strong contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
