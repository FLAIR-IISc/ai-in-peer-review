- Proposes SPORE: spectral-norm regularized orthonormal embedding for graph transduction and an IIP algorithm for efficient computation.
- Motivation for focusing specifically on orthonormal representations is under-justified; preference over other unit-sphere embeddings is not convincingly established.
- Theoretical contribution: PAC learnability and a generalization bound (Theorem 1) provide useful justification for the regularizer.
- Practical role of the Lovász θ function in selecting optimal embeddings is not fully explored.
- IIP algorithm is a strong technical contribution, using inexact proximal projection to scale beyond SDP solvers.
- Convergence analysis (Theorems 5 & 6) is rigorous and gives a solid theoretical foundation for IIP.
- Extension to multiple-graph transduction is natural and potentially impactful but under-detailed.
- Experimental comparisons to Un-Lap, N-Lap, and KS are informative but lack critical ablations.
- Unclear how much performance gain comes from spectral regularization versus simply enforcing orthonormal embeddings (Equation 3).
- Missing sensitivity analysis for the regularization parameter β.
- No quantitative evaluation of the IIP approximation error or how epsilon affects final solution quality.
- Relationship between the inexact proximal parameter and solution accuracy is not investigated.
- Multiple-graph implementation details and MKL-style rationale are high-level and insufficiently explained.
- Counterintuitive empirical result: single-graph SPORE outperforms MKL-SPORE on noisy graphs, contradicting expected multi-view benefits.
- Paper is dense, relies heavily on supplementary material, and uses cumbersome notation, hurting clarity and accessibility.
- Strengths: novel regularization framework, scalable IIP algorithm, rigorous convergence theory, multi-graph extension, and empirical improvements on datasets.
- Weaknesses: weak justification for orthonormal focus, lack of ablations and error analysis, high-level multi-graph treatment, counterintuitive results, and poor clarity.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded method and a practical scalability solution with promising empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient justification of core approach, missing ablations/error analyses, and unclear presentation.
- Overall recommendation: Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing justification, ablations, approximation quantification, and clarity.
