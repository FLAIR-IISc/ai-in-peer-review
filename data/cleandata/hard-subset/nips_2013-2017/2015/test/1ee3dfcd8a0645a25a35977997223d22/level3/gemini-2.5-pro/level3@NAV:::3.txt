This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal orthonormal graph embeddings for graph transduction. The authors begin by reviewing prior work, particularly the Laplacian-based generalization bound in [1], which motivates embedding graphs on a unit sphere but offers little guidance on selecting the best embedding. The central contribution of this work is the derivation of a new PAC-based generalization bound that depends on the maximum eigenvalue of the kernel matrix, λ1(K). This novel theoretical insight motivates the primary methodological proposal: a spectral norm regularized optimization criterion called SPORE (SPectral regularized ORthonormal Embedding). To solve the resulting non-smooth convex optimization problem, the authors develop an efficient and scalable Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm, which is a significant contribution on its own. The framework is also extended to a multiple-kernel learning setting (MKL-SPORE).

The paper has several significant strengths across all evaluation criteria.

**Quality and Originality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work, from theory to practical implementation. The primary innovation lies in shifting the focus from trace-based regularizers to a spectral norm-based one, a clever and well-justified leap. The derivation of the new generalization bound and the subsequent SPORE formulation are novel and directly address an open question in the field. Furthermore, tackling the challenging non-differentiable optimization with a custom inexact proximal method, rather than relying on non-scalable SDP solvers, is a strong algorithmic contribution.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to have a considerable impact. The SPORE framework provides a principled method for learning graph embeddings that demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art, as shown by significant performance improvements in the experiments. The proposed IIP algorithm makes the approach practical for large-scale problems where previous methods would fail. The MKL-SPORE extension further increases the work's applicability to multi-view learning scenarios.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established, the theoretical derivations are well-explained, and the proposed algorithm is detailed sufficiently. The connection between the theory and the final algorithm is logical and easy to follow.

A minor weakness is that a deeper analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity for C and β would be beneficial for practitioners. However, this does not detract from the paper's core contributions.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel theoretical analysis, a practical and efficient algorithm, and compelling empirical validation. It represents a solid and impactful contribution to the field of graph-based learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
