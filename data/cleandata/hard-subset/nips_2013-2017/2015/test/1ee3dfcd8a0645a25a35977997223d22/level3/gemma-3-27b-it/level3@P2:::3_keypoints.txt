- Addresses graph transduction by optimizing orthonormal graph embeddings to improve generalization.
- Identifies limitations of Laplacian regularization and lack of guidance for kernel/embedding selection.
- Proposes a novel spectral norm regularization criterion (SPORE) for embedding selection.
- Formulates SPORE as minimizing a convex function over the elliptope, well-motivated and effective.
- Introduces an efficient Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm that approximates projection for scalability.
- Provides convergence analysis for IIP with a proven convergence rate (Theorem 5).
- Establishes PAC learnability and derives new generalization bounds (Theorem 1) tied to the Lovász θ function and kernel spectral norm.
- Gives an improved sample complexity estimate (Theorem 4), strengthening theoretical claims.
- Extends the framework to multiple graph transduction (MKL-SPORE), broadening applicability.
- Demonstrates empirical effectiveness on synthetic and real-world datasets with performance gains over baselines.
- Detailed algorithm descriptions and supplementary convergence proofs are provided and commendable.
- Presentation is generally clear and rigorous; mathematical notation is standard and well-defined.
- Suggests minor notation consistency improvements (e.g., consistently use K for kernel matrices).
- Novelty lies in combining spectral-norm regularization, PAC analysis, and an inexact proximal method in this context.
- Significance is substantial due to practical scalability, theoretical grounding, and improved large-scale performance.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, innovative algorithm, empirical gains, clear motivation, and multi-graph extension.
- Arguments against acceptance: experiments could include more diverse datasets and baselines.
- Arguments against acceptance: sensitivity to parameter settings (e.g., RBF kernel choices) needs more study.
- Arguments against acceptance: practical convergence behavior of IIP could be further explored experimentally.
