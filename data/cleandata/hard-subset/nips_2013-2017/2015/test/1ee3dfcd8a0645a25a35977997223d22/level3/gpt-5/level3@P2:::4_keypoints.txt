- Paper studies graph transduction with unit-sphere/orthonormal embeddings and proposes a spectral-regularized convex formulation (SPORE) and solver (IIP).
- Main contributions: PAC-style generalization bound depending on spectral norm and Lovász θ; SPORE convex objective minimizing SVM dual + spectral norm over the elliptope; an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) solver using prox-of-support approximations (FISTA inner loop); extension and bound for multiple graphs (MKL-style).
- Situates work relative to Laplacian regularization, prior orthonormal-consistency results, and SDP/θ literature; claims novelty in combining λ1(K)-tied PAC bound, spectral-regularized elliptope formulation, and scalable first-order solver.
- Theorem 1 gives a high-probability bound linking generalization to empirical hinge loss and √λ1(K); Lemmas connect λ1(K) to θ(Ḡ) and yield improved sample complexity in Theorem 4.
- Multi-graph bound inherits a dependence on min_k θ(G(k)), with a natural MKL-style extension.
- IIP solver is technically sound: uses prox of support function to approximate projections onto intersections, with FISTA inner loops and O(1/√T) convergence accounting for inexactness.
- Empirically, SPORE outperforms Laplacian baselines on UCI/MNIST graphs and synthetic multi-graph data and scales to a few thousand nodes where SDPs struggle.
- Modeling justification is weak: no ablation isolating effects of orthonormal constraint, spectral penalty βλ1(K), and the SVM dual objective choice.
- Approximation gap is unassessed: no empirical comparison of IIP solutions to exact SPORE SDP on small graphs to verify solver fidelity.
- Multiple-graph extension lacks algorithmic detail and intuition (how η is optimized, joint vs alternating updates, stopping criteria); empirics show cases where multiple-graph underperforms best single graph.
- Exposition is dense and relies heavily on the supplement; notation and some statements (e.g., SDP complexity) are imprecise.
- Suggested clarity fixes: move key proof sketches and proximal derivations into main text or give more intuition.
- Suggested algorithmic clarity: provide self-contained Algorithm 1 with explicit prox expressions, feasibility residuals, and inner/outer stopping criteria.
- Recommend unifying terminology (SPORE vs SEER) and clarifying assumptions behind the graph similarity model used in PAC analysis.
- Originality: novel combination of λ1(K)-θ PAC bound, convex spectral-regularized elliptope objective, and inexact proximal method using prox-of-support—an original application.
- Significance: could provide a principled alternative to Laplacian regularization and scalable substitute for SDP θ-relaxations; IIP may generalize to other convex problems with costly projections.
- Main pros: solid theoretical links to spectral norm and θ, convex formulation with clear inductive bias, efficient well-analyzed solver, scalability beyond SDP, and a plausible multi-graph theory.
- Main cons: insufficient ablations for modeling choices and β’s role, no empirical validation of solver-optimality gap, multi-graph underperformance in noisy settings, and dense exposition with some imprecise claims.
- Key requested ablations: (a) ωC only over K(G) (no spectral penalty), (b) spectral penalty only with fixed K, (c) alternative penalties (trace/nuclear), (d) relax diagonal/orthonormal constraints.
- Requested empirical checks: solve SPORE as an SDP on n ≤ 300 to report objective gaps, feasibility residuals, and test-accuracy comparison to IIP.
- Requested MKL details: clarify how η is optimized, whether α is re-solved after η updates, provide pseudocode and complexity.
- Requested diagnostics: report constraint violations (Mii=1, off-edge zeros), PSD residuals, and convergence plots across iterations.
- Theorem clarity: clarify assumptions behind Theorem 4 and whether constants depend on graph sparsity beyond θ.
- Question on regularization: explain why MKL-SPORE underperforms best single graph in noisy experiments and whether max_k λ1(K(k)) regularizer is overly conservative.
- Recommendation: borderline reject — interesting theoretical and algorithmic contributions but needing stronger empirical validation, clearer motivation of modeling choices, solver-gap analysis, and improved exposition.
