

This paper makes a significant contribution by reducing graph transduction to a kernel-based supervised learning problem, building upon Zhang and Ando's work. The main theoretical insight is an error bound showing that minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel improves generalization, leading to the novel SPORE formulation. The authors link error convergence to the Lovász number of the data graph and propose an efficient infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) algorithm for solving the resulting optimization problem, which scales to thousands of vertices compared to standard SDP approaches. The paper also extends to multiple graph transduction (MKL-SPORE) with convincing experimental results.

The paper excels in theoretical depth and practical applicability. The connection between spectral regularization and the Lovász number provides new insights into graph transduction, addressing a gap in prior work. The IIP algorithm is well-motivated and demonstrates impressive scalability, making the theoretical contributions practically relevant. The experimental results across multiple datasets validate the theoretical claims effectively.

However, the paper's density presents challenges. It packs several substantial contributions (theoretical analysis, new algorithm, MKL extension) into a single paper, making it difficult to follow. The notation in Section 2 (equation 5) is confusing with an unnecessary equals sign before ω_C. The paper should explicitly clarify that the correspondence between equations (3) and (1) makes SSL a supervised problem (learning on labeled samples only), to avoid confusion about sums over S only. Section 3 needs to explain how the embedding U is recovered from K and discuss practical factorization choices. Additionally, the computational cost of ensuring PSD via eigendecomposition in Algorithm 1 (steps 8-9) should be addressed—optimizing over a factorization like K=LL^T might be more efficient.

Minor corrections are needed: Zhang and Ando is NIPS 2005, not 2006. 

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a substantive theoretical contribution with practical implications for graph transduction. The strengths (novel theoretical insights, scalable algorithm, convincing experiments) significantly outweigh the weaknesses (density, some notational issues), making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
