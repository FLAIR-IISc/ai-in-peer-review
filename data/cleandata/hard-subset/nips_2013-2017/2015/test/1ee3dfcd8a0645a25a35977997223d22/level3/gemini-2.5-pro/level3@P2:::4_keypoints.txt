- Paper addresses graph transduction by learning optimal graph embeddings for label propagation, building on unit-sphere/orthonormal representations.

- Main contributions: a PAC generalization bound linking kernel spectral norm to performance, the SPORE formulation combining SVM loss with spectral-norm regularization over the elliptope, and the IIP first-order solver for scalable optimization.

- Theorem 1 provides theoretical motivation for spectral-norm regularization, moving beyond prior unit-sphere prescriptions without concrete objectives.

- SPORE (Eq. 5) operationalizes the theory by directly regularizing the kernel spectral norm while optimizing an SVM hinge loss over orthonormal representations.

- The Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) algorithm is a significant algorithmic contribution, offering a principled way to optimize non-smooth convex objectives with approximate/infeasible projections and an O(1/√T) convergence guarantee.

- Empirically, SPORE+IIP consistently outperforms standard graph transduction baselines and scales to graphs (~2000 nodes) where SDPs are intractable.

- Paper presentation is dense and hard to read: key algorithmic details and most proofs are deferred to the supplement, hindering assessment and reproducibility.

- The motivation for restricting to orthonormal representations versus other kernel families is insufficiently justified in the main text.

- Experimental analysis is incomplete: missing ablation comparing SPORE with β=0 (no spectral regularizer) to isolate the effect of spectral norm regularization.

- Experimental analysis also lacks evaluation of IIP approximation quality versus exact SDP solutions on small datasets (objective gap and classification accuracy).

- Multiple-graph (MKL) extension is underdeveloped, with weak motivation/description and troubling empirical results (MKL-SPORE worse than best single graph in the "Noisy" setting).

- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretically grounded framework (SPORE), original impactful optimization algorithm (IIP), and strong scalable empirical results.

- Arguments against acceptance: poor organization/clarity, missing crucial experiments (ablation and approximation analysis), and underdeveloped/misleading multi-graph results.

- Overall assessment: interesting framework and important optimization advance, but significant clarity and experimental validation gaps must be addressed for the paper to be convincing.
