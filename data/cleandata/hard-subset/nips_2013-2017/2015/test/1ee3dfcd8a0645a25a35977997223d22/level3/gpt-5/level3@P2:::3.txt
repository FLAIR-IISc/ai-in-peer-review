Summary
This paper studies graph transduction with unit-sphere graph embeddings and focuses on orthonormal representations. It first derives a high-probability generalization bound that depends on the spectral norm of the learned kernel matrix rather than the VC dimension (which is infinite here), thereby connecting generalization to Lovász’s theta function via the spectrum of feasible embeddings. Motivated by this bound, the paper proposes SPORE, a spectral-norm-regularized convex formulation over the elliptope that searches for the best orthonormal embedding jointly with the transductive predictor. A key technical contribution is an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) method that uses approximate projections obtained via proximal steps on the support function, computed efficiently with FISTA over the intersection of the PSD cone and an affine set. The method converges at O(1/√T) and scales to graphs with thousands of nodes, markedly beyond what SDP-based approaches can handle. The analysis extends to multiple graphs (MKL-SPORE) and shows improved error bounds when at least one view is “good.” Empirically, SPORE consistently improves over Laplacian-based kernels and K-scaling on UCI and MNIST graphs; MKL-SPORE outperforms single-graph and naïve multi-graph baselines on synthetic multi-view settings.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Ando & Zhang-type bounds for graph transduction by moving from diagonal normalization to a spectral-norm-driven bound and by focusing on orthonormal representations (Lovász θ connections).
- Improves on the consistency and sample complexity analysis of orthonormal embeddings recently studied at NIPS (e.g., Shivanna & Bhattacharyya).
- Methodologically, complements recent inexact proximal methods (e.g., Schmidt et al., Villa et al.) by addressing the nonsmooth case with approximate projections via the support function’s proximal operator.
- Connects to classical SDP-based θ computations but avoids intractability through a first-order method on the elliptope.

Strengths
- Technical: Clear, principled generalization bound (Theorem 1) tied to λ1(K), leading to a concrete, convex learning objective (SPORE). Elegant use of Lovász θ to derive graph-structure-dependent rates and labeled sample complexity.
- Optimization: IIP is well-motivated; the proximal-on-support approach for intersections of convex sets is neat and broadly applicable; convergence and complexity are established.
- Theory to practice: The spectral criterion is supported by empirical improvements over Laplacian and K-scaling baselines; scalability is demonstrated to thousands of nodes.
- Multi-graph extension: MKL-SPORE has a clean formulation, a bound that improves with the best view, and robustness to noisy graphs.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical tuning vs. theory: The bound-driven parameter choices involve θ(G) and θ(Ḡ), which are not tractable to compute. In practice, C and β are cross-validated, weakening the direct practical utility of the theoretical prescriptions. A discussion of proxies or data-driven surrogates for θ would help.
- Baselines and breadth: While Laplacian kernels and K-scaling are relevant, additional transductive baselines (e.g., Zhou et al.’s label propagation/harmonic functions, graph transduction games) and, where feasible, an SDP baseline on small graphs would strengthen the empirical case.
- Empirical reporting: More details on runtime, convergence curves (outer/inner iterations), sensitivity to graph construction (RBF bandwidth, thresholding), and variance across trials would improve reproducibility and credibility.
- Clarity of the IIP steps: The proximal operators for the support functions of the PSD cone and the affine constraint set are relegated to the supplement. Including closed forms or concise summaries in the main text would aid readability. Also, the choice and estimation of L, R, R̂ seem conservative; adaptive stepsizes or line-search variants could be discussed.
- Feasibility handling: The algorithm maintains PSD feasibility for SVM calls via an extra projection but may violate the affine constraints during iterations. While the convergence of f(K) is guaranteed, it would be useful to state conditions under which iterates approach feasibility in both sets or to report feasibility gaps empirically.
- Experimental scope: Real multi-view datasets (e.g., bioinformatics or web spam where multi-graph structure is native) would better substantiate the MKL-SPORE claims.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Strong. Claims are supported by solid theory (bounds, convergence) and experiments that, while limited in breadth, are consistent with the theory. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; minor gaps in practical parameterization remain.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized but dense. The main ideas are explained clearly; however, several key implementation details are only in the supplement. Minor typos/notation issues exist.
- Originality: High. The shift to spectral-norm-based generalization with explicit θ(G) links in transduction, combined with an inexact proximal method tailored to elliptope constraints, is novel and insightful.
- Significance: High. Advances the theory and practice of graph transduction with scalable optimization over orthonormal embeddings; the approach is likely to influence follow-up work on graph SSL and optimization over matrix intersection sets.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean bridge from generalization theory to a practical, scalable algorithm.
- New bounds yielding improved labeled sample complexity with interpretable graph-dependent rates.
- Optimization contribution (IIP) of independent interest for nonsmooth problems with hard projections.
- Consistent empirical gains and scalability beyond SDP limits.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited breadth of empirical baselines and datasets (especially for multi-graph).
- Dependence on cross-validation for parameters that theory ties to intractable quantities; lack of practical proxies.
- Several key algorithmic details and proofs are deferred to the supplement, which may hinder reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to label propagation/harmonic functions and, where feasible, θ-SDP on small graphs.
- Report wall-clock times, convergence curves, and feasibility gaps; include sensitivity to graph construction parameters.
- Provide practical heuristics to approximate θ(G), θ(Ḡ) (e.g., via spectral surrogates) to set C and β without exhaustive tuning.
- Summarize the proximal operators for σA and σB in the main text and discuss adaptive stepsize choices.

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper offers a well-justified and novel route to learning orthonormal embeddings for graph transduction with meaningful theoretical guarantees and a scalable optimization scheme. While the empirical section could be broader and some practical aspects clarified, the contribution is solid and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.
