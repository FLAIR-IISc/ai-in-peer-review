Summary and relation to prior work: The paper reduces semi-supervised learning (SSL) on graphs to a supervised, kernel-based problem via the Zhang–Ando formulation, and then develops new theory and algorithms from this reduction. The main theoretical result is a high-probability error bound showing that controlling the spectral norm of the graph kernel improves generalization; the bound connects the maximum feasible spectral norm to the Lovász theta of the complement graph, yielding graph-structure–dependent rates and improved labeled sample complexity. Algorithmically, the paper proposes SPORE, adding a spectral-norm regularizer to the Zhang–Ando objective, and introduces an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) method that handles cone–polytope intersections by an approximate projection computed with FISTA, with O(1/√T) convergence. An MKL extension is provided, with theory showing benefits when at least one view is “good.” Experiments on UCI/MNIST and synthetic multi-graph settings show consistent gains over Laplacian baselines. This builds on and goes beyond prior NIPS work on spectral kernel design for SSL (Zhang & Ando, NIPS 2005), Laplacian regularization, and orthonormal representations (Shivanna & Bhattacharyya, NIPS), while contributing new generalization bounds and optimization techniques for non-smooth problems over elliptopes.

Quality: The theory is careful and technically sound, with clear links from the PAC bound to spectral regularization and Lovász θ. The optimization contribution is novel for this setting and the convergence proof is credible. Experiments are convincing, though ablations (e.g., effect of β and inner-iteration budgets) would strengthen claims.

Clarity: The paper is dense; consider streamlining. Please explicitly state early that the correspondence between (3) and (1) makes SSL a supervised problem over labeled samples only (unlabeled nodes affect K), to prevent confusion about sums over S. Equation (5): the notation around “= ωC(·)” is confusing—why an equals sign just before ωC? Section 3: clarify how an embedding U is recovered from K in practice (eigendecomposition vs. Cholesky/low-rank factorization; rank selection). Section 4 / Alg. 1 (steps 8–9): ensuring Kt is PSD via eigendecomposition can be expensive—please quantify if this is the dominant cost and discuss feasibility of optimizing over a factorization K = LL^T to avoid repeated decompositions.

Originality: High. The spectral-norm generalization bound for graph transduction and its connection to Lovász θ, plus the inexact proximal scheme for cone–polytope intersections, are new.

Significance: Theoretical insights are substantial and practically actionable; the solver scales beyond SDP and MKL-SPORE is useful.

Minor: Zhang and Ando is NIPS 2005, not 2006.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong and novel theory tying spectral regularization to Lovász θ with improved sample complexity
- New optimization method with convergence guarantees and practical scalability
- MKL extension with theory and robust empirical gains
- Clear advancement over prior SSL kernel design work

Cons for acceptance:
- Paper is dense; some notation and algorithmic details need clarification
- Computational costs of PSD projections should be better analyzed; factored alternatives not explored experimentally
- More ablations/sensitivity analyses would improve empirical rigor
