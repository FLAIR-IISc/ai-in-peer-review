

This paper addresses a fundamental problem in graph transduction: the choice of optimal orthonormal graph embeddings and efficient computation of such embeddings. The authors build on [1] which showed that embedding graphs on a unit sphere improves generalization, but left open the question of optimal embedding choice. Previous work [16] established the statistical consistency of orthonormal embeddings but did not provide a method for selecting the optimal embedding or an efficient algorithm to compute it.

The main contribution is a new generalization bound (Theorem 1) that relates prediction error to the spectral norm of the kernel matrix (equation 4), rather than relying on VC dimension which is infinite for their function class. This bound motivates a spectral norm regularized formulation (SPORE, equation 5) for graph transduction, which minimizes a convex function over an elliptope. The authors then propose an Inexact Infeasible Proximal (IIP) algorithm to solve this non-smooth convex optimization problem, which avoids the O(n^6) complexity of standard SDP solvers. The IIP algorithm achieves O(1/√T) convergence rate and scales to graphs with 1000s of vertices, while SDP-based methods fail beyond a few hundred vertices. The authors further extend their approach to multiple graphs (MKL-SPORE) with improved generalization bounds.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical novelty: The paper offers a fresh perspective by using spectral norm regularization instead of the standard Laplacian-based approaches. The PAC analysis is particularly strong, avoiding the VC dimension pitfalls of prior work [1, 2].
2. Algorithmic innovation: The IIP method is a clever solution to the challenging problem of projecting onto the elliptope. The authors carefully design the approximation criteria and prove convergence guarantees.
3. Practical impact: The experiments demonstrate clear improvements over baselines (Table 1-2), with SPORE significantly outperforming existing methods. The scalability to large graphs (2000+ nodes) is a major practical contribution.
4. Theoretical depth: The connection between the Lovász θ function and graph properties (Lemma 2, 3) provides valuable insights into why certain graphs are easier to transduce than others.
5. Clear exposition: The paper is well-organized with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to algorithmic development to experiments.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not sufficiently address how to choose the regularization parameter β in practice. While they use cross-validation in experiments, a more principled approach would strengthen the contribution.
2. The theoretical analysis assumes the graph is known, but in real-world applications, the graph structure itself may be uncertain or noisy. The paper could better address this limitation.
3. The experiments, while well-conducted, focus primarily on binary classification. It would be valuable to see results on more complex tasks like multi-class or regression problems.

The paper makes significant contributions to the field of graph transduction. The new generalization bound (equation 4) is a major theoretical advance that directly addresses a limitation in prior work [1]. The SPORE formulation (equation 5) is both theoretically sound and practically useful, while the IIP algorithm solves a key computational bottleneck that has limited previous approaches.

Compared to related NIPS work, this paper stands out for its novel approach to the embedding problem. While [16] established the statistical consistency of orthonormal embeddings, this work provides the first practical algorithm for choosing the optimal embedding. The connection to the Lovász θ function (Lemma 2) is particularly elegant and provides deeper theoretical understanding than previous work.

The significance of this work is substantial. Graph transduction is a fundamental problem in machine learning with applications in social network analysis, bioinformatics, and computer vision. The scalability of SPORE to large graphs opens up new applications that were previously computationally intractable. The MKL-SPORE extension further enhances practical utility by handling multiple graph views.

The paper is of high technical quality, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by comprehensive experiments. The writing is clear and well-structured, making it accessible to a broad NIPS audience. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the problem space, carefully situating their work within the relevant literature.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation with a novel generalization bound; practical algorithm that scales to large graphs; clear experimental validation; significant improvement over prior work; elegant connection to Lovász θ function.

Con: Limited discussion of parameter selection; does not address graph uncertainty; experiments primarily on binary classification.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in graph transduction. The theoretical insights are valuable, and the practical algorithm solves a real computational bottleneck. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), makes several novel contributions (Originality), and addresses an important problem with significant practical impact (Significance). I recommend acceptance.
