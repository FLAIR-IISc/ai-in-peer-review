This paper presents a novel approach to graph transduction by jointly optimizing the embedding and labels with a spectral norm regularizer. The authors build upon the idea of Ando and Zhang, which suggests that orthonormal unit-sphere embeddings improve generalization. However, they provide a concrete method for selecting the optimal embedding. The main contribution of the paper is the SPORE formulation, which minimizes a convex function over an elliptope. The authors also propose an inexact proximal method to efficiently solve the SPORE problem, which can scale to graphs with thousands of nodes.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including asymptotic upper bounds on generalization error with favorable scaling and translated labeled sample complexity bounds. The experimental results suggest that the proposed method outperforms prior methods.

However, there are some concerns regarding the clarity and completeness of the paper. The main result, Theorem 4, is not easy to understand at a high level, and the reviewer had to spend significant time deciphering the theorem and its proof. Furthermore, the paper claims that learning is impossible without assumptions, stating that "edges link similar instances," but never defines "similar" or incorporates it into Theorem 4's assumptions. The proof of Theorem 4 also does not seem to use the "similar" assumption.

The reviewer's interpretation of Theorem 4 for random graphs is that the error bound is O(1/m * (n^{3/4} + log(1/δ))^{1/2}), which seems to imply that ~ω(n^{3/4}) labeled nodes are required to guarantee low error. However, this is not explicitly stated in the paper, and the reviewer is unsure if this is the correct interpretation.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting and novel approach to graph transduction, the clarity and completeness of the paper are concerns that need to be addressed.
