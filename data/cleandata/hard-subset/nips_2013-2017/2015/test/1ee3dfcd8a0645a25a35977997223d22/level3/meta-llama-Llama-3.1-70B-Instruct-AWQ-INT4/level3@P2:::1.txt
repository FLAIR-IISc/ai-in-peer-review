This paper proposes a novel approach to graph transduction, a fundamental problem in semi-supervised learning, by reducing it to an equivalent kernel-based supervised problem. The authors build upon the work of Zhang and Ando, and make significant theoretical and algorithmic contributions to the field.

The main theoretical contribution of the paper is the derivation of an error bound that shows minimizing the spectral norm of the graph kernel improves generalization. This result is interesting and has important implications for graph transduction. The authors also propose a proximal solver that handles cone-polytope intersection via an approximate projection step, which is a novel and efficient approach to solving the optimization problem.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their contributions. The notation is generally clear, although there are a few minor issues, such as the equals sign in equation (5) that may cause confusion. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of their algorithm and its convergence properties, which is a significant strength of the paper.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to provide new theoretical insights into spectral regularization for graph transduction. The authors' use of the Lovász number of the data graph to bound the error convergence is a novel and interesting approach. The experimental results are also convincing, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm on several datasets.

However, there are a few minor issues with the paper. The authors could benefit from explicitly stating the correspondence between equations (3) and (1), which makes semi-supervised learning a supervised problem. Additionally, the notation in equation (5) is confusing, and the authors could clarify how the embedding U is recovered from K.
