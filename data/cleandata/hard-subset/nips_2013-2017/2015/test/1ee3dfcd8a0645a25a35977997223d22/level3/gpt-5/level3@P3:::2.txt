Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles graph transduction (vertex label prediction) by jointly learning an orthonormal unit-sphere embedding and the labels. Building on Ando & Zhang’s observation that unit-sphere embeddings improve generalization, the authors give a concrete, theoretically motivated way to select the embedding: minimize the SVM dual objective plus a spectral-norm penalty over the elliptope K(G) (the set of unit-diagonal, edge-sparse PSD kernels). The generalization analysis avoids VC dimension (infinite here) by deriving a high-probability bound (Theorem 1) that depends on the spectral norm of the kernel, thereby motivating spectral-norm regularization. The theory relates to Lovász’s theta and uses ϑ(Ḡ) = maxK∈K(G) λ1(K), yielding asymptotic error and labeled sample complexity bounds (Theorem 4 and corollaries), with improved scaling compared to prior work on orthonormal representations. Optimization-wise, while the problem is an SDP, the paper proposes an infeasible inexact proximal (IIP) method that combines subgradient updates with an approximate projection computed by FISTA on the support functions of the PSD cone and a polytope, achieving O(1/√T) convergence and scaling to graphs with thousands of nodes. Experiments on UCI, MNIST subsamples, and synthetic multiple graphs show consistent gains over Laplacian baselines and a multiple-graph extension (MKL-SPORE) is proposed.

Quality
- Theory: Theorem 1 is clear and compelling: it directly ties generalization to the kernel’s spectral norm. The link to Lovász ϑ with Lemmas 2–3 is elegant and yields graph-structural dependence. However, I was unable to understand the main statistical claim (Theorem 4) at a high level because (i) the assumptions appear incomplete/implicit, and (ii) the bound’s form is ambiguous. The paper states that learning is impossible without assumptions and informally invokes “edges link similar instances,” but “similar” is never defined nor explicitly used in the theorem’s assumptions or proof. I could not locate where that assumption is employed in the proof of Theorem 4. As written, the result seems to hold without such a condition, which is puzzling. Moreover, equation (6) is syntactically ambiguous; my best reading is er^{0-1}[ŷ] = O( (1/m) (√(n ϑ(G)) + log(1/δ)) )^{1/2} = O( (n^{1/2}ϑ(G)^{1/2}/m)^{1/2} ). If ϑ(G)=Θ(√n) for random graphs, this becomes er^{0-1}[ŷ] = O( (n^{3/4}/m)^{1/2} ), implying one needs m ≫ n^{3/4} labels for small error. The paper’s informal discussion and sample-complexity translation should make this scaling explicit and reconcile it with the claimed improvements over [16]. If instead the intended bound is O( (n^{3/4}+log)/m ), that is a materially different rate.
- Optimization: The IIP framework is cleanly presented; the use of the proximal operator of the support function to control projection error is interesting and technically sound, and the convergence guarantee with inexact prox is useful. Applying FISTA to the sum of support functions over S+ and the affine set is a nice practical choice. The complexity discussion and the need for PSD feasibility before the SVM call are reasonable.
- Experiments: Results consistently favor SPORE/MKL-SPORE over Laplacian and K-scaling baselines. Ablations (effect of β=0, fixed embeddings) and stronger baselines (e.g., harmonic function/label propagation, game-theoretic transduction) would strengthen claims. Reporting wall-clock/runtime vs. an SDP baseline on moderate n would help quantify scalability.

Clarity
The paper is dense but mostly well organized. However:
- Define “similarity” precisely (e.g., assortativity or margin assumptions), and state it in Theorem 4 if it is needed. If not needed, clarify why, and how the graph structure enters solely via ϑ(G).
- Disambiguate equation (6) and the parameter choice; include an explicit corollary for G(n,p) and for simple stochastic block models to illustrate scaling.
- Summarize in the main text the key steps of the Rademacher-based argument that improve on [16].
- Provide a brief intuition for why the spectral-norm penalty, through ϑ(Ḡ), is the right regularizer.

Originality
- Jointly optimizing orthonormal embeddings with a spectral-norm penalty derived from a PAC bound is novel and well-motivated.
- The inexact proximal approach for a nonsmooth objective over an elliptope, with approximate projections via support-function prox, is a useful methodological contribution.
- The multiple-graph extension with a max spectral-norm regularizer is a natural and interesting adaptation, with a corresponding bound.

Significance
If the main theorem is clarified and its assumptions made precise, the work could have meaningful impact on graph SSL: it ties generalization to clear graph parameters (ϑ) and provides a scalable algorithm. The optimization technique may be of independent interest. At present, the ambiguity around Theorem 4 and the “similarity” assumption limits my ability to evaluate the core theoretical contribution.

Questions for the authors
1) What exact probabilistic/structural assumption is needed to make learning possible? Please formalize “edges connect similar instances” (e.g., stochastic block model with p>q, margin/noise, label smoothness) and include it in Theorem 4’s statement.
2) Where is that assumption used in Theorem 4’s proof? I could not find an invocation in the supplement.
3) Please disambiguate (6) and provide a clean corollary for random graphs (state ϑ(G)=Θ(√n)) and the resulting labeled sample complexity.
4) Can you provide an ablation with β=0 and/or fixed embeddings to isolate the benefit of the spectral-norm term?
5) How sensitive is performance to C and β? Any guidance from Theorem 4 for setting them without cross-validation?
6) Runtime: report wall-clock and memory vs. an SDP solver up to the largest feasible n.

Pros
- Clear motivation from a high-probability bound; principled spectral-norm regularization.
- Elegant use of Lovász ϑ; graph-structural dependence in bounds.
- Scalable, general inexact proximal method with convergence guarantees.
- Consistent empirical improvements; multi-graph extension with theory.

Cons
- Main theoretical result (Theorem 4) is hard to parse; assumptions unclear and seemingly unused.
- Ambiguity in the rate leads to different interpretations and sample-complexity conclusions.
- Limited baselines/ablations; limited runtime comparisons.
- Heavy reliance on supplement; some notation choices hinder readability.

Recommendation
Borderline/uncertain. The ideas and optimization are promising and potentially significant, but I cannot confidently assess the central theoretical claim due to the above confusions. Clarifying the assumptions, tightening the statement/proof of Theorem 4, and adding targeted empirical ablations would substantially strengthen the paper.

Confidence
Moderate.
