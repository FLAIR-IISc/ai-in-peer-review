## Review of "Spectral Norm Regularization of Orthonormal Representations for Graph Transduction"

This paper addresses the problem of graph transduction, specifically learning vertex labels by leveraging orthonormal graph embeddings. Building on prior work by Ando and Zhang, which suggests benefits of unit-sphere embeddings, the authors propose a novel optimization formulation (SPORE) that jointly optimizes the embedding and labels, regularized by the spectral norm of the graph kernel. The motivation stems from a newly derived generalization bound (Theorem 1) that links spectral norm regularization to improved generalization performance. The authors also present an inexact proximal method (IIP) to scale the optimization to larger graphs and demonstrate empirical success on several datasets.

The paper's strengths lie in its attempt to bridge the gap between theoretical generalization bounds and practical algorithm design. The introduction of the spectral norm regularizer, justified by Theorem 1, is a significant contribution. The IIP algorithm is a clever approach to address the scalability issues inherent in solving SDPs, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness, particularly on larger graphs. The extension to multiple graph transduction is also a valuable addition, addressing a relevant and increasingly important problem. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the mathematical formulations and algorithmic details.

However, the central theoretical claim, encapsulated in Theorem 4, is difficult to grasp at a high level. While the authors state a generalization error bound, the connection between the assumptions and the resulting error rate is not immediately clear. Critically, the paper asserts that learning is impossible without assumptions about edge similarity ("edges link similar instances") but fails to formally define "similar" or incorporate this notion into the assumptions of Theorem 4. This omission leads to a significant proof gap – the reviewer could not identify where this "similar instance" assumption is utilized within the theorem's proof. The informal interpretation of Theorem 4, suggesting a labeled sample complexity of ~ω(n<sup>3/4</sup>), feels unsupported given the lack of clarity surrounding the theorem itself. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to recent advances in graph neural networks (GNNs), which have become dominant in graph transduction tasks. While the paper cites relevant work, it doesn’t adequately position itself within the current landscape of GNN-based approaches.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel spectral norm regularization approach with theoretical justification.
*   Scalable IIP algorithm for practical implementation.
*   Extension to multiple graph transduction.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity and potential issues with the main theoretical result (Theorem 4).
*   Missing definition of "similar instances" and its absence in the theorem's assumptions.
*   Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art GNN methods.



**Overall:** Due to the significant confusion surrounding the main theoretical claim and the lack of a clear connection between the stated assumptions and the derived results, I am unable to confidently judge the paper's theoretical contributions. While the algorithmic and experimental aspects are promising, the unresolved issues with Theorem 4 prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the theoretical concerns and provide a more comprehensive comparison to existing GNN-based methods.
