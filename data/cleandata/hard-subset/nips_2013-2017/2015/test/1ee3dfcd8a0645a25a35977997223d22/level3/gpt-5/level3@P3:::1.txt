Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles graph transduction by reducing semi-supervised learning (SSL) to a supervised kernel problem on labeled data, in the spirit of Zhang–Ando’s spectral kernel design. Concretely, the authors adopt the dual SVM-style formulation ωC(K, yS) (their Eq. (3)), where only labeled indices S participate in the objective and constraints, and optimize over graph kernels K constrained to the elliptope defined by the graph. From this reduction, they build new theory and algorithms.

The main theoretical contribution is a high-probability generalization bound (Theorem 1) showing that the 0-1 error on unlabeled nodes is controlled by the hinge loss on S plus a term scaling with the spectral norm λ1(K). This motivates adding a spectral-norm penalty to the kernel-learning problem, leading to SPORE (Eq. (5)), a convex optimization over the cone–polytope intersection K(G) = S+ ∩ P(G). They further relate the optimal spectral norm over K(G) to the Lovász theta of the complement graph (Lemma 2), and bound the supervised objective via ϑ(G) (Lemma 3), yielding an error convergence rate (Theorem 4) that depends on ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ḡ). This links sample complexity to graph structure and improves on prior bounds for orthonormal embeddings.

Algorithmically, the paper proposes an Infeasible Inexact Proximal (IIP) method: a projected subgradient scheme that replaces exact projections onto the elliptope with approximate projections computed via the proximal operator of the support function, solved with FISTA for the intersection S+ ∩ P(G). They establish O(1/√T) convergence under a dual approximate-optimally criterion, and extend the approach to multiple graphs with an MKL-style combination and a corresponding generalization guarantee (Eq. (18)).

Experiments on UCI and MNIST benchmarks show consistent gains over Laplacian kernels and K-scaling, and the solver scales to a few thousand nodes, outperforming generic SDP approaches in practice.

Quality:
- Theory: The generalization bound that highlights the role of λ1(K), the connection to Lovász θ via tight spectral characterizations over K(G), and the improved labeled sample complexity are solid and well-motivated. The use of Rademacher complexity rather than VC arguments is appropriate for infinite VC classes. While key proofs are in the supplement, the statements are plausible and align with known properties of ϑ(·).
- Algorithms: The IIP framework is carefully designed around a dual inexactness criterion, which is crucial to preserve convergence rates for non-smooth objectives. The decomposition of the prox over the intersection via FISTA is elegant and practically implementable. Complexity discussion is helpful, though see questions below regarding eigendecompositions and top-eigenvector computations.
- Empirics: Results are convincing across datasets and notably strong for multiple-graph settings, with robustness to noisy views. Runtime evidence (scaling to 2000 nodes with few outer/inner iterations) supports practicality.

Clarity:
The manuscript is dense and packs many contributions, making it occasionally hard to follow. Specific suggestions:
- Emphasize the reduction: Explicitly state near Eq. (3) that, via the correspondence to Eq. (1), the SSL task becomes a supervised kernel-learning problem over labeled samples only; this will prevent confusion about sums and constraints being over S rather than V.
- Section 2 / Eq. (5) notation: The notation g(K) = ωC(K, yS) + β λ1(K) is clear, but in the current text an equals sign right before ωC appears to suggest a definition inline; please rewrite to avoid the extra “=”, which reads as if defining ωC again.
- Section 3: Clarify how an embedding U is recovered from K in practice. Since K ∈ S+ with Kij = u>i uj and Kii = 1, discuss choices for factorization (eigendecomposition with eigenvalue thresholding, Cholesky if strictly PD, or low-rank truncated EVD). Also comment on the chosen embedding dimension and its effect on predictions and computation.
- Section 4 / Alg. 1 (steps 8–9): Ensuring Kt is PSD by eigendecomposition can be expensive. Is this the dominant cost per outer iteration? Given that a top-eigenvector is also required for the subgradient of λ1(K), can a single partial EVD/power iteration serve both needs? More broadly, could optimizing over a factorization K = LL^T (Burer–Monteiro style) be feasible to avoid repeated PSD projections? This would trade convexity for efficiency; a discussion of pros/cons would be valuable.

Originality:
- The spectral-norm-based generalization bound for orthonormal representations and its clean link to Lovász θ are new and insightful for graph transduction.
- The IIP method, with a dual inexactness criterion for approximate projections onto cone–polytope intersections, is a useful algorithmic contribution beyond this application.
- The MKL extension with a bound depending on the best view (min over k of ϑ(G(k))) provides a principled multi-graph justification.

Significance:
- The theory advances understanding of what makes graph embeddings generalize (spectral-norm control and θ), likely influencing future designs of graph kernels/embeddings.
- The solver provides a scalable alternative to SDP for elliptope-constrained kernel learning, potentially applicable in other domains (e.g., correlation matrices).
- Empirical improvements are notable, though larger graphs and additional baselines (e.g., label propagation, harmonic functions) would further strengthen the case.

Additional questions/suggestions:
- Hyperparameters: Theorem 4 gives settings in terms of ϑ(G) and ϑ(Ḡ), which are generally unavailable. Please discuss practical tuning (cross-validation) and sensitivity, and whether proxy quantities (e.g., spectral norms of initial kernels) can guide choices.
- Approximate projection accuracy: How is the inner FISTA stopping criterion set in practice? Any empirical trade-off curves between inner accuracy, total runtime, and final objective?
- Complexity profiling: A breakdown of time per outer iteration into (i) SVM on S, (ii) PSD projection/eigendecomposition, (iii) top eigenvector for λ1(K), and (iv) FISTA inner steps would clarify the main bottlenecks.
- Reference fix: Zhang and Ando is NIPS 2005, not 2006.

Pros:
- Strong, novel theoretical insights linking generalization to spectral norm and Lovász number.
- Clean convex formulation (SPORE) with a principled inexact-projection solver and convergence guarantees.
- MKL extension with theory and solid empirical gains, including robustness to noisy graphs.
- Convincing improvements over common graph-transduction baselines.

Cons:
- Paper is dense; several presentation/notation issues hinder readability.
- Computational cost of repeated eigendecompositions may limit scaling; discussion of low-rank/factorized alternatives is missing.
- Experimental scope could be broader in baselines and larger graphs.

Overall evaluation:
An interesting and substantive contribution that advances both the theory of spectral regularization for graph transduction and practical algorithms for elliptope-constrained kernel learning. Despite clarity issues and some open questions about computational trade-offs, the paper’s insights and results merit acceptance.
