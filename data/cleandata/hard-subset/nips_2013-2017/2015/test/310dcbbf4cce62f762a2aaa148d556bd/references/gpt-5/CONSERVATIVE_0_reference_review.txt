Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single hard budget and a finite time horizon. Contexts are i.i.d. from a finite set; actions incur fixed costs; the process stops when either time or budget is exhausted. The authors focus first on unit-cost systems and known context distribution, then discuss extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.

The main idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle by a linear program (LP) that enforces an average budget constraint. A static LP gives an upper bound on oracle value; an Adaptive LP (ALP) recomputes the LP using the remaining average budget bτ/τ, taking the best action for a context with context-dependent randomized acceptance probabilities. With known statistics, ALP achieves O(1) regret (non-boundary) and O(√T) in boundary cases (ρ equal to a cumulative context probability). For unknown rewards, UCB-ALP combines UCB estimates with ALP and attains O(JK log T) regret (plus O(√T) in boundary cases). For unknown context distributions, an empirical variant (EALP) is proposed; analysis is provided for a truncated version. For heterogeneous costs, the LP and analysis extend in spirit; an ε-first ALP is suggested for learning.

This work relates to Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB), e.g., Badanidiyuru et al. (FOCS’13; COLT’14), and to Agrawal & Devanur’s bandits with concave rewards and global constraints. Those works provide O(√T) regret (often in more general settings, possibly infinite contexts, random costs). Here, by restricting to finite contexts, fixed costs, and by exploiting a gap/ordering structure, the authors claim the first logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and computationally light algorithms: ALP requires solving a small LP per round (J variables in the unit-cost case), and UCB-ALP augments it with standard UCB estimates.
- Clear structural insight: mapping the remaining-budget process under ALP to sampling without replacement (hypergeometric concentration) is neat and supports the O(1) gap to oracle (non-boundary).
- Decoupling of decision and estimation: ALP only needs correct ordering of expected rewards, enabling a clean integration with UCB and a clean regret decomposition (within-context action errors vs context-ranking/consumption fluctuations).
- Theoretical guarantees: O(1) regret versus oracle in the known-statistics case (non-boundary), and O(JK log T) in the unknown case. The boundary-case handling is explicit.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong simplifying assumptions. The main positive results rely on finite, i.i.d. contexts, unit costs, unique best action per context, and strictly ordered u*j across contexts. Many of these are nontrivial in practice; ties and near-ties are common. The “measure-zero boundary” caveat is theoretically convenient but practically brittle (finite samples can sit near boundaries; constants can blow up).
- Limited novelty relative to established primal-dual/LP-based approaches in BwK/RCB. Using average-budget tracking (bτ/τ) as a dynamic threshold is a standard idea in online knapsack/consumption-balancing algorithms. The paper’s contribution is largely an analysis that yields O(1)/logarithmic regret under discrete, gap-separated contexts. The paper should more clearly articulate what is fundamentally new versus prior primal-dual methods (beyond constants and the discrete setting), and explicitly characterize the gap assumptions driving the improvement.
- Missing empirical validation. There are no experiments to assess practical performance, constants, robustness near boundaries, or to compare against BwK/RCB baselines (e.g., Mixture Elimination, primal-dual methods). Given the algorithmic claims of computational efficiency and the focus on practical scenarios (clinical trials, wireless rate selection), some empirical evidence is important.
- Opaque dependence on instance parameters. The O(JK log T) bound hides dependence on reward gaps and π’s; UCB-style regrets typically scale with 1/Δ. The paper should expose explicit instance-dependent constants for both the ranking-errors term and the ALP constant gap, and discuss behavior when gaps are small.
- Extensions are either deferred to the supplement or are weaker. The unknown-π analysis relies on a truncated empirical approach; the main text lacks precise, finite-sample, non-asymptotic guarantees. The heterogeneous-costs case falls back to ε-first, which is generally inferior to UCB-type anytime methods; no logarithmic regret guarantee is claimed there in the learning setting.
- Scope limitations. Assumptions of known horizon T and budget B, i.i.d. contexts, and fixed costs limit applicability. It would be helpful to discuss robustness to misspecified π, non-i.i.d. contexts, or unknown horizons.

Quality
The core proofs sketched seem sound and build on standard concentration tools; the sampling-without-replacement perspective is a nice touch. However, key details are in the supplement, and several claims (unknown-π, heterogeneous costs) are only partially supported in the main text. No experiments are provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. Assumptions are stated, and the algorithmic steps are clear. However, the novelty over prior primal-dual/BwK methods should be clarified, and instance-dependent constants should be made explicit. Providing a complexity discussion per round would help.

Originality
Moderate. The adaptive LP idea and UCB integration are natural evolutions of known techniques. The main originality lies in achieving logarithmic/gap-dependent regret in a simplified constrained contextual setting and in the specific analysis tracking the remaining budget concentration.

Significance
Moderate. The results may be of interest to researchers working on constrained bandits, showing that under strong structure one can beat the O(√T) frontier. The practical impact is uncertain without experiments and with the current assumptions.

Pros
- Simple, implementable algorithms with clear intuition.
- Solid theoretical analysis yielding O(1)/O(log T) regret under non-boundary cases.
- Insightful budget-concentration argument enabling clean regret bounds.

Cons
- Heavy simplifying assumptions; sensitivity near boundaries not explored empirically.
- Novelty over established BwK/RCB approaches is incremental; lack of clear instance-dependent constants.
- No experimental validation; some extensions are weak or only sketched.

Questions for authors
1) Can you provide explicit instance-dependent constants (gap dependence) in Theorem 2 and in the ALP O(1) bound?
2) How robust is ALP/UCB-ALP to near-boundary ρ and to ties among u*j? Can you remove the uniqueness/strict-order assumptions formally?
3) How does UCB-ALP compare empirically to primal-dual BwK algorithms and Mixture Elimination on finite-context problems?
4) Can you provide non-truncated analyses for unknown π, or at least bounds quantifying the impact of using π̂ throughout?
5) What is the per-round computational complexity and numerical stability of the LPs, especially for heterogeneous costs?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical results are competent and the algorithms are simple, but the contribution feels incremental relative to established LP/primal-dual constrained bandit methods, relies on strong assumptions, and lacks empirical support. With clearer novelty positioning, explicit instance-dependent constants, and experimental validation (including robustness near boundaries), I would be more positive.

Confidence: High.
