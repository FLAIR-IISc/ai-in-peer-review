

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary
This paper addresses the constrained contextual bandit problem where the agent faces both time horizon T and budget B constraints. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm for known statistics and a UCB-ALP algorithm for unknown statistics, claiming logarithmic regret (O(log T)) for non-boundary cases. The key innovation is approximating the oracle solution with a linear programming relaxation that adjusts the budget constraint adaptively. The paper builds on prior work on contextual bandits (e.g., [1,2,3]) and constrained bandits (e.g., [11-16, 17]), but claims to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting.

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies an important gap in the literature: previous constrained bandit approaches achieve O(√T) regret, while the authors claim to achieve O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits.
2. The ALP approach is conceptually interesting, as it approximates the complex dynamic programming solution with a linear program that can be efficiently implemented.
3. The theoretical analysis is thorough for the unit-cost, known-context-distribution setting, with careful handling of the boundary cases.
4. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of previous approaches (e.g., [17] restricts to finite policy sets and achieves O(√T) regret).

## Weaknesses
1. **Significant Assumption Limitations**: The main results require several restrictive assumptions: unit costs (cj,k=1), known context distribution, finite discrete contexts, and a single budget constraint. The generalization to heterogeneous costs (Section 6) is only briefly sketched, and the analysis for unknown context distribution (Section 5) relies on a truncated version with limited theoretical guarantees. For a paper claiming to solve a "real-world" problem (e.g., clinical trials, crowdsourcing), these assumptions seem too strong.

2. **Boundary Cases Problem**: The paper's main regret bound (Theorem 2) only holds "except for a set of parameters of zero Lebesgue measure." This is a critical limitation—the algorithm fails to achieve logarithmic regret in boundary cases (where ρ = qj for some j), instead achieving O(√T) regret. The paper does not adequately address how common these boundary cases are in practice or whether they can be avoided in real applications. In many applications, the budget-to-time ratio ρ might naturally fall into boundary cases.

3. **Lack of Experimental Validation**: Despite claiming to have "numerical simulations" in Section 5, the paper provides no experimental results to support the theoretical claims. NIPS values both theoretical and empirical validation, and the absence of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the proposed algorithms.

4. **Regret Bound Dependence on Problem Size**: The regret bound for UCB-ALP includes a term O(JK log T), where J and K are the number of contexts and actions, respectively. This means the regret grows with the problem size, which is problematic for large-scale applications. The paper does not address whether this dependency is inherent to the problem or could be improved.

5. **Questionable Novelty**: The paper claims "to the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that shows how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits." However, the authors cite [20, 21, 22] which achieve O(√T) regret for similar problems. While the authors correctly argue that [20] achieves O(√T) regret in their setting due to using a fixed budget constraint, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish their approach from these prior works in terms of the fundamental mechanisms enabling logarithmic regret.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The theoretical analysis is rigorous for the restricted setting, but the paper fails to adequately address the practical limitations of the assumptions. The boundary cases issue significantly undermines the main claim of achieving logarithmic regret in most cases.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise mathematical formulations. However, the limitations of the approach (particularly regarding boundary cases and strong assumptions) could be more prominently emphasized.

**Originality**: The ALP approach is novel for constrained contextual bandits, but the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate it from prior work on constrained bandits. The key insight—that the oracle can be approximated with linear programming—is not entirely new (citing [17, 19]), and the adaptation to contextual bandits is not as novel as claimed.

**Significance**: The problem is important, but the restrictive assumptions and boundary cases limitations significantly reduce the practical significance. Achieving logarithmic regret would be a major theoretical contribution, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this is achievable in realistic settings.

## Recommendation
This paper presents an interesting theoretical approach to a challenging problem, but the significant limitations in the assumptions and the boundary cases issue substantially weaken the main contribution. The lack of experimental validation is also a major drawback for a NIPS submission.

While the theoretical analysis is sound within the restricted setting, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate that the approach is practically useful or that the main regret bound (O(log T)) is achievable in realistic applications. The boundary cases problem alone means the algorithm will fail to achieve logarithmic regret in a non-negligible fraction of practical scenarios.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More thorough discussion of the boundary cases and their practical prevalence
2. Experimental validation on synthetic or real data
3. A clearer explanation of how the approach scales with problem size (J and K)
4. More explicit comparison with recent work on constrained bandits [21, 22]

Given these concerns, I recommend rejection. The paper makes a marginal theoretical contribution that is limited by strong assumptions and unaddressed boundary cases, and lacks empirical validation to support its claims. The field of contextual bandits has seen many theoretical advances, but this paper does not significantly advance the state of the art in a way that would be compelling for NIPS.
