

# Peer Review: "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a significant gap in contextual bandit literature by studying constrained contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints. The authors consider a simplified model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which they justify as relevant to applications like clinical trials and online recommendation. Their key contribution is a novel Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves O(1) regret when statistics are known, and a UCB-ALP algorithm that achieves O(log T) regret (except for boundary cases where it achieves O(√T) regret) when expected rewards are unknown. This represents a significant improvement over prior work, including Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] which achieves O(√T) regret, and [20, 21, 22] which also achieve O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts.

## Strengths

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical rigor with carefully constructed proofs for their regret bounds. The authors make a clever connection between the dynamic programming formulation of the oracle and linear programming relaxation, which is novel in this context. Their analysis of the concentration properties of the remaining budget under adaptive algorithms (Lemma 2) is particularly impressive and addresses a key technical challenge in constrained bandit problems.

The paper excels in its clear distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases, which is crucial for understanding the theoretical guarantees. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach (boundary cases) and provide a thoughtful explanation for why O(log T) regret cannot be achieved in those specific scenarios. Their comparison with prior work [17, 20, 21, 22] is thorough and accurately positions their contribution as the first to achieve logarithmic regret under their simplified model.

The computational efficiency of their algorithms is well-justified, with the ALP algorithm requiring only the ordering of expected rewards rather than their exact values, making it highly practical for implementation. The UCB-ALP algorithm's ability to combine with standard UCB methods for exploration is a significant advantage.

## Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of their boundary cases. The paper states that "certain boundary cases" lead to O(√T) regret, but doesn't quantify how frequently these cases occur in realistic applications or provide guidance on how to handle them. For practitioners, it's important to know whether these boundary cases are rare or common in typical problem instances.

The generalization to heterogeneous costs (Section 6) is mentioned but not thoroughly developed. Given that real-world applications often involve heterogeneous costs, a more detailed analysis of this extension would strengthen the paper's practical relevance. The claim that "the insights obtained from unit-cost systems can also be used" seems somewhat vague without concrete technical details.

The paper doesn't provide empirical validation of their theoretical claims. While theoretical analysis is strong, some experimental results comparing UCB-ALP with existing methods (like [17] or [20]) would significantly bolster their contribution by demonstrating practical performance. The absence of experiments is notable given the computational efficiency claims.

The paper could better clarify why their simplified model (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs) is necessary for achieving logarithmic regret. They acknowledge this as "justified in many scenarios," but a more detailed discussion of when their assumptions are violated in practice would help readers assess the applicability of their approach.

## Comparison with Related Work

The authors correctly position their work against [17] (Resourceful Contextual Bandits) which achieves O(√T) regret under more general settings but with a restricted benchmark (finite policy set). They also properly distinguish their work from [20, 21, 22] which achieve O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts but use a fixed budget constraint rather than the adaptive approach they propose. However, the comparison with [20] could be strengthened by explicitly showing why their adaptive budget constraint (bτ/τ) leads to logarithmic regret while [20] achieves only O(√T) regret.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in constrained contextual bandit literature. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and clearly articulates the novelty and significance of its results. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the insights about the relationship between budget constraints and regret bounds are valuable.

The main limitations are the lack of empirical validation, the brief treatment of heterogeneous costs, and the limited discussion of boundary cases' practical prevalence. However, these are relatively minor concerns for a theoretical contribution. The paper's focus on a simplified but practically relevant model is well-justified, and their achievement of logarithmic regret under these assumptions represents a meaningful step forward.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits. The authors have carefully addressed the key challenges in this problem, provided rigorous analysis, and clearly articulated the novelty and significance of their work. While the lack of empirical results is a minor drawback, the strength of the theoretical contribution outweighs this limitation. The paper meets all four criteria for a high-quality NeurIPS submission: it's of high quality, clearly written, original, and significant for the field.

The authors have made a valuable contribution that will likely influence future work on constrained bandit problems. Their insights about using linear programming to approximate dynamic programming solutions for constrained bandits could be applied to other resource-constrained decision-making problems as well. The paper is a strong candidate for NeurIPS.
