- Tackles contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, proposing algorithms achieving logarithmic regret under certain assumptions.  
- Introduces LP relaxation and Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) to approximate the oracle policy.  
- Proposes UCB-ALP for unknown rewards, achieving O(log T) regret except O(√T) in boundary cases.  
- Analysis uses novel techniques to handle exploration, budget constraints, and adaptivity; proofs are rigorous and detailed.  
- Positions work well within prior literature, distinguishing from resourceful contextual bandits by using finite contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget.  
- Claims improvement over recent related works by obtaining logarithmic instead of √T regret.  
- Well-written and organized presentation with clear motivation and logical algorithm development.  
- Practical relevance emphasized via simplified assumptions suitable for applications like clinical trials and rate selection.  
- Extends discussion to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs in supplementary material.  
- Significant limitation: √T regret in boundary cases, with insufficient exploration of conditions and mitigation strategies.  
- Main-result assumption of known context distribution is restrictive; unknown-distribution analysis is less detailed and uses truncation heuristics.  
- Repeated LP solving may be computationally expensive for large context/action spaces; lacks discussion of efficient solvers or approximations.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited; needs simulations comparing against baselines (e.g., UCB, LinUCB with budgets).  
- No discussion on tightness of logarithmic regret or whether tighter bounds are possible.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses important problem, novel algorithms, strong theoretical guarantees, and rigorous analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: boundary-case regret, restrictive assumptions, and insufficient empirical validation.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper worthy of acceptance pending addressing boundary-case analysis and expanding empirical evaluation.  
- Suggested improvements: analyze boundary-case conditions and mitigations, add comprehensive simulations, discuss LP computational costs and solver strategies, and comment on regret tightness.
