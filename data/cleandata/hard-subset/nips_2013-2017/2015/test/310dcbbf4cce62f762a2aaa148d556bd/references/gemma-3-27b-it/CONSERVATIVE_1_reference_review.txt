This paper addresses the important problem of contextual bandits with budget constraints, a natural extension of the standard model with relevance to applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors identify a gap in the literature, demonstrating that existing algorithms for constrained bandits don't directly translate to the contextual setting. They propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, coupled with UCB for the unknown reward case (UCB-ALP), and provide theoretical regret guarantees.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is strong, and the authors do a good job of positioning their work within the existing literature, specifically referencing [17] and highlighting its limitations. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and the proposed solution. The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the LP formulations are presented concisely.

However, despite the clear presentation, I remain skeptical of the novelty and significance of the contributions, particularly given the extensive analysis and the claimed logarithmic regret. While the authors correctly identify the limitations of directly applying existing techniques, the core idea of approximating the oracle with linear programming feels incremental. LP relaxations are a common technique, and the adaptive nature of the budget constraint, while sensible, doesn’t represent a paradigm shift. 

My primary concern is the reliance on a somewhat idealized setup. The assumption of finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint significantly simplifies the problem.  While the authors justify these assumptions, the applicability of the results to more realistic settings is unclear.  The extension to unknown context distributions feels tacked on, with a reliance on a truncated empirical estimator that requires further justification. The analysis in Section 6 regarding heterogeneous costs also appears less developed, relying heavily on referencing supplementary material.

The regret analysis, while thorough, feels overly complex given the relatively straightforward nature of the proposed algorithm. The numerous lemmas and theorems, while mathematically rigorous, make it difficult to grasp the core insights. The boundary cases, leading to √T regret, are also concerning and require further scrutiny. The authors acknowledge these cases but don't offer a clear explanation of when they are likely to occur in practice.

Furthermore, the comparison with related work is somewhat selective. While [17] is adequately addressed, the recent extensions mentioned at the end of the introduction [21, 22] achieving √T regret in dynamic contexts are dismissed too quickly. A more thorough comparative analysis is needed to demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed UCB-ALP algorithm. The claim of being the “first work that shows how to achieve logarithmic regret” seems a bit strong given the existing literature.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and practical problem.
*   Clear presentation and well-organized.
*   Provides theoretical guarantees (logarithmic regret under specific conditions).
*   Identifies a gap in existing literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty – relies heavily on standard LP relaxation techniques.
*   Strong simplifying assumptions limit applicability.
*   Complex regret analysis makes it difficult to assess key insights.
*   Boundary cases with √T regret are concerning.
*   Incomplete comparison with recent related work.
*   Claims of being the first to achieve logarithmic regret are overstated.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is technically sound and clearly presented, it lacks sufficient novelty and significance to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The simplifying assumptions and the complexity of the analysis outweigh the incremental improvement over existing techniques. I encourage the authors to explore the applicability of their approach to more realistic scenarios and to provide a more comprehensive comparison with recent related work. A stronger focus on practical implications and a more concise presentation of the theoretical results would significantly strengthen the paper.
