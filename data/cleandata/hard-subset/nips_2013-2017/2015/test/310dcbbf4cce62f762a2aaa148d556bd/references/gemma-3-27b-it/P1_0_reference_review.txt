## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a realistic scenario often overlooked in standard bandit literature. The authors make a significant contribution by developing algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret under specific, practical assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs). They build upon the idea of approximating the optimal policy with Linear Programming (LP) and introduce the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, demonstrating its near-optimality.  This is then extended to the unknown reward setting with a UCB-ALP algorithm. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, detailing regret bounds and outlining a clear roadmap for extending these results to more general settings.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on contextual bandits (Langford & Zhang, 2007; Li et al., 2010), resource-constrained bandits (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., 2013, 2014; Jiang et al., 2014), and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Badanidiyuru et al., 2017). It effectively highlights the limitations of previous approaches in handling both budget *and* context, and distinguishes itself by focusing on achieving logarithmic regret – a crucial performance guarantee.  The authors acknowledge and differentiate themselves from recent work extending bandits with constraints (Agrawal et al. 2015, 2016) and RCB (Badanidiyuru et al., 2017, recent extensions in [21, 22]), emphasizing the novelty of their logarithmic regret guarantee.  

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Addressing budget constraints in contextual bandits significantly increases the practical relevance of the work. The provided examples (crowdsourcing, clinical trials) clearly illustrate the motivation.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the proposed algorithms, including regret bounds and detailed proofs (with additional details in the supplementary material). The use of LP approximation is well-justified and analyzed.
* **Adaptive Approach:**  ALP is a clever and intuitive approach that adapts to the remaining budget and time, leading to improved performance.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors carefully introduce the problem, provide necessary background, and logically present their approach and analysis.
* **Insights for Generalization:** The discussion of extending the algorithms to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs demonstrates a strong understanding of the broader implications of their work.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors emphasize the computational tractability of their algorithms, which is a crucial advantage over more complex approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The requirement of finite contexts and fixed costs, while practical in some cases, limits the generality of the results. The authors acknowledge this and outline future directions, but it’s a notable constraint.
* **Boundary Cases:**  The O(√T) regret in boundary cases is a limitation. While acknowledged, the conditions defining these boundary cases could be more clearly characterized, and potential strategies for mitigating the impact of these cases explored.
* **Detailed Experimental Validation:** While the paper emphasizes theoretical results, some experimental validation, even on relatively small instances, would strengthen the claims and demonstrate practical performance. The lack of empirical results is somewhat surprising.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** While rigorous, the proofs are dense and require a significant effort to follow. More intuitive explanations of key steps would improve readability.
* **Comparison to Recent Work:** The authors mention recent extensions ([21, 22]) achieving √T regret but don’t fully elaborate on the differences beyond stating their work achieves logarithmic regret. A more detailed comparison showcasing the trade-offs would be beneficial.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Strong. The paper is technically sound, the analysis is rigorous, and the claims are well-supported. The completeness of the work is good, given the scope.
* **Clarity:**  Good. The paper is generally clearly written, although the proofs can be challenging. More detailed explanations and intuitive motivations would improve clarity further.
* **Originality:**  Good. The problem setting and the proposed approach (ALP, UCB-ALP) are novel, and represent a significant contribution to the field.
* **Significance:**  Good.  The work addresses a practically relevant problem and offers a promising approach for achieving logarithmic regret. The results are likely to be influential in the development of algorithms for resource-constrained contextual bandits.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant theoretical contribution with rigorous analysis.
* Addresses an important and practical problem.
* Novel approach (ALP) and algorithm (UCB-ALP) with strong performance guarantees.
* Well-written and clearly organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limitations due to simplifying assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs).
* √T regret in boundary cases.
* Lack of experimental validation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. Despite some limitations, the work represents a significant and valuable contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The strong theoretical foundation, novel approach, and clear presentation outweigh the weaknesses.  The authors have identified an important problem, developed a promising solution, and provided a rigorous analysis.  I encourage the authors to consider adding some experimental results and providing a more detailed comparison to recent related work in a revised version.



**Confidence:** 8/10
