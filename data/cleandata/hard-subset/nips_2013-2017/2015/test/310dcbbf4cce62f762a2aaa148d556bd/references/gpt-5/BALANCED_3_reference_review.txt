Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget constraint and finite horizon. Contexts are i.i.d. from a finite set; actions have fixed costs (unit cost in the main development) and rewards depend on context-action pairs. The key challenge is that the oracle with known statistics is a dynamic program that must trade off immediate reward against future budget/time, making analysis and learning difficult.

The authors propose to approximate the oracle by replacing the hard budget with an average budget constraint via a linear program (LP). They first analyze a static LP whose optimal value gives an upper bound on the oracle’s expected reward. They then propose Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP), which at each round re-solves the LP with the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. For known statistics and unit costs, ALP is shown to incur O(1) regret relative to the oracle for all non-boundary values of the budget ratio ρ = B/T (when ρ is not equal to a cumulative context probability), and O(√T) at boundary cases.

For unknown rewards, they combine ALP with UCB estimates (UCB-ALP), and prove O(JK log T) regret (non-boundary) and O(√T + JK log T) (boundary), where J is number of contexts and K actions per context. They also discuss extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates and a truncated analysis) and to heterogeneous costs (generalizing the LP and proposing an ε-first approach), again obtaining O(1) or O(√T) regret depending on boundary conditions. The analysis leverages a mapping of ALP’s budget consumption to sampling without replacement, yielding concentration of the remaining budget around its mean.

This work relates to Bandits with Budgets/Knapsacks (BwK) (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al. FOCS’13; Combes et al. SIGMETRICS’15) which achieve O(√T) regret in non-contextual settings, and to Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) (Badanidiyuru, Langford, Slivkins, COLT’14) and subsequent contextual BwK work (Agrawal and Devanur and follow-ups), which obtain O(√T) regret in more general settings (possibly infinite contexts/policies). The present paper focuses on finite contexts/actions and obtains logarithmic regret by exploiting the adaptive LP structure and the tolerance of ALP to estimation errors, a contrast to prior contextual results.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The idea of adaptive LP relaxation is well-motivated and the hypergeometric concentration of bτ is elegant. The decomposition of regret into action-ranking and context-threshold errors is plausible. However, several statements in the main text appear imprecise or contain typos (e.g., Theorem 1’s bound formatting; notation Θ(o) and constants), and many key proofs are deferred to the supplement. The claim that ALP “consumes one unit with probability b/τ” needs a careful, explicit derivation under the randomized LP implementation across random contexts. For UCB-ALP, the presented O(JK log T) bound is gap-independent; an instance-dependent bound (with Δ’s) would strengthen the result and better align with UCB literature.
- Completeness: The heterogeneous-cost and unknown-context-distribution cases are only sketched; the ε-first strategy’s regret and design choices (exploration length) deserve explicit statements. Empirical evaluation is only briefly mentioned; even a small synthetic study illustrating boundary vs. non-boundary behavior and robustness to mis-specification of π would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, with a clear algorithmic description and an intuitive proof sketch. That said:
- Theorems and constants should be carefully restated with correct notation and units; some equations are malformed in the text.
- Assumptions (unique best action per context; strict ordering u∗1 > … > u∗J; i.i.d. contexts; known π in Sections 3–4) should be highlighted upfront, with a brief discussion of how ties and near-ties are handled.
- The relationship between the static LP upper bound Û(T,B) and the oracle could be expanded (e.g., when the bound is tight; connection to primal-dual analyses in online ad allocation/inventory balancing).

Originality
Achieving logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts/actions and a single budget via an adaptive LP relaxation appears novel relative to RCB and BwK work that largely obtained O(√T). The mapping to sampling without replacement for budget dynamics is a neat analytical device. The adaptive use of the remaining budget ratio distinguishes this from prior contextual BwK algorithms that use fixed per-round constraints.

Significance
The results are theoretically meaningful: they show that in a practically relevant but structured regime (finite contexts/actions, fixed costs), one can decouple learning and resource management to achieve log regret with a simple, efficient algorithm. This could influence subsequent work in constrained bandits and online allocation. The scope is narrower than general contextual BwK, but the clarity and computational simplicity are strengths.

Pros
- Conceptually simple and computationally light algorithms (ALP and UCB-ALP).
- Near-oracle O(1) regret with known statistics (non-boundary) and clear explanation of boundary behavior.
- Logarithmic regret with unknown rewards; principled decomposition of regret sources.
- Useful analytical insights (budget concentration via hypergeometric distribution).
- Extensions sketched to unknown π and heterogeneous costs.

Cons
- Strong assumptions (finite i.i.d. contexts; unique best actions; strict ordering across contexts; single resource; unit costs in main results).
- Boundary cases degrade to √T without evidence of optimality or strategies to mitigate near-boundary scenarios.
- Proofs and several key details deferred; some notational/typographical issues in statements.
- No empirical evaluation in the main paper; robustness to model misspecification not demonstrated.
- Gap-independent regret bound; constants and dependence on Δ’s not characterized.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide instance-dependent bounds for UCB-ALP with explicit Δj,k terms and high-probability guarantees.
- Clarify and correct theorem statements/notation; explicitly justify the “consume with probability b/τ” step.
- Discuss handling of ties and near-ties (both within-context best-action ties and across-context threshold ties); propose tie-breaking that preserves guarantees.
- Add experiments illustrating: (i) non-/near-boundary behavior; (ii) sensitivity to mis-specified π; (iii) heterogeneous-cost cases; (iv) computational overhead.
- Compare more explicitly with primal-dual/adaptive inventory balancing approaches in online allocation (e.g., Devanur-Hayes, Buchbinder et al.), which also use adaptive dual prices.
- For unknown π, present the full UCB-EALP regret theorem in the main text for the truncated variant, with choice of truncation time.
- Discuss multiple resource constraints and whether the ALP insights extend.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a clear, original, and technically interesting path to logarithmic regret in a constrained contextual bandit setting, with simple algorithms and insightful analysis. While the scope is restricted and some presentation/proof details need polishing, the contribution should be of interest to the NeurIPS community and could spur further advances in constrained bandits.
