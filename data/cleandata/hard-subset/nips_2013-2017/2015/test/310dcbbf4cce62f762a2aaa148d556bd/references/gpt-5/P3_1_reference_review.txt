Summary
The paper studies stochastic contextual bandits with a hard budget (single knapsack) and a finite horizon. Contexts are i.i.d. from a finite set; actions incur fixed, known costs (initially unit cost). The oracle (with known reward means and context distribution) must balance exploitation with conservation of budget across time and contexts, which is a dynamic program.

The key idea is to relax the hard budget to an average budget and solve a per-round LP. The Static LP upper-bounds the oracle’s value. The Adaptive LP (ALP) replaces the average budget ρ = B/T by the instantaneous ratio bτ/τ, producing a simple thresholding policy in unit-cost systems that depends only on the ordering of per-context best expected rewards. With known statistics, ALP achieves O(1) regret relative to the oracle in non-boundary cases (ρ not equal to a cumulative context probability), and O(√T) on the “boundary.”

For unknown rewards, UCB-ALP combines UCB estimation of per-context best actions with ALP’s adaptive decision rule, proving RUCB-ALP(T,B) = O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on the boundary. For unknown context distribution, empirical estimates are used (EALP/UCB-EALP); a truncated variant is analyzed via bounded-differences/coupling to retain O(1) regret (non-boundary). The paper also sketches an extension to heterogeneous costs with an ε-First ALP and similar regret rates.

Relation to prior work
- Bandits with knapsacks (BwK) and “resourceful contextual bandits” (RCB) obtain O(√T) regret in more general settings, sometimes relative to a finite policy class. This work obtains logarithmic (or constant) regret under more specialized assumptions (finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, single resource).
- UCRL and related RL approaches can yield log regret in Markovian contexts; this paper treats i.i.d. contexts but with budget constraints.
- Agrawal & Devanur’s “bandits with concave rewards and convex knapsacks” and follow-ups achieve O(√T) regret with static (or later dynamic) contexts; the present work leverages adaptivity to bτ/τ to beat √T in the finite-context single-budget setting.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: the adaptive LP relaxation is simple, computationally light, and insightful. The fact that ALP only needs an ordering of u* across contexts is powerful and enables clean coupling with UCB.
- Strong guarantees: O(1) regret with known statistics (non-boundary) and O(JK log T) with learning are notable improvements over O(√T) results in more general models. The boundary phenomenon is clearly identified and analyzed.
- Analysis techniques: mapping ALP’s budget evolution to sampling without replacement (hypergeometric concentration) is elegant and yields tight control of bτ/τ. The decomposition of regret into action-ranking and context-threshold errors for UCB-ALP is well-motivated and leads to clean bounds.
- Practicality: per-round decisions reduce to thresholding and a simple randomization; complexity is O(J + number of observed actions) per round.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of assumptions: Results rely on finite, i.i.d. contexts, known horizon and budget, fixed costs, uniqueness and strict ordering of per-context best rewards, and (for the main theorems) known context distribution. These limits are sensible for theory but narrow applicability. Please clarify which parts of the proofs truly require uniqueness/strict ordering and how ties can be handled without changing rates.
- Boundary regime: The O(√T) upper bound is plausible (variance of budget trajectory at the threshold), but is there a matching lower bound even for unit-cost two-threshold cases? Establishing an Ω(√T) lower bound would strengthen the claim that the boundary is fundamentally harder.
- Dependence on J and K: RUCB-ALP = O(JK log T) is natural but potentially pessimistic. Can you provide instance-dependent bounds in terms of reward gaps (e.g., ∑ Δ−2 log T) separating within-context action gaps and the cross-context threshold gap? Lemma 3 suggests this is feasible.
- Unknown context distribution: The truncated EALP analysis is only sketched. How large must the truncation window be vs. T to retain O(1) (non-boundary)? What is the explicit dependence on min πj? Is there degradation when some πj are small?
- Robustness: How sensitive is ALP/UCB-ALP to mild nonstationarity (e.g., slowly drifting π or uj,k) or to random costs? The approach seems amenable to tracking via sliding windows; a brief discussion would help.
- Heterogeneous costs: The extension is important but under-specified. Is there a greedy solver exploiting the LP’s structure (e.g., sort by η and respect per-context single-action constraint), or do we require a full LP solve per round? What’s the per-round complexity and is the regret still logarithmic with UCB learning?
- Empirical evaluation: The paper promises simulations but they are not in the main text. Including at least a small experimental section would help assess constants, sensitivity near boundary, and compare with primal-dual BwK or RCB baselines.

Quality
- The technical core is sound and novel. Key steps (LP upper bound, concentration of bτ/τ under adaptivity, regret decomposition) are convincing. Some proofs are deferred; the main text would benefit from more intuition on why bτ/τ concentration remains valid under UCB-ALP.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. A few typesetting issues (e.g., Eq. (4) formatting) and minor notational glitches (Θ(o) √T is unusual—do you mean O(√T) with explicit constant Θ0?) should be fixed. Please restate the tie-handling and remove the strict ordering assumption from the main statements if it’s only for exposition.

Originality
- The adaptive LP and O(1)/O(log T) guarantees in this constrained contextual setting are, to my knowledge, new and substantially improve known rates under the stated assumptions.

Significance
- The work advances the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits under practically relevant simplifications and offers techniques (adaptive relaxation, budget concentration) that may inform more general algorithms.

Pros
- Simple, efficient algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Clear identification of boundary hardness.
- Insightful analysis linking adaptive control to sampling without replacement.
- Potentially influential for broader constrained bandit design.

Cons
- Relies on restrictive assumptions; boundary case unresolved lower bounds.
- Sparse details for heterogeneous costs and unknown π analysis.
- No experiments in main paper.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an instance-dependent regret bound with explicit gap dependencies.
- Provide a lower bound in the boundary case or, at least, empirical evidence of √T necessity.
- Expand the heterogeneous-costs section with algorithmic details and complexity.
- Include a brief experimental section comparing to primal-dual BwK/RCB baselines.
- Clarify handling of ties and remove unnecessary ordering assumptions from theorem statements.

Recommendation: Accept (7/10). The paper offers a clean, computationally simple method with strong regret guarantees and novel analysis that will interest the NeurIPS community working on online learning with resource constraints. The limitations are clearly delineated and suggest fertile directions for follow-up work.
