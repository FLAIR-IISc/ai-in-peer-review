

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a problem that extends traditional contextual bandits by incorporating resource limitations. The authors focus on a simplified model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which is justified by applications like clinical trials and wireless networks. They propose two key algorithms: Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for the known-statistics case, and UCB-ALP for the unknown-statistics case.

The main contribution is demonstrating that logarithmic regret O(log T) can be achieved for constrained contextual bandits, which is a significant improvement over prior work achieving O(√T) regret [17, 20-22]. The authors' key insight is using linear programming to approximate the oracle solution (which would require dynamic programming), avoiding the computational complexity and analytical challenges of direct DP implementation. They show ALP achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases) when statistics are known, and UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret when statistics are unknown, by combining ALP with UCB.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work: It builds on contextual bandit literature [1-7] and addresses limitations of Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] (which achieves O(√T) regret with a different benchmark definition), while distinguishing from recent works on dynamic contexts [20-22] that also achieve O(√T) regret. The paper correctly cites and differentiates from prior work, particularly noting that their logarithmic regret result is the first for constrained contextual bandits.

Quality is strong: The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful treatment of boundary cases and concentration properties. The authors provide detailed proofs (in supplementary material) for key lemmas and theorems. The paper correctly acknowledges the limitations of their simplified model and carefully analyzes the impact of estimation errors. The technical depth is appropriate for NIPS.

Clarity is excellent: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions of the problem, algorithms, and regret bounds. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the main ideas are presented logically. The authors effectively use the LP approximation as a unifying framework to simplify analysis. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Originality is high: To the best of our knowledge, this is the first paper to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under reasonable assumptions. The ALP approximation technique, while inspired by prior work on LP for DP [17, 19], is novel in its application to constrained contextual bandits and its ability to tolerate estimation errors. The combination with UCB for unknown statistics is a significant contribution.

Significance is strong: Budget constraints are prevalent in real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing, making this problem highly relevant. The logarithmic regret bound represents a substantial improvement over prior work (O(√T) vs. O(log T)), which could enable more efficient resource allocation in practical settings. The insights about decoupling information acquisition from decision making could inform future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

Strengths include the rigorous theoretical analysis, clear presentation of the novel ALP approximation, and the demonstration of how to combine exploration and exploitation in constrained settings. The authors carefully distinguish their work from prior literature and acknowledge limitations.

Weaknesses include the simplified model (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint), which may limit direct applicability to some real-world scenarios. The boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T) rather than O(log T) are not fully resolved, though the authors properly acknowledge this limitation. The paper lacks empirical validation (common in theoretical bandit papers, but would strengthen the contribution).

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to a relevant problem with rigorous analysis. It advances the state of the art in contextual bandits by addressing a practical constraint that has been overlooked in prior work. The results are clearly differentiated from previous NIPS papers on related topics, particularly in their logarithmic regret bound.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality at NIPS: it is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims, clearly written, original in its approach to constrained contextual bandits, and significant for advancing the field toward more practical bandit algorithms. The simplifications made are well-justified and lead to meaningful theoretical advances. The work provides a promising foundation for addressing more general constrained contextual bandit problems, which aligns with NIPS's focus on impactful theoretical contributions.
