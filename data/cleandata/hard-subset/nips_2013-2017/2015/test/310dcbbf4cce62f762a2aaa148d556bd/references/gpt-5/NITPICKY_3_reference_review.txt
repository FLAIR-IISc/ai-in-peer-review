Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with both a time horizon T and a hard budget B (with B/T = ρ), focusing on finite discrete contexts and actions, initially unit costs, i.i.d. contexts, rewards in [0,1]. The main technical idea is to approximate the dynamic programming oracle by a linear program that replaces the hard budget with an average budget constraint. A static LP yields a simple per-round upper bound v(ρ) and total benchmark Û(T,B)=Tv(ρ). Building on this, the Adaptive LP (ALP) adjusts the LP to the instantaneous average remaining budget bτ/τ, and is shown to achieve O(1) regret (non-boundary ρ ≠ qj) and O(√T) regret on boundary cases (ρ = qj). For unknown rewards, combining UCB with ALP (UCB-ALP) yields O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on boundaries. Extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates) and heterogeneous costs are discussed; for the latter, ALP generalizes and an ε-first approach is suggested.

Relative to prior work such as BwK/RCB (e.g., [17], [20]–[22]), which typically obtain O(√T) regret in more general settings, the paper leverages stronger structural assumptions (finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, known context distribution) to obtain logarithmic regret, and claims to be first to achieve log regret in constrained contextual bandits under these assumptions.

Strengths:
- Clear and appealing idea: turning a hard knapsack over time into an adaptive average-constraint LP; the bτ/τ control is intuitive and computationally cheap.
- The O(1) regret for ALP (known statistics) in non-boundary regimes is strong and insightful; mapping the remaining budget dynamics to (a form of) sampling without replacement and leveraging concentration is elegant.
- UCB-ALP is simple, scalable (per-round O(J log J) for sorting contexts), and the instance-dependent, gap-based flavor is sensible.
- The paper situates itself w.r.t. BwK/RCB and explains why an adaptive constraint seems critical to improving beyond O(√T) when contexts arrive randomly.

Weaknesses and concerns:
Technical soundness/details:
- Several key claims are only sketched and rely on nontrivial steps that are not justified in the main text:
  - The assertion that Lemma 2 (hypergeometric-like concentration of bτ) “still holds” under UCB-ALP is questionable: when rankings are incorrect, the total action probability ∑j p̂j(b/τ)πj need not equal b/τ, so the “consume with probability b/τ” martingale property breaks. If the lemma is replaced by an approximate statement, the deviation must be quantified, and its contribution to regret must be accounted for explicitly.
  - The bound E[T(s)] = O(JK log T) for context-ranking errors requires delicate control of cross-context UCB misorderings; the main text gives no formal argument or gap-dependent constants.
- The log-regret bound necessarily depends on gaps:
  - Within contexts: Δj,k = u∗j − uj,k.
  - Across contexts: Δctx between u∗j around the threshold j̃(ρ).
  The theorem statements omit explicit dependence on these, which obscures the regime where the guarantees are meaningful (e.g., very small Δctx near boundary).
- ALP O(1) regret bounds have typos and appear dimensionally inconsistent:
  - “RALP(T,B) ≤ u∗1 − u∗J − e−2δ2” is nonsensical as written (subtracting an exponential). Please provide a correct, explicit constant and its dependence on δ and gaps.
  - Similarly, “Θ(o) √T” is unclear; likely Θ(√T)? Please fix and define constants.
- Exploration under limited budget and rare contexts: Because the algorithm only explores a context j on rounds when ALP chooses to act under j, contexts below the current threshold may be starved, yet may become relevant due to fluctuations in bτ. A formal lower bound on the expected number of pulls Cj,k(T) for all action-context pairs that can affect decisions is needed, especially when ρ is small and πj is small.
- Unknown context distribution: The main text only sketches a truncated EALP and mentions a coupling/bounded-differences argument. There is no precise theorem for UCB-EALP (rates, additional terms due to estimation error). This is a significant gap given the prominence of the unknown-π setting.
- Heterogeneous costs: For known statistics, the generalization of ALP is plausible. For unknown statistics, proposing ε-first is unsatisfying: it is known to be suboptimal in many bandit settings compared to optimistic methods; no log-regret bound is stated. This weakens the “logarithmic regret” headline in the general cost case.

Clarity/presentation:
- Numerous notational and formatting issues that hinder readability:
  - Equation (4): the third case is blank; presumably pj=0 for j>j̃(ρ)+1.
  - Algorithm 1: “û∗j (t)← û∗j,k∗j (t)(t)” duplicates (t); unclear.
  - π̂j(t) definition is missing the factor 1/t formatting (“1t”).
  - In several places sums and indices are malformed (e.g., qj = ∑j j′=1 πj′ without proper notation).
  - Theorems 1–2 contain typos as noted above; please restate cleanly with all constants and conditions.
- Citations are incomplete (many entries have missing years/venues/pages); some attributions are odd (e.g., using [24] to justify a smaller UCB coefficient).
- The claim “only requires the ordering of the expected rewards” should explicitly discuss tie handling, both within a context (argmax uniqueness) and across contexts (u∗1 > … > u∗J). The current assumptions are strong; please clarify how the algorithms and proofs adapt to ties.

Significance/originality:
- The adaptive-LP idea and the O(1)/O(log T) guarantees in this specific finite, unit-cost, i.i.d.-context setting are interesting. However, the novelty is somewhat limited by the strong assumptions and by the connection to BwK/RCB relaxations; the paper’s primary technical novelty lies in the concentration analysis of bτ and its use in regret decomposition.
- The “first to achieve log regret” claim should be carefully scoped to the finite discrete context/action, fixed cost, known π setting.

Empirics:
- There are no experiments in the main text. Given the practical motivation (crowdsourcing, clinical trials), even small-scale simulations would help illustrate constants, boundary vs. non-boundary behavior, sensitivity to ρ, and comparisons to BwK/RCB baselines.

Pros:
- Simple, computationally light algorithmic framework.
- Strong guarantees in the known-statistics case; plausible log-regret in unknown-statistics case.
- Conceptually clean LP relaxation and adaptive budgeting idea; potentially useful beyond this setting.

Cons:
- Technical gaps/omissions in key lemmas and regret decomposition; missing gap-dependent constants.
- Overreliance on strong simplifying assumptions; tie handling unclear.
- Numerous typos/notation/citation issues.
- Weak treatment of unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.
- No experimental validation.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The core ideas are promising, but the current version has too many technical and presentation issues. With corrected and fully detailed proofs (especially the UCB-ALP concentration of bτ), explicit gap-dependent bounds, clarified assumptions and tie handling, and at least basic experiments, this work could be a strong resubmission.

Confidence: High.
