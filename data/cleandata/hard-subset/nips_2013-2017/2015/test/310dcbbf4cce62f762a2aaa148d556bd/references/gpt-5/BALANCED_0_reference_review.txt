Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget and a fixed horizon (constrained contextual bandits). Unlike standard contextual bandits, each action consumes budget; the process terminates when either time or budget is exhausted. The authors target the proportional regime (B/T → ρ ∈ (0,1)) and finite, discrete contexts and actions.

With known statistics, they approximate the dynamic-programming oracle via a linear program (LP) that relaxes the hard budget to an average budget. They first use a static LP to define a tractable upper bound Û(T,B)=Tv(ρ) on the oracle’s performance and then propose an Adaptive LP (ALP) that, at each round with remaining budget b and remaining time τ, solves the LP with average budget b/τ. A key observation is that under ALP the budget consumption in each round is exactly b/τ, inducing a “sampling-without-replacement” dynamics for the remaining budget; this yields concentration (hypergeometric) of bτ/τ around ρ. They prove ALP attains O(1) regret w.r.t. the optimal oracle except for “boundary” cases where ρ coincides with a cumulative context probability qj, in which case regret is O(√T).

With unknown rewards, they combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP): UCB estimates of all context–action expected rewards produce a ranking that ALP needs. They prove instance-dependent RUCB-ALP(T,B)=O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T+JK log T) in boundary cases. They also discuss extensions to unknown context distributions (empirical estimates with a truncation trick used in the analysis) and to heterogeneous costs (solve an LP over all context–action pairs and propose an ε-first variant).

This work connects to Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB): prior general algorithms (e.g., Mixture Elimination, primal-dual BwK) achieve O(√T) regret in more general settings, often with policies chosen from a finite set. Here, by restricting to finite contexts/actions and leveraging an adaptive LP that tracks remaining budget, the paper improves to O(log T) regret (non-boundary), which to my knowledge is new in this constrained contextual setting with stochastic contexts.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The LP upper bound and the adaptive LP design are sensible. The “sampling without replacement” view and concentration of bτ are elegant and drive the O(1) gap in the known-statistics case. The UCB-ALP regret decomposition into action-ranking and context-threshold errors is standard yet carefully adapted to the constrained setting. However, some proofs are deferred; key ideas are sketched convincingly.
- Rigor of claims: The paper sometimes blurs whether regret is measured vs Û or the true oracle. Since Û ≥ U*, showing Û − UALP = O(1) implies U* − UALP = O(1), but it would help to state this explicitly in Theorem 1. Boundary-case lower bounds are not provided; it remains unclear whether o(√T) is possible there.
- Completeness: Empirical validation is absent; while theory-focused papers are fine, small simulations could substantiate constant factors, behavior near boundaries, and robustness to misspecification.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Still, there are typos/missing pieces in equations (e.g., the piecewise definition of pj(ρ) in Eq. (4) appears truncated; “Θ(o) √T” likely means Θ(√T)). Assumptions (unique best action per context; strict ordering u*1>…>u*J) are stated but their removal/impact could be discussed more. The role of the upper bound Û in the regret definitions should be clarified.

Originality
The adaptive LP with budget-ratio tracking and the hypergeometric analysis of remaining budget are, to my knowledge, novel in this literature. Achieving O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits (non-boundary) is a meaningful advance relative to O(√T) from BwK/RCB in more general settings.

Significance
The setting is practically motivated (budgets in recommendation, trials, procurement). The results give a clean, computationally light algorithm (thresholding) with strong guarantees under common assumptions (finite contexts/actions, i.i.d. stochastic contexts, single budget). The impact is moderated by the restrictive assumptions (unit costs, known π for main results, single resource) and by the boundary caveat.

Pros
- Clear conceptual contribution: decoupling estimation from constrained decision-making via adaptive LP; only reward ranking is needed.
- Strong, instance-dependent regret: O(1) gap (known stats) and O(JK log T) (unknown stats) in non-boundary cases.
- Elegant analysis using “sampling without replacement” to control remaining budget fluctuations.
- Extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs are outlined.

Cons
- No experiments; constants and practical performance unknown.
- Several assumptions limit scope: finite i.i.d. contexts, single hard budget, unit costs for main results, known horizon, unique best actions.
- Boundary cases remain with O(√T) regret and no lower bound; unclear if this is inherent.
- Some clarity/notation issues and reliance on supplementary material for core proofs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the regret benchmark throughout (explicitly state that ALP’s O(1) is w.r.t. the true oracle via Û ≥ U*).
- Provide instance-dependent lower bounds (at least matching Ω(JK log T) for identification) and discuss whether o(√T) is impossible in boundary cases.
- Add experiments comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to BwK/RCB-style primal-dual or Mixture Elimination baselines under finite contexts, showing budget trajectories and performance near/at boundaries.
- Mitigate boundary behavior: consider adding a small slack or randomized “jitter” to the target budget ratio to avoid being exactly at qj, and analyze its effect.
- Discuss robustness: unknown T, misspecified π, non-i.i.d. contexts (e.g., Markov), and multiple resource constraints; outline how adaptive LP might extend via primal-dual updates.
- Fix typos and complete Eq. (4); define all constants in Theorem 1 precisely.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a neat, computationally simple idea with solid theoretical guarantees and a novel analytical technique. While the scope is restricted and empirical validation is missing, the contribution is clear and relevant to NeurIPS. Addressing the clarity issues and adding experiments would strengthen the submission further.
