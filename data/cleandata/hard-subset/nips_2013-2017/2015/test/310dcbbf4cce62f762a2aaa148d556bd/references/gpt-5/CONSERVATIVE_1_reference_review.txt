Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single hard budget and finite horizon. Contexts are i.i.d. from a finite set, rewards are bounded, and (first) unit costs are assumed. The key idea is to approximate the dynamic programming (DP) oracle by a linear program (LP) that enforces an average budget constraint. Building on the static LP, the authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that uses the current average remaining budget bτ/τ to decide which contexts to act on. With known statistics, ALP is shown to be within O(1) regret of the oracle for non-boundary instances (and O(√T) on “boundary” instances where the average budget ρ coincides with a cumulative context probability). For unknown rewards, they combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) and prove O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on boundary cases. Extensions to unknown context distributions (empirical estimates with a truncated update to control dependence) and to heterogeneous costs (LP over all context-action pairs; an ε-first variant under unknown rewards) are discussed at a high level. A notable technical device is the mapping of the budget evolution under ALP to “sampling without replacement,” yielding hypergeometric concentration of the remaining budget.

Strengths
- Clear, simple algorithmic framework: the adaptive LP relaxation is computationally lightweight and natural for a single hard budget.
- Technical insight: the hypergeometric concentration for remaining budget under ALP/UCB-ALP is elegant and seems broadly useful.
- Theoretical guarantees: logarithmic regret (up to JK factors) under unknown rewards for non-boundary instances is stronger than the O(√T) bounds in more general resourceful/contextual settings.
- The regret decomposition separating action-ranking and context-ranking errors is clean and may inform further analyses.

Weaknesses
- Heavy restrictions: finite discrete contexts, i.i.d. arrivals, fixed known (unit) costs (later relaxed), a single budget, and known context distribution (later estimated). These assumptions limit applicability. In particular, known π and i.i.d. contexts are strong; many applications have nonstationarity or Markovian contexts.
- Boundary and uniqueness assumptions: the main results assume unique best actions per context and a strict ordering u∗1 > … > u∗J, with “boundary” instances excluded as measure-zero. In practice, small gaps or ties are common; constants in the O(1) bound depend on δ (distance to boundary) and can be arbitrarily large as ρ approaches a boundary. The robustness of ALP/UCB-ALP to near-boundary regimes is not convincingly quantified in the main text.
- Limited novelty relative to established methods: the combination “UCB + LP relaxation” feels incremental. LP relaxations and primal-dual style resource-aware bandit methods are well known (e.g., Bandits with Knapsacks; Agrawal & Devanur’s primal-dual UCB variants). The chief novelty here is the adaptive use of remaining-budget ratio and its analysis; however, the setting is significantly simplified compared to prior work that already handles broader models with O(√T) regret.
- Empirical validation is missing. The paper makes claims about computational efficiency and practical performance, but no experiments compare ALP/UCB-ALP against Mixture Elimination [17], BwK/primal-dual, Thompson Sampling with budgets, or recent contextual knapsack methods. For an applied bandit problem, this is a notable gap.
- Extensions are sketchy: unknown π is handled via a truncated empirical estimate, but concrete algorithms, parameter choices (when to freeze), and finite-time constants are deferred. The heterogeneous-cost case proposes an ε-first variant, yet precise guarantees and comparisons are absent.

Quality
The theoretical arguments are plausible and build on standard tools (UCB concentration, gap-dependent bounds) plus the new budget concentration lemmas. However, the main paper provides only sketches; critical proofs and precise constants are deferred, and the dependence on gaps and δ in the O(1) term should be made explicit. The reliance on strict-ordering and “non-boundary” assumptions weakens the practical import. Without experiments, claims of efficiency and robustness are untested.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. Some notation/equation formatting is rough (e.g., Eq. (4) cases). The assumptions (uniqueness, strict ordering) are introduced “for ease of exposition,” but it is unclear which results actually depend on them, versus merely simplifying proofs. The LP for heterogeneous costs should be fully written in the main text (variables, constraints), and the algorithmic complexity per round should be stated.

Originality
Moderate. Adaptive LP with remaining-budget ratio and the hypergeometric analysis are nice twists, but the overall approach (LP relaxation + UCB) is conventional. The result of O(log T) regret under the restricted model is interesting, but not surprising given the discreteness and gap assumptions.

Significance
Mixed. The theoretical bounds improve over O(√T) guarantees in more general models, but under restrictive assumptions. Without empirical evidence or applications demonstrating a tangible gain, the impact seems limited. The technical lemmas on budget concentration could be useful beyond this paper.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide explicit constants in the O(1) regret for ALP as a function of δ and gaps? How does performance degrade as ρ approaches a boundary?
- Do your results hold without the strict global ordering u∗1 > … > u∗J and unique best actions? Please state the general version precisely.
- Can you compare to primal-dual BwK-style algorithms on synthetic and real data? How large are the observed constants?
- For unknown π, how do you choose the truncation time; what is the resulting regret bound (with constants) and its dependence on J, gaps, and ρ?
- In the heterogeneous-cost case, what is the regret guarantee for the ε-first ALP under unknown rewards?

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, analyzable algorithm with interesting concentration technique.
- Improves regret rate (to logarithmic) in a constrained contextual setting under specific assumptions.
- Potentially useful insights for designing adaptive budget policies.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong simplifying assumptions and sensitivity to boundary/gap conditions.
- Lack of empirical validation and limited practical evidence.
- Incremental feel relative to established LP/primal-dual bandit frameworks.
- Many key technical details relegated to the supplement; unclear robustness.

Recommendation
Reject (leaning). The theory is solid within a narrow model and includes a nice technical idea, but the contribution feels incremental without experiments or broader scope, and the sensitivity to boundary conditions/assumptions is under-addressed. I encourage the authors to add empirical comparisons, present full heterogeneous-cost results, and strengthen robustness claims.
