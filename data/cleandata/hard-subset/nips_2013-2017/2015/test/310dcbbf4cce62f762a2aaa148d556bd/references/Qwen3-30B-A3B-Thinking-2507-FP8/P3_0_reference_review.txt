

# Peer Review: "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the important problem of constrained contextual bandits, where an agent must maximize reward while respecting a budget constraint. The authors make a significant contribution by proposing the first algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret (O(log T)) in non-boundary cases, whereas previous work on constrained bandits typically achieved O(√T) regret. The paper builds on foundational work in contextual bandits (e.g., [1-7]) and constrained bandits (e.g., [11-16]), but identifies a critical gap: existing constrained bandit algorithms don't handle observable contexts effectively.

The key insight is using linear programming to approximate the optimal policy (oracle), which balances immediate and long-term rewards under budget constraints. The authors introduce Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for known statistics and UCB-ALP for unknown statistics, achieving O(1) and O(JK log T) regret respectively, except for boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T). This approach differs fundamentally from prior work that typically uses fixed budget constraints or applies non-contextual techniques to contextual settings.

## Quality Assessment

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs of regret bounds and careful handling of boundary cases. The authors establish an important upper bound using static LP, then show how the adaptive version (ALP) achieves near-optimality by tracking the average remaining budget. The key Lemma 2 on the concentration of the remaining budget is particularly elegant and well-justified.

The paper properly acknowledges limitations: the simplifying assumptions (discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget) are justified as reasonable for many applications (clinical trials, rate selection in wireless networks), and the boundary cases are clearly identified as a limitation of the current approach. The analysis of UCB-ALP carefully separates regret sources and bounds them appropriately.

The paper's completeness is strong: it covers the known-statistics case (Sections 3-4), extends to unknown context distributions (Section 5), and handles heterogeneous costs (Section 6). The supplementary materials appear to contain the necessary details for the more challenging generalizations.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly defined, the approach is logically presented, and the key insights are effectively communicated. The distinction between non-boundary and boundary cases is particularly well-handled, with clear explanations of why the boundary cases behave differently.

The notation is consistent throughout, and the theorems are well-motivated. The sketch of the proof for Theorem 1 is helpful, as is the detailed explanation of how the ALP algorithm maps to "sampling without replacement" (Lemma 2). The authors effectively use the static LP problem as a benchmark for regret analysis, which simplifies the complexity of the analysis.

## Originality Assessment

This paper makes a substantial original contribution. While constrained bandits have been studied before (e.g., [11-16]), and contextual bandits have been studied extensively (e.g., [1-7]), the combination of both with budget constraints has been largely unaddressed. The authors' key innovation—using linear programming to approximate the optimal policy for constrained contextual bandits—is novel and highly effective.

The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work. For example, the authors note that [20] achieves O(√T) regret in a static context setting, while their UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) by using an adaptive budget constraint. The comparison with [17] is also clear: they address the computational efficiency gap that [17] leaves open.

## Significance Assessment

The significance of this work is high. Constrained contextual bandits model important real-world scenarios like clinical trials (where treatment costs are high and budget is limited) and crowdsourcing (where the number of workers is constrained). The paper provides the first algorithm that achieves logarithmic regret in this setting, which is the optimal regret bound in contextual bandit literature.

The authors' approach of using LP to approximate the oracle policy is not only theoretically sound but also computationally efficient, making it practically relevant. The insights about how to decouple information acquisition from decision making in constrained bandits could inform future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

## Strengths

1. First to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits (O(log T) in non-boundary cases)
2. Elegant theoretical insight: using LP to approximate the oracle policy
3. Clear distinction between non-boundary and boundary cases with appropriate regret bounds
4. Well-justified simplifying assumptions that are reasonable for many applications
5. Strong theoretical analysis with careful handling of regret sources

## Weaknesses

1. The simplifying assumptions (discrete contexts, fixed costs) limit applicability to some real-world scenarios. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of how to extend to continuous contexts or variable costs would strengthen the paper.

2. The paper mentions two recent papers [21, 22] that also address dynamic contexts, but doesn't fully clarify how their approach differs. A more detailed comparison would help position their contribution.

3. The paper lacks empirical validation. While theoretical results are strong, some numerical experiments would help demonstrate the practical performance of their algorithms.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Solves an important problem with significant real-world applications
- First to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits
- Novel approach using LP approximation of the oracle policy
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear regret bounds
- Well-written and well-organized paper that clearly distinguishes its contribution

**Con:**
- Makes simplifying assumptions that limit general applicability
- Boundary cases have higher regret (O(√T)) rather than O(log T)
- Lacks empirical validation of the proposed algorithms
- Comparison with very recent work [21, 22] could be more detailed

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to bandit algorithms. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is novel and elegant, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The limitations are clearly acknowledged, and the paper makes a clear step toward solving an important open problem in constrained bandits.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of empirical validation, but given the theoretical focus and the fact that the paper is for NIPS (which prioritizes theoretical contributions), this is not a critical flaw. The authors' approach represents a substantial advance over previous work, and their insights about using LP to approximate the oracle policy could have broader implications for constrained bandit problems.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. This paper makes a clear contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits and provides a foundation for future work on more general constrained bandit problems.
