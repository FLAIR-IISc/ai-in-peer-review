Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget constraint over a finite horizon. Focusing on finite discrete contexts, fixed (unit) costs, and a single budget, the authors propose: (i) an LP relaxation (Static LP) that yields an upper bound Û(T,B) to the oracle’s value U*(T,B); (ii) an adaptive LP policy (ALP) that re-solves the LP with the average remaining budget bτ/τ, shown to have O(1) regret relative to the oracle except at certain “boundary” cases (when ρ = B/T equals cumulative context probabilities qj), where regret becomes O(√T); (iii) UCB-ALP, which uses UCB estimates in place of true rewards and attains O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. Extensions to unknown context distribution (via empirical estimates with a truncated update) and heterogeneous costs are outlined; proofs are mostly deferred to the supplement.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and practically motivated variant of contextual bandits with hard budget constraints.
- Clear structural idea: approximate a DP oracle by a per-round LP constrained by the adaptive average remaining budget; the “threshold” nature of the LP is well-exploited.
- Theoretical guarantees: ALP achieves constant regret in non-boundary cases; UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) in non-boundary cases; both are computationally simple for unit-cost settings.
- Comparison baseline defined against the true oracle (not a finite policy class), which is stronger than some prior RCB work.
- The mapping of remaining budget dynamics to a concentration argument enables clean bounds.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on restrictive assumptions (finite contexts/actions, unit costs, i.i.d. contexts, known context distribution for main results; unique best actions and strictly ordered context maxima u*j), with fragile behavior in boundary cases.
- The “measure-zero boundary” caveat is not compelling in practice: ρ is typically design-chosen and can easily coincide with qj, yielding O(√T) regret.
- Many technical details and key proofs are deferred; some derivations (e.g., the exact hypergeometric/sampling-without-replacement coupling) are nontrivial and deserve more in-paper justification.
- Limited discussion of lower bounds in the presented setting; it remains unclear whether the boundary O(√T) is necessary or an artifact.
- Limited empirical validation (mentioned but not shown here) and no comparative evaluation against established baselines (e.g., BwK, Mixture Elimination) even in regimes where they apply.
- Clarity issues: some formulas are garbled; assumptions and boundary definitions should be more explicit; heterogeneous-cost extension is sketched but not fully developed in main text.

Quality
The core idea is sound: using an LP relaxation with adaptive average remaining budget is a principled way to bypass the DP’s state explosion. The technical path—concentration of bτ around ρτ and reduction of regret to ranking errors plus deviations of bτ/τ—is standard but apt. However:
- The reliance on exact uniqueness and strict ordering of u*j, and on ρ not matching any qj, is strong. Near-ties (or discretization/integer budgets) are common and can degrade behavior; the paper’s framing as “zero Lebesgue measure” underplays practical significance.
- The coupling that yields hypergeometric concentration for bτ is a delicate point; the text states “consumes one unit of budget with probability b/τ,” but this is an unconditional probability over random contexts under the ALP policy. A rigorous coupling argument (and whether independence or martingale concentration is used) should be visible in the main paper.
- For unknown context distributions, the truncated empirical scheme is plausible, but details (stopping time choice, parameter dependence, and how it affects constants) are missing from the main text.
- The heterogeneous-cost treatment is promising but incomplete; the ε-first approach for unknown statistics is known to be suboptimal vs UCB-type methods, and guarantees are not clearly stated in the main paper.

Clarity
The exposition is mostly clear at a high level, but several formulas are malformed (e.g., Eq. (4)’s cases) and some definitions are hard to parse without the supplement. The notion of “boundary cases,” the assumptions (uniqueness of k*j, strict order u*1>…>u*J), and the exact regret expressions should be clearly stated and discussed up front. Pseudocode for ALP/UCB-ALP is helpful; complexity per round (especially in the heterogeneous-cost LP) could be clarified.

Originality
Relative to RCB and BwK literature, the paper’s main novelty is achieving logarithmic regret (outside boundaries) in constrained contextual bandits by an adaptive LP relaxation benchmarked to the true oracle, under simplified but practical assumptions. Prior works typically obtain O(√T) regret or compare to a finite policy class. However, the approach builds on well-known tools (LP relaxations, UCB, concentration), and the novelty lies in their combination and analysis in this specific constrained contextual setting. The claim “first to show logarithmic regret” should be carefully scoped to the assumptions.

Significance
The result is potentially useful for applications with known i.i.d. context distributions and unit/known costs, where an easily-implemented policy with near-oracle performance is valuable. However, the practical impact is tempered by:
- Fragility at boundaries and near-ties, which may be non-rare in practice.
- Limited generality (finite contexts/actions, i.i.d. contexts).
- Lack of empirical comparisons with established baselines.
That said, the structural insights (adaptive LP, decomposition of regret sources, concentration of remaining budget) could influence subsequent work.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the boundary issue be mitigated by smoothing the budget ratio (e.g., randomized dithering of ρ) to restore logarithmic regret, or by a refined tie-breaking scheme?
- Please clarify the coupling or martingale argument that yields the hypergeometric distribution (or equivalent concentration) of bτ under ALP/UCB-ALP.
- For unknown context distributions, specify the truncation schedule and its parameter dependence; can one obtain full UCB-ALP with online π̂j(t) without truncation?
- Provide lower bounds in this discrete, unit-cost, known-π setting to justify the boundary √T barrier, or discuss whether it is an analysis artifact.
- Include experimental comparisons against RCB/Mixture Elimination and BwK-style baselines in regimes where they apply, and stress-test near-boundary cases.
- Clean up notation and equations (especially Eq. (4)) and state all assumptions explicitly.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful approach with solid high-level ideas, but relies on strong assumptions, has fragile boundary behavior, and lacks sufficient in-paper rigor and empirical validation against established baselines. Strengthening the boundary-case treatment, moving key proof ideas into the main text, and adding careful experiments would significantly improve the paper.
