This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon T and a budget B. The process terminates when either constraint is met. The authors focus on a setting with a finite set of discrete contexts, known fixed costs for each action, and a single budget. The objective is to design computationally efficient algorithms with low regret compared to the optimal oracle policy, which has full knowledge of the system statistics (reward distributions and context arrival probabilities).

The core challenge is that the oracle policy is a complex dynamic program (DP), making it intractable to use directly for algorithm design and regret analysis. The authors' main contribution is a novel methodology to overcome this. First, for the case with known statistics, they propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. ALP approximates the DP at each step by solving a simple LP that uses the *current* ratio of remaining budget to remaining time. This adaptive approximation is shown to be nearly optimal, achieving O(1) regret except for a specific set of "boundary" parameter settings (where the budget-to-time ratio aligns perfectly with cumulative context probabilities), where it achieves O(sqrt(T)) regret.

Building on this insight, for the more practical case of unknown reward distributions, the authors propose the UCB-ALP algorithm. This algorithm combines the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method for learning rewards with the ALP policy for decision-making. The key observation is that ALP's performance depends primarily on the correct *ordering* of expected rewards, not their exact values, making it robust to the estimation errors inherent in UCB. The paper provides a rigorous proof that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) in boundary cases. This is a significant improvement over the typical O(sqrt(T)) regret bounds for related problems. The paper also discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper presents the first logarithmic regret bound for a contextual bandit problem with budget and time constraints. This is a substantial theoretical advance over prior work (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., 2017; Agrawal et al., 2015), which established O(sqrt(T)) regret in more general settings. The problem is well-motivated and has clear connections to practical applications.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical approach is elegant and sound. The idea of using an adaptive LP as a tractable and near-optimal surrogate for the intractable DP oracle is the key innovation. The regret analysis is non-trivial and well-executed. In particular, the analysis of the ALP algorithm, which connects the evolution of the remaining budget to sampling without replacement and uses concentration bounds for the hypergeometric distribution, is clever and insightful. The decomposition of the UCB-ALP regret into components arising from action-ranking errors and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation errors provides a clear and effective proof strategy.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It builds its case logically, starting with the simplified known-statistics case to introduce the core ALP idea, before moving to the main UCB-ALP algorithm for the unknown-statistics setting. The proof sketches provide clear intuition for the main analytical steps.

4.  **Key Insight:** A major conceptual contribution is the effective decoupling of exploration/learning from planning/decision-making. The ALP policy is designed to be robust to a certain level of estimation error, requiring only correct rankings. This allows the UCB exploration component to operate effectively without being overly confounded by the complex state-dependent decisions imposed by the budget constraint. This principle could be valuable for other online learning problems with complex constraints.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The logarithmic regret bound is achieved under a set of simplifying assumptions: finite discrete contexts, known context distribution (for the main result), and fixed, known costs. While these assumptions are well-justified to isolate the core contribution, they limit the direct applicability of the result. The proposed extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are important but are largely deferred to the supplementary material, making it difficult to fully evaluate their rigor and implications from the main paper alone.

2.  **Boundary Cases:** The analysis carves out "boundary cases" where regret degrades to O(sqrt(T)). While the authors correctly argue these form a set of measure zero, the behavior of the algorithm *near* these boundaries is not discussed. In practice, parameters may be close to, but not exactly on, a boundary. It is unclear if the constant in the O(log T) regret bound grows excessively as the budget ratio `ρ = B/T` approaches a boundary `q_j`, which could limit the practical relevance of the logarithmic bound.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, an empirical evaluation would have been valuable. A comparison against relevant baselines, such as the algorithm from RCB [17] or Agrawal et al. [20], on an instance that fits this paper's setting would help contextualize the practical impact of the improved theoretical regret bound.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion on the behavior of the regret bound as the budget ratio `ρ` approaches a boundary. Does the constant in the `O(log T)` term depend on `δ = min |ρ - q_j|`, and if so, how? This would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's performance.
*   While space is limited, including a more detailed summary of the results for heterogeneous costs in the main paper (perhaps in an appendix) would make the claims of generality more concrete. The current description is quite high-level.
*   For the final version, I would strongly encourage the authors to add an experimental section. Even a simple synthetic experiment demonstrating the logarithmic vs. square-root regret scaling in non-boundary vs. boundary cases, and comparing against a baseline that uses a static LP, would significantly enhance the paper's impact.

### Summary of the Review

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the study of contextual bandits. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is novel, and the achievement of O(log T) regret is a clear advance for this problem class. The analysis is rigorous and based on the elegant idea of using an adaptive LP to approximate the optimal policy. The main weaknesses are the reliance on a simplified model and the absence of empirical validation. However, the theoretical novelty and significance are strong enough to warrant publication. The paper is well-written, and its ideas are likely to influence future work on bandits with constraints.

**Decision:** Accept.
