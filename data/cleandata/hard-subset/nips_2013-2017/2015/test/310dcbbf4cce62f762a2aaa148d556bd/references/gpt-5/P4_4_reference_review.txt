Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single hard budget constraint and a finite time horizon, focusing on the proportional regime B/T → ρ ∈ (0,1). Contexts are i.i.d. from a finite set; actions incur fixed, known costs (unit-cost in the core analysis) and yield bounded stochastic rewards with unknown means. The authors’ key idea is to approximate the dynamic programming oracle with a linear program (LP) that enforces an average budget constraint. They propose:
- A static LP upper bound Û(T,B) = T v(ρ) on the oracle’s expected reward via relaxing the hard budget to an average constraint.
- An Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that, at each round, solves the LP with the current average remaining budget bτ/τ and acts accordingly. For known rewards, ALP achieves O(1) regret except for boundary cases (where ρ equals a cumulative context probability), in which the regret is O(√T).
- For unknown rewards, a UCB-ALP algorithm that plugs standard UCB estimates into ALP, achieving RUCB-ALP(T,B) = O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases.
- Extensions to unknown context distribution via an empirical variant (EALP/UCB-EALP) with analysis for a truncated estimator, and to heterogeneous costs via an LP over all context–action pairs with a single-action-per-context constraint, along with an ε-first learning phase.

Relative to prior work: Bandits with knapsacks (BwK) and resourceful contextual bandits (RCB) achieve O(√T) regret in more general settings; some use regret against a finite policy class. The present work targets a more specialized but practically motivated model (finite contexts, fixed costs, single budget) and establishes logarithmic regret for non-boundary instances with random context arrivals, which, to the best of my knowledge, is not present in prior work that allows dynamic contexts (which obtain O(√T)).

Strengths:
- Clear modeling of constrained contextual bandits with a natural regret notion against the true oracle (not a finite policy set).
- Conceptually simple and computationally efficient algorithms (ALP, UCB-ALP) based on an adaptive LP with a threshold structure; per-round complexity is modest.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) regret for ALP (known rewards) and O(JK log T) for UCB-ALP (unknown rewards) in non-boundary cases, with rigorous handling of budget dynamics via a “sampling without replacement” viewpoint and concentration of the remaining budget.
- Insightful regret decomposition that separates action-ranking errors within contexts from context-ranking errors due to the LP threshold, enabling precise bounds.

Weaknesses:
- Boundary cases incur O(√T) regret; it remains open whether one can improve beyond √T, and the analysis does not provide instance-dependent constants to illuminate when the √T term dominates JK log T.
- Assumptions such as unique best action per context and strict ordering u∗1 > … > u∗J simplify analysis; robustness to ties or near-ties is discussed implicitly via “measure-zero boundaries,” but practical handling (e.g., tie-breaking, perturbations) could be specified more explicitly.
- The unknown context distribution case requires a truncated empirical estimator for analysis; full UCB-EALP without truncation is not analyzed in the main text.
- Limited discussion of empirical validation in the excerpt; numerical experiments would help illustrate finite-sample behavior and robustness.
- Minor clarity issues and typos in theorem statements (e.g., constants and notation around exponential terms and Θ(·)).

Quality:
- The analysis appears technically sound. The LP upper bound is standard and appropriate; ALP’s budget evolution indeed ensures per-round expected consumption equals bτ/τ, yielding hypergeometric-like concentration. The use of UCB to obtain correct rankings with O(log T) samples per pair is standard, and the regret decomposition is appropriate. Proof details are deferred to the supplement; the provided sketches are plausible.
- For heterogeneous costs, the extension to an LP over all context–action pairs with single-action-per-context constraint is natural; the ε-first approach for unknown rewards is standard, though less adaptive than UCB-ALP.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and explains intuition for ALP and UCB-ALP. The threshold structure and the role of bτ/τ are clearly described. Some notation and theorem statements could be cleaned up (e.g., explicit constants in Theorem 1 bounds, resolving minor typos).
- The relation to prior work is adequate, though a brief comparison to online stochastic packing/primal-dual approaches could further contextualize the adaptive LP idea.

Originality:
- The adaptive-LP approach is known broadly, but using it to obtain logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits with random contexts and regret to the true oracle (under finite contexts, fixed costs) is novel. The concentration analysis of the remaining budget under the adaptive policy and careful accounting of context-ranking errors are technically interesting.

Significance:
- Within constrained contextual bandits, the results are significant: simple algorithms with logarithmic regret in non-boundary instances and decoupled learning/control are attractive. The model restrictions limit scope relative to fully general RCB/BwK, but the insights could inform extensions.
- Practical relevance is supported for applications with finite contexts and fixed costs (e.g., clinical trials, rate selection).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Establishes logarithmic regret in a constrained contextual setting with random contexts, improving on O(√T) from more general frameworks.
- Algorithms are simple, computationally efficient, and well-motivated.
- Analysis provides clear insights (adaptive LP, budget concentration) that may generalize.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Boundary-case regret remains √T with no lower bound or improved algorithm; practical prevalence of boundary conditions is not empirically quantified.
- Unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs extensions rely on truncated or ε-first methods; analysis and empirical support are limited in the main text.
- Minor clarity/notation issues and reliance on supplemental proofs.

Suggestions and questions:
- Clarify and correct theorem statements (constants, notation) and explicitly state whether Theorem 1’s regret is against U∗ or Û (it is implied to be against U∗ via Û−UALP).
- Discuss a principled tie-breaking or perturbation scheme to avoid boundary cases (e.g., slight randomization of ρ or π to guarantee non-boundary with high probability).
- Provide experiments to compare ALP/UCB-ALP to BwK/RCB baselines in finite-context settings and to quantify the boundary effect and finite-sample behavior.
- For unknown π, can one analyze continuous updating (no truncation) via self-normalized martingale concentration?
- Comment on possible extensions to linear contextual rewards and multiple resource constraints, and expected regret behavior there.
