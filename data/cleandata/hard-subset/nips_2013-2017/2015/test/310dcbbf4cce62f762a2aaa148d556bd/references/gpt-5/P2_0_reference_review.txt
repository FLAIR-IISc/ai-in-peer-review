Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single budget constraint and a fixed horizon, in the i.i.d. context setting with finite contexts and actions and fixed known costs (unit-cost in the core development). The key idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle (optimal policy with known statistics under a hard budget constraint) by a linear program that enforces only an average budget constraint. The Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy uses the LP solution with the instantaneous average remaining budget bτ/τ, rather than a fixed ρ = B/T. With known statistics, ALP achieves O(1) regret to the oracle in non-boundary cases (ρ not equal to a cumulative context probability qj) and O(√T) regret in boundary cases. For unknown rewards, the paper combines ALP with UCB to form UCB-ALP, and proves RUCB-ALP = O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases, where J is the number of contexts and K the number of actions per context. The paper further sketches extensions to unknown context distribution (using empirical estimates and a truncated update scheme) and to heterogeneous costs (LP over all context-action pairs plus an ε-first exploration stage).

This work relates to Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Mixture Elimination, O(√T) regret and finite policy benchmarks), Bandits with Knapsacks and their contextual/concave extensions (typically O(√T) regret; often static contexts or finite policy sets). The paper claims the first logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits with random contexts under finite discrete models, leveraging an adaptive LP relaxation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The analysis is carefully structured. The main novelty is to show that under ALP, the remaining budget evolves like sampling without replacement, implying hypergeometric concentration of bτ and enabling tight control of the threshold-switching events that cause suboptimality. This yields an O(1) gap to the oracle (except on measure-zero boundaries). For learning, the regret decomposition cleanly separates action-misranking within each context (handled by standard UCB arguments) from context-level threshold errors induced by UCB estimates and bτ fluctuations (controlled using concentration and ranking-error counting).
- Correctness and support: The proofs are mostly deferred, but the arguments presented (concentration of bτ, event partitioning for ranking errors, using UCB sample complexity to ensure correct ordering with high probability) are standard and plausible. One point to clarify is the exact benchmark used in Theorem 2: the sketch first bounds regret versus the LP upper bound Û(T,B); tying this back to oracle regret relies on the O(1) gap between Û and U* via ALP. Making the final bound relative to U* explicit in the main text would improve clarity.
- Scope and assumptions: Results hinge on i.i.d. finite contexts, a single budget, fixed known costs (unit-cost for the core), uniqueness and strict ordering assumptions on per-context best actions and u*j across contexts, and known T and B. Boundary cases degrade to O(√T). The unknown context distribution case requires a truncated empirical update for concentration. These are reasonable for a first step but limit generality.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear problem setup and algorithm descriptions. The “sampling without replacement” coupling is a particularly clear and useful insight.
- Some notation and statements have minor glitches (e.g., formatting typos in Theorem 1 bounds; explicitly state whether Theorem 2’s regret is to U* or Û).
- The heterogeneous-cost and unknown-context extensions are only sketched; more intuition or a small worked example would help.

Originality
- Adaptive LP relaxations are known in RL/RCB, but proving that ALP is within O(1) of the DP oracle in non-boundary cases, and leveraging this to obtain O(log T) regret with UCB under random contexts and a hard budget, is new in this constrained contextual bandit setting.
- The hypergeometric coupling of the remaining budget and the partitioning of context-ranking error events are neat and appear novel in this context.

Significance
- The work provides a computationally simple, practically implementable approach (solve a tiny LP per round and run standard UCB) with strong instance-dependent guarantees. Achieving logarithmic regret in this constrained contextual setting (under finite, i.i.d. contexts and a single resource) improves on the O(√T) state of the art in broader RCB settings.
- The applicability is currently limited by the assumptions (finite i.i.d. contexts, single resource, fixed costs, boundary degradation). Nonetheless, the techniques (adaptive relaxation, budget concentration) are promising and could inform future generalizations.

Pros
- Clear, simple algorithm (ALP and UCB-ALP) with strong, interpretable guarantees.
- Elegant analysis via budget concentration/sampling-without-replacement.
- First O(log T) regret under random contexts and budget constraints in a natural finite setting.
- Computationally light; relies on ordering rather than precise values.

Cons
- Restricted model: finite i.i.d. contexts, single budget, fixed costs, uniqueness/strict ordering assumptions.
- Boundary cases remain at O(√T); no lower bound to show tightness there.
- Unknown context distribution handled via truncated empirical estimates; full analysis deferred.
- Limited empirical evaluation in the main text (though not strictly required for a theory paper).

Questions and suggestions
- Please make explicit whether Theorem 2’s bound is with respect to U* and show how the O(1) ALP–oracle gap composes with the UCB-ALP bound.
- Discuss the computational cost per round (sorting contexts by UCB estimates and solving the simple LP) and scalability with J,K.
- Comment on robustness when the strict ordering assumptions fail (ties in u*j or multiple best actions). Can the analysis be adapted with tie-breaking and margin conditions?
- For the unknown context distribution case, can you bound the bias introduced by freezing the empirical distribution at time t0 as a function of t0?
- Any empirical illustration (even small-scale) comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to BwK/RCB baselines would strengthen the paper.

Recommendation
Accept. Strong theoretical contribution with a simple, implementable algorithm and clear advances over prior work in this constrained contextual setting. While assumptions limit scope and some details are deferred, the core ideas and guarantees are novel and significant.
