

# Review: Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, where both budget and time constraints limit the agent's actions. The authors propose two algorithms: Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for known statistics and UCB-ALP for unknown statistics. They show that ALP achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases), and UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret (except for boundary cases where it achieves O(√T) regret). This work extends previous results on constrained bandits [11-16, 17] by achieving logarithmic regret rather than the previous O(√T) regret, which is a significant theoretical advance. The paper builds on prior work in contextual bandits [1-3, 5-7] and constrained bandits [11-17], while addressing a key gap in the literature: the inability to achieve logarithmic regret in the contextual setting with budget constraints.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, which is superior to previous O(√T) results. The key insight of approximating the oracle with linear programming (ALP) is novel and well-motivated.

2. **Rigorous Analysis**: The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis with carefully constructed lemmas and theorems. The analysis of the ALP algorithm's regret, particularly Lemma 2 showing the concentration properties of the remaining budget, is particularly strong.

3. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The constrained contextual bandit problem is well-defined, with clear explanations of the budget constraint, time horizon, and the oracle benchmark. The distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases is well-justified.

4. **Practical Insights**: The paper provides valuable insights into how to decouple information acquisition from decision making in constrained bandit problems, which could be applicable to other constrained learning settings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Evaluation**: The paper lacks any empirical results demonstrating the practical performance of the proposed algorithms. Given the computational efficiency claims, empirical validation would strengthen the paper significantly.

2. **Simplified Assumptions**: While the paper justifies the assumptions of finite contexts, fixed costs, and single budget constraint, the practical relevance of these simplifications in real-world applications (e.g., clinical trials, crowdsourcing) could be better discussed. How do these assumptions limit the applicability of the results?

3. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper mentions two recent papers [21, 22] that extend [20] to dynamic contexts, but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of their results with the current work. A more thorough comparison would help situate the contribution more precisely.

4. **Boundary Cases Treatment**: While the paper acknowledges that boundary cases lead to O(√T) regret, it doesn't provide sufficient intuition about how common these boundary cases are in practice or how they might be handled.

5. **Supplementary Material Dependency**: Key generalizations to heterogeneous costs and unknown context distribution are relegated to supplementary material, which may raise concerns about completeness.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add Empirical Results**: Include experiments comparing UCB-ALP with baseline algorithms (e.g., Mixture Elimination [17] or standard contextual bandit algorithms) on synthetic and/or real-world datasets. This would demonstrate practical effectiveness and validate the computational efficiency claims.

2. **Expand Discussion of Assumptions**: Provide a more detailed discussion of how the simplifying assumptions affect the applicability to real-world scenarios. For example, how might the algorithm be adapted for continuous contexts or multiple budget constraints?

3. **Clarify Boundary Cases**: Provide more intuition about how frequently boundary cases occur in practice and whether there are practical ways to handle them (e.g., randomization to avoid boundary cases).

4. **Strengthen Comparison with Recent Work**: Add a table comparing the key results of this paper with [20-22], highlighting the specific improvements in regret bounds and algorithmic design.

5. **Integrate Key Generalizations**: Move the most important generalizations (e.g., to heterogeneous costs) from supplementary material to the main paper to ensure completeness.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to constrained contextual bandits, providing the first known algorithms achieving logarithmic regret in this setting (except for boundary cases). The analysis is rigorous and well-structured, and the key insights about decoupling information acquisition from decision making are valuable for the field. However, the lack of empirical validation and some over-reliance on simplifying assumptions weaken the paper's overall impact. With the suggested improvements, particularly adding empirical results and strengthening the discussion of assumptions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution but would benefit from empirical validation and a stronger discussion of practical implications. I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would address the weaknesses in the camera-ready version. The theoretical results are strong enough to warrant acceptance if the empirical component can be added or the practical relevance of assumptions is better justified.
