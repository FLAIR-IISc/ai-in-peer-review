### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a resource budget (B). The authors introduce a novel framework to tackle the key challenge of this problem: the intractability of the optimal policy, which would require solving a complex dynamic program (DP). The core idea is to approximate the DP with a much simpler Linear Program (LP).

First, for the case with known statistics, the authors propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. Instead of using a fixed average budget (B/T), ALP dynamically adjusts its policy based on the current average remaining budget at each step. This simple yet powerful adaptive policy is shown to be nearly optimal, achieving O(1) regret except for a specific set of "boundary" parameter configurations where the regret is O(√T). This analysis provides a strong theoretical foundation and a tractable benchmark for the problem.

Building on this insight, the paper then tackles the more practical setting of unknown rewards by combining the ALP framework with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) approach, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. The authors prove that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases, a significant improvement over the O(√T) regret achieved by prior work in related constrained bandit settings. The paper also discusses extensions to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The theoretical analysis, particularly for the ALP algorithm, is elegant. The key insight of mapping the budget evolution under ALP to a "sampling without replacement" process and leveraging the concentration properties of the hypergeometric distribution is clever and effective. The subsequent analysis for UCB-ALP, which decomposes regret into components due to action-ranking errors and context-ranking errors, is non-trivial and appears sound. The authors are upfront about the limitations of their approach, clearly identifying the boundary cases where performance degrades.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It builds the reader's intuition progressively, starting from the oracle problem, moving to the static LP relaxation, then to the adaptive ALP policy, and finally to the full UCB-ALP algorithm for the bandit setting. This pedagogical structure makes the core, novel ideas very easy to follow.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is its primary strength. The idea of using an *adaptive* linear program to approximate the DP-based oracle is a significant conceptual leap. While LPs have been used to approximate DPs before, the dynamic adaptation to the remaining budget/time ratio is a novel mechanism in this context. This approach elegantly decouples the complex interaction between learning and resource allocation, allowing for the first logarithmic regret bounds in this setting. This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior art (e.g., [17], [20]), which was limited to O(√T) regret and often relied on more complex algorithms or more restrictive benchmarks.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. Achieving logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits is a major theoretical breakthrough. The paper provides not just a new algorithm, but a new analytical framework (ALP) that could inspire further research into other resource-constrained online learning problems. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient and easily implementable, in stark contrast to prior, more complex approaches. This makes the work valuable to both theoreticians and practitioners interested in applications like clinical trials or online advertising with budgets.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty:** The central idea of approximating the intractable DP oracle with a simple, adaptive LP policy (ALP) is a bold and highly original contribution. It represents a new way of thinking about this class of problems.
2.  **Significant Theoretical Advance:** The paper is the first to establish logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits. This is a substantial improvement over the state-of-the-art and resolves an important theoretical question.
3.  **Powerful Analytical Technique:** The analysis of the ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms introduces insightful techniques for handling the interplay between learning, budget depletion, and adaptive decision-making. The decoupling of regret sources is particularly compelling.
4.  **Clarity and Potential for Impact:** The paper is clearly written, and the proposed algorithm is simple and efficient. This combination makes it likely that these ideas will be used and built upon by other researchers.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The main results are derived under the assumption of finite discrete contexts and (initially) known context distributions. While extensions are discussed, this setting is less general than that considered in some prior work (e.g., [17]).
2.  **Existence of Boundary Cases:** The analysis identifies specific parameter settings ("boundary cases") where the regret degrades from O(log T) or O(1) to O(√T). Although these cases have zero Lebesgue measure, they represent a theoretical gap in the performance guarantee.

### Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty of the ALP framework and the significance of achieving the first logarithmic regret bounds for this problem are outstanding. This work represents precisely the kind of bold, conceptually-driven research that pushes the field forward. While the analysis relies on some simplifying assumptions and is limited by the existence of boundary cases, these are minor weaknesses in the face of a major conceptual breakthrough. This paper introduces a powerful new tool for analyzing a challenging and important class of problems and is likely to have a significant impact on future work in bandits and reinforcement learning with constraints.
