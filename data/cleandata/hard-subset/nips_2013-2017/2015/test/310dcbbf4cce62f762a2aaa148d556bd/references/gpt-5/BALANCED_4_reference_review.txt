Summary
The paper studies contextual multi-armed bandits under a hard budget and finite horizon, where each action incurs a known cost and the process ends when budget or time runs out. The authors propose an adaptive linear programming (ALP) relaxation of the dynamic program (DP) oracle: they solve each round a static LP that replaces the hard budget by an average resource constraint set to the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. For known statistics and unit costs, they prove ALP attains O(1) regret relative to an LP-based upper bound Û(T,B) (and hence near the oracle) except for boundary cases (when ρ equals a cumulative context probability), where regret is O(√T). Building on the observation that ALP only needs a correct ordering of context values u∗j, they propose UCB-ALP, which replaces u∗j by upper confidence bounds and solves the same adaptive LP. They show UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They also sketch extensions to unknown context distributions via empirical estimates (with a truncated-update analysis) and to heterogeneous costs via a generalized LP and an ε-first exploration scheme.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits between “resourceful contextual bandits” (RCB) and “bandits with knapsacks” (BwK). Prior work on RCB (e.g., [17]) and BwK (e.g., [12]) provides O(√T) regret guarantees, often for more general settings (infinite contexts, multiple/unknown costs) or with regret measured against a finite policy class. UCB-type methods for convex constraints (e.g., [20–22]) obtain O(√T) regret and typically use fixed per-round budgets. This work restricts to finite contexts/actions and known unit costs, but achieves the stronger O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases by adapting the LP to the average remaining budget and exploiting concentration of the budget process. The key technical novelty is the mapping of the ALP budget dynamics to “sampling without replacement,” yielding hypergeometric concentration that drives the tight regret bounds.

Strengths
- Clear, principled algorithmic idea (adaptive LP with bτ/τ) that is computationally simple (per-round LP reduces to thresholding/sorting) and interpretable.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) gap to Û(T,B) for ALP and O(JK log T) for UCB-ALP in non-boundary cases; clean separation of sources of regret (action-ranking vs. context-ranking vs. budget fluctuation).
- Novel analysis technique: concentration of the remaining budget under ALP/UCB-ALP via a sampling-without-replacement viewpoint (hypergeometric tails), enabling a tight comparison to the LP upper bound.
- Thoughtful discussion of extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs; the structure seems broadly reusable.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope/assumptions: Results hinge on i.i.d. contexts, finite discrete context/action sets, known costs (unit cost for main results), and assume unique best action per context and strictly ordered u∗j across contexts. While the authors claim “for simplicity,” some proofs (LP thresholding and ranking analysis) seem to rely on these uniqueness and strict-order assumptions; please clarify which results survive with ties and how to modify ALP and proofs in the presence of ties.
- Boundary cases: The O(√T) term at ρ = qj is asserted as unavoidable but no lower bound is provided. Is there an instance-dependent lower bound showing Ω(√T) regret is necessary at the boundary? If not, can you argue why standard techniques cannot push below √T here?
- Oracle benchmark: The main theorems compare ALP/UCB-ALP to Û(T,B), and Theorem 1 additionally bridges Û to U∗. For UCB-ALP the analysis is partitioned relative to Û. Please make explicit the final bound with respect to U∗ and isolate any constant gap Û − U∗ to avoid ambiguity about the benchmark.
- Unknown context distribution: The rigorous analysis uses a truncated empirical estimator. This is slightly unnatural algorithmically. Is it possible to establish the same concentration without truncation (e.g., via self-normalized bounds, martingale methods, or adaptive Bernstein/Freedman inequalities), or to quantify the performance loss due to truncation?
- Heterogeneous costs: The move to an ε-first scheme is conservative and may over-explore; can the UCB-ALP paradigm extend to this setting (e.g., UCBs on ηj,k with feasibility handled by the LP) to retain O(log T) in non-boundary cases? Clarify computational complexity when the LP includes all context-action pairs and a per-context action constraint.
- Empirics: The paper mentions simulations but presents no results in the main text. A small empirical section illustrating budget concentration, sensitivity to boundary proximity, and comparisons to BwK/RCB baselines would strengthen the case and aid intuition.

Quality
The technical development appears sound, with careful decomposition of regret and a clean concentration argument for bτ. Many proofs are deferred; given the novelty of the hypergeometric concentration step under adaptivity, having a self-contained proof sketch in the main text would help readers verify key steps. Some notation and typographical issues (e.g., incomplete equations around (4)) should be fixed.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable. Suggestions:
- State assumptions (uniqueness/order, i.i.d. contexts, bounded rewards, known costs) upfront and annotate which theorems require which.
- Make the LP structure explicit for both unit-cost and heterogeneous-cost cases (including the per-context single-action constraint), and spell out the per-round computational complexity.
- Define “boundary cases” prominently with examples and discuss their practical frequency.
- Provide the exact constants in the O(1) bound in Theorem 1 and in the O(JK log T) term in Theorem 2 when possible.

Originality
The adaptive LP idea tailored to average remaining budget and the budget-concentration analysis are, to my knowledge, novel in the constrained contextual bandit literature and provide a crisp path to logarithmic regret under budget constraints in the finite-context setting.

Significance
Achieving logarithmic regret under hard budget constraints for contextual bandits is an impactful contribution, likely to influence subsequent work on resource-constrained online learning and to inform practical systems (e.g., recommendation under spend limits, clinical trials). While the scope is restricted, the techniques may be extensible.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, computationally efficient algorithm with strong guarantees.
- Novel and elegant analysis (budget concentration via sampling-without-replacement).
- Clear improvement over prior O(√T) results in comparable settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Restricted assumptions; weaker treatment of boundary cases.
- Limited empirical validation; some extensions (unknown contexts, heterogeneous costs) are only partially developed.
- Heavy reliance on supplemental material for core proofs.

Recommendation
Accept, provided the authors clarify assumptions, tighten the benchmark presentation w.r.t. the true oracle, add at least a brief empirical illustration, and polish notation and missing equation fragments. The theoretical advance and technique are strong and of clear interest to the NeurIPS community.
