### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), where the process terminates as soon as either constraint is met. The authors aim to design computationally efficient algorithms with low regret in the asymptotic regime where B/T remains constant.

The main challenge identified is that the optimal policy (the oracle) is a solution to a complex dynamic program (DP), making it intractable for both implementation and analysis. The central contribution of this work is to approximate this intractable DP with a much simpler policy derived from Linear Programming (LP). The key idea is to use an *adaptive* LP (ALP) that, at each step, makes a decision based on the *remaining* time and budget. For the case with known reward and context statistics, the authors show that this ALP policy is surprisingly effective, achieving constant regret (O(1)) relative to an LP-based upper bound on the oracle's performance, except for a specific set of "boundary" parameter values where the regret is O(sqrt(T)).

Building on this insight, the authors propose the UCB-ALP algorithm for the more practical setting where expected rewards are unknown (but the context distribution is assumed known). This algorithm integrates the UCB principle for exploration with the ALP framework for decision-making. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) in boundary cases. This is a significant improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) regret achieved by prior work on related constrained contextual bandit problems, such as Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] and Bandits with Knapsacks [20]. The paper also briefly discusses extensions to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

### Strengths

*   **Novelty and Significance of the Main Result:** The paper tackles an important and practical extension of the contextual bandit problem. The primary contribution—achieving logarithmic regret—is a substantial theoretical advance. To my knowledge, this is the first work to establish a logarithmic regret bound for a contextual bandit problem with a hard budget constraint, moving beyond the more common O(sqrt(T)) results in the literature.
*   **Elegant Core Idea:** The proposed Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) framework is simple, intuitive, and powerful. The idea of dynamically adjusting the policy based on the ratio of remaining budget to remaining time (b/τ) is a very natural heuristic, and the authors provide a rigorous analysis showing it is near-optimal. This approach elegantly sidesteps the full complexity of the underlying DP.
*   **Sound and Insightful Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is well-executed. A particularly clever step is mapping the evolution of the remaining budget under the ALP policy to a "sampling without replacement" process, which allows the use of concentration bounds for the hypergeometric distribution (Lemma 2). This provides the key technical tool for proving the O(1) regret of ALP and forms a solid foundation for analyzing the UCB-ALP variant.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The argument is built logically, starting with the simplified known-statistics setting to introduce the ALP concept, before moving to the main UCB-ALP algorithm for the unknown-reward setting. This progression makes the core ideas and their motivation transparent.

### Weaknesses

*   **Strong Assumption of Known Context Distribution:** The main results for UCB-ALP (Section 4) rely on the assumption that the context arrival probabilities (`π_j`) are known. This limits the direct applicability of the proposed algorithm in many real-world scenarios where this information would not be available. While Section 5 proposes a UCB-EALP variant using empirical estimates, the analysis provided is for a "truncated" version and the details are deferred to the supplement. This makes the solution to the more general problem feel less complete.
*   **Brief Treatment of Extensions:** The extensions to unknown context distributions (Section 5) and heterogeneous costs (Section 6) are discussed very briefly. For the heterogeneous cost setting, the structure of the corresponding LP is non-trivial and would have been helpful to include in the main paper. Furthermore, the proposed algorithm for this case is an `ε-First ALP` approach, which contrasts with the UCB-based method used in the main part of the paper. A justification for this different choice of exploration strategy would strengthen the paper. A more detailed treatment of these extensions in the main body would make the paper's contributions feel more comprehensive.
*   **Impact of "Boundary Cases":** The paper distinguishes between non-boundary (O(log T) regret) and boundary (O(sqrt(T)) regret) cases. The authors correctly state that the boundary cases (`ρ = Σ π_j`) constitute a set of zero Lebesgue measure. However, the pre-asymptotic performance near a boundary is a practical concern. The constant in the regret bound for ALP in Theorem 1 appears to depend on `1/δ^2`, where `δ` is the distance to the nearest boundary. This suggests that for `ρ` very close to a boundary, the O(log T) regret may only be realized for an impractically large T. A brief discussion on this aspect would add valuable nuance.

### Suggestions for Improvement

1.  It would significantly strengthen the paper to elaborate on the UCB-EALP algorithm for unknown context distributions in the main text. Specifically, how does the error in estimating `π_j` interact with the UCB estimates for rewards and the concentration properties of the remaining budget? Does this interaction prevent the achievement of logarithmic regret?
2.  For the heterogeneous cost case, please consider including the formulation of the more complex LP in the main paper. This would give the reader a much better understanding of the challenges involved. Also, a brief motivation for choosing an `ε-First` strategy over a UCB-style one would be helpful.
3.  A short discussion on the practical performance when the budget ratio `ρ` is close to a boundary `q_j` would be insightful. Does the regret transition smoothly from O(log T) to O(sqrt(T)), or is there a sharp performance degradation as one approaches a boundary?

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a novel approach and a strong main result. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the scope and presentation of the extensions rather than a fundamental flaw in the core contribution. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature on bandit algorithms.
