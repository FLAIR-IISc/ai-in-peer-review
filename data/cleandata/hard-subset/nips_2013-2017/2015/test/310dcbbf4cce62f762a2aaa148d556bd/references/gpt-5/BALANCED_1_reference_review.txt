Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with both a hard budget B and a horizon T. Contexts are i.i.d. from a finite set; actions incur fixed, known costs; rewards are stochastic in [0,1] with unknown expectations. The benchmark is the oracle policy with full knowledge of rewards and context distribution. The main idea is to approximate the oracle via linear programming that relaxes the hard budget to an average budget. A “static” LP with average budget ρ = B/T yields an upper bound on the oracle’s value; an Adaptive LP (ALP) that plugs in the instantaneous average remaining budget bτ/τ is then proposed for decision making. With known rewards, ALP is shown to be near-optimal: O(1) regret except when ρ equals a context cumulative probability boundary qj, where regret is O(√T). With unknown rewards, UCB-ALP combines UCB estimates with ALP decisions and achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) at boundaries. The analysis hinges on viewing ALP’s budget consumption as sampling without replacement, yielding hypergeometric concentration for the remaining budget and enabling decomposition of regret into ranking and budgeting components.

This work sits between bandits with knapsacks (BwK) and resourceful contextual bandits (RCB). Prior works on BwK and concave rewards/convex constraints (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al. 2013; Agrawal & Devanur 2014/2015; Agrawal, Devanur & Li 2015) typically obtain O(√T) regret, including contextual variants with a finite policy class. RCB (Badanidiyuru, Langford & Slivkins 2014) achieves O(√T) regret relative to a finite policy set but is computationally heavier. This paper restricts to finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, and i.i.d. contexts, and shows logarithmic regret via an adaptive LP relaxation. The use of an adaptive budget ratio bτ/τ, rather than a fixed average budget, appears to be the key to improved regret.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Adaptive LP relaxation that decouples exploration and constrained decision-making while preserving near-optimality against the oracle with known statistics.
- Theoretical guarantees: O(1) regret for ALP (non-boundary) and O(JK log T) for UCB-ALP, with a clear explanation of the boundary pathology. The “sampling without replacement” viewpoint and hypergeometric concentration are elegant and technically useful.
- Practicality: ALP only needs the ordering of u∗j; UCB-ALP is computationally light (sort contexts by UCB and apply a threshold rule) and scales linearly with J,K per round.
- Connections to prior work: Positions results w.r.t. BwK/RCB and explains why earlier approaches stay at O(√T).

Weaknesses
- Assumptions and scope: Finite contexts/actions, i.i.d. context arrivals, fixed known costs, and strict ordering assumptions (unique best action per context, strictly ordered u∗j) limit generality; the boundary set, albeit measure-zero in ρ, is very relevant in discrete problems.
- Boundary behavior: O(√T) regret at ρ = qj is a qualitative degradation. No mitigation strategies are proposed to recover log T near boundaries.
- Gap dependence and constants: The proofs rely on standard UCB arguments with gap-dependent sample complexity; the final O(JK log T) hides instance-dependent constants (∑(u∗j − uj,k)/Δ2). The paper does not state explicit instance-dependent bounds or discuss small-gap regimes.
- Unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs: Core ideas are sketched with details deferred to the supplement; for heterogeneous costs, the switch to an ε-first approach is less satisfying than a fully adaptive UCB-style method and may be budget-inefficient.
- Empirical support: The main text provides little empirical evaluation and no ablations contrasting static LP vs ALP vs UCB-ALP or sensitivity near boundaries.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The LP upper bound and ALP analysis via hypergeometric concentration are convincing. The regret decomposition for UCB-ALP is reasonable; however, explicit constants, gap dependencies, and practical guidance for parameter choices (e.g., exploration coefficient, truncation length for empirical context estimates) are missing in the main paper.

Clarity
Well written and organized, with helpful intuition. Many key proofs and the more general settings are in the supplement; adding a small, worked-through example (e.g., J=3) in the main text would aid understanding. A brief complexity discussion per round (sorting and thresholding) would help.

Originality
The adaptive use of bτ/τ in the LP relaxation and the consequent O(1) vs O(√T) separation (boundary vs non-boundary) seem novel for constrained contextual bandits. The analytical technique leveraging hypergeometric concentration under adaptive policies is a nice addition.

Significance
Achieving O(log T) regret in constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts/actions is an important step that may influence follow-up work on more general constraints. The impact is somewhat limited by the strong assumptions and boundary behavior, but the ideas are broadly useful.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, simple algorithmic idea with strong theoretical guarantees and low computational cost.
- Provides the first log T regret in this constrained contextual setting under finite contexts/actions.
- Techniques (adaptive LP and concentration under adaptive consumption) are likely reusable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical scope limited by assumptions; boundary cases common in discrete settings.
- Missing explicit instance-dependent bounds and empirical validation in the main paper.
- Extensions (unknown context distribution, heterogeneous costs) are not fully developed in the main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit gap-dependent regret bounds and discuss behavior in small-gap regimes.
- Add mitigation for boundary cases (e.g., a vanishing slack in the target budget ratio, randomized buffering of bτ, or smoothing the threshold to avoid sticking at qj), and analyze whether this recovers log T when ρ is near qj.
- Include a brief complexity analysis and an empirical section comparing static LP vs ALP vs UCB-ALP, and sensitivity to ρ near boundaries.
- Relax strict ordering assumptions (ties) and state how to handle them (e.g., tie-breaking with small perturbations) and how bounds change.
- For unknown context distributions, give a concrete truncation schedule and bounds in the main text; for heterogeneous costs, consider a fully adaptive confidence-set method rather than ε-first.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a simple, insightful method with strong guarantees in a relevant constrained contextual setting. Addressing boundary behavior and adding empirical support would strengthen the submission.

Reviewer confidence: High.
