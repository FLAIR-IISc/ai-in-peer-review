Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget and a finite horizon, a setting that captures important practical constraints (e.g., clinical trials, ad allocation, rate selection) but has been difficult to analyze due to dynamic coupling of budget and time. The central idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming (DP) oracle via a linear programming (LP) relaxation with an average budget constraint and to adapt this relaxation over time using the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. 

Contributions:
- ALP (Adaptive Linear Programming): With known statistics, ALP chooses actions according to the optimal solution of the LP with constraint set to bτ/τ. A key structural observation is that under ALP the probability of spending the budget in a round equals bτ/τ, inducing a “sampling without replacement” process; this yields tight concentration of bτ and, consequently, an O(1) regret to an LP upper bound (and thus to the oracle) in non-boundary cases, and O(√T) in boundary cases (ρ equal to a cumulative context probability).
- UCB-ALP: For unknown rewards, combine per-context UCB estimates with ALP; in non-boundary cases the regret is O(JK log T), driven by learning the ordering of expected rewards; in boundary cases O(√T + JK log T).
- Extensions: (i) Unknown context distribution via empirical estimates (EALP/UCB-EALP), with rigorous analysis for a truncated estimator and similar performance claims; (ii) Heterogeneous costs via a generalized LP (and an ε-first variant for learning).

Relation to prior work: The paper significantly strengthens earlier “bandits with knapsacks” and “resourceful contextual bandits” results that achieve O(√T) regret (often with finite policy sets or static contexts) by showing how to obtain O(log T) regret for finite contexts and actions under a single hard budget. The adaptive LP relaxation and the hypergeometric concentration of remaining budget are the key novelties enabling this improvement and clean analysis.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The adaptive LP relaxation that turns a DP into an analyzable, near-optimal policy is elegant. Mapping the remaining budget dynamics to sampling without replacement is a simple but deep insight that underpins both performance and analysis.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) regret to the oracle in non-boundary cases with known statistics; O(JK log T) regret when learning, which is instance-dependent and essentially optimal for the need to learn per-context rankings. The comparison baseline is the true oracle (not a finite policy class), which is significant.
- Practicality: ALP/UCB-ALP are computationally lightweight (simple thresholding and at most solving small LPs); they only need reward orderings, making them robust to estimation error.
- Scope: Clear discussion of extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, indicating the approach is not a one-off trick.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions and scope: The main results hinge on i.i.d. contexts, finite context/action sets, a single resource, and fixed known costs (unit costs in the core analysis). While motivated and common, these assumptions exclude important cases (multiple resources, stochastic costs, rich/infinite contexts), where prior work focused and achieved O(√T) regret.
- Boundary cases: The O(√T) regret at boundaries is acknowledged; while measure-zero in a continuous ρ sense, in practice B/T is rational and may coincide with qj. It would help to discuss robustness to slight budget misspecification (e.g., ρ ± O(1/T)).
- Unknown context distribution: The rigorous analysis is for a truncated empirical estimator; the full UCB-EALP guarantee is stated as “similar” without proof details in the main text. This leaves a small gap in completeness.
- Empirical validation: The manuscript, as provided, includes no experimental results beyond a brief mention. Given the strength of the theory, this is not fatal, but even small-scale simulations comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to RCB/BwK baselines would substantiate the claims and illustrate constants.
- Clarity: A few assumptions are stated “for simplicity” (e.g., unique best action per context and strictly decreasing u∗j across contexts). The exact places where these are used should be clarified; if they can be relaxed (e.g., tie-breaking), it would strengthen the contribution.

Quality
The technical development appears sound, with meaningful proof sketches and references. The key lemmas (Û(T,B) upper bound, hypergeometric concentration of bτ, and UCB ranking error bounds) support the main theorems. The reliance on the LP upper bound as the comparator is clean and avoids DP complexities. I would like to see a more explicit justification of the “probability of spending equals b/τ” property under ALP for every round and context arrival, but the argument seems correct given the LP structure.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. Suggested improvements:
- Precisely state where the uniqueness and strict ordering assumptions are used and whether results extend under tie-breaking.
- For unknown context distribution, summarize the truncated estimator and coupling argument in the main text (even at a high level).
- Provide pseudocode or a one-line description of how to compute pj(b/τ) efficiently in the unit-cost case (thresholding), and briefly discuss per-round complexity.

Originality
High. The adaptive LP relaxation with constant-gap analysis to the oracle and the resulting O(log T) regret in constrained contextual bandits with finite contexts appears new and impactful. The “sampling without replacement” view of remaining budget is a neat analytical device.

Significance
High within the constrained bandits literature. Achieving logarithmic regret under a hard budget with a computationally simple algorithm fills a notable gap and is likely to influence subsequent work (e.g., multi-resource settings, rich contexts, better handling of boundary cases).

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, novel idea (adaptive LP relaxation) leading to strictly better regret (logarithmic) than prior art in a meaningful, practical setting.
- Strong, clean theoretical results with intuitive proofs and broad implications.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with robust behavior to estimation error.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical evidence in the current form.
- Some assumptions restrict scope; boundary case behavior remains suboptimal.
- Rigorous treatment for unknown context distribution relegated to a truncated variant.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling and novel approach with strong theoretical guarantees and practical promise. While experiments and some extensions could be strengthened, the conceptual contribution is significant enough to merit acceptance.

Questions/suggestions to authors
- Can you provide a simple perturbation result showing that if ρ is within O(1/T) of a boundary, ALP/UCB-ALP still enjoys O(log T) regret?
- Are there lower bounds showing that Ω(JK log T) regret is unavoidable for learning the per-context rankings in this setting?
- Any empirical comparisons to RCB/BwK baselines on synthetic tasks and one real-world proxy (e.g., rate selection) would greatly help. 
- Briefly discuss the feasibility of extending ALP to multiple resource constraints and whether the “spend with probability b/τ” property generalizes.
