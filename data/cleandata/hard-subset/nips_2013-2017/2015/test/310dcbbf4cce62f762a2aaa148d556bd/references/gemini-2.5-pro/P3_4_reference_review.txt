### Summary

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), focusing on the asymptotic regime where B/T is a fixed ratio ρ. The key challenge in this problem is that the optimal policy is a complex dynamic program (DP), making it difficult to analyze and to adapt for the case of unknown reward statistics. The authors make a significant contribution by proposing a novel and computationally efficient approach that circumvents these difficulties.

First, for the known-statistics case, they introduce the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. ALP approximates the intractable DP at each step by solving a simple linear program based on the *remaining* time and budget. The paper provides a rigorous analysis showing that ALP achieves O(1) regret (i.e., is asymptotically optimal) except for a specific set of "boundary" parameter values, where the regret is O(√T). This analysis hinges on a clever insight: the evolution of the remaining budget under ALP can be modeled as sampling without replacement, leading to strong concentration bounds via the hypergeometric distribution.

Second, for the practical case of unknown rewards, the paper proposes UCB-ALP, which combines the ALP decision-making framework with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) approach for learning reward parameters. The authors prove that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases. This is the first result to achieve logarithmic regret for this problem setting, representing a substantial improvement over prior work which typically achieves O(√T) regret.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles an important and practical extension of the contextual bandit problem. The primary contribution—achieving O(log T) regret—is a major theoretical breakthrough. Prior work on budgeted bandits or resourceful contextual bandits (e.g., [17, 20]) established O(√T) regret bounds. Closing this gap to O(log T) is a significant advance. The approach of using a state-dependent (adaptive) LP to approximate the DP is novel and elegant.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is deep and appears sound. The key technical innovation is the analysis of the remaining budget process. By showing that the average remaining budget `bτ/τ` concentrates tightly around the initial ratio ρ, the authors demonstrate that the complex stochastic control problem can be effectively decoupled into a sequence of simpler, myopic decisions guided by the ALP solution. This is a powerful analytical technique. The decomposition of regret for the UCB-ALP algorithm into action-ranking and context-ranking components is also very well-executed, carefully handling the interaction between the learning process and the adaptive policy.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It builds its case effectively, starting with the simpler known-statistics setting to introduce the core ALP idea and its analysis, before moving to the more complex unknown-statistics case. The proof sketches provide excellent intuition for the main analytical arguments, particularly the role of the budget concentration and the classification of ranking errors.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scope of "Boundary Cases":** The regret degrades to O(√T) in "boundary cases," defined as when ρ exactly equals a cumulative context probability `q_j`. The authors argue this is a set of measure zero. However, the analysis in Theorem 1 suggests that the O(1) regret in non-boundary cases depends on `1/exp(δ^2)`, where `δ` is the distance to the nearest boundary. This implies that if the system is *near* a boundary, the "constant" regret could be very large. A discussion on the practical implications of this sensitivity would strengthen the paper. For instance, how does the regret scale as `ρ -> q_j`?

2.  **Details on Extensions:** The extensions to unknown context distributions (Section 5) and heterogeneous costs (Section 6) are very briefly sketched, with almost all details deferred to the supplementary material. The heterogeneous cost case, in particular, seems substantially more complex than the unit-cost case (the LP is no longer a simple thresholding rule but a more general knapsack-type problem over all context-action pairs), and the main paper provides little intuition on how the analysis extends. While space is limited, a slightly more detailed summary of the key ideas for these generalizations would have made the main paper more self-contained and convincing.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The work is entirely theoretical. While this is common and acceptable for a strong theory paper, the claims would be further solidified by even simple simulations. For example, an experiment demonstrating the performance difference between a "boundary" and "non-boundary" setting, or comparing UCB-ALP against a baseline algorithm from [20] or [21], would provide valuable empirical verification of the theoretical findings and their practical relevance.

### Detailed Comments

-   The connection to prior work, especially [20, 21, 22], could be sharpened. The paper states that these works use a "fixed budget constraint". It would be more precise to say that they typically use a fixed *Lagrange multiplier* (or dual variable) for an epoch, which is then updated. The key novelty here is the per-round adaptation based on the *primal state* `(b, τ)`, which is a more direct and finer-grained control mechanism. Highlighting this distinction would make the paper's contribution even clearer.

-   The proof sketch for Theorem 1 is excellent and intuitive. Mapping the budget evolution to a known stochastic process (hypergeometric distribution) is the crux of the matter and is explained well.

-   In Section 5, for unknown context distributions, the paper analyzes a "truncated" version of the EALP algorithm. It would be helpful to briefly state why the standard (non-truncated) UCB-EALP is difficult to analyze. The presumed reason is the complex dependencies introduced by continuously updating `π_hat`, which breaks the martingale properties often used in bandit analysis, but explicitly stating this would be useful.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** First paper to achieve O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits, a fundamental improvement over the state-of-the-art O(√T).
2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The ALP framework and its analysis via concentration of the remaining budget process is a powerful and elegant new technique for analyzing budgeted sequential decision-making problems.
3.  **Clarity and Impact:** The paper is clearly written and addresses a well-motivated problem. The ideas presented are likely to influence future research on bandits with constraints and related reinforcement learning problems.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** The sensitivity to being near a "boundary case" is not fully explored, potentially limiting the applicability of the strong O(1) and O(log T) results in practice.
2.  **Incomplete Presentation of Extensions:** The important generalizations to heterogeneous costs and unknown contexts are treated too superficially in the main paper, relying heavily on the appendix.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept.

Despite the minor weaknesses, the paper's primary contribution is a significant and elegant theoretical advance on an important problem. The novel analytical approach and the achievement of logarithmic regret substantially push the frontier of our understanding of constrained bandits. This is a high-quality paper that would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
