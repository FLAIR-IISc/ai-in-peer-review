This paper tackles the important and relatively unexplored problem of constrained contextual bandits, specifically with both budget and time limitations. The core idea – adapting linear programming to approximate the oracle policy and then building an adaptive algorithm (ALP) – is quite clever and represents a genuinely novel approach. The authors successfully demonstrate O(1) regret for the known-parameters case and extend this to the unknown-parameters setting with UCB-ALP, achieving logarithmic regret (with caveats for boundary cases). This is a significant result, particularly given the complexity of the problem. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the technical analysis appears rigorous (though a complete verification is beyond the scope of this review).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of LP relaxation and adaptive budgeting is a fresh perspective on constrained bandit problems. It moves beyond direct extensions of existing unconstrained bandit algorithms.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis, while complex, appears sound and provides strong theoretical guarantees. The treatment of boundary cases is appreciated.
* **Practical Relevance:** Budget constraints are ubiquitous in real-world applications of contextual bandits (clinical trials, ad placement, etc.), making this work highly relevant.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and relatively easy to follow, given the technical depth. The authors clearly outline the challenges and their proposed solutions.
* **Extension to Unknown Parameters:** Successfully bridging the gap to the unknown parameter setting with UCB-ALP adds significant value.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Simplified Assumptions:** The assumption of finite discrete contexts and fixed costs, while simplifying the analysis, limits the immediate applicability of the results. The extension to more general cases (mentioned briefly in sections 5 and 6, largely relegated to the supplementary material) feels somewhat underdeveloped in the main paper.
* **Boundary Cases:** The O(√T) regret in boundary cases is a concern. While acknowledged, the conditions defining these cases, and their likely prevalence in practice, aren’t thoroughly discussed. More insight into the practical impact of these cases would be valuable.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical validation. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, demonstrating the algorithm’s performance in simulated environments would significantly strengthen the contribution. Even a limited set of experiments could provide confidence in the practical viability of the approach.
* **Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper acknowledges related work published concurrently ([21, 22]), but the discussion feels slightly dismissive. While those papers achieve √T regret, the authors’ logarithmic regret, even with caveats, represents an improvement. A more nuanced comparison – highlighting the trade-offs and specific scenarios where each approach excels – would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel and promising approach to a challenging problem.
* The theoretical guarantees are strong and well-supported.
* The work addresses a practically important problem with significant real-world applications.
* The clarity of the presentation makes the complex technical details accessible.
* Even with the caveat of boundary cases, achieving logarithmic regret is a substantial accomplishment.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The simplified assumptions limit the direct applicability of the results.
* The lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the algorithm’s practical performance.
* The boundary case regret of √T is a potential limitation.
* The comparison to recent concurrent work could be more thorough.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits. While the simplified assumptions and lack of empirical validation are weaknesses, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the approach outweigh these concerns. The work is likely to stimulate further research in this important area.  I lean strongly towards acceptance. The potential impact of this work on areas like resource allocation and personalized decision-making is significant.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I encourage the authors to consider including some initial empirical results, even if limited in scope, in a future version of the paper to further strengthen their claims. A more detailed discussion of the boundary cases and their potential prevalence in practical applications would also be valuable.
