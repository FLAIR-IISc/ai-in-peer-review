### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), a setting referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The goal is to maximize the cumulative reward before either T or B is exhausted. The authors identify that the optimal policy (oracle) is computationally intractable and difficult to analyze as it would require dynamic programming.

To circumvent this, the paper introduces a novel two-stage approach. First, for the case where environment statistics (reward means, context probabilities) are known, they propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. ALP approximates the optimal policy by repeatedly solving a small linear program that relaxes the hard budget constraint to an average one, using the *remaining* time and budget at each step. The authors prove that this simple and efficient ALP algorithm achieves a remarkable O(1) regret (i.e., a constant gap from an LP-based upper bound on the oracle's performance) in most cases, and O(√T) regret in specific "boundary cases."

Second, for the more practical setting where reward means are unknown, they combine the ALP decision-making framework with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle for learning. The resulting UCB-ALP algorithm is shown to achieve O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases. This is, to the best of my knowledge, the first result demonstrating logarithmic regret for this problem setting. The paper also discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

The work is positioned relative to prior research on bandits with budgets (e.g., [12, 16]) by highlighting the additional complexity of contexts, and to resourceful contextual bandits (e.g., [17]) by trading some generality (e.g., assuming finite contexts and fixed costs) for significantly improved regret bounds (O(log T) vs. O(√T)) and a computationally simpler algorithm.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by being the first to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits. This is a substantial improvement over the typical O(√T) regret found in related, more general settings. The core idea of using an adaptive LP relaxation to decouple the complex interaction between learning and resource allocation is both novel and insightful. It provides a powerful and practical template for tackling constrained sequential decision-making problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and impressive. The key technical steps are elegant:
    *   Establishing a tractable upper bound for the oracle via a static LP relaxation is a clever way to make the regret analysis feasible.
    *   The analysis of the ALP algorithm, by mapping the evolution of the remaining budget to a "sampling without replacement" process and leveraging concentration properties of the hypergeometric distribution (Lemma 2), is a highlight of the paper. This provides a clean and powerful tool to show that the average remaining budget stays concentrated around its mean, which is central to the O(1) regret result.
    *   The analysis of UCB-ALP effectively partitions the regret into components arising from action-ranking errors (standard in UCB analysis) and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation errors. The careful categorization of ranking errors (Erank,s(t)) shows a deep understanding of how estimation errors interact with the threshold-based structure of the ALP policy.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It builds its case logically, starting from the simplified known-statistics setting to motivate the ALP algorithm, and then naturally extending it to the UCB-ALP algorithm for the unknown-statistics setting. The challenges are clearly articulated, and the high-level ideas behind the proofs are well-explained, making a technically dense paper surprisingly accessible.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas where further clarification and discussion would enhance its contribution.

1.  **Impact of "Boundary Cases":** The main logarithmic regret results rely on the assumption that the budget-to-time ratio `ρ = B/T` is not a "boundary" value (i.e., `ρ ≠ Σ_{j'=1 to j} π_{j'}`). While the authors correctly state that these boundaries form a set of Lebesgue measure zero for continuous `ρ`, in practice `B` and `T` are integers and `π_j` may be simple rationals, making it possible to land on or very near a boundary. It would be valuable to include a discussion on the practical implications of this assumption.
    *   **Suggestion:** Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of the regret bounds to `ρ` being close to a boundary? For example, does the constant in the O(1) or O(log T) term grow as the distance `δ` to the nearest boundary shrinks? A brief simulation study showing performance as `ρ` approaches a boundary point would be very insightful and strengthen the practical relevance of the results.

2.  **Extensions in Supplementary Material:** The extensions to unknown context distributions (Section 5) and heterogeneous costs (Section 6) are important for practical applications, but their details are largely deferred to the supplementary material. The main paper would be more self-contained and impactful if it provided more insight into these extensions. The heterogeneous cost case, in particular, seems non-trivial as the LP formulation becomes more complex and one can no longer simply select the best action within a context.
    *   **Suggestion:** Briefly summarize the structure of the LP for the heterogeneous cost case in the main text. Explain why the problem is more complex than simply using normalized rewards (`u_{j,k}/c_{j,k}`) and how the analysis framework is adapted to handle this more complex decision space.

3.  **Computational Efficiency:** The paper claims the algorithm is "computationally efficient." While solving a small LP at each step is generally fast, it is more demanding than the simple index computations in standard UCB.
    *   **Suggestion:** It would be helpful to explicitly state the per-step computational complexity of UCB-ALP. For the unit-cost case, this depends on sorting `J` values. For the heterogeneous cost case, it involves solving an LP with `JK` variables. Quantifying this would provide a clearer picture for practitioners.

### **Decision**

This paper presents a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The theoretical results are strong, achieving logarithmic regret in a constrained setting for the first time, and the analytical techniques are elegant. The work is presented with exceptional clarity. The identified weaknesses are minor and relate to clarifying the practical scope and details of extensions, which can be readily addressed. This is a solid paper that advances the state of the art and will likely inspire further research in bandits with resource constraints. I strongly recommend acceptance.
