Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with both time and a hard budget constraint (“constrained contextual bandits”). Contexts are i.i.d. from a finite set; actions yield bounded stochastic rewards with known fixed costs (initially unit cost), and the process stops when budget or time is exhausted. The key difficulty is that the oracle with known statistics is a budget-aware dynamic program, which is computationally and analytically unwieldy. The authors propose to approximate the oracle with a linear program (LP) that enforces only an average budget constraint. They first analyze a static LP (with average budget ρ=B/T), which gives an explicit upper bound on the oracle’s expected reward. They then propose Adaptive LP (ALP), which, at each round, solves the LP with the average remaining budget bτ/τ, leading to a simple threshold/randomization policy over contexts. A key technical observation is that, under ALP, the budget consumption process behaves like sampling without replacement; the remaining budget concentrates hypergeometrically around ρτ. They show ALP achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary cases (ρ not equal to any cumulative context probability qj) and O(√T) on boundaries.

For unknown rewards, they combine ALP with UCB (“UCB-ALP”), using UCB estimates û∗j(t) of the best action per context to plug into the LP each round. They prove UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on boundaries. They also discuss unknown context distributions: replacing π with empirical estimates (EALP) works empirically; for theory they analyze a truncated estimator to recover concentration of bτ/τ and O(1) regret (non-boundary). An extension to heterogeneous costs is outlined; an LP over all context-action pairs is used, and for unknown rewards an ε-first variant is proposed.

The work contrasts with Bandits with Knapsacks and related primal-dual methods [12,17,20–22], which typically yield O(√T) regret and sometimes restrict to a finite policy set. Here the authors obtain logarithmic regret under finite contexts/actions and fixed costs by adaptively matching budget consumption to remaining time. To my knowledge this is the first demonstration of log regret in a constrained contextual bandit with stochastic contexts, under these assumptions.

Strengths
- Clear, principled approximation of a hard DP oracle by an adaptive LP with a simple threshold structure.
- Elegant budget-process analysis via a “sampling without replacement” view leading to sharp concentration of bτ and O(1) regret for ALP in non-boundary cases.
- UCB-ALP is computationally simple (sorting contexts by û∗j and solving a tiny LP each round) and achieves O(log T) regret beyond prior O(√T) bounds in comparable constrained settings.
- Careful decomposition of regret sources (action ranking vs context ranking vs budget fluctuations) and handling of context randomness.
- Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, with reasonable algorithmic ideas and some theoretical guarantees.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions are restrictive: finite discrete contexts/actions, fixed known costs (unit-cost for main results), i.i.d. contexts with known distribution (main theorems), and unique strict orderings u∗1>…>u∗J. Practical systems often have continuous or large context spaces and random costs.
- Boundary cases (ρ=qj) degrade to O(√T). While measure-zero in a continuum sense, ρ is user-chosen and πj are fixed; in practice such boundaries are not that rare. Is there a simple smoothing (e.g., dithering ρ or adding slack) to recover log regret on or near boundaries?
- Unknown-context case: the theoretically analyzed algorithm freezes the empirical distribution after a burn-in; this is a technical workaround. It would be helpful to precisely state the regret and conditions for UCB-EALP without truncation, or quantify the additional error due to continuing updates.
- Heterogeneous costs: the unknown-reward case uses ε-first ALP. Does this achieve O(log T) regret (non-boundary), and how does ε depend on gaps and J,K? The main text leaves the guarantees to the supplement.
- Regret bounds are stated as O(JK log T) but constants likely depend on reward gaps through UCB analysis; making the gap dependence explicit would be informative and align with standard instance-dependent bandit bounds.
- Clarity issues: several displayed equations/typesetting are garbled (e.g., Theorem 1 bounds), and Algorithm 1 could be more explicit about LP solving and ordering. Proofs central to novelty (e.g., that bτ retains hypergeometric-like concentration under UCB-ALP) are deferred; a brief intuitive justification in the main text would help.
- No empirical evaluation is presented in the excerpt; even a small-scale simulation to illustrate constant-regret of ALP and the log slopes of UCB-ALP, as ρ varies near/away from boundaries, would strengthen the paper.

Quality
The technical core appears sound and well-motivated. The mapping to sampling without replacement and the regret decomposition are insightful. Some key arguments are deferred to the supplement; making the main ideas self-contained would improve confidence. Extensions are promising but, in places, light on detail.

Clarity
Generally well organized, with a clear model and algorithm descriptions. Improve notation consistency, fix typos/equation glitches, and explicitly discuss computational complexity per round (sorting û∗j; LP closed-form threshold). Clarify how ties in u∗j or among actions are handled.

Originality
The adaptive LP approach to achieve log regret under budget constraints in contextual bandits with stochastic contexts is novel. Prior work on resource constraints tends to achieve √T regret or restrict policies. The sampling-without-replacement analysis within this setting is a nice contribution.

Significance
The results are theoretically significant for constrained bandits, providing a simple, efficient method with strong guarantees in a practically relevant, albeit simplified, setting. This could influence future work on more general resourceful contextual bandits.

Questions for the authors
- Can you propose a simple modification to eliminate the √T boundary degradation (e.g., a small slack in the LP budget or randomized ρ), and analyze it?
- What is the precise regret (including gap dependence) of UCB-ALP? Are there instance-dependent lower bounds matching O(log T)?
- For unknown π, can you bound the regret of the fully empirical UCB-EALP (without truncation)?
- In heterogeneous-costs with unknown rewards, what regret do you obtain for ε-first ALP? How is ε chosen?
- How sensitive is performance to ties (non-unique best actions/contexts)?

Pros
- Conceptually simple, computationally light algorithm with strong guarantees.
- Advances the state of the art from √T to log T in this constrained setting.
- Insightful analysis technique likely reusable.

Cons
- Restricted assumptions; boundary degradation; partial treatment of extensions; limited clarity in places; no experiments shown.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a clean and impactful theoretical contribution with practical algorithmic ideas. Addressing clarity issues and adding a brief empirical section would strengthen it further. My confidence is medium-high.
