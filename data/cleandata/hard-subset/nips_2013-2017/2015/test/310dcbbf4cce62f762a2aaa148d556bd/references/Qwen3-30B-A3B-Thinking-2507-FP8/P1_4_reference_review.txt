

# Peer Review: "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in the contextual bandit literature by studying contextual bandit problems with budget constraints, which are crucial for real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors formalize the problem as "constrained contextual bandits" where both budget and time constraints limit the agent's actions. While previous work on budget-constrained bandits exists for non-contextual settings [11-16], and resourceful contextual bandits [17] for more general settings, the authors note that these approaches either don't handle contexts properly or suffer from computational intractability. 

The paper makes two key contributions: (1) For known statistics, they propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases with O(√T) regret) by approximating the oracle solution through dynamic linear programming with adaptive budget constraints. (2) For unknown statistics, they develop a UCB-ALP algorithm that combines ALP with UCB methods to achieve O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases (O(√T + log T) for boundary cases). This represents a significant improvement over previous work, which achieved O(√T) regret for similar problems [17, 20-22].

The paper builds on prior work in contextual bandits [5-7, 17] and budget-constrained bandits [11-16], but the authors clearly identify and address the gap in handling contextual bandits with budget constraints. They also acknowledge related work [21, 22] that recently achieved O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts, but note that their approach achieves logarithmic regret through adaptive budget constraints.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide complete proofs for all key claims, including Lemma 1 (upper bound), Theorem 1 (ALP regret), and Theorem 2 (UCB-ALP regret). The analysis carefully considers multiple interacting factors like action/context ranking errors, remaining budget fluctuations, and context arrival randomness. The paper is complete as it provides algorithms, theoretical guarantees, and discussions of generalizations to heterogeneous costs and unknown context distributions. The authors are honest about limitations, explicitly identifying boundary cases where regret is O(√T) rather than O(log T).

### Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates it within the literature. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The key concepts (ALP, UCB-ALP) are explained with appropriate intuition before presenting formal algorithms. The sketch of proofs provides valuable insight without overwhelming detail, and the paper makes good use of figures and tables (though none are shown in the excerpt). The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

### Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by addressing the constrained contextual bandit problem with budget constraints, which has not been adequately handled in prior work. The key innovation is the use of adaptive linear programming to approximate the oracle solution, which enables the logarithmic regret guarantee. This approach differs substantially from previous methods that either used fixed budget constraints [20-22] or relied on finite policy sets [17]. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work, including recent papers [21, 22] that achieve O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts. The combination of ALP with UCB to create UCB-ALP is a novel and elegant solution.

### Significance
The problem addressed is highly significant for practical applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing where budget constraints are fundamental. The paper's main result—achieving logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits—is a major theoretical advance over previous O(√T) results. This represents a substantial improvement in the theoretical understanding of constrained bandit problems. The insights about decoupling information acquisition from decision making through adaptive linear relaxation could be valuable for other constrained bandit problems. The paper opens new research directions in constrained bandit algorithms.

## Weaknesses

### Experimental Validation
A notable weakness is the lack of experimental results to validate the theoretical claims. While the paper provides thorough theoretical analysis, NeurIPS typically expects empirical validation of algorithms, especially for bandit problems where the theoretical guarantees can be sensitive to constants and practical considerations. The authors could have included simulations comparing their UCB-ALP algorithm with existing methods to demonstrate practical performance.

### Boundary Cases
The paper acknowledges that boundary cases (where ρ = qj for some j) achieve only O(√T) regret, which is not as strong as the non-boundary case. While the authors note this is a limitation, they don't provide a clear path forward for handling these cases, which could be significant in applications where the budget-to-time ratio happens to align with these boundary values.

### Scope of Generalizations
The paper focuses primarily on unit-cost systems, then discusses generalizations to heterogeneous costs and unknown context distributions in later sections. While this is a reasonable approach, the main theoretical results are for unit-cost systems, which may limit the immediate applicability. More detailed discussion of how the algorithm would perform in real-world settings with heterogeneous costs would strengthen the paper.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the constrained contextual bandit literature, addressing a problem of practical importance with a novel solution that achieves logarithmic regret—previously unattained in this setting. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed, and the paper is clearly written.

The main weakness is the lack of experimental validation, which is a common expectation for NeurIPS papers. However, the theoretical contribution is so strong and the problem is so important that this omission does not outweigh the paper's merits. The authors could address this in a revised version by adding experimental results.

## Key Arguments for Acceptance

- **Novel problem formulation**: Addresses constrained contextual bandits with budget constraints, a significant gap in the literature.
- **Theoretical advance**: First to show logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits (vs. O(√T) in previous work).
- **Rigorous analysis**: Thorough theoretical justification of all claims with careful consideration of interacting factors.
- **Practical insight**: The ALP approach provides a useful framework for decoupling information acquisition from decision making.

## Key Arguments Against Acceptance

- **Lack of experimental results**: NeurIPS typically expects empirical validation alongside theoretical results.
- **Boundary cases**: Regret is only O(log T) in non-boundary cases, not universally.
- **Limited scope**: Main results focus on unit-cost systems before generalizing.

## Final Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in constrained bandit problems. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with significant practical implications. While the lack of experimental results is a minor weakness, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work [17, 20-22] and provides a clear path forward for constrained contextual bandit research. I believe this paper would be valuable to the NeurIPS community and deserves acceptance.
