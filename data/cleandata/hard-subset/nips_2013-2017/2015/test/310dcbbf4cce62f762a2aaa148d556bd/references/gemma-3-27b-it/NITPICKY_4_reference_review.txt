This paper addresses the challenging problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a natural extension of the standard contextual bandit model. The authors aim to develop algorithms with logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over existing approaches, particularly for resource-constrained applications. The core idea revolves around approximating the optimal policy using linear programming (LP) and developing an adaptive version (ALP) that adjusts the budget constraint dynamically. This is then combined with UCB for the unknown reward setting (UCB-ALP).

While the problem statement is well-motivated and the proposed approach presents a conceptually sound direction, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor – yet cumulatively substantial – flaws in its technical presentation, analysis, and justification. These issues, despite the potential novelty of the core ideas, lead me to recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Constrained contextual bandits are an important and practically relevant extension.
*   **Conceptual Approach:** The LP relaxation and adaptive budgeting strategy (ALP) are promising ideas for tackling the complexity introduced by the constraints.
*   **Logarithmic Regret Claim:** Achieving logarithmic regret in this setting is a significant contribution if proven rigorously.
*   **Clear Structure:** The paper generally follows a logical structure, building from known statistics to unknown statistics.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overly Optimistic Presentation:** The paper consistently frames results as “firsts” or “significant improvements” without sufficient nuance. For example, claiming this is “the first work that shows how to achieve logarithmic regret” ignores related work extending previous results, even if those results achieve √T regret.
*   **Lack of Rigor in Approximation Justification:** The justification for using the LP relaxation as a benchmark is weak. The statement that it “provides an upper bound” is true but doesn't explain *why* this relaxation is a good proxy for the true optimal value, especially in the context of regret analysis.  More detailed justification is needed beyond simply stating Lemma 1.
*   **Boundary Cases:** The frequent mention of "boundary cases" with √T regret is troubling. The paper doesn't adequately define the size or practical significance of these boundary cases.  Are they measure zero, as claimed, or are they prevalent in real-world scenarios? This needs clarification.
*   **Technical Imprecision:** Throughout the paper, mathematical notation and definitions are often imprecise. For instance, the definition of  `j̃(ρ)` and its connection to the LP solution is not thoroughly explained. The meaning of `Θ(o)` in Theorem 1 is unclear without referring to the appendix.
*   **Insufficient Detail in Analysis:** The proof sketches provided are too high-level. Crucial steps, like the concentration properties of `bτ` under UCB-ALP, are mentioned but not detailed within the main body, pushing the core analysis to the supplementary material, which isn’t fully assessable within the review process.
*   **Redundant/Unnecessary Claims:** Assertions like “the algorithm is quite computationally efficient” lack quantitative support.  Comparing the computational complexity to alternative approaches would strengthen this claim.
*   **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, spacing issues) and typographical errors distract from the clarity of the presentation. The reference list formatting is also inconsistent.
* **Missing Baseline:** Comparing performance to simpler baselines (e.g. epsilon-greedy with a budget constraint) is missing.

**Specific Concerns & Detailed Comments:**

*   **Introduction:** The motivation is repetitive. The connection to applications (crowdsourcing, clinical trials) feels superficial.
*   **Section 3.1:** The optimality argument for the LP solution needs more clarity. Why is this particular solution optimal?
*   **Section 3.2:** The proof sketch relies heavily on a coupling argument without fully explaining the connection.
*   **Section 4.3:** The justification for using a smaller coefficient in the UCB exploration term is unclear.
*   **Section 5:** The discussion of truncating the empirical distribution feels ad-hoc and lacks a clear theoretical basis.
*   **Section 6:** This section is very brief and relies heavily on references to the supplementary material.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The problem is important and the proposed approach has the potential to lead to significant algorithmic improvements.
*   The core idea of using LP relaxation and adaptive budgeting is novel and intuitively appealing.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The technical presentation lacks rigor and precision.
*   The analysis is incomplete and relies heavily on references to the supplementary material.
*   The significance of the "boundary cases" is unclear.
*   Numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the paper.
* The lack of comparison to simpler baselines makes it hard to assess the value of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential importance of the problem and the interesting initial ideas, the paper’s numerous technical deficiencies and lack of rigor prevent me from recommending acceptance. The flaws are not catastrophic in isolation, but their cumulative effect is substantial. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above is required before this work would be suitable for NIPS. I believe a resubmission after significant technical strengthening would be worthwhile.
