Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits under a single hard budget and finite horizon, with i.i.d. discrete contexts. For unit costs, the authors propose an adaptive linear programming (ALP) policy that, given known statistics, sets a threshold on contexts based on the average remaining budget bτ/τ and randomizes only at a single boundary context. They prove ALP attains O(1) regret (relative to an LP upper bound that equals T v(ρ)) in non-boundary cases (ρ ≠ qj), and O(√T) at boundary cases (ρ = qj). With unknown rewards (known context distribution), they combine UCB estimates with ALP (UCB-ALP), proving O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) at boundaries. They sketch extensions to unknown context distribution (empirical estimates with truncation) and to heterogeneous costs (generalized LP; ε-first exploration).

Relation to prior work
- Extends “Bandits with knapsacks”/RCB to finite discrete contexts with a single budget and unit costs, achieving instance-dependent logarithmic regret where prior general results were O(√T) (Agrawal & Devanur; Badanidiyuru et al.).
- The use of LP relaxation as an oracle surrogate aligns with prior approximate DP/LP ideas, but here the specific adaptive thresholding plus a concentration argument (sampling-without-replacement view) is the main technical novelty.
- For non-contextual budgeted bandits, O(log T) is known; this paper bridges to finite-context settings.

Strengths
- Clear, appealing algorithmic idea: adapt a simple LP threshold by bτ/τ. The mapping of budget dynamics to sampling without replacement is elegant and yields sharp concentration.
- Strong instance-dependent guarantees: O(1) regret (known stats) and O(JK log T) (unknown stats) off measure-zero boundaries.
- The regret decomposition for UCB-ALP (action-ranking vs context-ranking errors) is insightful and seems broadly applicable.
- Computationally light for unit costs (closed-form greedy solution); conceptually simple.

Weaknesses and concerns
Major technical/clarity:
- Heavy reliance on the supplement. Key proofs (Lemma 1, Theorem 1, preservation of hypergeometric concentration under UCB-ALP) are only sketched. The argument that bτ retains hypergeometric concentration under UCB-ALP despite mis-rankings (when the LP constraint may not be tight) needs a precise, self-contained proof in the main text, as it underpins Theorem 2.
- Boundary cases: The claim that they are “measure zero” is not entirely convincing in a discrete, integer B,T setting where ρ = B/T hits rational qj frequently (particularly when πj are rational). This weakens the practical significance of the O(√T) exceptional set. It would be valuable to propose a simple regularization (e.g., dithering in b/τ, tie-breaking with ε-margins) to avoid landing on boundaries and to recover logarithmic regret.
- Known context distribution: The main logarithmic regret result assumes known π. Section 5 treats unknown π via truncated empirical estimation; the truncation device and precise guarantees are deferred to the supplement and not fully spelled out. As a result, the practical online algorithmic story is incomplete in the main paper.
- Heterogeneous costs: The generalization claims O(1)/O(√T) bounds and proposes an ε-first strategy, but details are again deferred. The per-round LP has JK variables and additional constraints; no discussion of computational overhead or whether a greedy structure persists.

Methodological details:
- The upper bound Û(T,B) = T v(ρ) relies on the LP being tight (sum πj pj = ρ). This holds for strictly decreasing u∗j, but the paper should explicitly cover tie handling and cases where the inequality could be slack (e.g., at boundaries) and how this affects the bound and ALP’s budget dynamics.
- The UCB exploration term uses √(log t / (2 C)), citing [24], but guarantees (Lemma 3) assume high-probability ordering with constants tailored to this coefficient. Please provide a precise, adapted proof or a citation with the exact constants, as the term 2 t−1 is unusual.
- Lack of lower bounds in this setting: The paper asserts order optimality for non-boundary cases; a formal reduction showing that O(JK log T) is unavoidable (due to per-context action identification) would strengthen the claims.

Presentation and formatting issues (numerous, detracting from clarity):
- Eq. (4) is missing the third case value; presumably pj = 0 for j > j̃(ρ)+1.
- Theorem 1 bounds are malformed: expressions like “≤ u∗1−u∗J −e−2δ2” and “Θ(o) √T” are unclear. Define constants properly and remove stray minus signs. δ, δ′ definitions seem mismatched to the earlier threshold notation.
- UCB definition: “ûj,k(t) = 1 for Cj,k(t − ) = 0” has a missing index. Similarly, “û∗j (t)← û∗j,k∗j (t)(t)” has duplicated “(t)”.
- Algorithm 1 mixes τ,b updates and action selection ambiguously at b=0; clarify termination and dummy action handling.
- Several sentences are ungrammatical or incomplete; numerous missing bibliographic fields in references; inconsistent capitalization (Notations and Property), and missing equation numbering continuity.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide a simple modification (e.g., ε-margin in the LP constraint) that avoids boundary pathologies and recovers logarithmic regret uniformly?
2) For unknown π, can UCB-ALP with a continuously updated empirical π̂ be analyzed without truncation? If not, how should one pick the truncation time in practice?
3) Under UCB-ALP, when context rankings are wrong, the LP constraint may not be tight; how do you ensure the “probability of spending equals b/τ” property needed for the hypergeometric argument? Please include a rigorous argument in the main paper.
4) Complexity: For heterogeneous costs, what is the per-round LP size and can it be solved in o(JK) time using sorting/greedy?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean idea with clear impact on a timely problem (learning under resource constraints).
- Strong, instance-dependent bounds and a novel analysis tool (sampling-without-replacement view).

Cons for acceptance
- Many presentation errors and incomplete arguments in the main text; over-reliance on the supplement.
- Boundary-case exception undermines the headline claim in practical discrete settings.
- Unknown π and heterogeneous-cost extensions are underspecified.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The core idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current version has too many technical and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards without substantial revision.

Confidence: High.
