Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single knapsack-style budget constraint and a fixed horizon. Unlike standard contextual bandits, each action consumes cost and the process stops when either time or budget runs out. The key contribution is an adaptive LP relaxation (ALP) of the dynamic programming (oracle) benchmark: at each round, given remaining time τ and budget bτ, the algorithm solves a simple LP with an average budget constraint bτ/τ and plays the LP-induced threshold/randomized policy. When rewards are unknown, ALP is combined with UCB estimates (UCB-ALP). The main theoretical results are: (i) in unit-cost systems with known statistics, ALP achieves O(1) regret relative to the optimal oracle except at boundary values of the budget ratio ρ where regret is O(√T); (ii) with unknown rewards and known context distribution, UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret (or O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases). The analysis hinges on showing the remaining budget under ALP behaves like sampling without replacement (hypergeometric concentration), enabling tight control of bτ/τ and a clean decoupling of decision and estimation error.

This builds on and contrasts with: (a) Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) and Mixture Elimination [17], which allow more general settings but achieve O(√T) regret and compare to a finite policy class; (b) Bandits with Knapsacks and subsequent contextual extensions [20–22], which in comparable stochastic settings obtain O(√T) regret and do not adapt the budget ratio per round; and (c) classic contextual bandits (e.g., linear) without resource constraints. The paper claims to be the first to obtain logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits in a finite-context/action stochastic setting.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The technical core (LP relaxation; adaptive use of bτ/τ; mapping of budget evolution to a hypergeometric process) appears solid and insightful. The O(1) gap to the DP oracle in non-boundary cases is particularly compelling, providing a strong benchmark for learning. The UCB-ALP regret proof strategy (decomposing regret into within-context action misranking and cross-context threshold misranking, then bounding both via standard UCB inequalities and the concentration of bτ/τ) is reasonable. The boundary-case degradation to √T is carefully delineated. Some crucial arguments (e.g., the coupling that yields hypergeometric behavior and the truncated EALP analysis for unknown context distributions) are in the supplement; the main paper’s sketches are plausible but condensed.
- Completeness: The unit-cost known-distribution case is fully developed. Extensions to unknown context distribution (EALP) and heterogeneous costs are outlined with guarantees stated, but proofs and algorithmic specifics (e.g., ε-first parameters) are deferred to the supplement.
- Practicality: The per-round LP has a closed-form threshold solution; the algorithm is computationally simple (sorting contexts by UCBs). The claim of efficiency is credible.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful intuition (e.g., only ordering of expected rewards is needed for ALP). However:
  - The “sampling without replacement”/hypergeometric argument is central; a slightly more detailed intuitive explanation in the main text would help.
  - Clarify how ties are handled (beyond “measure zero” caveat) and how sensitive bounds are to small gaps (instance-dependent constants).
  - For UCB-ALP, briefly justify that sufficient exploration occurs even when ρ is small (since actions occur only when budget is spent).
  - Give the per-round computational complexity explicitly.

Originality:
- The adaptive LP relaxation to a per-round average budget constraint bτ/τ, and the proof that this yields an O(1) gap to the dynamic oracle (non-boundary) are, to my knowledge, new in this contextual knapsack setting. The hypergeometric coupling for remaining budget under ALP is a neat and original technical device.
- The combination with UCB is natural, but the analysis in the presence of budget/time coupling and random contexts is nontrivial and distinguishes this work from prior UCB-with-knapsack analyses that fix budget rates.
- Prior works [20–22] achieve O(√T) in dynamic contexts; obtaining O(log T) here (under finite discrete contexts/actions) is a meaningful advance.

Significance:
- The results matter theoretically: they identify a simple, efficient policy class (ALP) that is near-optimal with known stats and supports logarithmic regret when learning, sharpening the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits.
- The assumptions (finite contexts/actions, i.i.d. contexts, unit or known costs, known or estimable context distribution) fit several applications (clinical trials, wireless rate selection) but limit generality relative to RCB/BwK. Still, the techniques may inspire broader extensions.

Pros:
- Clear, simple algorithmic idea with strong guarantees.
- First logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under a natural finite setting.
- Elegant analysis via hypergeometric concentration; insightful decomposition of regret.
- Computationally efficient; regret benchmarked to the true oracle (not a finite policy class).
- Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

Cons:
- Boundary cases still incur √T regret; no lower bound showing necessity.
- Heavy reliance on i.i.d. finite contexts, known (or well-estimated) context distribution, and uniqueness/separation assumptions.
- Empirical evaluation is minimal/absent in the main text; experiments could illustrate constants and robustness.
- Some key proofs relegated to the supplement; main text could better motivate the coupling argument.
- The O(JK log T) term is coarse; tighter instance-dependent bounds (in gaps) would strengthen the result.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Can you provide instance-dependent regret bounds (sum over inverse gaps) to avoid the JK factor and better reflect necessary exploration, especially when ρ is small?
- Is there a lower bound indicating that √T is unavoidable in boundary cases?
- For unknown context distributions, can you analyze the fully online EALP (without truncation) or quantify the truncation’s practical impact?
- Could the hypergeometric argument be extended to Markovian contexts?
- Include experiments (synthetic and a simple application) to corroborate constants and robustness to misspecification.

Recommendation: Accept (weak). The paper offers a clean and impactful advance—logarithmic regret via a simple adaptive LP relaxation—backed by solid analysis. Despite some assumptions and the lack of experiments, the conceptual novelty and theoretical significance make it a worthwhile contribution.
