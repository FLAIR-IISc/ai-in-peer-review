Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies contextual bandits under a hard budget constraint and finite horizon. Each action under a context incurs a fixed cost; the process stops when time or budget is exhausted. The authors’ central idea is to approximate the optimal dynamic program (oracle) by a linear program (LP) with an average budget constraint and to adapt that LP online to the remaining budget/time ratio. For known statistics and unit costs, they propose Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP), which selects the best action per context (by expected reward) with probabilities determined by the LP solution for the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. They prove ALP achieves O(1) regret versus the oracle for non-boundary parameters (ρ ≠ cumulative context probabilities) and O(√T) in boundary cases. For unknown rewards, they combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP), maintaining UCB estimates û∗j per context and solving the adaptive LP each round. They prove RUCB-ALP(T,B) = O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They extend the approach to unknown context distributions via empirical estimates (analyzed via a truncated update variant) and to heterogeneous costs via a generalized LP and an ε-First variant.

This work differs from and complements:
- Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17], which considers more general settings and achieves O(√T) regret w.r.t. a finite policy class using Mixture Elimination, but with higher complexity and a different benchmark. Here, the benchmark is the true oracle with known statistics; contexts and actions are finite; costs are fixed; and the algorithmic design is substantially simpler.
- Bandits with concave rewards/convex knapsacks [20–22], which obtain O(√T) regret; the present paper achieves O(log T) by exploiting discrete contexts and an adaptive average-budget LP.
- Non-contextual budgeted bandits [11–16], where logarithmic or sublinear regret is known; this paper brings analogous performance to (restricted) contextual settings with random contexts.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: The LP relaxation as an analysis benchmark and the adaptive LP (ALP) policy are clean and effective. The mapping of the budget evolution under ALP to sampling without replacement yields a hypergeometric concentration result that underpins the O(1) gap for known statistics.
- Regret guarantees: For known statistics, ALP attains constant regret (non-boundary), making the LP-relaxation gap negligible. For unknown statistics, UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret, which is near-optimal instance-dependently and improves over O(√T) results in related resource-constrained contextual settings under the paper’s assumptions.
- Algorithmic simplicity and efficiency: In unit-cost systems, the adaptive LP reduces to a thresholding policy over sorted contexts, requiring only the ordering of u∗j and tolerating moderate estimation error.
- Clear decomposition of regret sources and careful handling of context-ranking versus action-ranking errors, leveraging the threshold structure to show many ranking mistakes are harmless.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Boundary cases: The O(√T) regret when ρ equals a cumulative context probability is left as an open question; it is unclear whether this is fundamental. The paper could discuss whether budget-smoothing or dithering techniques might reduce variance and improve rates near boundaries.
- Unknown context distribution: The main analysis for EALP relies on truncating updates to empirical context frequencies to regain concentration. This is somewhat artificial; a full analysis without truncation would strengthen the contribution. The dependence of regret on mixing/time to convergence of π̂ should be made explicit.
- Heterogeneous costs: The treatment is brief. The proposed ε-First ALP is only outlined; precise regret bounds and conditions (dependence on gaps and cost magnitudes) are not stated in the main text.
- Assumptions and scope: Results rely on i.i.d. contexts, finitely many contexts/actions, fixed known costs (except Section 6), unique best actions and strictly ordered u∗j (ties relegated to boundary cases). While reasonable for some applications, these limit applicability relative to more general RCB/BwK frameworks.
- Exposition details: Some statements in Theorem 1 appear to have typographical issues (e.g., terms like “−e−2δ2” without clear multipliers; “Θ(o)√T”). Exact constants and gap dependencies are deferred to the supplement, making it harder to assess instance dependence. Computational complexity per round (especially for heterogeneous costs) is not quantified precisely. Empirical evaluation is not presented in the main text.

Quality:
- The technical approach is sound under the stated assumptions. The LP upper-bound benchmark and concentration analysis via a hypergeometric model are appropriate and convincing. For UCB-ALP, the decomposition into action-ranking and context-ranking regret and bounding E[T(s)] = O(JK log T) are in line with UCB analyses.
- Many proofs are in the supplement; the main text provides sketches that are plausible. Clarifying constants and explicit dependence on reward gaps Δ and π would improve reproducibility and interpretability.

Clarity:
- Overall clear and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated; algorithm descriptions are understandable. Some notational/typographical issues and the boundary-case presentation could be refined. A brief complexity analysis and a figure illustrating the thresholding policy would help.

Originality:
- Achieving logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits with random contexts under finite, fixed-cost settings appears novel. The adaptive LP approach tailored to the budget state and its combination with UCB to separate decision-making from learning are insightful.

Significance:
- The results advance understanding of budget-constrained contextual bandits, showing that with mild structural restrictions one can surpass the O(√T) barrier common in more general formulations. The techniques (adaptive LP relaxation, budget concentration under adaptive policies) may inform broader resource-constrained online learning problems.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, simple algorithms with strong regret guarantees (O(1) with known stats; O(log T) with learning) under an important constrained setting.
- Novel analytical techniques and insights likely to be useful beyond the specific model.
- Solid connection to and improvement over prior work that achieved √T regret.

Cons against acceptance:
- Boundary-case rate suboptimality left open; limited discussion of potential remedies.
- Partial treatment of unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs; main results rely on restrictive assumptions.
- Lack of empirical validation in the main paper; constants and complexity details are deferred.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and correct theorem statements, and provide explicit constants and gap dependencies in the main text.
- Add a brief per-round complexity analysis (unit-cost threshold vs. general LP).
- Include empirical comparisons against RCB/Mixture Elimination and BwK baselines, examining sensitivity to boundary conditions and unknown π.
- Discuss budget-smoothing variations to mitigate boundary-case variance.
- Expand the heterogeneous-cost section with formal regret guarantees and a practical implementation recipe.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution under a well-motivated specialization of resource-constrained contextual bandits. I lean toward acceptance.
