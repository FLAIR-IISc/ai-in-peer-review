Summary
This paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints (constrained contextual bandits). The key technical idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle with a linear program that uses an average budget constraint, and to adapt this LP at each round using the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. For known statistics and unit costs, the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy is shown to be near-optimal: its gap to the oracle is O(1) in non-boundary cases (ρ not equal to cumulative context probabilities qj), and O(√T) on boundary instances. Building on the observation that ALP only needs the correct ordering of expected rewards, the authors combine ALP with UCB to obtain UCB-ALP, achieving O(JK log T) regret (non-boundary) and O(√T + JK log T) (boundary). They also discuss unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates and a truncated analysis) and heterogeneous costs (generalized LP and an ε-first variant). The work positions itself against RCB/Mixture Elimination and primal-dual UCB-type methods for global constraints, emphasizing computational simplicity and improved (logarithmic) regret in the finite, discrete-context setting.

Relation to prior work
- Mixture Elimination for RCB [17]: handles more general resources but with O(√T) regret and finite policy class; the present work focuses on discrete contexts/actions and provides simpler, faster algorithms with stronger instance-dependent regret.
- Primal-dual/UCB approaches for Knapsack/concave rewards with constraints [20–22]: typically achieve O(√T) regret; here, adaptivity to bτ/τ and discrete contexts yields O(log T).
- Budgeted non-contextual bandits [11–16]: results are not directly applicable with observable contexts; this paper fills that gap under simplified assumptions.

Strengths
- Novel and clean idea: the adaptive LP relaxation anchored at the remaining-average budget is simple, computationally light, and effective. The mapping of budget evolution under ALP to “sampling without replacement” with hypergeometric concentration is elegant and technically insightful.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) gap to the oracle (not just to the LP upper bound) in non-boundary instances is striking; the combination with UCB yields O(log T) regret in a constrained contextual setting where prior general approaches give O(√T).
- Decoupling of exploration and resource management: the analysis neatly separates action-ranking errors (within contexts) and context-ranking/threshold errors, which clarifies where regret comes from.
- Practicality: in unit-cost cases the LP reduces to a threshold rule with trivial computation; the algorithm only needs reward orderings, making it robust to small estimation error.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope/assumptions: The main results hinge on finite, discrete contexts, fixed known costs (unit costs in core sections), single resource, i.i.d. contexts, and rewards in [0,1]. While justified in some applications, these constraints limit generality compared to RCB. The heterogeneous-cost extension is less tight (ε-first) and would benefit from a UCB-style online variant with guarantees.
- Boundary cases: Regret degrades to O(√T) when ρ hits a cumulative context mass. Can this be mitigated by slight, data-driven dithering of the target ratio or smoothing around the threshold to recover logarithmic regret (e.g., randomization around j̃(ρ) independent of bτ/τ, or adding a small slack in the LP constraint)?
- Unknown context distribution: The rigorous analysis requires a truncated EALP that stops updating π̂ after some time. This choice is somewhat artificial; can one avoid truncation via martingale/self-normalized concentration or adaptive confidence regions over π? Empirically the non-truncated version seems fine—formalizing this would strengthen the paper.
- Optimality and lower bounds: The paper asserts order-optimality of O(JK log T) in non-boundary instances via “obtaining correct rankings requires O(log T).” A formal instance-dependent lower bound (e.g., in the style of bandit lower bounds with gaps Δ) for this constrained setting would sharpen the contribution.
- Comparison to prior work: The “first to achieve logarithmic regret” claim should be carefully scoped (finite discrete contexts/actions; single resource; i.i.d. contexts). A more explicit comparison to [21,22] under matching assumptions would help.
- Clarity/minor issues:
  - Equation (4) is truncated in the text; please give the full piecewise definition including pj=0 for j>j̃(ρ)+1.
  - The “measure-zero” comment about boundary instances is mathematically correct but practically B/T can land on boundaries; guidance on how to avoid these cases (e.g., slight budget slack) would be useful.
  - State the per-round computational cost for the LP (threshold computation is O(J) in unit-cost; heterogeneous-cost LP complexity and practical solver hints would help).
  - Assumptions like unique best action per context and strict ordering u∗1>…>u∗J are for exposition; please clarify how ties are handled in proofs and algorithms.

Quality
Technically sound with clear, well-motivated proofs (sketches in main text; details in supplement). The hypergeometric concentration argument is a nice touch. The unknown-π truncation is the main analytical gap. No significant empirical section is described here; even small-scale simulations illustrating concentration of bτ and regret curves would bolster the paper.

Clarity
Generally well-written and well-organized. A few notation/typo fixes and clearer scoping of claims would improve readability.

Originality
High. The adaptive LP relaxation with provable O(1) gap and its coupling to UCB to achieve O(log T) in constrained contextual bandits is a fresh, compelling angle relative to existing primal-dual/RCB approaches.

Significance
Meaningful. Budget constraints are ubiquitous; a simple, fast method with near-optimal performance and logarithmic regret in a standard discrete setting is valuable, and the analytical techniques (budget concentration, regret decomposition) are likely reusable.

Recommendation: Accept
Despite some limitations (scope, boundary cases, and the truncated analysis for unknown π), the paper proposes a simple and powerful idea, advances the state of the art in a concrete setting, and opens a promising direction for more general constrained contextual bandits. Constructive additions: clarify comparisons and assumptions, address boundary behavior, and, if possible, include minimal experiments and a non-truncated unknown-π analysis.
