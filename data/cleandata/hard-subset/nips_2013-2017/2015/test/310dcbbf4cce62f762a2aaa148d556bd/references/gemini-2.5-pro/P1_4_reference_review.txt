### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of contextual bandits with both a time horizon (T) and a resource budget (B), a practical setting not fully captured by standard contextual bandit models. The key challenge is that the optimal policy is a complex dynamic program (DP), making it intractable for algorithm design and analysis. The authors' central contribution is to propose and analyze an elegant approximation of this DP using a sequence of linear programs (LPs).

The core idea is developed in two stages. First, for the case with known reward statistics, the authors introduce the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. Instead of using a fixed policy, ALP solves a simple LP at each time step based on the *remaining* time and budget. This adaptive approach is shown to be remarkably effective, achieving O(1) regret (i.e., a constant performance gap to the optimal) except for a specific set of "boundary" parameters where the regret is O(√T). This ALP policy serves as a powerful and analyzable benchmark.

Second, for the more realistic case of unknown rewards, the authors propose the UCB-ALP algorithm. This algorithm combines the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle of UCB with the decision-making structure of ALP. At each step, it uses UCB estimates of the rewards to solve the adaptive LP. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) regret in boundary cases. This is a significant result, as it is the first to establish logarithmic regret for this problem setting, improving upon prior work like Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] which achieved O(√T) regret in a more general but computationally complex setting. The paper also briefly discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by being the first to achieve logarithmic regret in a constrained contextual bandit setting. The core idea of using an *adaptive* linear program to approximate the DP is both novel and elegant. It provides a clean way to decouple the complex interaction between exploration (learning rewards) and resource management (spending the budget), which is a key difficulty in this problem. This approach represents a clear advance over prior works [17, 20] that used fixed LPs and achieved O(√T) regret.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is deep and appears to be sound. The proof sketch reveals non-trivial techniques. For instance, analyzing the evolution of the remaining budget by mapping it to a "sampling without replacement" process and leveraging the concentration properties of the hypergeometric distribution is a clever and powerful analytical tool. The decomposition of regret into components from action-ranking errors and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation errors is also well-executed and provides clarity.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It builds the argument logically by first introducing the simplified known-statistics setting with the ALP algorithm. This provides the reader with the core intuition and the analytical benchmark before tackling the more complex UCB-ALP algorithm for the unknown-statistics case. The problem statement, assumptions, and main results are all clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **The "Boundary Cases" Limitation:** The distinction between O(log T) and O(√T) regret hinges on whether the budget ratio `ρ = B/T` falls on a "boundary" `q_j`. While the authors correctly state these boundaries form a set of zero Lebesgue measure, this could be a practical concern. If `ρ` is very close to a boundary, the constants in the O(log T) term could be large, making the performance closer to O(√T) for realistic values of T. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the sensitivity of the regret bounds as `ρ` approaches a boundary point. An intuitive explanation of *why* these cases are hard (e.g., the optimal policy becomes highly sensitive to small estimation errors and budget fluctuations near the threshold) would also be valuable.

2.  **Limited Scope of Main Results:** The main body of the paper focuses on a simplified model (unit costs, known context distribution). The more general and challenging cases of heterogeneous costs and unknown context distributions are discussed only briefly and deferred to the supplementary material. The proposed solution for unknown context distribution (UCB-EALP) is analyzed for a "truncated" version, which may not be practical and feels more like a proof-of-concept. While this is understandable due to space constraints, it leaves the reader wondering about the robustness and performance of the approach in these more general settings.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the addition of even simple experiments would have been highly beneficial. Simulations could have provided valuable intuition on: (i) the practical performance difference between non-boundary and boundary cases, (ii) how performance degrades as `ρ` approaches a boundary, and (iii) a comparison against existing O(√T) algorithms to demonstrate the real-world advantage of the logarithmic regret.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty and Impact:** The paper presents the first logarithmic regret algorithm for constrained contextual bandits, a significant theoretical breakthrough for an important problem.
*   **Elegant Technique:** The Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation is a clean, intuitive, and powerful idea that could inspire further research in other resource-constrained online learning problems.
*   **High Technical Quality:** The analysis is rigorous and introduces interesting techniques (e.g., the hypergeometric distribution argument for budget concentration).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Practicality of Assumptions:** The sharp distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases may overstate the performance in practice. The strong results rely on simplified assumptions (unit cost, known context distribution) in the main paper.
*   **Incomplete Extensions:** The extensions to more general settings feel underdeveloped in the main paper, making the core contribution feel somewhat narrow.
*   **No Experiments:** The lack of any empirical results makes it hard to gauge the practical significance of the asymptotic improvements and the impact of the noted limitations.

### **Overall Recommendation**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature on contextual bandits. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm and its analysis are novel and technically impressive. The achievement of logarithmic regret is a noteworthy result. While the limitations regarding the boundary cases and the simplified model in the main text are real, they do not fundamentally undermine the importance of the core contribution. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the adaptive LP approach and the quality of the theoretical results—outweigh the weaknesses. This paper will likely be of high interest to the theoretical machine learning community at NIPS.

I recommend **acceptance**. I would strongly encourage the authors to add a discussion on the practical implications of the boundary cases and, if possible, include some simulation results in the final version to further strengthen the paper.
