This paper addresses the problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a relevant extension to the standard contextual bandit framework. The authors aim to design algorithms achieving logarithmic regret, a challenging goal given the added complexity. The paper builds upon existing work in constrained bandits and resource allocation, identifying a gap in extending logarithmic regret guarantees to the contextual setting. They present an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm and, combined with UCB, a UCB-ALP algorithm, demonstrating theoretical guarantees under simplified assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs).

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction provides adequate motivation and context, relating the problem to real-world applications. The authors clearly outline their contributions and position their work relative to prior art, including recent extensions like [21, 22]. The mathematical formulations are generally accessible, and the arguments are presented logically. The presentation of the ALP algorithm and its analysis is particularly strong, showcasing a clever approach to approximating the oracle policy.  The handling of boundary cases and the extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs (presented largely in the supplementary material) are valuable additions.

The key strength of this work lies in its technical soundness. The authors provide rigorous regret analysis, demonstrating the effectiveness of their algorithms. The use of linear programming as an approximation tool is well-justified, and the adaptation of the budget constraint is insightful. The analysis, while complex, appears to be carefully conducted, building on existing concentration inequalities and providing clear explanations. The separation of analysis into known vs. unknown statistics, and fixed vs. heterogeneous costs, is a sensible approach.

However, several weaknesses exist. First, the assumptions, while acknowledged as simplifications, limit the immediate applicability of the results. Finite contexts and fixed costs are restrictive. While the authors address the unknown context distribution, the analysis appears more involved and relies on a truncated empirical distribution, potentially impacting performance in practice. The dependence on a proportional relationship between budget and time horizon (B/T) is also a significant restriction.

Second, the regret bounds, particularly for the UCB-ALP algorithm, contain a term of O(JK log T), where J is the number of contexts and K the number of actions.  This dependence on the number of actions can be problematic for problems with a large action space. While acknowledged, this is a significant factor in the algorithm's scalability.  The boundary cases also lead to a √T term, raising concerns about performance in specific scenarios.

Third, the empirical evaluation is lacking. The paper focuses heavily on theoretical analysis, with no experimental results to validate the performance of the proposed algorithms. While theoretical guarantees are important, empirical validation would strengthen the claims and provide insights into practical performance.  Comparing against existing contextual bandit algorithms, even in a simplified setting, would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to constrained contextual bandits with a focus on logarithmic regret.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the ideas.
*   Addresses a relevant and practically important problem.
*   Extension to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong simplifying assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs, proportional budget/time).
*   Regret bound dependence on the number of actions (JK log T) potentially limiting scalability.
*   Lack of empirical evaluation.
*   √T regret in boundary cases.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits. The authors address a challenging problem with a novel and well-analyzed approach. However, the strong assumptions and lack of empirical validation raise concerns about practical applicability.  While the theoretical results are impressive, the limitations warrant careful consideration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the inclusion of some empirical results, even if limited in scope, to demonstrate the performance of the algorithms in practice. Addressing the scalability concerns regarding the dependence on J and K would also strengthen the paper.
