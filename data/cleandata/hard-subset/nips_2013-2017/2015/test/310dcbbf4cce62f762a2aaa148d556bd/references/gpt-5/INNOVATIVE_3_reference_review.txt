Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits under a hard budget and finite horizon, a setting that better matches many real applications (e.g., clinical trials, crowdsourcing) than standard contextual bandits. The key idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle by a linear program that enforces an average budget constraint. The authors first analyze a static LP that yields an upper bound Û(T, B) on the oracle’s value, then introduce an adaptive linear programming (ALP) policy that plugs in the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. In unit-cost systems with known context distribution and expected rewards, ALP is shown to achieve O(1) regret (to the oracle) except on boundary parameters ρ equal to cumulative context probabilities, where regret is O(√T). Building on this, the paper proposes UCB-ALP for unknown rewards by combining ALP with UCB confidence bounds; UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. The analysis hinges on a neat connection between ALP’s consumption process and sampling-without-replacement, yielding hypergeometric concentration for the remaining budget. The authors further sketch extensions to unknown context distributions (via “empirical ALP” and a truncated variant) and to heterogeneous costs (via a larger LP and an ε-first approach).

Strengths
- Novel direction: Achieves logarithmic regret in contextual bandits with a hard budget, under i.i.d. contexts, finite contexts/actions, and fixed costs. Prior work in resourceful contextual bandits either focuses on finite policy classes or attains O(√T) regret; here the adaptive relaxation achieves substantially tighter rates.
- Conceptual simplicity and clarity: The ALP idea—adapting the LP budget to bτ/τ—and the observation that only the ordering of u∗j matters (unit-cost) make the approach practical and robust to small estimation errors.
- Clean analysis: Mapping the budget dynamics to a hypergeometric process is elegant and provides sharp concentration. The regret decomposition for UCB-ALP clearly separates action-ranking and context-ranking contributions.
- Broader impact: The decoupling of learning (ranking) and resource allocation (adaptive relaxation) is insightful and likely reusable in more general constrained bandit settings.

Weaknesses and questions
- Boundary cases: While measure-zero in a continuous sense, practical systems can be near-boundary, and finite-sample behavior may degrade. It would help to state explicit, instance-dependent constants (δ, δ′) in the main text and discuss robustness when ρ is close to a boundary.
- Assumptions: The analysis assumes (i) i.i.d. contexts with known distribution (in Sections 3–4), (ii) unit costs (until Section 6), (iii) unique best action per context and strict ordering of u∗j. These are relaxed later, but the main results rely on them. Some discussion of tie-handling and how rates change without uniqueness would improve applicability.
- Gap between oracle and LP bound: The regret analysis for UCB-ALP is carried out against Û(T, B). Theorem 1 gives O(1) gap between ALP and oracle (non-boundary), so Theorem 2 implies O(log T) to the oracle in those cases, but this connection should be made explicit in the main text to avoid confusion.
- Unknown context distribution: The main text only sketches results via a truncated empirical ALP and references the supplement. Given the centrality of this case in practice, a concise formal theorem (assumptions, rates) in the main paper would be valuable.
- Experiments: The paper mentions simulations but does not include them in the main body. Even small-scale experiments comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to Mixture Elimination, BwK-style methods, and static budget policies would strengthen the empirical case and illustrate behavior near boundaries.
- Heterogeneous costs: The extension is compelling but summarized briefly. Complexity and regret guarantees (beyond O(1)/O(√T) characterizations) would benefit from a more explicit statement and discussion of solving the per-round LP at scale.

Quality
Theoretical results are technically sound and well-motivated. The main proofs rely on standard concentration for UCB and novel application of hypergeometric concentration for the remaining budget process. Some parts crucial to practice (unknown context distribution, heterogeneous costs) are deferred to the supplement; bringing at least one formal theorem for each into the main text would improve completeness. Experimental evidence is currently missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The LP formulations and the ALP/UCB-ALP procedures are easy to follow. Some notational glitches in the piecewise definition of p (Eq. 4) should be fixed. It would help to clarify early that Theorem 2’s regret is to the oracle (by combining with Theorem 1’s O(1) gap), and to explicitly state the dependence of constants on J, K, and gaps.

Originality
High. The adaptive LP relaxation and the hypergeometric analysis deliver the first logarithmic regret guarantees in this constrained contextual bandit setting with random contexts, to the best of my knowledge, and offer a fresh angle on resource-constrained exploration.

Significance
High for the constrained bandits community. The approach is simple, computationally light (especially in unit-cost settings), and likely to influence subsequent work on multiple resources, stochastic costs, and more complex context models.

Pros
- Conceptually novel and elegant adaptive relaxation
- Strong regret guarantees (O(1) for ALP; O(log T) for UCB-ALP) under realistic assumptions
- Insightful analysis technique (sampling without replacement)
- Practical algorithms with low per-round complexity (unit-cost)

Cons
- Boundary-case behavior and near-boundary robustness not deeply explored
- Key extensions and empirical validation are relegated to the supplement
- Reliance on uniqueness and known π in the main results
- Limited discussion of scalability in heterogeneous-cost LPs

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling, novel idea with strong theoretical guarantees and clear potential for impact. The minor gaps (experiments, boundary robustness, fuller statements for extensions) are addressable and do not detract from the central contribution.

Reviewer confidence
High on the theoretical assessment; medium on empirical/practical aspects due to absent experiments.
