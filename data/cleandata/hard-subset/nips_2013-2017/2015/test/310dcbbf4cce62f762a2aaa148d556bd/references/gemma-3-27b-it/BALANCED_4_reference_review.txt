This paper tackles the challenging problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a realistic scenario often encountered in applications like crowdsourcing and clinical trials. The authors make a significant contribution by providing algorithms with logarithmic regret, a notable achievement given the complexities introduced by the constraints. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and builds upon existing literature in a sensible manner. 

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper extends the contextual bandit framework to incorporate budget constraints, bridging the gap between traditional contextual bandits [1, 2, 3] and budget-constrained multi-armed bandits [11, 12, 13].  It acknowledges the limitations of directly applying existing budget-constrained MAB algorithms to the contextual setting. The work builds on the Resourceful Contextual Bandit (RCB) framework [17] but simplifies it by focusing on finite contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, allowing for tractable analysis and algorithm design. The key innovation lies in the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which cleverly approximates the oracle policy using linear programming relaxations and adaptively adjusts the budget constraint.  The UCB-ALP algorithm then combines ALP with UCB to handle unknown rewards. This approach is shown to achieve logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases), a significant improvement over the √T regret achieved by Mixture Elimination [17] and recent extensions [21, 22]. The authors correctly position their work relative to concurrent efforts [21, 22], noting their √T regret bounds and focus on potentially infinite contexts.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Constrained contextual bandits are highly relevant to real-world applications.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** Achieving logarithmic regret in this setting is a significant technical accomplishment.
* **Clear and Well-Organized:** The paper is well-written, with a logical flow and clear explanations. The motivation is compelling.
* **Novel Approach:**  The ALP algorithm and its adaptive nature are innovative. The use of LP relaxation to approximate the oracle policy is a clever technique.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, including regret bounds and proofs, addressing the complexities of the adaptive algorithms and stochastic environments.
* **Generalization:** The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs demonstrate the robustness and adaptability of the proposed approach.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Boundary Cases:** The √T regret in boundary cases remains a concern, although the authors acknowledge it.  The conditions defining these boundary cases could be discussed more intuitively.
* **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the practical implementation and computational cost of solving the LPs at each step could be significant, particularly for large action spaces. This is not discussed.  A brief discussion of potential optimization techniques or heuristics to address this would be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of known context distribution in the initial analysis (and its relaxation later) feels somewhat limiting. While reasonable, a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to inaccuracies in the estimated context distribution would be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks empirical results. While the theoretical analysis is thorough, experimental validation would significantly strengthen the claims and provide insights into the algorithm's performance in practice.  Even a small-scale simulation study would be beneficial.
* **Notation:** The introduction of new notation (like `qj`) can be a bit dense, and a quick reference table or summary might improve readability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and practically relevant problem.
* It presents a novel algorithmic approach (ALP) with strong theoretical guarantees (logarithmic regret).
* The analysis is rigorous and demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem.
* The work contributes meaningfully to the field of contextual bandits and resource allocation.
* It builds upon existing work in a clear and logical manner.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The √T regret in boundary cases is a drawback, though acknowledged.
* The lack of empirical evaluation weakens the practical impact of the findings.
* The computational cost of the LP solver is a potential limitation that is not addressed.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The theoretical results are impressive, and the proposed algorithms are well-motivated and clearly presented. While the lack of empirical evaluation and the existence of boundary cases with √T regret are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with the request that the authors:

1. **Discuss the computational complexity** of solving the linear programs in practice and potentially suggest optimization techniques.
2. **Provide a more intuitive explanation** of the boundary cases and the conditions under which they occur.
3. **Include a small-scale simulation study** to demonstrate the algorithm's performance and validate the theoretical results.  
4. **Consider adding a summary of notation** for improved readability.
