**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget (B). The process terminates when either T rounds are completed or the budget B is exhausted. The goal is to design an algorithm that maximizes the total expected reward. The authors focus on the setting with a finite number of contexts and actions, and where T and B grow proportionally (B/T = ρ is constant).

The core challenge identified is that the optimal policy (the oracle) is a complex dynamic program (DP), which is computationally intractable and difficult to analyze, especially when reward statistics are unknown. The paper's main contribution is to propose a novel and efficient way to approximate this oracle using linear programming.

For the case with known statistics, the authors first derive an upper bound on the oracle's performance using a static Linear Program (LP). They then propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that dynamically adjusts its policy based on the remaining budget and time. They prove that this ALP algorithm achieves O(1) regret (a constant gap from the oracle) except for a specific set of "boundary" parameters where the regret is O(√T).

For the more practical case of unknown rewards, they combine the ALP framework with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle to create the UCB-ALP algorithm. This is the paper's central result. They show that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in the non-boundary cases and O(√T) in the boundary cases. This is a significant improvement over the typical O(√T) regret found in related literature on constrained bandits. The paper also briefly discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

The work builds upon the literature of both contextual bandits [1, 5, 7] and bandits with budgets [11, 12, 16], but distinguishes itself by being the first, to my knowledge, to achieve logarithmic regret in a *contextual* setting with budget constraints. It offers a more efficient and better-performing alternative to prior work on resourceful contextual bandits [17] by making simplifying, yet practical, assumptions (e.g., discrete contexts, fixed costs).

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The main strength of this paper is the achievement of logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits. This is a novel and important result, advancing the state-of-the-art from the common O(√T) regret in similar settings [17, 20]. The approach of using an adaptive LP to decouple the complex DP is both elegant and effective.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is solid and contains several novel components. The key insight is to show that the proposed ALP policy keeps the average remaining budget `b_τ/τ` concentrated around the initial ratio ρ. The analysis of this, by mapping the budget evolution to "sampling without replacement" and using properties of the hypergeometric distribution (Lemma 2), is very clever. The proof sketch for UCB-ALP, which decomposes the regret into action-ranking and context-ranking errors and analyzes their interaction with budget fluctuation, is also non-trivial and well-conceived.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It builds the argument logically, starting from the known-statistics case (oracle approximation) and then moving to the unknown-statistics case (UCB-ALP). This structure makes the core ideas easy to follow. The problem setup, challenges, and contributions are clearly articulated in the introduction.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Justification for Key Analytical Steps:** The paper claims that the concentration properties of the remaining budget (Lemma 2) still hold under the UCB-ALP algorithm. This is a crucial step in the analysis of the `R(c)` regret term. However, it is not immediately obvious why this should be true. The UCB estimates introduce dependencies over time that are not present in the known-statistics ALP case, which could affect the budget evolution. The main text should provide at least a high-level justification for why the budget dynamics remain analogous to "sampling without replacement" even with learning, as this underpins the entire regret analysis.

2.  **Treatment of Extensions:** The sections on unknown context distribution (Sec 5) and heterogeneous costs (Sec 6) feel less developed than the core contribution. The analysis for unknown context distribution is for a "truncated" version of the proposed algorithm, which is a common analytical tool but weakens the claim for the actual UCB-EALP algorithm. Furthermore, the proposed solution for heterogeneous costs, an "ε-First ALP" algorithm, is a departure from the more integrated exploration/exploitation of UCB-ALP and seems less elegant. It would be valuable to understand why a more direct UCB-style generalization was not pursued for this case.

3.  **The "Boundary Cases":** The O(√T) regret at the "boundary cases" (when ρ exactly matches a cumulative context probability `q_j`) is an interesting phenomenon. The paper presents this as a result but does not offer much intuition. Is this poor performance an artifact of the ALP approximation/analysis, or is it a fundamental property of the problem at these specific budget ratios? A brief discussion on this point would strengthen the paper's contribution.

4.  **Practical Implications of Constants:** The logarithmic regret bound O(JK log T) hides a linear dependency on the number of context-action pairs (J*K). For problems with many contexts or actions, this term could dominate the O(√T) term for realistic values of T. While this is an instance-dependent bound and standard in bandit theory, a short discussion on the practical performance implications, especially in relation to the O(√T) boundary case, would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel and significant result: the first logarithmic regret algorithm for the important problem of constrained contextual bandits.
*   **Pro:** The technical approach of using an adaptive LP to approximate the DP is elegant and powerful, and may be applicable to other resource-constrained online learning problems.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical analysis is deep and introduces new techniques for analyzing the interplay between learning and resource consumption.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written and well-structured, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The analysis relies on some key simplifying assumptions (known context distribution in the main part, discrete state-action space).
*   **Con:** Some crucial steps in the proof sketch (e.g., budget concentration under UCB-ALP) are asserted without sufficient justification in the main text.
*   **Con:** The extensions to more general settings are presented with less rigor and elegance than the main result.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The achievement of logarithmic regret is a clear advance for the field. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarifying analytical details and the completeness of the extensions, rather than fundamental flaws in the core idea. These points can likely be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
