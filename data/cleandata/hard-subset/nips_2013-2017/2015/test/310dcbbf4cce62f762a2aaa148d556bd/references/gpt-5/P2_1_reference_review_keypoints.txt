- Studies contextual bandits with a single budget and finite horizon where actions have fixed costs and the process stops at time or budget exhaustion.  
- Proposes an adaptive linear programming (ALP) relaxation using current average remaining budget bτ/τ to approximate the dynamic programming oracle.  
- Shows ALP is near-optimal with known statistics: O(1) regret except O(√T) on boundary instances where ρ equals cumulative context probabilities.  
- Combines ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) for unknown rewards, proving O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases.  
- Extends to unknown context distribution via empirical estimates with a truncated EALP analysis, and to heterogeneous costs via an LP over context-action pairs and an ε-first variant.  
- Contrasts with prior BwK/RCB work that typically attains O(√T); claims first logarithmic regret in the constrained discrete-context/action, fixed-cost single-budget setting.  
- Builds on known LP-relaxation ideas but introduces an adaptive average-budget relaxation yielding constant-gap performance and easier UCB coupling.  
- Technical core is sound: maps ALP budget process to sampling without replacement to obtain hypergeometric-style concentration for remaining budget.  
- UCB-ALP regret decomposition into within-context action ranking and context-threshold errors is reasonable; JK log T term justified by needed pairwise ordering learning.  
- Some proof details are in the supplement; the “without replacement” law for bτ needs a more explicit derivation.  
- Analysis relies on strict ordering assumptions (unique best action per context, strict u*j ordering); boundary cases are handled separately with √T regret.  
- Unknown context-distribution extension is thoughtful but the truncated analysis leaves a full non-truncated proof as future work.  
- No empirical results in the main text; experiments against RCB/BwK baselines would strengthen practical claims.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, but some notation is dense and an algorithmic description of LP implementation via sorting would help.  
- The adaptive LP + budget-smoothing idea and O(1) gap claim in non-boundary cases are novel and insightful for this regime.  
- Significance is solid within the restricted regime (finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, single resource); techniques may inspire broader extensions.  
- Pros: principled ALP algorithm, strong guarantees, novel concentration use, computational simplicity (ranking-based).  
- Cons: restrictive assumptions, regret scales with JK, missing empirical validation, partial development of extensions (truncated analyses, ε-first).  
- Requests: provide explicit budget-process derivation, clarify per-round computational complexity (e.g., O(J log J) sorting), explore mitigating boundary √T term, bound unknown-π case without truncation, and add experiments.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with clear recommendation for acceptance pending clarification of budget-process analysis and addition of empirical evidence.
