Summary
This paper studies contextual bandits with both a time horizon T and a hard budget B, a setting that captures practical constraints in applications like clinical trials and online recommendation. The key technical idea is to approximate the dynamic programming oracle with a linear program that enforces the budget in expectation and to adapt this LP online to the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. For known statistics, the proposed Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy is shown to achieve a constant regret gap O(1) to the optimal oracle except on boundary cases (where ρ = B/T equals a cumulative context mass), where the regret is O(√T). For unknown rewards, the authors combine ALP with UCB estimates (UCB-ALP) and prove O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They further discuss extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates and a truncated analysis) and to heterogeneous costs (via a generalized LP and an ε-first variant).

Relation to prior work
- Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] allows very general resource constraints and infinite policy classes, but yields O(√T) regret relative to a finite policy benchmark and is computationally heavy. This paper restricts to finite contexts/actions and fixed costs, but gives simpler, efficient algorithms and sharper regret (logarithmic).
- Bandits with concave rewards/convex knapsacks [20–22] achieve O(√T), and largely consider static contexts or finite policy sets; the present paper handles random context arrivals and leverages an adaptive LP to get O(log T).
- Budgeted/non-contextual bandits [11–16] do not carry over to observable contexts; this paper fills that gap in the finite-context setting.
- The mapping of budget evolution under ALP to “sampling without replacement” and the resulting concentration of bτ appear to be new and are central to the regret analysis.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The adaptive LP relaxation is a simple, bold idea that effectively decouples exploration from budget management. Showing that the LP surrogate yields O(1) gap to the DP oracle (away from boundaries) is insightful.
- First logarithmic regret guarantee (to my knowledge) for constrained contextual bandits with random contexts and hard budgets, under a natural finite setting.
- Computational efficiency: per-round decisions reduce to a small LP/sorting; UCB-ALP is straightforward to implement.
- Clean analysis strategy: (i) concentration of remaining budget via a sampling-without-replacement view; (ii) leveraging that ALP only needs correct reward ordering; (iii) decomposition of regret sources and bounding context/action ranking errors.
- Extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs, with plausible algorithmic adjustments and proof sketches.

Weaknesses and questions
- Boundary cases: The O(√T) regret when ρ coincides with a cumulative context mass is nontrivial in practice (ρ is chosen by the designer). Calling these “measure zero” is mathematically convenient but not fully satisfying; can one smooth away the boundary by minor randomization in budget or a slackened constraint to recover log T?
- Clarity/typos: Some displayed equations are garbled (e.g., Eq. (4) formatting; Theorem 1’s bound expressions contain terms like “u∗1−u∗J −e−2δ2” that read incorrectly; “Θ(o)√T” is confusing; Algorithm 1 has “û∗j,k∗j (t)(t)”). Please fix to avoid ambiguity about whether Theorem 1 compares to the true oracle or to the LP upper bound. The proof sketch suggests they first bound Û − UALP and then conclude U∗ − UALP ≤ Û − UALP; making this explicit would improve clarity.
- Unknown context distribution: The main text only provides a truncated EALP analysis; details are deferred to the supplement. It would strengthen the paper to state a formal theorem (even for the truncated version) in the main paper and be explicit about truncation length, dependence on J, and whether UCB-EALP retains the JK log T term.
- Heterogeneous costs: The ε-first approach is more conservative than UCB-ALP and may be suboptimal. Is a UCB-style adaptive variant feasible here as well? Any insights into instance-dependent constants would be welcome.
- Lower bounds: The paper cites [26] for order-optimality but does not provide a tailored lower bound for constrained contextual bandits. A brief discussion clarifying optimality (beyond order) would help.
- Empirical validation: The paper is purely theoretical (which is fine), but even small synthetic experiments illustrating budget concentration and the boundary vs non-boundary behavior would aid intuition.

Quality
Technically solid overall. The main regret claims are plausible and the argumentative structure is convincing. Some statements and notation need cleanup to avoid confusion about the precise comparator (oracle vs LP). Proofs for the more general settings are deferred, but the main finite, unit-cost case is adequately argued.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable, but marred by several typos/equation formatting issues. Please correct the theorem statements and algorithm pseudocode, and more clearly state the comparator used in each regret bound.

Originality
High. The adaptive LP mechanism and the O(1) approximation to a DP oracle (away from boundaries), together with the log regret via UCB-ALP in this constrained contextual setting, are novel and impactful.

Significance
High within the constrained bandits community. The approach is simple and likely to be adopted/extended. It advances the state of the art from O(√T) to O(log T) in a widely relevant setting, with efficient algorithms.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel idea (adaptive LP) with strong theoretical guarantees.
- First log-regret result in this constrained contextual regime.
- Simple, efficient algorithms that may influence practice.
- Insightful analysis techniques (budget concentration).

Cons for acceptance
- Boundary case remains at √T; practical implications not fully addressed.
- Some proofs deferred; clarity/typos need attention.
- Limited empirical illustration.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a fresh and effective direction for constrained contextual bandits with compelling theoretical advances and practical simplicity. I’m willing to overlook the minor presentation issues and boundary-case limitation given the novelty and potential impact.

Confidence: 4/5
