This paper tackles the challenging problem of contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints, a crucial extension to the standard contextual bandit framework motivated by real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors make a significant contribution by providing algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret, a notable accomplishment given the complexity introduced by the constraints. The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the problem, related work, proposed algorithms (ALP and UCB-ALP), and rigorous analysis. 

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the contextual bandit literature (Langford & Zhang, Li et al., Zhou) and highlight the gap in extending existing budget-constrained bandit algorithms (Tran-Thanh et al., Badanidiyuru et al., Jiang et al.) to the contextual setting. They acknowledge the related work on Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Badanidiyuru et al., 2017) but demonstrate the limitations of the existing Mixture Elimination approach, particularly its high complexity. The core idea is to approximate the oracle policy using linear programming (LP) with an adaptive average budget constraint, leading to the ALP algorithm. This is then combined with UCB exploration to handle unknown rewards in UCB-ALP.  The contribution is significant because it addresses a practical limitation of standard contextual bandit algorithms and achieves a strong theoretical result (logarithmic regret) in a constrained setting. They appropriately contrast their approach with recent work extending linear contextual bandits with constraints (Agrawal et al.).

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Formulation:** The problem is well-motivated and clearly defined. The inclusion of both time and budget constraints is realistic and relevant.
* **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous and well-motivated. The use of LP relaxation and the adaptive budget constraint are clever and well-justified.  The proofs, while detailed (and largely in the supplementary material), seem sound.
* **Theoretical Results:** Achieving logarithmic regret in this setting is a strong result, significantly advancing the state-of-the-art.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations are clear, and the notation is consistent.  The progression of ideas – from known statistics to unknown statistics – is logical.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors emphasize the computational efficiency of their proposed algorithms compared to alternatives like Mixture Elimination.
* **Generalization:** The extension to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs demonstrates the robustness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Boundary Cases:** The O(√T) regret in boundary cases is a significant limitation. While acknowledged, the practical implications and prevalence of these cases should be discussed further.  What characteristics of the problem parameters lead to these boundary cases?  Could techniques be developed to avoid or mitigate them?
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of known context distribution (initially) and fixed costs is a simplification. While extensions are provided, the performance implications of relaxing these assumptions should be more thoroughly investigated. How sensitive is performance to errors in estimating the context distribution?
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks a comprehensive empirical evaluation. While the authors mention simulations, more detailed results comparing UCB-ALP to other relevant algorithms (even simpler baselines) would strengthen the paper considerably.  Real-world application results would be very compelling.
* **Complexity of Proofs:**  The proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, making it difficult for readers to fully assess the validity of the technical arguments without significant effort.  Providing key lemmas and sketches of proofs in the main paper would improve readability.
* **Connection to Dynamic Programming:** The initial motivation centers around the difficulty of using dynamic programming. However, the connection to DP is largely dropped after the introduction. Briefly revisiting how the LP approximation relates to the optimal DP solution would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and practically relevant problem in contextual bandits.
* The theoretical results are strong and represent a significant advance in the field.
* The proposed algorithms are well-motivated and technically sound.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The existence of boundary cases with suboptimal regret is a significant drawback.
* The lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation weakens the claims.
* The complexity of the proofs makes it difficult for readers to fully assess the technical contributions.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The theoretical results are impressive, and the proposed algorithms are well-motivated and clearly presented. However, the limitations related to boundary cases and the lack of empirical evaluation prevent me from giving a strongly positive recommendation. 

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with Strong Encouragement for Revision.** The authors should address the weaknesses identified above, particularly by providing a more detailed discussion of the boundary cases, conducting a comprehensive empirical evaluation, and providing more key proof insights within the main body of the paper.  If these concerns are adequately addressed, the paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to NIPS.
