Quality:
- Positive: The ALP idea is simple and computationally light (solve a J-variable LP per round) and the hypergeometric view of the remaining budget is insightful. For known statistics, the constant-regret (non-boundary) guarantee is strong and practically meaningful. The UCB-ALP decomposition into action-ranking vs context-ranking errors is reasonable, and the O(JK log T) term is consistent with instance-dependent learning of a finite number of gaps.

Originality:
- Within the restricted setting (finite i.i.d. contexts, unit costs), achieving O(log T) regret via an adaptive LP that tracks b/ŌĄ seems novel and interesting. The contrast with prior BwK/RCB work (O(‚ąöT) worst-case, finite policy classes) is clear. However, many ingredients (LP relaxation, threshold structure, UCB for ranking) are standard; the novelty is in how they are combined and analyzed.

Significance:
- The idea that an adaptive average-constraint relaxation can decouple exploration from resource management and yield near-oracle performance is promising and likely to influence follow-up work. That said, the impact is weakened by reliance on strong assumptions, a fragile boundary case, and incomplete treatment of unknown ŌÄ and heterogeneous costs. The lack of empirical validation (the text mentions simulations but none are shown) further limits immediate applicability.

Pros:
- Simple, computationally efficient algorithm with strong guarantees in a common special case.
- Clean conceptual insight (b/ŌĄ adaptation; hypergeometric budget evolution).
- Logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits with discrete contexts appears new.

Cons:
- Many clarity/notation/formatting errors; several key statements are ambiguous or informal.
- Some technical claims (concentration under UCB-ALP) are asserted without sufficient rigor.
- Boundary case remains weak; ‚Äúmeasure-zero‚ÄĚ justification is unsatisfactory.
