

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits with budget constraints, proposing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm and a UCB-ALP variant for unknown reward statistics. The authors claim to achieve O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases, which they assert is the first such result for constrained contextual bandits.

The paper is well-structured and provides a clear theoretical framework. The connection between dynamic programming and linear programming for approximating the oracle solution is theoretically interesting. The authors correctly identify the key challenge of budget constraints in contextual bandits and attempt to address it through adaptive linear programming. The paper builds upon prior work on contextual bandits [1-7] and constrained bandits [11-16, 17], and correctly notes the limitations of previous approaches for contextual settings.

**Strengths:**
- The problem formulation is well-motivated by real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing.
- The theoretical analysis is technically detailed with well-structured lemmas and theorems.
- The paper correctly identifies the limitations of prior approaches for contextual settings with budget constraints.
- The distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases is a valuable theoretical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper has significant shortcomings that prevent me from recommending acceptance. Most critically, the paper lacks any empirical validation. The authors make strong theoretical claims about achieving logarithmic regret but provide no experimental results to support these claims. NIPS has a strong tradition of requiring both theoretical and empirical validation for algorithmic contributions, and this paper falls short on the empirical front.

The boundary case analysis is problematic. The authors admit that for boundary cases (which occur when ρ equals certain cumulative probabilities), the regret is O(√T), which is significantly worse than the claimed logarithmic regret. The paper does not provide any guidance on how often these boundary cases occur in practice or how to handle them effectively. The claim that the regret is "logarithmic except for boundary cases" is misleading without context on the prevalence of these cases.

The scope of the problem is severely restricted. The paper assumes unit costs (cj,k=1), known context distributions, and finite discrete contexts. These are significant simplifications that may not hold in real applications. While the paper acknowledges these limitations in Sections 5 and 6, it does not thoroughly analyze how these simplifications affect the practical performance of the algorithms. The claim that this is "the first work that shows how to achieve logarithmic regret" is questionable given that the authors themselves acknowledge previous work [17, 20] that achieves O(√T) regret for more general settings.

The comparison with related work is incomplete. The paper cites [17] which achieves O(√T) regret for resourceful contextual bandits, but does not clearly explain why their approach is superior for the specific case they study. The paper also fails to address how their approach compares to [21, 22] which have recently extended [20] to dynamic contexts with O(√T) regret, making their claim of novelty questionable.

The theoretical analysis, while detailed, lacks practical insights. The paper does not discuss the actual constant factors in the regret bound, which would be crucial for understanding whether the algorithm is practically useful. The O(1) regret for ALP is only achieved "except for certain boundary cases," and the paper doesn't quantify how large these constants are or how they scale with the problem size.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the theoretical framework is interesting, the paper fails to meet NIPS standards for algorithmic contributions. The lack of empirical validation is particularly concerning, as the authors make strong claims about algorithmic performance without any experimental evidence. The boundary case issue is not adequately addressed, and the scope of the problem is too restricted to claim significant practical impact. The paper would benefit from substantial empirical validation, a more thorough discussion of boundary cases, and a clearer comparison with recent related work [21, 22].

The paper's theoretical contribution is marginal compared to the recent work [21, 22] that achieves similar regret bounds for more general settings. Without empirical validation and a more nuanced discussion of the boundary cases, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. I encourage the authors to conduct extensive experiments and thoroughly analyze the boundary cases before resubmitting.
