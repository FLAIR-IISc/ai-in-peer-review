Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget B and time horizon T, where each action under a context incurs a known cost and yields a random bounded reward with unknown mean. The process stops when budget or time runs out; the goal is to maximize expected total reward. Focusing on finite contexts and actions, unit costs (later extended), and i.i.d. contexts, the authors propose: (i) an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that, when statistics are known, uses a linear program with an average budget constraint bτ/τ to choose a threshold policy over contexts in each round; and (ii) a UCB-ALP policy that plugs UCB estimates into ALP when rewards are unknown. They analyze ALP via a “sampling without replacement” view of remaining budget, proving ALP is within O(1) of optimal (except at boundary parameters where ρ equals a cumulative context probability, yielding O(√T) regret). With unknown rewards, UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) at the boundaries. They also discuss unknown context distributions (empirical estimates with a truncated analysis) and heterogeneous costs (generalized LP and an epsilon-first variant).

The work is positioned against: Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) and Mixture Elimination [17] (O(√T) regret within a finite policy class, high complexity), Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) [12] (general constraints with O(√T) regret), and concave-reward/convex-knapsack contextual bandits [20–22] (O(√T) regret, static or dynamic contexts). The paper claims to be the first to obtain logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts/actions.

Strengths:
- Conceptual simplicity and computational efficiency: ALP reduces a dynamic program to an adaptive LP that in the unit-cost case has a closed-form threshold/randomization solution depending only on the ordering of u∗j. This is easy to implement and interpret.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: ALP is shown to be O(1)-optimal versus the oracle (non-boundary), and UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) instance-dependent regret, improving over prior O(√T) bounds in related constrained settings under the stated assumptions.
- Clean analytical techniques: Mapping remaining budget evolution under ALP/UCB-ALP to hypergeometric sampling yields sharp concentration of bτ/τ. The decomposition of regret into action-ranking and context-ranking parts, with careful event conditioning, is novel and instructive.
- Scope of discussion: The paper treats unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, outlines how the approach generalizes, and highlights why boundary cases are harder.

Weaknesses and points to clarify:
- Scope limitations: Main results rely on finite contexts, unit costs, i.i.d. contexts, known context distribution (for the core theorems), uniqueness of the best action per context, and a strict ordering u∗1 > … > u∗J. While the authors argue these are practical, they substantially narrow applicability relative to [12,17,20–22]. Please clarify how ties (equal u∗’s) are handled beyond a “measure-zero” statement and whether continuity arguments provide robustness guarantees.
- Boundary cases: For ρ = qj the regret degrades to O(√T). Are there algorithmic tweaks (e.g., intentional dithering of acceptance probabilities or conservative budgets) that can provably restore log regret except within o(1) neighborhoods of the boundary? A lower bound for boundary cases would strengthen the claim that O(√T) is unavoidable there.
- Unknown context distribution: The EALP/UCB-EALP analysis uses a truncation (stop updating π̂ after a fixed time). This seems algorithmically unnatural. Can you state explicit choices of the truncation time and the resulting regret constants, and explain whether a fully online estimator can be handled with a different concentration tool (e.g., self-normalized processes or martingale Efron–Stein)?
- Heterogeneous costs: The per-round LP is larger (O(JK) variables) and the unknown-reward solution adopts epsilon-first. What regret rate is achieved (as a function of gaps and JK)? Is epsilon-first necessary, or can a UCB-style primal-dual approach with adaptive budgets recover O(log T)?
- Relation to prior methods: There is a close connection to primal-dual or Lagrangian methods used in BwK and online stochastic knapsack; discussing this (and why your adaptive budget ratio control yields sharper dependence) would help situate the contribution. Also consider mentioning constrained MDP approximate LP literature beyond [19].
- Empirical validation: The paper makes strong claims about computational simplicity and robustness; even a small experimental section (finite J,K; comparisons to fixed-LP, BwK variants, and [20]) would be valuable. Without experiments, the paper reads as purely theoretical.
- Presentation details: Some equations/notation have minor typos (e.g., Eq. (4) formatting), and the uniqueness/ordering assumptions could be stated once and referenced to avoid repetition. Please ensure all constants and events used in proofs are precisely defined in the main text or clearly deferred to the supplement.

Quality: The theoretical development appears sound, with clear bounding strategies and appropriate use of concentration. The main theorems are plausible given the structure of ALP; complete proofs are in the supplement.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Adding a short pseudocode for ALP (unit-cost case) and a figure illustrating the threshold policy versus ρ would aid readers.

Originality: Combining adaptive LP relaxation with UCB to achieve O(log T) in constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts is, to my knowledge, novel. The O(1) gap to oracle under known statistics is a particularly nice and new insight.

Significance: Within the finite-context/action setting, the results improve the regret rate and may influence subsequent work on constrained contextual bandits and learning with resources. Practical impact depends on relaxing assumptions (unknown π, heterogeneous costs) and demonstrating empirical performance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, principled algorithmic idea with strong, sharp guarantees.
- Advances state of the art from O(√T) to O(log T) under a well-defined setting.
- Analysis techniques (budget concentration, regret decomposition) are of independent interest.

Cons against acceptance:
- Restricted setting and strong simplifying assumptions; boundary cases remain suboptimal.
- Limited treatment (and weaker algorithms) for heterogeneous costs and unknown π.
- No experiments to corroborate practicality and constants.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Can you provide explicit constants in the O(JK log T) term (gap-dependent bounds) and discuss dependence on minimal gaps between u∗’s?
- Is there a high-probability regret bound (not only in expectation)?
- For unknown π, can you avoid truncation via a two-timescale estimator or robust confidence bounds on π?
- For boundary cases, could a small random perturbation of ρ or soft-thresholding reduce the √T term?

Overall recommendation: borderline accept for a theory-focused track. The paper offers a clean algorithmic framework with improved regret guarantees and insightful analysis for constrained contextual bandits in a finite setting. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the extensions would solidify the contribution. Confidence: medium-high.
