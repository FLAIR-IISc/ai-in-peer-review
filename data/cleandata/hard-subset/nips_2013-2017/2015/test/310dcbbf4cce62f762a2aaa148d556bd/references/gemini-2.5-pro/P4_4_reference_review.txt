This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon `T` and a budget `B`. The authors propose and analyze computationally efficient algorithms that achieve logarithmic or sublinear regret in the asymptotic regime where `B/T` is a fixed constant `ρ`.

The main idea is to approximate the optimal policy, which is the solution to a computationally intractable dynamic program (DP), with a policy derived from a simpler linear program (LP). First, for the case with known statistics, the authors introduce a static LP that relaxes the hard budget constraint into an average one. The optimal value of this LP provides a tractable upper bound on the oracle's performance, which serves as a benchmark for regret. They then propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which at each step solves a similar LP but uses the current remaining budget and time horizon to set the average budget constraint. This adaptive approach is shown to be near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret, except for a set of "boundary cases" of measure zero where the regret is O(√T).

For the more practical setting with unknown reward distributions, the paper combines the ALP framework with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. The core theoretical contribution of the paper is proving that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in the non-boundary cases and O(√T) in the boundary cases. This is a significant improvement over the O(√T) regret achieved by prior and concurrent work. The paper also discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

This work relates to several lines of research. It extends the classic contextual bandit framework by incorporating budget constraints, a topic previously explored mainly in non-contextual bandits [12, 16]. The problem setting can be viewed as a specialization of the more general Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17]. While the RCB framework is more general (e.g., allowing infinite contexts), the proposed work trades this generality for a significantly improved, instance-dependent regret bound (O(log T) vs. O(√T)) and a much more computationally efficient algorithm. The key differentiator from concurrent work [20, 21, 22] appears to be the use of an adaptive budget constraint in the LP formulation, which is central to achieving logarithmic regret.

### Strengths:

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The analysis of the ALP algorithm, by mapping the evolution of the remaining budget to a sampling-without-replacement process and leveraging concentration inequalities, is elegant and rigorous. The subsequent analysis of UCB-ALP correctly decomposes the regret and builds upon established techniques for analyzing UCB-style algorithms. The main results (Theorems 1 and 2) are strong and well-supported by the provided proof sketches.
*   **Originality:** The primary contribution—the design and analysis of the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy—is novel. While using LP to approximate DPs is a known technique, its application in an adaptive, per-timestep manner based on the remaining resource ratio is a clever and effective idea for this problem. This specific approach appears to be the key innovation that enables the logarithmic regret bound, which, to my knowledge, is the first of its kind for this problem setting.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. Achieving O(log T) regret for a constrained bandit problem is a major theoretical advance. The problem is well-motivated by practical applications where resources are limited. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is simple to implement and computationally efficient, making it practical. The analytical framework developed in this paper could prove influential for tackling other online learning problems with resource constraints.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It builds its argument logically, starting from the oracle setting with known statistics to motivate the ALP algorithm, and then extending it to the unknown-reward setting with UCB-ALP. The introduction clearly situates the work in the literature, and the proof sketches provide valuable intuition for the main technical arguments.

### Weaknesses:

*   **Assumptions:** The main O(log T) regret result is derived under several simplifying assumptions: finite and discrete contexts/actions, unit costs for all actions, and, most importantly, a known context distribution. While the paper discusses extensions to unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs in Sections 5 and 6, the analysis for these more general cases is brief and largely deferred to the supplementary material. In particular, the analysis for the unknown context distribution is for a "truncated" version of the algorithm, which may not fully capture the dynamics of the more natural empirical approach.
*   **Boundary Cases:** The analysis identifies "boundary cases" (when `ρ = B/T` equals a cumulative context probability) where the regret degrades from logarithmic (or constant for ALP) to O(√T). While the authors argue this is a set of measure zero, it could be a practical issue if the system's budget ratio `ρ` is close to one of these boundaries. A more detailed discussion of the algorithm's sensitivity near these boundaries would be beneficial.
*   **Complexity of Extensions:** The description of the heterogeneous cost case suggests that the per-step LP becomes significantly more complex, involving all context-action pairs. This might impact the claimed computational efficiency, and a more explicit discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  The paper presents the first algorithm for constrained contextual bandits that achieves logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art O(√T) bounds.
2.  The core technical idea of using an adaptive LP relaxation is novel, elegant, and provides a powerful new tool for analyzing constrained online learning problems.
3.  The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is simple, computationally efficient, and addresses a well-motivated practical problem.
4.  The paper is of high quality, being technically sound and exceptionally clear in its presentation.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  The strongest results are obtained under restrictive assumptions (e.g., unit costs, known context distribution). The analysis for the more general and practical extensions is less complete in the main paper.
2.  The performance guarantee degrades for a specific set of "boundary case" parameters.

In summary, the strengths of this paper, particularly the novelty of the approach and the significance of the logarithmic regret bound, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It represents a clear and important advance in the field of multi-armed bandits.
