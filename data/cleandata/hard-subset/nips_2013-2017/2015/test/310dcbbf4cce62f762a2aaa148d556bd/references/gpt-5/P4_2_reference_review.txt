Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with both a time horizon T and a hard budget B, where each action under a context yields a reward and incurs a (fixed) cost; the process stops when time or budget is exhausted. The goal is to maximize expected total reward with B and T scaling proportionally (ρ = B/T). This setting is a special case of Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) but with finite contexts and actions, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint.

The main technical idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle by a linear program (LP) that relaxes the hard budget to an average budget. A “static” LP yields an explicit per-round value v(ρ) and an upper bound Û(T,B)=T v(ρ) on the oracle’s expected reward. Building on this, the authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that, at round t with remaining time τ and remaining budget bτ, solves the LP with average constraint bτ/τ and uses the resulting randomized threshold policy. For unit costs and known context distribution, ALP is shown to achieve O(1) regret vs. the optimal oracle except in boundary cases where ρ equals a cumulative context probability qj, in which case regret is O(√T). The analysis hinges on a coupling that shows budget consumption under ALP behaves as sampling without replacement; the remaining budget bτ is hypergeometric and concentrates around ρτ.

For unknown expected rewards, the paper combines ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP). It proves RUCB-ALP(T,B)=O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases (and O(√T+JK log T) on the boundary), where J is the number of contexts and K the number of actions. The regret is decomposed into within-context action-ranking errors (handled by standard UCB analysis) and errors due to mis-ranking contexts in the ALP threshold (bounded via concentration of bτ and high-probability correctness of UCB orderings). The paper also sketches extensions: empirical estimation of the context distribution (EALP/UCB-EALP, with a truncated-update analysis via bounded differences) and heterogeneous costs (generalized ALP over all context-action pairs and an ε-first variant for learning).

Relative to prior work, Mixture Elimination for RCB achieves O(√T) regret but uses a finite policy set and higher complexity; “Bandits with Knapsacks” and follow-ups obtain O(√T) in broader settings (often static or adversarial contexts). For non-contextual budgeted bandits, logarithmic regret is known. This paper appears to be the first to obtain logarithmic (instance-dependent) regret for constrained contextual bandits with random i.i.d. contexts under the stated simplifications.

Strengths
- Clear modeling of a practically relevant constrained contextual bandit with hard budget, iid contexts, and fixed costs.
- Conceptually simple, computationally efficient ALP/UCB-ALP policies; per-round LP reduces to a threshold rule in unit-cost case.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) regret for ALP vs. the oracle (most instances) and O(JK log T) for UCB-ALP; analysis carefully partitions regret sources and leverages a neat sampling-without-replacement coupling.
- Good positioning relative to RCB and Bandits with Knapsacks; explains why prior O(√T) results do not carry over and how adaptivity in the budget constraint is crucial.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Scope restrictions: finite discrete contexts/actions; iid contexts; known horizon; single budget; unit costs (main results). While reasonable for certain applications, this narrows significance relative to more general RCB settings.
- Boundary cases: regret degrades to O(√T) when ρ equals a boundary qj. Although measure-zero in a continuous sense, such parameter choices can occur in practice. It would help to discuss simple perturbations (e.g., small dithering in the target average budget) that provably remove the √T term while preserving feasibility.
- Assumptions on uniqueness and strict ordering (unique best action per context; strictly ordered u∗j across contexts) simplify the analysis. The paper should clarify how ties are broken and whether guarantees degrade gracefully under ties.
- Unknown context distribution: the main text defers the technical analysis to a truncated EALP in the supplement. Including a brief theorem statement and conditions in the main body would strengthen clarity and credibility.
- Heterogeneous costs: the ALP generalization and ε-first learning are only sketched; there is no log-regret counterpart, and guarantees remain O(1)/O(√T) for known rewards. A clearer statement of what is and is not proved here would be useful.
- Lack of empirical evaluation: the text alludes to simulations, but none are shown here. Even small-scale experiments would help illustrate the constant-regret behavior of ALP and the O(log T) scaling of UCB-ALP, and demonstrate robustness to boundary proximity and misspecified π.
- Lower bounds: the paper asserts order-optimality (non-boundary) by appealing to identification costs. An explicit statement referencing an appropriate instance-dependent lower bound (e.g., Lai–Robbins-type) for this constrained setting would strengthen the originality/significance claims.

Quality
- Technical development is sound overall. The coupling that yields hypergeometric bτ and concentration appears correct and is a key insight. The UCB analysis and regret decomposition are in line with standard techniques, adapted to the threshold structure. However, many proofs are deferred; adding more detail on the most novel steps (budget coupling; context-ranking error counts) in the main text would improve verifiability.

Clarity
- Generally well organized with helpful intuition and proof sketches. There are minor notation/formatting issues (e.g., piecewise definition in Eq. (4) seems truncated) and occasional typos. Algorithm 1 could be streamlined and the dependence on strict ordering assumptions made explicit.

Originality
- The adaptive LP approximation to a hard-budget contextual bandit and the demonstration of O(1) regret vs. the oracle (non-boundary) are novel in this constrained contextual setting. Achieving O(log T) with UCB-ALP under iid contexts and budget adaptivity appears new relative to prior RCB/BwK results.

Significance
- The results provide a simple, computationally light approach with strong guarantees under common practical assumptions. Although narrower in scope than general RCB, the techniques (adaptive relaxation, budget concentration) may inform broader settings. Impact is moderate to high within the restricted model class.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple algorithms with strong, interpretable guarantees; novel constant/log regret in constrained contextual bandits.
- Insightful analysis techniques (sampling-without-replacement coupling; error decomposition).
- Clear improvement over O(√T) benchmarks in related work under stated assumptions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Restricted setting and boundary-case degradation; limited treatment of unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.
- Absence of experiments; reliance on deferred proofs for key results; minor clarity issues.
- No explicit lower bounds in this exact setting.

Suggestions for the authors
- Discuss boundary smoothing strategies; clarify handling of ties.
- Summarize the unknown-π theorem and conditions in the main text.
- Provide small-scale empirical results.
- Tighten notation and fix minor formatting errors; precisely state assumptions in all theorems.
