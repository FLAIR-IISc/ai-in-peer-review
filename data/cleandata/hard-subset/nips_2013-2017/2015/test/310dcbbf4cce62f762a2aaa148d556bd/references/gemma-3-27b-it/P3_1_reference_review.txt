## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the important problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a realistic scenario often overlooked in traditional bandit literature. The authors make a significant contribution by developing algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret under certain assumptions, bridging a gap between theoretical advances in unconstrained and non-contextual constrained bandits. They begin by analyzing the oracle algorithm, approximating it with linear programming (LP) and introducing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. Building on this, they propose a UCB-ALP algorithm for the unknown reward setting, achieving O(log T) regret (except in boundary cases where it’s O(√T)). The analysis is thorough, employing novel techniques to handle the interplay between exploration, budget constraints, and adaptive algorithms.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the advancements in unconstrained contextual bandits (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 7]) and the successful application of logarithmic regret algorithms in non-contextual constrained bandits [11-16]. It clearly distinguishes its work from Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] by focusing on a simplified model with finite contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, allowing for more efficient and analytically tractable algorithms. The comparison with recent extensions to [20] [21, 22] is also accurate; this paper achieves logarithmic regret where those works currently achieve √T. The references are comprehensive and well-chosen.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem – constrained contextual bandits – that significantly expands the applicability of bandit algorithms.
* **Novel Approach:**  The combination of LP relaxation for oracle approximation and the development of ALP is a clever and innovative approach. Adapting this to the UCB framework for unknown rewards is a significant extension.
* **Strong Theoretical Analysis:** The paper’s analysis is rigorous and detailed.  The techniques used to handle the interplay between the budget, time, and adaptive nature of the algorithms (e.g., concentration properties under adaptive algorithms, bounding estimation errors) are impressive.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite the complexity of the problem and the analysis, the paper is generally well-written and organized.  The motivation is clearly articulated, and the steps in the algorithm development are logically presented.
* **Practical Relevance:** The simplified assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs) make the algorithm potentially more practical for applications like clinical trials and rate selection, as stated.
* **Insights into Generalization:** The discussion of extending the results to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs (in the supplementary material) demonstrates a broader understanding and potential for future work.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Boundary Cases:** The √T regret in boundary cases is a significant limitation. While acknowledged, the conditions defining these cases and their practical implications are not fully explored.  Further investigation into mitigating the impact of these cases would be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of known context distribution in the main result (while addressed in Section 5 and the supplementary material) feels restrictive, especially given the focus on practicality.  The analysis for the unknown context distribution case is less detailed and relies on truncating the empirical distribution, which might affect performance in some scenarios.
* **Complexity of LP solution:** Although the authors claim ALP is easily implementable, solving LP repeatedly is still computationally expensive, particularly for a large number of contexts and actions. Discussion on efficient LP solvers and approximation techniques would be useful.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the theoretical analysis is thorough, the paper lacks a comprehensive empirical evaluation.  Simulations demonstrating the algorithm's performance against baseline algorithms (e.g., UCB, LinUCB with budget constraints) would significantly strengthen the claims and provide insights into the practical behavior of the algorithm.
* **Lack of discussion on tightness of the logarithmic regret:** The authors state they achieve logarithmic regret. A discussion on whether it is theoretically possible to obtain tighter bounds would be valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important and under-explored problem in contextual bandits.
* The proposed algorithms are novel and achieve strong theoretical guarantees (logarithmic regret in most cases).
* The analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the challenges of constrained bandit problems.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The √T regret in boundary cases is a significant limitation.
* The reliance on simplifying assumptions (known context distribution) and lack of extensive empirical evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits. The theoretical results are impressive, and the proposed algorithms are promising. While the limitations regarding boundary cases and empirical validation are notable, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance at NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the boundary cases and potentially including a more thorough empirical evaluation.

**Rating:** Strong Accept (with minor revisions)

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

* **Boundary Cases:**  Investigate the conditions leading to boundary cases in more detail and explore potential strategies to mitigate their impact.
* **Empirical Evaluation:**  Include simulations comparing UCB-ALP with relevant baseline algorithms on a range of benchmark environments.
* **LP Solver Discussion:** Discuss the computational cost of solving the LP repeatedly and potential strategies for optimization.
* **Tightness discussion:** Briefly discuss the possibility of achieving tighter regret bounds.
