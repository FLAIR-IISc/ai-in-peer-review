This paper addresses the problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a relevant and practically motivated extension of the standard contextual bandit setting. The authors aim to design algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret in this constrained environment. The work builds upon existing literature on contextual bandits, resource allocation, and linear programming relaxations. 

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction outlining the problem, its motivations, and a connection to related work. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging related approaches like Mixture Elimination and highlighting their limitations. The core contribution lies in designing the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm and demonstrating its near-optimality, and then extending this to the unknown reward setting via UCB-ALP. The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are also valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** Constrained contextual bandits are highly relevant to real-world applications with budget limitations, such as clinical trials and online advertising.
*   **Clear Technical Presentation:** The paper presents the mathematical formulation and algorithmic details in a clear and concise manner. The use of linear programming to approximate the oracle is a well-motivated approach.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithms, showing logarithmic regret under certain conditions, a significant contribution.
*   **Adaptive Approach:** The adaptive nature of the ALP algorithm, adjusting the budget constraint over time, is a key strength that allows for better resource allocation.
*   **Comprehensive Extensions:** The extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs demonstrate the versatility of the approach.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the related literature and properly cite relevant work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Boundary Cases:** The logarithmic regret guarantee is not universally applicable, with a √T regret in boundary cases. While acknowledged, the practical implications and frequency of these boundary cases are not thoroughly discussed. Further investigation into mitigating the regret in these cases would be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on certain simplifying assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs). While acknowledged, the robustness of the algorithms to violations of these assumptions isn't fully addressed.
*   **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs, although presumably correct, are complex and detailed. It would benefit from high-level intuition behind key steps.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the paper focuses on theoretical analysis, a small-scale empirical evaluation could provide further insights and validation of the algorithms’ performance in practice.  Even simulated data could strengthen the claims.
*   **Comparison with Recent Work:** The authors acknowledge some recent papers [21, 22] appearing during the submission process, but the comparison is brief. A more detailed discussion of how the presented work differs from and complements these recent results is warranted.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important and challenging problem in reinforcement learning.
*   The proposed algorithms are novel and achieve strong theoretical guarantees.
*   The technical contributions are significant and advance the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits.
*   The presentation is clear and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The logarithmic regret guarantee is not universally applicable, and the boundary cases remain a concern.
*   The lack of empirical evaluation limits the practical validation of the algorithms.
*   The complexity of the proofs might hinder reproducibility and understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant technical contributions to the field of constrained contextual bandits. The authors present a well-motivated problem, a novel algorithm (ALP), and rigorous theoretical guarantees. While the limitations related to boundary cases and the lack of empirical evaluation are valid concerns, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.  The novelty of the approach and the theoretical rigor are compelling.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should consider providing more discussion on the practical impact of the boundary cases and adding a small-scale empirical evaluation to complement the theoretical analysis. A more detailed comparison with the recent related works [21, 22] is also desirable.
