- Tackles constrained contextual bandits (budget + time) and fills a practical gap beyond standard models.  
- Proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) using LP approximations to approximate the oracle policy.  
- Introduces UCB-ALP for unknown rewards and achieves O(log T) regret (with √T in boundary cases).  
- Strong theoretical analysis supporting logarithmic regret under reasonable assumptions.  
- Positions work well relative to prior constrained-bandit literature and highlights limitations of existing approaches.  
- Identifies [17] (Mixture Elimination) as related but complex and restrictive compared to this approach.  
- Improves over recent extensions ([21,22]) that only obtain √T regret.  
- Addresses an important, real-world-relevant problem (clinical trials, advertising, resource allocation).  
- ALP’s adaptive budget adjustment is a clever, performance-improving idea.  
- Clear presentation and logical organization; LP-based oracle approximation is well-explained.  
- Extends analysis beyond unit-cost systems to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.  
- Introduces novel technical tools (concentration under adaptive algorithms, error bounds with random contexts).  
- Relies on simplifying assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs) that limit applicability.  
- Performance degrades to √T in boundary cases; need clarity on how common these are in practice.  
- Generalization details are mostly in the supplement, reducing perceived accessibility and clarity.  
- Lacks empirical evaluation, leaving constant factors and practical performance unverified.  
- Discussion on Dynamic Programming challenges could use a more concrete illustration beyond the "curse of dimensionality."  
- Reviewer recommendation: strong accept, but urges adding experiments and moving generalization discussion into the main paper.
