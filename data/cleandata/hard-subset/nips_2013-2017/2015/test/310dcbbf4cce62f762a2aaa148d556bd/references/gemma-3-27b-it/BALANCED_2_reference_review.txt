This paper addresses the important problem of contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints. The authors provide a theoretically sound analysis and propose algorithms achieving logarithmic regret under certain conditions, a significant contribution to the field. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the problem setup, proposed algorithms, and theoretical analysis. It builds upon existing work in contextual bandits and resource allocation, appropriately citing relevant literature. The authors also acknowledge related work published concurrently, demonstrating awareness of the research landscape.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a practically relevant extension of contextual bandits by incorporating budget constraints, which are frequently encountered in real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** Achieving logarithmic regret in this setting is non-trivial. The authors successfully demonstrate this for a simplified, yet realistic, model, providing strong theoretical guarantees.
* **Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP):** The introduction of ALP as a key technique to approximate the oracle is insightful. The analysis of ALP and its connection to linear programming are well-presented.
* **UCB-ALP Algorithm:** Combining ALP with UCB provides a practical algorithm for the unknown reward setting, offering a good balance between exploration and exploitation.
* **Generalizations:** The paper extends the results to scenarios with unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, demonstrating the robustness of the proposed approach.
* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the technical details. The notation is consistent and the proofs seem logically sound (though detailed verification would require more time).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Simplifying Assumptions:** While justified, the assumptions of finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint limit the applicability of the results to more general settings. The paper acknowledges this, but a discussion of potential future work addressing these limitations would be beneficial.
* **Boundary Cases:** The O(√T) regret in boundary cases is a significant drawback. The authors provide some discussion, but it would be valuable to explore strategies to mitigate this issue or understand under what conditions these cases are likely to occur in practice.  A more detailed characterization of these boundary cases would be helpful.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the algorithms are described as computationally efficient, a more concrete analysis of the computational complexity (e.g., in terms of the number of contexts and actions) would strengthen the paper. Comparing the complexity to existing algorithms for related problems would be insightful.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks empirical results. While the theoretical analysis is strong, experimental validation on synthetic or real-world datasets would significantly enhance the credibility of the findings and demonstrate the practical performance of the proposed algorithms.
* **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** While the authors cite related work, the discussion feels somewhat brief. A more in-depth comparison of the proposed algorithms with other relevant approaches, particularly those addressing resource allocation in bandits, would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in contextual bandits.
* It provides strong theoretical guarantees for achieving logarithmic regret.
* The proposed algorithms are innovative and well-motivated.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and logically organized.
* It contributes to the advancement of the field of reinforcement learning and bandit algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The simplifying assumptions limit the generalizability of the results.
* The √T regret in boundary cases is a potential weakness.
* The lack of empirical evaluation weakens the practical impact of the work.
* A more thorough comparison with existing work would be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proposed algorithms are promising. While the simplifying assumptions and lack of empirical evaluation are drawbacks, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Elaborate on the boundary cases:** Provide a more detailed characterization of the conditions under which these cases occur and discuss potential strategies to mitigate their impact.
2.  **Add empirical evaluation:** Include experiments on synthetic or real-world datasets to demonstrate the practical performance of the proposed algorithms.
3.  **Expand the related work discussion:**  Provide a more comprehensive comparison of the proposed algorithms with other relevant approaches.
4.  **Include a complexity analysis**:  Provide an analysis of computational complexity.
