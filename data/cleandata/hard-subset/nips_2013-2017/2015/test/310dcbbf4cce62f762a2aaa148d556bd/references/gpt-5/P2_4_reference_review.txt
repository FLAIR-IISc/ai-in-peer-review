Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget and a finite horizon, focusing on the practically relevant regime where budget B and horizon T scale with fixed ratio ρ = B/T. The authors propose an adaptive linear programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle (which would otherwise be a dynamic program) by relaxing the hard constraint to an average budget and solving a per-round LP that uses the instantaneous average remaining budget bτ/τ. They show that, with known statistics and unit costs, ALP achieves O(1) regret against the true oracle for all non-boundary parameters (ρ not equal to a cumulative context probability), and O(√T) regret on boundary cases. For unknown rewards, they combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) and prove O(JK log T) regret (plus O(√T) in boundary cases), where J and K are the numbers of contexts and actions. They also discuss extensions to unknown context distributions (empirical estimation with a truncated-update analysis) and to heterogeneous costs (a generalized LP and an ε-first variant).

The work connects to Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB). Prior work achieves O(√T) regret under more general settings (e.g., mixture elimination in RCB, concave reward/convex constraints with static or later dynamic contexts), often with heavier computation and regret defined against a finite policy set. Here the authors focus on finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, and one budget, and claim the first logarithmic regret guarantees in this constrained contextual setting, using the true oracle as benchmark.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The analysis is careful and largely convincing. A key insight is that under ALP the budget consumption in each round is Bernoulli with success probability b/τ, yielding a “sampling without replacement” view; this gives exact expressions for E[bτ], Var[bτ], and sharp concentration bounds through Hoeffding-type inequalities for hypergeometric distributions. This is a neat and powerful simplification that enables the O(1) regret proof when ρ is not at a threshold. The regret decomposition for UCB-ALP into action-ranking and context-ranking errors is standard in flavor but adapted well to the adaptive-budget setting; bounding the number of mis-rank events via UCB concentration is reasonable and justifies the O(JK log T) term.
- Claims vs support: The main claims (O(1) for ALP with known means, O(log T) for UCB-ALP, and O(√T) on boundary) are supported by sketched arguments in the main text and more details in the supplement. The unknown-context case is addressed via a truncated empirical update and a coupling argument to re-establish concentration of bτ/τ; while plausible, the main text only sketches the approach and defers bounds to the supplement. The heterogeneous-cost case is also mostly outlined, with a switch to an ε-first scheme for learning.
- Completeness: Theoretical contributions are substantial. Empirical validation is minimal in the provided text (only brief remarks); while theory-only is acceptable at NeurIPS, a small empirical study demonstrating robustness to near-boundary ρ and finite-sample behavior would strengthen the work.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized. The ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms are clearly described; the threshold structure of the LP and its adaptive use are intuitive and easy to implement (sort contexts by u∗j and randomize at the threshold).
- Some notation/typos: Equation (4) seems truncated; presumably pj=0 for j> j̃(ρ)+1. A brief note on how to compute p̂j efficiently from sorted UCBs would help reproducibility. The assumptions (unique best action per context and strict ordering of u∗j) should be clearly labeled as tie-breaking simplifications, with a short remark on how ties are handled.
- The boundary discussion is clear; it would help to quantify how regret constants blow up as ρ approaches a boundary (via δ).

Originality:
- The adaptive LP relaxation, together with the budget-evolution equivalence to sampling without replacement yielding O(1) oracle gap for known statistics, appears novel and insightful.
- Achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits (finite J, K) against the true oracle seems new relative to RCB/BwK lines that achieve O(√T) and/or use finite policy benchmarks.
- The decomposition of regret for UCB-ALP leverages known UCB tools but in a new constrained contextual setting with an adaptive budget.

Significance:
- Constrained contextual bandits are important for applications with budgets (ads, clinical trials, crowdsourcing). The algorithms are computationally lightweight (solve a J-variable LP with a threshold structure; maintain JK UCBs) and thus practical.
- The results advance the state of the art in this specific but relevant setting by improving regret rates and clarifying how to decouple learning from budget allocation via adaptive relaxation.
- The insights are likely to influence subsequent work on more general resource constraints and richer context models.

Pros:
- Clear conceptual contribution: adaptive LP that tightly tracks the oracle while enabling clean analysis.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) regret gap for known means; O(JK log T) for learning; explicit handling of boundary cases.
- Computationally efficient and implementable.
- Good comparison to related literature and positioning of contributions.

Cons:
- Assumptions are somewhat restrictive: finite contexts/actions, iid contexts with known distribution (until Section 5), unit costs in core results, unique optimum per context, strict ordering across contexts. Extensions are sketched but less developed in the main text.
- Boundary cases yield O(√T) regret; although measure-zero in ρ, in practice B/T is discrete and may coincide with thresholds, and near-boundary constants can be large.
- Limited empirical evaluation in the provided text; experiments could illustrate robustness and practical gains over baselines (e.g., fixed-budget UCB, BwK variants).
- For heterogeneous costs, the learning algorithm is ε-first rather than a fully adaptive UCB-ALP analogue.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Provide a simple, efficient implementation note: since the LP solution is a threshold on sorted û∗j, you can pre-sort or maintain a heap; what is the per-round complexity?
- Clarify handling of ties in u∗j and k∗j; does the analysis extend with appropriate tie-breaking?
- Quantify the near-boundary regime: give explicit dependence on δ in the constants, and perhaps propose a small bias (e.g., conservative spending) to avoid boundary-induced variance.
- For unknown context distribution, bring a formal theorem with regret bounds (not only a sketch) into the main paper, even if for the truncated estimator.
- Include a small experimental section comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to BwK/RCB baselines on synthetic data, especially highlighting budget evolution and regret near boundary vs non-boundary ρ.
- Discuss lower bounds in this finite J,K setting to justify “order-optimal” more formally.

Verdict:
A strong theoretical contribution with novel ideas and clear impact within constrained bandits. Despite some restrictive assumptions and limited empirical validation, the results are significant and well supported. I recommend acceptance after addressing clarity and experimental suggestions.
