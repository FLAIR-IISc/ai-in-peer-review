

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, where an agent must balance reward maximization with budget constraints. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm for known statistics, and a UCB-ALP algorithm for unknown statistics, claiming logarithmic regret (O(log T)) in non-boundary cases, which they assert is the first such result in this setting.

The paper's main contribution is the ALP algorithm, which approximates the oracle with linear programming and adapts the budget constraint to the average remaining budget. They then combine this with UCB to handle unknown statistics, claiming O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases. The theoretical analysis is mathematically rigorous, with careful proofs for the regret bounds.

However, as a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes established methods and rigorous validation, I have several concerns about this paper's claims and presentation:

**Major Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated novelty and unverified claims:** The paper claims to be "the first work that shows how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits." However, the cited related work [20, 21, 22] achieves O(√T) regret for similar settings, and the paper doesn't clearly explain why their approach fundamentally differs from these previous methods to justify logarithmic regret. The paper's "non-boundary cases" where O(log T) holds are not well-defined or characterized, and the paper provides no evidence about how common these cases are in practice. The authors state "except for a set of parameters of zero Lebesgue measure," but don't quantify the practical significance of this measure-theoretic condition.

2. **Lack of empirical validation:** This is a critical omission in a bandit paper. The authors claim logarithmic regret but provide no experimental results to validate their theoretical claims. In bandit literature, empirical validation is standard practice to complement theoretical analysis, especially for algorithms with practical implications. Without experiments, it's impossible to assess whether the algorithm performs well in real-world scenarios or if the theoretical bounds are overly pessimistic.

3. **Weak regret bound:** The claimed regret is O(JK log T), which depends on the problem size (J contexts, K actions). For large J and K, this bound may not be significantly better than O(√T) in practice. The paper doesn't provide any insight into how this bound scales with problem size in realistic scenarios.

4. **Strong assumptions:** The main analysis assumes known context distribution, which is unrealistic for most applications. The paper only briefly discusses unknown context distribution in Section 5 without providing a rigorous analysis or empirical validation. Similarly, the unit-cost assumption is restrictive for many real-world applications.

5. **Boundary cases are poorly addressed:** The paper claims O(√T) regret for boundary cases but doesn't quantify how often these occur in practice. The paper states "except for a set of parameters of zero Lebesgue measure," but doesn't provide evidence that these boundary cases are rare in practical settings.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results.

2. The theoretical analysis is mathematically sound for the cases considered, with careful proofs of the regret bounds.

3. The connection between the LP approximation and the oracle algorithm is interesting and provides useful insights for constrained bandit problems.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to constrained contextual bandits, but the claims are overstated and not sufficiently supported by evidence. The key claim of logarithmic regret is limited to non-boundary cases with no practical characterization of these cases, and the paper lacks empirical validation that would demonstrate the algorithm's practical effectiveness.

While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the lack of empirical validation and the overstatement of the novelty and practical significance of the results make this paper less compelling for NIPS. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing empirical results comparing UCB-ALP with existing methods
2. More clearly characterizing the "non-boundary cases" and their practical prevalence
3. Discussing the practical implications of the O(JK log T) regret bound
4. Addressing the unknown context distribution case with more rigor

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution appears to be incremental rather than transformative, and the claims of achieving logarithmic regret are not sufficiently substantiated.

**Summary of key concerns:**

- Overstated novelty: Claiming to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret without sufficient differentiation from prior work
- Unverified claims: Logarithmic regret only holds for poorly characterized "non-boundary cases"
- Lack of empirical validation: Critical omission in bandit literature
- Weak regret bound: O(JK log T) depends on problem size and may not be practically better than O(√T)
- Strong assumptions: Known context distribution, unit costs

I recommend rejection for the above reasons, but would encourage the authors to address these concerns in future work with empirical validation and clearer characterization of the conditions under which logarithmic regret holds.
