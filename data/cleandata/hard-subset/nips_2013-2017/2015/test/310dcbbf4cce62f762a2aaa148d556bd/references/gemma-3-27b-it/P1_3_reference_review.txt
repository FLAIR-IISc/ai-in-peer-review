## Peer Review: Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a realistic scenario often overlooked in traditional bandit literature. The authors make a significant contribution by designing algorithms achieving logarithmic regret, a substantial improvement over the √T regret bounds obtained in related work (specifically referencing Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17]). The core idea is to approximate the optimal policy using linear programming (LP) and then adaptively adjust the budget constraint based on the remaining time and budget (ALP).  This approach is then extended to the unknown reward setting with a UCB-ALP algorithm.  The paper provides rigorous analysis, including handling boundary cases, and discusses generalizations to more complex settings like unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs (details in the supplementary material).

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on contextual bandits, multi-armed bandits, and constrained bandits. It clearly distinguishes its work from prior approaches on resource-constrained bandits [11-16], which are inapplicable to the contextual setting, and from [17], which focuses on a more general but computationally expensive setting and provides a weaker regret bound.  The authors acknowledge recent related work [21, 22] extending [20] to the dynamic context case, highlighting that those works achieve √T regret. The paper’s novelty lies in achieving logarithmic regret in this setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** Achieving logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits is a significant advancement. This problem is practically relevant and previously lacked efficient algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, backing up the claimed regret bounds. The use of LP relaxation and the adaptive budget adjustment are well-motivated and analyzed.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The progression from known statistics to unknown statistics is clear, and the different sections build upon each other effectively. The definitions and notation are relatively clear.
* **Practicality:** The proposed algorithms (ALP and UCB-ALP) seem computationally tractable, especially compared to the Mixture Elimination algorithm in [17]. The assumption of finite contexts and fixed costs, while simplifying, makes the results more immediately applicable to certain real-world scenarios.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper considers boundary cases, generalizes to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs (with details in the supplementary material), and provides detailed proofs (also in the supplementary material).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption of finite discrete contexts and fixed costs, while simplifying analysis, limits the immediate applicability of the results.  The authors acknowledge this, but it's worth emphasizing.
* **Boundary Cases:** While the authors address boundary cases, the √T regret in these scenarios is still a weakness. The practical impact of these boundary cases needs further investigation. More discussion on the prevalence of these cases would be helpful.
* **Complexity of Supplementary Material:** A significant portion of the analysis is relegated to the supplementary material. While this is acceptable, it makes it harder for the reviewer to quickly assess the full rigor of the work without dedicating substantial time to the supplement.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks experimental results. While the focus is on theoretical guarantees, empirical validation would significantly strengthen the claims. Even simple simulations could demonstrate the performance of the algorithm and the prevalence of boundary cases.
* **Comparison to Recent Work:** While the paper acknowledges [21, 22], a more detailed comparison of their approaches and a discussion of why the logarithmic regret result is still a significant improvement would be beneficial.  Specifically, what are the trade-offs between the √T regret algorithms and the proposed log T algorithm?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practically important problem with a strong theoretical solution.
* The logarithmic regret bound is a significant improvement over existing results.
* The technical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.
* The proposed algorithms are relatively simple and potentially practical.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The simplifying assumptions limit the immediate applicability of the results.
* The √T regret in boundary cases is a weakness.
* Lack of empirical evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The achievement of logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits is a significant result. While the assumptions and the √T regret in boundary cases are weaknesses, the overall quality of the work outweighs these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* **Expand Discussion of Boundary Cases:**  Provide more intuition about the conditions that lead to boundary cases and an estimate of how often they might occur in practice.
* **Consider Empirical Validation:** Include a section with simulations to demonstrate the performance of the UCB-ALP algorithm and the impact of boundary cases.
* **Detailed Comparison to Recent Work:**  Provide a more detailed comparison between UCB-ALP and the recent algorithms in [21, 22], focusing on the trade-offs between regret bounds, computational complexity, and assumptions.
* **Improve Clarity of Supplementary Material:** Ensure the supplementary material is well-organized and contains sufficient explanations for the core mathematical arguments.  Consider moving some key insights from the supplement to the main paper.
