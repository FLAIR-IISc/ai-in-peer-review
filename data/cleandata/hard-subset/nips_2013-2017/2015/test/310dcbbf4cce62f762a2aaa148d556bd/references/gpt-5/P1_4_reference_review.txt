Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget constraint and finite horizon, a setting that arises in applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The key difficulty is that the oracle (optimal policy with known statistics) must balance instantaneous reward and future opportunities under both time and budget constraints; dynamic programming is intractable and hard to analyze. The authors introduce a linear programming (LP) relaxation and then an adaptive LP policy (ALP) that, at each round, solves the LP with the average remaining budget bτ/τ. For unit-cost, finite-context systems with known context distribution, ALP does not need exact reward values—only the ordering of context-wise optimal rewards—making it amenable to combination with UCB. They prove that ALP achieves O(1) regret relative to the oracle, except in boundary cases (when ρ equals a cumulative context probability), where regret is O(√T). With unknown rewards, they propose UCB-ALP, which maintains UCBs for context-action pairs and plugs the current UCB estimates into the ALP. UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They further discuss unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates and a truncated analysis ensuring concentration) and heterogeneous costs (generalized ALP and an ε-first variant).

This work relates to Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB). Prior RCB and related constrained contextual works (e.g., [17, 20–22]) obtain O(√T) regret in broader settings, often relative to a finite policy class, and can be computationally heavy. The present paper focuses on finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, and a single budget, and achieves logarithmic regret in non-boundary cases with a computationally simple method, seemingly the first to obtain log regret in constrained contextual bandits under these assumptions.

Quality:
- The analysis appears technically sound. The LP upper bound Û(T,B)=Tv(ρ) provides a clean benchmark. The ALP analysis hinges on a neat observation: under ALP, the probability of spending one unit at time τ equals bτ/τ, which implies a “sampling without replacement” evolution for the remaining budget. This yields hypergeometric concentration and leads to O(1) regret (or O(√T) at boundaries).
- The UCB-ALP regret decomposition into within-context action-ranking errors (O(log T)) and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation errors (O(JK log T)) is plausible and well-argued; maintaining the LP constraint ensures the same budget concentration as ALP.
- Assumptions are clearly stated (finite J,K; iid contexts; bounded rewards; fixed costs). The handling of unknown context distribution via a truncated estimator and bounded differences is reasonable, though details are deferred to the supplement.
- One concern is the reliance on “non-boundary” conditions (ρ ≠ qj). While measure-zero in a continuous sense, in discrete applications ρ can easily equal a qj; the paper retains O(√T) in those cases. It would be valuable to discuss if simple smoothing/dithering can avoid the boundary regime or improve the √T term.
- Another mild concern is the use of a smaller UCB exploration coefficient; the paper claims standard guarantees still hold with adjusted constants, but it would be helpful to spell this out in the main text (Lemma 3 relies on it).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and clear. The unit-cost section is easy to follow and the threshold structure of the LP solution is intuitive.
- Some equations are hard to parse (Eq. (4) formatting/piecewise), and a few typos occur; tightening notation would help. The core ideas (LP bound, ALP, UCB-ALP) and their interactions are clearly conveyed.
- The dependence on appendices is heavy for several key proofs (ALP regret, unknown context distribution). A short, self-contained theorem statement for the truncated EALP and for heterogeneous-cost regret in the main text would strengthen clarity.

Originality:
- The main novelty is the adaptive LP relaxation that achieves O(1) regret to the oracle in non-boundary cases and enables O(log T) regret with learning. To my knowledge, logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits (with random contexts) has not been shown before under these assumptions. The “sampling without replacement” view of the budget process under ALP is a clean and useful technical insight.
- Prior works [17,20–22] achieve O(√T) in more general settings or relative to policy classes; the present instance-dependent, finite-context/action, hard-budget setting and results are distinct.

Significance:
- The results advance the state of the art for constrained contextual bandits, showing that in practically relevant finite settings, one can be both computationally efficient and statistically near-optimal. The ALP/UCB-ALP approach and the budget concentration argument are likely to influence subsequent designs for more general resource-constrained contextual problems.
- Practical impact is promising: ALP is simple (closed form in unit-cost), and UCB-ALP scales linearly in JK in terms of exploration burden. However, the JK factor may be large in high-cardinality problems, and the boundary-case √T term leaves a gap.

Pros:
- Conceptually simple, computationally efficient algorithms (ALP, UCB-ALP).
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) to oracle with known stats; O(log T) with learning (non-boundary).
- Clean LP benchmark and neat hypergeometric concentration of remaining budget.
- Clear positioning vs. RCB/BwK and contextual knapsack literature; addresses a notable open question.

Cons:
- Boundary cases incur O(√T) regret; limited discussion on whether this is improvable or avoidable by algorithmic smoothing.
- Heavy reliance on finite, discrete contexts/actions and fixed costs; generalization to richer contexts remains open.
- The unknown context distribution analysis is only sketched; main theorems for truncated EALP are deferred.
- No empirical results in the main text to illustrate constants and practicality.
- Assumption u∗1>…>u∗J (strict ordering) and unique best action per context are used for exposition; robustness to ties could be clarified.

Questions and suggestions:
- Can you propose a simple tie-breaking or dithering around the LP threshold to avoid boundary regimes and potentially improve the √T term?
- How sensitive are the constants in the O(JK log T) regret to small reward gaps (both across actions within a context and across contexts)? Can you provide gap-dependent bounds?
- For heterogeneous costs, can UCB-ALP (rather than ε-first) be analyzed to preserve O(log T) in non-boundary cases?
- Please clarify in the main text why the smaller UCB exploration term still yields Lemma 3 and the regret bounds.
- Minor: clean up Eq. (4) formatting and define all symbols upon first use.

Recommendation: Weak accept
This is a strong theoretical paper with a simple, elegant idea that closes a gap in constrained contextual bandits by attaining logarithmic regret under finite, fixed-cost assumptions. Despite some limitations (boundary cases, scope, reliance on supplements, lack of experiments), the contributions are novel, well-motivated, and technically solid, and should be of interest to the NeurIPS community.
