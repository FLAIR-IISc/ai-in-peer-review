- Problem addressed: constrained contextual bandits with budget and time limits relevant to real-world applications.  
- Contribution (known stats): ALP algorithm achieves O(1) regret except boundary cases with O(√T) by using adaptive linear programming.  
- Contribution (unknown stats): UCB-ALP combines ALP with UCB to get O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases (O(√T + log T) for boundary cases).  
- Improvement over prior work: Achieves logarithmic regret vs. prior O(√T) results for similar problems.  
- Theoretical rigor: Complete proofs and careful analysis of action/context ranking errors, budget fluctuations, and context randomness.  
- Completeness: Provides algorithms, theoretical guarantees, and discussions of generalizations (heterogeneous costs, unknown context distributions).  
- Clarity: Well-structured, consistent notation, intuitive explanations and helpful proof sketches.  
- Originality: Novel use of adaptive linear programming and its combination with UCB, differing from fixed-budget or finite-policy prior methods.  
- Significance: Addresses an important, practical problem (e.g., clinical trials, crowdsourcing) and opens new directions in constrained bandits.  
- Weakness — experimental validation: Lacks empirical results or simulations to validate theoretical claims and practical performance.  
- Weakness — boundary cases: Boundary values of budget-to-time ratio yield only O(√T) regret and no clear remedy is provided.  
- Weakness — scope of generalizations: Main results focus on unit-cost systems; limited detail on performance with heterogeneous costs in practice.  
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends acceptance based on the strong theoretical contribution.  
- Key arguments for acceptance: Novel problem formulation, theoretical advance (logarithmic regret), rigorous analysis, ALP decouples information acquisition from decision making.  
- Key arguments against acceptance: No experiments, suboptimal boundary-case regret, primary focus on unit-cost systems.  
- Final assessment: Strong, well-written theoretical contribution that justifies acceptance despite missing experiments.
