

# Peer Review: "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses the constrained contextual bandit problem, where the agent faces both budget and time constraints while making decisions based on observable contexts. The authors propose two key algorithms: Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for the known-statistics case and UCB-ALP for the unknown-statistics case. Their main contribution is showing that these algorithms achieve O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases (compared to previous O(√T) results), which is a significant theoretical advance. The paper builds upon prior work on contextual bandits [1-7] and budget-constrained bandits [11-16], while addressing limitations of the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17] and recent extensions [20-22] that achieve O(√T) regret.

The authors' key insight is using linear programming to approximate the oracle algorithm (which would solve the problem with known statistics), with the LP relaxation providing a tight benchmark for regret analysis. The ALP algorithm dynamically adjusts its budget constraint based on the remaining time and budget, while UCB-ALP combines this with upper confidence bounds to learn the optimal action ordering. The paper demonstrates that ALP achieves O(1) regret (near-optimal) in non-boundary cases, and UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret, which is order-optimal for this setting.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical advance by establishing logarithmic regret bounds for constrained contextual bandits, which is a major improvement over the O(√T) results in previous work [17, 20-22]. This is particularly impressive given the complex interactions between budget constraints, context arrivals, and exploration-exploitation tradeoffs.

2. **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis with well-constructed lemmas and theorems. The authors develop novel techniques for handling the concentration properties of the remaining budget under adaptive algorithms, which is non-trivial due to the interplay between the budget constraint and context arrivals.

3. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the constrained contextual bandit problem, distinguishes it from previous work, and justifies their simplified assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs) as both theoretically tractable and practically relevant for applications like clinical trials and wireless networks.

4. **Novel Insights:** The paper provides valuable insights about the relationship between linear programming relaxation and dynamic programming solutions for constrained bandits, which could inform future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper provides no experimental results to complement the theoretical analysis. While theoretical contributions are valuable, empirical validation would strengthen the paper's claims and provide insights into practical performance.

2. **Boundary Case Analysis:** The treatment of boundary cases (where regret becomes O(√T)) is somewhat brief. The authors could provide more intuition about why these cases behave differently and whether they are common in practice.

3. **Computational Complexity:** The paper mentions the computational efficiency of UCB-ALP but doesn't provide concrete complexity analysis of the LP problems, which would be important for practitioners.

4. **Connection to Dynamic Programming:** While the authors acknowledge the oracle would use dynamic programming, the connection between their LP approximation and the true DP solution could be more thoroughly explained.

## Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The claims are well-supported by theorems and proofs, and the authors carefully acknowledge the limitations of their approach (particularly the boundary cases). The supplementary material provides necessary details for reproducibility.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with good organization of the main contributions. The key concepts (ALP, UCB-ALP) are well-explained, though the connection between LP relaxation and the oracle solution could be clarified.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. The logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits is novel, and the combination of ALP with UCB represents a clever application of known techniques to a challenging problem. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work.

**Significance:** The constrained contextual bandit problem is highly relevant to real-world applications (clinical trials, online recommendation). Achieving logarithmic regret represents a major theoretical advance over previous work, and the algorithms are computationally efficient, making them practical for implementation.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the contextual bandit literature by establishing logarithmic regret bounds for constrained contextual bandits. The analysis is rigorous, the insights are valuable, and the problem is well-motivated. While the lack of empirical results is a weakness, the theoretical contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance.

The paper should be accepted as it represents a clear advance in the field with implications for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of bandit algorithms. The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of computational complexity and potentially including empirical results in future work.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- First work to establish logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with novel techniques for handling adaptive budget constraints
- Clear distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases
- Practical algorithms that are computationally efficient
- Strong connection to real-world applications

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Lack of empirical validation to complement theoretical claims
- Brief treatment of boundary cases
- Limited discussion of computational complexity of LP problems

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in contextual bandits. The significance of the results outweighs the minor weaknesses in the paper.
