

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, where the agent faces both budget and time constraints, and aims to maximize reward while respecting these constraints. The authors consider a simplified model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which is justified in applications like clinical trials and wireless networks. They introduce two key algorithms: Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for known statistics and UCB-ALP for unknown statistics. The main contribution is showing that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases (and O(√T) in boundary cases), which is the first result of its kind for constrained contextual bandits.

The paper builds upon previous work on contextual bandits [1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7] and budget-constrained bandits [11-16, 17, 20-22]. It specifically distinguishes itself from Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17], which achieved O(√T) regret but with a restricted benchmark and high computational complexity. It also differentiates from [20], which achieved O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts with a fixed budget constraint, whereas the authors use an adaptive budget constraint to achieve logarithmic regret.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with detailed proofs for all main claims. The authors establish a novel benchmark using linear programming relaxation to analyze regret, which simplifies the otherwise complex dynamic programming approach. The regret bounds for ALP (Theorem 1) and UCB-ALP (Theorem 2) are well-supported with careful analysis of budget fluctuations, context ranking errors, and concentration properties.

2. **Novelty:** The paper successfully addresses a gap in the literature by achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, which previous work had not accomplished. The ALP approximation approach, while using LP to approximate DP problems (known in other contexts [17, 19]), is novel in its application to constrained contextual bandits with adaptive budget constraints. The paper clearly differentiates from related work by showing how their approach overcomes the limitations of previous methods.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem formulation, key challenges, and algorithmic insights are presented logically. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results, with key proofs and technical details relegated to supplementary material. The distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases is well-explained and important for understanding the results.

4. **Significance:** The problem addressed has significant practical importance in applications like clinical trials and online recommendation systems. The proposed algorithms are computationally efficient, and the results provide a theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem. The paper opens up new research directions for constrained contextual bandits and demonstrates how to decouple information acquisition from decision making.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scope Limitations:** The paper focuses on a simplified model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint. While the authors acknowledge this and discuss extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs in Sections 5 and 6, these extensions are not as thoroughly analyzed as the main results. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the practical limitations of their simplifying assumptions.

2. **Boundary Cases:** The paper acknowledges that regret is O(√T) in boundary cases, which is significantly worse than the O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases. While the authors explain why these cases occur, they do not provide a method to handle them more effectively, which is a limitation for practical applications where boundary cases might be common.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The paper lacks experimental results to validate the theoretical claims. While theoretical analysis is strong, empirical results would have provided additional support for the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, especially regarding the performance in boundary cases and the impact of different parameters.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, which was an open problem. The technical analysis is rigorous, the approach is novel in the context of constrained bandits, and the paper is well-written with clear exposition of the key ideas. The main limitation is the restricted scope of the model and the lack of empirical validation, but these do not undermine the theoretical contribution.

The paper meets all four criteria for a high-quality NeurIPS submission:
- **Quality:** The technical analysis is sound and well-supported.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
- **Originality:** The work provides a novel solution to a previously unsolved problem in the literature.
- **Significance:** The results address an important practical problem and advance the state of the art in contextual bandits.

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of bandit algorithms and addresses an important gap in the literature. While the scope is somewhat limited, the theoretical results are significant and could inspire future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper presents a novel theoretical contribution with rigorous analysis that advances the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits, meeting all key criteria for NeurIPS.
