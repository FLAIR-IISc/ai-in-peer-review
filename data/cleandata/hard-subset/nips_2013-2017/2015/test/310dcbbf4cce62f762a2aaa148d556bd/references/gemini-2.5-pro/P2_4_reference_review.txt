**Summary**

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), a setting referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The key challenge identified by the authors is that the optimal policy (oracle) for this problem is a complex dynamic program (DP), which is computationally intractable, difficult to analyze, and hard to adapt to the learning setting (unknown rewards).

The core contribution is a novel and principled approach to circumvent this difficulty by approximating the DP with a much simpler Linear Program (LP). For the known-statistics case, they propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. Instead of solving a complex DP, ALP, at each step, solves a simple LP using the *current* average remaining budget as its constraint. This adaptive approach is shown to be near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret except for a specific set of "boundary" parameter conditions, where it achieves O(sqrt(T)) regret.

For the practical case of unknown rewards, the authors propose UCB-ALP, which integrates the UCB algorithm for reward estimation with the ALP framework for decision-making. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases, a significant improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) regret common in related literature. This is the first result to demonstrate logarithmic regret in this setting. The paper also discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears sound and is the main strength of the work. The key insight to model the evolution of the remaining budget under ALP as a process of "sampling without replacement," which leads to a hypergeometric distribution, is elegant and powerful. This allows the authors to establish strong concentration bounds on the remaining budget/time ratio, which is the cornerstone of the entire regret analysis.
*   **Completeness:** The work is presented as a complete story. It begins by identifying the intractability of the oracle, proposes a well-justified approximation (ALP) for the known-statistics case, and then successfully extends this to a learning algorithm (UCB-ALP) for the unknown-statistics case, providing strong regret guarantees for both.
*   **Evaluation:** The authors are careful to delineate the conditions under which their results hold, explicitly defining the "non-boundary" and "boundary" cases. This is an honest assessment of the algorithm's performance characteristics. They correctly position their work relative to prior art (e.g., [17, 20]), highlighting how their adaptive budget constraint and focus on a slightly simplified model enable the logarithmic regret bound.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.
*   The logical flow from the oracle problem, to the LP upper bound, to the ALP algorithm, and finally to the UCB-ALP algorithm is easy to follow and builds intuition effectively.
*   The inclusion of "Sketch of Proof" sections is highly commendable. These sections successfully convey the main ideas of the non-trivial analysis (e.g., partitioning the regret, analyzing the budget concentration, and categorizing ranking errors) without overwhelming the reader with technical details, which are deferred to the appendix.
*   A minor weakness is that Sections 5 (Unknown Context Distribution) and 6 (Heterogeneous Costs) are quite condensed. While this is understandable due to space constraints, it makes it difficult for the reader to fully appreciate the challenges and solutions in these more general settings without consulting the supplementary material. For instance, a more explicit explanation in the main text of why the naive "reward-to-cost ratio" heuristic is insufficient in the heterogeneous cost setting would have been beneficial.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions.
*   While constrained bandits have been studied, this work is the first to achieve a logarithmic regret bound for the *contextual* setting with both time and budget stopping conditions.
*   The central idea of using an *adaptive* LP, where the budget constraint is updated at each round based on the remaining resources (`b_τ/τ`), is a novel and key contribution. Previous work often used a fixed constraint derived from the initial B/T ratio. This adaptivity is precisely what enables the near-optimal performance and the move from O(sqrt(T)) to O(log T) regret.
*   The analytical technique of leveraging the hypergeometric distribution to analyze the budget dynamics of an adaptive algorithm is, to my knowledge, new in this context and represents a significant technical innovation.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to have an impact.
*   **Advancing the State of the Art:** The O(log T) regret bound is a demonstrable improvement over existing O(sqrt(T)) results for related, more general problems. It provides a new benchmark for what is achievable in constrained bandit settings.
*   **Practical Implications:** The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient, relying on sorting and solving a very simple LP at each step. This makes it a practical approach for applications like online advertising, clinical trials, or crowdsourcing, where budget constraints are common.
*   **Broader Impact:** The design principle of decoupling a complex planning problem into a learning component (UCB) and an adaptive, approximate planning component (ALP) is a powerful one. This idea could inspire similar approaches for other resource-constrained sequential decision-making problems beyond the specific model studied here.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents the first O(log T) regret algorithm for constrained contextual bandits, a significant theoretical advancement over prior work.
2.  **Technical Depth:** The analysis is non-trivial and introduces a novel technique (modeling budget dynamics with a hypergeometric distribution) to achieve its tight bounds.
3.  **Clarity and Insight:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The ALP framework provides a clear and intuitive way to think about the exploration-exploitation trade-off under budget constraints.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The problem is well-motivated by real-world applications, and the proposed algorithm is computationally efficient.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The main results are derived under simplifying assumptions (finite discrete contexts, unit costs, and initially, a known context distribution). While the authors discuss extensions, the core analysis relies on this cleaner setting. However, this is a standard and well-justified approach for making initial theoretical breakthroughs on a difficult problem.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on contextual bandits. The proposed ALP/UCB-ALP framework is both elegant and effective, and the supporting theoretical analysis is novel and strong. The achievement of logarithmic regret is a major step forward for this problem class. The clarity of the presentation further enhances the paper's quality. The strengths far outweigh the minor limitations regarding the simplifying assumptions. I strongly recommend acceptance.
