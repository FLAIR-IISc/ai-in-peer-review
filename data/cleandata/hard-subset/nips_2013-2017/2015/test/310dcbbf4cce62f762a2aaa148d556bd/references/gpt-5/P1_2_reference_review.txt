Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits under hard resource constraints: a single budget B over a horizon T, with i.i.d. discrete contexts, bounded rewards, and fixed known costs. The main idea is to approximate the dynamic programming (oracle) solution via an adaptive linear program (ALP) that replaces the hard budget with an average remaining budget constraint bτ/τ at time τ. For known rewards, ALP is shown to achieve O(1) regret relative to the true oracle in non-boundary cases (when ρ = B/T is not equal to a cumulative context probability qj) and O(√T) at boundary cases. For unknown rewards, UCB-ALP combines ALP with UCB estimates to obtain O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) at boundary cases. The paper also sketches extensions to unknown context distributions via empirical ALP (with a truncation device to recover concentration) and to heterogeneous costs (with an LP over all context-action pairs), retaining O(1) or O(√T) regret in known-parameter settings, and proposing an exploration-first variant under unknown rewards.

Relative to Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB), which obtain O(√T) regret in much more general settings (e.g., possibly infinite contexts/policies, random costs), this work shows that with finite discrete contexts and fixed costs, an adaptive average-budget relaxation suffices to achieve logarithmic regret (up to boundary cases) and near-optimality when rewards are known. It also relates to Agrawal–Devanur’s work on concave rewards and convex knapsacks, highlighting that static budget constraints per round yield √T regret whereas adaptively tracking bτ/τ unlocks smaller regret.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clean adaptive LP relaxation that closely tracks the oracle under hard budget constraints, together with a sharp analysis based on the hypergeometric “sampling without replacement” view of remaining budget. This is insightful and likely to influence future designs.
- Theoretical guarantees: O(1) regret with known rewards (non-boundary) and O(JK log T) regret with UCB (non-boundary), improving over √T rates common in constrained bandits. The decomposition of regret into action-ranking and context-ranking components is neat and reusable.
- Practicality: ALP requires only the ordering of per-context best rewards and tolerates moderate estimation errors. The unit-cost case admits an especially simple threshold structure; the hetero-cost extension is principled.
- Scope and positioning: Clear articulation of differences to RCB/BwK and linear contextual/concave knapsack work; the adaptive-budget insight is novel in this constrained contextual setting.

Weaknesses and questions
- Boundary cases: While measure-zero in a continuous parameter sense, ρ = qj can easily occur in practice (e.g., integral budgets, coarse-grained πj). The √T term may dominate for sizable T. Can the algorithm be stabilized near boundaries (e.g., with bias or dithering) to restore log regret in a margin region |ρ − qj| ≤ ε?
- Experimental evidence: The paper references simulations (and supplement), but no results are provided here. Empirics demonstrating: (i) regret scaling in boundary vs non-boundary regimes, (ii) robustness to misspecified πj, and (iii) heterogeneous-cost scenarios would strengthen significance.
- Assumptions: i.i.d. contexts, fixed known costs, finite discrete context-action sets, and known πj in the main results are restrictive for some applications. The unknown-π analysis requires truncation; a fully online version without truncation (with concentration under adaptive data reuse) would be impactful.
- Complexity details: In unit cost, the per-round ALP reduces to a threshold with at most one fractional context; an explicit complexity/runtime bound would help. For heterogeneous costs, solving a per-round LP over JK variables may be nontrivial for large-scale systems; any incremental or greedy structure?
- Lower bounds: The paper argues that O(JK log T) is order-optimal due to the need to identify action rankings, but a formal instance-dependent lower bound under budget constraints would solidify optimality claims.
- Minor clarity/typos: There are a few typographical issues (e.g., “Θ(o) √T” in Theorem 1 likely means a constant times √T; notation û∗j (t)← û∗j,k∗j (t)(t) in Algorithm 1 appears garbled). Make the dependence of constants explicit in theorems.

Quality
- The analysis appears technically sound and uses appropriate tools (hypergeometric concentration, UCB tail bounds, regret decomposition, coupling for unknown π). Proof sketches are convincing; full proofs are deferred to the supplement. Clear assumptions and benchmarks (true oracle under hard budget) are used. A formal lower bound and experiments would further enhance rigor.

Clarity
- Overall well-written and well-structured, with intuitive explanations for ALP and UCB-ALP. Some notation and boundary-case constants could be clarified; including a brief complexity discussion per round and a small running example would aid readability.

Originality
- The adaptive LP relaxation to the average remaining budget within contextual bandits under hard constraints, and the demonstration of O(1) regret (known rewards) and O(log T) regret (unknown rewards) in non-boundary cases, is novel in this scope. The mapping to “sampling without replacement” for budget evolution is elegant.

Significance
- The results advance the state of the art for constrained contextual bandits in the finite discrete setting, offering both conceptual and algorithmic tools. The adaptive-budget principle may translate to broader resource-constrained learning problems. Practical impact depends on scalability and robustness to modeling deviations, which could be reinforced by experiments.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical results covering known/unknown πj, boundary proximity, and heterogeneous costs.
- Discuss computational complexity and potential optimizations for the per-round LP in heterogeneous costs.
- Explore boundary smoothing (e.g., slight randomization of ρ or budget smoothing penalties) to mitigate √T behavior.
- Clarify constants and fix typos; define all symbols locally in theorems.
- Consider whether Thompson sampling or other rank-identification strategies could replace UCB while preserving log regret.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contribution with clear novelty: O(1)/O(log T) regret via adaptive LP.
- Insightful analysis techniques (budget concentration as sampling without replacement).
- Practical algorithmic design with interpretable structure.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental validation in the current draft.
- Dependence on restrictive assumptions (i.i.d. contexts, finite sets, known πj for main results).
- Boundary-case degradation to √T without a mitigation strategy.

Overall: A solid theoretical paper with a novel and useful idea; with empirical support and minor clarifications, it would be a strong NeurIPS contribution.
