### Summary

This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B). The authors introduce a framework based on an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the optimal dynamic programming policy. For the case of known reward statistics, this ALP algorithm is shown to achieve near-optimal performance, with O(1) regret in "non-boundary" cases and O(sqrt(T)) regret in "boundary" cases. For the more practical setting of unknown rewards, the paper proposes the UCB-ALP algorithm, which combines the ALP decision-making framework with Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) estimates of the rewards. The main theoretical contribution is showing that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases, which is a significant improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) regret achieved by prior work in related settings. The paper also briefly sketches extensions to systems with unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Problem and Novel Results:** The problem of contextual bandits with budget constraints is of high practical importance. Achieving logarithmic regret is a substantial theoretical improvement over existing O(sqrt(T)) results. To my knowledge, this is the first work to achieve this for this problem setting.
2.  **Elegant Core Idea:** The central idea of using an adaptive linear programming relaxation (ALP) to decouple the complex budget management from the reward learning is insightful. This approximation makes the problem tractable and allows for a clean integration with standard bandit learning techniques like UCB.
3.  **Strong Technical Analysis (for the core problem):** The analysis of the ALP algorithm, particularly the mapping of the remaining budget's evolution to a hypergeometric distribution (Lemma 2), is clever and provides a solid foundation for the subsequent regret bounds. The analysis of UCB-ALP, which carefully partitions the sources of regret and analyzes the impact of ranking errors, is also technically sound.

### Weaknesses

The paper, while presenting a strong core contribution, suffers from numerous minor flaws, a lack of precision in several places, and underdeveloped extensions.

1.  **Technical and Typographical Errors:**
    *   **Theorem 1:** The regret bound for the non-boundary case is given as `RALP(T,B) ≤ (u*1−u*J) / (-e^(-2δ^2))`. The denominator is negative, which cannot be correct. This appears to be a significant typo in a central theorem.
    *   **Theorem 1:** The notation `Θ(o)` is used without definition. I assume the authors meant `O(sqrt(T))`, but this is imprecise.
    *   **Algorithm 1:** Line 6, `û∗j (t)← û∗j,k∗j (t)(t);`, is confusing and seemingly redundant, given that line 5 already defines `k*j(t)` and the UCB values `ûj,k(t)`. It appears to be a copy-paste error.
    *   **Introduction, Footnote 1:** The footnote attempts to clarify the contribution over [20], but the main text's discussion of [21, 22] extending [20] to the "dynamic context case" makes the distinction confusing. A clearer, integrated discussion of how this work's i.i.d. context-per-round setting differs from the "static" and "dynamic" contexts in the cited works is needed.

2.  **Lack of Rigor in Extensions:**
    *   **Section 5 (Unknown Context Distribution):** The paper proposes UCB-EALP, which uses empirical estimates of the context distribution. However, the authors state that their "rigorous analysis focuses on a truncated version of EALP". This is a critical weakness. The algorithm presented and motivated by practicality is not the one that is formally analyzed. This bait-and-switch undermines the claims for this extension.
    *   **Section 6 (Heterogeneous Costs):** This section switches from the UCB-based approach to an "ε-First ALP algorithm". The reason for this switch is not provided. Does UCB-ALP not extend to the heterogeneous cost case? This inconsistency weakens the paper's narrative. Furthermore, this section, like Section 5, defers all meaningful details to the supplementary material, making the main paper's claims feel unsubstantiated.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:**
    *   **Simplifying Assumptions:** The assumption `u*1 > u*2 > ... > u*J` is made "for simplicity". While convenient for the LP solution structure in Eq. (4), the paper should briefly state that the algorithm works generally by sorting contexts based on their rewards at each step.
    *   **UCB Variant:** The paper uses a UCB exploration term `sqrt(log t / 2Cj,k(t-1))` and cites [24], which focuses on KL-UCB. The text mentions it has a "smaller coefficient" than traditional UCB [4], but a more explicit justification for this specific form and its theoretical properties in their analysis would be beneficial.
    *   **References:** The formatting of the reference list is sloppy, with many entries containing stray commas (e.g., "[1] ...NIPS),, , ,"). This lack of attention to detail is concerning.

### Assessment

*   **Quality:** The core theoretical contribution on UCB-ALP for unit-cost systems is of high quality. However, the paper's overall quality is diminished by the technical errors in the statement of theorems, the analytical gap in the extensions (especially Section 5), and a general lack of polish.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is mostly well-written, but the issues noted above (confusing discussion of related work, undefined notation, typos in algorithms) detract from its clarity. The organization of Sections 5 and 6, which present ideas without detail, makes the paper feel incomplete.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The ALP framework and the subsequent derivation of logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits is a novel and important contribution that advances the state of the art.
*   **Significance:** The results are significant. Budget constraints are a key feature of many real-world bandit applications, and achieving the optimal regret rate of O(log T) is a landmark result. The ideas presented could influence future work on constrained reinforcement learning problems.

### Recommendation

While the paper presents a novel and significant core idea, the execution is flawed. The presence of a clear error in a main theorem, undefined notation, typos in the algorithm pseudocode, and sloppy referencing indicates a lack of careful preparation. More importantly, the gap between the proposed algorithm and the analyzed one in Section 5 is a substantial scientific weakness. The combination of these numerous minor and major issues prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The paper has the potential to be a very strong contribution to the field, but it requires a thorough revision to address these shortcomings.

**Decision:** Reject
