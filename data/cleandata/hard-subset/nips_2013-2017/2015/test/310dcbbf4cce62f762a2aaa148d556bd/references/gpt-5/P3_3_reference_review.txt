Summary and contributions
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget and a finite horizon. Each action under a context consumes a fixed, known cost (unit-cost in the core analysis), and the process stops when budget or time runs out. The key difficulty is that the oracle (known statistics) is a dynamic program balancing current rewards with future budget needs. The authors propose to replace the DP by a linear program relaxation that enforces an average budget constraint. They first analyze a static LP that yields an upper bound Û(T,B)=Tv(ρ) on the oracle’s expected reward, and then introduce an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy: at each time step, solve the LP with the current average remaining budget bτ/τ and act by randomized thresholding across contexts.

Main theoretical results:
- Known statistics (unit-cost, finite contexts, i.i.d. arrivals): ALP achieves O(1) regret relative to the oracle except at “boundary” ρ values (when ρ equals a cumulative context probability qj), where the regret is O(√T). The analysis hinges on the fact that under ALP the budget consumption probability at time τ equals bτ/τ; the resulting remaining budget process concentrates (hypergeometric-type behavior), which controls deviations from the static LP threshold.
- Unknown rewards, known context distribution: Combine UCB estimates with ALP (UCB-ALP). In non-boundary cases, regret is O(JK log T); in boundary cases, O(√T + JK log T). The proof decomposes regret into within-context action ranking error and cross-context thresholding error, and uses that only O(log T) pulls per context-action are needed to recover ranking with high probability.
- Unknown context distribution: Use empirical frequencies (EALP) and a truncated update scheme; a coupling/bounded differences argument yields the same concentration of bτ/τ and O(1) regret in non-boundary cases (details in the supplement).
- Heterogeneous costs: Extend ALP to an LP over all context-action pairs with a “one action per context per round” constraint. For known statistics, O(1) regret off-boundary and O(√T) at boundaries; for unknown rewards, an ε-first variant is outlined.

Relation to prior work
- Resourceful/knapsack bandits (BwK; Badanidiyuru et al., Slivkins, Combes–Jiang–Srikant) and the RCB framework (Badanidiyuru–Langford–Slivkins) obtain O(√T) regret in general settings, often w.r.t. a finite policy set; algorithms can be computationally onerous. The present work restricts to finite contexts/actions and fixed costs but improves the rate to O(log T), claiming the first logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits with i.i.d. contexts.
- Agrawal and Devanur (concave rewards with convex knapsacks; later linear contextual) yield O(√T) regret; the authors argue those analyses use a fixed per-round budget and do not adapt to bτ/τ, which is key to getting log T here.
- The approach of LP relaxation for DP is classical; the novelty here is to exploit the adaptive average-budget LP to decouple learning and budgeting and get tight concentration and regret control.

Quality and technical correctness
- The high-level proofs are plausible and technically interesting. The core idea—that ALP renders the next spending probability equal to bτ/τ, inducing a “sampling without replacement” structure and strong concentration—is elegant and central to the O(1) gap in known-statistics settings.
- The regret decomposition for UCB-ALP is standard but carefully adapted to the thresholding structure; the observation that only certain context-ordering mistakes matter is insightful.
- Some aspects would benefit from clarification:
  - Uniqueness/strict ordering assumptions (unique best action per context; u∗1>…>u∗J). These simplify analysis but are nontrivial modeling restrictions. The claim that boundary/tie cases are “zero Lebesgue measure” is less meaningful for discrete instances; please delineate how ties are handled algorithmically and how regret degrades (e.g., tie-breaking, margin conditions).
  - Theorem 1 as written has typographical issues (e.g., expression “≤ u∗1−u∗J − e−2δ2”). Please restate cleanly with explicit constants and dependence on δ.
  - The claim that bτ has exactly a hypergeometric distribution under ALP deserves a brief, self-contained argument. It is clear that the next-spend probability equals b/τ in expectation over contexts/actions; the coupling to sampling without replacement (and the independence/exchangeability assumptions needed) should be made precise. Otherwise, concentration can be proved via martingale inequalities (Azuma/Freedman/Serfling).

Originality
- Within the finite-context/action, fixed-cost stochastic setting, the adaptive LP idea combined with UCB to obtain O(log T) regret appears novel and nontrivial. The insight that adapting to bτ/τ is the right lever to beat O(√T) is of independent interest.

Significance
- Constrained contextual bandits arise in recommendation with budgets, clinical trials, and procurement. The results show that, under natural i.i.d. contexts and finite discrete structure, one can get logarithmic regret with a simple, computationally light algorithm. This advances the state of the art in a practically relevant special case and provides analytic tools (budget concentration under adaptive policies) that may transfer to richer models.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The intuition for ALP and its tolerance to estimation errors is clear and compelling. However, important technical details are deferred to the supplement; the main text could better highlight the exact conditions under which concentration holds and the role of context distribution knowledge. Algorithm 1 has minor typos (e.g., û∗j,k∗j (t)(t)). Please also discuss per-round computational complexity (sorting contexts by UCBs and solving the simple LP).

Pros
- First logarithmic regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits with i.i.d. contexts and finite discrete structure.
- Simple, efficient algorithmic design (ALP/UCB-ALP) with clear intuition.
- Technically interesting analysis: adaptive LP relaxation, budget process concentration, careful regret decomposition.
- Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

Cons
- Strong simplifying assumptions: i.i.d. finite contexts, known costs, unique best actions, strict ordering; boundary/tie cases incur √T regret without matching lower bounds.
- Dependence on known context distribution in core results; the EALP analysis is truncated and deferred to the supplement.
- Limited discussion/experiments (in the main text) to validate robustness near boundaries and with heterogenous costs.
- Some notation/typos and imprecise statements (Theorem 1 bound; hypergeometric claim) that should be corrected.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you provide instance-dependent lower bounds for the boundary regime to justify the √T term, or a smoothing variant (e.g., randomized budget slack) that avoids boundary instability?
- How sensitive is UCB-ALP to misspecification of πj and to ties/small gaps between u∗j’s? Empirical plots near boundary values would help.
- Could KL-UCB or other tighter confidence bounds reduce constants in the O(JK log T) term?
- Please clarify the computational complexity per round and any practical heuristics for large J,K.
- For heterogeneous costs, can you provide regret guarantees for the unknown-reward ε-first ALP in the main text?

Recommendation
This is a solid and timely contribution: conceptually simple, computationally light, with strong regret guarantees and clear separation from prior O(√T) results. Despite some restrictive assumptions and clarity issues, I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying statements, tightening the presentation of Theorem 1 and the budget process argument, and expanding discussion of boundary/tie cases.
