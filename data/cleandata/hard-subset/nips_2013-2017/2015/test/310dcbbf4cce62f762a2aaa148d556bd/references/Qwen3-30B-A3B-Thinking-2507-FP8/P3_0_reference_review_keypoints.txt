- Main contribution: first algorithms for constrained contextual bandits achieving logarithmic regret O(log T) in non-boundary cases.  
- Identified gap: prior constrained bandit work typically yields O(√T) regret and doesn't handle observable contexts effectively.  
- Key idea: use linear programming to approximate the optimal policy (oracle) balancing immediate and long-term rewards under budget constraints.  
- Algorithms: Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for known statistics and UCB-ALP for unknown statistics.  
- Regret guarantees: ALP achieves O(1) and UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases; boundary cases incur O(√T) regret.  
- Technical quality: rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proofs and careful handling of boundary cases.  
- Important lemma: Lemma 2 provides elegant concentration results for the remaining budget via "sampling without replacement" analogy.  
- Analysis approach: uses static LP as a benchmark and tracks average remaining budget to show near-optimality of ALP.  
- Scope and completeness: covers known-statistics, unknown context distributions, and heterogeneous costs with supplementary details for generalizations.  
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written, well-organized, consistent notation, and helpful proof sketches.  
- Originality: novel combination of contextual bandits with budget constraints and LP-based oracle approximation.  
- Relation to prior work: clearly distinguishes from past O(√T) results (e.g., [20]) and addresses computational gaps left by [17].  
- Significance: high practical relevance for applications like clinical trials and wireless rate selection; theoretically impactful.  
- Computational relevance: LP-based approach is theoretically sound and computationally efficient.  
- Strength: provides the first logarithmic-regret solution in this setting with strong theoretical backing.  
- Limitation: simplifying assumptions (discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget) restrict applicability to some real-world scenarios.  
- Comparison gap: paper mentions recent dynamic-context works [21,22] but lacks a detailed differentiation from them.  
- Empirical gap: lacks numerical experiments or empirical validation of algorithm performance.  
- Boundary-case drawback: regret degrades to O(√T) in identified boundary scenarios.  
- Acceptance case: novel, rigorous, well-written theoretical advance worthy of acceptance despite empirical and scope limitations.
