This paper tackles the important and largely unexplored problem of contextual bandits with budget constraints, a scenario prevalent in many real-world applications. The authors make a strong case for the practical relevance of this problem, distinguishing it from prior work on budget-constrained bandits that doesn't account for contexts. The introduction is well-written and clearly motivates the research. The core idea of approximating the oracle with linear programming and developing the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm is novel and promising. The subsequent UCB-ALP algorithm, extending this to the unknown reward setting, is a logical and sensible step.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** This is the main strength. The combination of contextual bandits *and* budget constraints, with the focus on logarithmic regret, appears genuinely new within the NIPS community, particularly with the LP approximation approach. While related work exists in both areas individually, the synthesis and resulting algorithms are a significant contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The LP approximation and its analysis seem solid, and the theoretical regret bounds are clearly stated. The connection to resource allocation problems via LP is well-established, providing a strong foundation.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem is well-motivated with examples from clinical trials and crowdsourcing, demonstrating real-world applicability.
* **Clear Writing (Generally):** The paper is mostly clearly written, although some sections (particularly the proof sketches) require more detail to fully appreciate.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks extensive experimental validation. While theoretical results are important, demonstrating the algorithm’s performance on synthetic or real-world datasets would substantially strengthen the paper. The description of generalization to unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs is relegated to the supplementary material, which feels insufficient given their importance.
* **Boundary Cases:** The regret bounds include a √T term for “boundary cases”, diminishing the attractiveness of the logarithmic regret claim. The conditions defining these boundary cases and their likelihood in practical scenarios require more discussion. The paper acknowledges this, but more analysis of when and why these cases occur is needed.
* **Assumptions:** The paper relies on simplifying assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, known context distribution in early sections). The extent to which these assumptions limit the applicability of the results, and the effort needed to relax them in practical settings, is not fully explored.
* **Proof Sketches:** The analysis, while presented with lemmas and theorems, often relies on “proof sketches”. While some level of sketchiness is acceptable, several arguments felt underdeveloped, hindering full understanding.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the problem and the proposed approach is compelling. Logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits is a significant achievement.
* The technical foundation is strong, with a well-motivated LP approximation and a clear connection to existing literature.
* The paper opens up a new direction for research in constrained reinforcement learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of thorough empirical validation weakens the claims of practical effectiveness.
* The existence of √T regret in boundary cases detracts from the advertised logarithmic regret guarantee.
* The reliance on simplifying assumptions limits the immediate applicability of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a genuinely novel contribution. The theoretical results are promising, and the approach is well-motivated. While the lack of extensive empirical evaluation and the presence of the √T regret in boundary cases are weaknesses, I believe the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. I lean towards acceptance, but strongly encourage the authors to address the empirical validation and boundary case analysis in a revised version. The paper introduces a significant new concept and direction, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to NIPS, even in its current state.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions – primarily focused on empirical validation and a clearer discussion of boundary cases).
