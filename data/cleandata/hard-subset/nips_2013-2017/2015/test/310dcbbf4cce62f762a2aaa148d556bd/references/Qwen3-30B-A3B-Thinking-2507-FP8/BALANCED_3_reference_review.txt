

# Review: Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, where the agent faces both time and budget constraints, a problem that has received limited attention in the literature despite its importance in real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors propose a novel approach using linear programming approximations to achieve logarithmic regret, which is a significant improvement over previous work that typically achieves O(√T) regret.

The paper makes a clear contribution by bridging the gap between contextual bandits and budget constraints. It builds on previous work on contextual bandits [1-7] and budget-constrained bandits [11-16], while distinguishing itself from Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] by achieving computationally efficient algorithms with logarithmic regret. The key insight is using adaptive linear programming (ALP) to approximate the oracle solution, which allows them to decouple the complex interactions between information acquisition and decision making. The paper then extends this to the unknown statistics case with UCB-ALP, achieving O(log T) regret except for certain boundary cases.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis showing that ALP achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases) when statistics are known, and UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases. This represents a significant improvement over previous results.

2. **Novel Methodology:** The use of LP relaxation to approximate the oracle solution is both elegant and effective. The authors provide a careful analysis of how to adapt the LP solution to the remaining budget and time, which is the key innovation.

3. **Clear Technical Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis. The sketches of proofs are well-explained, and the key insights are well-articulated.

4. **Significance:** The constrained contextual bandit problem is highly relevant to real-world applications, and achieving logarithmic regret represents a meaningful advance over the O(√T) results in previous work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Assumptions:** The paper focuses primarily on unit-cost systems (Section 3-5), with heterogeneous costs only addressed in Section 6. This limits the immediate applicability of the results to many real-world scenarios where costs vary significantly.

2. **Boundary Cases:** While the paper acknowledges that boundary cases achieve O(√T) regret, it's unclear how common these cases are in practical applications. The authors should provide more discussion on when these boundary cases might occur in real problems and how they might be handled.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The paper lacks comprehensive experimental results to validate the theoretical claims. While the authors mention "numerical simulations" in Section 5, they don't provide specific results to demonstrate the performance of their algorithms compared to baselines.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper references [21, 22] but doesn't fully clarify the distinction between their approach and these recent works. The claim that their approach achieves logarithmic regret while [21, 22] achieve O(√T) needs more detailed justification, as the difference might be due to problem settings rather than algorithmic improvements.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in the constrained contextual bandit literature. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with a novel approach. The main limitations are the restricted scope of the assumptions (unit costs, known context distribution) and the lack of experimental validation.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

1. Add a brief discussion on how common boundary cases are in practical applications, and whether there are simple heuristics to handle them.
2. Include at least one table or figure showing experimental results comparing UCB-ALP with baselines (e.g., Mixture Elimination from [17] or UCB-based approaches from [20]).
3. Clarify the distinction between their work and [21, 22] in the related work section, emphasizing why their approach achieves logarithmic regret while others achieve O(√T) in the same problem setting.
4. Briefly discuss the practical implications of their unit-cost assumption and how it might be relaxed in future work.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and with these minor revisions, it would be a strong addition to NIPS. The theoretical results are novel, well-justified, and represent a meaningful step toward solving the open problem of general constrained contextual bandits.
