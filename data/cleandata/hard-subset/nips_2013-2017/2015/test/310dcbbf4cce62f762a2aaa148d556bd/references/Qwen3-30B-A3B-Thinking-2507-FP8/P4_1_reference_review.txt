

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, where the agent must maximize reward subject to both time horizon T and budget constraint B. The authors address a gap in the literature: while budget constraints have been studied in non-contextual bandits (achieving O(√T) regret), contextual bandits with budget constraints have previously only achieved O(√T) regret (e.g., [17]). The authors introduce a simplified model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and single budget constraint, which is justified in applications like clinical trials and wireless networks.

The key contribution is the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which approximates the oracle (optimal algorithm with known statistics) using linear programming. This provides a tractable benchmark for regret analysis. For known statistics, ALP achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases), while for unknown statistics, the UCB-ALP algorithm combines ALP with UCB to achieve O(log T) regret (except for boundary cases). This represents the first work to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, distinguishing it from recent work [20-22] that achieves O(√T) regret in dynamic context settings.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with well-structured proofs. The authors carefully define the problem, introduce the ALP approximation, and rigorously analyze both the known-statistics and unknown-statistics cases. They address the key challenges of dynamic programming complexity, benchmark definition, and estimation error impact. The regret analysis for ALP (Theorem 1) and UCB-ALP (Theorem 2) is thorough, with detailed proof sketches in the supplementary material. The paper properly acknowledges limitations (boundary cases) and carefully justifies their simplifying assumptions.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide precise mathematical definitions, intuitive explanations of key concepts (e.g., "sampling without replacement" analogy), and logical progression from problem statement to solution. The main contributions are clearly articulated in the introduction, and the technical sections build upon this foundation. The notation is consistent and well-explained. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution by developing the ALP approximation to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. This approach differs from prior work (including [17]) that used mixture elimination with O(√T) regret. The insight that ALP only requires the ordering of expected rewards (not their exact values) enables combining with UCB to achieve logarithmic regret. The boundary case analysis (where regret becomes O(√T)) is also novel and well-justified.

**Significance:** The problem addressed has high practical significance for applications like clinical trials, online recommendation, and crowdsourcing. Achieving logarithmic regret represents a substantial improvement over previous O(√T) results. The proposed algorithms are computationally efficient, and the insights about decoupling information acquisition and decision making could guide future work on more general constrained bandit problems. The paper clearly establishes how this work advances the state of the art.

## Weaknesses

**Scope Limitations:** While the authors justify their simplified model (finite contexts, fixed costs, single budget), the paper does not sufficiently address how their techniques might extend to more general settings (infinite contexts, random costs, multiple constraints). The discussion of generalization in Sections 5-6 is brief and lacks depth compared to the core contribution.

**Boundary Cases:** The paper identifies boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T), but does not quantify how common these cases are in practice or provide guidance on how to handle them. The theoretical treatment is sound, but the practical implications of boundary cases could be better addressed.

**Computational Efficiency Claim:** The authors claim UCB-ALP is "computationally efficient," but provide no complexity analysis or empirical results to support this claim. While the algorithm is described as simple, a brief discussion of computational complexity (e.g., time complexity per round) would strengthen this assertion.

**Comparison with Lower Bounds:** While the authors note their results don't contradict [17]'s lower bound, a more detailed explanation of why their setting (discrete contexts, instance-dependent regret) differs from the lower bound would be helpful for readers.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- First work to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with novel techniques for regret decomposition
- Well-structured, clear exposition with sufficient mathematical detail
- Practical significance for important real-world applications
- Insightful approach of approximating the oracle with LP to decouple exploration and exploitation

**Con:**
- Focus on a simplified model with limited discussion of generalization to more complex settings
- Boundary cases are not fully characterized in practical terms
- Computational efficiency claim lacks supporting complexity analysis or empirical validation

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the contextual bandit literature by introducing algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits. The technical soundness is excellent, with rigorous analysis that carefully addresses the challenges of the problem. The clarity of exposition is outstanding, making the complex analysis accessible to readers. The originality is significant, as the paper fills an important gap in the literature by achieving logarithmic regret where previous work only achieved O(√T) regret.

While the focus on a simplified model is a limitation, the authors properly justify this choice and demonstrate its practical relevance. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits and provides valuable insights for future work on more general settings. The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it represents a high-quality contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
