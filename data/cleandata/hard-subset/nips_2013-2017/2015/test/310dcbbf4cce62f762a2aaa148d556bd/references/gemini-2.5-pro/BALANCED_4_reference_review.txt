### Summary

This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), where actions incur costs. The process terminates when either T or B is exhausted. The authors introduce a simplified, yet practical, version of the "Resourceful Contextual Bandits" problem by assuming a finite set of discrete contexts, fixed known costs, and a known context arrival distribution.

The main challenge is that the optimal policy, which is the solution to a dynamic program (DP), is computationally intractable and difficult to analyze. The authors' core contribution is to circumvent this by approximating the DP with a tractable Linear Program (LP).
First, for the case where reward statistics are known, they propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. At each step, ALP solves an LP based on the *remaining* time and budget to decide which actions to consider. The authors prove that this ALP algorithm achieves a remarkable O(1) regret (i.e., a constant gap from the oracle's upper bound) in most cases, and O(√T) regret in specific "boundary" cases.

Second, for the more practical setting where expected rewards are unknown, they combine the ALP framework with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle to create the UCB-ALP algorithm. This algorithm uses UCB estimates of the rewards within the adaptive LP framework. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases. This is the first result to demonstrate logarithmic regret for this problem setting, a significant improvement over the O(√T) regret common in related literature. The paper also discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel theoretical contribution. Achieving O(log T) regret for a constrained contextual bandit problem is a significant advancement. Previous work on similarly constrained problems, such as Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] or bandits with knapsacks [12, 20], typically achieves O(√T) regret. By identifying a structured (but still relevant) version of the problem, the authors provide a new algorithmic approach and analysis that breaks this common barrier.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical approach is elegant and sound. The idea of approximating the intractable DP with an adaptive LP is clever and proves to be highly effective. The regret analysis is non-trivial and introduces interesting techniques. The proof sketch for the ALP algorithm, which connects the evolution of the remaining budget to sampling without replacement and leverages the concentration of the hypergeometric distribution, is insightful. Similarly, the decomposition of the UCB-ALP regret into components arising from action-ranking errors and context-ranking errors provides a clear and rigorous analytical path.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It builds its case logically, starting with the simpler known-statistics setting to introduce the ALP algorithm and its near-optimality, before moving to the more complex UCB-ALP for the unknown-statistics setting. This progression makes the core ideas easy to follow. The problem statement, motivations, and comparison to related work are clearly articulated.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a venue like NeurIPS, the claims would be substantially strengthened by including simulation results. Experiments could: (a) validate the theoretical regret bounds, showing the O(log T) and O(√T) scaling in their respective cases; (b) provide a performance comparison against relevant baselines (e.g., algorithms from [17] or [20]); and (c) demonstrate the practical impact of the "boundary cases" and how the algorithm performs near them.

2.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The logarithmic regret bound is achieved under a set of simplifying assumptions: finite contexts, fixed costs, and, for the main analysis, a known context distribution. While the authors justify these assumptions and discuss extensions, it's important to recognize that these are key enablers of their result. The comparison to prior work achieving O(√T) regret should be framed carefully, as those works often handle more general settings (e.g., infinite contexts or unknown costs).

3.  **Boundary Cases:** The analysis carves out "boundary cases" (where the budget ratio `ρ = B/T` matches a cumulative context probability) where the regret reverts to O(√T). The paper asserts these are a set of "zero Lebesgue measure," which is a valid theoretical argument. However, a deeper discussion on their practical implication would be beneficial. For example, how sensitive is the algorithm's performance as `ρ` approaches a boundary? In a real system where `B` and `T` are integers, `ρ` is rational, so the practical relevance of these boundaries could be further explored.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Add an Experimental Section:** Even a concise set of simulations would significantly bolster the paper. I strongly encourage the authors to include experiments in the final version to validate their theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of UCB-ALP compared to existing methods. This would also help in understanding the magnitude of the constants hidden in the big-O notation.

*   **Refine Positioning vs. Prior Work:** In the introduction and related work, it would be helpful to be even more explicit that the logarithmic regret is achieved by exploiting the specific structure of the problem (finite contexts, known distribution, etc.), which distinguishes it from the more general settings in works like [17, 21, 22]. This is not a criticism of the work, but rather a suggestion for precise positioning.

*   **Clarify Generalizations in Main Text:** Sections 5 and 6, which discuss crucial generalizations to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, are very brief and defer most details to the supplement. While this is understandable due to space constraints, adding a little more intuition to the main text would be helpful. For example, for the heterogeneous cost case, briefly describing the structure of the more complex LP problem in Section 6 would give the reader a better sense of the generalization without needing to immediately consult the appendix.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Arguments for:**
    *   Presents a novel and significant theoretical result (O(log T) regret) for an important problem.
    *   The algorithmic approach (Adaptive LP) is innovative and could inspire further work.
    *   The analysis is technically deep and well-executed.
    *   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

*   **Arguments against:**
    *   The complete lack of experiments leaves the practical performance of the algorithm unevaluated.
    *   The strongest results rely on a simplified problem setting compared to some of the related literature.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper presenting a significant theoretical contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The development of the ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms and the accompanying analysis that establishes a logarithmic regret bound are impressive. The clarity of presentation is commendable. The main weakness is the absence of empirical validation. However, the strength and novelty of the theoretical results are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. This work provides valuable insights into tackling constrained bandit problems and opens a new direction for achieving better-than-√T regret by leveraging problem structure.
