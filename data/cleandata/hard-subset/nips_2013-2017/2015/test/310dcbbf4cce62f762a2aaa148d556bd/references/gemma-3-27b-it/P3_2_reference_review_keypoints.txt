- Tackles constrained contextual bandits with budget and time limits, proposing algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret under finite-context, fixed-cost assumptions.  
- Introduces ALP (Adaptive Linear Programming) and UCB-ALP, combining LP-based policy approximation with UCB for unknown rewards.  
- Provides rigorous analysis and novel techniques to handle exploration–exploitation interplay under budget constraints, including boundary-case treatment.  
- Well-motivated problem formulation with practical examples (crowdsourcing, clinical trials).  
- Strong theoretical guarantees: logarithmic regret is achieved and thoroughly analyzed.  
- ALP is a clever LP-based approximation; UCB-ALP is a natural, effective extension for unknown rewards.  
- Novel analytical tools for adaptive algorithms and fluctuating budgets.  
- Clear, well-organized presentation and good positioning relative to related literature.  
- Assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs) are simplifying and limit applicability; generalization to continuous contexts or stochastic/heterogeneous costs needs more development.  
- Some parts of the analysis are dense and hard to follow; more intuition and intermediate steps would help accessibility.  
- Lacks empirical evaluation — simulations or real-data experiments are needed to validate practical performance.  
- Boundary cases yield O(√T) regret, which is less satisfactory and could merit mitigation strategies.  
- Comparison to two recent dynamic-context works ([21,22]) is brief; a more detailed contrast is warranted.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, novel algorithmic approach, rigorous analysis, clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: no empirical validation, simplifying assumptions, and high analytical complexity limiting accessibility.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper deserving acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions).  
- Suggested revisions: add small-scale empirical evaluation, expand heterogeneous-cost discussion in main text, provide more intuitive analysis steps, strengthen comparison with recent work, and discuss ways to mitigate boundary-case regret.
