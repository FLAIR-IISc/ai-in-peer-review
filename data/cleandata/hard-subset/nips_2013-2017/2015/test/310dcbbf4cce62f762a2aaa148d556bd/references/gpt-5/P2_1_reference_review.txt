Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single budget and a finite time horizon, where each action under a context consumes a fixed cost and the process stops when either time or budget is exhausted. The main idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle with an adaptive linear programming (ALP) relaxation that enforces the budget constraint in expectation using the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. With known statistics, ALP is shown to be near-optimal: O(1) regret except on boundary instances (ρ equal to cumulative context probabilities) where regret is O(√T). With unknown rewards, the authors combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) and prove O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They further discuss extensions to unknown context distribution (via empirical estimates, with a truncated analysis to recover concentration) and to heterogeneous costs (via an LP over all context-action pairs; an ε-first variant for learning).

Relation to prior work
- Bandits with knapsacks and resourceful contextual bandits typically achieve O(√T) regret under general settings, often with large policy classes or computational complexity. This paper restricts to finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, and a single budget, and claims the first logarithmic regret in such constrained contextual bandits.
- Compared with Agrawal–Devanur (concave rewards/convex constraints) and subsequent dynamic-context variants that obtain O(√T), this work leverages an adaptive average-budget relaxation to tighten regret.
- The ALP approach (LP relaxation of DP) is known, but here it is made adaptive in a way that yields constant-gap performance and enables simple coupling with UCB.

Quality
- The technical core appears sound. The LP upper bound is standard; the ALP policy uses the tightness of the budget constraint to ensure that the probability of spending per round equals b/τ. Mapping the budget process under ALP to sampling without replacement yields concentration (hypergeometric-style) for the remaining budget, which is key for the O(1) gap. The UCB-ALP regret decomposition into within-context action ranking vs. context-threshold errors is reasonable, and the JK log T term is well-motivated by the need to learn the ordering of JK pairs.
- Proof details are mostly in the supplement; the main text provides clear proof sketches. One place where a more explicit argument would help is the derivation of the exact “without replacement” law for bτ (currently stated as “can be verified”).
- The reliance on strict ordering assumptions (unique best action per context and strictly ordered u*j across contexts) simplifies analysis; boundary instances are handled separately but incur √T terms.
- The extension to unknown context distributions is thoughtful; the truncated EALP analysis addresses dependency, but a full non-truncated analysis would strengthen the contribution.
- No empirical results are shown in the main text; for a paper emphasizing computational efficiency and practical motivation, experiments comparing against RCB/BwK baselines would be valuable.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The model and algorithms are clearly specified; the role of the threshold j̃(ρ) and its adaptive version is well-explained. The regret sources are cleanly decomposed in the UCB-ALP analysis.
- Some notation is dense (e.g., pj(ρ), p̂j(·), thresholds under UCB re-ordering). A brief algorithmic description of how to implement the LP solution via sorting contexts by current û*j would aid readers.
- Minor typos and formatting inconsistencies exist, but nothing that impedes understanding.

Originality
- The combination of an adaptive LP relaxation with a budget-smoothing view that yields hypergeometric concentration, and the demonstration of O(1) gap to the oracle in non-boundary cases, is novel and insightful within this constrained setting.
- Achieving O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits with discrete contexts/actions under fixed costs appears new and sharp for this regime.
- The regret decomposition for UCB-ALP that isolates context-threshold errors (many do not contribute due to the threshold structure) is a nice analytical contribution.

Significance
- The results advance the frontier for constrained contextual bandits by showing that, under practical but restricted assumptions, one can do better than the common O(√T) rates. The methods are computationally light and potentially applicable in domains like clinical trials and rate selection.
- The scope is narrower than general RCB/BwK (finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, single resource, known or learnable context distribution), so impact will be strongest within this regime. Nonetheless, the techniques (adaptive budget smoothing, concentration under adaptive control, decoupling estimation from control via ALP) may inspire broader extensions.

Pros
- Clear, principled algorithm (ALP) with strong performance guarantees (O(1) gap known-stats).
- First logarithmic regret bounds for constrained contextual bandits in a natural discrete setting.
- Clean analysis leveraging hypergeometric concentration and careful regret decomposition.
- Computationally simple; only ranking is required, aligning well with UCB.

Cons
- Assumptions are restrictive: finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, known context distribution (main), strict ordering; boundary cases degrade to √T.
- Regret scales with JK due to pairwise ranking; no dimension-free or structure-exploiting improvements.
- Empirical validation is missing in the main text; comparisons to recent dynamic-context constrained bandit algorithms would strengthen the case.
- Extensions (unknown π, heterogeneous costs) are only partially developed theoretically (e.g., truncated EALP, ε-first).

Questions/requests for authors
- Provide a more explicit argument for the “sampling without replacement” characterization of the remaining budget under ALP/UCB-ALP.
- Clarify per-round computational complexity (e.g., sorting contexts by û*j and computing p̂j). Is there an O(J log J) implementation?
- Can the boundary-case √T term be mitigated (e.g., via slight budget randomization or smoothing) to recover log regret on average?
- For unknown context distributions, can you bound UCB-EALP without truncation (e.g., via self-normalized concentration)?
- Include experiments (synthetic and, if possible, application-driven) comparing to RCB/BwK-style algorithms and to non-adaptive budget baselines.

Overall evaluation
A solid theoretical contribution with a simple, effective idea and rigorous analysis that advances the state of the art in a well-motivated constrained contextual bandit regime. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the budget-process analysis and, if space permits, adding empirical evidence.
