Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single resource (budget) and a finite horizon, where each action under each context incurs a fixed, known cost and yields a bounded stochastic reward. The process stops when time or budget is exhausted. The goal is to maximize expected total reward with hard budget and time constraints, in the proportional regime B/T → ρ ∈ (0,1). The main technical challenge is that the oracle policy with known statistics is not myopic due to resource coupling; dynamic programming is intractable for analysis and implementation.

The authors propose:
- An LP relaxation of the oracle: replace the hard constraint with an average-per-round constraint and solve a static LP that assigns a play probability p_j to the best action in context j, subject to ∑_j π_j p_j ≤ ρ. This yields a threshold structure by ordering contexts by their best expected reward u*_j, and a per-round value v(ρ). Summed over T, Tv(ρ) upper bounds the oracle’s expected reward.
- Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP): at each round, recompute the LP with the current average remaining budget b_τ/τ and play in the observed context with the LP-prescribed probability. With known rewards and unit costs, ALP achieves O(1) regret except on “boundary” parameters where ρ equals a cumulative context probability q_j, in which case regret is O(√T). The analysis uses a key concentration property of the remaining budget under ALP, showing behavior akin to sampling without replacement (hypergeometric).
- UCB-ALP: when rewards are unknown, combine ALP with UCB estimates of u*_j. The algorithm sorts contexts by UCBs and solves the adaptive LP on those estimates. The regret is O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases, where J is the number of contexts and K the number of actions.
- Extensions: (i) unknown context distribution via empirical estimates (EALP), analyzed in a truncated-update variant to recover similar bounds; (ii) heterogeneous action costs, where the LP operates on all context–action pairs with a constraint of choosing at most one action per context; an ε-first ALP variant is proposed for unknown rewards.

This work builds on and contrasts with: Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17], which yields O(√T) regret against a finite policy class but is computationally heavy; bandits with knapsacks (BwK) [12,15], and budgeted bandits without context [11–16], where logarithmic regret is achievable; and concave-reward convex-constraint bandits [20–22], which obtain O(√T) regret (including with dynamic contexts) under broader models. The novelty here is achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits with finite context/action sets via an adaptive LP relaxation and proving that the gap from the oracle is constant except on measure-zero boundaries.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic ideas are clear, and the regret decomposition for UCB-ALP into action-ranking and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation components is standard and appropriate. The concentration of the remaining budget under ALP is the central technical device; mapping to “sampling without replacement” yields explicit mean/variance and exponential tails used to pin down the O(1) gap to the LP benchmark in non-boundary cases. The extension to UCB-ALP requires showing that this concentration persists under adaptive estimates; the paper claims this and outlines the argument.

Potential issues requiring clarification:
- The claim that b_τ “follows the hypergeometric distribution” under ALP appears stronger than necessary; demonstrating exact distributional equivalence would need independence conditions that may not hold as p_j depends on b_τ/τ. It may suffice to prove the stated mean/variance and tail bounds via martingale or coupling arguments; the paper should make this precise and avoid overclaiming exact distributional identity if not proved.
- In UCB-ALP, the concentration of b_τ and the preservation of the average-spend property when rankings are occasionally wrong need a more explicit argument (e.g., bounding deviations from b/τ spending probability due to misrankings).
- The boundary-case √T term lacks a matching lower bound; while plausible, either a lower bound or a more detailed discussion of tightness would strengthen the claim that √T is unavoidable at boundaries.
- The analysis for unknown context distribution is provided only for a truncated EALP; the choice of truncation time and its effect on constants should be stated explicitly.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear motivation for LP relaxation and adaptive control via b_τ/τ. The algorithm descriptions are readable. Some notation/formatting glitches should be fixed (e.g., the piecewise definition of p_j in Eq. (4) is incomplete in the current typeset; the term Θ(o)√T is unclear; a duplicated index appears in û terms). It would help to:
- State all assumptions (uniqueness of best action per context, strict ordering u*_1 > … > u*_J) up front and discuss how ties are handled.
- Make the threshold structure and its computational implications explicit (the LP solution can be computed in O(J) given sorted u*_j; no general LP solver is needed each round).
- Provide a brief dynamic-programming benchmark description to contextualize the LP relaxation beyond the inequality Û ≥ U*.

Originality
The adaptive LP idea tailored to budgeted contextual bandits with finite contexts/actions and hard constraints, and the resulting logarithmic regret (non-boundary) against the oracle, appear novel relative to prior work that either achieves O(√T) regret under more general models or works against a finite policy class. The “only ordering needed” property of ALP is a useful insight for combining with UCB.

Significance
Within the important BwK/RCB line, the paper contributes a computationally simple method with stronger instance-dependent regret guarantees under practically motivated restrictions (finite contexts/actions, fixed costs). This is likely to be used as a baseline and inspire methods for more general settings. The scope is limited by assumptions (single resource, known/finite contexts, fixed costs; unknown context distribution handled via truncation), but the methodological insight is valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and simple algorithmic framework (ALP and UCB-ALP) with strong regret guarantees (O(1) to the oracle in known-stats, O(JK log T) otherwise) under a well-motivated setting.
- Novel use of adaptive LP to decouple learning and resource control; insightful analysis via budget concentration.
- Addresses a gap between general RCB (computationally heavy, O(√T)) and practical constrained contextual problems.
- Potentially impactful for applications like recommendation with budgets, clinical trials, and online procurement.

Cons for acceptance
- Some technical claims (hypergeometric law for b_τ, preservation under UCB-ALP) need more rigorous, explicit proofs in the main text or clearer pointers to the supplement.
- Boundary-case behavior lacks lower bounds; it remains unclear if O(√T) is optimal there.
- Extensions (unknown context distribution, heterogeneous costs) are only partially analyzed (truncated updates; ε-first), with weaker guarantees than the main setting.
- Empirical evaluation is not discussed here; comparisons to RCB/Mixture Elimination and convex-knapsack UCB under finite contexts would help substantiate practical gains.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and tighten the analysis of b_τ (use martingale concentration or coupling), and explicitly prove the corresponding properties under UCB-ALP.
- Add a discussion or result on lower bounds, especially at boundary parameters.
- Provide explicit computational complexity per round and practical implementation details (maintaining sorted u*_j, handling ties).
- For unknown context distribution, specify the truncation schedule and trade-offs; consider whether self-normalized concentration can avoid truncation.
- Expand the heterogeneous-costs section with a UCB-style variant (if possible) and empirical comparisons.
- Include experiments comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to RCB and convex-knapsack baselines on synthetic and, if possible, real data.

Overall, the paper offers a solid theoretical contribution with a simple and implementable algorithm. With the above clarifications, it would be a strong addition to the literature on contextual bandits with resource constraints.
