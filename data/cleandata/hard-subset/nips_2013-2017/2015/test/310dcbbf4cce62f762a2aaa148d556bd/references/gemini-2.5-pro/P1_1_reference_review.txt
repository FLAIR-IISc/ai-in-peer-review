### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the constrained contextual bandit problem, where an agent must select actions over a time horizon T with a total budget B, receiving rewards that depend on a stochastically arriving context. The key challenge is that the optimal policy, which depends on the remaining time and budget, is a complex dynamic program (DP) that is computationally and analytically intractable.

The authors' main contribution is a novel and elegant method to approximate this DP. They first analyze the case with known statistics, proposing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. At each step, ALP solves a simple LP that relaxes the hard budget constraint to an average one, using the *current* ratio of remaining budget to remaining time. This adaptive approach is shown to be near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret compared to an LP-based upper bound on the oracle's performance. This result holds for most parameter settings, termed "non-boundary cases," while the regret is O(√T) in specific "boundary cases."

For the more practical setting of unknown rewards, the paper combines the ALP framework with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. This algorithm uses UCB estimates of the rewards as input for the adaptive LP at each step. The paper's main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases, a significant improvement over the O(√T) regret achieved by prior work on related problems. This is the first work to demonstrate logarithmic regret for this class of problems. The paper also discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is achieving logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, which is a substantial improvement over the state-of-the-art O(√T) bounds. The core idea of using an *adaptive* linear programming relaxation to approximate the optimal DP is novel and powerful. This approach elegantly decouples the complex interaction between resource allocation and information gathering, providing a clear and principled way to design low-regret algorithms.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and introduces several insightful techniques. A standout element is the analysis of the ALP algorithm, which cleverly maps the evolution of the remaining budget to a "sampling without replacement" process. This allows the authors to model the remaining budget with a hypergeometric distribution and leverage its strong concentration properties to prove the O(1) regret bound for ALP. The regret decomposition for the UCB-ALP analysis is also well-structured, carefully separating the regret from sub-optimal action selection within a context from the regret due to context-ranking errors and budget fluctuations.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors build their case logically, starting with the simplified known-statistics setting to introduce the core ALP approximation, which provides strong intuition before tackling the more complex unknown-statistics case. The introduction clearly lays out the problem, its challenges, the paper's contributions, and its relationship to prior work. The proof sketches provided in the main text are effective at conveying the key ideas of the analysis.

4.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient. At each time step, it requires solving a very small LP, whose size depends only on the number of contexts, not the time horizon or budget. This makes the algorithm practical, in stark contrast to the intractable nature of the optimal DP solution.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The strong logarithmic regret bounds are achieved under a set of simplifying assumptions: a finite number of discrete contexts, fixed and known costs (unit costs for the main analysis), and a known context distribution. While these assumptions are justified as a first step and relevant for some applications, they limit the generality of the work compared to related literature (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., 2014; Agrawal et al., 2015) that handles more general settings but with higher O(√T) regret. The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are sketched but feel less developed than the core contribution. For instance, the heterogeneous cost setting moves to an ε-first algorithm rather than a fully online one like UCB-ALP, which feels like a step back in elegance.

2.  **Boundary Cases:** The analysis carves out "boundary cases" where the budget-to-time ratio `ρ` exactly matches the cumulative probability of a set of top-ranked contexts. In these cases, the regret degrades to O(√T). While the authors correctly state that this set of parameters has zero Lebesgue measure, the practical implications are not fully discussed. If `ρ` is very close to a boundary, the "constant" in the O(1) and O(log T) regret bounds (which depends on the distance to the boundary) could be very large, making the asymptotic result less meaningful for finite T. A discussion on the sensitivity of the regret to proximity to these boundaries would have been valuable.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are significant, the inclusion of even a small set of experiments would have greatly strengthened the paper. A simulation comparing UCB-ALP to a non-adaptive baseline (e.g., based on Agrawal & Devanur, 2014) would have provided empirical validation for the theoretical improvement from O(√T) to O(log T) and demonstrated the practical advantage of the adaptive design. Experiments could also shed light on the performance in and near the boundary cases.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   The connection made between the ALP budget evolution and the hypergeometric distribution is a highlight of the paper. This is a very clean analytical tool that directly leads to the tight concentration results needed for the O(1) regret proof.
*   In Section 6 (Heterogeneous Costs), it is stated that an ε-First ALP algorithm is proposed. It would be helpful to briefly explain why the more integrated UCB-ALP approach was not extended to this case. Is it due to a fundamental analytical challenge where the simple reward-efficiency `u/c` is no longer a sufficient statistic for ranking actions?
*   The regret analysis for UCB-ALP in Section 4.3 cleverly partitions the sources of error. The categorization of ranking errors into `E_rank,0`, `E_rank,1`, and `E_rank,2` is particularly effective, as it shows that not all ranking mistakes lead to regret due to the thresholding nature of the ALP policy.

### **Conclusion**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of contextual bandits. It is the first to achieve logarithmic regret for the important problem of contextual bandits with budget constraints. The central idea of using an adaptive LP is both novel and elegant, and the supporting analysis is technically sound and insightful. While the results rely on some simplifying assumptions and the paper lacks experiments, the importance and novelty of the theoretical breakthrough are undeniable. The work advances the state of the art and provides a strong foundation for future research into more general constrained bandit problems. I strongly recommend acceptance.
