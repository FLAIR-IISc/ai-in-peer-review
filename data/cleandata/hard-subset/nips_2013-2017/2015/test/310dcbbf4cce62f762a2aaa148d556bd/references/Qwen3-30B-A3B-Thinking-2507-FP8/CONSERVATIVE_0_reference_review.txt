

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits with budget constraints, proposing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm for known statistics and a UCB-ALP algorithm for unknown statistics. The authors claim that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases and O(√T) for boundary cases, which they present as a significant advance over previous work.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on the constrained bandit literature (e.g., [17, 20-22]), but the authors claim their approach is the first to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. The key innovation is using an adaptive linear programming approximation of the oracle policy, which they argue tolerates estimation errors and can be combined with UCB for unknown statistics. The paper is well-organized and presents a technically sound theoretical analysis for the unit-cost case with known context distribution.

## Strengths

The paper has several notable strengths: The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear theorems and proofs for the unit-cost case. The authors acknowledge boundary cases where regret is O(√T) rather than logarithmic, showing appropriate scientific caution. The ALP approach cleverly leverages linear programming to approximate the oracle, which is a promising methodological insight. The paper also provides a clear distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases, which is important for understanding the limitations of the approach.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent it from meeting NIPS standards for a high-quality contribution:

1. **Lack of Experimental Validation**: The paper claims UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret but provides no experimental results to support this. Section 5 mentions "numerical simulations" but contains no actual results, making it impossible to assess whether the theoretical claims hold in practice. This is a critical omission for a paper claiming to solve a practical problem.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent Work**: The authors cite [20-22] as recent work on constrained bandits but fail to properly distinguish their approach. [21] explicitly addresses dynamic contexts and claims O(√T) regret, which contradicts the paper's claim of being "the first work that shows how to achieve logarithmic regret." The paper doesn't explain why their approach achieves logarithmic regret while [21] achieves O(√T), which is a major point of confusion.

3. **Limited Scope**: The theoretical results are restricted to unit-cost systems with known context distribution. The generalization to heterogeneous costs (Section 6) is only briefly sketched without full theoretical analysis. The paper claims to address "practical" applications but limits its analysis to highly restrictive assumptions.

4. **Boundary Case Ambiguity**: The paper claims logarithmic regret "except for a set of parameters of zero Lebesgue measure," but this is misleading. The boundary cases where regret is O(√T) are not rare in practice—they occur when the budget-to-time ratio ρ equals certain cumulative probabilities qj. The paper doesn't provide any intuition for how often these boundary cases occur in real applications.

5. **Lack of Practical Considerations**: The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity beyond noting that "the proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is quite computationally efficient." For a problem with potentially large context and action spaces, this claim needs substantial justification.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach that builds on established methods (linear programming and UCB), but the lack of experimental validation, insufficient comparison with recent work, and limited scope prevent it from meeting the high standards expected at NIPS. The claim of achieving logarithmic regret is only valid for a subset of cases (non-boundary cases), which the authors themselves acknowledge are "a set of parameters of zero Lebesgue measure." This is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to solve a practical problem with important applications.

While the ALP approximation is a clever idea, the paper fails to demonstrate through experiments that this approach actually outperforms existing methods in practice. The theoretical results are promising but incomplete, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address how their method differs from and improves upon the recent work they cite.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper has a theoretically sound core idea, it lacks the necessary experimental validation and thorough comparison with recent work to support its claims. The significant limitations in scope and the failure to provide empirical evidence for the theoretical claims prevent this paper from making a meaningful contribution to the field at this stage. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper with comprehensive experiments, clearer differentiation from recent work, and more thorough analysis of the boundary cases before it could be considered for acceptance.
