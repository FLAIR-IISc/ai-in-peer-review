## Peer Review: Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of contextual bandits, addressing the practically important problem of resource constraints (budget and time) alongside contextual information. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of their proposed algorithms, achieving logarithmic regret under reasonable assumptions. The paper is well-written and organized, and the technical details are generally clear. While the problem setting is somewhat restricted (discrete contexts, fixed costs), the results are a significant step forward and lay a strong foundation for future work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints – a critical extension of the standard contextual bandit model. Existing work on constrained bandits largely focuses on the non-contextual setting, and applying those results directly to contextual bandits is not straightforward.  The authors position their work relative to Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17], pointing out the computational complexity and generality of that approach. They strategically simplify the problem (discrete contexts, fixed costs) to design practical algorithms with provable logarithmic regret.  The key innovation is the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which approximates the optimal policy by relaxing the hard budget constraint and dynamically adjusting the average budget limit.  They then combine ALP with UCB to handle unknown rewards, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. The work builds upon existing UCB techniques [4, 24] and LP relaxations for MDPs [19], but importantly adapts these concepts to the constrained contextual bandit setting in a novel and insightful way. Recent preprints [21, 22] extend work on dynamic context bandits with constraints, but currently achieve only √T regret, highlighting the significance of this contribution.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:**  The paper addresses a very relevant and practically motivated problem. Budget constraints are pervasive in real-world applications of contextual bandits, and this work provides a principled way to handle them.
* **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide rigorous regret analysis, demonstrating logarithmic regret for UCB-ALP (except for boundary cases where it is √T). This is a significant achievement in the constrained contextual bandit setting.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The key ideas are clearly explained, and the mathematical notation is generally accessible.
* **Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP):** The ALP algorithm is a clever and effective approach to approximate the optimal policy under budget constraints. The dynamic adjustment of the budget limit based on remaining time and budget is a key insight.
* **Novel Techniques:** The analysis employs several novel techniques, like showing concentration properties under adaptive algorithms and bounding estimation errors under random contexts, contributing beyond the immediate problem.
* **Generalization Discussion:** The paper briefly discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, indicating potential for broader applicability.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Simplifying Assumptions:** The assumption of discrete contexts and fixed costs limits the direct applicability of the results to some real-world scenarios. However, the authors acknowledge this and provide discussion for extending to more general cases.
* **Boundary Cases:** The √T regret in the boundary cases is a concern. While the authors acknowledge this, further investigation into the prevalence and impact of these boundary cases would be beneficial.  Clarification on how frequently these occur in practical settings would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper lacks substantial empirical evaluation. Including some simulations to demonstrate the performance of the UCB-ALP algorithm in practice, especially compared to baseline methods, would strengthen the paper.
* **Tightness of Bounds:** The logarithmic regret bound may hide large constants. A discussion of the practical implications of these constants would be valuable.
* **Complexity:** The implementation of ALP (solving LPs repeatedly) could be computationally expensive, especially with a large number of contexts and actions.  A discussion of the computational complexity and potential optimizations would be helpful.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous analysis and well-supported claims. The completeness of the work is good, although some discussion of practical limitations and extensions could be added.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is generally clear, and the explanations are easy to follow.
* **Originality (8/10):** The problem of constrained contextual bandits is novel, and the proposed approach (ALP and UCB-ALP) represents a significant contribution. The combination of existing techniques in a new and effective way is also noteworthy.
* **Significance (9/10):** The results are important and have the potential to be widely used by researchers and practitioners working with contextual bandits in resource-constrained environments. It advances the state-of-the-art in this area.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical guarantees (logarithmic regret).
* Novel and insightful approach (ALP).
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Addresses a practically important problem.
* Significant contribution to the field.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Simplifying assumptions limit direct applicability.
* √T regret in boundary cases.
* Lack of substantial empirical evaluation.




**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically:

* **Empirical Evaluation:**  Include some simulations to demonstrate the performance of UCB-ALP in practice, compared to relevant baselines.
* **Boundary Case Analysis:** Provide more discussion of the boundary cases.  How prevalent are they? Under what conditions do they occur? What is their practical impact?
* **Computational Complexity:** Discuss the computational complexity of ALP and potential optimizations.
* **Constant Factors:** Briefly discuss the potential impact of constant factors hidden within the logarithmic regret bound.

These revisions would further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
