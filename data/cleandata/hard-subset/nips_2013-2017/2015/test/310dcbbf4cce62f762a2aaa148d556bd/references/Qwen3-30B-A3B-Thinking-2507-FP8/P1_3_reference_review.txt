

# Peer Review: "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in contextual bandit literature by studying constrained contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B constraints, where the total cost of actions cannot exceed B. The authors recognize that while budget constraints have been studied in non-contextual bandits (achieving O(√T) regret), these results don't apply to contextual settings due to the interaction between context and cost. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) For known statistics, they propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases where it achieves O(√T)), and (2) For unknown statistics, they combine ALP with UCB to develop UCB-ALP, which achieves O(JK log T) regret (except for boundary cases where it achieves O(√T + JK log T)). The authors demonstrate that their approach decouples the complex interactions between information acquisition and decision making in constrained contextual bandits.

The work builds on contextual bandit literature (e.g., [1-7]) and budget-constrained bandit results (e.g., [11-16]), but makes the crucial observation that budget constraints interact with contextual information in ways that previous approaches don't address. The paper is well-positioned to advance the state of the art, as it achieves logarithmic regret where previous work only achieved sublinear regret.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors carefully develop and justify their approach, providing complete proofs for all key results. They acknowledge limitations (boundary cases, assumptions about costs and context distribution), demonstrating scholarly honesty. The ALP approximation is clever and well-justified, and the regret analysis properly accounts for the complex interactions between context arrival, budget fluctuations, and estimation errors.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by addressing the constrained contextual bandit problem in a way that previous work hasn't. The ALP approach represents a novel combination of linear programming and contextual bandits, and the logarithmic regret bound (O(JK log T)) is a substantial improvement over previous O(√T) results. The paper properly cites and differentiates from related work, including the recent [21, 22] which also addresses dynamic contexts but achieves only O(√T) regret.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is clear, the intuition behind ALP is well-explained, and the regret analysis is logically structured. The authors use consistent notation, provide helpful definitions, and the flow from problem statement to algorithm design to analysis is seamless. The supplementary material appears to contain sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Significance:** The results have both theoretical and practical importance. The logarithmic regret bound represents a major theoretical advancement, and the computational efficiency of the proposed algorithms makes them applicable to real-world problems like clinical trials and crowdsourcing where budget constraints are critical. The paper addresses a natural extension to contextual bandits that has been overlooked in previous work.

## Weaknesses

The paper has a few limitations worth noting. First, while the boundary cases (where regret becomes O(√T)) are acknowledged as an open problem, the authors don't provide additional insights into how to address them, which would strengthen the paper. Second, the main analysis assumes unit costs and known context distribution, with extensions to heterogeneous costs and unknown context distribution relegated to supplementary material. While these extensions are reasonable, the main contribution would be stronger if they were integrated more fully into the core analysis.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the contextual bandit literature. The authors successfully address a natural extension to contextual bandits that has been overlooked in previous work, and they achieve a substantial improvement in regret bounds (logarithmic vs. sublinear) through a clever and well-justified approach. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful problem with practical implications.

The main limitations (boundary cases, assumptions about costs and context distribution) are acknowledged by the authors, and the paper would be strengthened by more detailed discussion of how to address these limitations. However, these do not detract from the paper's significant contribution.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a clear and substantial theoretical contribution to the contextual bandit literature with a well-executed analysis and practical algorithms. The results represent a meaningful advancement over previous work, and the paper is well-suited for NIPS.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Solves a significant gap in contextual bandit literature
- Achieves logarithmic regret where previous work only achieved O(√T)
- Provides computationally efficient algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees
- Well-written with clear, rigorous analysis
- Properly situates work within related literature

**Con:**
- Boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T) are not fully resolved
- Main analysis assumes unit costs and known context distribution
- Some extensions to more general settings are relegated to supplementary material

The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field.
