

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses the important problem of constrained contextual bandits, where the agent must maximize rewards while respecting both a budget constraint and time horizon. The authors make a significant contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret (O(log T)) for this problem under certain simplifying assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint), which is a notable advancement over previous work that achieved only O(√T) regret.

The paper builds on prior work in contextual bandits [1-7] and budget-constrained bandits [11-16], while distinguishing itself from Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] by achieving logarithmic regret rather than O(√T) under their specific assumptions. The key insight is using linear programming to approximate the oracle (which would require dynamic programming), leading to the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. The authors then combine ALP with UCB to create UCB-ALP, which achieves O(log T) regret except for certain boundary cases.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by demonstrating logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under practical assumptions, filling a significant gap in the literature.
2. The ALP approximation is elegant and novel, avoiding the computational complexity of dynamic programming while providing a tight regret bound.
3. The analysis is technically sound, with careful treatment of the interactions between budget fluctuations, context arrivals, and estimation errors.
4. The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions and logical progression from the unit-cost case to generalizations.
5. The authors provide valuable insights about how to decouple information acquisition from decision making in constrained bandits.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The simplifying assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs) limit the applicability to some real-world scenarios. The paper could better discuss how these assumptions might be relaxed in practice.
2. The boundary cases (where regret becomes O(√T)) are mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed. It would be helpful to know how often these cases occur in practice and whether they can be mitigated.
3. The paper lacks empirical validation. While the supplementary material mentions numerical simulations, the main paper would benefit from concrete experimental results showing the practical performance of UCB-ALP compared to baselines.
4. The connection to recent work [21, 22] is mentioned but not fully explored in the context of how their results relate to the authors' contributions. This is particularly important as these papers also address dynamic context bandits.
5. The paper could better explain why previous approaches (like [17] or [20]) cannot achieve logarithmic regret under the authors' assumptions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Include a small set of empirical results showing the regret performance of UCB-ALP in simulated environments to validate the theoretical claims.
2. Expand the discussion of boundary cases to include a quantitative assessment of how frequently they occur in typical problem instances.
3. Provide a more detailed comparison with recent work [21, 22] to clarify the distinct contributions of this paper.
4. Consider a brief discussion of how the assumptions might be relaxed in future work (e.g., how to handle continuous contexts or time-varying costs).
5. Include a more detailed explanation of why the O(log T) bound is optimal in the non-boundary cases, particularly in comparison to the lower bound in [17].

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to constrained contextual bandits, with a clear theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art. The results are significant for the field, as they provide the first logarithmic regret bound for this problem under reasonable assumptions. While the simplifying assumptions limit the immediate applicability, the insights and techniques are valuable and could guide future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

The paper is well-written and the technical analysis is thorough. The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation, which would strengthen the claim that the algorithm performs well in practice. With minor improvements to address the boundary cases and include empirical results, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the contextual bandit literature with significant implications for applications with resource constraints. The technical quality is high, and the results advance the state of the art in a clear and demonstrable way. The minor weaknesses noted above are not sufficient to warrant rejection, especially given the importance of the problem and the novelty of the solution.
