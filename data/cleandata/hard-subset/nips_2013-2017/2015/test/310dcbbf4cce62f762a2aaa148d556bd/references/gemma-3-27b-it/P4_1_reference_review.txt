This paper tackles the problem of constrained contextual bandits, where the agent operates under both a time and a budget constraint. The authors aim to develop algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over existing results for this problem setting. They make several key contributions: a simplified model assumption enabling tractable analysis, an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle, and a UCB-ALP algorithm for the unknown rewards setting, guaranteeing O(log T) regret (except in boundary cases).

The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the problem, related work, proposed algorithms, and theoretical analysis. The connection to existing literature is thorough, particularly regarding contextual bandits, resource allocation, and linear programming relaxations. The authors effectively position their work as filling a gap in the literature by addressing budget constraints in contextual bandit problems, a scenario common in practical applications. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and highlights the challenges. The model assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs) are explicitly stated and justified.

The strength of the paper lies in its theoretical analysis. The development of the ALP algorithm and its analysis is rigorous and insightful. The authors carefully address the difficulties of using dynamic programming by employing linear programming relaxations and adaptive budget allocation. The proof sketches provide a good overview of the key technical steps. The extension to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, while relegated to supplementary material, demonstrates the generality of the proposed approach. The thorough regret analysis, separating the cases for known and unknown statistics, and boundary conditions is commendable.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The boundary cases, where the regret becomes O(√T), are mentioned repeatedly but not fully explored. It’s unclear how prevalent these boundary cases are in practical scenarios, and further discussion on their implications would be beneficial. The analysis relies heavily on the assumption of known context distributions in the main body, with the unknown context distribution case being treated as an extension. A more detailed analysis of the empirical distribution estimation error's impact on the regret bounds would strengthen the paper. While the authors acknowledge limitations and potential for improvement, providing more specific directions for future work would be useful. The numerous lemmas and theorems, while mathematically sound, could be better integrated into the narrative flow to improve readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The problem of constrained contextual bandits with logarithmic regret is novel, addressing a practical limitation of existing contextual bandit algorithms.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithms (ALP and UCB-ALP) are technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis and regret bounds.
*   **Significance:** The results are significant, offering a practical solution for resource-constrained contextual bandit problems with potential applications in areas like clinical trials and online advertising.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the problem, methodology, and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Boundary Cases:** The O(√T) regret in boundary cases requires further investigation.
*   **Context Distribution Assumption:** The reliance on known context distribution in the main body and the limited analysis of the empirical estimation error are potential weaknesses.
*   **Readability:** The density of technical details and numerous lemmas slightly hinder the flow of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of contextual bandits. While some weaknesses exist, the novelty, technical soundness, and potential significance of the work outweigh these concerns. The rigorous analysis and clear presentation make this a strong submission. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the concerns raised regarding boundary cases and the impact of unknown context distributions. Specifically, authors should include a discussion on how often boundary cases are expected to occur in practice and expand the analysis for the empirical estimation case.
