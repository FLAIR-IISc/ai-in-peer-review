Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single hard budget and a finite horizon, where taking an action incurs a (fixed) cost and the process terminates when either budget or time runs out. Under i.i.d. finite contexts, fixed costs, and a single resource, the authors propose:

- An LP relaxation benchmark (static LP with an average budget constraint ρ = B/T) yielding an upper bound Û(T,B) on the oracle reward.
- An Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that replaces ρ with the instantaneous average remaining budget bτ/τ. For known rewards and unit costs, ALP is shown to be within O(1) of the oracle except at “boundary” values of ρ where regret is O(√T).
- For unknown rewards, a UCB-ALP algorithm that uses UCB estimates, claims O(JK log T) regret off-boundary and O(√T + JK log T) on-boundary.
- Extensions: empirical estimation of context distribution (EALP/UCB-EALP) with analysis for a truncated estimator, and a heterogeneous-costs generalization with an ε-first variant.

Relation to prior work
This builds on “Bandits with Knapsacks” and “Resourceful Contextual Bandits” (RCB), which attain O(√T) regret in more general settings (possibly infinite contexts/policies, multiple resources). It also relates to Agrawal–Devanur’s concave/convex knapsack bandits, which achieve O(√T). The paper’s main novelty claim is logarithmic regret under discrete, finite settings via adaptive LP control and UCB. The use of LP relaxations for DP/MDPs is well known, but the adaptation to remaining budget and the specific concentration analysis are tailored to this setting.

Strengths
- Clear and simple idea: adapt the LP relaxation to the remaining budget, leveraging a threshold structure in the unit-cost case. The concentration-of-budget argument (hypergeometric sampling) is neat and yields an O(1) gap to the LP bound off-boundary.
- For unknown rewards, the decoupling between decision-making (ALP) and learning (UCB) is appealing, and the regret decomposition is standard yet effective. The claimed O(JK log T) regret off-boundary is competitive given the discreteness assumptions.
- Computationally, the unit-cost ALP reduces to a threshold/randomization rule; this is implementable, and the approach is more lightweight than policy-elimination approaches for RCB.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Restrictive assumptions: finite contexts, i.i.d. arrivals, single resource, fixed known costs, and known context distribution in the main results. The unknown-π case is only handled via a truncated estimator and is technically more delicate; the main paper defers details. While specialization is reasonable, the gap to the general BwK/RCB settings is large, and practical applicability may be limited.
- Boundary cases: The assertion that boundary parameters form a measure-zero set in terms of ρ and π is mathematically true, but in discrete budget/time problems ρ = B/T is rational and easily aligns with cumulative probabilities qj, especially with coarse π. Near-boundary performance depends on δ = min{|ρ − qj|}, and the constants in concentration/regret can degrade severely for small δ. The paper should discuss robustness for near-boundary regimes and propose stabilizing heuristics (e.g., conservative budgeting, smoothing/perturbation).
- Regret bounds are coarse and lack instance dependence: RUCB-ALP = O(JK log T) elides dependence on reward gaps Δ, which typically drive instance-dependent performance. It would strengthen the work to provide Δ-dependent bounds for the action-ranking part and clarify how context-ranking errors scale with the inter-context gaps u∗j − u∗j̃(ρ)+1.
- Unknown context distribution: The full UCB-EALP analysis is not provided; only a truncated-empirical analysis is stated. Dependence on truncation time and the resulting constants are unclear. This is a significant limitation if π is unknown, which is common in applications.
- Heterogeneous costs: The move to an ε-first strategy is heavy-handed and likely suboptimal, discarding the elegance of UCB-ALP. No complexity or performance discussion beyond O(1)/O(√T) is given; solving an LP over JK variables per round is potentially nontrivial, and no incremental/greedy structure is identified.
- Comparison to prior work: The “first to achieve logarithmic regret” claim is qualified by strong assumptions; the paper should more carefully position against works that get O(√T) in broader settings and clarify whether any lower bounds preclude better than O(√T) without these assumptions. No lower bound tailored to the constrained contextual setting is provided.
- Empirics: The manuscript refers to simulations but includes none. Even for a theory paper, small-scale experiments illustrating robustness near boundary, unknown-π behavior, and heterogeneous-cost cases would improve credibility.
- Minor clarity issues: Several proofs and constants are deferred; some notation is terse (e.g., Θ(o) in Theorem 1 is unclear), and the role of the reduced UCB coefficient should be justified carefully. References are incomplete in places.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide instance-dependent bounds (with explicit gap dependence) for the UCB-ALP regret decomposition, at least for the within-context part and for context-ranking errors relative to the threshold?
- How sensitive is ALP/UCB-ALP to near-boundary δ? Any principled way to avoid O(√T) scaling when ρ is close to qj (e.g., smoothing ρ, confidence-based conservative consumption)?
- For unknown π, can you analyze the full UCB-EALP without truncation, perhaps via self-normalized concentration or mixing-time arguments if contexts are i.i.d.?
- In the heterogeneous-cost setting, can UCB be integrated into the LP (confidence regions over uj,k/cj,k) to avoid ε-first?
- What is the per-round complexity for heterogeneous costs, and can structure (e.g., sorting by indices) reduce it?

Pros
- Conceptually simple, computationally light method in the unit-cost case.
- Clean analysis for known statistics with a useful concentration argument.
- Potentially useful building block for more general constrained bandits.

Cons
- Strong assumptions; limited generality.
- Boundary/near-boundary sensitivity.
- Missing instance-dependent analysis, lower bounds, and empirical validation.
- Partial treatment of unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper contains a nice idea and solid analysis under restrictive assumptions, but in its current form lacks robustness, broader validation, and complete treatment of key practical cases (unknown π, heterogeneous costs with principled exploration). Strengthening these aspects and adding empirical results would significantly improve the contribution.

Confidence: 0.7
