### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of contextual bandits under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), a setting termed constrained contextual bandits. The key challenge identified by the authors is the intractability of the optimal policy, which would typically be solved via dynamic programming (DP). The paper's core contribution is to propose a tractable and near-optimal approach by approximating the DP with a sequence of linear programs (LPs).

First, for the case with known reward statistics, the authors introduce the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. At each step, ALP solves a static LP using the ratio of remaining budget to remaining time as the average budget constraint. This elegant approach is shown to be remarkably effective, achieving O(1) regret—a constant gap from the optimal—except for a specific set of "boundary cases" where the regret is O(√T).

Second, for the more practical setting with unknown rewards, the paper combines the ALP framework with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. This algorithm uses UCB estimates of the rewards within the adaptive LP framework. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases. This logarithmic regret is a significant improvement over the typical O(√T) regret found in related literature for constrained bandit problems. The paper also briefly discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is achieving a logarithmic regret bound for a contextual bandit problem with budget constraints. This is a substantial theoretical advance. Prior and concurrent work on similar problems, such as Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] or bandits with knapsacks [12, 20], typically establish O(√T) regret. The demonstration that O(log T) is achievable, even under simplifying assumptions, is an important and impactful result for the community.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Methodology:** The idea of using an *adaptive* LP relaxation is the key technical innovation and is both simple and powerful. It effectively decouples the complex state-dependent decision-making (handled by the LP) from the information-gathering aspect (handled by UCB). This provides a clear and principled framework for tackling the problem, moving beyond fixed-policy benchmarks used in some prior work [17].

3.  **Technically Sound Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and introduces novel techniques. The mapping of the ALP budget evolution to "sampling without replacement" and the subsequent use of concentration bounds for the hypergeometric distribution (Lemma 2) is a clever and effective way to analyze the fluctuation of the remaining budget. The regret decomposition for UCB-ALP into action-ranking and context-ranking errors is well-reasoned and provides a clear path for the proof.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. It builds intuition by first tackling the simpler known-statistics case with ALP before moving to the UCB-ALP algorithm for the unknown-statistics case. This progression makes the core ideas easy to follow and appreciate.

### Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement)

1.  **The "Boundary Case" Problem:** The distinction between non-boundary and boundary cases is central to the results. The paper claims the boundary cases correspond to a set of parameters of "zero Lebesgue measure," which is technically true if the budget ratio ρ is a real number. However, the performance degradation to O(√T) is a significant limitation. More critically, the analysis of the non-boundary case seems to hide a dependency on the distance to the nearest boundary. The O(1) regret bound in Theorem 1 depends on `δ`, the distance to the nearest boundary `qj`. As ρ approaches a boundary, `δ` -> 0, and the constant regret bound explodes. This suggests that performance is poor not just *at* the boundary but also *near* the boundary, which is a much larger and more realistic set of scenarios. A discussion of this "near-boundary" behavior is crucial for a complete understanding of the algorithm's performance and is currently missing.

2.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The main results (Theorems 1 and 2) are derived under several strong assumptions: finite discrete contexts, known context distribution (`π_j`), and unit costs for all actions. While these assumptions are acknowledged and help isolate the core challenges, they limit the direct applicability of the main results. The extensions to unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs are deferred to the supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess the robustness of the proposed framework. The analysis for the unknown context distribution, in particular, relies on a "truncated" version of the algorithm, which feels less natural than the main algorithm.

3.  **Computational Efficiency:** The paper describes the UCB-ALP algorithm as "computationally efficient." However, it requires solving a small LP at *every time step*. While sorting the J context UCBs and applying the closed-form solution (Eq. 4) is fast, the generalization to heterogeneous costs (Section 6) requires solving a more general LP over all JK context-action pairs, which could become computationally intensive if J or K is large. The claim of efficiency should be better qualified.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Clarity on Heterogeneous Costs:** In Section 6, the paper states that the ALP needs to solve an LP "accounting for all context-action pairs." Does this mean the decision variables are `p_j,k` (probability of choosing action k for context j)? This would be an LP with JK variables and J+1 constraints. It would be helpful to state the LP formulation explicitly in the main text to clarify this, as it's a significant departure from the simple thresholding rule in the unit-cost case.

*   **Relation to Concurrent Work:** The footnote mentioning Agrawal et al. [21, 22] is appreciated. A more integrated discussion in the related work section would strengthen the paper by placing its contributions in a clearer context. For example, contrasting the assumptions (finite vs. infinite contexts) and results (logarithmic vs. square-root regret) would highlight this paper's unique contribution more effectively.

### Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on bandit problems with constraints. The introduction of the ALP framework and the achievement of O(log T) regret are novel and important. The paper is well-written and the analysis is solid.

The main weaknesses are the unaddressed issue of near-boundary performance, which could be a major practical limitation, and the simplifying assumptions under which the main results are proven. Despite these concerns, the novelty and significance of the core ideas and results outweigh the limitations. This paper will likely inspire further research on achieving low-regret algorithms for more general constrained reinforcement learning problems.

**Recommendation: Accept**
