This paper addresses the important problem of contextual bandits with budget constraints, a realistic extension of the standard model. The authors aim to develop algorithms with logarithmic regret, a challenging goal given the added complexity. The paper builds upon prior work in both constrained bandits and contextual bandits, appropriately identifying the gaps and motivations for their work. The proposed Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, and its combination with UCB (UCB-ALP), are presented as key contributions.

However, despite the relevance of the problem and the clear presentation, I have significant reservations about the paper's strength. My primary concern revolves around the limited empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is extensive, the lack of substantial experimental results – beyond a brief mention of numerical simulations (not included in the submission) – leaves me unconvinced of the practical performance of the proposed algorithms. The theoretical guarantees, especially the boundary cases with √T regret, raise concerns about real-world applicability. The constant factors hidden in the logarithmic terms are also not discussed, which is crucial for assessing the practical utility of these algorithms.

The extension to unknown context distributions through the empirical estimate seems reasonable, but the analysis relying on a truncated version introduces an artificial constraint and its impact on performance isn't fully explored. The handling of heterogeneous costs is also presented somewhat briefly, and further justification for the design choices would be beneficial.

The paper's strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The approximation of the oracle with LP and the subsequent adaptive budget adjustment are clever ideas. The attempt to decouple information acquisition and decision making is commendable. However, the theoretical results, while mathematically sound, are presented without sufficient connection to practical scenarios. The paper feels heavily focused on analytical tractability at the expense of empirical demonstration.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the problem and proposed solution.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms.
*   Novel approach using adaptive linear programming.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation.
*   Theoretical guarantees with potentially large constant factors.
*   Analysis of the truncated empirical distribution may not reflect practical performance.
*   Limited discussion of the practical implications of the boundary cases.
*   The complexity of the algorithm, while not explicitly stated, seems high, and the paper does not address computational efficiency concerns beyond stating the algorithms are "easily implementable."

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for solving a challenging problem.
*   The analysis is mathematically rigorous and provides valuable insights into the behavior of constrained contextual bandit algorithms.
*   The proposed ALP algorithm offers a potentially efficient way to approximate the optimal policy.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation raises serious concerns about the practical relevance of the work.
*   The theoretical guarantees, particularly the √T regret in boundary cases, may limit the algorithm's applicability.
*   The paper primarily focuses on theoretical analysis, lacking a thorough discussion of implementation details and computational complexity.
*   The connection to existing literature, particularly more recent work extending to dynamic contexts ([21, 22] as acknowledged by the authors), needs to be more thoroughly addressed. While the authors mention these papers achieve √T regret, they don’t discuss the specific differences or advantages of their approach beyond the logarithmic regret claim (which is not substantiated by experiments).



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject** with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after significantly expanding the experimental evaluation. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the lack of empirical support prevents me from recommending acceptance at this time. A thorough experimental comparison with existing algorithms (including [21, 22]) is crucial to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed UCB-ALP algorithm and to assess the significance of the logarithmic regret guarantee. Furthermore, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and practical considerations would strengthen the paper. The paper, in its current form, feels incomplete and overly focused on theoretical guarantees without sufficient attention to practical feasibility.
