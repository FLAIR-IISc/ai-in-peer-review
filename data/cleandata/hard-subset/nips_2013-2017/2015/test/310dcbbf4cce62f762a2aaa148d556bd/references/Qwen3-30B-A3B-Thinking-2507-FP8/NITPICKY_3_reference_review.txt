

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, proposing ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms that achieve O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases. The authors make a reasonable simplification by assuming finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which they justify with applications in clinical trials and wireless networks. The key insight is approximating the oracle with linear programming, which provides a tractable benchmark for regret analysis.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear contribution to a relevant problem (constrained contextual bandits), with a well-motivated approach that combines linear programming with bandit algorithms. The problem is well-positioned within the literature, with appropriate references to related work on budgeted bandits and contextual bandits. The theoretical analysis is generally sound, with careful attention to the nuances of the constrained bandit setting. The paper also provides valuable insights into how to decouple information acquisition from decision making in constrained bandit problems.

**Weaknesses (major):** Despite the paper's claims of novelty, the authors fail to properly address recent related work [21, 22] that also claims to handle dynamic contexts with O(√T) regret. The paper states "to the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that shows how to achieve logarithmic regret," but [21, 22] explicitly extend [20] to dynamic contexts, which contradicts this claim. This omission is significant and undermines the novelty claim.

**Weaknesses (minor but critical):** 
1. In Theorem 1, the regret bound for non-boundary cases is stated as RALP(T,B) ≤ u*₁ - u*J - e^{-2δ²}, which is mathematically inconsistent. The left-hand side is a regret that should depend on T, while the right-hand side is a constant. This appears to be a typo, but it's a critical error that casts doubt on the entire analysis.

2. The paper claims "a set of parameters of zero Lebesgue measure" for boundary cases, but never explicitly defines these parameters or explains why they form a set of zero measure. This lack of precision is concerning in a theoretical paper.

3. The paper states "the regret due to the linear relaxation is negligible in most cases" but provides no analysis or evidence for why this is true for "most cases" beyond the boundary cases. The term "negligible" is too vague for a theoretical paper.

4. The paper uses inconsistent notation: "j̃(ρ)" with a tilde in some places but not others, and the definition of qj in Section 3 (qj = ∑j j'=1 πj') appears to have a typo (should be qj = ∑_{j'=1}^j πj').

5. The paper claims to achieve O(log T) regret but fails to provide the precise constant in the big-O notation, which is unusual for bandit regret analyses.

6. In Section 4.3, the regret partitioning is described as RUCB-ALP ≤ R(a) + R(c), but the definitions of R(a) and R(c) are not clearly separated in the text, making it difficult to follow the analysis.

**Technical concerns:** The paper assumes known context distribution throughout most of the analysis, which is a strong assumption that significantly limits the practical applicability of the results. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address how their approach would scale to unknown context distributions beyond the truncated EALP analysis in Section 5, which lacks rigorous analysis.

**Clarity and presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized, but the proof sketches are insufficiently detailed for a NIPS paper. The connection between the ALP approximation and the oracle algorithm isn't clearly established, and the intuition for why the LP approximation works isn't sufficiently developed. The paper's claim that "the regret due to the linear relaxation is negligible" needs stronger justification.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some interesting insights, the technical flaws (particularly the incorrect regret bound in Theorem 1) and the failure to properly address recent related work [21, 22] are significant enough to undermine the paper's contribution. The minor formatting inconsistencies and notation errors, while seemingly small, collectively indicate a lack of careful proofreading that suggests the paper may not be ready for publication. The paper's claims of novelty are unsubstantiated by a proper literature review, and the theoretical results contain critical errors that would require substantial revision to fix.

The paper has potential but currently falls short of NIPS's standards for technical rigor and scholarly completeness. The authors would need to significantly revise the theoretical analysis, properly address related work, and clarify their definitions before this work could be considered for publication.
