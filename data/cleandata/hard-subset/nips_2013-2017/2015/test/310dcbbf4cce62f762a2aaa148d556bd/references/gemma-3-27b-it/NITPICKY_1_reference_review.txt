This paper addresses the important problem of contextual bandits with budget constraints, a realistic scenario often overlooked in standard bandit formulations. The authors aim to develop algorithms with logarithmic regret, a challenging goal given the added complexity of the budget constraint. The paper builds upon existing work in both constrained bandits and contextual bandits, attempting to bridge the gap and provide a practical, efficient solution. While the core idea of approximating the oracle with linear programming and the resulting Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm are promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws – both in technical presentation and the rigor of the analysis – that, cumulatively, raise concerns about its readiness for NIPS publication.

**Summary of Ideas and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the limitations of existing contextual bandit algorithms when budget constraints are present. It positions itself relative to work on non-contextual budgeted bandits (e.g., [11-16]) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], highlighting the applicability challenges of the former and the complexity of the latter. The key contribution is the ALP algorithm, framed as an approximation to the optimal policy and demonstrated to achieve near-optimal performance with known statistics, paving the way for a UCB-ALP algorithm in the unknown statistics setting. The reliance on LP relaxation and adaptive budget adjustments is a reasonable approach, drawing parallels to techniques used in other constrained optimization problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevance:** The problem is relevant and practical, with clear applications in areas like clinical trials and crowdsourcing.
*   **Conceptual Contribution:** The idea of approximating the constrained oracle with LP and adapting the constraint over time is a potentially valuable contribution.
*   **Algorithm Design:** The UCB-ALP algorithm appears to be a sensible combination of established techniques (UCB and LP relaxation).
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper attempts to address both known and unknown statistics scenarios, and considers heterogeneous costs (although relegated to the supplementary material).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Rigor & Presentation:** The presentation is often dense and lacks sufficient detail. Lemmas and theorems are stated without full proofs in the main text, with references to the supplementary material. This makes it difficult to verify the correctness of the analysis. Crucially, many steps in the proofs – particularly those involving concentration inequalities – are hand-waved or lack complete justification.
*   **Boundary Cases:** The "boundary cases" where the regret becomes O(√T) are glossed over. The paper does not adequately define or characterize these cases, leaving the reader unsure of their practical significance or how to avoid them. The dependence on J (number of contexts) in the boundary case regret also seems suboptimal.
*   **Notations & Definitions:** There are inconsistencies in notation and definitions. For example, the definition of 'u*' changes throughout the paper. Similarly, the conditions for the boundary cases are not consistently applied.
*   **Minor Errors:** I identified several minor mathematical inconsistencies and typos (e.g., missing summation limits, incorrect index ranges). While individually small, these errors erode confidence in the overall correctness of the work.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, the discussion is often superficial. For instance, the connection to other adaptive optimization techniques (beyond LP) is not explored. The recent papers [21, 22] extending [20] to dynamic contexts are mentioned but not adequately compared. The authors claim their work achieves logarithmic regret where these papers only achieve sqrt(T) regret, but don't properly address the different problem setting of [21, 22] (infinite contexts).
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** There is *no* empirical evaluation of the algorithms. The paper relies entirely on theoretical analysis. This is a significant weakness, as it makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of the proposed algorithms.
* **Clarity of Assumptions**: The assumption of fixed costs is stated but the implications for generalization aren’t thoroughly discussed.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Presents a novel algorithmic approach with theoretical guarantees.
*   Could potentially lead to practical algorithms for constrained contextual bandits.

**Con:**

*   Technical presentation lacks rigor and detail.
*   Analysis contains gaps and relies on hand-waving arguments.
*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Insufficient discussion of boundary cases.
*   Minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially valuable ideas presented in this paper, I recommend **rejection**. The multitude of technical flaws, coupled with the lack of empirical evaluation, prevent me from confidently endorsing this work for NIPS publication. The paper requires significant revisions to address the identified weaknesses before it can be considered a robust and reliable contribution to the field. Specifically, the authors need to:

1.  Provide complete proofs for all lemmas and theorems in the main text or a well-structured supplementary material.
2.  Clearly define and characterize the boundary cases.
3.  Correct all identified errors and inconsistencies.
4.  Include a thorough empirical evaluation of the algorithms.
5.  Provide a more comprehensive comparison to related work.
6.  Improve the overall clarity and readability of the presentation.



I acknowledge the effort put into this work, but the current state of the manuscript does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS.
