- Studies contextual bandits with a hard budget and finite horizon (B/T = ρ), proposing an adaptive LP (ALP) that relaxes the hard constraint to average budget using bτ/τ.
- ALP (known statistics, unit costs) achieves O(1) regret for non-boundary ρ and O(√T) regret on boundary cases.
- UCB-ALP (unknown rewards) attains O(JK log T) regret plus O(√T) boundary term; extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs are sketched.
- Key technical insight: under ALP, budget consumption is like sampling without replacement, yielding exact E[bτ], Var[bτ], and sharp concentration via hypergeometric/Hoeffding bounds.
- Regret decomposition into action-ranking and context-ranking errors is adapted to the adaptive-budget setting and bounded via UCB concentration.
- Main claims are supported by thorough analysis; unknown-context and heterogeneous-cost results are sketched with full details deferred to the supplement.
- Theoretical contributions are substantial but empirical validation is minimal; experiments illustrating near-boundary behavior would strengthen the paper.
- Paper is well organized and algorithms are clear; minor notation/typos and tie-handling assumptions should be clarified for reproducibility.
- Adaptive LP approach and achieving logarithmic regret against the true oracle in this constrained contextual setting appear novel relative to BwK/RCB work.
- Results are significant and practical: computationally lightweight (J-variable LP, JK UCBs) and relevant to budgeted applications.
- Pros: clear conceptual contribution, strong theoretical guarantees, computational efficiency, good literature comparison.
- Cons: restrictive assumptions (finite J,K, iid contexts, unit costs, unique optima), boundary/near-boundary issues, limited experiments, heterogeneous-cost learning uses ε-first rather than adaptive UCB-ALP.
- Suggestions: provide per-round complexity, clarify tie-breaking, quantify near-boundary dependence on δ, add a formal theorem for unknown contexts, include experiments vs BwK/RCB baselines, and discuss lower bounds.
- Verdict: Strong theoretical contribution with recommendation to accept after addressing clarity and experimental suggestions.
