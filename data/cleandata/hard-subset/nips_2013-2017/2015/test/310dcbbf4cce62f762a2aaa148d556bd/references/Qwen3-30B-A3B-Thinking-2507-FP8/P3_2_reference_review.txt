

# Peer Review: "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, a critical extension of contextual bandits where the agent faces both a budget constraint (total cost) and time horizon constraint. The authors propose a novel approach using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) to approximate the optimal oracle policy, and extend it to UCB-ALP for unknown reward settings. This work builds on prior contextual bandit literature [1-7] and budget-constrained bandits [11-16], while addressing a significant gap in the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17] which achieved only O(√T) regret. The paper claims to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits.

## Quality

The paper presents technically sound theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs. The key insight of using LP to approximate the DP oracle is novel and well-executed. The authors carefully analyze the evolution of the remaining budget using hypergeometric distribution properties (Lemma 2), which is a significant technical contribution. The regret bounds for ALP (Theorem 1) and UCB-ALP (Theorem 2) are well-supported, with proofs in the supplementary material.

However, the paper has notable limitations:
- The unit-cost assumption (Section 3) is quite restrictive for real-world applications where costs vary significantly.
- The boundary cases (where ρ = qj) still suffer from O(√T) regret, which the authors acknowledge as an open problem without suggesting a clear path forward.
- The comparison with recent work [21,22] is inadequate. The authors note these papers achieve O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts, but don't clearly explain how their approach differs in terms of theoretical guarantees or practical implications.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is precise, the technical contributions are well-motivated, and the key insights are clearly articulated. The use of lemmas and theorems to build up the main results is effective. The distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases is well-explained, though the boundary cases could be made more intuitive for practitioners.

The paper could benefit from:
- A more intuitive explanation of why the LP approximation works well beyond the technical proofs.
- A clearer discussion of how the boundary cases manifest in practical applications.
- More concrete examples illustrating the budget constraint's impact on decision-making.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by establishing a framework for achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. The key innovation—using LP to approximate the DP oracle—represents a novel approach to handling the exploration-exploitation tradeoff under budget constraints. This is particularly valuable as it avoids the computational complexity of DP while maintaining near-optimal performance.

The paper's main limitation in originality is its inadequate comparison with [21,22], which also addresses dynamic context bandits with budget constraints. While the authors acknowledge these works, they don't sufficiently differentiate their approach. The paper claims to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret, but this isn't clearly supported when considering [21,22] which also claims to handle dynamic contexts.

## Significance

The constrained contextual bandit problem is highly relevant to real-world applications like clinical trials and online recommendation systems with budget constraints. The paper's contribution of achieving logarithmic regret (vs. O(√T) in prior work) represents a substantial improvement in theoretical guarantees for these applications. The UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient and practical to implement, making it suitable for real-world deployment.

The paper also provides a valuable framework for approaching constrained bandit problems, which could be extended to other settings with multiple budget constraints or more complex resource limitations. The authors successfully demonstrate how to decouple the complex interactions between information acquisition and decision making through adaptive linear relaxation.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- First to establish logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under reasonable assumptions
- Novel insight of using LP to approximate the DP oracle, avoiding computational complexity
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-supported regret bounds
- Clear and well-structured presentation
- Practical algorithm design with good computational efficiency

**Weaknesses:**
- Inadequate comparison with recent works [21,22] that also address dynamic contextual bandits with budget constraints
- Boundary cases still suffer from O(√T) regret without clear resolution
- Unit-cost assumption limits applicability to many real-world scenarios
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a practically important problem, and the results are well-supported by rigorous analysis. The main weakness is the inadequate comparison with recent work [21,22], which should be addressed in the revision. The paper would also benefit from more discussion of how to handle boundary cases and a clearer explanation of how their approach differs from [21,22].

The paper advances the state of the art in contextual bandits with budget constraints, providing a theoretically sound and computationally efficient solution that addresses a significant gap in the literature. The theoretical insights about decoupling information acquisition from decision making could also influence future work on constrained bandit problems.

This work would be a valuable addition to NIPS, providing a strong theoretical foundation for constrained contextual bandits while offering practical algorithmic insights that could benefit researchers and practitioners in online learning and recommendation systems.
