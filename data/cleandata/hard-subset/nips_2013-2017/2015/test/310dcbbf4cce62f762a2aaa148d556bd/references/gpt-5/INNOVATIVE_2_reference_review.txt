Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies contextual bandits with both a hard budget and a time horizon, focusing on the practically important regime B,T→∞ with fixed ratio ρ=B/T. The core idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle (intractable due to the budget/time coupling) with a linear program that enforces only an average budget. The authors then propose an adaptive LP (ALP) policy that plugs in the instantaneous average remaining budget bτ/τ, proving that with known statistics ALP attains O(1) regret for all non-boundary instances (and O(√T) at measure-zero boundaries where ρ hits a cumulative context probability). Building on this, they combine ALP with UCB to learn action rankings (UCB-ALP), achieving instance-dependent RUCB-ALP(T,B)=O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T+JK log T) in boundary cases. They also discuss how to learn unknown context distributions (via an empirical distribution with a truncation argument) and extend to heterogeneous costs (ALP over all context-action pairs with a one-per-context constraint; for unknown rewards, an ε-first + ALP approach).

This work connects to Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB): RCB [17] gives O(√T) regret with a finite policy class and a heavy algorithm (Mixture Elimination), while BwK [12] and related works [13–16,20–22] typically yield O(√T) regret or treat static contexts/finite policies. The paper’s distinctive angle is that in finite discrete contextual settings with random context arrivals and a single hard budget, an adaptive LP relaxation suffices to achieve logarithmic regret when learning is needed, and constant regret when parameters are known.

Strengths:
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The adaptive-LP idea is simple yet powerful. Showing that ALP yields O(1) regret (non-boundary) against the oracle in a hard-budget contextual setting is a notable conceptual step. The UCB-ALP decoupling—use UCB only to get correct orderings while ALP handles budget adaptivity—is elegant.
- Technical contributions: The hypergeometric “sampling without replacement” perspective on the remaining budget bτ and the resulting concentration bounds are insightful and enable clean regret decompositions. The regret analysis for UCB-ALP carefully separates action-ranking vs. context-ranking errors and leverages the ALP threshold structure.
- Significance: Hard budget constraints are ubiquitous (clinical trials, crowdsourcing, wireless rate selection). The algorithms are computationally lightweight (especially in unit-cost) and likely to be adopted. Achieving O(log T) regret in this constrained contextual setting is impactful relative to prior O(√T) results.
- Scope and extensions: The paper treats known stats, unknown rewards, unknown context distribution (via truncated EALP), and heterogeneous costs, outlining a coherent path from simple to more general settings.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Assumptions/scope: Results hinge on finite discrete contexts/actions, i.i.d. contexts, fixed costs (initially), uniqueness of best actions, and non-boundary ρ. While “boundary” is measure zero, practical systems may operate near boundaries; the sensitivity analysis (via δ in the bounds) is helpful but additional discussion or smoothing techniques would be useful.
- Unknown context distribution: The rigorous treatment relies on a truncation (stop updating π̂ after some time) to regain concentration. This is reasonable but somewhat artificial; can the concentration be established without truncation (e.g., via martingale techniques) or with an online convex optimization–style estimator?
- Heterogeneous costs with unknown rewards: The proposed ε-first approach is conservative and can be data-inefficient. Is a UCB-style online variant feasible that maintains confidence sets on uj,k/cj,k and uses ALP with optimistic estimates?
- Relation to primal-dual BwK: While [20–22] are cited, a sharper comparison to primal-dual methods (e.g., dual variable updates vs. ALP with bτ/τ) would strengthen positioning, including why those approaches yield O(√T) under random contexts while ALP achieves O(log T).
- Clarity/notation: The write-up is generally clear, but there are a few typos/garbled equations in the provided text (e.g., bounds in Theorem 1 include odd “−e−2δ2” fragments). Please proofread and ensure finite-time constants are explicit.
- Empirical evaluation: The paper mentions simulations but they are not visible in the provided text. Even minimal experiments would help illustrate finite-time behavior, sensitivity near boundaries, and computational overhead (especially for heterogeneous costs where per-round LPs are larger).

Quality: The main claims are supported by rigorous analysis with plausible proof techniques; the key reductions and concentration arguments are sound. Some extensions (unknown context distribution, heterogeneous costs with unknown rewards) are sketched with proofs in the supplement; ensuring those are complete and polished is important.

Clarity: Generally well-organized and readable. Suggestions: explicitly separate regret versus the oracle U∗ vs. the LP upper bound Û in the main text; summarize constant factors in the O(·) terms; and add a brief discussion on computational complexity per round (unit-cost vs. heterogeneous-cost cases).

Originality: High. Achieving O(1)/O(log T) regret via adaptive LP in hard-budget contextual bandits with random contexts appears new and nontrivial; the budget-concentration insight is particularly nice.

Significance: High for the constrained bandits community; likely to influence algorithm design beyond the considered setting.

Questions for the authors:
- Can the boundary-case O(√T) regret be improved, or is there an instance-dependent lower bound showing √T is unavoidable at ρ=qj?
- Could KL-UCB or other sharper confidence bounds reduce constants in UCB-ALP, and does the analysis extend?
- How does ALP/UCB-ALP behave under mildly non-i.i.d. contexts (e.g., mixing Markov), given [8]-style results for Markovian arrivals?

Pros:
- Conceptually novel adaptive LP idea with strong guarantees.
- Simple, computationally efficient algorithms.
- First O(log T) regret in this constrained contextual setting (to my knowledge).
- Careful and convincing theoretical analysis; useful insights (budget concentration).

Cons:
- Strong assumptions; boundary-case behavior suboptimal.
- Truncation trick for unknown context distribution.
- ε-first for heterogeneous costs with unknown rewards may be improvable.
- Limited empirical evidence in the provided text; some notation glitches.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper introduces a compelling and novel approach with solid theory and practical relevance. While some extensions could be strengthened, the main contribution is substantial and likely to spur follow-up work.
