Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget and a finite horizon, under i.i.d. contexts and per-action costs. The key idea is to approximate the dynamic programming oracle with an adaptive linear program (ALP) that enforces the budget only in expectation on the remaining horizon, by solving at each round an LP with right-hand side equal to the average remaining budget bτ/τ. For unit-cost and known context distribution, ALP is shown to be near-optimal: O(1) regret in non-boundary instances (ρ ≠ cumulative context masses qj) and O(√T) at boundary instances (ρ = qj). Building on the observation that ALP depends only on the ordering of expected rewards within contexts, the paper proposes UCB-ALP, which couples UCB estimation with ALP decisions. UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. Extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates and a truncated estimator) and to heterogeneous costs (via a larger LP and an ε-first variant) are sketched. The analysis leverages a neat mapping of the budget evolution under ALP to sampling without replacement, yielding hypergeometric concentration for the remaining budget.

Relation to prior work
- Bandits with knapsacks and resourceful/contextual bandits [BwK, RCB] typically achieve O(√T) regret under general settings, often with respect to a finite policy class. This paper restricts to finite contexts/actions, known costs, single resource, and i.i.d. contexts, in exchange for improved regret (logarithmic, up to boundary cases) and efficient algorithms.
- Compared to Agrawal & Devanur (concave rewards/convex knapsacks and later contextual extensions), the main difference is the adaptive average budget enforcement and the i.i.d. dynamic context arrivals; prior rates are O(√T).
- The LP relaxation idea is classical; the novelty is the adaptive use bτ/τ and showing its performance gap to the true oracle is O(1) in most instances.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: reducing the knapsack-coupled contextual decision to a simple per-round LP with an adaptively updated RHS is elegant and computationally attractive.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) optimality gap for ALP in non-boundary instances, and O(JK log T) regret for UCB-ALP, which appears to be the first logarithmic regret result in constrained contextual bandits under these assumptions.
- Technical novelty: the “sampling without replacement” view of budget dynamics under ALP and the ensuing hypergeometric concentration are insightful and enable clean regret decomposition.
- Practical appeal: ALP requires only reward rankings and is robust to small estimation errors; UCB-ALP is simple and efficient.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope limitations: the main results rely on finite discrete contexts and actions, i.i.d. contexts, known costs, and a single budget. While justified, it narrows applicability relative to general BwK/RCB settings.
- Boundary instances: these yield O(√T) regret. While described as measure-zero, in practice near-boundary ρ can occur. Is there any adaptive smoothing (e.g., randomized tie-breaking or dithering) to improve scaling near boundaries or at least to bound constants?
- Oracle benchmark and regret accounting: proofs for UCB-ALP compare to the LP upper bound Û(T,B). The paper should make explicit that regret to the real oracle U∗ adds only the ALP-vs-oracle gap (O(1) or O(√T)), thereby preserving the stated orders.
- Concentration under UCB-ALP: Lemma 2 is proved for ALP; under UCB-ALP the consumption probability deviates due to ranking errors. The text claims a similar concentration still holds; a precise statement and bound (with dependence on JK and log T) would strengthen the argument.
- Unknown context distribution: the truncated EALP analysis is only sketched. Please specify the stopping time, explicit bounds on the estimation error, and how it affects the budget concentration and regret. Does UCB-EALP retain O(JK log T) with constant overhead, or are additional terms needed?
- Heterogeneous costs: the main text promises O(1)/O(√T) for ALP and proposes ε-first for unknown rewards, but does not state explicit regret bounds for ε-first ALP or how ε is tuned. Given the popularity of primal-dual BwK approaches, an empirical and theoretical comparison would be valuable.
- Experimental validation: The manuscript refers to simulations, but no results are included in the provided text. Empirical comparisons against BwK (primal-dual), RCB (mixture elimination), and recent contextual-constrained methods would bolster significance, especially near boundary cases and under model misspecification (e.g., mild nonstationarity or unknown costs).

Quality
- Generally solid and careful theoretical development for the core (unit-cost, known π) setting. Some important arguments are deferred to the supplement; the main text could be clearer about assumptions (e.g., uniqueness and strict ordering are for exposition only) and explicitly connect regret to U∗, not just Û.
- Notation has minor glitches (e.g., Theorem 1 display lines are garbled; ensure constants and exponents are correct).

Clarity
- Well organized and readable, with clear high-level ideas and algorithm descriptions. Adding a short worked example (J=3) illustrating ALP’s threshold and the bτ/τ dynamics would help. Explicitly state computational complexity per round and how to implement the LP via sorting rather than solving a generic LP.

Originality
- Within the restricted setting, achieving logarithmic regret with an adaptive LP is a notable contribution. The budget-as-hypergeometric analysis is a nice touch. The combination with UCB is natural but well executed.

Significance
- For applications with discrete contexts/actions and a single resource, the results improve the state of the art and may influence practical designs. The insights (adaptive average-budget control, decoupling estimation from resource control) could inspire advances in broader constrained contextual bandit frameworks.

Pros for acceptance
- First logarithmic regret guarantees in constrained contextual bandits under stated assumptions.
- Elegant, efficient algorithm with insightful analysis.
- Potential to influence how resource constraints are handled in contextual bandits.

Cons for acceptance
- Narrow assumptions; boundary-case limitation.
- Several technical details deferred; clarity on UCB-ALP concentration and unknown-π setting needed.
- Missing or underspecified empirical validation.

Suggestions
- Clarify regret definition vs oracle throughout and aggregate bounds cleanly.
- Provide a precise lemma for remaining budget concentration under UCB-ALP.
- Detail the truncated EALP procedure and its regret bound; include experiments comparing ALP/UCB-ALP against BwK/RCB baselines.
- Discuss how to handle ties and near-boundary ρ, and report sensitivity.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is clear and valuable within its scope; addressing the clarity points and adding empirical evidence would strengthen the paper. Confidence: High.
