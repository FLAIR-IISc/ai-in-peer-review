Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget and a finite horizon, where each action consumes a fixed cost and the process ends when either time or budget is exhausted. The main idea is to approximate the (intractable) dynamic-programming oracle with a linear program that enforces an average budget constraint. Building on this, the authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that, at each round, solves the LP with the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. For known rewards and unit costs, ALP achieves O(1) regret relative to the optimal oracle, except on measure-zero boundary cases (when the budget ratio equals a cumulative context probability), where regret is O(√T). For unknown rewards and known context distribution, ALP is combined with UCB to form UCB-ALP, which achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. The analysis leverages a neat mapping of the remaining budget process to sampling without replacement (hypergeometric concentration). The approach is extended (sketched) to unknown context distributions via empirical estimates (with a truncation technique for analysis), and to heterogeneous costs via a generalized LP and an ε-first variant for learning.

This work connects to: Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and subsequent contextual/resourceful bandit formulations [Badanidiyuru et al., Slivkins; Agrawal and Devanur], which typically obtain O(√T) regret under more general settings (concave rewards/convex constraints, finite policy sets, possibly infinite contexts). The paper differs by assuming finite contexts/actions and fixed costs, and by exploiting the adaptive average-budget LP to obtain logarithmic regret. It also builds on approximate linear programming for MDPs and classical UCB analysis.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: reducing a hard constrained DP to an adaptive LP that tracks average remaining budget is simple and compelling.
- Strong guarantees: O(1) regret with known statistics (non-boundary) and O(JK log T) with unknown rewards, a substantial improvement over O(√T) in prior general frameworks like RCB/BwK under the paper’s assumptions.
- Analytical novelty: mapping the budget evolution under ALP/UCB-ALP to a hypergeometric process yields clean concentration and enables a crisp regret decomposition between action-ranking and context-threshold errors.
- Practical aspects: in unit-cost settings, ALP reduces to a threshold/randomization rule depending only on the ordering of u∗j, making the approach computationally lightweight.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope of assumptions: finite discrete contexts/actions, i.i.d. contexts with known distribution for the main UCB-ALP result, fixed costs, uniqueness and strict ordering of best actions across contexts. While some relaxations are discussed, the core logarithmic result relies on these simplifying assumptions.
- Boundary cases: the O(√T) regret at ρ = qj highlights a sensitivity to exact budget ratios aligning with cumulative context probabilities; in practice, discretization or small estimation errors may push systems near such boundaries.
- Empirical validation: the paper mentions simulations but none are shown here; empirical comparisons to RCB/BwK baselines and to naive policies would help assess robustness and constants.
- Heterogeneous costs: the known-statistics result is promising, but for unknown statistics the ε-first approach is only sketched; it is unclear whether one can retain O(log T) regret without stronger assumptions or whether the exploration budget can be tuned adaptively.
- Known vs unknown context distribution: the analysis for UCB-EALP with unknown π uses a truncation argument; explicit finite-time bounds and their dependence on truncation length would strengthen the claim.

Quality
- The core results appear technically sound. The ALP-to-hypergeometric correspondence is elegant; the UCB-ALP decomposition into action and context ranking errors is standard but well adapted to the threshold structure.
- Some proofs and important constants are deferred to the supplement; providing more intuition about why boundary cases induce √T would be valuable.
- A discussion on computational complexity for the heterogeneous-cost LP per round would be useful.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The LP formulations and threshold structure are clear. Assumptions (unit cost, unique best actions, strict ordering u∗1>…>u∗J) are stated, though a short subsection on how ties and near-ties are handled would improve clarity.
- The relation to prior work is fair, but a more explicit comparison to CBwK/RCB bounds and assumptions would help a broader NeurIPS audience.

Originality
- The adaptive average-budget LP heuristic is known in spirit, but the way it is leveraged to obtain O(1)/O(log T) regret in constrained contextual bandits with random contexts appears novel. The hypergeometric analysis and the emphasis on order-only requirements are nicely original.

Significance
- Under its assumptions, achieving logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits is a meaningful step that may influence subsequent algorithms in budgeted decision-making. While the general BwK/RCB settings remain harder, the paper’s insights are likely to inform practical systems with finite context sets and fixed costs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide experiments comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to Mixture Elimination (RCB), BwK algorithms, and non-adaptive LP baselines under varying ρ, J, K, and cost heterogeneity. Include boundary and near-boundary regimes.
- Discuss tie-handling and stability when u∗j are close; quantify how small gaps affect the O(log T) term constants.
- For unknown π, present explicit regret bounds for UCB-EALP (with truncation length and its tuning), and a brief discussion on misspecified π or nonstationary contexts.
- For heterogeneous costs, clarify per-round LP complexity and whether a UCB-ALP-style algorithm (rather than ε-first) is possible while retaining logarithmic regret.
- Comment on unknown horizons (doubling trick) and how boundary behavior changes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong theoretical advance: first logarithmic regret under budget constraints in contextual bandits with random contexts and finite sets.
- Clean, generalizable analytical ideas (adaptive LP, hypergeometric concentration).
- Potentially impactful for practical budgeted personalization problems (clinical trials, recommendation).

Arguments against acceptance
- Narrower scope due to simplifying assumptions; boundary behavior remains suboptimal.
- Limited empirical evidence presented; heterogeneous-cost and unknown-π results are less developed.
- Some dependence on strict orderings and known distributions may limit applicability.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a clear, technically solid contribution with novel analysis and improved regret guarantees in an important constrained bandit setting. With added empirical evaluation and clarification of extensions, it would be a strong NeurIPS contribution.
