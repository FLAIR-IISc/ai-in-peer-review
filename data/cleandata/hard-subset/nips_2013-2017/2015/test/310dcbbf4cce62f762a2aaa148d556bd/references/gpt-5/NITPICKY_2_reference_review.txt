Summary
The paper studies constrained contextual bandits with a finite set of contexts and actions under a hard budget constraint and a fixed time horizon. The core idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle via a linear program that relaxes the hard budget to an average budget constraint. For unit-cost systems with known statistics, the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy adjusts the LP to the current average remaining budget bτ/τ and is shown to be within O(1) of an LP upper bound (and thus of the oracle) except on boundary parameter values (where ρ equals a cumulative context probability), in which case the gap is O(√T). For unknown expected rewards, ALP is combined with UCB to form UCB-ALP, which achieves O(JK log T) regret off the boundary and O(√T + JK log T) on the boundary. The paper sketches extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical frequencies and a truncated analysis) and heterogeneous costs (via an ALP that considers all context–action pairs and an ε-first variant for learning).

Relation to prior work
This work sits between “Bandits with Budgets/Knapsacks” (BwK) and contextual bandits. Prior RCB/BwK algorithms typically achieve Õ(√T) regret in general settings or with infinite policy classes (e.g., Mixture Elimination, Agrawal & Devanur). For finite discrete contexts/actions and known context distribution, the paper claims to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret under hard budget constraints by exploiting an adaptive LP relaxation. The paper also discusses differences from [20–22] (concave/convex knapsacks), which attain Õ(√T) in settings closer to its own when contexts are dynamic.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and computationally light policy (ALP) with clean intuition: track the average remaining budget and use an LP thresholding rule.
- Appealing instance-dependent guarantees: O(1) gap to an LP upper bound for known stats, and O(JK log T) for learning, except on a well-identified boundary set.
- The “only ordering needed” property is valuable and enables robust coupling with UCB.
- The analysis path (concentration of bτ/τ; decoupling action- and context-level ranking errors) could be reusable in broader constrained bandit settings.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and presentation:
- The key claim that under ALP the remaining budget bτ has a hypergeometric distribution is only sketched. The statement “the system consumes one unit with probability b/τ” is true marginally (averaged over contexts), but consumption also depends on the realized context. A rigorous coupling argument is needed to justify the exact hypergeometric law (and hence the stated variance formula and Hoeffding-style tails). As written, this step is pivotal and under-explained.
- The discussion of UCRL [8] claiming logarithmic regret for Markovian contexts is inaccurate; standard UCRL-type bounds are Õ(√T). This overclaim undermines parts of the positioning.
- Theorem 1 statements contain multiple typographical issues that obscure meaning: e.g., terms like “≤ u∗1−u∗J −e−2δ2” are dimensionally odd; Θ(o) notation is unclear; piecewise cases appear garbled. Equation (4) omits the “0” case for pj (j > j̃(ρ)+1). Algorithm 1 has typos (“û∗j,k∗j (t)(t)”).
- Lemma 3 uses a nonstandard UCB exploration coefficient; the constants in the probability bound should be rederived and clearly stated (especially since they drive the O(JK log T) term).
- Many crucial arguments are deferred to the supplement (e.g., full proofs of Theorems 1–2; unknown context distribution analysis), and the main text gives only high-level sketches. Some steps (e.g., bounding R(c) via event partition Erank,s) need more transparency about dependencies introduced by random contexts and adaptive probabilities.

Modeling and scope:
- Strong assumptions: finite discrete contexts/actions; i.i.d. contexts; uniqueness of the best action in each context and strict ordering of u∗j across contexts; known context distribution in the main results. Ties and near-ties, which are common in practice, are not handled in the main text.
- Boundary cases: the O(√T) term can dominate for “near-boundary” ρ values in finite samples; the paper does not propose a robustification (e.g., margin-aware smoothing) or provide finite-sample guidance when ρ is close to qj.
- Unknown context distribution: the use of a truncated empirical estimate is somewhat ad hoc; the corresponding regret guarantees are not stated explicitly in the main text and could degrade performance in practice versus continuously updated estimates.
- Heterogeneous costs: the ε-first approach is introduced without a clear characterization of the regret–ε trade-off or a comparison to UCB-ALP-style adaptive learning; the LP in this setting may be nontrivial per-round, and complexity is not discussed.

Experimental validation:
- There are no experiments. Given the practical motivation, empirical results comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to BwK/RCB baselines and to more naive policies (e.g., fixed-threshold, non-adaptive LP) would significantly strengthen the paper and illuminate constants and boundary behavior.

Clarity and formatting:
- Numerous typographical and notation issues (missing cases in equations, undefined or confusing symbols, incomplete references lacking years/pages). These detract from readability and reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a rigorous, self-contained proof that bτ under ALP is exactly hypergeometric (or otherwise establish the stated mean/variance/tails) despite context-dependent action probabilities?
- How sensitive are the regret and empirical performance to ρ near qj? Can you design a margin-aware ALP variant that avoids √T blow-up in a band around the boundary?
- For unknown context distributions, can you analyze a fully online estimator (without truncation) using martingale concentration under adaptivity?
- In the heterogeneous-cost setting, can UCB-ALP be adapted directly rather than ε-first? What is the per-round LP complexity and any practical speedups?

Pros
- Simple, implementable idea with strong theoretical guarantees under clear assumptions.
- Potentially impactful insight: adaptive LP relaxation decouples learning and constrained control.

Cons
- Several technical statements are under-justified or misstated in the main text.
- Many presentation/notation errors; incomplete references.
- No empirical evaluation; boundary behavior not robustified.
- Limited scope (finite discrete contexts; strong uniqueness/ordering assumptions).

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea appears promising and may merit publication after substantial revision, but the accumulation of technical presentation issues, a potentially shaky key probabilistic claim, lack of experiments, and several clarity/rigor gaps outweigh the contributions in the current form. I encourage the authors to (i) tighten and fully detail the proofs (especially the budget concentration result), (ii) correct inaccuracies and typos, (iii) add empirical evidence, and (iv) discuss robustness to ties/near-boundary regimes and to unknown context distributions without truncation.
