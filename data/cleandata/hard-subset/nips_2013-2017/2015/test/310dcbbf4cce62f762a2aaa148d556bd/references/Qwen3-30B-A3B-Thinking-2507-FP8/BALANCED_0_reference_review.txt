

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, where the agent faces both budget and time constraints while making decisions based on observable contexts. The authors extend the literature on constrained bandits (which previously focused on non-contextual settings with O(√T) regret) to the contextual setting, achieving O(log T) regret under certain conditions. They relate their work to Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17], noting that their approach with simplified assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint) enables logarithmic regret, unlike RCB's O(√T) bound. The paper also distinguishes itself from recent work [20-22] that achieves O(√T) regret in dynamic context settings, arguing that their adaptive budget constraint approach enables logarithmic regret.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant technical contribution by developing the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which approximates the oracle solution with a linear programming relaxation. This approach is novel in the constrained contextual bandit setting and provides a clean theoretical framework for regret analysis.

2. **Rigorous Analysis**: The regret analysis is thorough and well-executed. The authors carefully handle boundary cases where the regret becomes O(√T) instead of O(log T), and provide a detailed characterization of when these cases occur. The concentration properties of the remaining budget under ALP (Lemma 2) are particularly elegant.

3. **Practical Algorithm Design**: The UCB-ALP algorithm combines ALP with UCB to handle unknown statistics, resulting in a computationally efficient solution that achieves logarithmic regret in non-boundary cases. The paper provides a clear explanation of how the ALP approximation tolerates estimation errors, which is crucial for the algorithm's success.

4. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key insights are well-explained with appropriate technical depth. The sketches of proofs provide sufficient intuition without overwhelming the reader.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Contribution**: The main results are restricted to unit-cost systems (Section 3-5), and while the paper mentions generalizations to heterogeneous costs (Section 6), the details are relegated to supplementary material. This significantly weakens the significance of the contribution, as many practical applications (e.g., clinical trials with varying treatment costs) require handling heterogeneous costs.

2. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper provides no empirical results to validate the theoretical findings. Given the practical importance of the problem, some numerical experiments demonstrating the performance of UCB-ALP compared to baseline algorithms would greatly strengthen the paper.

3. **Boundary Cases Not Fully Addressed**: While the paper correctly identifies boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T), it doesn't provide any insights into how to improve performance in these cases. The authors note this is an open problem, but the paper could have at least proposed a heuristic approach to handle these cases.

4. **Comparison with [20-22] Could Be More Detailed**: The paper mentions that [20-22] achieve O(√T) regret in dynamic contexts, but doesn't fully explain why their approach achieves logarithmic regret. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution claim.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the constrained bandit literature by demonstrating how to achieve logarithmic regret in the contextual setting. The ALP approach is elegant and provides important insights into the structure of constrained contextual bandits. The main weakness is the limited scope (unit-cost systems) and lack of empirical validation.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with significant practical implications. While the unit-cost restriction is a limitation, the theoretical insights are substantial and could potentially be extended to more general settings. The authors' approach of using ALP to approximate the oracle solution is particularly clever and could inspire future work on constrained bandit problems.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed, and the weaknesses can be addressed in a revision. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Add a brief discussion of how their approach could be extended to heterogeneous costs (even if details remain in supplementary material).
2. Include at least a small set of numerical experiments to validate the theoretical results.
3. Provide a more detailed comparison with [20-22] to clarify why their approach achieves logarithmic regret where others achieve O(√T).
4. Discuss potential strategies for handling boundary cases, even if only as a future work direction.

With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field and be suitable for NeurIPS.
