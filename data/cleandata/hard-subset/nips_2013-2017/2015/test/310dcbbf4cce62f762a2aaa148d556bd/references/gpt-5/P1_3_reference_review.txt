Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with both a hard budget B and a time horizon T, where pulling an action consumes cost and the process terminates upon exhausting budget or time. The main novelty is an adaptive linear programming (ALP) relaxation of the dynamic programming oracle that uses the average remaining budget bτ/τ as a dynamic constraint to decide, per context, whether to act or skip. With known statistics and unit costs, ALP is shown to achieve O(1) regret (constant gap to the oracle) for all but a measure-zero set of “boundary” parameters (ρ=B/T equal to cumulative context probabilities), where regret is O(√T). Building on the observation that ALP decisions only need the correct ranking of context-wise best expected rewards, the authors combine ALP with UCB to propose UCB-ALP for unknown rewards. They prove RUCB-ALP(T,B)=O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases, where J is number of contexts and K actions. They further sketch extensions to unknown context distributions (empirical estimates with a truncation/coupling analysis) and heterogeneous costs (LP over all context-action pairs and an ε-first exploration).

This work connects to and extends several lines: “bandits with budgets/knapsacks” (BwK) typically achieve O(√T) regret without observable contexts; resourceful contextual bandits (RCB, [17]) obtain O(√T) regret against a finite policy benchmark and high computational cost; and bandits with concave rewards/convex knapsacks ([20] and follow-ups [21,22]) also give O(√T) in our setting, especially with dynamic contexts. The present paper shows how to get logarithmic regret in finite-context constrained settings via an adaptive LP that tracks remaining resources, with analysis leveraging concentration of a hypergeometric process for budget consumption.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling and LP relaxation are standard; the adaptive use of bτ/τ is natural. The proof strategy—mapping ALP’s budget evolution to sampling without replacement, yielding hypergeometric concentration—is elegant and convincing for unit costs. The regret decomposition for UCB-ALP into action-ranking within context and context-threshold errors is reasonable, and the use of standard UCB tail bounds to ensure correct rankings after O(log T) plays is appropriate.
- Rigor/completeness: Many key details are deferred to the supplement; in the main text some statements are terse or contain typos that obscure exact constants. For example, Eq. (4) appears truncated (missing pj=0 for j>j̃+1), and Theorem 1’s bound expression as written seems dimensionally off (a difference of rewards minus an exponential term). The claim that under UCB-ALP the remaining budget still concentrates as in ALP is asserted but not fully formalized in the main text. The extensions (unknown π, heterogeneous costs) are primarily sketched; the unknown-π analysis relies on truncation/coupling, but the precise regret statements are not in the body.
- Empirical validation: The paper mentions simulations, but none are in the main text; no empirical results are summarized. While this is primarily a theory paper, small simulations could illustrate robustness and constants.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a clean narrative: oracle difficulty → LP upper bound → adaptive LP → learning with UCB. The intuition for why ALP needs only rankings and why bτ behaves like sampling without replacement is helpful.
- Suggested improvements:
  - Fix typos and incomplete equations (notably Eq. (4); clarify pj(ρ) for j>j̃(ρ)+1; clean up Theorem 1 bound).
  - State explicitly the assumptions (unique best action per context, strict ordering u1*>…>uJ*, iid contexts) where each theorem applies, and how to handle ties.
  - For UCB-ALP, include a lemma stating the budget consumption concentration under the algorithm, with conditions under which it matches Lemma 2.
  - Summarize at least one empirical figure/table in the main paper or provide concrete qualitative observations.

Originality
- The idea of LP relaxations for resource-constrained bandits/MDPs is known, but the adaptive use of the remaining average budget to drive a simple, implementable policy, and the proof of O(1) regret gap to the DP oracle (off-boundary) are, to my knowledge, new in this contextual, hard-budget setting.
- Achieving O(log T) regret in constrained contextual bandits with random contexts and unknown rewards (under finite, discrete, unit-cost assumptions) appears to be the first such result, improving on O(√T) from RCB and convex-knapsack bandits in comparable settings.
- The insight that ALP only needs reward rankings (enabling tight coupling with UCB) is a nice conceptual contribution.

Significance
- The results are significant for the “bandits with knapsacks” family, showing that in finite-context settings one can close the gap from √T to log T by adapting the resource constraint online. This is likely to influence future algorithm design in constrained contextual bandits and possibly in more general resourceful RL.
- Practicality: ALP/UCB-ALP are computationally simple (small LPs per round) and conceptually easy to implement.
- Limitations tempering significance: strong assumptions (iid finite contexts, known π for main results, unit costs, single resource, unique gaps) and boundary cases where √T persists. The extension to heterogeneous costs and unknown π is less mature (ε-first and truncated empirical updates).

Pros
- Clear, simple algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) (known stats) and O(JK log T) (unknown) regret, beating prior √T bounds under stated assumptions.
- Elegant analysis via hypergeometric concentration and careful decomposition of regret sources.
- Good positioning vs. RCB/BwK/convex knapsack literature; uses an oracle benchmark rather than finite policy sets.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on simplified assumptions; practical settings may have unknown π, multiple resources, non-unit or stochastic costs; those cases are only partially addressed.
- Boundary cases yield √T regret; no lower bound is shown to justify necessity.
- Some presentation issues (typos, incomplete equations) and reliance on supplement for critical details.
- No empirical results presented in the main paper.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide a lower bound showing that √T regret is unavoidable at boundary parameters, or discuss whether smoothing/randomization of ρ could remove the boundary effect?
- How sensitive are the constants to small reward gaps (both across actions within a context and across context-wise best rewards)? Can you state instance-dependent constants in Theorems 1–2?
- Under UCB-ALP, can you formalize the claim that budget consumption per round is still close to bτ/τ and quantify the deviation due to ranking errors?
- For unknown π, can UCB-ALP be run with online π̂ without truncation while preserving the key concentration lemma?

Recommendation
- Overall: accept (clear theoretical advance within a well-motivated constrained contextual bandit setting).
- Confidence: high on novelty and main results, medium on extensions.
