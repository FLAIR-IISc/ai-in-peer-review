Summary and contributions
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard, single budget constraint over a finite horizon. Contexts are i.i.d. from a finite set, actions are finite, rewards are bounded and stochastic, and each action incurs a fixed cost (unit cost in the core development). The objective is to maximize expected total reward subject to budget and time constraints, with B and T growing proportionally (ρ = B/T).

The methodological core is an adaptive linear programming (ALP) relaxation of the dynamic program (oracle). The static LP that replaces the hard budget by an average per-round budget yields an upper bound Tv(ρ) on the oracle value. The proposed ALP uses, in each round, the LP solution with the instantaneous average remaining budget bτ/τ. A key insight is that under ALP, the budget consumption process behaves like sampling without replacement, leading to concentration of bτ around ρτ and enabling tight control of the performance gap relative to the static LP. In unit-cost, known-context-distribution settings with known expected rewards, ALP achieves O(1) regret compared to the oracle (and O(√T) in “boundary” cases where ρ hits a cumulative context mass qj).

Building on this, the UCB-ALP algorithm estimates expected rewards via UCB, plugs UCB estimates into the per-round LP, and thereby attains RUCB-ALP(T,B) = O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases (and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases). The approach extends to unknown context distributions via an empirical estimate (EALP) and to heterogeneous costs (via a generalized LP and an exploration-first variant). The authors position this work against prior RCB/BwK work, which achieves O(√T) regret in more general settings, arguing that their specialized setting admits logarithmic regret with a computationally simple algorithm.

Relation to prior work
- Compared to Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Mixture Elimination; Badanidiyuru et al. 2014) and Bandits with Concave Rewards and Convex Knapsacks (Agrawal & Devanur), this paper restricts to finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, and a single budget, but improves the regret to O(log T) in non-boundary instances while maintaining low per-round complexity. The benchmark is the true oracle (not a finite policy class).
- Prior non-contextual budgeted bandits (BwK, budgeted MAB variants) cannot be directly applied since the decision depends on the context. The novelty here is the adaptive LP relaxation tailored to random contexts and hard budgets.
- The mapping of the budget dynamics under ALP to a “sampling without replacement” process is a technical device that underlies the concentration arguments; this is distinct from earlier BwK analyses.

Strengths
- Clear, principled algorithmic idea: adapt a simple LP relaxation with the instantaneous resource ratio bτ/τ, sidestepping DP complexity while nearly matching the oracle.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) regret for ALP (except boundary ρ), and O(JK log T) for UCB-ALP, which is near-optimal up to instance-dependent constants dictated by gaps.
- Insightful analysis: the coupling to sampling without replacement and concentration of remaining budget are elegant and broadly useful.
- Computational efficiency: in unit-cost settings with known π, ALP reduces to a threshold structure; per-round work is minimal. The approach scales linearly with J,K in the heterogeneous-cost LP.
- Relevance: budget constraints are ubiquitous in applications (clinical trials, crowdsourcing), and the work addresses a noted gap between general theory (O(√T)) and what may be possible in structured, finite settings.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong assumptions: i.i.d. contexts from a known finite support (then partially relaxed), fixed costs, unique best action per context, and strictly ordered u∗j across contexts. The “boundary cases” where ρ = qj are claimed measure-zero but are not rare in discrete-budget/discrete-π practical settings; the O(√T) erosion there is substantial. It would help to give simple safeguards to avoid boundary sensitivity (e.g., slight randomization of ρ or budget reservation).
- Proof reliance on supplementary: Several crucial arguments (e.g., Lemma 2’s exact characterization of bτ under UCB-ALP, the O(1) gap between LP upper bound and oracle, and the decomposition/union bounds for context ranking errors) are deferred. The mapping to hypergeometric behavior is plausible but nontrivial because spending probabilities depend on the realized context; a rigorous justification in the main would strengthen the paper.
- Benchmark clarity: The paper first uses the LP as an upper bound Û(T,B) but then claims ALP’s regret versus the oracle is O(1). It would be valuable to isolate and bound Û − U∗ explicitly (or make clear how the analysis circumvents this by working directly against U∗).
- Unknown π: The EALP/UCB-EALP analysis requires a truncated estimator to regain concentration. The practical algorithm that keeps updating π̂ is not fully analyzed. This gap is important if the claim is that the approach is robust to unknown context distributions.
- Heterogeneous costs: The extension is only sketched; the -First ALP’s exploration budget/time and the resulting regret constants are not specified. Without a UCB analogue (instead of -first), the extension feels less complete.
- Empirical validation: The main paper lacks experiments. Given the simplicity of ALP/UCB-ALP, a small simulation comparing against Mixture Elimination/BwK-style baselines and showing constant-gap and O(log T) curves would increase confidence and illuminate boundary behavior and sensitivity to gaps/π estimation.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you explicitly bound Û(T,B) − U∗(T,B) for unit costs? Is this also O(1) uniformly in T?
2) The “sampling without replacement” claim: under ALP/UCB-ALP, the per-round spending probability equals b/τ only in expectation over Xt. Do you have a martingale/coupling proof that the distribution of bτ matches a hypergeometric (or is concentrated similarly), given the context-dependent spend decisions?
3) Can you propose a simple, parameter-free modification to avoid boundary O(√T) (e.g., budget dithering or softening the threshold) and quantify the resulting regret?
4) For unknown π, is there a way to show concentration for the fully online π̂ without truncation (e.g., via self-normalized martingales under adaptivity)?
5) Heterogeneous costs: can UCB-ALP be extended directly (learning ηj,k and solving the per-round LP with UCBs), and what regret rate would you obtain?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Eq. (4) is missing a case in the piecewise definition; please fix typesetting and define pj(ρ)=0 for j>j̃(ρ)+1.
- Clarify the uniqueness and strict ordering assumptions and how the analysis adapts to ties (beyond “measure-zero”).
- Use consistent notation for time t vs remaining time τ; define all events and variables when introduced.
- Some references are missing years/venues; a quick cleanup would help.

Overall assessment
- Quality: High for theory; proofs mostly deferred. The main technical ideas are sound and interesting, but some key details should be brought into the main text or strengthened.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, with a few notational/typographical issues.
- Originality: Good. The adaptive LP relaxation and the budget-concentration analysis in contextual settings appear novel and compelling.
- Significance: Strong within the constrained contextual bandits niche; the approach is simple and potentially influential.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the oracle vs LP gap, tightening the budget concentration argument, and adding at least a small experimental section. Confidence: High.
