This paper tackles the problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a realistic extension of the standard contextual bandit setting. The authors aim to design algorithms achieving logarithmic regret, a challenging goal given the added constraints. They make several contributions: simplifying the problem setting to finite contexts and fixed costs, proposing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves near-optimal performance when statistics are known, and extending this approach to the unknown statistics case via a UCB-ALP algorithm with logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases).

The paper is well-motivated, clearly presenting the limitations of existing work and highlighting the relevance of the constrained contextual bandit problem to real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The connection to Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) is acknowledged, and the justification for the simplifying assumptions is sound. The technical approach of approximating the oracle with linear programming and then adaptively adjusting the constraint is a reasonable and potentially effective strategy. The rigorous analysis, including the derivation of regret bounds, is a significant strength. The extension to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, although relegated to supplementary material, demonstrates the generality of the approach.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The notation is dense and occasionally obscures the core ideas. While the authors acknowledge the complexity of the analysis, the presentation could be more streamlined. The discussion of boundary cases feels somewhat incomplete; it's not entirely clear *when* these cases are likely to occur in practice or how significant their impact might be. The empirical evaluation is absent. While the authors focus on theoretical guarantees, some simulations demonstrating the algorithm's performance on synthetic or real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.  Furthermore, the comparison to recent related work [21, 22] feels somewhat dismissive, highlighting their √T regret while neglecting potential differences in problem setup or assumptions.  Finally, while the connection to dynamic programming is discussed, the justification for *why* DP is intractable feels a bit superficial. A more detailed explanation of the dimensionality curse and analytical challenges would be helpful.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** Addresses a practically important extension of the contextual bandit problem.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithms and analysis appear technically correct and well-reasoned.
*   **Novelty:** The ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms represent a novel approach to constrained contextual bandits.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous regret bounds.
*   **Generalizability:** The extension to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs demonstrates the generality of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dense Notation:** Makes the paper harder to follow.
*   **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** Missing simulations limit confidence in the practical performance.
*   **Incomplete Discussion of Boundary Cases:** The impact and occurrence of boundary cases are not fully explained.
*   **Dismissive Comparison to Recent Work:** The authors downplay potential contributions of related papers [21, 22].
*   **Superficial Justification of DP Intractability:** A more detailed explanation of the challenges with DP would be beneficial.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a significant and realistic problem.
*   The technical approach is novel and well-motivated.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees.
*   The work advances the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical evaluation weakens the overall impact.
*   The dense notation and somewhat convoluted presentation make the paper challenging to read.
*   The discussion of boundary cases and comparison to related work could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The theoretical results are strong, and the proposed algorithms are novel and promising. However, the lack of empirical validation and the issues with presentation prevent me from giving it a highly enthusiastic recommendation. I would recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically requesting the inclusion of simulations and a more thorough discussion of the boundary cases and related work. The authors should also consider simplifying the notation and improving the overall clarity of the presentation.
