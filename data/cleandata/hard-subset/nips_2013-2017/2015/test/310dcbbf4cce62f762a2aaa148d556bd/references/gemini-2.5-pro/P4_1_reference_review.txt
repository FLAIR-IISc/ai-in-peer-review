### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B). The process terminates when either the time horizon is reached or the budget is exhausted. The objective is to design an algorithm that minimizes regret, defined as the performance gap against an optimal oracle with full knowledge of the system statistics. The authors focus on the asymptotic regime where B and T grow proportionally.

The central challenge in this problem is that the optimal policy is a solution to a dynamic program (DP), which is computationally intractable and difficult to analyze. To overcome this, the paper introduces a novel approximation framework based on linear programming (LP). First, for the case with known statistics, the authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. At each step, ALP solves a simple LP that relaxes the hard budget constraint, using the *remaining* budget and *remaining* time to set an adaptive average budget constraint. The key theoretical result for this setting is that ALP achieves constant O(1) regret, except for a set of "boundary" parameter values where the regret is O(√T). This demonstrates that the LP relaxation is near-optimal.

Building on this insight, the paper tackles the more realistic setting where expected rewards are unknown. The authors propose the UCB-ALP algorithm, which integrates the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method for reward estimation with the ALP decision-making framework. The main contribution of the paper is the theoretical analysis showing that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases. This logarithmic regret bound is a significant improvement over the O(√T) regret achieved in prior work on related problems, such as Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] and bandits with global constraints [21, 22]. The paper also discusses extensions to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs for different actions.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of Results:** The primary strength of this paper is its achievement of a logarithmic regret bound for a constrained contextual bandit problem. This is a substantial theoretical advance, as prior work in similar settings has typically been limited to O(√T) regret. The problem setting is of high practical importance in areas like computational advertising and clinical trials, making this result highly significant.

2.  **Elegant Technical Approach:** The introduction of the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) framework is a novel and elegant method for handling the complexity of the optimal DP. The idea of dynamically adjusting the LP constraint based on the ratio of remaining budget to remaining time is intuitive yet powerful. The theoretical analysis of ALP, which connects the evolution of the remaining budget to the hypergeometric distribution to establish strong concentration bounds, is a key technical contribution that is both clever and sound.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It builds its argument logically, starting from the simplified setting with known statistics to motivate the ALP algorithm, and then extending it to the UCB-ALP algorithm for the unknown-statistics case. This progression makes the core concepts easy to understand. The proof sketches provide valuable intuition into the non-trivial analysis.

4.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient. At each time step, it requires solving a small LP problem which, for the unit-cost case, simplifies to a thresholding rule. This makes the algorithm practical and implementable, in contrast to more complex approaches like the Mixture Elimination algorithm in [17].

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The core O(log T) regret result is derived under a set of simplifying assumptions: a finite set of discrete contexts, fixed and known costs (initially unit costs), and a known context arrival distribution. While extensions are discussed, the strength of the main result is limited by this setting. Much of the related work (e.g., [17, 21]) considers more general settings, such as infinite context spaces. A deeper discussion on the challenges of extending the logarithmic regret bound to these more general settings would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Boundary Cases:** The logarithmic regret guarantee does not hold for "boundary cases," where the average budget ratio `ρ = B/T` aligns perfectly with the cumulative probabilities of the top-ranked contexts. In these cases, the regret degrades to O(√T). While the authors correctly state that these points have zero Lebesgue measure, a system designer might inadvertently choose parameters that fall near such a boundary. The practical implications of being near, but not exactly on, a boundary are not fully explored.

3.  **Treatment of Generalizations:** The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are presented briefly, with most details relegated to the supplementary material. The proposed solution for heterogeneous costs, an ε-First ALP algorithm, involves a pure exploration phase followed by exploitation. This feels less integrated than the UCB-ALP approach and departs from the fully online nature of the main algorithm. Providing more insight into the unique analytical challenges posed by these generalizations within the main body of the paper would have been beneficial.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel algorithm that achieves an O(log T) regret bound for constrained contextual bandits, a significant theoretical improvement over the state-of-the-art O(√T) bounds.
*   The Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) framework is an innovative and insightful technique for approximating the optimal policy in resource-constrained problems.
*   The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is simple and computationally efficient, making it a valuable practical contribution.
*   The paper is very clearly written, well-motivated, and positions itself effectively against related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The strongest results rely on a simplified model (finite contexts, known context distribution), which may limit their direct applicability compared to more general frameworks.
*   The performance guarantee degrades from O(log T) to O(√T) in "boundary cases," an important caveat to the main claim.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on bandit algorithms. The achievement of logarithmic regret in a constrained setting is a novel and important result. The technical approach is elegant, and the resulting algorithm is practical. While the results are derived under some simplifying assumptions, the work provides fundamental insights that could pave the way for future research in more general settings. The strengths of the paper substantially outweigh its weaknesses.
