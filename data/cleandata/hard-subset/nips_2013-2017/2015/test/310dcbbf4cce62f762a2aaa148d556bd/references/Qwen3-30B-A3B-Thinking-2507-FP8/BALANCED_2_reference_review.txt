

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary
This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, a problem where the agent faces both time and budget constraints, extending the standard contextual bandit framework. The authors propose two key algorithms: Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for the known-statistics case and UCB-ALP for the unknown-statistics case. They show that ALP achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases) and UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret (except for boundary cases), which represents a significant improvement over prior work. The paper builds on the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17] but addresses its limitations (high complexity and restricted benchmark). The authors make a novel connection between dynamic programming and linear programming to approximate the oracle algorithm, which enables their theoretical guarantees.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Problem Formulation and Theoretical Contribution**: The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature (constrained contextual bandits with budget constraints) and provides the first rigorous analysis showing logarithmic regret is achievable in this setting. The problem is well-motivated with concrete applications in crowdsourcing and clinical trials.

2. **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides thorough theoretical guarantees with careful handling of boundary cases. The analysis of the ALP algorithm's regret (Theorem 1) is particularly impressive, showing near-optimality with a clean O(1) bound. The connection between the ALP algorithm and hypergeometric distribution (Lemma 2) is a key technical contribution.

3. **Insightful Algorithm Design**: The authors demonstrate how to decouple the complex interactions between information acquisition and decision making by using LP relaxation. The UCB-ALP algorithm cleverly combines the desirable properties of ALP (tolerating estimation errors) with UCB's exploration mechanism.

4. **Clear Organization and Presentation**: The paper is well-structured, with clear progression from problem formulation to algorithm design to theoretical analysis. The sketch of proofs provides sufficient detail for the reader to follow the key arguments.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical evaluation of the proposed algorithms. This is a significant gap, especially given the computational efficiency claims. A comparison with baseline methods on synthetic or real-world datasets would strengthen the paper considerably.

2. **Limited Discussion of Related Work**: While the paper cites [21, 22], it doesn't adequately contrast its approach with these recent works. The authors claim their method achieves logarithmic regret while [21, 22] achieve O(√T), but the differences in problem assumptions (e.g., fixed vs. adaptive budget constraints) need clearer explanation.

3. **Practical Limitations of Assumptions**: The paper focuses on unit-cost systems and finite discrete contexts, which, while justified, may limit applicability to real-world scenarios with heterogeneous costs or continuous contexts. The extension to heterogeneous costs (Section 6) is mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed.

4. **Boundary Cases Handling**: While the paper carefully analyzes boundary cases, it doesn't provide practical guidance on how to handle these cases in applications. The O(√T) regret in boundary cases is a significant limitation, but the paper doesn't discuss whether these cases are rare in practice.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Include Empirical Results**: Add a section with experiments comparing UCB-ALP against baseline algorithms (e.g., variations of UCB with fixed budget constraints) on synthetic datasets. This would validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical utility.

2. **Clarify Relationship to Recent Work**: Provide a more detailed comparison with [21, 22], explicitly stating the key differences in problem formulation and assumptions that enable logarithmic regret in this paper.

3. **Expand on Boundary Cases**: Discuss the practical implications of boundary cases. For example, how common are these cases in real-world applications? What heuristics could be used to mitigate the O(√T) regret in these cases?

4. **Consider More General Settings**: Briefly discuss how the techniques might extend to continuous contexts or heterogeneous costs beyond the supplementary material. This would strengthen the significance claim.

## Final Assessment
This is a technically strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the constrained bandit literature. The authors successfully demonstrate how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, which is a notable advancement over prior work. The paper is well-written and the theoretical analysis is rigorous.

The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation, which is a notable omission for a paper claiming practical algorithms. However, the theoretical contribution is substantial and the paper is well-positioned to advance the field.

**Decision**: Accept. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution with strong mathematical results that advance the state of the art. While empirical validation would strengthen the paper, the theoretical results are sufficiently compelling for publication. The paper's technical depth, clarity, and significance outweigh the absence of empirical results, especially given the complexity of the problem.
