## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, where an agent must maximize reward while adhering to both a time and a budget constraint.  The authors make a significant contribution by developing algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret – a key benchmark in bandit literature – under reasonable assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs). They build upon linear programming relaxations of the optimal policy to create an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, and then combine this with Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) techniques to handle the unknown reward setting (UCB-ALP). The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis and considers generalizations to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of contextual bandits (Langford & Zhang, 2008; Li et al., 2010) and budget-constrained bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2015; Jiang et al., 2015).  It explicitly addresses the limitations of existing work on resource-constrained bandits (Badanidiyuru et al., 2017), which often rely on restrictive assumptions or achieve suboptimal regret bounds.  The authors acknowledge recent extensions to dynamic contexts (Agrawal et al., 2015, 2016; Xia et al., 2016), correctly noting that these primarily achieve √T regret, while their work achieves logarithmic regret. The connection to dynamic programming and its challenges (difficulty in obtaining a regret benchmark, computational complexity) is clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** Achieving logarithmic regret in the constrained contextual bandit setting is a substantial advance, as it represents a fundamental improvement over previously known bounds. The problem itself is practically relevant in many applications (clinical trials, online advertising, crowdsourcing).
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated. The use of linear programming relaxations as a proxy for the optimal policy is a clever approach. The regret analysis, though complex, appears to be carefully executed, and the boundary case analysis is a strength.
* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clear, and the presentation of the algorithms (ALP, UCB-ALP) is concise. The breakdown of the analysis into stages (known statistics, unknown statistics, generalizations) is helpful.
* **Thoroughness:**  The authors address several extensions of the core problem (unknown context distribution, heterogeneous costs) and provide sufficient detail to understand the adaptations required.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper demonstrates a strong awareness of related work and carefully differentiates its contributions from those of others.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** While the assumption of finite contexts and fixed costs makes the analysis tractable, it limits the direct applicability of the algorithms to certain real-world scenarios. The impact of relaxing these assumptions could be further discussed.
* **Complexity of Analysis:**  The regret analysis is undeniably complex and requires substantial mathematical expertise to fully grasp.  While the authors have done a good job of presenting the analysis, it may be inaccessible to a broader audience.  More intuitive explanations of key steps could be beneficial.
* **Boundary Cases:** The √T regret in boundary cases is a limitation. Further investigation into the frequency and impact of these cases in practical settings would be valuable.  Are there strategies to mitigate the regret in these scenarios?
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks empirical evaluation. While the theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the performance of the algorithms on simulated or real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.  Comparisons to baseline algorithms (e.g., ε-greedy with a budget constraint) would be particularly useful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The logarithmic regret bound is a major achievement and significantly advances the state-of-the-art in constrained contextual bandits.
* **Strong Technical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates a high level of technical expertise and provides a thorough analysis.
* **Well-Motivated Problem:** The problem of constrained contextual bandits is practically relevant and addresses a gap in the existing literature.
* **Novel Approach:** The use of adaptive linear programming is a clever and effective technique.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of empirical results weakens the impact of the theoretical findings.
* **Restricted Assumptions:** The assumptions of finite contexts and fixed costs limit the applicability of the algorithms.




**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While the lack of empirical evaluation is a concern, the theoretical contributions are significant and the analysis is robust. The paper addresses a challenging and important problem and provides a valuable contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the relevant literature and offer a novel and effective approach to achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. I would encourage the authors to consider adding empirical results in a future version of the paper to further solidify their findings.

**Confidence:** 9/10
