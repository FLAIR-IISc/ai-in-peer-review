- Studies contextual bandits with a single knapsack-style budget and fixed horizon where actions consume cost and stop when time or budget runs out.  
- Main contribution: adaptive LP relaxation (ALP) that at each round solves an LP using remaining budget/time ratio bτ/τ and plays its threshold/randomized policy.  
- When rewards are unknown, combines ALP with UCB estimates (UCB-ALP) to learn online.  
- Theoretical results: in unit-cost known-distribution case ALP achieves O(1) regret except at boundary budget ratios where regret is O(√T).  
- UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret with known context distribution (or O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases).  
- Key technical device: remaining budget under ALP couples to sampling without replacement (hypergeometric), giving tight concentration of bτ/τ and decoupling estimation and decision error.  
- Builds on and improves prior work (RCB, Mixture Elimination, BwK) by adapting budget ratio per round and achieving logarithmic regret in the finite-context/action stochastic setting.  
- Technical core and proofs appear solid; main paper gives plausible sketches with detailed proofs in the supplement.  
- Completeness: unit-cost known-distribution case fully developed; extensions (unknown context distribution, heterogeneous costs) are outlined but proofs/parameters are deferred.  
- Practicality: per-round LP has closed-form threshold; algorithm is computationally simple (sorting by UCB).  
- Clarity: generally clear and organized, but the hypergeometric coupling needs more intuitive explanation in the main text.  
- Requests for clarifications: specify tie-breaking handling, sensitivity to small gaps, and per-round computational complexity.  
- Novelty: adaptive per-round average-budget LP and hypergeometric coupling are original contributions in this setting.  
- Significance: theoretically meaningful—identifies a simple, near-optimal policy class and attains logarithmic regret under natural finite assumptions.  
- Pros: simple algorithm with strong guarantees, first log-regret result in this constrained contextual setting, elegant analysis, computational efficiency, compares to true oracle.  
- Cons: boundary cases still incur √T regret without matching lower bound, strong i.i.d./finite-context assumptions, key proofs in supplement, minimal empirical evaluation, coarse O(JK log T) term lacking instance-dependent gaps.  
- Suggested follow-ups: provide instance-dependent regret bounds, prove lower bounds for boundary cases, analyze fully online EALP without truncation, explore extensions to Markovian contexts, and add experiments.  
- Recommendation: Accept (weak) — clean, impactful theoretical advance despite assumptions and limited experiments.
