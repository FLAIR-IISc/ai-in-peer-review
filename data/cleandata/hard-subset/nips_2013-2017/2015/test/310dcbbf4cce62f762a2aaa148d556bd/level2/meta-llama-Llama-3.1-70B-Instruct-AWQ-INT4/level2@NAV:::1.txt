**Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"**

**Summary**

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, a variant of the classic multi-armed bandit problem where the agent has to make decisions under budget and time constraints. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle algorithm, achieving near-optimal performance while tolerating certain estimation errors of system parameters. They also combine ALP with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method to handle unknown expected rewards, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the regret of these algorithms, showing that they achieve logarithmic or sublinear regret in various scenarios.

**Claims**

The main claims of the paper are:

1. The ALP algorithm achieves near-optimal performance for constrained contextual bandits with known system statistics.
2. The UCB-ALP algorithm achieves logarithmic or sublinear regret for constrained contextual bandits with unknown expected rewards.
3. The algorithms can be extended to handle more general scenarios, such as unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.

**Support for Claims**

The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the regret of the ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms, using techniques such as linear programming, concentration inequalities, and coupling arguments. They also provide numerical simulations to support their theoretical results.

**Usefulness**

The algorithms proposed in this paper are practically useful for various applications, such as clinical trials, online recommendation, and online hiring in crowdsourcing, where budget and time constraints are common.

**Field Knowledge**

The paper demonstrates a good understanding of the field of contextual bandits and the challenges introduced by budget constraints. The authors also provide a comprehensive review of related work in the field.

**Novelty**

The paper presents a novel approach to handling budget constraints in contextual bandits, using adaptive linear programming to approximate the oracle algorithm. The combination of ALP with UCB is also a new contribution.

**Completeness**

The paper provides sufficient details for reproducibility, including the algorithms, analysis, and numerical simulations.

**Limitations**

The paper acknowledges the limitations of the algorithms, such as the assumption of finite discrete contexts and fixed costs. The authors also discuss the challenges of extending the algorithms to more general scenarios.

**Constructive Criticism**

One potential criticism of the paper is that the analysis of the UCB-ALP algorithm is complex and may be difficult to follow. The authors could provide more intuition and explanations to help readers understand the key insights and techniques used in the analysis. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more numerical simulations to demonstrate the practical performance of the algorithms in various scenarios.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

Pro:

* The paper presents a novel and rigorous approach to handling budget constraints in contextual bandits.
* The algorithms proposed in the paper are practically useful for various applications.
* The paper provides a comprehensive review of related work in the field.

Con:

* The analysis of the UCB-ALP algorithm is complex and may be difficult to follow.
* The paper assumes finite discrete contexts and fixed costs, which may not be realistic in all scenarios.
* The paper could benefit from more numerical simulations to demonstrate the practical performance of the algorithms.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, as it presents a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits and provides a rigorous analysis of the algorithms proposed.
