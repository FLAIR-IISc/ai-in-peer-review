Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints (constrained contextual bandits) under finite contexts and actions. The key contribution is an adaptive linear-programming (ALP) approximation to the dynamic-programming oracle that, when statistics are known, achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary cases (and O(√T) on measure-zero “boundary” instances where the budget ratio equals a cumulative context probability). Building on the observation that ALP only requires correct orderings of per-context best expected rewards, the paper proposes UCB-ALP, which combines ALP with UCB-style confidence bounds. For unknown rewards and known context distribution, UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases, and O(√T + JK log T) on boundary cases. The paper further sketches extensions to unknown context distributions via an empirical ALP (EALP) with a truncated-update analysis, and to heterogeneous costs with an LP that includes all context-action pairs and an ε-first learning phase.

This direction complements prior work on resourceful/contextual bandits. Compared to Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17], which achieves O(√T) regret against a finite policy benchmark with potentially infinite contexts and multiple resources, this work focuses on a simpler and practically relevant setting (finite contexts/actions, a single hard budget, fixed costs) and establishes logarithmic regret to the oracle benchmark. It also differs from Bandits with Knapsacks [12] and related budgeted bandit works [11–16], which either lack observable contexts or achieve √T regret. Works on bandits with concave rewards and convex constraints [20–22] obtain O(√T) regret and do not exploit an adaptive per-round budget ratio under random contexts; the adaptive LP view here yields sharper bounds under the stated assumptions.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Adaptive LP relaxation as a surrogate for the oracle under hard budget constraints, with a clean concentration analysis of the remaining budget via a “sampling without replacement” viewpoint. This is a simple and powerful idea.
- Theoretical advances: First logarithmic regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts/actions and unit (and then heterogeneous) costs, with tight O(1) approximation to the oracle when statistics are known (non-boundary).
- Algorithmic practicality: UCB-ALP is computationally light. In the unit-cost case the LP reduces to a threshold rule based on ordered u∗j, and per-round computation is modest (maintain UCBs, solve a small LP/sorting problem).
- Clear separation of regret sources: The analysis cleanly partitions regret into within-context action ranking and cross-context thresholding errors, and leverages concentration of the average remaining budget.
- Extensions: Discussion of unknown context distribution (EALP/UCB-EALP) and heterogeneous costs with a plausible LP-based extension.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Boundary cases: When the budget ratio ρ equals a cumulative context probability qj, the regret degrades to O(√T). Although a measure-zero set in parameter space, discrete deployments may inadvertently hit boundaries (e.g., rational ρ and simple π). A robustness mechanism (slight dithering of the effective ratio, tie-breaking or randomized smoothing around the threshold) could mitigate this.
- Unknown context distribution: The main text provides only a truncated-update analysis for EALP to re-establish concentration. A complete finite-time analysis for the fully online UCB-EALP (without truncation) is not fully detailed, and the dependence on J, K, gaps, and π remains implicit.
- Gaps and constants: The regret statements are big-Oh, but the gap-dependent constants (e.g., in Lemma 3 and the JK log T term) can be large when contexts’ best rewards are close. An explicit, instance-dependent bound would strengthen the claims and facilitate comparisons with classical Lai–Robbins-style lower bounds [26].
- Lower bounds and optimality: The paper argues order-optimality informally by noting that learning per-context best actions costs O(log T). A more explicit lower bound tailored to this constrained setting (e.g., mapping to per-context bandit identification under a binding budget) would make the optimality claim more concrete.
- Empirical validation: No experiments are shown. Even small synthetic experiments could illustrate: (a) O(log T) scaling vs. √T baselines, (b) behavior near boundary cases, (c) robustness to mis-specified π and heterogeneous costs.

Quality:
The technical development is sound and well-motivated. The ALP analysis via the hypergeometric distribution for the remaining budget and the separation of regret components under UCB-ALP are convincing. Assumptions (finite contexts/actions, i.i.d. contexts, uniqueness of best action per context, unit costs initially) are clearly stated, and extensions are discussed.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. The oracle LP, threshold structure, and adaptive scheme are easy to follow. It would help to summarize per-round computational complexity, state explicitly how ties in u∗j and û∗j are handled, and centralize the definition and implications of “boundary cases.”

Originality:
The adaptive LP perspective applied to constrained contextual bandits and its coupling with UCB to attain logarithmic regret appear novel relative to prior RCB/BwK and convex-knapsack bandit literature.

Significance:
The results are significant for budgeted decision-making with contexts. Achieving logarithmic regret to an oracle benchmark under a realistic, finite setting is a meaningful advance. The approach is simple enough to influence subsequent work and practice.

Suggestions for improvement (feasible for revision):
- Add a small robustness modification to avoid boundary-case degradation (e.g., enforce an ε margin around the threshold in ALP, or randomize the effective budget ratio by ±O(1/√T)); analyze its effect on regret.
- Provide explicit, instance-dependent regret bounds showing dependence on reward gaps and π, and discuss constants.
- Include a short experimental section to validate scaling and illustrate the boundary phenomenon and heterogeneous costs.
- Clarify the analysis for unknown context distributions without truncation or, at minimum, bound the additional bias introduced by truncation.
- Discuss per-round runtime and memory, and include pseudocode for ALP/EALP.
- Briefly relate to known lower bounds (e.g., per-context Lai–Robbins) to substantiate optimality claims.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, simple algorithmic idea with strong and novel theoretical guarantees (logarithmic regret).
- Solid analysis with insightful techniques (budget concentration under adaptive control).
- Practical relevance and computational efficiency.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Degradation on boundary instances without a robustness fix.
- Incomplete finite-time analysis for the fully online unknown-π case in the main text.
- Lack of empirical validation.
