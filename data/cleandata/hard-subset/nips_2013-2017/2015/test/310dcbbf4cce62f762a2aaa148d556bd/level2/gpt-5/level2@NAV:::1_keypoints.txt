- Problem & approach: Studies contextual bandits with hard budget and horizon, proposing an Adaptive Linear-Programming (ALP) approximation to the DP oracle.  
- Main ALP result: With known context distribution and unit costs, ALP achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary regimes and O(√T) on measure-zero boundary cases.  
- UCB-ALP: Combines ALP with UCB to handle unknown rewards, proving O(JK log T) regret (non-boundary) and O(√T + JK log T) at boundaries.  
- Extensions: Proposes truncated EALP for unknown context distributions and ε-first ALP for heterogeneous costs.  
- Novelty: Claims first logarithmic-regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits with finite contexts/actions and stochastic arrivals (beyond finite-policy baselines).  
- Technical quality: Analysis is careful and convincing, using an LP upper bound and hypergeometric concentration for remaining-budget control.  
- Proofs and constants: Some proofs for extensions deferred to the supplement; constants and certain boundary behaviors need clearer discussion.  
- Clarity & presentation: Paper is well organized with explicit algorithms; minor notation slips and incomplete citations should be fixed.  
- Computational note: Solving the per-round LP reduces to thresholding, but computational complexity should be highlighted.  
- Significance: Potentially strong impact for budgeted recommendation and clinical-trial design; approach is computationally light and reusable.  
- Constructive suggestions: Add empirical comparisons to BwK/RCB and related work, provide high-probability bounds and constants, give lower bounds for boundary regimes, and discuss robustness to nonstationarity and approximate LP solves.  
- Pros: Clear, elegant theory; near-oracle ALP with minimal requirements; first log-regret guarantees and computational simplicity.  
- Cons: No experiments, some proofs deferred and constants opaque, boundary cases remain at O(√T) with limited insight, and assumption of i.i.d. contexts (unknown π handled via truncation).
