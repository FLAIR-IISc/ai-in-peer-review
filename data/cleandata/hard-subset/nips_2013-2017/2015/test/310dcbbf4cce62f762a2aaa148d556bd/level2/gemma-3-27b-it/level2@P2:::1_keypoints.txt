- Problem: Constrained contextual bandits with time and budget limits, aiming for logarithmic regret.  
- Key contribution: First algorithms achieving logarithmic regret under both time and budget constraints.  
- Method: Approximate the oracle via adaptive linear programming (ALP) and combine with UCB for unknown rewards.  
- Relation to prior work: Fills a gap vs non-contextual constrained bandits and improves on recent works that get √T regret or restrict policy sets.  
- Novelty/significance: Provides a strong theoretical foundation for applying contextual bandits in resource-limited settings.  
- Technical soundness: Rigorous analysis using LP, concentration inequalities, and bandit theory; clear link between ALP and optimal policy.  
- Clarity: Well-written, organized, and easy to follow with consistent notation.  
- Practical relevance: Assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs) are reasonable and the algorithm is computationally feasible.  
- Thoroughness: Covers known/unknown rewards, heterogeneous costs, and unknown context distributions with careful analyses.  
- Weakness — boundary cases: Logarithmic regret fails in “boundary cases,” degrading to √T; frequency and mitigation not well discussed.  
- Weakness — empirical evaluation: No simulations or real-data experiments to demonstrate practical performance or robustness.  
- Weakness — context distribution estimation: Treatment of estimated context distributions (Section 5) is brief; effect of estimation error needs deeper analysis or experiments.  
- Weakness — comparisons: Lacks detailed comparison of computational complexity and constant factors in regret bounds against related algorithms.  
- Acceptance arguments: Addresses a challenging problem, offers novel and sound theory, and is clearly presented.  
- Rejection arguments: Missing empirical validation, unresolved boundary-case behavior, and limited analysis of unknown context distribution.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept, with requests to add empirical results and a fuller discussion/mitigation of boundary cases in revision.
