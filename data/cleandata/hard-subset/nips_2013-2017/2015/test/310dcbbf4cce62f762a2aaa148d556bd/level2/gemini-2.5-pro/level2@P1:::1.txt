This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time-horizon and a budget constraint. The authors refer to this as the "constrained contextual bandit" problem, where the process terminates as soon as either the time horizon is reached or the budget is depleted. The key challenge is that these constraints create a temporal coupling of decisions, making the optimal policy a complex dynamic program (DP). This work makes progress on this problem by proposing computationally efficient algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over existing sublinear regret bounds for related problems.

The main idea is to first approximate the intractable DP-based oracle with a more manageable policy. For the case with known statistics, the authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. Instead of solving the full DP, ALP makes a decision at each step by solving a simple linear program that uses the current average remaining budget (remaining budget / remaining time) as its constraint. This elegant approximation is shown to be near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret except for a specific set of "boundary" parameter settings, where it achieves O(sqrt(T)) regret.

For the more practical case where expected rewards are unknown, the authors leverage a key property of ALP: its decisions depend only on the *ordering* of the expected rewards, not their exact values. This allows for a clean integration with standard learning techniques. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm combines the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method for learning reward estimates with the ALP policy for decision-making. The main theoretical contribution is showing that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in the non-boundary cases, and O(sqrt(T)) regret in the boundary cases. The paper also discusses extensions to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper provides the first logarithmic regret bounds for a constrained contextual bandit problem. This is a substantial theoretical advance over prior work on resourceful bandits [17] and concurrent work on related problems [20, 21] which achieve O(sqrt(T)) regret. The problem setting is practical and important for applications like online advertising, clinical trials, and crowdsourcing.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The approach of approximating the DP oracle with an adaptive LP relaxation is novel and insightful. The analysis, which connects the evolution of the remaining budget to sampling without replacement and uses concentration bounds to analyze the regret, is clever and appears sound. This technique of decoupling the complex decision-making problem (via ALP) from the information acquisition problem (via UCB) is a powerful conceptual contribution that could be influential for other constrained online learning problems.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It builds intuition effectively by first analyzing the simpler case with known statistics before tackling the full bandit problem. The motivation for moving away from a direct DP approach is clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **The "Boundary Cases":** A key limitation is that the main O(log T) result does not hold for "boundary cases," where the budget-to-time ratio `rho` exactly matches the cumulative probability of a set of top-ranked contexts. While the authors correctly state that these points have zero measure over the reals, the performance degradation to O(sqrt(T)) is significant. More importantly, the analysis for the non-boundary case relies on a parameter `delta` which measures the distance to the nearest boundary. The regret bound appears to degrade as `delta` approaches zero. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance in the neighborhood of these boundary points, as this is critical for understanding its practical applicability.
2.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The core results are derived under the assumptions of finite contexts, unit costs, and known context distributions. While the authors sketch extensions to unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs in Sections 5 and 6, the analysis for these more general settings is less complete and relies on different or modified algorithms (e.g., a truncated update rule or an epsilon-first approach). These assumptions make the setting less general than that of, for instance, Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], slightly tempering the significance of the results.
3.  **Analysis of UCB-ALP:** The proof sketch for the regret of UCB-ALP is very high-level. A key claim is that the concentration properties of the remaining budget (Lemma 2) still hold under UCB-ALP. This is not immediately obvious, as the policy is now non-stationary and depends on the history of observations in a more complex way than in the original ALP. A more detailed justification of why the "sampling without replacement" analogy remains valid would strengthen the paper.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

**Pro:**
*   First work to achieve O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits, a significant theoretical improvement.
*   The ALP approximation of the DP oracle is a novel and elegant technical contribution.
*   The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient and conceptually clean.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly motivated.

**Con:**
*   The logarithmic regret bound does not hold in "boundary cases," and performance sensitivity near these boundaries is not discussed.
*   The main results rely on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., unit costs, known context distribution).
*   The analysis for extensions to more general settings is less developed.

Despite the weaknesses, the paper's core contribution is strong. The novel idea of using an adaptive LP as a near-optimal and learning-friendly policy for a constrained online problem is a significant advance. The achievement of logarithmic regret, even under some simplifying assumptions, is an important benchmark for future work in this area. I believe this is a high-quality paper that will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
