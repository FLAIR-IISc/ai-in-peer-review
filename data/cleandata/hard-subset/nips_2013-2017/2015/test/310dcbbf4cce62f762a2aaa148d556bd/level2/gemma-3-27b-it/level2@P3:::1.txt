This paper addresses the problem of constrained contextual bandits, a relevant and important extension of the standard contextual bandit setting. The work aims to achieve logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over existing results for constrained bandits. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a challenging problem with a novel approach. The combination of adaptive linear programming (ALP) with the upper confidence bound (UCB) method appears promising and the analysis, while complex, seems carefully conducted.

The key strength of the paper lies in the introduction of the ALP algorithm as an approximation of the oracle in constrained settings. By relaxing the hard budget constraint to an average constraint, the authors derive a tractable and insightful approximation. The analysis demonstrating the near-optimality of ALP is a significant contribution. Furthermore, extending this approach to the unknown reward case via UCB-ALP and achieving logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases) is a substantial achievement.  The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs further broaden the applicability of the proposed framework. The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature, referencing relevant works and highlighting its novel contributions. The detailed discussion of related work, including comparisons with existing approaches like Mixture Elimination and those focused on linear contextual bandits with constraints, is commendable.

However, some areas could benefit from further clarification or exploration. While the boundary cases are acknowledged, the implications of these cases could be discussed in more detail. What types of problem instances fall into these boundary cases? What is the practical significance of the √T regret in these scenarios? A brief discussion or example would be valuable. The connection between the chosen LP relaxation and the optimality gap could be more explicitly stated and perhaps visualized.  While the analysis seems rigorous, a more intuitive explanation of the key steps, particularly regarding the concentration properties and the handling of context ranking errors, could enhance clarity.

The presentation of the theoretical results is dense and might be difficult to follow for readers not deeply familiar with bandit algorithms. Including a small example illustrating the algorithm's behavior with concrete numbers would significantly improve understanding. Also, the empirical evaluation is absent. While the authors acknowledge the computational complexity and focus on theoretical guarantees, including simulations to validate the performance of the algorithms and compare them with existing methods would strengthen the paper.  Even a limited set of experiments could provide valuable insights into the practical performance and robustness of the proposed approach.  The phrase “order-optimal” in Theorem 2 seems strong given the √T regret in boundary cases; perhaps “near-optimal” would be more appropriate.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits.
*   Clear and well-motivated problem statement.
*   Sound theoretical analysis with logarithmic regret guarantees.
*   The ALP algorithm provides a valuable approximation of the oracle.
*   Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of empirical evaluation.
*   Boundary cases and their implications could be discussed in more detail.
*   The theoretical analysis is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the area of constrained contextual bandits. The introduction of the ALP algorithm and the UCB-ALP approach is novel and promising. While the lack of empirical evaluation and the complexity of the analysis are drawbacks, the theoretical results are compelling.  Given the importance of the problem and the novelty of the proposed solution, the paper is recommended for acceptance, contingent on addressing the concerns regarding empirical validation and clarifying the discussion of boundary cases. A more accessible explanation of key theoretical concepts would also be beneficial.
