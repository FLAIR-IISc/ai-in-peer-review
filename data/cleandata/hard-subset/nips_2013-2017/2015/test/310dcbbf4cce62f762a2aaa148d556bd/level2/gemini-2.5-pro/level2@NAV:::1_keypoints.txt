- Problem & contribution: Addresses constrained contextual bandits and proposes UCB-ALP, claimed as the first algorithm to achieve logarithmic regret for this setting.  
- Known-statistics method: Introduces Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) as a computationally efficient approximation of the optimal policy, achieving constant regret in most cases.  
- Unknown-reward method: Combines ALP with UCB to form UCB-ALP, handling unknown rewards.  
- Regret guarantees: UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) regret in boundary cases.  
- Significance/originality: Substantially improves over typical O(√T) bounds; ALP’s reliance only on reward ordering is elegant and novel.  
- Technical quality: Analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with logical decomposition from oracle to bandit setting and concentration-based proofs.  
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear motivation, related work, and step-by-step algorithm development.  
- Weakness — boundary sensitivity: Needs discussion on practical sensitivity when budget ratio ρ is near a boundary and whether constants in O(log T) grow large.  
- Weakness — empirical validation: Lacks simulations comparing UCB-ALP to prior methods to demonstrate practical performance and boundary vs. non-boundary behavior.  
- Weakness — heterogeneous costs: Analysis for heterogeneous costs is condensed; main text should give more intuition on LP adaptation and analysis.
