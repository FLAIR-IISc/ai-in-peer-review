This paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget and horizon and proposes an adaptive linear-programming approximation (ALP) to the dynamic-programming oracle. With known context distribution and unit costs, ALP solves a per-round LP with the average remaining budget bτ/τ and, remarkably, achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary regimes (and O(√T) on measure-zero boundary cases). Building on the fact that ALP only needs the ordering of expected rewards, the authors combine ALP with UCB to obtain UCB-ALP, proving O(JK log T) regret (non-boundary) and O(√T + JK log T) at boundaries. They further discuss extensions to unknown context distributions via empirical estimates (truncated EALP) and to heterogeneous costs (ε-first ALP). Relative to prior work on Bandits with Knapsacks and Resourceful Contextual Bandits, which achieve O(√T) regret—often against a finite policy class—the paper claims the first logarithmic regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts/actions and stochastic arrivals.

Quality: The technical development is careful and largely convincing. The LP upper bound provides a clean benchmark; the analysis via hypergeometric concentration for remaining budget is elegant; the regret decomposition for UCB-ALP is standard but well adapted. Proofs for extensions rely on additional truncation/ε-first devices and are deferred to the supplement; constants and some boundary behaviors could be discussed more.

Clarity: The paper is well organized; algorithms are explicit. A few notation slips and incomplete citations should be cleaned. It would help to highlight computational complexity (solving LPs reduces to thresholding) and to summarize all bounds in a single table.

Originality: High. Achieving logarithmic regret in this constrained contextual setting (beyond finite policy baselines) is novel; the adaptive LP perspective is insightful.

Significance: Potentially strong impact on budgeted recommendation/clinical trial design; the approach is computationally light and conceptually reusable.

Constructive suggestions:
- Add empirical evaluation comparing to BwK/RCB and to [20–22], including robustness to misspecified π and heterogeneous costs.
- Provide high-probability bounds and discuss constants.
- Offer lower bounds or impossibility for boundary regimes.
- Discuss robustness to nonstationary contexts and to approximate LP solves.

Pros:
- Clear, elegant theory; near-oracle ALP with minimal requirements.
- First log-regret guarantees in this setting; computationally simple.
- Thoughtful extensions beyond the base model.

Cons:
- No experiments; some proofs deferred and constants opaque.
- Boundary cases remain at O(√T) with limited insight.
- Assumes i.i.d. contexts; unknown π handled via truncation.
