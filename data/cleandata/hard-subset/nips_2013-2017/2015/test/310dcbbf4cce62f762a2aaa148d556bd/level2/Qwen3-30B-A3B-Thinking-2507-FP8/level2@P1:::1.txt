

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, where both budget and time constraints limit the agent's actions, a realistic but overlooked constraint in contextual bandit literature. The authors propose a novel approach based on Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) that achieves logarithmic regret in most cases, significantly improving upon previous work that achieved only O(√T) regret.

The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing a practical problem with theoretical rigor. Previous work on contextual bandits (e.g., [1-7]) typically ignores budget constraints, while the closest related work on Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] achieves O(√T) regret with a restricted benchmark and high computational complexity. The authors' key insight is to approximate the oracle solution with a linear programming approach that adapts to the remaining budget and time, which they call ALP. They then combine ALP with UCB to handle unknown statistics, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm that achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide detailed regret bounds for both known and unknown statistics cases, and carefully address the challenges of budget constraints. Their approach of using ALP to approximate the oracle solution is elegant and provides a clear path to handling the complex interactions between exploration and exploitation under budget constraints. The analysis is well-structured, with a clear progression from unit-cost systems to more general cases.

The paper is well-written and well-organized. Key concepts are clearly introduced, and the technical details are presented logically. The authors effectively differentiate their work from related papers [17, 20-22], clarifying how their approach achieves logarithmic regret where others achieve only sublinear regret. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The main strengths include:
1) The novel ALP approach that achieves near-optimal performance with simple implementation
2) The clear demonstration of how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits
3) The thorough theoretical analysis with appropriate regret bounds
4) The practical insights about decoupling information acquisition and decision making

The main limitation is that the paper focuses on unit-cost systems in the main text, with more general cases addressed in supplementary material. While this is reasonable for a journal submission, it would strengthen the contribution to briefly discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms in the main text, as this is an important consideration for practical implementation.

The paper is highly original and makes a significant contribution to the field. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous contributions, and the results represent a substantial improvement over existing methods. The problem addressed is important for real-world applications, and the results have clear implications for practitioners in areas like clinical trials, online recommendation, and resource allocation.

The significance of this work is high. It provides a computationally efficient approach to a problem that has been challenging due to the complex coupling between contexts, budget, and time constraints. The insights about using linear programming approximations to handle budget constraints could be valuable for future work on constrained bandit problems.

The paper meets all criteria for NIPS:
- Quality: Strong technical contribution with rigorous analysis
- Clarity: Well-written with clear exposition
- Originality: Novel approach to a problem previously addressed with only sublinear regret
- Significance: Important problem with practical applications and theoretical advances

I recommend acceptance with the minor suggestion to briefly discuss computational complexity in the main text. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the contextual bandit literature, with results that are theoretically sound, well-analyzed, and relevant to real-world applications.
