## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper tackles the important and challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, introducing budget and time constraints to the standard contextual bandit framework. The authors convincingly argue that existing solutions for standard contextual bandits, and even those for budget-constrained *non*-contextual bandits, are insufficient for this setting. The core contribution lies in proposing the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, and subsequently combining it with UCB to achieve logarithmic regret (with some boundary cases resulting in sublinear regret) – a significant advancement as, to the best of my knowledge, this is the first work demonstrating such performance guarantees in this context.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear motivation and introduction of the problem. The development of ALP, initially for the case of known rewards, is logically presented and provides a strong foundation for the more general UCB-ALP algorithm. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, utilizing techniques like concentration inequalities and careful partitioning of regret sources. The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs further demonstrate the versatility of the proposed approach.  The authors acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding the boundary cases, and provide a clear roadmap for future work. 

The strength of this work lies in its ability to bridge the gap between optimization techniques (linear programming) and the exploration-exploitation dilemma inherent in bandit problems.  The ALP algorithm's reliance only on reward ordering is a particularly appealing feature, facilitating its combination with UCB. 

However, the paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the boundary cases.  While acknowledged, the conditions under which they occur and their impact on real-world performance aren’t fully explored. Furthermore, a more thorough comparison to existing contextual bandit algorithms (even those with suboptimal performance in the constrained setting) through simulations would strengthen the empirical validation. The extensive list of references demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: First work to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.
*   Significance: Addresses a practical and important problem with real-world applications.
*   Quality: Rigorous theoretical analysis and well-defined algorithms.
*   Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Boundary cases: Impact of these cases on practical performance isn’t fully explored.
*   Empirical Validation: Could be strengthened with more comprehensive simulations.

**Overall:**  I recommend acceptance. Despite minor areas for improvement, this paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits and offers a promising approach to solving a challenging problem. Encouraging the authors to elaborate on the boundary cases and include more simulations would further enhance the value of this work.
