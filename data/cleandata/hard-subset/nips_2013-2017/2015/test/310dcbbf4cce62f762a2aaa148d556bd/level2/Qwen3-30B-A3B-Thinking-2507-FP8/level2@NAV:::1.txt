

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a problem with significant practical relevance in applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors claim to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret for this problem, which represents a substantial improvement over prior work (e.g., Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] with O(√T) regret).

The key contributions are the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm for known statistics and the UCB-ALP algorithm for unknown statistics, both achieving logarithmic regret except for boundary cases where regret is O(√T). The core insight is approximating the oracle with linear programming, which decouples information acquisition from decision making. The paper rigorously proves ALP achieves O(1) regret (non-boundary cases) and UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret, which is order-optimal for non-boundary cases.

The paper is technically strong with well-structured theoretical analysis, clear proofs, and detailed supplementary material. The algorithms are computationally efficient and easily implementable. The authors provide thorough extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, demonstrating the approach's flexibility.

Strengths include the clear theoretical contribution, strong practical motivation, and elegant insight that simplifies the complex exploration-exploitation tradeoff under constraints. The writing is clear and well-organized.

Weaknesses include the limitation to unit-cost systems in the main analysis (though extensions are provided) and the boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the paper could better address the practical implications of these boundary cases in real-world applications.

The novelty is significant: this is the first work to establish logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, representing a major theoretical advance. The significance is high, as it provides a practically applicable solution to an important problem in personalized decision-making systems.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. The technical content is strong, the writing is clear, and the problem is well-motivated. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better address the practical implications of boundary cases.
