Summary
This paper studies contextual bandits with a single budget and a finite time horizon (“constrained contextual bandits”), where each play incurs a cost and the process stops when budget or time runs out. The key challenge is the coupling between contexts over time due to the knapsack constraint. The authors propose an adaptive linear programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle (optimal dynamic policy with known statistics) and show ALP attains O(1) regret (relative to the oracle) in unit-cost systems with known context distribution, except on measure-zero “boundary” parameters where the regret is O(√T). Building on the fact that ALP only needs an ordering of expected rewards, they combine ALP with UCB to obtain UCB-ALP, which achieves O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They further discuss extensions to unknown context distributions via empirical estimates (EALP/UCB-EALP, with a truncated analysis for concentration) and to heterogeneous costs via a generalized LP and an ε-first exploration scheme. The paper positions itself against prior work such as Bandits with Knapsacks and Resourceful Contextual Bandits, which provide O(√T) regret (and sometimes against finite policy classes); here, under finite contexts/actions and a single budget, the authors obtain logarithmic regret with a computationally simple algorithm.

Relation to prior work
- Bandits with budget constraints (non-contextual) have achieved logarithmic or sublinear regret, but do not handle context arrival (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al., Combes et al., Slivkins, Xia et al.).
- Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Badanidiyuru et al.) and later contextual bandits with concave rewards/convex knapsack constraints (Agrawal & Devanur et al.) obtain O(√T) regret, often over finite policy classes or with static contexts; the present work treats i.i.d. random contexts and gives O(log T) regret in a simpler but practically relevant setting.
- The approach of LP relaxations for constrained MDPs is known, but the adaptive LP with a clean concentration argument on the remaining budget (hypergeometric “sampling without replacement”) and its use to decouple learning (UCB) from constrained decision-making is novel in this setting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The LP upper bound Û(T, B) and the ALP design are well motivated. The key observation that under ALP the budget decrement is 1 with probability b/τ induces a hypergeometric evolution, enabling tight concentration bounds and an O(1) regret gap except at LP threshold boundaries. The UCB-ALP regret decomposition into action-ranking and context-ranking components is standard yet carefully adapted to the threshold structure. The boundary analysis is plausible and transparently acknowledged.
- Assumptions: i.i.d. contexts; finite context/action sets; unit costs (with an extension to heterogeneous costs); known time horizon; known context distribution for the main results. The unknown context distribution case is handled via empirical estimates with a truncated analysis to restore concentration, which is reasonable although somewhat conservative.
- Completeness: Proof sketches are included with detailed arguments deferred to the supplement. The arguments appear consistent with prior UCB analyses and concentration for sampling without replacement.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The threshold structure of the LP and its adaptive version is explained cleanly. The regret sources and event partitioning for UCB-ALP are described clearly. A brief complexity discussion (per-round computation) would help, especially for heterogeneous costs.
- Minor suggestions: clarify how ties and near-ties are handled (unique best actions per context are assumed, but state what happens if violated); state more explicitly the practical impact of being “near boundary” (ρ close to qj), as this can affect finite-sample performance.

Originality
- Achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits with random contexts via an adaptive LP relaxation combined with UCB is, to my knowledge, new under the stated assumptions. The reduction of the constrained problem to a setting where only the ordering is needed is an insightful contribution.

Significance
- The results provide a computationally efficient method with strong guarantees in a constrained contextual setting, addressing an important practical need (budgets in recommendation, clinical trials, crowdsourcing). The clean analysis and design may inspire extensions to richer models (multiple budgets, linear/infinite contexts, stochastic costs).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, computationally efficient algorithm (ALP) with strong performance and an elegant concentration-based analysis.
- UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in a constrained contextual setting, improving on prior √T results in related but broader settings.
- Clear modularity: ALP handles constraints and tolerates estimation error; UCB handles learning of rankings.
- Extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs are discussed, with proof sketches and practical heuristics.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Boundary cases incur √T regret; while measure-zero, being near a boundary can degrade performance in finite samples. Practical guidance to detect/handle near-boundary regimes would be valuable.
- The unknown context distribution analysis relies on truncation to obtain concentration; more direct online guarantees (without truncation) would strengthen the contribution.
- Scope is restricted to finite discrete contexts/actions and a single budget; extensions to linear/infinite contexts or multiple constraints remain open.
- Experimental validation (not visible in the excerpt) should compare against baselines such as Mixture Elimination, BwK variants, and primal-dual heuristics, and report sensitivity to ρ, J, K, and proximity to boundaries.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a margin-dependent bound quantifying regret as a function of dist(ρ, {qj}) to capture “near-boundary” behavior?
- How sensitive is UCB-ALP to misspecification of πj when using empirical estimates online (without truncation)? Any empirical evidence?
- For heterogeneous costs, can UCB-ALP-style online estimation replace ε-first while retaining logarithmic regret under separability/margin assumptions?
- What is the per-round computational cost in the heterogeneous case (LP with “one action per context” constraint), and can it be reduced via threshold-like structure?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a finite-sample, margin-based theorem showing how regret degrades as ρ approaches boundaries.
- Provide a practical boundary-robust variant (e.g., slight randomization or smoothing of the effective budget ratio).
- Include detailed experiments comparing against √T algorithms, with wall-clock time and regret, and sensitivity studies (J, K, ρ, proximity to qj).
- Discuss whether KL-UCB or Thompson sampling could improve constants in the JK log T term.
- Clarify assumptions and tie-breaking; include a brief complexity analysis per round.

Overall evaluation
A solid and well-executed theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art for constrained contextual bandits under practical assumptions. The paper is clear, technically sound, and proposes a simple algorithmic idea with strong guarantees. I recommend acceptance, contingent on strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying boundary/near-boundary behavior.
