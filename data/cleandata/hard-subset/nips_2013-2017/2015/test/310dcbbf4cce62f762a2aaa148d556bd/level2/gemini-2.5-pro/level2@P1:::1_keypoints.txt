- Addresses constrained contextual bandits with both time-horizon and budget constraints where process stops when time or budget ends.  
- Main challenge: temporal coupling makes the optimal policy a complex dynamic program (DP).  
- Key contribution: computationally efficient algorithms achieving logarithmic regret, improving over prior O(sqrt(T)) results.  
- ALP (known statistics): replaces DP with an adaptive LP using average remaining budget and attains O(1) regret except at boundary settings (O(sqrt(T))).  
- UCB-ALP (unknown rewards): combines UCB learning with ALP’s ordering-based decisions and achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) in boundary cases.  
- Extensions: discusses handling unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, with sketchy analyses and modified algorithms.  
- Strength — significance/originality: first to obtain O(log T) regret for this constrained contextual bandit setting; practically relevant.  
- Strength — technical quality: novel ALP relaxation, clever analysis linking remaining budget to sampling without replacement, and decoupling decision vs. learning.  
- Strength — clarity: well-written, structured presentation that builds intuition from known-statistics case.  
- Weakness — boundary cases: O(log T) fails at boundary rho values, performance degrades to O(sqrt(T)), and sensitivity near boundaries (dependence on delta) is not thoroughly discussed.  
- Weakness — simplifying assumptions: core results assume finite contexts, unit costs, and known context distribution; extensions are less fully analyzed.  
- Weakness — UCB-ALP analysis: the claim that budget-concentration properties hold under UCB-ALP is not fully justified and needs more detailed proof.  
- Overall pro: novel, elegant ALP idea and strong theoretical advance likely to influence constrained online learning.  
- Overall con: limited by boundary sensitivity, simplifying assumptions, and incomplete analyses for extensions.
