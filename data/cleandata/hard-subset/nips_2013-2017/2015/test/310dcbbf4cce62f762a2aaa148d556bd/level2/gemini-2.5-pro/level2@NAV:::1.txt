This paper addresses the constrained contextual bandit problem, where an agent must maximize rewards subject to both a time horizon and a budget constraint. The core contribution is the development of an algorithm, UCB-ALP, which is claimed to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret for this problem setting. The authors' approach is to first analyze the case with known statistics, for which they propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm as a computationally efficient approximation of the intractable optimal policy. ALP achieves constant regret in most cases. This insight is then combined with the Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) method to handle unknown reward distributions, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. The paper provides theoretical proofs showing that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in "non-boundary" cases and O(sqrt(T)) regret otherwise.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by achieving a logarithmic regret bound, which is a substantial improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) bounds common in related work on budgeted bandits. The method of using an adaptive linear program relaxation as a tractable decision-making module, which only requires reward ordering, is elegant and novel. This work clearly advances the state of the art for this important and practical problem.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The decomposition of the problem—from the known-statistic oracle to the unknown-reward bandit setting—is logical and insightful. The proof sketches, which leverage concentration properties of the remaining budget, provide a clear intuition for why the approach is effective.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, surveys related work, and outlines the paper's contributions. The step-by-step development of the algorithms makes the core ideas easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
*   The distinction between "non-boundary" and "boundary" cases is crucial to the results. The paper notes that boundary cases have zero Lebesgue measure, but a discussion on the practical sensitivity of the algorithm when the budget ratio ρ is *close* to a boundary would be valuable. Does the constant factor in the O(log T) regret term grow excessively large near these boundaries?
*   While the theoretical results are strong, the paper would be further strengthened by empirical validation. A simulation study comparing UCB-ALP to prior methods (e.g., from [17] or [20]) would help demonstrate its practical performance and verify the theoretical regret bounds, particularly highlighting the difference between boundary and non-boundary regimes.
*   The analysis for heterogeneous costs is quite condensed. A slightly more detailed intuition in the main text on how the LP formulation and analysis are adapted for this more complex setting would be beneficial.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel and significant result. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
