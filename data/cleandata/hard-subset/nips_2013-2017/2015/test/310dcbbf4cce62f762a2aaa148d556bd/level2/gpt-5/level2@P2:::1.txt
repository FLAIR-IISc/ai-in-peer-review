Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single knapsack-type budget and a finite horizon (“constrained contextual bandits”). The key difficulty is that budget and time constraints couple decisions across contexts and time, so the oracle is dynamic and non-myopic. The authors propose a linear programming relaxation of the oracle that replaces the hard budget with an average budget constraint. They then (i) design an adaptive linear programming (ALP) policy that re-solves the LP with the current average remaining budget bτ/τ and prove it is within O(1) of the oracle in non-boundary cases (and O(√T) on boundary cases where the budget ratio hits a context-cdf breakpoint), and (ii) combine ALP with UCB estimates to handle unknown rewards (UCB-ALP), achieving O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They extend the approach to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates and a truncated update scheme) and to heterogeneous costs (single budget, per-context-action costs), obtaining analogous bounds (ε-first variants for learning in the general-cost case). The analysis hinges on showing that under ALP (and UCB-ALP) the remaining budget evolves like sampling without replacement, yielding hypergeometric concentration.

Relation to prior work
- Bandits with knapsacks (BwK) and budgeted MAB (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., Combes et al., Tran-Thanh et al.) study non-contextual settings and achieve sublinear regret, typically O(√T).
- Resourceful contextual bandits (RCB; Badanidiyuru, Langford, Slivkins) allow rich resources/policies but get O(√T) regret vs. a finite policy class with high complexity.
- Agrawal & Devanur’s “concave rewards and convex knapsacks” and later contextual extensions achieve O(√T), often with static contexts or finite policy sets.
This paper focuses on finite contexts/actions, i.i.d. contexts, known or empirically-estimated context distribution, fixed costs, and a single budget, and shows logarithmic regret (non-boundary) with computationally simple policies, which appears novel in this constrained contextual setting.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The adaptive LP relaxation is simple, computationally efficient, and decouples learning from budget management.
- Strong and mostly tight guarantees: O(1) regret to the dynamic oracle with known rewards (non-boundary), and O(log T) with unknown rewards, improving over O(√T) prior results under their assumptions.
- Technical contributions: The hypergeometric concentration argument for remaining budget under adaptive control, and careful decomposition of regret into action-ranking vs. context-ranking components.
- Extensions: Discussion of unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs with associated algorithms and analyses.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Boundary cases: The O(√T) term appears unavoidable in their analysis when ρ equals a cumulative context probability. In practice, B/T is discrete and may frequently hit such values. The paper should discuss how to mitigate this (e.g., randomized thinning or slight budget smoothing) or quantify performance as ρ approaches a boundary (sensitivity to δ).
- Dependence on gaps and constants: The O(JK log T) hides instance-dependent constants (e.g., Δ−2 terms). Making gap dependencies explicit would improve interpretability and comparisons.
- Unknown context distribution: The main text relies on a truncated empirical distribution update to recover concentration; guidance on how to pick the truncation point and the practical performance trade-off is limited in the main paper.
- Heterogeneous costs: The learning algorithm resorts to an ε-first strategy. It would be valuable to investigate a true UCB-style online version with adaptivity akin to UCB-ALP (or justify why ε-first is preferable here).
- Scope of assumptions: i.i.d. contexts with known or stationary distribution and finite discrete sets are reasonable but limit applicability relative to RCB/BwK. A short discussion on robustness to mild nonstationarity or model misspecification would help.
- Empirical evaluation: The main submission provides essentially theory; more comprehensive experiments comparing to BwK/RCB baselines and simple heuristics (e.g., greedy with bτ/τ thresholding) would strengthen the case, especially for boundary regimes and heterogeneous costs.

Quality
The technical development is careful and largely convincing. The use of LP upper bounds as a clean benchmark and the mapping to sampling without replacement are elegant. Some key proofs and extensions are deferred to the supplement; bringing small, illustrative lemmas/examples into the main text would aid readers. Clarifying that bounds are ultimately with respect to the true oracle (not only the LP benchmark) is important; the statements of Theorems 1–2 reflect this, but the proof sketch could make the bridge explicit.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Notation is mostly standard. A few suggestions:
- State the computational complexity per round (sorting û∗j and a closed-form threshold for unit costs).
- Explicitly state the gap-dependent constants in Theorem 2 or add a remark with the dependence on min gaps within and across contexts.
- Provide an intuitive figure for the thresholding structure (j̃(ρ)) and how ALP adapts as bτ/τ fluctuates.

Originality
Adapting LP relaxations to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits with i.i.d. contexts and a single budget appears new. The concentration argument for bτ/τ under adaptive control is a neat technical idea.

Significance
The results are practically relevant for budgeted decision-making with contexts (e.g., clinical trials, crowdsourcing, wireless rate selection). The approach is simple enough to be implementable and could influence subsequent algorithm design in resource-constrained contextual learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments comparing UCB-ALP to RCB/Mixture Elimination (finite policy proxy), BwK baselines, and greedy heuristics, especially near boundary ρ and with heterogeneous costs.
- Provide a simple scheme to avoid boundary-induced √T regret (e.g., deliberately randomizing budget usage to keep bτ/τ away from qj) and analyze its effect.
- Make Theorem 2 gap dependence explicit; add a lower-bound discussion for the constrained contextual setting to contextualize optimality.
- For unknown πj, give a concrete rule for truncation time and a sensitivity plot in experiments.
- Explore a UCB-ALP variant for heterogeneous costs, or justify the necessity of ε-first.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and strong theoretical guarantees (logarithmic regret) in a constrained contextual setting with efficient algorithms.
- Clean, intuitive algorithmic ideas with solid analysis.
- Relevance to multiple application domains.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation in the main text.
- Degradation to √T in boundary cases without concrete mitigation.
- Some dependence on simplifying assumptions (finite, i.i.d. contexts; single budget).

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a clear, technically solid contribution that advances our understanding of budgeted contextual bandits and proposes algorithms that are both effective and simple.
