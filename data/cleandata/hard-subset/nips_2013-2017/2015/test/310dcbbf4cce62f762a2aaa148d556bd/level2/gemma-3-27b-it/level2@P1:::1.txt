This paper addresses the problem of constrained contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a significant extension of the standard contextual bandit setting. The authors make a strong contribution by demonstrating how to achieve logarithmic regret in this challenging setting, a feat previously unaccomplished. The paper is well-structured, clearly motivated, and provides a thorough analysis of their proposed algorithms.

**Summary of Ideas and Relation to Previous Work:**

The core idea is to approximate the optimal policy using adaptive linear programming (ALP), leveraging the fact that the oracle only requires ordering the expected rewards.  This is then combined with an upper confidence bound (UCB) approach for the unknown reward setting (UCB-ALP). The paper builds upon existing work in contextual bandits [1, 2, 3], multi-armed bandits [4], and budgeted bandits [11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16]. Crucially, the authors point out the limitations of applying previous results on budgeted bandits to the contextual setting.  They distinguish their work from Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] by focusing on a more tractable setting with finite contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, allowing for stronger theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms. Recent extensions to RCB [21, 22] achieve √T regret, highlighting the novelty of the logarithmic regret bound presented here.  The work also relates to dynamic programming approaches but avoids their computational complexity by using linear programming relaxations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits is a significant theoretical advance.
* **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous and well-motivated, with careful consideration given to the interplay between exploration and exploitation under budget constraints. The use of linear programming relaxation and the development of ALP are insightful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and its importance, and the algorithm descriptions are easy to follow.
* **Completeness:** The authors address both the case with known and unknown system statistics, and generalize to heterogeneous costs and unknown context distributions (with some assumptions).
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The proposed algorithms are presented as computationally efficient, a crucial aspect for practical applications. The authors address a key open problem in constrained contextual bandits.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Boundary Cases:** The logarithmic regret guarantee is not universally applicable, with √T regret occurring in boundary cases. While acknowledged, the implications of these boundary cases and their practical significance could be discussed further. More intuition on why these cases occur would be helpful.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of known context distribution in the main UCB-ALP result (later relaxed) is a simplification. The practical impact of estimating the context distribution and the resulting performance degradation could be quantified.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** While the authors state that detailed proofs are in the supplementary material, the length and density of these proofs might make them difficult for readers to fully grasp.  A higher-level summary of the key ideas behind the more complex proofs within the main body of the paper could be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks empirical results. While theoretical guarantees are important, showcasing the algorithm’s performance on simulated or real-world datasets would strengthen the findings and provide insights into its practical behavior.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result, overcoming a long-standing open problem in constrained contextual bandits.
* The proposed algorithms are well-motivated, technically sound, and appear computationally efficient.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to researchers in the field.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact research on resource-constrained learning and decision-making.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The logarithmic regret guarantee does not hold for all parameter settings (boundary cases).
* The lack of empirical evaluation limits the assessment of the algorithm’s practical performance.
* Some of the theoretical analysis is quite complex and may be inaccessible to a broad audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The achievement of logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits is a substantial advance in the field. The clarity of the presentation and the thoroughness of the analysis are commendable. I **strongly recommend acceptance** contingent on the authors considering the following:

1.  **Elaborate on Boundary Cases:** Provide more discussion and intuition regarding the boundary cases and their practical relevance.
2.  **Consider Empirical Validation:**  Include some empirical results, even on simulated data, to demonstrate the performance of the proposed algorithms.
3.  **Summarize Key Proof Ideas:** Offer a more accessible summary of the key ideas behind the complex proofs within the main body of the paper.
