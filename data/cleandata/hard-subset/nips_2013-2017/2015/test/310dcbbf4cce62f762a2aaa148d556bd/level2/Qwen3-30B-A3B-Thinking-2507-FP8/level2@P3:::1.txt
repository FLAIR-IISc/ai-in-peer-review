

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, where the agent faces both budget and time constraints, a significant extension of traditional contextual bandits that captures important real-world applications like clinical trials and online recommendation systems. The main contribution is showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting, which represents a significant improvement over previous O(√T) regret results.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by developing the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm for the case with known statistics, which achieves O(1) regret except for certain boundary cases. This is then combined with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method to create UCB-ALP, which achieves O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases where J is the number of contexts and K the number of actions. The paper correctly identifies that previous work on contextual bandits with budget constraints either did not consider the contextual aspect (e.g., [11-16]) or achieved only O(√T) regret (e.g., [17, 20]), making this a meaningful advancement.

The quality of the paper is excellent. The theoretical results are well-supported with rigorous proofs, and the authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach. The paper correctly acknowledges the boundary cases where regret is O(√T) rather than O(log T), and provides a clear explanation for why this occurs. The analysis of the evolution of the remaining budget under both ALP and UCB-ALP is particularly strong, with the authors using novel techniques to handle the concentration properties under adaptive algorithms.

The clarity of the paper is outstanding. The main ideas are presented logically, with each section building on the previous one. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The notation is well-defined, and the algorithms (ALP and UCB-ALP) are clearly described. The paper effectively distinguishes between the unit-cost and heterogeneous cost cases, and the generalization to unknown context distribution is handled with appropriate care.

The originality of the work is strong. The key insight of using LP relaxation with adaptive budget constraints to decouple information acquisition from decision making is novel and well-justified. The paper correctly identifies that previous approaches to constrained contextual bandits either used dynamic programming (which is computationally complex) or relied on static budget constraints (which limits regret performance). The authors provide a thorough comparison with related work, particularly clarifying how their approach differs from [17, 20, 21, 22].

The significance of the paper is high. Constrained contextual bandits are an important class of problems with numerous real-world applications. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm achieves significantly better regret bounds than previous methods, while remaining computationally efficient. The results provide valuable insights into how to handle the complex interactions between exploration and exploitation in constrained settings, which could inform future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

The paper could be strengthened by more detailed discussion of the boundary cases. While the paper acknowledges these cases, it would be helpful to provide more intuition about when these boundary cases might occur in practice and how significant the O(√T) regret might be compared to the O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases. A more explicit comparison with the recent papers [21, 22] that also address dynamic contexts would further clarify the novelty and advantages of the current approach.

The paper's focus on unit-cost systems before generalizing to heterogeneous costs is a reasonable approach that simplifies the initial theoretical analysis while maintaining practical relevance. The authors correctly justify this simplification with real-world examples like clinical trials and rate selection in wireless networks.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field that advances our understanding of constrained contextual bandits. The theoretical results are sound, the analysis is rigorous, and the approach is novel and well-motivated. The paper makes a significant improvement over previous work by achieving logarithmic regret in a practically important setting, and the results have clear implications for real-world applications. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a theoretically sound contribution to an important problem in online decision making with clear significance for the broader machine learning community.
