

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints, proposing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm and combining it with UCB to create UCB-ALP, which achieves logarithmic regret in non-boundary cases (O(log T)) and O(√T) regret in boundary cases. The authors claim this is the first work to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, representing a significant improvement over prior work [17, 20] that achieves O(√T) regret.

The paper's main strengths are its technical soundness and rigorous regret analysis. The ALP approximation approach is well-motivated, with careful consideration of the oracle algorithm's complexity and the challenges of budget constraints. The authors provide a thorough analysis of how ALP achieves near-optimality (O(1) regret) with known statistics, and how UCB-ALP combines ALP with exploration to achieve logarithmic regret in the unknown statistics case. The paper clearly distinguishes between boundary and non-boundary cases, with appropriate handling of the technical challenges in the analysis.

The paper is well-written and well-structured, with a clear progression from problem formulation to algorithm design to regret analysis. The connection to prior work is well-established, particularly with [17] and [20], and the authors effectively highlight their contributions. The supplementary material provides additional details that would help with reproducibility.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. First, the main results assume unit-cost systems with known context distribution, which limits practical applicability. While the authors discuss extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs in Sections 5-6, these are not as thoroughly developed as the main results, and the analysis of the unknown context distribution case relies on a truncated version of the algorithm, which may not be practical.

Second, the paper does not sufficiently justify why the boundary cases (where regret is O(√T)) are rare or unimportant in practice. The authors claim these cases have "zero Lebesgue measure," but this theoretical property doesn't necessarily translate to practical irrelevance. The paper should provide more intuition about how often these boundary cases occur in typical applications.

Third, while the authors cite [21, 22] as recent extensions of [20] that achieve O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts, they don't explicitly confirm that no prior work has achieved logarithmic regret. The novelty claim would be stronger with a more thorough literature search for works that might have addressed this problem.

The significance of the work is high, as constrained contextual bandits are important for applications like clinical trials and online recommendation systems. The logarithmic regret bound is a substantial theoretical improvement over prior work, and the authors effectively demonstrate how their approach decouples information acquisition and decision making.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1) Provide more discussion of the practical implications of the boundary cases and whether they are truly rare in typical applications
2) Include a more thorough comparison of the computational complexity of UCB-ALP versus prior algorithms
3) Expand on the extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs in the main text (not just supplementary material)
4) Verify that no prior work has achieved logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, particularly in the period between the paper's submission and the conference

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in constrained contextual bandits. The results are technically sound, the analysis is rigorous, and the problem is well-motivated. While the practical applicability could be strengthened by addressing the limitations mentioned above, the paper is suitable for acceptance at NIPS.
