### Review

**Summary**

This paper investigates the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B). This setting, referred to as constrained contextual bandits, introduces significant challenges to the standard exploration-exploitation tradeoff due to the coupling of decisions over time. The work proposes a novel algorithmic framework based on approximating the optimal policy, which is computationally intractable, with an adaptive linear program (ALP).

For the case where environment statistics are known, the proposed ALP algorithm is shown to achieve near-optimal performance, with O(1) regret in most cases ("non-boundary") and O(sqrt(T)) regret in specific "boundary" cases. This ALP policy has the desirable property that it only requires the relative ordering of expected rewards. Leveraging this, the paper then develops the UCB-ALP algorithm for the more practical setting where expected rewards are unknown. UCB-ALP combines an Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) approach for learning with the ALP policy for decision-making. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases, a significant improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) regret common in related literature. The work also outlines extensions to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs. This work appears to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret for this problem setting, positioning it as a notable advance over prior work on budgeted bandits and resourceful contextual bandits which typically report O(sqrt(T)) regret bounds.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a practical and important generalization of the contextual bandit problem. The introduction of budget constraints is relevant to many real-world applications like crowdsourcing and clinical trials. The primary contribution—achieving logarithmic regret—is a substantial theoretical advance for this problem class. The approach of using an adaptive linear programming relaxation to approximate the intractable dynamic programming solution is both novel and effective.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical contributions are solid and the analysis is rigorous. The development from the static LP upper bound to the adaptive ALP algorithm is a clear and logical progression. The analysis of the ALP algorithm, which connects the evolution of the remaining budget to a hypergeometric distribution and leverages its concentration properties, is particularly insightful. The proof sketch for the UCB-ALP regret bound correctly identifies and decomposes the different sources of regret (action-ranking errors, context-ranking errors, budget fluctuations) and outlines a credible path to the final result.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The strategy of first presenting the simpler case with known statistics (Section 3) to build intuition before tackling the full problem with unknown rewards (Section 4) is highly effective. This structure makes the core ideas accessible and the progression of the technical arguments easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Treatment of Extensions:** The extensions to unknown context distributions (Section 5) and heterogeneous costs (Section 6) are highly relevant but are discussed too briefly in the main text. While page limits are a constraint, providing slightly more intuition for how the analysis is extended would strengthen the paper. For instance, in Section 5, the introduction of dependencies via empirical estimates of `π_j` is a non-trivial issue; a sentence or two explaining how the "coupling argument" helps manage this dependency would be very valuable for the reader. Similarly, for heterogeneous costs, a brief explanation of why the problem structure changes to require considering all actions (not just the one with the best reward/cost ratio) would improve clarity.

2.  **Discussion of Boundary Cases:** The distinction between "non-boundary" (O(log T) regret) and "boundary" (O(sqrt(T)) regret) cases is central to the results. The paper correctly notes that the set of boundary points has zero Lebesgue measure. However, a discussion on the practical implications would be beneficial. The regret bound for non-boundary cases in Theorem 1 depends on `1/δ^2`, where `δ` is the distance to the nearest boundary. This suggests performance degrades significantly as the budget ratio `ρ` approaches a boundary point `q_j`. A brief discussion of this sensitivity would add practical context to the theoretical results. It might also be worth noting that O(sqrt(T)) regret is a strong result in its own right and is often a worst-case lower bound in bandit settings, so its appearance in these specific cases is not unexpected.

3.  **Computational Aspects of the LP:** The proposed algorithms rely on solving a small LP at each time step. While these LPs are small (J variables), the overall algorithm still requires T such solves. It would be helpful to briefly comment on the computational complexity. Since the LP has a simple structure, its solution (Eq. 4) is analytic and does not require a general-purpose solver. Mentioning that the per-step complexity is dominated by sorting the contexts (O(J log J)) would further highlight the efficiency of the proposed method.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in the bandit literature.
*   Presents a novel and elegant algorithmic approach (ALP) that effectively decouples learning and constrained decision-making.
*   Delivers a state-of-the-art theoretical result, being the first to establish logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits.
*   The analysis is technically sound and introduces useful analytical techniques.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The presentation of the important extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs is overly condensed, deferring nearly all substance to the supplement.
*   The practical implications of the boundary case conditions could be explored in more depth.

**Conclusion**

This is a high-quality paper with significant contributions to the field of contextual bandits. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm and its accompanying analysis, which establishes a logarithmic regret bound, represent a clear and important advance. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to the completeness of the discussion in the main paper, which could be addressed in a final version. The work is technically strong, original, and addresses a problem of high interest. It is a strong candidate for acceptance.
