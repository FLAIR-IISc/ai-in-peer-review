This paper studies budget-constrained contextual bandits with discrete contexts and actions. The authors propose a computationally efficient algorithm that achieves a logarithmic regret bound via linear programming (LP) approximation. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it a pleasure to read.

The main contributions of the paper are the design and analysis of the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm and its combination with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method, referred to as UCB-ALP. The ALP algorithm is shown to achieve near-optimal performance when the system statistics are known, while tolerating certain estimation errors of system parameters. The UCB-ALP algorithm is then proposed for the case where the expected rewards are unknown, leveraging the advantages of ALP and UCB to achieve a logarithmic regret bound except for certain boundary cases.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the regret bounds for both ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms. The authors also discuss the challenges introduced by budget constraints and provide insights into the design and analysis of algorithms for constrained contextual bandits.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors have done an excellent job in presenting the technical details in a clear and concise manner. The writing style is engaging, making it easy to follow the flow of ideas.

One potential suggestion for improvement is to consider parametric reward models for scalability when contexts are numerous. This could be an interesting direction for future research.

Another suggestion is to clarify the rationale for combining UCB with ALP in Section 4.1. The authors could explicitly state the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle and its relation to UCRL2 policy updates.

Including numerical experiments in an extended version of the paper would also be beneficial in demonstrating the practicality of the proposed algorithms.
