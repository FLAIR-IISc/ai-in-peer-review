- Studies contextual bandits with joint time and budget constraints and proposes an adaptive linear programming (ALP) surrogate and UCB-ALP learning algorithm.  
- Positions work against prior contextual bandit and resource-constrained bandit literature (epoch-greedy, linear/similarity CB, BwK, RCB) which typically get O(√T) regret or finite-policy optimization.  
- Core idea: replace dynamic-programming oracle by a per-round linear relaxation adapting to remaining average budget bτ/τ.  
- The ALP achieves O(1) regret when statistics are known (except at boundary instances).  
- Combining ALP with UCB yields O(JK log T) regret plus an additional boundary-term.  
- Technical development is sound; ALP analysis via “sampling without replacement” and concentration of bτ is elegant.  
- Baseline (adaptive LP) is stronger than standard CB baselines since it permits abstention (“skip”) under a global budget, avoiding a fixed fraction of suboptimal rounds.  
- Boundary cases ρ = qj (especially j>1) are treated specially; reviewer questions whether the √T regret there is fundamental or an artifact and suggests tie-breaking, randomized smoothing, or budget dithering as potential fixes.  
- Clarity is generally good; proofs are sketched with details deferred, but figures for threshold structure and an explicit contrast between Û(T,B) and the true oracle would help.  
- ALP surrogate that needs only reward orderings and its coupling with UCB is novel relative to BwK/RCB and concave/convex bandits.  
- Significance: advances state of the art by achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual settings with a computationally light algorithm.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited; comparisons against RCB/Mixture Elimination and BwK variants on discrete contexts are recommended.  
- Pros: elegant ALP surrogate and clean regret guarantees; first logarithmic regret in this constrained contextual regime with random contexts; practical low-complexity implementation path.  
- Cons: boundary-case analysis feels ad hoc and unclear if √T is necessary; limited empirical validation against strong constrained baselines; main results assume known context distribution which needs more practical discussion.
