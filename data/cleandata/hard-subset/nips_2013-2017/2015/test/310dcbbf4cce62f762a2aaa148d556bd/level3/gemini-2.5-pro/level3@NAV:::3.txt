This paper addresses the challenging problem of contextual bandits under both a finite time horizon T and a budget B. This setting is more complex than standard contextual bandits because the budget constraint couples decisions across time, making the exploration-exploitation tradeoff significantly harder. The central contribution is the UCB-ALP algorithm, which, to my knowledge, is the first to achieve a logarithmic regret bound of O(log T) for this problem under a mild non-boundary condition. This significantly improves upon the O(√T) regret achieved by prior work [16, 17] in related settings. The authors' approach is simple and interesting: they first develop an approximation algorithm for the offline oracle (Adaptive Linear Programming, or ALP), and then cleverly combine it with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategy for the online learning problem.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality & Significance:** The paper's primary contribution—achieving an O(log T) regret bound—is a substantial theoretical advance for this important problem space. Budgeted bandits have numerous practical applications, and this work demonstrably pushes the state of the art. The method of decoupling the complex budget management from the reward learning via the ALP approximation is insightful and could inspire future research.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The theoretical results are well-supported, building logically from the known-statistics case to the full online learning problem. The authors are commendably honest about the limitations of their analysis, clearly stating the conditions under which the logarithmic regret holds and acknowledging the Ω(√T) lower bound in boundary cases. The core of the analysis relies on Lemma 1; while I initially had doubts about its correctness, the authors' rebuttal was convincing.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-motivated, and easy to follow. The progression from the simplified unit-cost model to the more general cases makes the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses/Questions:**
*   The main limitation is the reliance on the "non-boundary" condition for the O(log T) result. While mild, developing an algorithm with stronger universal guarantees would significantly increase the impact of this work.
*   The paper raises some interesting technical questions. Is computing the exact optimal action sequence for the offline problem NP-hard? This would further motivate the paper’s approximation approach.
*   From a practical standpoint, can these results be extended to the case of an unknown time horizon T?
*   A minor clarifying question: in the unknown context distribution case, is the assumption u*1 > u*2 > ... > u*J made without loss of generality? This seems plausible but could be stated explicitly.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its limitations. It presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. I strongly recommend acceptance.
