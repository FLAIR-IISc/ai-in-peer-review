- Problem: Studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, modeling practical scenarios (clinical trials, crowdsourcing).
- Approach: Proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) to approximate the oracle and combines it with UCB for unknown statistics.
- Contribution (unit-cost, known stats): ALP achieves O(1) regret (except boundary cases) by dynamically adjusting budget-to-time ratio.
- Contribution (unknown rewards): UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases (ρ ∉ {q₁,...,q_{J-1}}), first to show logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits.
- Contribution (extensions): Extends results to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs, giving a comprehensive framework.
- Technical quality: Rigorous, well-structured proofs—uses static LP upper bounds, concentration of remaining budget, and separates action/context ranking errors.
- Reproducibility and exposition: Exceptionally clear exposition, intuitive insights (e.g., sampling without replacement), precise algorithms, and sufficient supplementary details.
- Originality: Novel ALP design and analysis; distinguishes from prior work and leverages the insight that only reward-ordering is needed to combine with UCB.
- Significance: Substantial advance filling a gap (no prior logarithmic-regret algorithms), computationally efficient and near-optimal in most cases.
- Main weakness: Boundary cases (ρ = q_j) are treated technically but lack a clear intuitive explanation for why they behave differently.
- Minor issue: Comparison to a baseline that skips a fixed percentage of suboptimal rounds needs clarification on its relation to actual constraints and whether it is a meaningful upper bound.
- Recommendation: Strong accept—important contribution with high technical quality and practical relevance.
