This paper tackles the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, where both time and budget are limited, significantly complicating the exploration-exploitation tradeoff. The authors make a compelling case for the practical relevance of this problem, citing examples in crowdsourcing and clinical trials. Their key contribution lies in developing algorithms with logarithmic regret, a significant advancement in this area, especially given the complexities introduced by the constraints. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms.

The work builds upon existing literature in contextual bandits [1, 2, 3], multi-armed bandits [4], and resource allocation [17], carefully positioning its contributions within this landscape. The authors acknowledge related work on budget-constrained bandits [11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16] but highlight its inapplicability to the contextual setting. They also differentiate their work from Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] by focusing on a simplified model – finite contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint – which allows for the development of computationally efficient algorithms and tighter regret bounds.  The connection to linear programming and dynamic programming is also well-articulated, explaining the difficulties in applying these techniques directly and motivating the development of the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation.  Relevant recent work [20, 21, 22] extending to dynamic contexts is appropriately cited and differentiated, with the authors highlighting their achievement of logarithmic regret.

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The introduction of the ALP algorithm is insightful, offering a practical and theoretically sound approach to approximate the optimal policy. The rigorous analysis, demonstrating logarithmic regret (with caveats for boundary cases), is a significant accomplishment. The extensions to unknown context distributions (Section 5) and heterogeneous costs (Section 6), while less detailed, demonstrate the potential for broader applicability of the proposed techniques. The paper's clarity is commendable, with well-defined notations and a logical flow of arguments. The detailed citations demonstrate a thorough understanding of the relevant literature.  The authors are careful to discuss both the strengths and limitations of their approach, adding to the credibility of the work.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The reliance on simplifying assumptions, while justified for theoretical tractability, limits the immediate practical applicability of the algorithms. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth discussion of the impact of these assumptions on real-world performance would be beneficial.  More significantly, the lack of experimental results is a notable omission. Demonstrating the performance of the algorithms on simulated or real-world datasets would substantially strengthen the paper’s impact. Although the theoretical analysis is thorough, it's difficult to assess the constant factors hidden within the logarithmic regret bound. The treatment of the boundary cases, resulting in a √T regret, feels somewhat underdeveloped; more insights into the characteristics of these cases and potential mitigation strategies would be valuable. Sections 5 and 6 feel rushed compared to the detailed analysis in Sections 3 and 4. While the authors mention that details are in the supplementary material, a more integrated presentation within the main text would improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The paper presents a novel approach to constrained contextual bandits and achieves the first logarithmic regret bound for this problem (to the best of the authors’ knowledge).
*   Theoretical Soundness: The analysis is rigorous and convincing, providing strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithms.
*   Clarity and Presentation: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the problem, the proposed solutions, and the analysis.
*   Significance: The problem addressed is important and relevant to numerous real-world applications.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of Empirical Validation: The absence of experimental results weakens the impact of the paper and makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of the algorithms.
*   Simplifying Assumptions: While justified, the simplifying assumptions limit the immediate applicability of the results.
*   Limited Exploration of Boundary Cases: The analysis of the boundary cases with √T regret could be more thorough.



**Overall:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the lack of experimental results is a drawback, the theoretical contributions are significant and the paper is exceptionally well-written and presented. The novelty of the approach and the achievement of logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits are substantial contributions to the field. The authors have made a strong case for the importance of this work and have provided a solid foundation for future research. Addressing the weaknesses in future work – by conducting experiments and further investigating the boundary cases – would significantly enhance the impact of this already valuable contribution.
