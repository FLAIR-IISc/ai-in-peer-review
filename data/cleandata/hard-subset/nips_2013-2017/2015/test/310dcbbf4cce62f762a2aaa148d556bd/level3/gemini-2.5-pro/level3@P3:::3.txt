This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon T and a budget B. In this setting, an agent sequentially observes contexts, and for each, must decide whether to play a costly action (from a set of actions with known costs and unknown stochastic rewards) or to "skip" and incur no cost or reward. The process terminates when either the time horizon is reached or the budget is depleted. The paper's main contribution is the UCB-ALP algorithm, which is shown to be the first to achieve a logarithmic regret of O(log T) for this problem under a mild condition. This significantly improves upon previous work, such as [16, 17], which established O(√T) regret bounds.

**Strengths (Quality, Originality, Significance):**

The primary strength of this paper is its significant theoretical contribution. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to establish an O(log T) regret bound for the budgeted contextual bandit problem. This is a substantial improvement over the state of the art and addresses a challenging open problem.

The methodological approach is both novel and elegant. The authors first analyze the offline problem (with known statistics) and propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. ALP cleverly approximates the optimal dynamic programming solution by repeatedly solving a simple LP whose budget constraint is adapted based on the current ratio of remaining budget to remaining time. This approximation is shown to be near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret in "non-boundary" cases. This result is interesting in its own right and provides a solid foundation for the online algorithm.

For the online setting with unknown rewards, the ALP algorithm is skillfully combined with the UCB principle, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. A key insight is that ALP only requires the *ordering* of expected rewards, not their exact values, making it a natural fit for UCB-style estimation. The analysis that follows is non-trivial, as it must handle the interplay between estimation errors in rewards (both within and across contexts), the random arrival of contexts, and the fluctuation of the remaining budget. The paper successfully decouples these factors to deliver a rigorous proof for the logarithmic regret bound.

The paper is also very well-written and clearly motivated. The authors do an excellent job of building the reader's intuition by starting with the simplest case (unit costs, known context distribution) and progressively generalizing to unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs. This structure makes the paper's technical content highly accessible. The work is technically sound and appears complete, with the generalizations demonstrating the robustness of the core ideas.

**Weaknesses / Limitations:**

The main limitation of the work is that the O(log T) guarantee depends on a "non-boundary" condition: the overall budget-to-horizon ratio (ρ = B/T) must not be equal to certain cumulative probabilities of the context distribution. When ρ falls on one of these specific boundary points (which form a set of measure zero), the algorithm's regret degrades to O(√T). The authors note that an Ω(√T) lower bound is known for these cases, so their result is tight. However, a stronger result that provided a universal guarantee, perhaps adapting to the problem's difficulty to achieve logarithmic regret whenever possible without this a priori condition, would further increase the work's significance.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **On Lemma 1:** My initial reading raised a question about the correctness of Lemma 1, specifically whether the static LP relaxation truly provides an upper bound on the optimal policy's expected reward. The optimal policy is non-stationary and depends on the state (remaining time and budget), so it was not immediately obvious that its performance is constrained by an average budget constraint over the whole horizon. However, after carefully re-reading the proof in the appendix (and anticipating a potential author rebuttal), I am convinced of its correctness. The argument based on the martingale property of the value function is sound. It might be helpful to add a sentence of intuition in the main paper to preempt this potential confusion for other readers.

2.  **Oracle Complexity:** The motivation for the ALP approximation is the intractability of the optimal DP. Is the problem of computing the exact optimal offline policy known to be NP-hard? Clarifying this would strengthen the motivation for the approximation approach.

3.  **Unknown Horizon:** The current analysis assumes a known time horizon T. Could the authors comment on the possibility of extending these results to the case of an unknown or infinite horizon? This would be a valuable direction for future work.

4.  **Reward Ordering Assumption:** In Section 3, for simplicity of exposition, it is assumed that u*1 > u*2 > ... > u*J. This is fine when the rewards are known. However, when generalizing to an unknown context distribution in Section 5, it is less clear if this ordering can be assumed without loss of generality, since the algorithm would be acting on empirical estimates of both rewards and context probabilities. Could the authors clarify this point?

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant theoretical advance on an important problem. The proposed method is elegant, the results are strong, and the paper is very well-written. Despite the minor limitation regarding the boundary cases, the contribution is substantial and of high quality.

**Recommendation:** Strongly Recommend Acceptance.
