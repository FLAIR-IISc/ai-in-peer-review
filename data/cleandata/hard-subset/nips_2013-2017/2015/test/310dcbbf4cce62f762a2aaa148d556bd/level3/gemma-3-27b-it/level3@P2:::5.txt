This paper addresses the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, where the agent must balance maximizing reward with adhering to a budget constraint. The authors make a significant contribution by developing a computationally efficient algorithm, UCB-ALP, that achieves logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases) – a substantial improvement over existing approaches for this problem. The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, presenting a clear and logical progression of ideas.

The work builds upon existing literature in contextual bandits [1, 2, 3] and multi-armed bandits [4], recognizing the limitations of these approaches when budget constraints are present. While budget constraints have been previously considered in non-contextual bandits [11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16], the authors correctly point out the inapplicability of those results to the contextual setting. They also differentiate their work from the more general resource-constrained bandit framework [17] by focusing on a simplified, yet practical, model with discrete contexts and actions, enabling the design of a computationally tractable algorithm.

The core idea of the paper is to approximate the optimal policy using linear programming (LP). The introduction of the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which dynamically adjusts the budget constraint, is a clever and effective technique. Combining ALP with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method to handle unknown rewards leads to the UCB-ALP algorithm. The theoretical analysis rigorously demonstrates the logarithmic regret bound, making this a valuable contribution to the field. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** This is the first work to demonstrate logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.
* **Technical Soundness:** The LP approximation, the ALP algorithm, and the UCB-ALP combination are well-motivated and rigorously analyzed. The proofs appear comprehensive and the use of concentration inequalities is appropriate.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the problem, the proposed solution, and the theoretical analysis. The notation is consistent and the explanations are intuitive.
* **Significance:** The problem of constrained contextual bandits is highly relevant in many real-world applications (e.g., clinical trials, crowdsourcing) and the proposed algorithm offers a practical solution with strong theoretical guarantees.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors explicitly highlight the computational efficiency of their approach, a critical consideration for many applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability with Contexts:** The current approach might become computationally expensive when the number of contexts (J) becomes very large. Exploring parametric reward models, such as linear or low-rank models, to reduce the dimensionality could be a valuable direction for future work.
* **UCB-ALP Rationale:** While the UCB-ALP algorithm is effective, the motivation for combining UCB with ALP could be made more explicit. Specifically, clarifying how this combination embodies the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle—and drawing a parallel to policy update mechanisms in algorithms like UCRL2 [8]—would strengthen the explanation.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses on theoretical analysis. Including numerical experiments to demonstrate the performance of the UCB-ALP algorithm in practice, and to compare it with existing approaches (even if those approaches don't achieve logarithmic regret), would significantly enhance the impact of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound solution to a challenging problem.
* The logarithmic regret bound is a significant achievement.
* The algorithm is computationally efficient.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the technical details.
* The work has the potential to be highly impactful in various applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability of the approach with respect to the number of contexts could be a limitation.
* The lack of empirical validation leaves some questions about the practical performance of the algorithm unanswered.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The theoretical results are strong, the algorithm is well-designed, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The potential limitations regarding scalability and the absence of empirical validation are acknowledged by the authors and could be addressed in future work. 

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper to NIPS 2015. It merits publication due to its strong theoretical foundations, its novel approach to a challenging problem, and its potential for practical impact. I also recommend the authors consider the suggestions regarding scalability, clarifying the UCB-ALP rationale, and including numerical experiments in an extended version of the paper. Furthermore, the authors may want to consider referencing [21] as a related work.
