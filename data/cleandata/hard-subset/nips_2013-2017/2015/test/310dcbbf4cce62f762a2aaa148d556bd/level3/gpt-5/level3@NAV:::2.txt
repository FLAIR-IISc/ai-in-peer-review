This paper studies contextual bandits under both time and budget constraints. The authors focus on a practically motivated but simplified setting: finite discrete contexts, fixed per-action costs, and a single budget. They approximate the dynamic programming oracle via an adaptive linear program (ALP) that enforces the budget on average using the remaining budget/time ratio. With known rewards, ALP attains near-oracle performance with O(1) regret off a simple LP upper bound, except for measure-zero boundary cases (O(√T)). Building on this, they combine ALP with UCB to learn action rankings (UCB-ALP), proving O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) otherwise. They also sketch extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical frequencies) and heterogeneous costs. Relative to prior work on resourceful contextual bandits and bandits with knapsacks, which achieves O(√T) regret (e.g., Mixture Elimination; concave rewards with convex constraints), this is, to my knowledge, the first to obtain logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits by leveraging the discrete/finite setting and an adaptive LP relaxation.

Quality: The technical contributions are solid and carefully argued. The mapping of remaining budget dynamics to sampling without replacement and the resulting concentration for bτ are elegant and appear sound (though I did not fully verify all proofs). The regret decomposition for UCB-ALP is well-motivated. Practical aspects are thin; Sections 5–6 are mostly conceptual, and there are no experiments.

Clarity: The paper is clear, well organized, and well referenced; the motivation is explicit. I suggest clarifying the boundary-case measure-zero assertion, reporting per-round LP complexity and constants in regret, and better surfacing what parts of Sections 5–6 are proved in the main text vs. supplement.

Originality: High. While LP relaxations are known, the adaptive ALP construction and its regret analysis for budgeted contextual bandits appear novel, and the move from O(√T) to O(log T) under reasonable simplifications is meaningful. Please more clearly position against contemporaneous works on dynamic contexts with global constraints.

Significance: The results advance the theoretical frontier and provide a computationally light approach. Impact on practice is constrained by assumptions and the lack of empirical validation.

Pros for acceptance:
- First logarithmic regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits under clear assumptions.
- Clean, computationally efficient algorithms (ALP, UCB-ALP) with convincing analyses.
- Good positioning relative to RCB/BwK literature.

Cons for acceptance:
- No experiments; thin treatment of practical issues and boundary cases.
- Assumptions exclude multiple constraints, random costs, and infinite contexts.
- Some extensions are deferred to the supplement without detailed guarantees in the main text.
