Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies contextual bandits with both a time horizon and a hard budget, a setting that creates nontrivial coupling across contexts over time. The authors first analyze the known-statistics case with discrete contexts and actions and unit costs. They introduce an LP relaxation of the dynamic programming oracle by replacing the hard budget with an average budget constraint ρ = B/T, yielding a static LP upper bound and an adaptive linear programming (ALP) policy that plugs in the average remaining budget bτ/τ. ALP attains O(1) regret in non-boundary cases (and O(√T) in boundary cases of measure zero) relative to the oracle.

They then consider unknown rewards and propose UCB-ALP: maintain UCB estimates per context-action pair and, at each round, solve the ALP with optimistic estimates. This yields O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. The analysis hinges on showing the remaining budget process under ALP (and UCB-ALP) behaves like sampling without replacement, implying hypergeometric concentration around ρ. The paper also sketches extensions to unknown context distributions (UCB-EALP via empirical context frequencies) and to heterogeneous costs (ALP over all context-action pairs with a per-context single-action constraint, with an ε-first learning phase).

Relative to prior work: Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) provide O(√T) regret in more general settings (e.g., [Badanidiyuru et al. FOCS’13], [Badanidiyuru–Langford–Slivkins COLT’14]), often with higher computational complexity or regret relative to a finite policy class. The present paper restricts to discrete contexts/actions and fixed costs, and in exchange obtains computationally light algorithms and near-oracle O(log T) regret in most instances. It also clarifies why prior static-budget UCB approaches (e.g., [Agrawal–Devanur EC’14], and later dynamic-context extensions arXiv:1506.03374, 1507.06738) incur √T regret in this setting, whereas adapting the budget constraint over time is key to logarithmic regret.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The LP relaxation and the ALP policy are well motivated. The mapping of the remaining-budget evolution to hypergeometric sampling is elegant and underpins tight concentration bounds. UCB-ALP’s regret decomposition into action-ranking and context-ranking components is clear, and standard UCB arguments yield the O(JK log T) term. Assumptions (unique best action per context; strict ordering of u∗j across contexts) are explicitly handled, with boundary cases acknowledged. The unknown-context-distribution analysis via truncation and coupling is reasonable, though details are deferred.
- Completeness and honesty: Claims are appropriately qualified (boundary cases, known π for the main results), and extensions are discussed. The lack of experiments is a gap for NeurIPS.
- Computational efficiency: The decision step reduces to thresholding by optimistic û∗j and a single fractional context, giving O(J log J) sorting or O(J) if maintained incrementally; per-step LP is trivial.

Clarity
- The paper is very well written and easy to follow. The roles of the static LP upper bound, ALP approximation, and UCB-ALP are cleanly delineated. Proof sketches convey the main ideas; notation is consistent. A few clarifications (below) would further help.

Originality
- Introducing ALP with adaptive budget and showing O(1) regret to the oracle in non-boundary cases is novel for constrained contextual bandits. The hypergeometric viewpoint and the way optimism and ALP are combined are insightful. Achieving O(log T) regret with hard budget constraints in this discrete setting appears to be the first such result.

Significance
- The work advances the state of the art on constrained contextual bandits with a practical, fast algorithm and near-optimal regret guarantees, likely to influence subsequent research and applications where budgets are pervasive (ads, clinical trials, crowdsourcing). The modularity of ALP suggests portability to other estimators and models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Scalability via parametric models: When contexts/actions are numerous or feature-based, per (j, k) UCB becomes impractical. Consider a parametric reward model (e.g., linear or GLM bandits) within UCB-ALP, replacing per-pair counts by shared parameter estimation (à la LinUCB). Discuss how estimation error in parametric models would enter the ALP analysis and whether logarithmic regret is retained under standard margin/gap assumptions.
- Clarify Section 4.1 (rationale for UCB + ALP): Explicitly articulate the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle: at each round, construct an optimistic model within confidence sets and act optimally for that model. Emphasize that solving the ALP with UCBs is an optimistic planning step analogous to UCRL2’s policy updates for MDPs (compute the optimal policy for an optimistic MDP). This connection will help readers situate UCB-ALP within the optimism paradigm.
- Numerical experiments: Include a small empirical section (even in an extended version) to illustrate: (i) ALP’s constant-gap performance vs. the oracle and vs. static-LP policies; (ii) UCB-ALP’s O(log T) scaling; (iii) behavior near boundary cases; (iv) comparisons to BwK/RCB-style baselines using static budget constraints.
- Related work: Please discuss more prominently the relationship to Agrawal–Devanur–Li arXiv:1506.03374 (and 1507.06738): assumptions (finite policy set vs. finite contexts/actions), dynamic vs. static contexts, and how adaptive budget control enables log regret here.
- Minor: State the per-round computational complexity explicitly; justify the smaller UCB exploration coefficient and confirm that all concentration lemmas still hold with your constants.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation of practical importance; strong, interpretable theory.
- Novel ALP approximation with elegant hypergeometric analysis; O(1) gap to oracle (non-boundary).
- UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret with hard budgets; computationally simple.
- Extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.
- Well written and organized.

Cons
- No empirical validation.
- Assumes discrete contexts/actions; unique best actions and strict ordering (ties are boundary).
- Boundary cases incur √T regret; discussion on whether this is improvable is limited.
- Scalability to large or continuous context spaces not addressed experimentally.

Questions for authors
- Can the boundary-case √T term be reduced for specific structure (e.g., margin conditions on π or u∗ gaps)?
- For unknown context distribution, can you avoid truncation in practice while preserving guarantees?
- Any remarks on lower bounds confirming instance-optimality (up to constants) for discrete contexts/actions?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper provides a computationally efficient and conceptually clean approach to constrained contextual bandits, with near-oracle performance and logarithmic regret in most cases. The theoretical advances and clarity outweigh the lack of experiments; adding a brief empirical section would further strengthen the submission.
