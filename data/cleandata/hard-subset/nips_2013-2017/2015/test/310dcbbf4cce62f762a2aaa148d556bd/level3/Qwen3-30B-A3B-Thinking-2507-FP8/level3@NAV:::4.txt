

This paper investigates constrained contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints, proposing novel algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret in non-boundary cases. The authors develop an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation for the oracle when statistics are known, which achieves O(1) regret except for boundary cases where it attains O(√T) regret. Building on this insight, they combine ALP with UCB to create UCB-ALP, which achieves O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases (ρ ≠ q_j) and O(√T + JK log T) for boundary cases. The work represents a significant advancement over previous approaches like Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], which achieves O(√T) regret with a more restrictive benchmark and higher computational complexity, and the recent extensions of [20] that achieve O(√T) regret in dynamic contexts.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous theoretical analysis, including novel concentration properties for the adaptive budget constraint. The key insight that ALP only requires reward ordering (not exact values) enables the elegant combination with UCB. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured exposition and careful distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases. The problem is clearly situated within the contextual bandit literature, and the authors adequately reference relevant NIPS work [1-7, 17, 20-22].

The originality is compelling: this appears to be the first work showing logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under the considered assumptions. The significance is high, as budget constraints are critical in real applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing, and the paper provides both theoretical guarantees and practical algorithmic insights.

The primary weakness concerns the boundary case analysis (ρ = q_j). The paper questions why these cases must be treated differently but doesn't fully justify why ρ = q_j is special for j > 1. The comparison to a baseline using adaptive linear programming is potentially misleading since the baseline already incorporates similar techniques. While the boundary case regret is O(√T), the paper could better explain why these cases are inherently problematic and whether they represent a meaningful fraction of the parameter space.

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear technical merit and practical relevance. The minor weaknesses don't detract from the significant theoretical advance. I recommend acceptance. Pro: Novel algorithmic approach, rigorous analysis, significant improvement over prior work. Con: Boundary case justification could be strengthened, baseline comparison might be overly favorable.
