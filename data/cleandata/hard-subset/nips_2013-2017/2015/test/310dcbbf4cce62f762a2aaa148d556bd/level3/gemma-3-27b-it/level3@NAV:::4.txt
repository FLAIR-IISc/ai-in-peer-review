## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, introducing algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret – a significant step forward in this area. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing work, particularly the inapplicability of prior results for non-constrained bandits and the limitations of existing approaches for constrained settings like those in [17]. The core idea of approximating the oracle via Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) is clever and provides a strong foundation for their subsequent algorithms. The resulting UCB-ALP algorithm is well-motivated and the analysis demonstrates, under certain conditions, achieves logarithmic regret, addressing a gap in the literature. 

The paper is well-structured, building from the simpler case of known statistics to the more realistic scenario of unknown rewards and context distributions.  The careful breakdown of the regret analysis, especially the handling of boundary cases, demonstrates a strong understanding of the theoretical challenges. The extension to heterogeneous costs in Section 6, while brief, indicates the potential for broader applicability. The connection to Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] is clearly articulated, and the authors effectively position their contribution as a more computationally tractable solution with a stronger regret guarantee in a specific, yet relevant, setting. 

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. The justification for why ρ = B/T should be treated specially, and specifically, why it creates “boundary cases” with worse regret, feels somewhat hand-wavy. Further intuition or explanation around these cases would be valuable.  Furthermore, the comparison to the baseline in [17] is interesting – leveraging adaptive linear programming to skip suboptimal rounds – but the paper doesn't explicitly quantify how their approach compares in terms of computational complexity to this stronger baseline that employs a fixed percentage skip. While the authors highlight computational efficiency as a benefit, a direct comparison would strengthen the argument.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the first work to demonstrate logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, a significant advancement.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, with careful consideration given to boundary cases and the impact of estimation errors.
*   **Significance:** The problem is relevant to many real-world applications, and the proposed algorithms offer a practical path towards efficient exploration-exploitation in constrained environments.
*   **Interesting Setup & Solutions:** The ALP approximation and its subsequent use in UCB-ALP demonstrate a creative and effective approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Justification for Boundary Cases:** The reason for the increased regret in boundary cases could be more clearly explained.
*   **Missing Complexity Comparison:** A more explicit comparison to the adaptive linear programming baseline from [17] regarding computational complexity would be beneficial.



**Overall:** Despite the minor weaknesses, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The novel algorithms, rigorous analysis, and clear presentation make it a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
