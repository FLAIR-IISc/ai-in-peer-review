This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon T and a budget B, where actions incur costs and the process terminates when either constraint is met. The authors identify that the budget constraint couples decisions over time, making the optimal policy an intractable dynamic programming (DP) problem. The main contribution is a novel and computationally efficient approach to approximate this oracle and achieve logarithmic or sublinear regret.

The core idea is to first analyze the setting with known statistics. The authors approximate the DP oracle with an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy. At each step, this policy solves a simple LP using the current average remaining budget (remaining budget / remaining time) to decide whether to act on the current context. This ALP policy is shown to achieve O(1) regret in most cases ("non-boundary") and O(sqrt(T)) regret in specific "boundary" cases. Building on this, for the practical setting with unknown expected rewards, the paper proposes the UCB-ALP algorithm. This algorithm combines the UCB estimation method to learn rewards with the ALP policy for decision-making. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) in boundary cases, which, to my knowledge, are the first logarithmic regret bounds for this problem setting. The paper also sketches extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses a practical and important variant of the contextual bandit problem. In many real-world applications (e.g., crowdsourcing, clinical trials, ad allocation), decisions are subject to resource constraints. The paper's setup, which considers the interplay between a time horizon and a hard budget constraint, is a significant and well-motivated extension of the standard model.

2.  **Elegant and Effective Solution:** The central idea of using an adaptive linear programming relaxation (ALP) to approximate the intractable oracle is both clever and effective. It decomposes a complex, state-dependent DP into a sequence of simple, stateless LPs that can be solved efficiently. This approach of decoupling the reward learning (via UCB) from the resource allocation (via ALP) is a powerful and unique contribution.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides the first logarithmic regret bounds for this constrained contextual bandit setting. This is a substantial theoretical advance over previous work like [17], which obtained O(sqrt(T)) regret under more general but different settings. The analysis appears sound, and the technique of modeling the evolution of the remaining budget as a hypergeometric distribution to establish concentration is particularly insightful.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Intuition Behind Boundary Cases:** The distinction between "boundary" and "non-boundary" cases is central to the paper's results, but the manuscript could do more to build intuition around why these cases are fundamentally different. A boundary case is defined as `ρ = q_j`, where `ρ = B/T` is the average budget and `q_j` is the cumulative probability of the top `j` contexts. Why should the performance degrade from O(1) or O(log T) to O(sqrt(T)) precisely at these points? My current understanding is that when `ρ` is very close to a `q_j`, the ALP policy is on a knife's edge, frequently changing its threshold decision based on small fluctuations in the average remaining budget `bτ/τ`. This heightened sensitivity to the variance of `bτ/τ` (which is O(T)) likely leads to the O(sqrt(T)) regret term. A paragraph explicitly explaining this intuition would significantly improve the paper's clarity and impact. Is the optimal policy itself discontinuous or just highly sensitive at these points?

2.  **Scope of Assumptions:** The main results rely on several simplifying assumptions: finite contexts, unit costs for all actions, and, most importantly, a known context distribution. The authors acknowledge this and provide sketches of extensions in Sections 5 and 6. However, these sections are quite brief. For instance, the analysis for unknown context distributions is for a "truncated" version of the proposed algorithm, which is less satisfying than a full analysis of the UCB-EALP algorithm. While these assumptions are reasonable for a first paper achieving logarithmic regret, their limiting nature is a weakness. It would strengthen the paper to elaborate slightly more on the key challenges that arise when relaxing these assumptions (e.g., how the dependency introduced by estimating `π_j` complicates the concentration bounds for the budget).

### Detailed Comments on Evaluation Criteria

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by the proof sketches provided. The theoretical analysis is non-trivial, particularly the analysis of the budget's evolution and its concentration properties under an adaptive algorithm. This is a complete piece of work, moving from the known-statistics case to the learning case with strong theoretical backing.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The progression from the oracle problem to the LP approximation and finally to the UCB-based learning algorithm is logical and easy to follow. As mentioned in the weaknesses, the primary area for improvement is in providing more intuition for the analysis of the boundary cases.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While constrained bandits have been studied before (e.g., [12, 17]), this paper is the first to achieve logarithmic regret in a contextual setting with hard budget and time constraints. The combination of an adaptive LP for resource management with a UCB-style algorithm for exploration is a novel and powerful technique for this problem class. The work is clearly differentiated from related papers, which either deal with non-contextual settings, achieve higher regret, or have different constraint structures.

*   **Significance:** The results are significant. This work provides a new, computationally efficient framework for a practical and challenging class of learning problems. The core insight—that the complex exploration-exploitation-expenditure trade-off can be managed by an adaptive relaxation—is likely to be influential. Researchers and practitioners working on resource-constrained sequential decision-making problems will likely find these ideas and algorithms very useful.
