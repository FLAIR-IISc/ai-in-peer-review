- Problem & contribution: Studies contextual bandits with time and budget constraints and proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) that replaces the hard budget by an adaptive average-budget constraint.  
- Core idea: Use bτ/τ (remaining budget per remaining time) as an effective constraint, yielding a tractable LP upper bound and a simple thresholding decision rule.  
- Main theoretical results: For known statistics and unit costs, ALP has O(1) regret except at boundary ρ values; combining ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) gives O(JK log T) regret (plus O(√T) at boundaries).  
- Extensions: Methods extended to unknown context distributions (via empirical frequencies) and to heterogeneous costs (via a generalized LP with per-context action constraints).  
- Relation to prior work: Complements resourceful contextual bandits and BwK/concave-knapsack results by achieving logarithmic regret under finite discrete contexts and adaptive normalization.  
- Strength — conceptual clarity: Replacing hard budget with adaptive average budget yields a clear analysis benchmark and practical decision rule.  
- Strength — technical novelty: New hypergeometric concentration argument for bτ and a careful regret partitioning are elegant and reusable.  
- Strength — performance guarantees: First known logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under these assumptions; strong bounds for known and unknown statistics.  
- Strength — computational simplicity: Unit-cost case requires only per-round sorting/thresholding, making it lightweight.  
- Weakness — boundary cases: Need more intuition/lemmas quantifying why ρ = qj causes O(√T) regret and how often bτ/τ crosses the kink.  
- Weakness — smoothing idea: Suggest exploring smoothing ALP near kinks (e.g., reserve ε budget or add slack) to reduce crossings and improve regret.  
- Weakness — assumptions: Unique best action per context and strict ordering of u*j are strong; need discussion of ties and robustness.  
- Weakness — comparator clarity: Analysis uses a strong static-LP upper bound; main text should state regret is relative to the true oracle and Û(T,B) is a surrogate.  
- Weakness — unknown π treatment: Truncated EALP relegated to supplement; include a concise theorem, intuition, and sample-complexity comment in main text.  
- Weakness — heterogeneous costs complexity: Generalized LP has JK variables; request discussion of possible greedy/normalized-value algorithms and computational cost.  
- Weakness — experiments: Empirical comparison to Mixture Elimination, BwK methods, and concave-knapsack bandits is missing; sensitivity to ρ (near boundaries) should be reported.  
- Quality assessment: Proofs appear technically sound with careful concentration arguments; boundary rates likely reflect fundamental kinks.  
- Clarity suggestions: Add figure/pseudocode for ALP thresholding, expand boundary intuition, summarize unknown-π result in main text, and state per-round computational complexity.  
- Originality & significance: Adaptive-LP relaxation is a fresh idea with practical modularity and significant applicability to resource-constrained problems.  
- Specific open questions for authors: Provide formal lower-bound or coupling for boundary Ω(√T) regret, whether KL-UCB/Thompson could reduce constants, and when per-context greedy selection by normalized value is suboptimal.  
- Pros summary: Clear, principled approach; strong theory (logarithmic regret) and practical simplicity in core settings.  
- Cons summary: Boundary-case intuition and optimality not fully explored, limited empirical validation, and limited discussion on computational aspects for heterogeneous costs.  
- Recommendation: Leaning toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying boundary behavior and adding empirical comparisons.
