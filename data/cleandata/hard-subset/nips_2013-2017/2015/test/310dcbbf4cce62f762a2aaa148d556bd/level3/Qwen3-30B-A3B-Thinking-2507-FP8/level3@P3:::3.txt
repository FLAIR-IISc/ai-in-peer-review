

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the contextual bandit literature by studying constrained contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints. The authors consider a setting where each action under a context incurs a cost, and the agent must operate within a fixed budget B and time horizon T. The paper makes a major theoretical contribution by showing that, for unit-cost systems with known context distribution, an O(log T) regret bound can be achieved under mild conditions, whereas prior work (e.g., [16]) achieved only O(√T) regret.

The key insight is the development of an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation for the oracle policy, which achieves near-optimality (O(1) regret) for non-boundary cases. This ALP approach is particularly elegant because it only requires the ordering of expected rewards rather than their exact values, making it compatible with UCB-style estimation methods. The authors then combine ALP with UCB to create the UCB-ALP algorithm, which achieves O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases and O(√T) for boundary cases.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, with a strong connection to practical applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors carefully position their work against prior results, noting that while [17] considers a more general Resourceful Contextual Bandits framework, it achieves only O(√T) regret with a restricted benchmark, and the proposed algorithm suffers from high computational complexity. The current paper's focus on unit-cost systems with known context distribution allows for a simpler, more efficient algorithm with superior regret guarantees.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful handling of the complex interactions between budget constraints, context arrivals, and estimation errors. The key Lemma 1 (proving that the LP relaxation provides an upper bound on the oracle reward) was initially doubted by the reviewer but was convincingly verified in the rebuttal. The regret analysis for UCB-ALP is particularly impressive, as it carefully disentangles the contributions from different error sources (context ranking errors, budget fluctuations). The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that O(log T) regret requires a mild condition (non-boundary cases), and that Ω(√T) lower bounds hold for boundary cases.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem setup is clearly defined, the key insights are presented with intuitive explanations, and the technical contributions are logically structured. The authors effectively use the LP relaxation as a unifying framework, making complex ideas accessible. The proof sketches provide sufficient detail for verification without overwhelming the reader.

**Originality**: This work represents a significant advancement in the constrained bandit literature. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first paper to achieve O(log T) regret for contextual bandits with budget constraints, whereas prior work achieved only sublinear (O(√T)) regret. The approach of using an adaptive LP approximation to decouple exploration and exploitation in constrained settings is novel and could inspire future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

**Significance**: The results have substantial theoretical significance, demonstrating that the exploration-exploitation tradeoff in constrained contextual bandits can be managed with logarithmic regret under mild conditions. The practical significance is also strong, as the proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient and directly applicable to real-world problems like clinical trials and resource-constrained online services. The insights about how to approximate the oracle policy with linear programming could be extended to other constrained learning settings.

**Weaknesses**: The main limitation is that the O(log T) guarantee requires the mild condition that the budget-to-time ratio ρ does not coincide with any cumulative context probability q_j (a "boundary case"). For these cases, the regret becomes O(√T), which is the same order as prior work. While the authors correctly note that these boundary cases form a set of measure zero, a stronger universal guarantee would increase the paper's significance. Additionally, the paper leaves open the question of whether computing the exact optimal action sequence is NP-hard, which would have implications for the practical implementation of their approach.

**Recommendation**: I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper makes a clear, important contribution to the contextual bandit literature by achieving the first O(log T) regret bound for constrained contextual bandits under mild conditions. The theoretical results are sound, the writing is excellent, and the insights provide a valuable foundation for future work in constrained bandit problems. The paper's focus on practical implementation (via the simple UCB-ALP algorithm) further enhances its significance. The minor limitation regarding boundary cases does not diminish the paper's overall contribution, as the authors provide a clear characterization of when their improved regret bound applies.
