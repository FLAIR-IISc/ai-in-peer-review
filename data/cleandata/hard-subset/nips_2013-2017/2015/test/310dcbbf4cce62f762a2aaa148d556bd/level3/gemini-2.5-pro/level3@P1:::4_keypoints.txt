- Problem: studies contextual bandits with both time horizon T and hard budget B where actions incur costs and process stops when either constraint is met.  
- Challenge: budget constraint couples decisions over time, making the optimal policy an intractable dynamic program.  
- Main contribution: proposes a computationally efficient approximation achieving logarithmic or sublinear regret.  
- ALP idea: introduce an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that solves a simple LP each step using current average remaining budget to decide actions.  
- ALP guarantees: ALP achieves O(1) regret in "non-boundary" cases and O(sqrt(T)) regret in "boundary" cases.  
- Learning algorithm: propose UCB-ALP combining UCB reward estimates with ALP decision rules for unknown rewards.  
- UCB-ALP guarantees: achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) in boundary cases — claimed first logarithmic bounds for this setting.  
- Extensions: sketches for unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, but analyses are brief.  
- Strength — novelty: addresses a practically important constrained contextual bandit variant motivated by real-world resource limits.  
- Strength — method: ALP decouples DP into simple stateless LPs and cleanly separates reward learning (UCB) from resource allocation (ALP).  
- Strength — theory: provides strong, non-trivial theoretical guarantees and novel concentration analysis (hypergeometric modeling of budget evolution).  
- Weakness — boundary intuition: paper needs clearer intuition why performance drops at ρ = q_j and whether optimal policy is discontinuous or just highly sensitive there.  
- Weakness — assumptions: results rely on finite contexts, unit costs, and known context distribution; extensions are sketched but lack full analysis.  
- Evaluation summary: high technical quality, generally clear exposition, highly original approach, and significant potential impact for resource-constrained sequential decision-making.
