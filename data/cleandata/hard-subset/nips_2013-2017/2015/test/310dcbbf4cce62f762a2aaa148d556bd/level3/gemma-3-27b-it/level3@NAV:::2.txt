## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper tackles the important problem of contextual bandits under both budget and time constraints, a common scenario in real-world applications. The authors make a significant contribution by presenting algorithms achieving O(log T) regret, claiming this is the first such result for constrained contextual bandits. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides a thorough review of related work, situating their contribution within the existing literature on bandits and constrained optimization. The theoretical analysis appears solid, and the proofs, while not fully vetted by this reviewer, seem convincing. 

The core idea is to approximate the oracle solution using an adaptive linear programming (ALP) approach. This allows the authors to navigate the complex exploration-exploitation trade-off imposed by the constraints. Combining ALP with the UCB method leads to the UCB-ALP algorithm, which achieves the desired logarithmic regret under reasonable assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint).  The paper builds upon existing LP relaxation techniques but introduces a novel adaptive approach that is key to their theoretical guarantees.  The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are also valuable, showcasing the adaptability of their framework.

However, a significant weakness lies in the limited practical evaluation. While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks experimental validation. Sections 5 and 6, dealing with extensions to more general scenarios, are particularly thin on details, making it difficult to assess the practical implications of their work. The absence of any empirical results is a major drawback, preventing a full assessment of the algorithm’s performance in realistic settings. Furthermore, the simplifying assumptions, while justifiable for theoretical tractability, may limit the applicability of the proposed algorithms in more complex, real-world scenarios. It's unclear how robust the algorithms are to violations of these assumptions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: First claimed logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits.
*   Strong Theoretical Foundation: Solid analysis and convincing proofs (pending full verification).
*   Clear Presentation: Well-written, well-organized, and easy to understand.
*   Practical Relevance: Addresses a practically important problem with clear motivation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of Experimental Validation: No empirical results to support the theoretical claims.
*   Strong Assumptions: Simplifications limit the direct applicability to complex scenarios.
*   Thinly Treated Extensions: Sections 5 and 6 lack detailed discussion and practical insight.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a good paper with a strong theoretical contribution. However, the lack of experimental results significantly weakens the overall impact. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors including experimental validation, even if limited in scope, to demonstrate the practical viability of their algorithms. Otherwise, the work remains primarily a theoretical contribution.
