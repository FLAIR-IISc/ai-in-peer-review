- Problem addressed: Introduces "constrained contextual bandits" (budget and time constraints) and proposes algorithms for this setting.  
- Algorithms: Proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for known statistics and UCB-ALP for unknown statistics.  
- Main claim: Achieves logarithmic regret O(log T) in most cases, improving over prior O(√T) results.  
- Generalization: Extends results to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, offering a comprehensive framework.  
- Strength — analysis: Provides rigorous proofs and regret bounds for both problem-dependent and -independent cases, with treatment of boundary cases where regret is O(√T).  
- Strength — clarity: Writing is precise and well-organized; ALP is well-motivated and clearly explained.  
- Strength — practical relevance: Algorithms are computationally efficient and applicable to real-world constrained problems (e.g., clinical trials, recommendations).  
- Strength — significance: Logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits is novel and offers useful insights about separating information acquisition from decision-making.  
- Weakness — novelty: Problem setting appears close to existing budgeted/action-constrained bandits (e.g., Resourceful Contextual Bandits); distinctions from prior work need clearer articulation.  
- Weakness — regret expressions: Needs cleaner, arm-gap-dependent expressions for problem-dependent regret bounds.  
- Weakness — lower bounds: No matching lower bounds are provided, leaving optimality of the algorithms unclear.  
- Weakness — theorem detail: Theorem 2’s regret bound may be missing a √K dependence and would benefit from expression in terms of arm gaps.  
- Weakness — context complexity: Lacks exploration of lower bounds or analyses that account for context-space complexity.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, contingent on clarifying novelty and adding theoretical insights (lower bounds and arm-gap-dependent regret).  
- Pro/con summary: Acceptance merits — significant theoretical advance, rigorous analysis, practical algorithms; rejection concerns — unclear novelty, missing lower bounds, and limited context-complexity analysis.
