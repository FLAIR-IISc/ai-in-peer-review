This paper studies contextual bandits under joint time and budget constraints and proposes an adaptive linear programming (ALP) surrogate for the oracle and a learning algorithm, UCB-ALP, that achieves logarithmic regret except on boundary instances. The work is positioned against prior NIPS/ML literature on contextual bandits (e.g., epoch-greedy, linear/similarity CB) and resource-constrained bandits, notably Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB), which typically obtain O(√T) regret or optimize over finite policy classes. The core idea is to replace the dynamic-programming oracle with a per-round linear relaxation that adapts to the remaining average budget bτ/τ, yielding O(1) regret when statistics are known (except at boundaries) and, when combined with UCB, O(JK log T) regret plus a boundary term. The paper also discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

Quality: The technical development is sound and careful. The ALP analysis via “sampling without replacement” and concentration of bτ is elegant. The baseline used for comparison is stronger than standard CB baselines: the adaptive LP permits abstention (“skip”) under a global budget, effectively allowing the algorithm to avoid a fixed fraction of suboptimal rounds. However, the special treatment of boundary cases ρ = qj deserves deeper justification: why must ρ = qj (especially for j > 1) fundamentally incur √T regret? Is this due to a proof artifact (discontinuity in the LP threshold) or an inherent lower bound? Could tie-breaking, randomized smoothing around the threshold, or budget dithering eliminate the √T term?

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized; the proofs are sketched clearly, with detailed arguments deferred. It would help to add intuitive figures for the threshold structure, and to more explicitly contrast Û(T,B) vs. the true oracle.

Originality: The ALP surrogate that needs only reward orderings and its coupling with UCB for constrained CBs is novel and insightful relative to BwK/RCB and concave-reward/convex-constraint bandits.

Significance: Strong. The results advance the state of the art by achieving logarithmic regret in this constrained contextual setting with a computationally light algorithm. Empirical comparisons against RCB/Mixture Elimination and BwK variants on discrete contexts would further strengthen the claims.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant ALP surrogate and clean regret guarantees; unique and interesting setup and solutions.
- First logarithmic regret in this constrained contextual regime with random contexts.
- Clear path to practical, low-complexity implementations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Boundary-case analysis feels ad hoc; unclear if √T is necessary or removable.
- Limited empirical validation against strong constrained baselines.
- Assumptions (known context distribution in main results) could be discussed more thoroughly for practice.
