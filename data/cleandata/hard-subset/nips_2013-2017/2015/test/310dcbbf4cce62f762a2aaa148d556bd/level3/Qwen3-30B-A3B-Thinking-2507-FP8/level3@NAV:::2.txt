

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a significant extension of traditional contextual bandits that better models real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle algorithm, which achieves near-optimality with only the ordering of expected rewards. They then combine ALP with UCB to develop UCB-ALP, showing it achieves O(log T) regret except for certain boundary cases. The authors claim this is the first work to obtain logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, which appears well-supported by their theoretical analysis.

The paper's strengths are substantial: it is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and thoroughly motivated with explicit connections to relevant prior work (e.g., [17] on Resourceful Contextual Bandits). The theoretical contributions are strong, with convincing proofs for the ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms. The simplifying assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint) are well-justified as reasonable for many applications and enable the elegant theoretical results.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The practical aspects are underdeveloped: Sections 5 and 6 discuss generalizations to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, but provide no experimental validation. Without empirical results to support the theoretical claims, the practical relevance remains speculative. The lack of implementation details and performance metrics limits the paper's utility for practitioners.

In terms of the four criteria: Quality is strong theoretically but weakened by missing experiments; Clarity is excellent; Originality is significant as the authors establish a new approach to a challenging problem; Significance is high for the theoretical contribution but tempered by the absence of empirical validation.

Overall, this is a technically sound theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits. While the lack of empirical results prevents it from being fully compelling, the paper's clear presentation, strong theoretical results, and novel approach make it a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the experimental gap in future work.
