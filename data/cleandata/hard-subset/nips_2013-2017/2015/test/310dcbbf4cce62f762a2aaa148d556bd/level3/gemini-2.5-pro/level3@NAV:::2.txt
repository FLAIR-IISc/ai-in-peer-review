This paper addresses the challenging problem of contextual bandits under both budget and time constraints, where the coupling of decisions across contexts complicates the exploration-exploitation tradeoff. The authors make significant progress by first introducing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) framework to approximate the intractable oracle policy. This ALP approach is then cleverly combined with an Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) method to create the UCB-ALP algorithm for the more practical setting where expected rewards are unknown. The central claim is that this algorithm achieves O(log T) regret, which is a substantial improvement over the O(√T) regret bounds found in related, more general work.

The primary strength of this paper is its significant and novel theoretical contribution. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to establish a logarithmic regret bound for the constrained contextual bandit problem, which represents a clear advance in the state of the art. The introduction of the adaptive LP approximation is an elegant and effective technique that makes the problem theoretically tractable. The paper is of high quality: it is clearly written, well-organized, and thoroughly contextualizes its contributions with respect to prior literature. The theoretical analysis appears sound and the proof sketches are convincing.

The main weakness of the paper is the complete absence of experimental validation. The analysis relies on simplifying assumptions—finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint—which, while reasonable for a theoretical study, makes empirical evaluation all the more important. Without experiments, it is difficult to assess the practical performance of UCB-ALP, particularly the constants hidden in the O(log T) notation or the real-world impact of the "boundary cases" where the regret degrades to O(√T). The extensions discussed in Sections 5 and 6 feel preliminary without any empirical support.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a high-quality theoretical contribution that is both original and significant. The achievement of logarithmic regret is a noteworthy result for the community. While the lack of experiments is a clear limitation, the novelty and importance of the theoretical findings are sufficient to recommend acceptance. The authors are strongly encouraged to include an experimental section in the final version to demonstrate the practical implications of their algorithm.
