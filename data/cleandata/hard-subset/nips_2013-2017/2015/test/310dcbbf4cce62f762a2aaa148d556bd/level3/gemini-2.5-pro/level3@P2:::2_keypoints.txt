- Studies contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B, a practical extension relevant to crowdsourcing and clinical trials.  
- Key challenge: budget/time coupling makes the oracle policy an intractable dynamic program.  
- Simplifies to finite discrete contexts, known context distribution, and unit costs to derive insights.  
- Proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) using an LP relaxation with budget adapted by remaining budget/time ratio.  
- ALP approximates the oracle effectively, achieving O(1) regret in most known-statistics cases.  
- Extends to unknown rewards with UCB-ALP, combining UCB exploration with ALP decision-making.  
- Main theoretical result: UCB-ALP attains O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases.  
- This O(log T) improves on prior O(√T) results (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al.) and is claimed to be novel.  
- Strength: significant, original theoretical advance demonstrating adaptive policies can beat √T regret.  
- Strength: strong technical analysis decomposing regret and showing ALP preserves budget concentration.  
- Strength: paper is very well-written, clearly motivating and positioning contributions.  
- Weakness: no experimental results to empirically validate theoretical claims or illustrate boundary vs non-boundary behavior.  
- Weakness: analysis relies on simplifying assumptions (finite contexts, single budget, fixed costs) limiting direct applicability.  
- Weakness: extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are only briefly sketched and deferred to supplementary material.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — theoretical contribution is strong and novel despite lack of experiments.
