This paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints. The main ideas are: (i) approximate the dynamic-programming oracle with a static LP that enforces an average budget ρ = B/T, and an adaptive variant (ALP) that replaces ρ by the instantaneous remaining-average bτ/τ; (ii) prove that ALP is near-optimal (O(1) regret except at measure-zero “boundary” cases where O(√T) holds); (iii) combine ALP with UCB to estimate rewards online (UCB-ALP), yielding O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on the boundary. The work relates to Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], Bandits with Knapsacks [12–16], and concave rewards/convex knapsack contextual bandits [20–22]. Compared to RCB/Mixture Elimination (O(√T) and high complexity), the claimed novelty is the adaptive LP relaxation that enables instance-dependent, logarithmic regret for finite contexts/actions with unit or heterogeneous costs.

Strengths
- Clear modeling of budget-constrained contextual bandits with hard constraints and finite contexts/actions; useful for applications (clinical trials, recommendation, wireless rate selection).
- ALP is conceptually simple, computationally efficient, and achieves a tight O(1) gap from the oracle in non-boundary regimes; the concentration-of-budget (hypergeometric) argument is neat.
- UCB-ALP leverages the fact that ALP only needs reward orderings, enabling a clean separation of estimation and allocation; the regret decomposition is well motivated.
- Extensions to unknown context distribution (via empirical estimates and truncation) and to heterogeneous costs suggest robustness of the approach.

Weaknesses and questions
- Coupling and independence across contexts: The introduction claims that for large horizons the coupling “vanishes.” Yet the hard budget imposes global coupling across contexts at all times. Please formalize which sense of decoupling you mean: is it that bτ/τ concentrates around ρ so the LP threshold is correct with high probability, reducing cross-context interference to exponentially rare events? If so, make this precise early and clarify why this does not reduce the problem to J independent bandits (each context) plus a shared budget controller. Relatedly, please explain precisely why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as an independent bandit instance with a global average-budget throttle; which part of the regret analysis uses cross-context dependencies critically?
- Novelty vs. reductions: With finite J, K and fixed ρ, your problem can be viewed as a “sleeping” JK-armed bandit with global resource constraints and exogenous availability (contexts). The key novelty is the adaptive LP that achieves O(1) oracle-approximation plus the observation that only reward rankings are needed. Please sharpen the comparison to [20–22] and BwK [12–16]: what structural property or analysis trick allows you to go from O(√T) to O(log T)? Is it solely the adaptive bτ/τ rebalancing? If so, could similar adaptation be grafted onto prior BwK approaches?
- Problem-dependent upper bounds: The main theorems state O(JK log T). It would be more informative to give gap-dependent bounds. For example, express the estimation term as sum over suboptimal context-actions (j,k ≠ k*): O(Σ (log T)/Δj,k) with Δj,k = u*j − uj,k, and isolate any additional cross-context terms that depend on the “threshold gap” Δthresh = u*_{j̃(ρ)} − u*_{j̃(ρ)+1} and the slack δ = min{ρ − q_{j̃(ρ)}, q_{j̃(ρ)+1} − ρ}. Right now the KJ factor looks loose; the supplementary bound would benefit from reworking into a clean, instance-dependent form.
- Lower bounds: Please provide matching lower bounds. Problem-dependent: can you show Ω(Σ (log T)/Δj,k) (plus any necessary threshold terms) even with budgets? Problem-independent: show Ω(√T) at the boundary, and clarify whether any dependence on J and K is necessary in non-boundary regimes. Nontrivial lower bounds that incorporate the context process (beyond trivial reductions to standard bandits) would strengthen the contribution.
- Theorem 2 scaling: You state O(√T + JK log T) at the boundary. Is the √T term truly independent of K and J (up to constants), or should it scale like √(K T) (or with J) as in problem-independent bandit bounds? Please clarify the source of the √T term and whether its constant depends on the margin δ′ or the threshold gap.
- Proof technique and ordering events: The analysis via ordering events Erank,s is intuitive, but may hide looseness. Can you replace the O(JK log T) counting with per-pair gap-based counts via Lemma 3, leading to Σ (log T)/Δj,k? Also, does the ALP threshold structure eliminate contributions from most mis-rankings except those crossing the threshold? If yes, an explicit bound that isolates only “threshold-crossing” mistakes could be much tighter.
- Boundary cases: While measure-zero in a continuous parameterization, they may occur frequently in discrete settings (e.g., rational ρ or ties in cumulative π). Please discuss practical mitigation (e.g., randomizing ρ slightly, budget smoothing) and whether high-probability concentration bounds can replace expectation bounds.
- Unknown context distribution: The truncated EALP analysis is interesting; please move a concrete, finite-time guarantee (with explicit constants/parameters) into the main text. Also discuss the computational complexity of the heterogeneous-cost LP (per-round sorting vs full LP), and whether you can avoid a separate ε-first phase by using UCB-type confidence sets within the heterogeneous ALP.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the concentration arguments and regret decomposition are plausible. However, the current bounds are mostly problem-independent and may be loose; adding gap-dependent upper bounds and lower bounds would improve rigor.

Clarity
- Generally well written. I recommend an upfront “decoupling via concentration” lemma and a clearer comparison to prior BwK/RCB work explaining why adaptive LP achieves logarithmic regret. Move key parts of Section 5’s guarantees into the main paper.

Originality
- The adaptive-LP design and its use to get O(1) oracle-approximation plus O(log T) online regret appear new in this constrained contextual setting. That said, the result may feel unsurprising given reductions to JK-armed sleeping bandits with budgets. Strengthening novelty claims via tighter bounds and lower bounds would help.

Significance
- If tightened, the results would be useful: a simple, efficient algorithm with near-oracle performance and logarithmic regret under budgets is valuable for practical systems.

Pros
- Simple, computationally efficient algorithm (ALP/UCB-ALP)
- Near-oracle performance known-stats; logarithmic regret unknown-stats
- Clear decomposition and useful concentration insights
- Extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs

Cons
- Novelty relative to reductions and prior BwK/RCB not fully convincing
- Bounds are coarse (JK log T); lack of gap-dependent bounds and matching lower bounds
- Boundary-case treatment and scaling in Theorem 2 need clarification
- Limited empirical validation in the main paper

Recommendation
- Neutral at this stage. I encourage the authors to address the novelty positioning, provide gap-dependent upper bounds and meaningful lower bounds, clarify Theorem 2’s scaling, and strengthen the unknown-context and heterogeneous-cost analyses in the main text.
