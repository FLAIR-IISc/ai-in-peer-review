

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, where the agent faces both budget and time constraints while making decisions based on observed contexts. The authors make a significant contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting, which is the first work to do so. Previous work on budget-constrained bandits [11-16] focused on non-contextual settings, while Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] achieved O(√T) regret but with restricted benchmarks and high computational complexity. The paper's key insight is using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) to approximate the oracle solution, which requires only the ordering of expected rewards rather than their exact values. This enables combining with UCB for unknown statistics, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm that achieves O(log T) regret except for boundary cases.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The problem formulation is well-defined, and the authors systematically build from unit-cost systems to more general cases. The ALP algorithm and its regret analysis are particularly elegant, with Lemma 2 providing crucial concentration properties for the remaining budget. The connection between ALP and LP relaxation is well-motivated, and the paper effectively demonstrates how the adaptive nature of ALP addresses the key challenge of budget-time coupling.

The technical quality is strong. The authors provide rigorous regret bounds with careful analysis of multiple interacting factors, including action/context ranking errors and remaining budget fluctuations. The proof sketches are sufficiently detailed to convince the reader of the correctness of the results. The paper also appropriately references related work, including the recently submitted arXiv:1506.03374 which extends previous work [20] to dynamic contexts but achieves only O(√T) regret.

The originality is substantial. While linear programming has been used in bandit problems [19], the paper's specific application to constrained contextual bandits with adaptive budget allocation represents a novel approach. The ALP algorithm's property of only requiring reward orderings rather than exact values is particularly innovative and enables the combination with UCB. This differs significantly from prior approaches that either focused on non-contextual settings or achieved suboptimal regret bounds.

The significance of this work is high. Constrained contextual bandits model important real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing where both budget and time constraints are critical. The paper's algorithm is computationally efficient and achieves near-optimal regret, providing a practical solution to a challenging problem. The results shed light on addressing the open problem of general constrained contextual bandits, with potential for further extensions.

Several suggestions could strengthen the paper further. The authors should explicitly state how UCB-ALP leverages the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle (as in UCRL2 [8]) to justify combining UCB with ALP in Section 4.1. While the paper mentions parametric reward models as a scalability consideration, including a brief discussion of how these could be integrated would be valuable. Adding numerical experiments would provide concrete evidence of the algorithm's performance, especially for the boundary cases where regret increases to O(√T). The authors should also reference the recent arXiv:1506.03374 paper, which addresses similar problems with different assumptions.

The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of computational complexity. While the authors claim UCB-ALP is "computationally efficient," they don't provide concrete complexity analysis for the LP solving step, which could become prohibitive with large context sets. However, this is a minor concern given the paper's focus on regret bounds.

The paper is well-written and accessible to NIPS readers. The organization flows logically from problem definition to solution to analysis, and the mathematical presentation is clear. The key contribution is the novel ALP approximation and its combination with UCB, which achieves logarithmic regret where prior work could only achieve O(√T).

Pro arguments for acceptance: First, this is the first work to show how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. Second, the ALP approach provides a computationally efficient solution that tolerates estimation errors. Third, the results are theoretically sound with rigorous regret analysis. Fourth, the paper has significant practical implications for applications with budget constraints.

Con arguments against acceptance: The boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T) are not fully resolved, and the computational complexity of the LP steps could be a practical limitation for large-scale problems. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's overall contribution.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the bandit literature. The authors successfully address a challenging problem with a novel approach, providing both theoretical guarantees and practical insights. The paper merits acceptance at NIPS.
