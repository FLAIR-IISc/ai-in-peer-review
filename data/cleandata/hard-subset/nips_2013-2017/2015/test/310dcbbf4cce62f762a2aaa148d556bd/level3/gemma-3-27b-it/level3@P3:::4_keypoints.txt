- Tackles constrained contextual bandits and achieves logarithmic regret, improving over prior sublinear bounds.
- Core idea: approximate the oracle with Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) and combine it with UCB—both interesting and effective.
- Rigorous theoretical analysis that carefully handles complex sources of regret.
- Extends method to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, broadening applicability.
- Well-motivated and clearly situated within contextual bandit and resource-constrained literature.
- Good exposition: starts with known-statistics case, develops ALP intuition, then extends to UCB for unknowns.
- Distinguishes and analyzes boundary cases, separating logarithmic from sublinear regret regimes.
- Notable novelty: first to obtain logarithmic regret in this constrained setting compared to prior √T or weaker baselines.
- Practical advantage: algorithm only needs ordering of expected rewards, useful when exact estimates are hard.
- Comparison to stronger baselines shows UCB-ALP outperforms simple skip-based strategies under budget constraints.
- Weakness: initial focus on unit costs is restrictive; heterogeneous-cost extension is secondary and less developed.
- Weakness: discussion and intuition for boundary cases (why ρ = qj matters) are dense and could be clearer.
- Weakness: brief mention of a concentration property under adaptive algorithms needs more elaboration and significance.
- Overall recommendation: accept—novel, technically sound, well-written work with minor areas for improvement.
