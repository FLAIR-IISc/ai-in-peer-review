- Paper tackles constrained contextual bandits and proposes UCB-ALP, combining Adaptive Linear Programming with UCB to handle unknown rewards.
- UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret in most cases, with √T regret in boundary cases — claimed first to obtain logarithmic regret for this problem.

Strengths:
- Novel ALP-based framework and the UCB-ALP combination are original and address a clear gap in the literature.
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous with clear derivations of regret bounds and explicit treatment of boundary cases.
- Algorithms are computationally efficient and relevant to practical applications like clinical trials and recommender systems.
- Clear distinction between non-boundary (logarithmic regret) and boundary (√T regret) regimes.
- Method is extended to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, showing adaptability.

Weaknesses:
- Analysis of boundary cases is limited; unclear if √T regret can be improved with refinements.
- Empirical validation is lacking; no simulations comparing UCB-ALP to existing methods.
- Presentation is dense and may be hard for readers unfamiliar with constrained contextual bandits.
- Some sections assume known context distributions, which may limit applicability despite partial addressing in Section 5.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Significant theoretical advance achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.
- Computationally efficient algorithms with clear practical applications.
- Robust theoretical treatment and extensions to more general settings.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing empirical results leaves practical performance unverified.
- √T regret in boundary cases remains an unresolved limitation.

Recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance, contingent on encouraging authors to add empirical results and further investigate boundary-case regret in revisions.
