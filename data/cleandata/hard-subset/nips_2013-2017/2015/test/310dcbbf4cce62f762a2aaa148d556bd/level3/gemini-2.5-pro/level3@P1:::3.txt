### **Summary**

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon T and a budget B. In this setting, an agent sequentially observes contexts, chooses an action (which has an associated cost and reward), and aims to maximize total reward before T or B is exhausted. The key challenge is that the budget constraint couples decisions across time, making the standard exploration-exploitation tradeoff much more complex.

The authors' main contribution is a new algorithm, UCB-ALP, which achieves O(log T) regret for a simplified but important version of the problem: unit costs and a known context arrival distribution. This is a significant improvement over prior work on budgeted bandits (e.g., [17]), which typically achieves O(sqrt(T)) regret. The core of their approach is an elegant approximation of the computationally intractable optimal policy (oracle). They first analyze the known-parameter case and propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. ALP approximates the optimal dynamic program by solving a simple LP at each step, using the ratio of remaining budget to remaining time as the resource constraint. This approximation is shown to have O(1) regret in most cases. For the online setting with unknown rewards, ALP is combined with the Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) principle, leading to the UCB-ALP algorithm. The paper also discusses extensions to the more general cases of unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to establish a logarithmic regret bound for a constrained contextual bandit problem. This is a substantial theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art from the O(sqrt(T)) regret bounds common in the literature. The problem itself is well-motivated and has clear applications in areas like crowdsourcing and clinical trials.

2.  **Technical Quality and Method:** The paper's core idea—approximating a complex DP with an adaptive LP—is both simple and powerful. The ALP algorithm is intuitive, and its analysis, which connects the budget evolution to sampling without replacement and uses concentration bounds, is technically sound. The subsequent combination with UCB to handle unknown rewards is a natural and effective way to bridge the gap between the offline and online settings. The analysis carefully dissects the sources of regret, providing a clear and convincing argument.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It builds its case logically, starting from the oracle, moving to the ALP approximation for the known-parameter case, and finally presenting the UCB-ALP algorithm for the bandit setting. This clear progression makes the technical contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Conditional Logarithmic Guarantee:** The O(log T) regret bound holds only for "non-boundary" cases, where the budget-to-time ratio ρ does not align perfectly with the cumulative probabilities of the best contexts. In the "boundary" cases, the regret reverts to O(sqrt(T)). While this is likely an inherent feature of the problem (and the O(sqrt(T)) rate is likely optimal in those instances), it does mean the headline result is not universal. A discussion of the practical implications of these boundary cases would be welcome. However, this is a minor weakness, as the characterization itself is a valuable contribution.

### **Questions and Suggestions for Authors**

The paper is very strong, but I have a few questions and suggestions that could further improve it:

*   **Complexity of the Oracle:** The paper motivates the LP approximation by stating that the exact DP solution is "computationally intractable." Could the authors formalize this? Is computing the optimal policy NP-hard? Establishing hardness would provide a stronger justification for the approximation approach.

*   **Assumption in the Unknown Distribution Case:** In Section 5 (Unknown Context Distribution), it seems the assumption u\*_1 > u\*_2 > ... > u\*_J is implicitly carried over. However, if the context distribution π is unknown, the agent would not know the identities of the contexts a priori to order them in this way. The algorithm would need to learn both the context probabilities *and* the ordering of their optimal rewards. Could you please clarify how this is handled? This seems to add a significant challenge that is not fully addressed in the current text.

*   **Extension to Unknown Horizon:** The current analysis relies on a known time horizon T for calculating the adaptive budget constraint (bτ/τ). What are the prospects for extending this framework to settings with an unknown or infinite horizon?

*   **Intuition for Lemma 1:** The proof of Lemma 1, which establishes that the static LP provides an upper bound on the oracle's performance, is critical to the entire analysis. While the proof in the appendix seems correct, the result is not immediately obvious. Adding a sentence or two of high-level intuition in the main text about why this relaxation provides a valid upper bound would be helpful for readers.

### **Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on bandit problems. The development of an algorithm with logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits is a novel and important result. The methodology is clever and elegant, and the paper is presented with outstanding clarity. Despite the minor limitation of the non-universal log-regret guarantee, the work is a clear advance for the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
