This paper studies constrained contextual bandits with discrete contexts/actions and a hard budget over a finite horizon. The key idea is to approximate the dynamic programming oracle with a linear program that imposes an average budget constraint. In the known-statistics case (unit costs, known context distribution), the authors propose an Adaptive Linear Program (ALP) that optimizes at each round for the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. They show ALP attains O(1) regret except on boundary parameter values (ρ equal to a cumulative context mass), where regret is O(√T). For unknown expected rewards, they combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP), proving O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on boundaries. They further discuss extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates and a truncated EALP analysis) and heterogeneous costs (generalized LP with a single-action-per-context constraint, plus an exploration-first variant for unknown rewards). Relative to prior work, this improves over the O(√T) regret of Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Mixture Elimination) by exploiting discrete contexts and the ALP structure, and is computationally lightweight compared to DP-based or policy-elimination approaches.

Relation to prior work: The paper carefully situates itself among bandits with knapsacks/budgets (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., Slivkins, Combes et al.), concave rewards with convex knapsacks (Agrawal and Devanur), and RCB (Badanidiyuru, Langford, Slivkins). It also clarifies that UCRL-style methods apply to Markovian contexts but are not directly budget-aware in this setting. The main novelty is showing logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits with random context arrivals, via an adaptive LP that only needs reward orderings and enjoys concentration of the budget process (hypergeometric/sampling-without-replacement view).

Quality: The technical development is sound and well-argued. The LP upper bound Û(T,B) is a clean benchmark; the ALP analysis via concentration of bτ and the threshold structure is elegant; and the regret decomposition for UCB-ALP isolates action-ranking vs. context-ranking errors. Handling unknown context distributions via a truncated empirical estimate and bounded averaged differences is a thoughtful treatment of dependence issues. Assumptions and boundary cases are clearly stated. I would have liked explicit constants and a short complexity analysis for solving LPτ,b per step.

Clarity: Very well written and easy to follow. The narrative from oracle approximation to learning is compelling. Some notational glitches in Eq. (4) could be cleaned for readability. The role of “boundary cases” could be more explicitly highlighted early, with guidance on practical mitigation.

Originality: Combining ALP with learning to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits with random contexts appears novel. The mapping of budget evolution to sampling without replacement for concentration is a nice technical device. The insight that ALP needs only reward orderings is leveraged effectively.

Significance: The results are important for online decision-making with budgets (ads, clinical trials, crowdsourcing). The algorithms are computationally light, and the regret guarantees improve the state of the art under the considered assumptions. The work should be of broad interest to the online learning community.

Pros:
- Clear problem formulation and motivation; in-scope for NIPS.
- Elegant ALP approximation with tight (O(1)/O(√T)) regret to the oracle.
- UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases; analysis is careful and instance-dependent.
- Computational efficiency: per-round LP is simple; ALP only needs reward orderings.
- Extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs, with reasonable analyses.

Cons:
- No experiments; empirical validation would strengthen the case and illustrate boundary vs. non-boundary behavior.
- The boundary regime remains at O(√T); practical strategies to avoid or detect boundaries could be discussed.
- Scalability when J, K are large is not fully addressed; discrete tabular treatment may be limiting without structure.
- For heterogeneous costs, the exploration-first approach can be suboptimal relative to fully adaptive UCB-style learning; no regret guarantee beyond the presented scaling is given.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Section 4.1: Explicitly articulate the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle behind UCB-ALP and relate the ALP-based policy updates to the optimistic policy evaluations in UCRL2 (but adapted to budgeted settings).
- Provide a brief complexity analysis per round (sorting û∗j, solving LPτ,b), and discuss numerical stability and tie-handling.
- Discuss mitigation of boundary cases: e.g., introduce a small slack/regularization on ρ, randomized tie-breaking, or adaptive throttling to keep bτ/τ away from qj.
- Consider parametric or structured reward models (e.g., linear contextual bandits) to improve scalability when J is large; relate to [21,22].
- Add numerical experiments (even in an extended version): synthetic setups to show concentration of bτ, comparisons to Mixture Elimination (RCB), Bandits with Knapsacks, and “fixed-ρ” UCB baselines; include unknown context distribution and heterogeneous-cost scenarios.
- The related work section already cites arXiv:1506.03374; strengthen the comparison by clarifying why static-context methods incur √T regret in your random-arrival setting and how adaptivity in ALP closes the gap.
- Clarify how to choose the truncation time for UCB-EALP with unknown context distribution and the resulting finite-time constants.

Minor:
- Tighten notation in Eq. (4) to avoid ambiguity; ensure the piecewise definition is clear.
- Move the core concentration lemma (Lemma 2) proof sketch into the main text if space permits.

Recommendation: Accept. This paper presents a computationally efficient approach with strong theoretical guarantees that advances our understanding of constrained contextual bandits, and it is clearly written. My confidence is high.
