## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of contextual bandits under both budget and time constraints, a realistic scenario often overlooked in standard bandit literature. The authors make a significant contribution by providing the first algorithms achieving logarithmic regret in this setting, under reasonable simplifying assumptions of finite contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint. The core idea is an adaptive linear programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle, combined with a UCB approach for unknown rewards. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and thoroughly references relevant work, including connections to resource-constrained bandits and prior work on UCB algorithms. The theoretical analysis appears solid, and the proofs, while not fully checked by this reviewer, seem convincing in their structure and approach. The introduction effectively positions the problem within the broader bandit framework and highlights the limitations of existing solutions when constraints are present. The use of LP relaxation and adaptation to the remaining budget is a clever technique that allows for tractable analysis and algorithm design.

However, a significant weakness lies in the limited practical evaluation. While Sections 5 and 6 extend the results to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, these extensions are presented with less detail, and crucially, the paper lacks any experimental results. Demonstrating the performance of the proposed algorithms, even on simulated data, would substantially strengthen the contribution and provide valuable insights into their practical applicability. The boundary cases where logarithmic regret doesn’t hold are also mentioned but could benefit from further discussion regarding their prevalence and potential mitigation strategies. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents the first logarithmic regret algorithms for constrained contextual bandits, a significant theoretical advance.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and well-supported.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is important and relevant to many real-world applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of experimental results is a major drawback.
*   **Limited Practical Discussion:** The practical implications and potential challenges of implementing the algorithms are not thoroughly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper with strong theoretical contributions. The simplifying assumptions are justified and allow for a significant advance in our understanding of constrained contextual bandits. However, the lack of experimental validation prevents a higher assessment. I recommend acceptance with the strong encouragement that the authors include experimental results in a future version. Addressing the boundary cases with more discussion would also be beneficial.
