This paper addresses the challenging problem of contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a setting that significantly complicates the exploration-exploitation tradeoff. The authors make a strong contribution by presenting algorithms achieving logarithmic regret under certain conditions – a notable improvement over existing work that typically achieves sublinear, but slower, rates. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the technical approach appears sound. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Summary:**

The paper introduces constrained contextual bandits, where each action incurs a cost and a total budget limits the number of actions that can be taken. The authors first analyze a simplified case with known context distributions and unit costs, developing an “Adaptive Linear Programming” (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle policy and achieves near-optimality.  They then extend this approach to the more general case of unknown rewards using an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) combination (UCB-ALP), demonstrating logarithmic regret (except for certain boundary cases).  Finally, they generalize the approach to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.  

This work builds upon existing contextual bandit literature [1, 2, 3] and multi-armed bandit work with budget constraints [11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16], but crucially adapts these ideas to the contextual setting.  The authors explicitly point out the inapplicability of previous results on budgeted bandits to the contextual case, and differentiate their work from more general resource allocation frameworks [17] by focusing on a simplified, more tractable model.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** This appears to be the first work demonstrating logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, a significant advance. The authors clearly articulate this contribution and contrast it with existing literature.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithms (ALP and UCB-ALP) appear well-designed and the analysis is thorough. The use of linear programming to approximate the oracle and the subsequent combination with UCB are clever.  The handling of boundary cases is also commendable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the problem is well-defined, and the technical details are presented in a logical and accessible manner.
* **Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to many real-world applications, such as clinical trials [10], online advertising, and resource allocation in crowdsourcing [9].  An algorithm achieving logarithmic regret represents a substantial improvement over existing approaches, potentially enabling practical application of contextual bandits in resource-constrained settings.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors consider both known and unknown context distributions, and both unit and heterogeneous costs, demonstrating the generality of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Boundary Cases:** The logarithmic regret guarantee does *not* hold for all parameter settings. The existence of boundary cases where the regret degrades to O(√T) is a limitation, although the authors acknowledge this and provide some characterization of these cases.  Strengthening the guarantees to cover these cases would significantly enhance the impact of the work.
* **Model Assumptions:** The assumption that `u*1 > u*2 > ... > u*J` in the unknown context distribution case, while simplifying the analysis, feels somewhat restrictive. It's not clear how sensitive the results are to relaxing this assumption, and addressing this would improve the generality of the work.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper emphasizes computational efficiency, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of solving the LP problems involved would be valuable, particularly for large action and context spaces. Is computing the exact optimal action sequence NP-hard, as hinted in the key points?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a challenging and important problem.
* The proposed algorithms achieve state-of-the-art performance.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
* The technical analysis is rigorous and convincing.
* The results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The logarithmic regret guarantee is not universal and degrades in boundary cases.
* The model assumption `u*1 > u*2 > ... > u*J` could be limiting.
* A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its limitations. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits by developing algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret in a challenging and practically relevant setting. The clarity of presentation and the rigor of the analysis are particularly commendable. I believe this paper is of high quality and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I therefore strongly recommend acceptance.
