

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a significant extension of traditional contextual bandit problems that captures important characteristics of real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors propose a novel approach using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) to approximate the oracle solution and combine it with UCB methods for the unknown statistics case. This work addresses a gap in the literature where previous budgeted bandit approaches (e.g., [11-16]) do not handle observable contexts, while Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] achieves only O(√T) regret with a restricted benchmark.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, for unit-cost systems with known statistics, the authors introduce ALP, which achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases) by dynamically adjusting the budget constraint according to the remaining time and budget. This is a significant improvement over previous approaches that either require complex dynamic programming solutions or fail to provide tight regret bounds. Second, the authors combine ALP with UCB to create UCB-ALP, which achieves O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases (ρ ∉ {q₁, ..., q_{J-1}}), where q_j = ∑_{j'=1}^j π_{j'}. This is the first work to demonstrate logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, resolving an open problem mentioned in [17]. Third, the authors extend their results to systems with unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, providing a comprehensive framework for constrained contextual bandits.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors carefully characterize the regret of their algorithms through multiple steps: they establish an upper bound using static LP, analyze the ALP algorithm's performance through concentration properties of the remaining budget, and then bound the regret of UCB-ALP by separating action ranking errors from context ranking errors. The proofs are well-structured, and the paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the analysis. The authors also appropriately address limitations, noting that boundary cases (where ρ equals certain cumulative probabilities) require O(√T) regret, which is an important caveat.

The paper is exceptionally clear in its exposition. The authors provide intuitive explanations of the key insights (e.g., the connection between ALP and "sampling without replacement"), and the algorithm descriptions are precise. The organization flows logically from problem formulation to analysis to extensions, with well-motivated technical sections. The supplementary material appears to contain the necessary details for full reproducibility.

The originality of the work is strong. While some elements (like using LP to approximate dynamic programming) appear in related work [17, 19], the authors' specific design and analysis of ALP for constrained contextual bandits is novel. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from prior work, particularly [17] which achieves O(√T) regret with a restricted benchmark, and [20] which achieves O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts. The authors' insight that ALP only requires the ordering of expected rewards (rather than their exact values) is particularly clever and enables the combination with UCB methods.

The significance of this work is substantial. Constrained contextual bandits model important real-world scenarios where both time and budget constraints exist, yet no efficient algorithms with logarithmic regret existed prior to this work. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient and achieves near-optimal performance in most cases. This work provides a foundation for addressing the open problem of general constrained contextual bandits mentioned in [17] and [20].

The main weakness of the paper is the handling of boundary cases. While the authors acknowledge that ρ = q_j for some j leads to different regret bounds, they do not sufficiently explain why these cases are special beyond the mathematical definition. The reviewer questions why boundary cases (particularly for j > 1) should be treated differently, as the paper's analysis suggests the difference is merely technical rather than conceptual. A more intuitive explanation of why these points represent "boundaries" in the problem structure would strengthen the paper.

Another minor point is that the paper compares its algorithm to a baseline that uses adaptive LP to skip a fixed percentage of suboptimal rounds. While this is a valid comparison to the oracle, the paper could better clarify how this benchmark relates to the actual problem constraints and whether it represents a reasonable upper bound for the problem.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the contextual bandit literature. The technical quality is high, the problem is important, and the solution is both novel and practically useful. The paper's main weakness is the somewhat technical treatment of boundary cases, but this does not undermine the overall contribution. The work is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing a fundamental problem in sequential decision making with important applications.

Pro: Novel problem formulation, elegant solution using ALP, strong theoretical results with O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases, comprehensive analysis including generalizations to unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs.

Con: Boundary cases handled in a technically precise but not intuitively clear manner, minor clarification needed on the benchmark comparison.

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field with clear theoretical insights and practical algorithms that advance the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits.
