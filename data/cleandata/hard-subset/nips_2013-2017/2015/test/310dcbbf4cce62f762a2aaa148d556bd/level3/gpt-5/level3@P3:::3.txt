Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits with both a hard budget B and a known horizon T. At each round a context j is drawn i.i.d. from a finite distribution π; the learner can either skip (action 0) or choose a costly action k > 0 that incurs known cost c_{j,k} and yields a stochastic reward in [0,1]. The objective is to maximize total expected reward before either time or budget runs out. The key benchmark is an oracle policy with full knowledge of π, rewards, and costs; regret is measured against this oracle.

The main contribution is a simple, computationally efficient approximation of the oracle via an adaptive linear program (ALP) that replaces the hard budget by an average budget constraint, but adapts the constraint online to the remaining average budget b/τ. In the special case with known π and unit costs (c_{j,k}=1), ALP is shown to be near-optimal (O(1) regret) except at measure-zero “boundary” cases; combining ALP with UCB estimation (UCB-ALP) yields O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) otherwise. The approach is then extended to unknown π (via empirical estimates and a truncated-analysis variant) and to heterogeneous costs, where analogous guarantees are established.

This is positioned against prior work on budgeted bandits and resourceful contextual bandits. Notably, Mixture Elimination for resourceful contextual bandits [17] achieves O(√T) regret under more general settings but with higher complexity and different regret definition. Works on bandits with knapsacks or concave rewards and convex constraints [11–16, 20–22] also achieve O(√T). To the best of my knowledge, this is the first to obtain O(log T) regret in contextual bandits with hard budget constraints under the stated (finite) setting, which is a meaningful advance.

Strengths
- Methodological simplicity and insight: Approximating the DP oracle by an average-budget LP and then adapting the LP to the remaining budget is elegant and intuitive. The “only ordering needed” property is particularly useful for learning.
- Theoretical guarantees: ALP achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary cases; UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in the same regime (and O(√T + JK log T) at boundaries). The analysis carefully separates regret due to action-ranking errors, context-ranking errors, and remaining-budget fluctuations, using hypergeometric concentration for bτ.
- Novelty: Prior contextual works with budget-like constraints provide O(√T) regret; achieving O(log T) under a natural finite setting is new and important.
- Extensions: The paper goes beyond the clean unit-cost/known-π case to tackle unknown π (EALP/UCB-EALP with a truncated empirical estimate) and heterogeneous costs (generalized ALP and an exploration-then-exploitation variant).
- Writing and motivation: The paper is very clearly written, well organized, and well motivated with examples (clinical trials, crowdsourcing, wireless rate selection).

Weaknesses and limitations
- The O(log T) rate requires a mild non-boundary condition (ρ ≠ cumulative mass qj). At boundary instances, an Ω(√T) lower bound applies, and the algorithm matches this up to logs. While this is reasonable, stronger universal guarantees (or adaptive detection of boundary regimes) would further raise the paper’s impact.
- Reliance on Lemma 1: The results lean heavily on an LP upper bound that assumes the existence of an optimal sequence satisfying the averaged constraint. I initially questioned whether an optimal sequence satisfying the LP-average constraint always exists; the rebuttal clarified this point and I am now convinced the lemma and its use as a benchmark are correct.
- Some extensions (unknown π, heterogeneous costs) defer important details to the supplement. While standard at NIPS, a brief in-paper roadmap of key technical steps would help.

Quality
The technical development is solid, with carefully stated assumptions, clean decompositions, and concentration arguments tailored to the adaptive setting. The regret rates and their dependence on J and K are plausible and well justified. The empirical section (as indicated) aligns with theory. Proofs that hinge on concentration of the remaining budget and correctness of rankings under UCB are standard but well adapted here.

Clarity
Excellently written and easy to follow. The ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms are described clearly, with helpful intuition. The boundary/non-boundary distinction is made explicit. Notation is consistent.

Originality
High. The adaptive LP surrogate, its near-optimality, and its combination with UCB to secure logarithmic regret in this budgeted contextual setting appear new.

Significance
High. Budget constraints are ubiquitous. A simple, computationally light algorithm with tight regret in many cases will likely be adopted and extended. The analysis techniques (e.g., mapping budget evolution to sampling without replacement) could inform future work on constrained online learning.

Technical concerns and questions
- Exact oracle complexity: Is computing the exact optimal action sequence (for finite T,B with known π and rewards/costs) NP-hard, or merely pseudo-polynomial via DP? A formal complexity statement would contextualize the need for LP approximation.
- Unknown horizon T: Can ALP/UCB-ALP be adapted to unknown or variable horizons? A standard doubling trick should preserve rates, but here ρ=B/T matters. If only B is known, can one maintain decisions based on remaining b/τ without knowing the final T, and what regret guarantees would follow under anytime stopping?
- Assumption u*1 > u*2 > … > u*J: In the unknown π case, can one still assume a strict ordering w.l.o.g. by tie-breaking, and does tie-breaking interact with estimation of π in EALP/UCB-EALP? Clarifying this would help reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- First O(log T) regret for this constrained contextual setting under mild and explicit conditions.
- Simple, insightful method with rigorous analysis and practical extensions.
- Clear writing and strong motivation.
- Potentially broad impact for constrained online learning.

Cons for acceptance
- O(log T) requires non-boundary condition; at boundaries the regret degrades.
- Some dependence on a key lemma and on supplemental proofs for extensions.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper is clear, original, technically sound, and significant. It advances the state of the art for budgeted contextual bandits with a simple and elegant approach and will be of broad interest to the NIPS community.
