This paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints and proposes an adaptive linear programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle (known statistics) and a learning algorithm UCB-ALP (unknown statistics). The idea is to relax the hard budget into an average constraint and solve a linear program that decides, per context, the probability of acting versus skipping. ALP adapts this LP to the remaining time τ and remaining budget bτ by using the instantaneous average budget bτ/τ; with known rewards it achieves O(1) regret except in boundary cases. Combining ALP with UCB yields UCB-ALP, which attains O(JK log T) regret (resp. O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases), with J contexts and K actions. The analysis relies on mapping budget consumption under ALP/UCB-ALP to sampling without replacement, yielding concentration of bτ. The paper relates to RCB/Mixture Elimination (O(√T) regret, finite policy set benchmark) and to “bandits with concave rewards and convex knapsacks,” extending the latter’s static budgeting to adaptive pacing under random contexts to obtain logarithmic regret. Extensions to unknown context distribution (via empirical estimates) and heterogeneous costs are sketched, with details in the supplement.

Relation to prior work: The paper targets a setting stricter than classical contextual bandits (adds a hard budget) and different from RCB: finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, single budget. Compared to Mixture Elimination (O(√T) regret, finite-policy benchmark, high complexity), it gives instance-dependent, oracle-based guarantees with much simpler computation. Compared to knapsack-style bandits and convex-constraints contextual bandits (which achieve O(√T) with static pacing), the adaptive LP pacing is the key algorithmic novelty enabling O(log T) regret.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Adaptive LP pacing decouples learning and resource allocation. The “only ordering matters” property is insightful and practically useful.
- Theoretical contribution: First logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits (non-boundary), with clean, interpretable dependence O(JK log T).
- Analysis technique: The sampling-without-replacement view of budget evolution and hypergeometric concentration is elegant and broadly reusable.
- Computational efficiency: Solving the ALP per round is simple for unit-cost (threshold structure) and remains tractable in heterogeneous costs (small LP with one action-per-context constraint).

Weaknesses and questions
- Boundary cases: Why is ρ = qj special, especially for j > 1? Intuitively, v(ρ) is kinked at cumulative mass qj; bτ/τ fluctuates around ρ with variance Θ(1/τ), so even vanishingly small deviations cross the threshold, flipping the identity of the “marginal” context and causing Θ(1) loss per fluctuation. Summed over τ, this yields Θ(√T) regret (via CLT-scale fluctuations). This discontinuity persists for any j where a fractionally served marginal context exists; hence all qj are “special.” It would help to make this intuition explicit, and to clarify whether a margin (e.g., acting at a rate ρ − ε) or dithering around the threshold could recover O(log T) or whether an Ω(√T) lower bound holds.
- Robustness of “measure-zero” caveat: Although ρ = qj has zero Lebesgue measure, for discrete π and fixed ρ it can arise naturally (e.g., rational ρ,π). Please discuss how sensitive performance is when ρ is within O(1/√T) of a boundary and whether a principled margin policy can stabilize regret.
- Baseline and regret benchmark: The analysis leverages Û(T,B), an upper bound from the static LP. Please make explicit throughout whether the stated regrets (Theorems 1–2) are w.r.t. the true oracle U∗ or the LP upper bound, and bound Û − U∗ where used. Clarify fairness of comparison versus prior work that measures regret against finite policy classes.
- Assumptions and ties: The uniqueness assumptions (strict u∗1 > … > u∗J and unique best action per context) simplify exposition but may be fragile in practice. Please discuss tie-handling and whether results extend with ε-margins or generic tie-breaking.
- Unknown context distribution: The truncated EALP analysis is relegated to the supplement. Since this increases dependency in bτ/τ, having a concise main-text statement (algorithmic description, truncation epoch scaling, explicit bound) would aid clarity.
- Heterogeneous costs: The extension is promising but under-specified in main text. A brief derivation of the LP (variables, constraints, and resulting threshold/marginal structure) would strengthen the contribution.
- Empirics: It would be valuable to empirically probe (i) sensitivity near boundaries, (ii) scaling in J,K, (iii) heterogeneous costs, and (iv) comparisons to Mixture Elimination and concave-knapsack bandits.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: Technically sound overall; proofs hinge on standard UCB concentration and novel control of bτ. The boundary-case regime deserves either a lower bound or a clearer explanation of optimality gaps.
- Clarity: Generally well-written. The role of Û(T,B), the boundary-case intuition, and the unknown-π algorithm could be clarified in the main text. Notation is consistent; assumptions are transparent.
- Originality: The adaptive LP pacing in constrained contextual bandits and the regret guarantees are novel and significant relative to prior O(√T) results.
- Significance: Results are important for applications with hard budgets (ads, clinical trials, crowdsourcing). The simplicity and efficiency of UCB-ALP make downstream adoption likely.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide intuitive and formal discussion of boundary regimes; consider adding a stylized lower bound or practical margin heuristic with provable trade-offs.
- State clearly the regret baseline and how bounds translate from Û to U∗.
- Elevate the unknown-π algorithm and main theorem to the paper body.
- Add experiments examining proximity to boundaries and heterogeneous-cost scenarios.
- Discuss computational complexity per round (sorting contexts, LP size) and memory.

Arguments for acceptance
- First logarithmic regret guarantees in constrained contextual bandits under practical assumptions.
- Clean, computationally efficient algorithm with strong and interpretable analysis.
- Techniques (adaptive LP pacing; hypergeometric concentration) are likely to influence follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Boundary-case treatment leaves open whether O(√T) is inherent; practical regimes may frequently be near boundaries.
- Some important algorithmic and analytical details (unknown π, heterogeneous costs) are deferred to the supplement.
- Limited empirical validation and comparison to strong baselines.
