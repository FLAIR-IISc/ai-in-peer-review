

# Review for "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
This paper studies constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, extending traditional contextual bandit models to incorporate resource limitations. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle that achieves near-optimal performance in unit-cost systems with known statistics, and combine it with UCB to develop UCB-ALP for the unknown statistics case. They claim this is the first work to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, contrasting with previous Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) work [17] that achieves O(√T) regret.

The paper builds on contextual bandit literature [1,2,3,5,6,7] and extends non-contextual budget-constrained bandit results [11-16]. However, the relationship to recent work on contextual bandits with constraints [20-22] requires careful clarification, particularly regarding the dynamic context case.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a clear contribution to the understanding of constrained contextual bandits through the ALP approximation technique, which provides a clean theoretical benchmark for regret analysis.

2. The UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient and demonstrates a practical approach to balancing exploration and exploitation under budget constraints.

3. The analysis of the ALP algorithm's near-optimality (O(1) regret except for boundary cases) provides valuable insights into the structure of constrained contextual bandits.

4. The paper addresses a practically important problem with applications in clinical trials, crowdsourcing, and online recommendation systems.

## Weaknesses
1. **Novelty concerns**: The main result (logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits) appears less novel than claimed. The problem can be viewed as a time/budgeted bandit with JK arms (one for each context-action pair) and action constraints, which is a standard bandit extension. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from this straightforward interpretation.

2. **Theorem 2 rate**: The regret bound O(√T + JK log T) appears questionable. For a problem with JK arms, the standard regret bound for bandits with budget constraints would be O(√(JK T)) rather than O(√T + ...). The authors should clarify why the √T term dominates the JK log T term, especially when JK is large.

3. **Problem-dependent bounds**: The problem-dependent bound O(JK log T) is not expressed in terms of arm gaps (as standard in bandit literature), but rather as a sum over all context-action pairs. A cleaner bound using sums of inverse arm gaps would be more meaningful and comparable to existing results.

4. **Lack of matching lower bounds**: The paper lacks matching lower bounds for the problem-dependent and problem-independent settings. Without these, it's difficult to assess whether the regret bounds are tight.

5. **Clarification needed on context independence**: The claim that "the coupling of contexts vanishes for large horizon" requires justification. Why can't each context occurrence be treated as an independent bandit instance? This is a critical point that needs clarification.

## Specific Comments
1. The paper should explicitly address the connection to [20-22], which also studies contextual bandits with constraints. The claim of novelty needs stronger justification.

2. The proof approach using "context ranking events" (Erank,0(t), Erank,1(t), Erank,2(t)) appears unnecessarily complex. A simpler analysis based on standard UCB regret decomposition would be preferable.

3. The supplementary material's bound needs reworking to express the problem-dependent regret in terms of arm gaps rather than the KJ factor.

4. The boundary cases analysis requires more clarity. The paper states that the regret is O(√T) for boundary cases, but this seems inconsistent with the general case's logarithmic regret.

## Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting approach to constrained contextual bandits and makes a technical contribution to the analysis of these problems. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the theoretical contribution is not as significant as presented. The regret bound for the non-boundary cases is not properly justified, and the lack of matching lower bounds weakens the main claim.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing a clear and rigorous justification for the novelty compared to existing work
2. Correcting the regret bound to O(√(JK T) + ...) or explaining why the current bound is tight
3. Deriving problem-dependent bounds in terms of arm gaps
4. Providing matching lower bounds for both problem-dependent and problem-independent settings

Given these concerns, I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper has technical merit but does not sufficiently advance the state of the art in a way that justifies acceptance at NIPS. The authors should address the above concerns in a rebuttal before reconsideration.
