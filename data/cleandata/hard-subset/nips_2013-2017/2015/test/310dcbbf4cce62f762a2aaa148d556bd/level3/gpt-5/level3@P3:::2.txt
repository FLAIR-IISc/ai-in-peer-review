Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies contextual bandits under both time and budget constraints (“constrained contextual bandits”). The central technical challenge is that a hard budget couples decisions across time and across contexts, making the oracle policy (with known statistics) inherently dynamic and difficult to analyze or implement. The authors propose a linear-programming-based approximation of the oracle that adapts to the average remaining budget, coined Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP). In the unit-cost, finite discrete context setting with known context distribution: (i) ALP achieves O(1) regret except on boundary instances (where the budget ratio ρ aligns with cumulative context mass), for which the regret is O(√T). Building on ALP’s tolerance to ranking errors, the paper then proposes UCB-ALP, which learns the reward means and attains O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on boundary cases. The paper sketches extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates and a truncated analysis) and to heterogeneous costs (single budget, fixed known costs), where analogous O(1)/O(√T) guarantees are shown for known statistics and a simple explore-then-ALP variant for unknown statistics.

Relative to prior work: Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) and Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) obtain O(√T) regret under more general settings (e.g., infinite policy sets/contexts, random costs, multiple resources), but with high complexity or weaker benchmarks. Work on concave rewards with convex knapsacks similarly yields O(√T) in dynamic settings. To the reviewer’s knowledge, this paper is the first to show logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits by leveraging finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget, and by using an adaptive LP relaxation that tracks the remaining budget.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: The ALP relaxation is simple, computationally light, and provably near-optimal, providing both a benchmark and a decision rule that decouples learning from control.
- Theoretical guarantees: O(1) regret for ALP (non-boundary) and O(JK log T) regret for UCB-ALP are strong results for constrained contextual bandits; the proofs use neat concentration arguments for the remaining budget via a “sampling without replacement” view.
- Clarity and organization: The problem is well-motivated; related work is covered; assumptions are explicit; the paper clearly separates known/unknown statistics, known/unknown context distribution, and unit-cost/heterogeneous-cost cases.
- Novelty: The adaptive use of LP with the average remaining budget is, to my knowledge, the first to obtain logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope of assumptions: Results hinge on finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget. While justified in several applications, this excludes many practical scenarios (e.g., continuous features, multiple resources, stochastic costs).
- Boundary cases: Performance degrades to O(√T); while measure-zero in parameter space, near-boundary behavior may occur in practice. No matching lower bounds for boundary cases are provided.
- Practical aspects are thin: Sections 5–6 sketch extensions but defer details to the supplement; there are no experiments (synthetic or real) to validate the algorithms, constants, or robustness.
- Unknown context distribution: The analysis relies on truncating the empirical estimation phase. It would be valuable to quantify the practical overhead and to compare with fully online estimation.
- Algorithmic details for heterogeneous costs: The -First ALP approach is simple but may be suboptimal; comparisons to UCB-style approaches or Thompson sampling are not explored.

Quality
The technical development is solid and internally consistent. The linear relaxation bound Û(T, B) is a clean benchmark; the ALP analysis via hypergeometric concentration is elegant; the regret decomposition for UCB-ALP is standard but carefully adapted to the contextual and budgeted setting. While I did not line-by-line verify all proofs, the arguments are convincing and well-motivated. Missing are empirical validation and sensitivity analyses (e.g., to mis-specified context distributions or near-boundary parameters).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The main algorithms are described precisely; key lemmas and proof sketches provide good intuition. Suggestions:
- Add explicit pseudo-code for ALP/EALP and for the heterogeneous-cost ALP variant.
- Provide a small running example illustrating the threshold structure and how ALP adapts bτ/τ.
- Clarify computational complexity per round (solving small LPs; sorting û∗j) and overall.

Originality
The adaptive LP approximation of the oracle and the resulting O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits (under the stated assumptions) appear novel. The connection between budget evolution and sampling without replacement is a nice analytical insight. The work is clearly differentiated from RCB/BwK and concave knapsack bandits.

Significance
The results advance our theoretical understanding of constrained contextual bandits by showing that logarithmic regret is attainable under reasonable simplifying assumptions with efficient algorithms. This can influence subsequent work on more general resource-constrained contextual learning. Practical impact would be strengthened by empirical demonstrations and by relaxing assumptions toward richer settings (e.g., continuous contexts, multiple resources).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is UCB-ALP to near-boundary parameters (ρ close to qj)? Can one adaptively “push away” from the boundary (e.g., via slight budget slack) to regain logarithmic regret?
- Can the analysis handle sub-Gaussian rewards with unknown variance or heavy-tailed rewards?
- How does UCB-ALP perform if the context distribution is mis-specified or non-i.i.d. (e.g., slowly varying or Markovian)?
- In heterogeneous costs, can a UCB-style online variant (rather than -First) achieve similar guarantees?
- Are there instance-dependent lower bounds for boundary cases?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include experiments comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to BwK/RCB baselines on synthetic data and, if possible, a crowdsourcing or clinical-trial–like simulator. Report regret, budget utilization, and runtime.
- Provide practical guidance on hyperparameters (e.g., UCB exploration constants) and ablations on J, K, and budget ratios.
- Explore heuristics to mitigate boundary effects (e.g., budget smoothing, randomized throttling).
- Discuss how to extend to multiple resource constraints and to continuous contexts (e.g., via discretization or linear models).

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear and timely problem with explicit motivation.
- Elegant algorithmic idea (ALP) with strong, near-tight guarantees.
- First logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under stated assumptions.
- Careful and convincing theoretical analysis; well-written and well-situated in prior work.

Arguments against acceptance
- No empirical validation; practical sections are thin.
- Restricted setting (finite contexts, fixed costs, single budget); boundary cases remain weaker.
- Some extension analyses are deferred and rely on truncation or simplified exploration.

Overall assessment
A good theoretical paper: the simplifying assumptions are well-justified and enable strong, clean results that move the state of the art. Despite the lack of experiments, the clarity and technical contributions warrant acceptance. I lean accept, and I encourage the authors to add empirical studies and further discussion of boundary robustness.
