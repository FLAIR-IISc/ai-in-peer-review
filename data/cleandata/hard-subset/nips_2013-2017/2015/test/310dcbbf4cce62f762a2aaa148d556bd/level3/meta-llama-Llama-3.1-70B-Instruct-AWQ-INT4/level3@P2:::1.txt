This paper studies the problem of constrained contextual bandits, where the agent is given a budget B and a time-horizon T, and must make decisions to maximize the expected total reward subject to the budget and time constraints. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves near-optimality and only requires the ordering of expected rewards. They then combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to propose a UCB-ALP algorithm for constrained contextual bandits with unknown expected rewards.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of the ALP algorithm, which is shown to achieve O(1) regret except for certain boundary cases, where it achieves O(√T) regret. The UCB-ALP algorithm is then proposed, which combines ALP with UCB to achieve logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the regret of the UCB-ALP algorithm, including a partition of the regret into two parts: the regret from action ranking errors within a context, and the regret from the fluctuations of the remaining budget and context ranking errors. They show that the expected number of context ranking errors satisfies E[T(s)] = O(JK log T), and that the regret of UCB-ALP is O(JK log T) for non-boundary cases.

The authors also discuss the extension of their results to bandits with unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs. They propose an Empirical ALP (EALP) algorithm, which replaces the probability πj in ALP with its empirical estimate, and show that EALP achieves similar performance as ALP. They also propose an -First ALP algorithm for the unknown statistics case, which implements an exploration stage first and then an exploitation stage according to ALP.

The strengths of this paper include the development of the ALP algorithm, which is shown to achieve near-optimality and only requires the ordering of expected rewards. The combination of ALP with UCB to propose the UCB-ALP algorithm is also a significant contribution. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the regret of the UCB-ALP algorithm, including a partition of the regret into two parts.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The reviewer notes that the problem can be viewed as a time/budgeted bandit with JK arms and action constraints, and questions the novelty of the result. The reviewer also questions the rate of Theorem 2, suggesting that it should be O(√(KT) + …) instead of O(√T + …). Additionally, the reviewer suggests that problem-dependent bounds should use sums of inverse arm gaps instead of a KJ factor, and questions the proof approach based on UCB ordering events.

In terms of originality, the reviewer concludes that there is insufficient evidence to determine whether the paper is original or not. The reviewer asks the authors to clarify the novelty of their result in the rebuttal.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the regret of the UCB-ALP algorithm, and the proofs are clear and concise. However, the reviewer notes that the supplementary material's bound needs reworking, and that the paper could benefit from a clean problem-dependent upper bound expressed in terms of arm gaps.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits. The development of the ALP algorithm and the combination of ALP with UCB to propose the UCB-ALP algorithm are significant contributions. However, the reviewer notes that the paper could benefit from nontrivial lower bounds that incorporate context complexity beyond trivial classical-bandit cases.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the regret of the UCB-ALP algorithm, and the proofs are clear and concise. However, the reviewer notes that the paper could benefit from a clean problem-dependent upper bound expressed in terms of arm gaps.

Overall, the reviewer concludes that the paper is neutral, due to insufficient evidence of originality. The reviewer asks the authors to clarify the novelty of their result in the rebuttal.
