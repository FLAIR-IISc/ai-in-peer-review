Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies contextual bandits with a finite budget B and horizon T (ρ = B/T), arguing that budget and time constraints couple contexts across time and complicate exploration–exploitation. The authors’ main idea is to approximate the dynamic oracle by a linear program that relaxes the hard budget to an average constraint. They then adapt this LP round-by-round to the current average remaining budget bτ/τ (ALP), and, when rewards are unknown, combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP). The analysis shows ALP is within O(1) regret of the oracle except on boundary cases, and UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret (again, with an additional O(√T) term only on boundary cases where ρ equals a cumulative context probability qj). Key technical tools include mapping the budget evolution under ALP to sampling without replacement and establishing hypergeometric concentration of bτ.

Compared to prior work:
- Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB; Badanidiyuru et al., COLT’14) provide O(√T) regret for more general settings but with finite policy sets and higher complexity; here the focus is on finite contexts/actions with algorithms that are computationally light and achieve O(log T) in most cases.
- Bandits with knapsacks (BwK; Badanidiyuru et al., FOCS’13) and later works on concave rewards/convex knapsacks (Agrawal & Devanur) achieve sublinear regret under budgets, but do not obtain logarithmic regret in the dynamic-context setting considered here.
- Relative to static baselines, the paper deliberately measures against a stronger benchmark: an adaptive linear program that interprets the budget as permission to skip a fixed fraction of rounds, and steers decisions with bτ/τ. This makes the comparison conservative and meaningful for constrained settings.

Strengths:
- Interesting setup and unique solution approach: the adaptive LP that needs only the reward ordering is both elegant and practically appealing. The “sampling-without-replacement” view for budget evolution is insightful and leads to clean concentration bounds.
- Theoretical guarantees that are tight for non-boundary cases: O(1) regret with known rewards (ALP) and O(JK log T) with UCB-ALP.
- Extensions to unknown context distributions (via EALP/UCB-EALP) and heterogeneous costs are outlined with principled arguments.
- Clear separation of regret sources (action-ranking vs. context-ranking/budget fluctuation) and careful accounting.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Boundary cases: Why must ρ = qj be fundamentally different, especially for j > 1? The analysis attributes O(√T) regret to threshold switching when bτ/τ fluctuates near knife-edge boundaries. However, it remains unclear whether O(√T) is intrinsic or an artifact of the hard-threshold policy and proof technique. Could adding slack (e.g., a safety margin around the threshold, conservative acceptance that targets ρ − γ/√T, or a smoothed acceptance function) reduce crossings enough to achieve O(log T)? Is there a lower bound ruling this out? Some intuition and either a negative result or a refined algorithmic tweak would strengthen the case.
- Dependence on known π in the main results; while Section 5 addresses unknown π via truncation and coupling, more explicit guarantees and practical guidance on the truncation schedule would help.
- Practical complexity details are light: per-round complexity of solving the LP (sorting û∗j and computing a single fractional pivot) is small, but it would help to state explicit bounds (e.g., O(J log J) per round) and discuss the heterogeneous-cost LP size and constraints.
- Empirical evaluation (not shown here) should include baselines beyond naive rules: BwK variants, global-constraint UCB with static budgets, and the Mixture Elimination approach, to demonstrate the advantage of adaptive budgeting.

Quality:
- Technical soundness appears strong. The use of hypergeometric concentration for bτ and the partition of regret into interpretable components are convincing. The boundary-case analysis would benefit from either a matching lower bound or a constructive mitigation.
- The claims are clearly supported by theorems and sketches; full proofs are deferred to the supplement.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized. A small suggestion: bring key pieces from the supplement into the main text (e.g., a concise statement of Lemma 2 and an intuitive figure for threshold switching at ρ = qj). Clarify tie-handling when u∗j are equal and explicitly state the per-round LP solution procedure (sorting plus one fractional context).

Originality:
- The adaptive-LP approximation that only requires reward ordering, and the sampling-without-replacement lens for budget evolution, are novel and insightful. Combining this with UCB produces a simple, computationally efficient algorithm with strong guarantees.

Significance:
- Achieving logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits with random contexts is a meaningful advance. The approach is likely to influence subsequent work on computationally efficient algorithms for resource-constrained bandits and inventory-balanced decision-making.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Boundary cases: provide sharper intuition, a lower bound, or a smoothed/robustified variant (e.g., inventory-balancing with safety stock, conservative policy targeting ρ − γ/√T, or randomized dithering near thresholds) and analyze whether this yields O(log T).
- Expand on the unknown-π setting with clear guidance on truncation time and its effect on constants.
- Discuss constant factors in O(JK log T), since these can dominate when JK is large and T is moderate.
- Add empirical comparisons to BwK-style methods, and to a static LP policy that enforces a fixed average budget per round (to highlight the value of adaptivity).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean and computationally efficient approach with strong theoretical guarantees.
- New insights (adaptive LP + hypergeometric concentration) that are broadly useful.
- Clear significance for constrained decision-making with contexts.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Unresolved boundary-case behavior; no lower bound to justify the O(√T) gap.
- Limited practical discussion of complexity and constants; empirical comparisons to strong baselines may be needed.

Overall recommendation: lean accept. The setup is compelling, the algorithmic ideas are unique and well-argued, and the theoretical results advance the state of the art. Addressing the boundary-case question and expanding empirical comparisons would make the contribution even stronger.
