This paper addresses the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits with discrete contexts and actions, a crucial extension to standard contextual bandit settings found in many real-world applications. The authors provide a computationally efficient algorithm achieving a logarithmic regret bound, a significant improvement over existing work. The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, presenting a clear and logical progression of ideas.

The core contribution lies in the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which approximates the oracle policy using linear programming relaxation. This allows for efficient computation while maintaining near-optimal performance. Combining ALP with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method yields the UCB-ALP algorithm, guaranteeing logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases). This is, to the best of my knowledge, the first work to demonstrate logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. The paper also extends the analysis to scenarios with unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, further highlighting the generality of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing a logarithmic regret bound for the UCB-ALP algorithm. The proofs, while likely detailed in the supplementary material, appear sound based on the presented overview.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The ALP algorithm offers a computationally attractive alternative to dynamic programming, making it practical for large-scale applications.
* **Clear Exposition:** The paper is remarkably well-written. The problem statement is clear, the methodology is explained in a concise and understandable manner, and the notation is consistent.
* **Novelty:** The approach of utilizing LP relaxation in conjunction with UCB for constrained contextual bandits is novel and demonstrates a strong understanding of both theoretical and algorithmic considerations.
* **Generalizability:** The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs broaden the applicability of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks numerical experiments to validate the theoretical results and demonstrate the practical performance of the proposed algorithms. Including simulations would significantly strengthen the paper and provide insights into the algorithm's behavior in different settings.
* **UCB-ALP Combination Rationale:** While the combination of UCB and ALP is intuitive, Section 4.1 could benefit from a more explicit articulation of *why* this combination works so well. Specifically, connecting the UCB exploration strategy directly to the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle and relating it more concretely to policy update mechanisms in algorithms like UCRL2 would be helpful. The current explanation feels slightly hand-wavy.
* **Boundary Case Regret:** The O(√T) regret in boundary cases, while acknowledged, warrants further investigation. Understanding the frequency and impact of these cases in practical scenarios is important.

**Suggestions:**

* **Empirical Validation:** The most significant improvement would be to include numerical experiments. Comparing UCB-ALP against baseline algorithms (e.g., epsilon-greedy, LinUCB with a budget constraint) on synthetic and/or real-world datasets would demonstrate its effectiveness.
* **Clarify UCB-ALP Rationale:** Expand the explanation in Section 4.1 to explicitly link the UCB optimism to the broader reinforcement learning framework and policy update procedures like UCRL2.
* **Boundary Case Analysis:** Investigate the conditions leading to the boundary cases and their prevalence in practice. Consider whether techniques to mitigate the √T regret in these scenarios exist.



**Related Work:** The authors appropriately cite relevant work, including contributions from Agarwal et al. (2014) and Badanidiyuru et al. (2016). However, they should consider referencing arXiv:1506.03374 (Agrawal, Devanur, Li), which is directly related to bandits with global constraints and objective, and could provide further context.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Significant theoretical contribution with a logarithmic regret bound.
* Novel approach combining LP relaxation and UCB.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Potential for practical impact due to computational efficiency.

**Con:**

* Lack of empirical evaluation.
* Slightly weak rationale for the UCB-ALP combination.
*  √T regret in boundary cases.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the lack of empirical validation, this paper represents a significant advancement in the field of constrained contextual bandits. The theoretical results are compelling, the approach is novel, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. Therefore, I **recommend acceptance** with the caveat that the authors are strongly encouraged to include numerical experiments in an extended version. The insights provided by this work are valuable and contribute significantly to our understanding of this important problem.
