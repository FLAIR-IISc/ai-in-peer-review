This paper addresses the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, introducing algorithms with logarithmic regret—a significant advance over prior work which often achieved only sublinear regret or relied on stronger benchmarks. The core idea of approximating the oracle using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) and then combining it with UCB is both interesting and effective. The rigorous analysis, despite the complexity of the setting, is commendable, and the extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs broaden the applicability of the proposed approach.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly situating the problem within the broader context of contextual bandits and highlighting the limitations of existing solutions when budget constraints are introduced. The authors effectively build intuition by first analyzing the case with known statistics, developing the ALP approximation, and then extending it to the unknown case via UCB. The consideration of boundary cases and the detailed regret analysis – distinguishing between logarithmic and sublinear bounds – demonstrate a thorough understanding of the problem and proposed solutions. The connections drawn to related work, particularly resource-constrained bandits and dynamic programming, are helpful in positioning the contribution.

A major strength is the novelty of achieving logarithmic regret in this constrained setting. Prior work, including the referenced Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], suffered from weaker regret bounds (√T) or restricted benchmark assumptions. The ALP approximation is a clever way to tackle the complexity of the oracle, and its adaptation to the unknown reward case through UCB is a natural and effective extension. The paper rightly points out that the algorithm only requires ordering of expected rewards, making it potentially applicable in scenarios where accurate reward estimation is difficult. The analysis is also strong, meticulously considering sources of regret and employing techniques like bounded averaged differences and coupling arguments.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. The initial focus on unit costs, while simplifying the analysis, feels somewhat restrictive. Although the extension to heterogeneous costs is presented, it’s relegated to Section 6, reducing its prominence. The discussion surrounding the boundary cases could be more detailed. While the paper identifies them, the implications of encountering these cases for practical applications aren’t fully explored.  Furthermore, the paper briefly mentions a concentration property under adaptive algorithms, but further elaboration on how this property is leveraged and its significance for the overall regret analysis would be beneficial.

The authors correctly question why boundary cases require different analysis, and specifically why ρ = qj should be special for j > 1. However, the explanation provided in the theorem statements and proofs is somewhat dense and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. A reader unfamiliar with the LP relaxation and its sensitivity to the context distribution might struggle to understand the origin of these boundary cases.

The comparison to the stronger baseline employing adaptive linear programming with a budget is apt and insightful. It effectively demonstrates the advantage of the proposed UCB-ALP algorithm over simply skipping suboptimal rounds, highlighting the importance of intelligent exploration within the budget constraint.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Achieves logarithmic regret in a challenging setting where previous results were weaker.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a practically relevant problem with applications in clinical trials, online advertising, and resource allocation.
*   **Technical Soundness:** Rigorous analysis, employing appropriate mathematical tools.
*   **Clarity:** Well-written and organized, effectively explaining the core ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The discussion of boundary cases could be more insightful and accessible.
*   The extension to heterogeneous costs feels slightly underdeveloped compared to the core unit-cost analysis.
*   The explanation of why certain parameter regimes lead to slower regret could be further elaborated.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. Despite minor areas for improvement, the work represents a significant advance in the field of constrained contextual bandits. The combination of novel algorithmic ideas, rigorous analysis, and practical relevance make it a valuable contribution to NIPS. The strengths substantially outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper addresses a challenging and important problem with a compelling solution.
