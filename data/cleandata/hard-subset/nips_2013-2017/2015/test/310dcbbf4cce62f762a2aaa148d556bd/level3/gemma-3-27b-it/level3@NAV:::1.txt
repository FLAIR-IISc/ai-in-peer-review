## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, introducing algorithms aimed at achieving logarithmic regret, a feat previously unaccomplished in this setting. The authors begin by analyzing a simplified unit-cost system, proposing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approach that approximates the oracle and achieves near-optimality. They then extend this to a UCB-ALP algorithm for general unknown reward scenarios, claiming logarithmic regret (with caveats for boundary cases) and generalizing to heterogeneous costs and unknown context distributions.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing contextual bandit literature when faced with budget and time constraints. The approach of approximating the oracle with LP and then adapting it via UCB is intuitive and logically presented. The exploration of both known and unknown context distributions, and the extension to heterogeneous costs demonstrate a thorough investigation of the problem space. The connection to existing work, particularly regarding resource allocation and knapsack problems, is appropriately acknowledged. 

However, the claimed novelty feels somewhat overstated. The core idea of approximating a complex problem with LP is not entirely novel (as the authors acknowledge referencing prior work) and the extension to UCB seems a natural combination. A significant weakness lies in the handling of the “boundary cases” where regret escalates to O(√T). This casts doubt on the universality of the logarithmic regret claim. The supplemental material’s bound reworking is mentioned, but a clean, problem-dependent upper bound expressed in terms of arm gaps (as requested by the reviewer guidelines) is still lacking. The current bound, mentioning “KJ”, is not insightful. Further, matching lower bounds are absent, limiting the ability to assess the tightness of the proposed algorithm’s performance in both problem-dependent and problem-independent settings.

Furthermore, Theorem 2’s rate of O(√T + ...) in boundary cases feels suspect. Given the contextual nature of the problem, O(√(KT) + ...) might be a more appropriate expectation. The proof approach based on UCB ordering events, while standard, doesn’t seem to fully justify the logarithmic regret claim, particularly when considering the potential for correlated context arrivals. The discussion of nontrivial lower bounds beyond classical bandit cases is also missing. A more detailed analysis incorporating context complexity would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem.
*   Clear and logical presentation of the proposed algorithms.
*   Exploration of both known and unknown context scenarios.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the approach feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
*   Unsatisfactory handling of boundary cases and the resulting √T regret.
*   Lack of tight lower bounds to support the claimed performance.
*   Insufficiently insightful regret bounds.

**Overall Assessment:** Neutral. The paper presents a reasonable approach to a difficult problem, but falls short of demonstrating significant originality or a fully convincing performance guarantee. I request the authors to clarify the novelty of their contribution in the rebuttal, addressing the concerns regarding the boundary cases, lower bounds, and regret bound expressiveness.
