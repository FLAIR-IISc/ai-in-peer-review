- Problem: studies constrained contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints; motivated by clinical trials and crowdsourcing.  
- Proposal: Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for known statistics and UCB-ALP for unknown statistics.  
- Main claim: ALP approximates oracle by adjusting budget constraint to remaining budget-to-time ratio and achieves O(1) regret (except boundary cases).  
- Main claim: UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases, claiming logarithmic regret.  
- Strength: clear problem formulation, well-structured theoretical analysis, and practical, computationally efficient algorithms.  
- Strength: good positioning and citation of related work in contextual and budgeted bandits.  
- Novelty concern: problem can be reduced to JK arms with a budget constraint (bandits with knapsacks), so novelty is questionable.  
- Missing comparison: authors should explicitly compare to bandits-with-knapsacks results (e.g., [12,13]) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17].  
- Misleading phrasing: “logarithmic regret” conceals linear dependence on JK; dependency on number of context-action pairs is not properly contextualized.  
- Theorem 2 issue: regret stated as O(√T + JK log T) needs verification—standard budgeted bandits often have O(√(KT)) worst-case rates.  
- Problem-dependent bound issue: O(JK log T) should be expressed in terms of arm gaps (sums of inverse gaps), not just JK.  
- Supplementary material: regret bounds and derivations need reworking and clearer statements in gap-dependent form.  
- Lower bounds missing: no matching lower bounds are provided, so optimality/tightness of logarithmic claims is unestablished.  
- Boundary cases: analysis/exclusion of boundary cases (where regret is O(√T)) is insufficiently justified.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and well-written, but limited originality and incomplete theoretical analysis; recommend rejection absent stronger novelty or matching lower bounds.
