This paper tackles the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, introducing algorithms aiming for logarithmic regret. The core idea of approximating the oracle with Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) and combining it with UCB is promising, particularly given the scarcity of work achieving logarithmic regret in this setting. The authors rightly point to the complexities introduced by budget and time constraints, highlighting the difficulty of directly applying existing contextual bandit techniques.

**Summary of the Approach:** The paper begins by analyzing the problem and proposing an LP relaxation to approximate the optimal policy when system statistics are known. This leads to the ALP algorithm, which dynamically adjusts a budget constraint. Extending this to the unknown reward setting, the UCB-ALP algorithm combines ALP with UCB for exploration. The authors provide theoretical regret bounds, claiming O(log T) regret under certain conditions, and generalize the approach to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Constrained contextual bandits are highly relevant to real-world applications, and the problem is significantly more complex than unconstrained versions.
* **Novelty of the ALP Approach:** The adaptive linear programming approach appears to be a novel way to tackle the constrained setting, providing a useful approximation of the oracle.
* **Clear Presentation of Ideas:** The paper is generally well-written and presents the key concepts in a relatively accessible manner. The motivation for the work is clearly stated.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The authors attempt a thorough analysis, considering various scenarios including unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately relates to prior work in contextual bandits, knapsack problems, and resource allocation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Vanishing Context Coupling Claim:** The claim that context coupling vanishes with a large horizon, allowing for treatment of each context occurrence as an independent bandit instance, is not fully convincing. The budget constraint *inherently* couples contexts, as spending in one context limits possibilities in others. A stronger justification is needed.
* **Problem-Dependent Upper Bound:** The paper requests a clean problem-dependent upper bound expressed in terms of arm gaps, and rightly points out that the supplementary material's bound requires reworking. The current bound (O(JK log T)) feels loose and doesn’t clearly leverage the gap information. Focusing on inverse arm gaps, as suggested, is a promising direction.
* **Lower Bounds:** The absence of matching lower bounds is a significant weakness. Showing logarithmic regret is important, but demonstrating it’s optimal (or close to optimal) is crucial. The authors acknowledge this. A lower bound demonstrating the inherent difficulty of the problem, beyond the trivial classical bandit cases, is necessary to establish the significance of the results.
* **Theorem 2 Rate:** The authors question the O(√T + ...) rate in Theorem 2, suggesting it might be O(√(KT) + …). This is a valid point and requires careful justification in a rebuttal. The connection between K (number of actions) and T seems plausible, and the current rate should be revisited.
* **Proof Approach:** The reliance on UCB ordering events, while standard, feels somewhat ad-hoc given the LP-based framework. The use of the ALP approximation seems to necessitate this approach, but exploring alternatives or providing a more intuitive connection to the underlying optimization problem would be beneficial.
* **Incremental Contribution:** While the results are technically sound, the overall contribution feels incremental. The extension to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are natural extensions of the core ALP idea. The primary novelty lies in the application of ALP to constrained contextual bandits, but the degree to which this represents a significant advancement is debatable.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and challenging problem.
* Introduces a novel algorithmic approach (ALP) with promising theoretical properties.
* Provides a thorough analysis and demonstrates a clear understanding of the related literature.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* Lack of strong theoretical justification for key claims (e.g., vanishing context coupling).
* Absence of matching lower bounds.
* Incremental nature of the contribution.
* Questionable rate in Theorem 2.

**Overall Assessment:**

I remain neutral regarding acceptance at this time. The paper presents a technically sound approach to a challenging problem. However, the lack of strong theoretical justification for certain claims, the absence of lower bounds, and the somewhat incremental nature of the contribution prevent me from strongly advocating for acceptance. The authors’ rebuttal will be crucial in addressing the concerns raised, particularly regarding the novelty of the approach and the correctness of the regret bounds. I encourage the authors to clarify the novelty of their contribution compared to existing work on knapsack-based bandits and resource allocation. A clear justification of the O(√T) term in Theorem 2 is also essential. Finally, exploring the possibility of deriving tighter problem-dependent bounds and investigating potential lower bounds would significantly strengthen the paper.
