Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints (“constrained contextual bandits”). The main idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle with a linear program that enforces only an average budget, yielding (i) a clean, tractable upper bound on the optimal expected reward and (ii) an adaptive decision rule, Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP), that uses the current remaining-budget-per-remaining-time bτ/τ as the effective constraint. With known statistics and unit costs, ALP is shown to incur O(1) regret relative to the oracle except at boundary parameters (defined by ρ = B/T coinciding with a cumulative context mass qj), where it incurs O(√T) regret. Building on a key feature—that ALP needs only the ordering of expected rewards—the paper combines ALP with UCB to learn unknown rewards. The resulting UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret (except boundary cases with an additional O(√T)). The approach is then extended to unknown context distributions (via empirical frequencies) and to heterogeneous costs (via a generalized LP with per-context action constraints).

The work is well positioned relative to prior literature. It complements resourceful contextual bandits [Badanidiyuru et al., COLT’14], which achieve O(√T) regret under more general settings but relative to a finite policy class and with substantial computational cost. It also relates to bandits with budgets/knapsacks [e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., FOCS’13; Combes et al., SIGMETRICS’15] and to bandits with concave rewards and convex knapsacks [Agrawal & Devanur], which typically obtain O(√T) regret and either assume static contexts or use fixed per-round budgets. Here, exploiting finite discrete contexts and adaptive budget normalization enables logarithmic regret under non-boundary parameters. The paper also connects to UCRL-style RL with Markovian contexts, but the budget constraint creates a different coupling.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: replacing the hard budget with an adaptive average budget produces both a strong analysis benchmark and a practical decision rule.
- Technical novelty: the hypergeometric concentration argument for bτ and the careful partition of regret sources (action vs. context ranking errors; budget fluctuation) are elegant and reusable.
- Strong guarantees: O(1) regret for known statistics (non-boundary) and O(JK log T) for unknown rewards; to my knowledge, the first logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under these assumptions.
- Computationally simple in the unit-cost case: per-round sorting/thresholding suffices.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Boundary cases: Please provide more intuition for why ρ = qj is special (particularly for j > 1). My understanding is that v(ρ) is piecewise-linear with kinks at qj; when ρ equals a kink, small fluctuations of bτ/τ around that kink switch the threshold j̃(·), causing linear-in-τ per-round variability that aggregates to O(√T). An explicit lemma quantifying how often bτ/τ crosses the kink and how each crossing translates to lost reward would help. Also, can one smooth ALP near the kink (e.g., reserve ε budget or add a small random slack) to reduce crossings and improve the √T term?
- Assumptions: The unique best action per context and strictly ordered u∗j across contexts simplify analysis but are somewhat strong. What changes when ties exist? Are boundary-like behaviors more frequent then? A brief discussion would clarify robustness.
- Comparator/baseline: The analysis leverages a strong upper bound Û(T,B) from the static LP; this baseline is stronger than the more common “best fixed policy” comparator. This is a plus, but it would help to explicitly state in the main text that the regret is relative to the true oracle (hard constraint) and that Û is only used as an analyzable surrogate.
- Unknown context distribution: The truncated EALP analysis is relegated to the supplement. Given its practical relevance, a concise theorem and intuition in the main paper would improve clarity. Please also comment on sample complexity to estimate π well enough so that the bτ/τ concentration and ranking properties kick in.
- Heterogeneous costs: The generalized LP can be larger (JK variables). Is there a greedy characterization (e.g., sorting by normalized value ηj,k with feasibility corrections) that yields the same solution more efficiently? Some complexity discussion would be useful.
- Empirics: The paper would benefit from experiments comparing UCB-ALP to Mixture Elimination [RCB], to BwK-style methods, and to the concave/convex knapsack bandits under finite contexts. Reporting sensitivity to ρ, especially near boundaries, would validate the theory.

Quality:
- The proofs appear technically sound, with careful use of concentration for sampling-without-replacement and a precise regret decomposition. The boundary-case rate likely reflects fundamental kinks in v(ρ). More intuition for the boundary phenomenon and a short discussion of potential lower bounds in boundary instances would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and well organized. I recommend:
  - Add a high-level figure or pseudocode explaining ALP’s thresholding and the role of bτ/τ.
  - Expand the intuition for boundary cases (as above).
  - Summarize the unknown-π result in the main text with a statement and brief proof sketch.
  - State per-round computational complexity for unit-cost and heterogeneous-cost variants.

Originality:
- The adaptive-LP relaxation as both algorithm and analysis scaffold is a fresh and effective idea for constrained contextual bandits. Achieving logarithmic regret under these constraints (outside measure-zero parameters) is novel.

Significance:
- Resource constraints are ubiquitous in applications (ads, crowdsourcing, clinical trials). The method is simple, modular (plugging into UCB or other estimators), and should be adopted and extended.

Specific questions for the authors:
- Boundary cases: Why exactly must ρ = qj be treated differently? Is there a simple coupling or change-of-measure argument showing that any algorithm incurs Ω(√T) regret at the kink due to inevitable threshold toggling? Does the phenomenon depend on j > 1 in any subtle way beyond the location of the kink?
- Could KL-UCB or Thompson sampling reduce the JK log T constant, especially in context ranking errors?
- For heterogeneous costs, can you characterize when focusing only on k∗j by ηj,k is suboptimal, and provide a structural greedy algorithm that respects per-context single-action constraints?

Pros:
- Clear, principled approach with strong theoretical guarantees.
- First logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts and unit costs.
- Practical and computationally light in core settings.

Cons:
- Boundary-case intuition and potential optimality not fully explored in the main text.
- Limited empirical validation and limited discussion of computational aspects for heterogeneous costs.
- Some simplifying assumptions (unique bests, strict ordering) could be relaxed or discussed more.

Overall recommendation:
I lean toward acceptance. The paper addresses an important setting with a simple, insightful algorithmic idea and strong results. Clarifying the boundary-case behavior and adding empirical comparisons would further strengthen the submission.
