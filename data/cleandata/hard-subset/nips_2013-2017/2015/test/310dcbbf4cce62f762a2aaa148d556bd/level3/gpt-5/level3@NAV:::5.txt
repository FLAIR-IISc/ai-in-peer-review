Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies contextual bandits under a single hard budget and finite horizon, with discrete contexts and actions. The core idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle by a linear program with an average-budget constraint and to adapt the LP online to the instantaneous average remaining budget b/τ (ALP). When means are known, ALP has O(1) regret except at measure-zero “boundary” parameters (O(√T)). With unknown means, combining ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) yields RUCB-ALP(T,B) = O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) at boundaries. The paper further sketches extensions to unknown context distributions (empirical estimation) and heterogeneous costs. This advances over resourceful contextual bandits (Badanidiyuru et al., COLT’14) and the concave-reward/convex-knapsack line (Agrawal & Devanur, arXiv:1506.03374; 1507.06738), which obtain O(√T) regret and/or finite policy sets, by delivering computationally simple, instance-dependent logarithmic regret in a discrete setting. The use of optimism echoes UCRL2 (Auer & Ortner, NIPS’07), but here the LP structure and adaptive budget control are key.

Quality: The theory appears sound and carefully argued. The hypergeometric concentration of the remaining budget and the decomposition of regret into action-ranking and context-threshold errors are novel and persuasive. Boundary-case handling is honest about limits. A notable omission is empirical validation.

Clarity: Very well written, with clean problem setup and proofs sketched clearly. One place to improve is Section 4.1: explicitly state the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle guiding UCB-ALP, and clarify its relation to optimistic value updates in UCRL2; explain why ALP only needs correct reward rankings, making optimism particularly effective here.

Originality: The adaptive LP relaxation with tight O(1) gap (non-boundary) and the resulting log-regret algorithm under hard budget constraints are new for discrete contextual bandits and computationally attractive.

Significance: The paper offers an efficient, near-optimal algorithmic template likely to influence follow-up work on constrained bandits and budgeted decision-making.

Pros:
- Clear model and contributions; strong, fine-grained regret bounds.
- Computationally efficient, scalable template (ALP + optimism).
- Solid extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.

Cons:
- No experiments; practicality and constants untested.
- Scalability to many contexts/actions may benefit from structure.

Suggestions:
- Add numerical experiments (even in an extended version).
- Consider parametric reward models (e.g., linear/GLM) to reduce JK dependence.
- More explicit comparison and positioning relative to Agrawal & Devanur (arXiv:1506.03374).

Recommendation: Accept. The paper provides an efficient algorithm with near-optimal regret for constrained contextual bandits and advances the state of the art.
