This paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints (a.k.a. constrained contextual bandits), focusing on the practically motivated regime where the horizon T and budget B grow proportionally. The main technical idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle with a linear program that enforces an average-budget constraint. Building on this, the authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that, at each round, plugs the current average remaining budget bτ/τ into the LP and acts according to the corresponding threshold/randomization solution. In unit-cost systems with known context distribution and known rewards, ALP is shown to be near-optimal: O(1) regret except for measure-zero boundary cases where the budget ratio hits a context-mass boundary, for which regret is O(√T). The analysis hinges on a mapping to sampling without replacement and a hypergeometric concentration of the remaining budget.

When expected rewards are unknown, the authors combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP). A key property is that ALP needs only the ordering of the context-wise best expected rewards, and tolerates moderate estimation errors; UCB then ensures the correct ordering with O(log T) samples per (context, action). The resulting regret is O(JK log T) outside boundary cases, and O(√T + JK log T) at the boundaries. Extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates and a truncated-update analysis using bounded-differences coupling) and to heterogeneous costs (via an LP over all context-action pairs and an ε-first variant) are sketched, with details deferred to the supplement.

Relation to prior work: The setting is a special case of Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17], which attains O(√T) regret with higher complexity and a regret benchmark restricted to a finite policy set. The work also contrasts with Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and concave-reward/convex-constraint bandits [11–16, 20–22], which in comparable constrained settings achieve O(√T) regret and often assume static contexts or different benchmarks. The authors’ main novelty is to obtain logarithmic regret in a constrained contextual bandit model with stochastic contexts by an adaptive LP relaxation coupled with UCB.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Theorems are well-stated and the proof ideas are convincing. The ALP analysis via hypergeometric concentration and the regret decomposition for UCB-ALP are thoughtful. The handling of ranking errors (action vs. context) and the persistence of budget concentration under UCB-ALP are particularly nice.
- Completeness: The core unit-cost, known-context-distribution case is fully developed. Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are more cursory but plausible, with proof outlines and references to appendices.
- Evaluation: There are no experiments. While the focus is theoretical, even small-scale simulations would help illustrate constants, boundary effects, and robustness to misspecification.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation is concrete (clinical trials, crowdsourcing), and related work is appropriately cited. The LP solution structure and threshold j̃(ρ) are explained cleanly. Notation is standard. A brief complexity discussion (per-round cost, dependence on J, K) would improve readability.

Originality
- The adaptive LP approach that yields O(1) regret vs. the oracle (non-boundary) and enables O(log T) regret with learning appears novel in this constrained contextual setting. The boundary-case analysis and the decoupling of information acquisition from constrained decision-making are interesting contributions relative to RCB/BwK lines.

Significance
- Achieving logarithmic regret under budget constraints is an important advance for contextual, resource-limited decision-making. The approach is computationally light (solving a simple thresholded LP each round) and could influence algorithm design in more general resourceful contextual bandits.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated problem formulation with practical assumptions (finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, single budget).
- Conceptually simple and computationally efficient algorithm (ALP, UCB-ALP) with strong guarantees: O(1) regret with known rewards; O(JK log T) with learning; explicit treatment of boundary cases.
- Insightful analysis tools (sampling-without-replacement view, concentration of remaining budget; careful decomposition of regret sources).
- Good positioning relative to RCB/BwK and constrained contextual bandit literature; novelty in obtaining logarithmic regret.

Weaknesses
- Scope restrictions: finite discrete contexts/actions, single budget, fixed costs, known T and B, and i.i.d. contexts. Applicability to continuous/large context spaces or adversarial/nonstationary contexts is unclear.
- Dependence on known context distribution in core results; the unknown-distribution case requires truncation and is less clean.
- Boundary cases remain O(√T) with no matching lower bound; it is unclear whether improved rates are possible or if the boundary hardness can be characterized.
- Practical validation is thin; no empirical results. The constants hidden in O(JK log T) may be large for realistic J, K.
- Some assumptions (unique best action per context, strict ordering of u∗j) could be relaxed; sensitivity to near-ties merits discussion.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide empirical results (synthetic and a small application) to illustrate regret scaling, the effect of boundary cases, and robustness to estimation errors in π?
- Is there a lower bound showing that O(√T) is unavoidable at boundaries, or can your approach be refined (e.g., budget smoothing) to improve rates there?
- Can the requirement of knowing T and B be relaxed (e.g., anytime variants or doubling tricks)?
- What are the per-round computational costs in J, K, and how do you maintain/update the ordering efficiently?
- For heterogeneous costs, could UCB-ALP be used instead of ε-first, and under what conditions would it preserve logarithmic regret?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical result (first logarithmic regret guarantee) in a relevant constrained contextual bandit model.
- Elegant, simple algorithms with clear intuition and solid analysis.
- Good fit for NIPS: advances learning under resource constraints with potential impact.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of experiments and limited practical validation.
- Restricted modeling assumptions; extensions are only sketched.
- Boundary-case gap and absence of matching lower bounds.

Overall recommendation
A solid theoretical contribution with clear novelty and well-executed analysis. Despite the limited empirical component and scope restrictions, the paper advances our understanding of constrained contextual bandits and proposes computationally practical algorithms. I lean toward acceptance and encourage the authors to add empirical evidence and discuss boundary-case hardness and extensions more thoroughly.
