This paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget B and known horizon T, where contexts j arrive i.i.d. from π, actions include a skip action (0) and costly actions k>0 with known costs c_{j,k} and stochastic rewards in [0,1]. The central idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle via an adaptive linear-program (ALP) that replaces the hard constraint with an average remaining budget constraint bτ/τ. In the special case with known π and unit costs (c_{j,k}=1), combining ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) yields O(log T) regret under a mild non-boundary condition (ρ not equal to cumulative context probabilities), and O(√T) otherwise. The paper then extends the approach to unknown π (via empirical estimates) and heterogeneous costs, preserving similar guarantees. Relative to prior work on resourceful/budgeted bandits (e.g., [17], [16]) that achieve O(√T), the O(log T) rate in this budgeted contextual setting appears to be the first.

Quality: The technical development is careful and largely convincing. The analysis leverages a neat “sampling without replacement” view to obtain concentration of the remaining budget, enabling tight bounds. The results rely heavily on Lemma 1 (LP upper bound via static relaxation). I initially doubted the optimal sequence satisfying the average constraint, but the rebuttal clarified the argument; still, highlighting the key proof idea in the main text would help.

Clarity: The paper is very clearly written and well motivated. The LP/ALP construction and the decomposition of regret into action-ranking and context-ranking errors are explained crisply.

Originality: The adaptive LP surrogate and its coupling with UCB for budgeted contextual bandits are simple yet novel. Achieving O(log T) in this setting is a meaningful advance over O(√T).

Significance: The results advance the state of the art theoretically and suggest computationally efficient algorithms. The insights (adaptive relaxation; concentration of bτ/τ) may transfer to broader resource-constrained bandit problems.

Limitations and questions:
- The O(log T) guarantee requires a non-boundary condition; boundary cases have an Ω(√T) lower bound. Stronger universal guarantees would boost impact.
- Is computing the exact offline optimal action sequence NP-hard?
- Can the approach handle unknown horizon T (e.g., via doubling/anytime ALP preserving bτ/τ behavior)?
- In the unknown π case, can one assume u*1 > u*2 > … > u*J w.l.o.g., or does unknown π break this ordering?
- For heterogeneous costs, what is the per-round LP complexity in J,K and can one exploit structure further?

Pros:
- First O(log T) regret for budgeted contextual bandits; elegant and practical algorithm.
- Clear writing and strong motivation; solid proofs.
- Extensions to unknown π and heterogeneous costs.

Cons:
- Dependence on a mild condition; boundary degradation.
- Heavy reliance on Lemma 1; key ideas should be surfaced in main text.
- Unknown horizon not addressed.

Recommendation: Strong accept.
