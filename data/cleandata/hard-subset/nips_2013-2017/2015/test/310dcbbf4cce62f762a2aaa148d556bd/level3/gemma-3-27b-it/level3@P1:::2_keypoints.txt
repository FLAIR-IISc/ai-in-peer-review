- Tackles constrained contextual bandits with limited time and budget, highlighting practical relevance (crowdsourcing, clinical trials).  
- Main contribution: algorithms achieving logarithmic regret under constraints.  
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and provides a solid theoretical foundation.  
- Positions itself within prior work on contextual bandits, MABs, and resource allocation, and distinguishes from budget-constrained bandits.  
- Focuses on a simplified model (finite contexts, fixed costs, single budget) to enable computationally efficient algorithms and tighter bounds.  
- Connects problem to LP and DP, motivating the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation.  
- Introduces the insightful ALP algorithm as a practical, theoretically sound approximation.  
- Rigorous analysis demonstrates logarithmic regret, with noted caveats for boundary cases.  
- Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are presented but less developed.  
- Notable clarity: well-defined notation, logical flow, and thorough citations.  
- Authors transparently discuss strengths and limitations, enhancing credibility.  
- Weakness: simplifying assumptions limit immediate practical applicability and need deeper discussion of real-world impact.  
- Weakness: lack of experimental results (simulated or real-world) undermines assessment of practical performance.  
- Weakness: hidden constant factors in regret bounds are not quantified, making practical implications unclear.  
- Weakness: boundary cases yield √T regret and are underdeveloped; more insight and mitigation strategies needed.  
- Weakness: Sections 5 and 6 feel rushed; reliance on supplementary material reduces main-text readability.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach, first to claim logarithmic regret in this setting, rigorous theory, clear presentation, and significant problem importance.  
- Rejection arguments: absence of empirical validation, limiting simplifying assumptions, and insufficient exploration of boundary cases.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, but recommends adding experiments and deeper treatment of boundary cases in future work.
