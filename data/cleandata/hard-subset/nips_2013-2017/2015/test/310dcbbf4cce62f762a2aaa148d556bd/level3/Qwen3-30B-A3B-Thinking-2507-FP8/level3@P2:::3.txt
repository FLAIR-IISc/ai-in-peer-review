

# Review for "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The problem is motivated by practical applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing where both time and resource constraints are present. The authors consider a setting with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which is justified in many real-world scenarios. They make a significant contribution by developing algorithms that achieve O(log T) regret for this problem under certain conditions, which is a notable improvement over prior work that achieved only O(√T) regret.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) It introduces an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation for the oracle when statistics are known, which achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases); (2) It combines ALP with UCB to create the UCB-ALP algorithm that achieves O(log T) regret for the unknown statistics case; and (3) It generalizes these results to systems with unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature. Previous work on contextual bandits [1,2,3,5,6,7] does not consider budget constraints, while prior work on budgeted bandits [11-16] doesn't handle contextual bandits. The paper properly cites and distinguishes from [17] (Resourceful Contextual Bandits), which achieves O(√T) regret with a restricted benchmark. The authors convincingly argue that their work is the first to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits.

The quality of the paper is excellent. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-structured proofs and careful treatment of boundary cases. The authors provide a clear upper bound (Lemma 1) that enables the regret analysis, and they address potential concerns about the correctness of this bound in the supplementary material. The paper is technically sound and complete, not just a position paper. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, noting that the O(log T) guarantee holds only for non-boundary cases where ρ ≠ q_j for any j ∈ {1, ..., J-1}, with O(√T) regret in boundary cases.

The clarity of the paper is outstanding. The writing is precise and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to analysis. The key insights (ALP approximation, combining with UCB) are clearly explained, and the paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The supplementary material contains additional technical details for those interested in deeper exploration.

The originality of the work is strong. While the idea of approximating a DP problem with LP has been used before [17,19], the specific design and analysis of ALP in this constrained contextual bandit setting is novel. The paper successfully combines this approximation with UCB to achieve the first known logarithmic regret bound for this problem. The authors carefully distinguish their work from related contributions, including recent extensions [21,22] that also consider dynamic contexts but achieve only O(√T) regret.

The significance of the work is high. The problem addressed is important in practice, and the O(log T) regret bound represents a substantial improvement over prior O(√T) results. The algorithms are computationally efficient and can be implemented with minimal overhead. The paper provides valuable theoretical insights about how to decouple information acquisition and decision making in constrained bandit settings, which could guide future work on more general constrained contextual bandits.

The main limitation is the condition for the O(log T) regret bound, which requires that the budget-to-time ratio ρ doesn't equal any cumulative probability q_j = ∑_{j'=1}^j π_{j'} (the boundary cases). The authors acknowledge this limitation and show that in these cases, the regret is O(√T). While the authors argue that these boundary cases have zero Lebesgue measure, it would be interesting to explore whether a more universal guarantee could be achieved. The paper also leaves open questions about extending the results to unknown time horizon T and whether computing the exact optimal action sequence is NP-hard.

The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The technical content is sound, the analysis is rigorous, and the results are significant. The authors have carefully addressed related work and positioned their contribution within the broader literature.

**Recommendation**: Strong Accept

**Pro**: 
- First work to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits
- Novel ALP approximation with clear theoretical justification
- Rigorous regret analysis with careful treatment of boundary cases
- Well-written and clearly organized paper
- Practical algorithms with computational efficiency

**Con**:
- O(log T) guarantee requires a mild condition (non-boundary cases)
- Boundary cases require O(√T) regret, which is less desirable than logarithmic
- Open questions about NP-hardness of exact optimal sequence and extension to unknown horizon

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the contextual bandit literature by addressing a practically important constraint (budget) and providing a theoretically sound solution with logarithmic regret. The work is well-executed, well-motivated, and represents a meaningful advance in the field.
