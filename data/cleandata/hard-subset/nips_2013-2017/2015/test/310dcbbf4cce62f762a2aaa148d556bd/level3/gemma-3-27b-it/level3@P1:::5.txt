This paper addresses the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, where the agent must balance exploration and exploitation under both time and budget limitations. This is a significant extension of the standard contextual bandit framework and a problem with clear relevance to real-world applications like clinical trials and online hiring. The authors make a strong contribution by developing an algorithm, UCB-ALP, that achieves logarithmic regret – a key milestone in this area, and to the best of their knowledge, the first to do so.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It begins with a thorough introduction to the problem, motivating it with practical examples and effectively outlining the limitations of existing approaches. The authors carefully define their system model and clearly state their assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint). They convincingly argue that these simplifications, while limiting the generality, are crucial for designing computationally tractable algorithms and achieving strong theoretical guarantees.

The core idea of approximating the oracle using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) is elegant and insightful. The authors demonstrate that ALP achieves near-optimality when system statistics are known and then cleverly combine it with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method to handle the unknown reward case. The UCB-ALP algorithm appears well-designed, leveraging the strengths of both component methods. 

The theoretical analysis is rigorous and detailed. The authors provide strong regret bounds, demonstrating logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases) and carefully outlining the challenges associated with the analysis, particularly when dealing with fluctuating budgets and context arrivals. They also extend their analysis to address scenarios with unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, showcasing the flexibility of their approach. 

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the authors address scalability concerns, future work could explore leveraging parametric reward models, especially when dealing with high-dimensional context spaces.  This would be a natural extension to consider.

In Section 4.1, the rationale for combining UCB with ALP could be more explicitly articulated. While the authors allude to it, explicitly stating the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle and drawing a parallel to policy updates in algorithms like UCRL2 would solidify the connection for readers familiar with reinforcement learning.

The absence of numerical experiments is a minor weakness. While the theoretical analysis is compelling, demonstrating the performance of UCB-ALP on synthetic or real-world datasets would further bolster the paper's claims and provide valuable insights into its practical performance.  

Finally, the authors should consider referencing the work in arXiv:1506.03374, which tackles similar constrained bandit problems, though with a different focus and regret bounds.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** This is the first work to demonstrate logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under reasonable assumptions.
* **Significance:** The problem addressed is important and has practical relevance. Achieving logarithmic regret is a significant advance.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with the field.
* **Originality:**  The combination of LP approximation and UCB is a novel approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of numerical experiments is a limitation, though not a fatal one.
* **Limited Generality:** The assumptions of finite contexts and fixed costs restrict the applicability to certain scenarios. However, the authors acknowledge this and provide a clear justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits. The authors present a well-motivated problem, a novel and effective algorithm, and a rigorous theoretical analysis. Despite the minor limitations, the paper merits acceptance to NIPS 2015. It is a high-quality scientific contribution that is likely to be influential in future research.
