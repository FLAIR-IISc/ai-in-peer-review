## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, introducing a novel approach to achieve logarithmic regret – a significant step forward in this area. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing work on constrained bandits, particularly the inapplicability of prior results to contextual settings and the computational complexity of existing solutions. The paper’s core contribution lies in approximating the oracle solution using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP), and then combining this with the UCB method to handle unknown reward distributions. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the practical relevance of budget-constrained bandit problems in domains like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors build a strong theoretical foundation, starting with a detailed analysis of the oracle in known-statistics scenarios, and progressively generalizing to unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs. The use of LP relaxation and the adaptive budget constraint in ALP are insightful and well-justified. The extension to unknown context distributions via empirical estimates, while acknowledged as requiring further analysis, demonstrates a practical direction for future work. 

A key strength of the paper is its rigorous analysis. The proofs, while complex, appear sound and the authors carefully address the challenges introduced by the budget constraint and adaptive algorithms. The discussion of boundary cases and their impact on regret is also commendable. The comparison to existing work, including recent extensions of [20] and [17], is thorough and accurately positions the paper’s contributions.

However, the paper does raise some questions. The justification for treating ρ = q<sub>j</sub> as a “special” case, particularly for j > 1, feels somewhat underdeveloped. While the authors acknowledge these as boundary cases with higher regret, a more intuitive explanation of *why* these cases are problematic would be beneficial. Furthermore, the comparison to the baseline in [17] highlights that the paper focuses on a stronger baseline that uses adaptive linear programming to skip a fixed percent of suboptimal rounds (a “budget”). It would be helpful to discuss why this baseline is stronger and how it impacts the interpretation of the results. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel approach to constrained contextual bandits, achieving logarithmic regret – a first in this area.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous and well-supported by theoretical results.
*   **Significance:** The results are important for applications with budget constraints and advance the state-of-the-art in constrained bandit problems.
*   **Interesting Setup:** The problem formulation and proposed solutions are insightful and well-motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The justification for the special treatment of ρ = q<sub>j</sub> could be strengthened.
*   Further clarification on the comparison to the baseline in [17] would be helpful.

**Overall:**

Despite these minor concerns, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field. The authors have tackled a challenging problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
