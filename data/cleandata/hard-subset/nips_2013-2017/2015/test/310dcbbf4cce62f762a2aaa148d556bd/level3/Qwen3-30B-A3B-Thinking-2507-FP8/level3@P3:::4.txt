

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, a significant extension of traditional contextual bandits where the agent faces both budget and time constraints. The authors propose a novel approach combining Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) with Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) methods to achieve logarithmic regret, which is a major contribution as prior work on constrained bandits typically achieves only sublinear regret. The paper builds upon the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17] but makes meaningful simplifications (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint) that enable computationally efficient algorithms with improved regret bounds.

The paper's key insight is recognizing that the oracle solution for constrained contextual bandits can be approximated by linear programming when the system statistics are known. The ALP algorithm adjusts the budget constraint dynamically based on remaining time and budget, achieving O(1) regret except for boundary cases where it achieves O(√T) regret. This approximation is particularly valuable because it only requires the ordering of expected rewards rather than their exact values, making it compatible with UCB methods for unknown statistics. The authors then design UCB-ALP, which combines UCB with ALP, and prove it achieves O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases, a significant improvement over the O(√T) regret of prior methods like Mixture Elimination [17].

The paper makes a strong case for the novelty of its approach. While [20] and subsequent works [21,22] address similar problems, they focus on static contexts or achieve only O(√T) regret in the dynamic context setting. The authors correctly note that their adaptive budget constraint (bτ/τ) is key to achieving logarithmic regret, which is not possible with fixed budget constraints. The paper also makes a notable contribution by providing a rigorous analysis of how to handle the complex interactions between information acquisition and decision making in constrained bandits.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a clear theoretical analysis. The proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 are well-structured and build upon solid foundations. The authors carefully address the challenging interactions between budget fluctuations, context arrivals, and ranking errors. However, I have one concern about the treatment of boundary cases. The paper identifies boundary cases where ρ = qj for some j, but doesn't sufficiently explain why these specific points are special, particularly for j > 1. The analysis shows that at these points the regret becomes O(√T), but the authors don't provide a clear intuition for why ρ = qj should be special. This is a weakness in the theoretical analysis that could be strengthened.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The key insights about ALP's ability to tolerate estimation errors and the importance of adaptive budget constraints are well-explained. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the supplementary material appears to contain the necessary technical details. The comparison to prior work is thorough, though the paper could better clarify that ALP is not a baseline but the core technique being proposed.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original in its approach to constrained contextual bandits. To my knowledge, this is the first work to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting under the specified assumptions. The combination of ALP with UCB is novel and the analysis techniques for handling boundary cases are particularly valuable. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, citing relevant work on contextual bandits [1-7], non-contextual bandits with budget constraints [11-16], and resourceful contextual bandits [17].

**Significance:** This work is highly significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. The problem of constrained contextual bandits is important in applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing, where budget constraints are ubiquitous. The paper provides the first algorithm with logarithmic regret for this setting, which represents a substantial improvement over prior O(√T) results. The insights about decoupling information acquisition and decision making through adaptive linear relaxation could inform future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

**Strengths:**
- First to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits
- Insightful analysis of the ALP approximation and its regret properties
- Clear demonstration of how to combine ALP with UCB for unknown statistics
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with careful handling of boundary cases
- Practical computational efficiency compared to prior approaches

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient explanation for why ρ = qj (for j > 1) is special in boundary cases
- The paper compares to Mixture Elimination [17] but doesn't clearly establish why their approach is superior beyond the regret bound
- The claim that "this is the first work that shows how to achieve logarithmic regret" could be strengthened by more thorough comparison with the recently published works [21,22] that also address dynamic context bandits

Overall, this is an excellent contribution to the contextual bandit literature. The paper's theoretical insights are valuable and its practical implications are significant. The only notable weakness is the insufficient explanation of boundary cases, but this does not diminish the paper's strong contribution. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS 2015 and would be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers working on bandit problems. The work represents a meaningful step toward solving the open problem of efficient constrained contextual bandits. I recommend acceptance.
