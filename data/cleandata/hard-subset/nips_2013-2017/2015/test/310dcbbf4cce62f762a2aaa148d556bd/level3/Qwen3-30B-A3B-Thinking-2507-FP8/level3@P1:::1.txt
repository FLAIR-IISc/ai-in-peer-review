

The paper studies constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, proposing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approach for known statistics and combining it with UCB for unknown statistics (UCB-ALP), claiming logarithmic regret except for boundary cases. The problem is well-motivated by applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing, where both budget and time constraints exist alongside contextual information.

The main technical contribution is the ALP algorithm, which approximates the oracle solution by dynamically adjusting the budget constraint to the remaining budget-to-time ratio. The authors show ALP achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases) and UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases. This is positioned as the first work achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.

Strengths include a clear problem formulation, well-structured theoretical analysis, and practical algorithm design. The connection to prior work on contextual bandits (e.g., [1-3,7]) and budgeted bandits (e.g., [11-16]) is appropriately acknowledged, and the paper demonstrates a good understanding of the field. The algorithms are computationally efficient and well-suited for real-world implementation.

However, the novelty claim is questionable. The problem can be naturally viewed as a standard bandit problem with JK arms (each context-action pair is an arm) and a budget constraint, which has been studied under the bandits with knapsacks framework (e.g., [12,13]). The authors should explicitly compare their approach to these results. The claimed "logarithmic regret" is logarithmic in T but linear in JK, which is a significant factor not properly contextualized. The paper states this is "logarithmic" without clarifying this dependency on the number of context-action pairs.

The regret bound in Theorem 2 (O(√T + JK log T)) requires clarification. For standard bandits with budget constraints, regret is typically O(√(KT)) in the worst case, not O(√T). The authors should verify this rate. Additionally, the problem-dependent bound (O(JK log T)) should be expressed in terms of arm gaps (as standard in bandit literature), not just as a factor of JK, which is insufficiently informative. The supplementary material's bound needs reworking to address this.

Most significantly, the paper lacks matching lower bounds, making it impossible to assess the tightness of their results. The authors claim "logarithmic regret" without providing lower bounds showing this is optimal. The problem-dependent bound should use sums of inverse arm gaps, not a KJ factor. The analysis of the boundary cases (where regret is O(√T)) is not fully justified, and it's unclear why these cases are excluded from the logarithmic regret guarantee.

The paper's key insight that the constrained contextual bandit problem can be approximated with ALP is valuable, but this approach appears to be a natural extension of existing methods rather than a novel contribution. The authors should clarify how this differs from the bandits with knapsacks setting [12,13] and how their results advance beyond the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17].

Quality is generally good, with sound technical analysis, but the lack of lower bounds and insufficient novelty weaken the contribution. Clarity is high, with well-organized exposition. Originality is the main concern, as the problem is well-studied in related contexts, and the solution doesn't appear substantially novel. Significance is moderate, as the problem is important but the solution doesn't provide a dramatic advance.

Pro acceptance: Clear problem formulation, well-motivated applications, technically sound analysis of the ALP approach.

Con acceptance: Overstated novelty, missing lower bounds, unclear regret rate in Theorem 2, problem-dependent bound not expressed in terms of arm gaps.

The paper makes a technical contribution to constrained contextual bandits, but the novelty is limited and the analysis is incomplete without lower bounds. The authors should clarify the relationship to bandits with knapsacks and provide matching lower bounds to strengthen their claims. Without these clarifications, the paper's contribution appears marginal compared to existing work. I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence of novelty and incomplete theoretical analysis.
