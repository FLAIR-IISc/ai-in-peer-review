This paper proposes algorithms with logarithmic or sublinear regret for constrained contextual bandits, a problem that has attracted significant attention due to its wide applications in personalized services. The authors' approach is to decouple the close interactions between information acquisition and decision making by adaptive linear relaxation, which achieves near-optimal performance while tolerating certain estimation errors of system parameters.

The paper starts by questioning why boundary cases must be analyzed differently and why ρ = q_j should be special, especially for j > 1. This is a valid concern, as the current analysis of the paper focuses on non-boundary cases and may not be applicable to boundary cases.

The authors then introduce a stronger baseline that uses adaptive linear programming to skip a fixed percent of suboptimal rounds, referred to as a "budget." This is an interesting setup, as it allows for a more nuanced analysis of the tradeoff between exploration and exploitation.

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of the UCB-ALP algorithm, which combines the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method with adaptive linear programming (ALP). The authors show that UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases, where it achieves O( √ T ) regret.

The strengths of the paper include its interesting setup and the uniqueness of its solutions. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the regret bounds of UCB-ALP, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, there are some weaknesses to the paper. Firstly, the analysis of boundary cases is not as thorough as the analysis of non-boundary cases. The authors should provide more insight into why boundary cases are special and how they can be handled.

Secondly, the paper assumes that the context distribution is known, which may not be the case in practice. The authors should provide more discussion on how to handle unknown context distributions.

Lastly, the paper could benefit from more numerical simulations to demonstrate the effectiveness of UCB-ALP in practice.

In conclusion, this paper provides a significant contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits. The authors' approach of decoupling information acquisition and decision making by adaptive linear relaxation is novel and effective. However, there are some weaknesses to the paper that should be addressed in future work.
