This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors address the exploration-exploitation tradeoff in the presence of budget and time constraints, which introduces complex coupling among contexts over time. The paper proposes an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle, which achieves near-optimality and only requires the ordering of expected rewards. The authors then combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to propose a UCB-ALP algorithm for constrained contextual bandits with unknown expected rewards.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and adequately references related work. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem, including a clear explanation of the challenges introduced by budget constraints. The technical contributions are solid, and the proofs are convincing. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the regret bounds for the proposed algorithms.

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of the UCB-ALP algorithm, which achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases. The authors claim that this is the first work to obtain logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits. The paper also provides insights into the design of computationally efficient algorithms for general constrained contextual bandits.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposal of a novel algorithm that achieves logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits.
* A thorough analysis of the regret bounds for the proposed algorithms.
* A clear and well-organized presentation of the problem and the proposed solutions.
* A detailed analysis of the impact of budget constraints on the exploration-exploitation tradeoff.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The paper assumes finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which may not be realistic in all scenarios.
* The paper does not provide experimental results to validate the performance of the proposed algorithms.
* The paper does not discuss the practical implications of the proposed algorithms in real-world applications.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the technical contributions are solid. The proposal of the UCB-ALP algorithm is a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits, and the analysis of the regret bounds provides valuable insights into the design of computationally efficient algorithms.
