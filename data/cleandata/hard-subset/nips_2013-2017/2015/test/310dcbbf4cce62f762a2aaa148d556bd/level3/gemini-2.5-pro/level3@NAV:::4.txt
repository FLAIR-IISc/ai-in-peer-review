This paper addresses the challenging problem of contextual bandits under both budget and time constraints. The key contribution is the design and analysis of an algorithm, UCB-ALP, that achieves logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over previous sublinear bounds found in related work like Resourceful Contextual Bandits. The authors first develop an elegant Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) framework to approximate the intractable oracle policy. This ALP serves as a strong, adaptive baseline that, unlike prior work using fixed budget constraints, adjusts to the remaining budget-to-time ratio. They then skillfully combine this with a UCB-style exploration mechanism to handle unknown rewards, demonstrating logarithmic regret in most scenarios.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its novel and significant results. The problem setup is well-motivated and the proposed solution is unique, providing the first logarithmic regret bounds for this setting. The introduction of the ALP approximation is a particularly strong contribution; by comparing their learning algorithm to this powerful adaptive baseline, the authors provide a more compelling case for its performance than comparison to a static oracle would allow. The theoretical analysis appears sound, building logically from the known-statistics case to the more general learning problem.

The main weakness is in the exposition surrounding the "boundary cases." While the analysis shows a performance degradation to O(sqrt(T)) when the budget ratio ρ equals a cumulative context probability q_j, the paper lacks a clear intuitive explanation for why these points are fundamentally different. A clearer discussion on why ρ = q_j should be a special, more difficult case would significantly strengthen the paper's clarity and impact. Why does the ALP approximation become less effective precisely at these thresholds?

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a significant theoretical contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The novel UCB-ALP algorithm achieves a state-of-the-art logarithmic regret bound for an important problem.
*   The ALP framework is an elegant and powerful analytical tool that is of independent interest.
*   The work clearly advances the field of constrained reinforcement learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The lack of intuition regarding the boundary cases is a notable omission that hampers a full understanding of the core result.

The paper's strengths in originality and significance are substantial. I recommend acceptance, strongly encouraging the authors to elaborate on the intuition behind the boundary case analysis in the final version.
