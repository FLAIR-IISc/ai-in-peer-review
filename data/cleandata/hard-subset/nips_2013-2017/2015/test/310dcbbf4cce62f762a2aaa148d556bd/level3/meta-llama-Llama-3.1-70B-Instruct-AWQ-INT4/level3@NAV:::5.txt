This paper presents a novel approach to solving the constrained contextual bandit problem, where the agent has to make decisions under budget and time constraints. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle solution and achieves near-optimal performance. They also extend ALP to the case with unknown expected rewards by combining it with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the problem, the proposed algorithms, and the theoretical analysis. The authors provide a thorough review of the related work and clearly highlight the contributions of their paper.

The main contributions of the paper are:

* The proposal of the ALP algorithm, which achieves near-optimal performance for constrained contextual bandits with known expected rewards.
* The extension of ALP to the case with unknown expected rewards by combining it with UCB, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm.
* The theoretical analysis of the regret bounds for both ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms.

The strengths of the paper are:

* The proposal of a novel algorithm that achieves near-optimal performance for constrained contextual bandits.
* The extension of the algorithm to the case with unknown expected rewards.
* The thorough theoretical analysis of the regret bounds.

The weaknesses of the paper are:

* The assumption of discrete contexts and actions, which may not be realistic in all scenarios.
* The lack of numerical experiments to validate the performance of the proposed algorithms.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits and provides a novel approach to solving this problem. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Recommendation:

I recommend accepting this paper for publication. The paper presents a novel approach to solving the constrained contextual bandit problem and provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the regret bounds. The authors clearly highlight the contributions of their paper and provide a thorough review of the related work.

However, I suggest that the authors consider the following:

* Providing numerical experiments to validate the performance of the proposed algorithms.
* Discussing the limitations of the assumption of discrete contexts and actions and potential extensions to more general scenarios.
* Clarifying the rationale for combining UCB with ALP in Section 4.1 and explicitly stating the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle and its relation to UCRL2 policy updates.
* Considering parametric reward models for scalability when contexts are numerous.
* Including a citation to arXiv:1506.03374.
