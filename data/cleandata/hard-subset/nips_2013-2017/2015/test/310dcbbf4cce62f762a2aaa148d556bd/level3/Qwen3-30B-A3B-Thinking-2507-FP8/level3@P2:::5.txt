

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, where the agent faces both time and budget constraints, and makes a significant contribution by providing the first algorithm that achieves logarithmic regret in this setting. The authors propose UCB-ALP, a computationally efficient algorithm that combines Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method. The key insight is that ALP approximates the oracle solution with near-optimal performance (O(1) regret in non-boundary cases) while only requiring the ordering of expected rewards rather than their exact values, making it compatible with UCB for unknown statistics.

The paper builds on previous NIPS work on contextual bandits (e.g., [1,2,3,7]) and budget-constrained bandits (e.g., [11-16]), but addresses a critical gap: previous budget-constrained bandit results don't apply to contextual settings. The authors correctly note that Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] achieves O(√T) regret but with a restricted benchmark and high computational complexity, while their work shows how to achieve logarithmic regret under practical assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint).

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, with clear organization and logical progression. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful distinction between boundary and non-boundary cases, and the regret analysis handles complex interactions between context ranking errors and budget fluctuations. The authors demonstrate that UCB-ALP achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases (where J is the number of contexts and K the number of actions), which is order-optimal.

Strengths:
- The ALP approximation is elegant and provides a novel approach to decoupling information acquisition and decision making in constrained bandit problems.
- The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, a problem not previously solved.
- The analysis is thorough, with careful handling of boundary cases and clear separation of regret sources.
- The paper provides valuable insights for future work on general constrained contextual bandits.

Weaknesses:
- The focus on unit-cost systems may limit immediate applicability to some real-world scenarios with heterogeneous costs (though Section 6 addresses this extension).
- The paper lacks numerical experiments to validate theoretical results and demonstrate practical performance.
- The relationship with arXiv:1506.03374 (Agrawal et al.) should be more explicitly discussed in the related work section.

Suggested improvements:
1. Add numerical experiments to validate the theoretical results and demonstrate practical performance.
2. Clarify the rationale for combining UCB with ALP in Section 4.1 by explicitly stating how the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle applies and relating it to UCRL2 policy updates.
3. Consider parametric reward models for scalability when contexts are numerous.
4. Explicitly discuss the relationship with arXiv:1506.03374 in the related work section.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First paper to demonstrate logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.
- High technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis.
- Practical relevance to applications like clinical trials and online recommendation.
- Well-written with clear exposition and logical flow.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scope to unit-cost systems in the main analysis.
- Lack of empirical validation.

Overall, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It makes a clear, important contribution to the field of contextual bandits with budget constraints. The work advances the state of the art by providing a computationally efficient algorithm with near-optimal regret, and the theoretical insights will be valuable to researchers working on constrained bandit problems.

The paper is technically sound, well-organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses (lack of experiments, limited scope) are common in theoretical papers and could be addressed in future work. The authors' suggestions for future work on scalability and numerical validation are reasonable.

I recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear, important contribution to the field of contextual bandits, with results that are both theoretically interesting and practically relevant. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, advancing the state of the art in a demonstrable way while maintaining the high technical standards expected at the conference.
