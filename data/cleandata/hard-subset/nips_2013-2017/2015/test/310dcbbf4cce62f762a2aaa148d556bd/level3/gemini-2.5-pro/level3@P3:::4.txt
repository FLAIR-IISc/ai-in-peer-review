This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a resource budget (B). This setting, which the authors call "constrained contextual bandits," is a practical and important extension of the standard model, but the dual constraints introduce significant coupling between decisions over time, making the optimal policy intractable. The authors' main contribution is a computationally efficient algorithm, UCB-ALP, that achieves logarithmic regret for this problem, a significant improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) regret of prior work.

The core of the paper's approach is to first develop a near-optimal policy for the setting where all statistics (reward expectations, context probabilities) are known. Instead of solving the full dynamic program, they propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy. At each step, ALP solves a simple LP using the *remaining* budget and time to decide whether to act on the current context. This adaptive policy is shown to achieve O(1) regret except for a specific set of "boundary cases" where regret is O(sqrt(T)). Building on this, the authors introduce the UCB-ALP algorithm for the bandit setting where rewards are unknown. By combining the UCB principle for exploration with the ALP framework for budget management, they prove that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) in boundary cases. The paper also discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs. This work relates to previous research on bandits with knapsacks and resourceful contextual bandits but is the first to achieve logarithmic regret in this specific constrained setting.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and challenging problem. The addition of a budget constraint fundamentally changes the nature of the optimal policy from a simple greedy approach to a complex planning problem. The proposed solution is novel and elegant. The idea of decoupling the problem by using an adaptive LP as a near-optimal oracle approximation is a key insight that allows the authors to cleanly integrate a UCB-style learning component. Achieving logarithmic regret is a significant theoretical advance for this problem class.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The formulation of the static LP as an upper bound and the development of the Adaptive LP (ALP) policy is a clever way to create a tractable benchmark and algorithm. The analysis showing that ALP achieves constant regret (in non-boundary cases) is impressive and provides a solid foundation for the main bandit results. The regret analysis for UCB-ALP, which separates the regret into components due to action-ranking errors and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation errors, is rigorous and well-executed. The comparison is made against a strong baseline—the near-optimal ALP oracle—which strengthens the final result.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It builds its case effectively, starting from the simplified known-statistics case to motivate the ALP algorithm and then progressing to the full UCB-ALP algorithm for the bandit setting. The sketch of the proof provides good intuition for the main analytical steps.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Explanation of Boundary Cases:** The analysis hinges on the distinction between "boundary" (`ρ = q_j`) and "non-boundary" cases. While technically defined, the paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why these specific points are so problematic. The regret for the known-statistics ALP algorithm jumps from O(1) to O(sqrt(T)) at these boundaries, which then carries over to the UCB-ALP analysis. A deeper discussion on why the system dynamics become so sensitive to the budget-to-time ratio `b_τ/τ` at exactly these cumulative probability thresholds would be very insightful for the reader. Is it because the optimal LP solution becomes degenerate or highly sensitive to its inputs? Clarifying this would turn a potential point of confusion into a strength.

2.  **Simplified Assumptions:** The analysis is performed under a number of simplifying assumptions, such as a discrete and finite number of contexts, i.i.d. context arrivals, and unit costs (in the main sections). While this is a standard and reasonable approach to make initial theoretical progress, it does limit the direct applicability compared to more general frameworks like that of Badanidiyuru et al. (2014), even if that work achieves a worse regret bound.

**Questions for Authors:**

*   Could you please elaborate on the intuition behind the boundary cases? The proof sketch mentions relating the regret to the variance of the remaining budget `b_τ`, but a more direct explanation of why the expected reward function `v(·)` becomes sensitive around points `ρ = q_j` would be helpful. Why is `ρ = q_1` (i.e., the budget is exactly enough to serve the best context on average) a non-boundary case, while `ρ = q_j` for `j > 1` is?
*   The extension to unknown context distributions in Section 5 analyzes a "truncated" version of the proposed algorithm. Could you comment on the technical challenges that prevent a direct analysis of the fully online UCB-EALP algorithm, where the empirical context distribution is updated at every step?

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the contextual bandit literature. It tackles an important problem with a novel and effective method, delivering a state-of-the-art theoretical result. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to the clarity of certain technical points. The work is of high quality, original, and significant, and I strongly recommend acceptance.
