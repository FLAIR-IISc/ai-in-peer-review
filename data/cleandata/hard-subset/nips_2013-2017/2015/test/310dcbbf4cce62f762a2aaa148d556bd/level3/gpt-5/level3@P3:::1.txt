Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with a global budget and time horizon. With finite contexts and actions, unit costs, and known context distribution, it proposes an LP relaxation of the DP oracle, and an adaptive version (ALP) that plugs in the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. ALP is shown to be within O(1) of the oracle except on measure-zero “boundary” parameters where ρ equals a cumulative context probability, in which case the gap is O(√T). For unknown rewards, ALP is combined with UCB (UCB-ALP) to achieve O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. The paper sketches extensions to unknown context distribution via empirical estimates and to heterogeneous costs.

Relation to prior work
The setting is a special case of Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) and Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK). RCB/BwK offer general frameworks with O(√T) regret but high computational cost and policy-set restrictions. Here, the authors restrict to finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, and a single budget, and design a simple, efficient algorithm. The paper also relates to contextual bandits (linear/similarity) and prior budgeted non-contextual bandits that achieve logarithmic or sublinear regret. The main claimed novelty is achieving logarithmic regret in a constrained contextual bandit with random context arrivals by adaptively tracking bτ/τ in the LP.

Quality
Theory: The decomposition via a static LP upper bound and concentration of bτ is clean. For known rewards, the O(1) non-boundary gap is technically interesting and helps isolate the learning cost. For UCB-ALP, the O(JK log T) term is standard for identifying JK means, and the boundary √T term is attributed to bτ fluctuations. Proof sketches are sensible; full details are deferred to the supplement.

Experiments: Limited discussion; empirical evaluation seems secondary.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written, with a clear problem statement and algorithm descriptions. Some presentation could be streamlined (e.g., emphasize early that ALP only needs ordering). The supplement’s problem-dependent bound would benefit from a cleaner statement in terms of arm gaps (see comments below).

Originality
The ALP idea (adaptive LP using bτ/τ) is intuitive and has appeared in spirit in BwK/RCB relaxations, but the O(1) analysis for unit-cost, finite contexts is a nice refinement. The UCB-ALP combination is natural. The main question is whether, under these simplifications, the logarithmic regret is fundamentally new or largely a restatement of running UCB over JK “context-action” arms with an action-rate constraint.

Significance
If the logarithmic rates hold with tight instance-dependent constants and extend robustly to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs, the work could be useful in budgeted personalization applications. The computational simplicity of ALP is a plus.

Strengths
- Clear formulation and efficient algorithms (ALP, UCB-ALP) with simple implementation.
- Technically neat concentration argument for bτ and O(1) optimality gap (non-boundary).
- Achieves O(log T) regret in a constrained contextual bandit with random contexts.
- Thoughtful discussion of extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty: With finite J, K and large T, the coupling among contexts seems to vanish due to concentration of bτ/τ, making the problem close to a budgeted bandit over JK arms with an action-rate (or activity) constraint. This makes the main O(log T) result unsurprising. Please clarify the conceptual novelty beyond this reduction.
- Treating contexts independently: If coupling vanishes for large T, why can’t we treat each occurrence of a context as an independent bandit instance with an appropriate per-context budget share (e.g., ρπjT), and run standard UCB per context? What precisely fails in such a reduction under the hard budget and stopping time? Providing a formal comparison or counterexample would help.
- Regret rates in Theorem 2: The boundary-case rate is stated as O(√T + JK log T). Is the √T term truly independent of K (driven only by budget randomness), or should it be O(√(K T) + JK log T) when learning is active? Please justify the dependence carefully.
- Problem-dependent bounds: The current bounds are problem-independent up to JK. A cleaner, instance-dependent upper bound in terms of arm gaps Δj,k is desirable. In particular, the learning term should scale like sum over suboptimal actions of (log T)/Δj,k plus a “threshold-gap” term involving the context-gap Δctx = u∗j̃ − u∗j̃+1. This would be sharper than a crude JK factor and align with classical UCB analyses.
- Proof approach: The current argument based on UCB ordering events across contexts seems to create loose JK factors. A decomposition that counts pulls of each suboptimal action (within-context) and only accounts for context-ranking mistakes near the LP threshold should yield a sum-of-inverse-gaps structure. Consider reworking the analysis accordingly.
- Lower bounds: Please provide matching lower bounds:
  - Problem-dependent: Ω(∑(log T)/Δj,k) plus a term that captures the threshold-gap and budget coupling, not reducible to JK independent bandits.
  - Problem-independent: Nontrivial Ω(√T) (or Ω(√(K T))) that incorporates context complexity and the budget coupling, beyond trivial reductions to classical MAB.
- “Coupling vanishes” claim: The paper informally argues that with large T the coupling across contexts disappears. This is central to the story; it deserves a precise statement and conditions. What rates of concentration are necessary to justify LP decisions being optimal up to lower-order terms? How does this extend to heterogeneous costs where per-context action mixes matter?
- Unknown context distribution: The truncated EALP analysis is interesting but somewhat ad hoc. Can you quantify the bias introduced by stopping updates and show that fully online UCB-EALP attains the same rates?
- Novelty claim: Please situate your O(log T) result vis-à-vis RCB/BwK and budgeted MABs. Why is this not simply a constrained JK-armed bandit with an activity constraint and thus expected to be log(T), given finite arms and i.i.d. contexts?

Requested clarifications and suggestions
- Provide a clean, instance-dependent upper bound in terms of Δj,k and Δctx; avoid JK prefactors where possible.
- Add lower bounds in both regimes, explicitly capturing context complexity and the budget threshold.
- Clarify whether each context occurrence can be treated as an independent bandit with a per-context budget, and precisely why not.
- Justify the √T term’s dependence (or independence) on K in boundary cases.
- For heterogeneous costs, characterize instance-dependent bounds involving normalized gaps ηj,k and show when multiple actions per context are optimal under ALP.

Pros
- Simple, efficient algorithm with strong empirical intuition.
- Solid concentration-based analysis yielding small oracle gap.
- Addresses a practical variant of contextual bandits with budgets.

Cons
- Novelty relative to known reductions is uncertain.
- Bounds are problem-independent and potentially loose; lack of matching lower bounds.
- Some key claims (vanishing coupling) are informal; boundary-case rates need sharper justification.

Overall evaluation
Borderline. The paper is well-executed and potentially useful, but I remain neutral on originality. I encourage the authors to clarify the novelty beyond reductions, tighten the bounds to be instance-dependent, and add meaningful lower bounds. A convincing rebuttal addressing these points could shift my recommendation.
