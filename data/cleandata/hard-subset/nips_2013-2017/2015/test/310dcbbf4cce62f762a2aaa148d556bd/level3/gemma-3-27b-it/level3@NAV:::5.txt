## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits with discrete contexts and actions, a significant extension of standard contextual bandit formulations that more closely reflects real-world applications. The authors make a substantial contribution by presenting a computationally efficient algorithm, UCB-ALP, and proving a logarithmic regret bound – a crucial result in this setting. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the problem is precisely defined, and the technical exposition is rigorous yet accessible. The gradual build-up of complexity, starting with approximations of the oracle under known statistics and progressing to the full UCB-ALP algorithm, is a particularly effective approach. The connection to existing work in contextual bandits and knapsack problems is well-established, and the authors appropriately situate their work within the broader landscape.

The core strength of this work lies in the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) technique. Approximating the oracle with LP and then intelligently adapting the constraint based on remaining budget is a novel and insightful approach. Combining this with UCB provides a practical and theoretically sound algorithm. The analysis, while complex, appears robust, and the handling of boundary cases is commendable. 

However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing a few points. The suggestion to consider parametric reward models for scalability when contexts are numerous is a valuable one; the current approach might suffer in high-dimensional context spaces.  Furthermore, the rationale for combining UCB with ALP in Section 4.1 could be more explicitly explained. A clearer articulation of how the UCB component provides the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” and its relationship to policy updates in algorithms like UCRL2 would enhance understanding. 

Finally, the inclusion of numerical experiments, as suggested, would greatly bolster the paper’s impact. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, empirical validation would provide further confidence in the algorithm's practical performance. The citation of arXiv:1506.03374 is a good addition to the related work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a novel and efficient algorithm for a challenging problem.
*   Achieves a near-optimal logarithmic regret bound.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Potential scalability issues with high-dimensional contexts.
*   The connection between UCB and ALP could be more explicitly explained.

**Overall:**

This paper represents a significant advance in the field of constrained contextual bandits. Despite the minor suggestions for improvement, the quality of the work and the importance of the results strongly support acceptance to a conference like NIPS. The authors have made a valuable contribution that is likely to be influential in future research.
