- Problem: studies contextual bandits with a single hard budget and finite horizon, discrete contexts/actions.
- Core idea: approximate DP oracle by an LP with an average-budget constraint and adapt it online to remaining average budget (ALP).
- Known-means result: ALP achieves O(1) regret except at measure-zero boundary parameters (where regret is O(√T)).
- Unknown-means result: UCB-ALP yields O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) at boundaries.
- Extensions: sketches for unknown context distributions (empirical estimation) and heterogeneous action costs.
- Relation to prior work: improves over Badanidiyuru et al. and Agrawal & Devanur by giving instance-dependent logarithmic regret in discrete settings.
- Optimism connection: approach echoes UCRL2-style optimism, but leverages LP structure and adaptive budget control.
- Theoretical quality: proofs appear sound; novel use of hypergeometric concentration and regret decomposition into action-ranking and context-threshold errors.
- Boundary handling: authors candidly acknowledge limitations and provide boundary-case analysis.
- Empirical gap: notable omission of experiments to validate practicality and constants.
- Clarity: paper is well written with clear setup and proofs; Section 4.1 should more explicitly state the optimism principle and relation to UCRL2.
- Originality: adaptive LP relaxation with tight O(1) gap and resulting log-regret algorithm are novel for discrete contextual bandits.
- Significance: provides an efficient, near-optimal algorithmic template likely to influence constrained bandit research.
- Pros: clear model and contributions with fine-grained regret bounds; computationally efficient and scalable ALP+optimism template; solid extensions.
- Cons: lacks experiments; scalability to many contexts/actions may require additional structure.
- Suggestions: add numerical experiments; consider parametric reward models (linear/GLM) to reduce JK dependence; clarify positioning relative to Agrawal & Devanur.
- Recommendation: Accept — efficient algorithm with near-optimal regret that advances the state of the art.
