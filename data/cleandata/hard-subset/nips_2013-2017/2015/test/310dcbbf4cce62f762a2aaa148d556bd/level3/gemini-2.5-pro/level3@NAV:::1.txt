This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both time and budget constraints, where these constraints create a complex coupling among decisions for different contexts. The authors propose an algorithm, UCB-ALP, which combines an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) approach for learning unknown rewards. For a setting with a finite number of contexts and actions, the authors show their algorithm achieves O(log T) regret for a large time horizon T in "non-boundary" cases. This work relates to prior research on bandits with knapsacks and resourceful contextual bandits, but aims to achieve a stronger logarithmic regret bound under a more restricted setting.

The paper's primary strength is that it addresses a significant and practical problem. The proposed ALP framework is an intuitive and elegant method for simplifying the complex oracle policy, and the logical progression from the known-parameter setting to the learning setting is clear.

However, I have several concerns regarding the work's originality and theoretical completeness. The central claim that the coupling between contexts vanishes for a large horizon T seems to reduce the problem to a more standard one. Specifically, with J contexts and K actions, the problem can be viewed as a multi-armed bandit with JK arms under a knapsack constraint. In such a formulation, achieving O(log T) regret is an expected result, which calls into question the novelty of the contribution. The authors should clarify why each context occurrence cannot be treated as an independent bandit problem from the outset.

The theoretical analysis also requires strengthening.
1.  **Lower Bounds:** The paper would be significantly improved by providing matching lower bounds for both problem-dependent and problem-independent settings. Non-trivial bounds that explicitly incorporate the complexity from context arrivals are needed to properly contextualize the results.
2.  **Upper Bounds:** The problem-dependent upper bounds presented in the supplement should be reworked into a cleaner form expressed in terms of the sum of inverse squared arm gaps (Δ⁻²), which is standard, rather than a coarse KJ factor.
3.  **Technical Clarity:** There is a potential ambiguity in Theorem 2’s regret rate. Is the O(√T + …) term correct, or should it have a dependency on K, such as O(√(KT) + …)? This should be verified.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an important problem with an interesting approach, I remain neutral due to insufficient evidence of originality and the need for a more robust and complete theoretical analysis. I encourage the authors to use their rebuttal to clarify the novelty of their framework over existing literature and address the questions regarding the theoretical bounds.
