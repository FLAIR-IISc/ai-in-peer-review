- Tackles constrained contextual bandits with budget/time limits, extending contextual bandits to realistic applications (clinical trials, crowdsourcing).
- Proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle that attains near-optimality using only reward ordering.
- Combines ALP with UCB to create UCB-ALP, which achieves O(log T) regret except in certain boundary cases.
- Claims to be the first work obtaining logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits; theoretical analysis supports this.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and well-motivated with explicit links to prior work.
- Theoretical contributions are strong, with convincing proofs for ALP and UCB-ALP.
- Simplifying assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget) are reasonable and enable elegant theory.
- Practical aspects are underdeveloped; Sections 5–6 generalizations lack experimental validation.
- Absence of empirical results makes practical relevance speculative.
- Missing implementation details and performance metrics limit usefulness for practitioners.
- Evaluation: Quality strong theoretically but weakened by missing experiments; Clarity excellent; Originality significant; Significance high but tempered by lack of empirical validation.
- Overall recommendation: technically sound and valuable; recommend acceptance with request that authors address the experimental gap in future work.
