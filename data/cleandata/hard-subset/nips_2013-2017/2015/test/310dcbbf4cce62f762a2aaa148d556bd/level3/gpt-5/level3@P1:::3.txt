This paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget and a known horizon, focusing first on discrete contexts with unit costs and known context distribution. The authors propose an adaptive linear-programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle (the optimal dynamic policy with full information), obtained by relaxing the hard budget to an average budget and solving a simple LP with a threshold structure. ALP uses the average remaining budget bτ/τ to adapt that threshold over time. With known rewards, ALP achieves near-optimality: O(1) regret in non-boundary cases (and O(√T) at boundaries where ρ aligns with cumulative context probabilities). Building on the key property that ALP only needs the ordering of expected rewards, they combine ALP with UCB to learn action rankings online, yielding UCB-ALP with regret O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on boundaries. They further discuss extensions to unknown context distribution via empirical estimates (EALP/UCB-EALP, analyzed via a truncated estimator and coupling/concentration arguments) and to heterogeneous costs via a generalized LP over all context-action pairs, with an ε-first variant for learning. The analysis hinges on a clean concentration of the remaining budget under ALP/UCB-ALP by mapping to sampling without replacement (hypergeometric), enabling separation of regret due to budget fluctuations versus ranking errors.

Relation to prior work: Compared to Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], which attains O(√T) regret relative to a finite policy benchmark with a more complex algorithm, this work achieves logarithmic regret (except at boundaries) against the oracle in discrete settings with hard budgets and is computationally simple. Prior non-contextual “bandits with knapsacks/budgets” [11–16] obtain logarithmic/sublinear regret without observable contexts; those techniques do not directly handle coupling across contexts. Recent works on bandits with concave rewards and convex knapsacks and their contextual extensions [20–22] typically yield O(√T) regret in dynamic-context settings; here the adaptive LP with hard-budget tracking sharpens the rate to O(log T) off boundary. The paper also connects to classical contextual bandits [1,5–7] and UCB [4], but crucially addresses hard budget constraints absent there.

Quality: The technical claims are precise and largely well-supported. The LP upper bound (Lemma 1) justifies using Û(T,B) as a benchmark; the ALP analysis (Theorem 1) is elegant and leverages hypergeometric concentration; and the UCB-ALP regret decomposition and counting of ranking errors is standard yet carefully adapted to the thresholded ALP structure. My initial concern about whether the static LP truly upper bounds the dynamic oracle was resolved by the authors’ argument; including a brief intuition/proof sketch in the main paper would help readers. Boundary-case rates (O(√T)) are plausible and seem near-tight, though a formal lower bound in this constrained contextual setting would be valuable.

Clarity: The paper is very clearly written and well organized. The motivation is compelling, the algorithms are simple to state, and the key ideas are explained intuitively (average remaining budget, threshold structure, rank-only requirement). Some technical details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., unknown π analysis and heterogeneous costs), but the main thread remains self-contained.

Originality: To my knowledge, this is the first work to obtain logarithmic regret in contextual bandits with hard budget constraints and random contexts. The adaptive LP surrogate for the oracle, its near-optimality, and the way it decouples learning from budgeting are novel and insightful.

Significance: The results are theoretically strong and practically appealing: ALP/UCB-ALP are computationally efficient, require only rank information, and extend to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs. This has potential impact on applications like clinical trials and online recommendation under budgets, and may influence algorithm design for more general resourceful contextual bandits.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual contribution: adaptive LP relaxation that tracks the hard budget via bτ/τ.
- Strong regret guarantees: O(1) (known rewards) and O(JK log T) (unknown rewards) off boundary.
- Computational simplicity and scalability; clean separation of learning and budgeting.
- Extensions to unknown π and heterogeneous costs with supporting analyses.
- Well written and thoroughly motivated; connections to relevant literature are made.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Guarantees degrade to O(√T) at boundary cases; stronger universal guarantees (or matching lower bounds) would strengthen the contribution.
- Analysis for unknown π relies on truncating the empirical estimator; more discussion of how this affects practice would help.
- The heterogeneous-costs algorithm requires an ε-first phase; a fully adaptive alternative (with explicit regret constants) would be valuable.

Questions and suggestions:
- Complexity of the exact oracle: Is computing the optimal dynamic policy NP-hard in this setting? A formal statement (or reference) would clarify why LP relaxations are the right surrogate.
- Unknown horizon: Can ALP/UCB-ALP be made anytime (e.g., with a doubling trick) without losing guarantees? Since decisions use bτ/τ, it seems plausible that an anytime variant is straightforward.
- Ties/ordering assumptions: In the unknown-π case, can one assume u∗1 > u∗2 > … > u∗J w.l.o.g.? Please clarify tie-breaking and how exact ties affect regret and boundary behavior.
- Practical guidance: Reported constants in the O(JK log T) term can be large. Any heuristics (e.g., KL-UCB, bootstrapped UCB, or elimination) that reduce constants while preserving rates?
- Empirical validation: If space permits, add experiments comparing to [17,20–22] and ablations highlighting the benefit of adaptive budgeting versus fixed average-budget policies.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First logarithmic regret result for constrained contextual bandits with hard budgets and random contexts.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with clear insights and strong theory.
- Broad relevance and potential to influence follow-up work on resourceful contextual bandits.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Boundary-case deterioration and lack of a matching lower bound in the contextual setting.
- Some technical details (unknown π, heterogeneous costs) are deferred and could be expanded.

Recommendation: Strong accept. The paper makes a clear, original, and significant contribution with clean theory and practical algorithmic ideas.
