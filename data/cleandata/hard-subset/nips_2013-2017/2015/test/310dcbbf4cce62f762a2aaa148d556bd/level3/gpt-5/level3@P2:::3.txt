Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies constrained contextual bandits: at each round a context j is drawn i.i.d. from a known distribution π, the learner observes j and either skips or selects an action k>0 that yields a bounded stochastic reward with expected value uj,k and incurs a cost cj,k, under a hard budget B and fixed horizon T. The goal is to maximize cumulative reward before time or budget runs out. The setting captures many applications (e.g., clinical trials, crowdsourcing) where both time and budget matter.

The main technical idea is to approximate the oracle dynamic program by an adaptive linear program (ALP) that replaces the hard budget constraint with an average budget constraint equal to the current remaining budget per remaining round, bτ/τ. In the unit-cost, known-π case, the static LP yields a simple threshold policy and an upper bound Û(T,B) on the oracle value. The proposed ALP policy adapts the threshold to bτ/τ and achieves O(1) regret for all non-boundary budget ratios ρ=B/T (and O(√T) in boundary cases ρ equal to a cumulative context probability). Building on this, the paper combines ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) to learn the uj,k and shows RUCB-ALP(T,B)=O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases (O(√T + JK log T) on the boundary). The approach is then extended to unknown π (via empirical estimates and a truncated analysis) and to heterogeneous costs (via a generalized LP and an exploration-then-ALP procedure), again yielding O(1) or O(√T) regret depending on boundary conditions.

Relative to prior work: resourceful contextual bandits (RCB) [17] achieve O(√T) regret under more general settings but with a finite policy benchmark and higher complexity; bandits with knapsacks and budgeted bandits [11–16] do not handle observable contexts; recent “concave rewards and convex knapsacks” works [20–22] attain O(√T) and focus on static or more general contexts/constraints. To my knowledge, this is the first result attaining logarithmic regret for budgeted contextual bandits with i.i.d. discrete contexts and a hard budget.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and insight: the adaptive LP relaxation decouples resource allocation from learning and yields a threshold policy that is easy to implement and analyze.
- Strong regret guarantees: O(1) regret for ALP and O(JK log T) for UCB-ALP in non-boundary cases; clear characterization and inevitability of O(√T) behavior on boundary instances.
- Computational efficiency: each round solves a small LP (reduces to sorting/thresholding in unit-cost); far simpler than DP or mixture-elimination-type procedures.
- Clear relation to an oracle and a tight upper bound Û(T,B), enabling clean regret decomposition and analysis.
- Writing is clear and well organized; motivation and technical ideas are well explained.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Boundary cases: the main O(log T) rate requires a mild non-boundary condition; when ρ lies on a cumulative mass boundary, the regret worsens to O(√T). Stronger, instance-independent guarantees would further increase impact.
- Heavy reliance on Lemma 1 (LP upper bound). I initially questioned whether an optimal sequence must satisfy the average-constraint LP, but the rebuttal clarified the argument; still, highlighting the precise coupling between the hard constraint and the average LP bound in the main text would help.
- Extensions are mostly sketched: unknown π analysis uses a truncated estimator and coupling; heterogeneous costs use an exploration-first phase. More main-text detail or empirical validation would strengthen confidence in these nontrivial extensions.

Quality
The technical development is careful and largely convincing. Mapping ALP’s budget consumption to sampling without replacement and deriving hypergeometric concentration (Lemma 2) is elegant and underpins the O(1) regret proof. The UCB-ALP analysis cleanly separates action- and context-ranking errors and leverages standard UCB tools (e.g., Lemma 3). The boundary/non-boundary split is well motivated. After reading the rebuttal, I am satisfied that Lemma 1 is correct and that Û(T,B) is a valid benchmark upper bound. Suggested clarifications:
- Make explicit the per-round computational complexity (sorting J contexts by UCB per round; LP reduces to thresholding).
- In boundary cases, quantify constants to indicate when the JK log T term dominates √T in practice.

Clarity
The paper is very clearly written and well structured. The intuition behind ALP and UCB-ALP is conveyed effectively. Minor suggestions:
- In Section 5 (unknown π), precisely state when the empirical distribution is frozen and summarize the main concentration lemma in the body.
- In Section 6 (heterogeneous costs), include a concise statement of the LP and the regret theorem in the main text.
- Clarify the assumption u∗1 > u∗2 > … > u∗J: in the known-π analysis it is w.l.o.g. via relabeling, but when π is unknown the analysis must tolerate unknown ordering; please state explicitly how this is handled (e.g., using empirical ordering only).

Originality
High. The adaptive LP-based control of budget usage to recover O(log T) regret in contextual, budgeted bandits appears novel. Prior contextual-resource works achieve O(√T) or use finite policy classes; this paper’s instance-dependent, oracle-based benchmark and logarithmic rate in the discrete i.i.d. setting are new.

Significance
The results advance the state of the art for budgeted contextual bandits by achieving logarithmic regret with efficient algorithms and clean analysis. The approach should influence work on resource-constrained online learning and RL. Limitations to discrete, i.i.d. contexts and boundary instances temper universality, but the conceptual contribution is impactful.

Arguments for acceptance
- First logarithmic regret guarantees for this constrained contextual bandit setting.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with clear theoretical insights and practical relevance.
- Strong, well-supported analysis and clear writing.

Arguments against acceptance
- Degradation to O(√T) on boundary instances; stronger uniform guarantees would be desirable.
- Some extensions (unknown π, heterogeneous costs) are deferred to the supplement; empirical results are limited.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Complexity of the exact oracle: is computing the optimal policy with hard budget and i.i.d. contexts NP-hard, or can you formalize its complexity? A discussion would contextualize the need for LP relaxations.
- Unknown horizon: can UCB-ALP be adapted to unknown T (e.g., via a doubling trick or anytime confidence bounds) without losing rates?
- Boundary instances: can you design simple perturbations (e.g., randomized tie-breaking or slight budget smoothing) that recover logarithmic regret with high probability? How would one detect in practice that ρ is near a boundary?
- Unknown π: when π is unknown, you cannot reorder contexts by true u∗j; please clarify whether the analysis assumes u∗j are distinct only for exposition, and how ties are handled.
- Empirics: adding experiments comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to RCB [17] and convex-knapsack methods [20–22] on synthetic and small real datasets would strengthen the paper.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper presents a simple and powerful idea—adaptive linear relaxation—that yields the first logarithmic regret guarantees for budgeted contextual bandits, with clean analysis and practical algorithms. Despite boundary-case limitations, the contribution is novel, technically solid, and significant.
