

# Review: "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits with time and budget constraints, where the agent must balance exploration and exploitation while respecting both a time horizon T and a budget B. The authors claim to achieve O(log T) regret for this problem, which they argue is the first such result in the literature. They introduce an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle solution by adjusting budget constraints based on remaining time and budget. This is then combined with UCB to form the UCB-ALP algorithm, which they claim achieves logarithmic regret in non-boundary cases.

The work builds on prior constrained bandit research, particularly the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17], which achieved O(√T) regret. The authors argue their work is novel because it achieves logarithmic regret under simplified assumptions (finite contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint). They also reference related work on bandits with knapsack constraints [11-16] and contextual bandits [1-3, 7].

## Strengths

The paper makes several technical contributions. The ALP approximation provides an elegant way to handle the complex coupling between contexts and budget constraints. The analysis of the ALP algorithm's performance (Theorem 1) is technically sound and shows the near-optimality of the approach. The UCB-ALP algorithm is well-motivated and the sketch of the regret analysis provides useful insights into how to handle the interactions between exploration and constrained decision-making.

The paper also makes a reasonable attempt to connect to practical applications, noting that their simplified model applies to clinical trials and wireless networks. The empirical approach to handling unknown context distributions via EALP is also a practical contribution.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

**Originality Concerns:** The paper's main claim of achieving logarithmic regret is questionable. The authors' approach essentially reduces the problem to a time/budgeted bandit with JK arms (where J is the number of contexts and K the number of actions), where each "arm" corresponds to a context-action pair. For such a problem with finite arms, standard UCB-based algorithms already achieve O(log T) regret [4, 13]. The coupling between contexts is not truly eliminated as claimed; rather, the authors' ALP approximation simplifies the problem by treating the context-action pairs as a single set of arms with a budget constraint, which is a standard approach in bandits with resource constraints.

**Theorem 2 and Regret Bound:** The claimed regret bound for UCB-ALP in Theorem 2 is O(JK log T) for non-boundary cases. This is problematic because:
1. The standard problem-dependent bound for contextual bandits should be expressed as a sum over inverse gaps (Σ_{j,k} (u^*_j - u_{j,k})^{-1} log T), not as a KJ factor. The KJ factor is overly pessimistic and doesn't reflect the actual dependence on the gap structure.
2. The paper claims the regret is O(JK log T), but the supplementary material's bound appears to require a different analysis. The proof approach based on UCB ordering events seems to overestimate the regret contribution from context ranking errors.

**Lower Bounds:** The paper lacks matching lower bounds. For problem-dependent regret, a lower bound should be of the form Ω(Σ_{j,k} (u^*_j - u_{j,k})^{-1} log T). For problem-independent regret, a lower bound of Ω(√(JK T)) should be provided. The absence of these lower bounds makes it difficult to assess the tightness of the claimed upper bounds.

**Boundary Cases:** The paper acknowledges that boundary cases (when ρ = q_j for some j) yield O(√T + JK log T) regret. However, the analysis of these cases is not sufficiently detailed, and the authors should clarify whether the √T term is truly necessary or if it's an artifact of their analysis.

**Theorem 2 Rate Question:** The paper claims O(√T + ...) for the boundary case, but it's unclear if this should be O(√(K T) + ...) as standard bandit results suggest. The authors should clarify the precise rate for the boundary case.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality:** The technical content is generally sound, but the regret analysis for the non-boundary case needs significant reworking to provide a clean problem-dependent bound. The proof approach for the regret bound appears to overestimate the contribution of context ranking errors.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear descriptions of the algorithms. However, the connection between the ALP approximation and the standard bandit problem is not sufficiently explained, which weakens the novelty claim.

**Originality:** The main contribution appears to be the ALP approximation, but the problem reduction to a standard bandit with JK arms is not novel. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing work on bandits with resource constraints [11-16, 20-22]. The claim of "first work to show how to achieve logarithmic regret" is questionable given that standard bandit techniques already achieve this for the reduced problem.

**Significance:** The problem is interesting, but the significance is diminished by the lack of a clear novelty argument. The result is largely a special case of known bandit results with resource constraints, rather than a fundamentally new approach to constrained contextual bandits.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a contribution to the constrained bandit literature, but the novelty claim is not sufficiently supported. The result appears to be a straightforward application of standard bandit techniques to a reduced problem, rather than a fundamentally new approach to constrained contextual bandits. The authors should clarify the novelty of their approach in the rebuttal, particularly by showing how their method differs from the standard approach of treating each context-action pair as a separate arm.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors must address the concerns about novelty and the regret bound formulation in the rebuttal. If they can provide a convincing argument for the novelty of their approach and a clean problem-dependent regret bound, this paper could be strengthened. However, as written, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from existing work on bandits with resource constraints.
