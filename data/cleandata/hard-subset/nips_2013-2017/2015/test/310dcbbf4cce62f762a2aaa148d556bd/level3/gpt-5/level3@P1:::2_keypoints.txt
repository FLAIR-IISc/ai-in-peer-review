- Problem: studies constrained contextual bandits with time and budget limits, focusing on T and B growing proportionally.  
- Main idea: approximate dynamic programming oracle by an LP enforcing an average-budget constraint.  
- Algorithm: Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) plugs current average remaining budget bτ/τ into LP each round and uses threshold/randomization solution.  
- Unit-cost, known context & rewards: ALP achieves O(1) regret except boundary cases where regret is O(√T).  
- Analysis tool: maps budget evolution to sampling without replacement and uses hypergeometric concentration for budget concentration.  
- Unknown expected rewards: combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP); ALP needs only ordering of context-wise best rewards.  
- Learning guarantee: UCB-ALP attains O(JK log T) regret off-boundary and O(√T + JK log T) at boundaries.  
- Unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs: extensions sketched (empirical estimates, truncated-update coupling, LP over context-action pairs, ε-first variant).  
- Relation to prior work: special case of Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) and contrasts with BwK/concave-convex bandit work that typically yield O(√T) regret.  
- Novelty: first to obtain logarithmic regret in this stochastic-context constrained setting via adaptive LP + UCB.  
- Technical soundness: theorems well-stated; proofs and key analyses (budget concentration, regret decomposition) are convincing.  
- Completeness: core unit-cost known-distribution case is fully developed; extensions are cursory and deferred to appendices.  
- Evaluation gap: no experiments; small-scale simulations would help illustrate constants, boundaries, and robustness.  
- Clarity: paper is clear and well organized; LP structure and threshold j̃(ρ) are explained cleanly.  
- Complexity discussion: missing brief analysis of per-round cost and dependence on J, K.  
- Strength: simple, computationally light algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees and insightful analysis tools.  
- Strength: explicit treatment of boundary cases and decoupling of learning from constrained decisions.  
- Weakness: modeling restrictions—finite discrete contexts/actions, single budget, fixed costs, known T and B, i.i.d. contexts—limit applicability.  
- Weakness: dependence on known context distribution in core results; unknown-distribution case less clean.  
- Weakness: boundary-case O(√T) remains without matching lower bound or characterization of hardness.  
- Weakness: no empirical validation and potentially large hidden constants in O(JK log T).  
- Weakness: some assumptions (unique best action per context, strict orderings) could be relaxed; sensitivity to near-ties not fully addressed.  
- Questions for authors: provide empirical results, lower bounds for boundary hardness, anytime variants relaxing knowledge of T/B, per-round computational costs, and whether UCB-ALP works for heterogeneous costs.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong novel theoretical result, simple algorithms, solid analysis, and relevant impact for resource-constrained decision-making.  
- Cons against acceptance: lack of experiments, restricted assumptions, boundary-case gap and missing lower bounds.  
- Recommendation: lean toward acceptance pending addition of empirical evidence and more thorough treatment of boundary cases and extensions.
