

This paper addresses contextual bandits with budget constraints, a significant extension of standard contextual bandits that better models real-world resource limitations in applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors develop the UCB-ALP algorithm, which achieves O(log T) regret for unit-cost systems with known context distribution under a mild non-boundary condition, representing the first work to establish logarithmic regret in this setting (prior work [16] achieves O(√T)). The key insight is using an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation to the offline optimal policy, which tolerates estimation errors and combines naturally with UCB for unknown rewards.

The paper excels in clarity and motivation, with a well-structured exposition that carefully explains the challenges of constrained contextual bandits and the novel connections between linear programming and bandit algorithms. The technical contribution is strong, with rigorous analysis that properly handles the complex interactions between budget constraints, context arrivals, and exploration. The authors convincingly demonstrate that ALP achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases) and that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases.

The main limitation is the dependence on the mild non-boundary condition for the logarithmic guarantee (boundary cases require O(√T) regret), though the authors properly acknowledge this with a lower bound. The reliance on Lemma 1 was initially concerning but was thoroughly justified in the rebuttal. The paper also appropriately addresses the assumption u^1 > ... > u^*_J in the known context case.

This work is highly original as it resolves a key open problem in constrained contextual bandits, significantly advancing the state of the art. The computational efficiency of the proposed algorithms further enhances its practical relevance. While extensions to unknown horizon T remain open, the current results provide a strong foundation for future work. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a substantial contribution to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
