This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon T and a budget B. This setup, termed constrained contextual bandits, is highly relevant to practical applications like crowdsourcing or clinical trials, but the dual constraints introduce complex temporal dependencies that make the exploration-exploitation tradeoff significantly harder than in standard bandit settings. The core contribution of this work is the development of computationally efficient algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret, a substantial improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) regret common in prior related work (e.g., [17]).

The authors' approach is to first understand the optimal policy (the oracle) in the case where all problem statistics are known. Since the true oracle policy, which can be formulated as a dynamic program, is intractable, they propose an elegant Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy as an approximation. ALP makes decisions at each step by solving a simple LP based on the *current* average remaining budget. The authors show this ALP policy is near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret except for a set of "boundary cases." Building on this insight, they propose the UCB-ALP algorithm for the practical setting where expected rewards are unknown. This algorithm combines the UCB estimation method with the ALP decision-making framework. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) regret in boundary cases. Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs are also discussed.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a challenging and practical problem. To my knowledge, this is the first work to demonstrate that logarithmic regret is achievable for constrained contextual bandits. This is a significant theoretical advance over previous O(sqrt(T)) bounds in similar settings. The results meaningfully advance the state-of-the-art.

2.  **Elegant Approach:** The two-stage approach of first approximating the oracle with ALP and then combining it with UCB is very clean. The ALP policy is insightful; it translates a complex sequential decision problem into a series of myopic, easy-to-solve linear programs. This provides a strong and principled foundation for the learning algorithm.

3.  **Strong Baseline:** The paper compares its performance against a powerful oracle. This oracle does not simply play the best arm for each context; it must intelligently manage its budget, for instance, by skipping contexts with good-but-not-great rewards to save the budget for potentially better opportunities later. The proposed ALP framework provides a tractable approximation of this sophisticated behavior. Achieving low regret against such a strong baseline highlights the strength of the proposed algorithms.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It builds the reader's intuition by starting with the simplified unit-cost, known-statistics case before moving to the full problem, which makes the core ideas easy to grasp.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

My main point of confusion regards the analysis of "boundary cases."

1.  **Intuition for Boundary Cases:** In Theorem 1 and 2, the regret bound degrades significantly (from O(1) to O(sqrt(T)) for ALP, and from O(log T) to O(sqrt(T)) for UCB-ALP) when the budget ratio ρ equals a cumulative context probability q_j = sum_{i=1 to j} π_i. The technical reason seems to be related to the stability of the threshold j̃(ρ), but the paper would be strengthened by providing more intuition. Why are these specific points in the parameter space fundamentally more difficult? Is it because at these points, the optimal policy is indifferent between acting on context j and context j+1, making the ALP policy's decisions highly sensitive to small fluctuations in the remaining budget b_τ/τ? A deeper explanation would make the results more complete.

2.  **Generality of Boundary Cases:** The paper states these boundary cases form a set of zero Lebesgue measure. While true, in practice, a ρ that is very close to a q_j might also exhibit worse performance. It would be helpful to understand how the constants in the non-boundary regret bounds behave as δ approaches zero. Does the O(1) term in Theorem 1 explode as ρ approaches q_j?

**Evaluation Summary:**

*   **Quality:** The paper appears technically sound and presents a complete piece of work with strong theoretical results. The analysis sketch, relying on the concentration of the remaining budget, is compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core ideas are communicated effectively.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The problem of achieving logarithmic regret in this setting is new, and the UCB-ALP algorithm is a novel combination of familiar techniques to solve it. It is clearly distinguished from prior work.
*   **Significance:** The results are of high significance. The problem is practical, and the achievement of logarithmic regret is a benchmark result that researchers in the field are likely to build upon. The computational efficiency of the algorithm also enhances its practical importance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical variant of the contextual bandit problem.
*   Presents the first algorithm to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting, a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The proposed ALP/UCB-ALP framework is novel, elegant, and computationally efficient.
*   The paper is well-written and the results are clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The justification for the performance degradation in "boundary cases" could be more intuitive, leaving a minor gap in the conceptual understanding of the results.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, elegant, and significant contribution to the literature on bandit problems. I recommend acceptance.
