Summary and contributions
The paper studies contextual multi-armed bandits with both time and budget constraints (“constrained contextual bandits”), a setting that introduces coupling across rounds because actions consume limited resources. The authors propose an adaptive linear-programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle policy when statistics are known. In unit-cost systems with known context distribution, ALP achieves a constant regret gap to the optimal oracle, except for measure-zero “boundary” budget ratios where the gap is O(√T). Building on ALP’s tolerance to rank estimation errors, they combine it with UCB to obtain UCB-ALP, which achieves O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They further discuss unknown context distributions (EALP/UCB-EALP with a truncation argument to recover concentration) and heterogeneous costs (an LP over context-action pairs with an ε-first exploration scheme). The analysis uses a mapping of the adaptive budget process to “sampling without replacement,” yielding hypergeometric concentration for the remaining budget, and a careful decomposition of regret into within-context action-ranking and cross-context threshold errors.

Relation to prior work
This work complements and sharpens results on bandits with resource constraints and contextual bandits. Compared to “Bandits with Budgets/Knapsacks” (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., Slivkins, Combes et al.), which mostly treat non-contextual or static-context settings, the present paper addresses random context arrivals and shows logarithmic regret under finite discrete contexts/actions. Relative to Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Badanidiyuru, Langford, Slivkins, COLT), which achieves O(√T) regret with the benchmark restricted to a finite policy class using a computationally heavy Mixture Elimination algorithm, this paper provides an instance-dependent, oracle-based benchmark and computationally efficient procedures. Compared to Agrawal and Devanur’s line of work on concave rewards/convex knapsacks and its contextual extensions (including arXiv:1506.03374 and its dynamic-context follow-ups), which yield O(√T) regret, the present paper’s adaptive LP with random context arrivals attains O(log T) under the discrete setting and known context distribution. The paper also relates to UCRL/UCRL2-style optimism for MDPs; here, the optimism principle is leveraged inside the ALP structure rather than via full MDP confidence sets.

Evaluation

Quality
- Technical soundness: The key steps—LP relaxation as an analyzable upper bound, ALP’s adaptive thresholding, concentration of the remaining budget via a hypergeometric view, and regret partitioning—are well-motivated and appear correct. The UCB-ALP regret bound follows standard UCB arguments paired with ALP’s stability.
- Completeness: Main results are proved for unit costs with known context distribution; extensions (unknown distribution, heterogeneous costs) are sketched with details in the supplement. Boundary cases are clearly delineated.
- Limitations: Assumes finite discrete contexts/actions and iid contexts; known π for main results; “boundary” regimes retain √T behavior. The extension to numerous contexts may be computationally and sample-inefficient without structure.

Clarity
- The paper is very well written and easy to follow. The ALP mechanism and its threshold structure are clearly explained, and proof sketches convey the main ideas effectively.
- Suggested clarifications:
  - In Section 4.1, explicitly state the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle and clarify why ALP’s tolerance to rank errors makes coupling with UCB effective; briefly relate this to UCRL2-style optimistic planning and why a full RL approach is unnecessary here.
  - Make the per-round LPs and their computational cost explicit (unit cost has a closed-form threshold; heterogeneous costs require a small LP).
  - Define and discuss “boundary cases” earlier, including the “zero Lebesgue measure” assertion.

Originality
- The combination of adaptive LP relaxation with UCB to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits (under the stated assumptions) is novel and insightful. The identification of hypergeometric concentration for the remaining budget is neat and enables clean regret analysis. The O(1) oracle gap for ALP (non-boundary) is a strong and somewhat surprising result.

Significance
- The results are important for budgeted decision-making with contexts, with potential impact in clinical trials, recommendation, and online procurement. Achieving O(log T) regret with a computationally simple procedure is practically appealing. The approach may inform more general resource-constrained contextual bandits.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Scalability: For large numbers of contexts, consider parametric models (e.g., linear or GLM contextual bandits) to share statistical strength and reduce the O(JK log T) factor; discuss how ALP/UCB-ALP could be adapted (e.g., replacing per-context UCB with LinUCB-style estimates).
- Empirical evaluation: Include numerical experiments in an extended version to demonstrate finite-sample performance, robustness to misspecification of π, and sensitivity near boundary regimes. Comparisons against policy-class baselines (e.g., Mixture Elimination on a finite policy set) and concave/convex knapsack approaches would be informative.
- Prior work: Clarify the relation to Agrawal and Devanur’s contextual global constraints work (arXiv:1506.03374) and subsequent dynamic-context extensions; position precisely where your assumptions differ and why your adaptive LP yields improved rates.
- Boundary regimes: Consider whether small randomization of ρ (or budget smoothing) could avoid boundary behavior in practice, and discuss any theoretical prospects for improving √T in boundary cases.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, technically solid analysis with new ideas (adaptive LP + hypergeometric concentration).
- First logarithmic regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits under discrete iid contexts, with efficient algorithms.
- Strong clarity and relevance to NIPS; builds meaningfully on and differentiates from prior work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Assumptions (finite discrete contexts/actions, iid contexts, known π in main results) limit generality; practical systems often need parametric structure.
- No experiments to validate constants and practical performance.
- √T regret persists on boundary cases; the prevalence and practical impact could be elaborated.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a high-quality, original, and significant contribution by delivering computationally efficient algorithms with near-optimal and often logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, and it is clearly written. I encourage the authors to add empirical results and to clarify the optimism rationale and connections to closely related contextual knapsack work.
