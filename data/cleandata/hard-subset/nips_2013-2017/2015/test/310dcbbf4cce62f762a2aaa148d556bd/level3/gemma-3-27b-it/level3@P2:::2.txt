This paper addresses the challenging problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a setting highly relevant to numerous real-world applications. The authors make a significant contribution by demonstrating that logarithmic regret is achievable in this setting, a result previously unknown. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and grounded in a solid theoretical foundation. While the simplifying assumptions made – finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint – limit the direct applicability to all scenarios, they are reasonable and allow for a rigorous analysis that would be intractable otherwise.

The core idea of approximating the oracle using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) is insightful. By relaxing the hard budget constraint to an average constraint, and then adaptively adjusting this constraint over time, the authors develop a tractable algorithm with provable performance guarantees. The subsequent UCB-ALP algorithm, combining ALP with the UCB method, leverages the desirable properties of ALP – its reliance on reward ordering rather than precise values – to achieve logarithmic regret in the unknown reward setting. 

The paper meticulously builds its argument. Starting with the known-statistics case, the authors establish a benchmark and develop the ALP approximation. They then extend this to the unknown-statistics setting via UCB-ALP, and finally consider generalizations to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs. The proofs, while detailed and lengthy (as expected for this type of work), appear convincing. The inclusion of lemmas to establish key properties, such as the concentration of the remaining budget, enhances the clarity of the analysis.  The extensive related work section demonstrates a thorough understanding of the existing literature, appropriately citing relevant work on multi-armed bandits, contextual bandits, and constrained bandits.  The citations to recent NIPS proceedings are particularly helpful in establishing the novelty of the contribution.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the practical implications are not fully explored. Sections 5 and 6, which address unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, feel somewhat cursory. The analysis in these sections relies on truncated versions of the algorithms or assumes known context distributions, and a more thorough investigation of the impact of these assumptions would be beneficial. Most significantly, the complete absence of experimental results is a notable omission. Even simple simulations could provide valuable insights into the performance of the proposed algorithms and their sensitivity to various parameters. This lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the theoretical findings. 

Furthermore, the boundary cases identified in Theorem 2, where the regret degrades to O(√T), warrant further investigation. While the authors acknowledge these cases, a deeper understanding of their characteristics and potential mitigation strategies would strengthen the overall contribution. The claim of novelty, while likely true given the reviewer’s knowledge, should be cautiously stated, as the field of constrained bandits is rapidly evolving. The recent papers [21, 22] cited by the authors, while achieving √T regret, demonstrate the intense current research activity in this area.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel approach to constrained contextual bandits and demonstrates the possibility of achieving logarithmic regret, a significant theoretical advance.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous and the proofs appear correct. The use of linear programming relaxation and adaptive algorithms is well-justified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to impact research in contextual bandits and related areas, particularly in applications with resource constraints.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The absence of experimental results limits the assessment of the practical relevance of the work.
*   **Simplifying Assumptions:** The strong assumptions made may limit the applicability of the algorithms to real-world scenarios.
*   **Boundary Cases:** The √T regret in boundary cases requires further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are significant and the analysis is well-executed. The lack of experimental validation is a concern, but the clarity and rigor of the theoretical work outweigh this drawback. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors should consider adding experimental results or a more detailed discussion of the practical implications in a future version of the paper.
