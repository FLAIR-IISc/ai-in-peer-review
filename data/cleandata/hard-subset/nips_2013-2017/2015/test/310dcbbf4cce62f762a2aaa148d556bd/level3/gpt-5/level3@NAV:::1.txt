Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies contextual bandits with a single budget and a finite horizon, proposing an LP-based oracle approximation (ALP) and a learning algorithm (UCB-ALP). With known statistics, ALP achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) on boundaries via a sampling-without-replacement/concentration argument on the remaining budget; with unknown rewards, UCB-ALP targets O(JK log T) regret (plus √T on boundaries). The work is positioned against Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) and Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK), which achieve O(√T) in more general settings (e.g., [Badanidiyuru-Langford-Slivkins COLT’14; Badanidiyuru-Kleinberg-Slivkins FOCS’13]) and against convex-knapsack contextual bandits (Agrawal-Devanur et al.). Here, finiteness and unit-cost simplify the structure and enable LP thresholding and adaptation.

Quality: The ALP analysis is crisp and the hypergeometric concentration is a neat device. The UCB-ALP proof strategy (decomposing action vs. context-ordering errors) is reasonable, but the bounds are not yet cleanly instance-dependent. Please clarify a key modeling point: as T grows, your analysis suggests the coupling across contexts “vanishes” via concentration of bτ/τ. If so, why can’t one treat each occurrence of a context as an independent bandit instance fed by its own budget share, with a top-level time/budget controller? Pinning down exactly where coupling persists (beyond boundary cases) would sharpen the contribution.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized. However, the supplementary problem-dependent bound needs reworking: please present regret explicitly in terms of gaps Δj,k with standard sums of 1/Δ-type terms rather than a blanket JK factor. The UCB “ordering events” argument could be streamlined to avoid double-counting.

Originality: With finite contexts/actions and sufficiently large T, a log(T) regret via adaptive LP plus UCB is plausible and practically useful. However, the main result is unsurprising if we view the problem as a time/budgeted bandit with JK arms and action constraints. Please sharpen the novelty claim relative to BwK/RCB and to recent dynamic-context knapsack works.

Significance: Useful practically if the bounds are tightened and matched. Please provide matching lower bounds in both problem-dependent and -independent forms, ideally capturing context complexity (beyond reductions to classical bandits). Also, for Theorem 2, is the boundary rate truly O(√T + JK log T) or should the √ term scale as O(√(KT)) (or depend on J) via the fluctuation and ranking complexity?

Pros:
- Simple, computationally light algorithms; nice LP–UCB synthesis
- Clear concentration-based analysis for ALP
- Addresses a relevant constrained setting

Cons:
- Novelty vs. BwK/RCB unclear; result feels expected under finiteness
- No matching lower bounds; gap-dependent bounds not clean
- Ambiguity about coupling/decoupling across contexts and boundary-rate scaling

Overall: Neutral. I encourage a rebuttal clarifying novelty, tightening gap-dependent bounds, and adding lower bounds.
