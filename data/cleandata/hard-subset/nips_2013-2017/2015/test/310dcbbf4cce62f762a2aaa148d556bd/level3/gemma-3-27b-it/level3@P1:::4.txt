This paper addresses the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, introducing budget and time constraints into the standard contextual bandit framework. The authors convincingly argue that existing solutions for budget-constrained multi-armed bandits are inapplicable in the contextual setting. The core contribution lies in developing algorithms – Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) and its extension UCB-ALP – that achieve logarithmic regret (except for certain boundary cases) under proportional scaling of budget and time horizon. The paper is well-motivated, clearly structured, and tackles a significant problem with a novel approach. The introduction does a good job of placing the work within the broader landscape of contextual bandits and highlighting the limitations of existing techniques when constraints are introduced.

The authors begin by analyzing the oracle algorithm under known statistics and introduce ALP as an approximation based on linear programming. This approach is clever and provides a valuable benchmark for analysis. The extension to the unknown reward case via UCB-ALP is a natural and well-justified step. The analysis appears rigorous, utilizing concepts like concentration properties and bounded averaged differences, though the sheer length of proofs in the supplementary material may make detailed scrutiny difficult for all reviewers.  A particularly strong aspect is the careful consideration of both unit-cost and heterogeneous cost scenarios, demonstrating the generality of the proposed approach.  The discussion of extending the framework to unknown context distributions using empirical estimates is also valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The work tackles a new and important problem: constrained contextual bandits. The proposed approach of leveraging linear programming approximations and adapting it to UCB is innovative.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees, demonstrating logarithmic regret under reasonable conditions. The analysis is detailed and appears sound.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The considered constraints (budget and time) are common in real-world applications, making the problem and its solutions highly relevant.
*   **Generality:** The extension to heterogeneous costs and unknown context distributions increases the applicability of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivations, algorithms, and analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Boundary Cases:** The logarithmic regret guarantee does *not* hold for boundary cases (when ρ = qj). While acknowledged, the justification for why these cases require separate consideration and result in a √T regret is not entirely convincing. Why should these specific parameter values be fundamentally different, especially for j > 1? A more intuitive explanation of the issues arising at these boundaries would be beneficial.
*   **Benchmark Comparison:** The comparison to previous work isn’t always direct. The authors highlight the limitations of existing approaches, but the ALP-based benchmark employed seems stronger than a baseline simply trying to learn the optimal policy. It’s not entirely clear how much of the improvement is due to addressing the constraints *specifically* versus a more sophisticated baseline.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper lacks empirical evaluation. While the focus is theoretical, including simulations or experiments on synthetic or real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the work and provide insight into the practical performance of the algorithms, especially in boundary cases.
*   **Complexity of Proofs:** The extensive supplementary material containing the detailed proofs could be a barrier to detailed review. While length is sometimes unavoidable, streamlining or highlighting key arguments within the main paper would improve accessibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a novel and important problem with a well-motivated and innovative approach.
*   The theoretical results are strong and provide rigorous guarantees on the performance of the proposed algorithms.
*   The generality of the approach, extending to heterogeneous costs and unknown contexts, adds to its value.
*   The work represents a significant step forward in understanding and solving constrained contextual bandit problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical evaluation limits the ability to assess the practical performance of the algorithms.
*   The explanation for the reduced regret in boundary cases is not fully satisfying.
*   The complexity of the proofs might hinder thorough review.


**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The novelty, theoretical rigor, and practical relevance of the work outweigh the drawbacks. The lack of empirical results is a concern, but the theoretical analysis is compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with a minor revision request to provide a more intuitive explanation of the boundary cases and, if possible, to include some preliminary empirical results.
