

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, claiming to achieve logarithmic regret under certain conditions. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm for known statistics and combine it with UCB for unknown statistics, claiming O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases.

The main technical contribution appears to be the ALP approximation for the oracle, which the authors claim achieves near-optimality with O(1) regret. However, the claimed novelty is questionable. The problem can be naturally viewed as a time/budgeted bandit with JK arms and action constraints, where the coupling of contexts vanishes for large T (as stated in Section 3.2). This makes the logarithmic regret result unsurprising, as it's a natural extension of known results for budgeted bandits (e.g., [12, 17]).

Several technical concerns need addressing: First, the problem-dependent upper bound in the supplementary material should be expressed in terms of arm gaps (sums of inverse gaps) rather than containing a KJ factor. Second, Theorem 2's regret rate O(√T + ...) should be O(√(KT) + ...) based on standard bandit analysis. Third, the paper lacks matching lower bounds that incorporate context complexity beyond trivial classical-bandit cases. The authors claim this is the first work achieving logarithmic regret, but this appears overstated given the connections to simpler problems.

The paper has strengths in clear problem formulation and a well-structured algorithmic approach. However, the novelty claim is weak. The main result is a natural extension of existing techniques rather than a significant advance. The authors need to clarify how their approach fundamentally differs from prior work on budgeted bandits and contextual bandits, particularly regarding the claimed logarithmic regret.

While the paper presents a coherent algorithm and analysis, the insufficient evidence of originality prevents strong recommendation. I suggest the authors provide a clearer comparison to prior work, rework the problem-dependent bound to use sums of inverse arm gaps, and provide nontrivial lower bounds before reconsidering acceptance.
