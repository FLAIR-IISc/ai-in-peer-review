## Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, introducing algorithms aiming for logarithmic regret. The authors correctly identify the gap in existing literature, where previous work on constrained bandits doesn’t readily extend to the contextual setting, and existing contextual bandit algorithms don’t account for budget limitations. The core idea of approximating the oracle with Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) and combining it with UCB is a reasonable approach, and the paper demonstrates achieving logarithmic regret under certain conditions.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear problem definition and motivation, followed by a detailed presentation of the proposed algorithms and their analysis. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature on contextual bandits, resource allocation, and dynamic programming. The extension to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs is a positive addition, demonstrating the potential generality of the approach. 

However, the claimed novelty feels somewhat overstated. The central contribution – achieving logarithmic regret – is presented as a first, but the connection to the broader literature on time/budgeted bandits (e.g., [11-16] cited in the introduction) isn't fully explored. The problem *can* be viewed as a time/budgeted bandit with JK arms and action constraints, and the authors don't convincingly demonstrate a significant advancement beyond adapting existing techniques to this specific contextual framing. 

Furthermore, Theorem 2’s rate of O(√T + …) in boundary cases seems suspect and warrants closer scrutiny; O(√(K T) + …) appears more plausible given the number of arms. The problem-dependent bounds could be strengthened by utilizing sums of inverse arm gaps, rather than the KJ factor, which feels overly conservative. The proof approach based on UCB ordering events, while standard, could benefit from further justification. 

Crucially, the paper lacks nontrivial lower bounds. While achieving logarithmic regret is a positive result, demonstrating that it’s *optimal* or *near-optimal* requires corresponding lower bounds in both problem-dependent and problem-independent settings, going beyond the trivial classical-bandit cases. The context complexity should be reflected in these lower bounds.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and relatively unexplored problem.
*   Presents a reasonable algorithmic approach (UCB-ALP).
*   Provides theoretical analysis demonstrating logarithmic regret under certain conditions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Claims of novelty are weak; the work feels incremental.
*   The regret rate in boundary cases (Theorem 2) is questionable.
*   Lacks nontrivial lower bounds to establish optimality.
*   Problem-dependent bounds could be tighter.



**Overall Assessment:** Neutral. While the paper presents a technically sound approach and achieves a positive result, the lack of strong novelty and the absence of lower bounds leave me unconvinced of its significant contribution to the field. I recommend the authors clarify the novelty of their work in the rebuttal, address the concerns regarding Theorem 2, and discuss the possibility of deriving meaningful lower bounds.
