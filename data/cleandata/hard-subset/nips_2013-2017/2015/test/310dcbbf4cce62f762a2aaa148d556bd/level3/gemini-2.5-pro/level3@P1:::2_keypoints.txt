- Paper studies contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B, a practically relevant constrained setting.  
- Main difficulty: optimal policy depends on remaining budget and time, creating complex temporal dependencies.  
- For known context distributions and rewards, authors propose Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) as a tractable approximation to the DP oracle.  
- ALP adapts decisions based on the remaining-budget-to-remaining-time ratio and is shown to be near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret in most cases.  
- For unknown rewards, authors introduce UCB-ALP combining UCB optimism with the ALP decision rule.  
- Main theoretical claim: UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret except for “boundary cases” where regret is O(√T).  
- This appears to be the first logarithmic-regret result for this class of constrained contextual bandits, improving on prior O(√T) bounds.  
- Technical contribution: elegant ALP idea and analysis decomposing regret into action-ranking and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation errors.  
- Quality: strong theoretical development and sound proof sketch; assumptions and limitations are clearly stated.  
- Major weakness: complete lack of empirical validation; no experiments to evaluate practical performance or constants in bounds.  
- Missing experiments leave open robustness questions when assumptions are violated and the empirical frequency/impact of boundary cases.  
- Extensions (unknown context distribution, heterogeneous costs) are treated briefly and feel underdeveloped compared to the core results.  
- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and builds intuition effectively from known to unknown statistics.  
- Originality: novel and important idea—adaptive LP—and its combination with UCB is a significant contribution distinct from related work.  
- Significance: theoretically impactful with relevance to applications (advertising, trials, crowdsourcing), though lack of experiments limits immediate practical impact.  
- Recommendation: accept for its novel theoretical advance, but strongly encourage adding basic simulations and strengthening the brief extensions.
