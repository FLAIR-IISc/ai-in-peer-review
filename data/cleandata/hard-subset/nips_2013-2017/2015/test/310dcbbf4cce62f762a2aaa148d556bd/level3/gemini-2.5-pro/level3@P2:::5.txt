This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B). This setting, referred to as constrained contextual bandits, introduces significant challenges because decisions are coupled over time through the shared budget, making the exploration-exploitation tradeoff more complex than in standard bandit problems. The main contribution is the design of a computationally efficient algorithm, UCB-ALP, which is the first to achieve a logarithmic regret bound of O(log T) for this problem (in non-boundary cases).

The authors' approach is to first simplify the problem by assuming the reward and context distributions are known. Even in this oracle setting, the optimal policy is computationally intractable, requiring a dynamic programming solution. The key insight is to approximate this oracle with an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy. This policy solves a simple LP at each step, using the ratio of remaining budget to remaining time as the average budget constraint. The authors show that this ALP approximation achieves near-optimal performance, with only a constant or O(sqrt(T)) gap to the true optimum. Building on this, for the practical case where expected rewards are unknown, they propose the UCB-ALP algorithm. This algorithm combines the ALP decision-making framework with Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) estimates of the rewards. The analysis shows this leads to an O(log T) regret in most cases, a significant improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) regret achieved by prior work on related problems, such as the Resourceful Contextual Bandits of Badanidiyuru et al. [17].

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses an important and practical variant of the contextual bandit problem. The primary result—an algorithm with logarithmic regret—is a major theoretical advance. It demonstrates that it is possible to achieve near-optimal regret in constrained settings without resorting to computationally expensive methods, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of using an adaptive linear programming relaxation is novel and effective. While LP relaxations for DP are common, the proposed *adaptive* nature, where the constraint is continuously updated based on the current state (remaining budget and time), is the key innovation that enables the strong theoretical guarantees.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The analysis is non-trivial, particularly in managing the interactions between the learning component (UCB) and the adaptive decision-making component (ALP). The proof sketch effectively communicates the main analytical tools, such as mapping the budget evolution to a "sampling without replacement" process to establish concentration bounds, and carefully decomposing the total regret into distinct sources of error.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The structure, which builds from the known-statistics case (ALP) to the unknown-statistics case (UCB-ALP), is logical and greatly aids the reader's understanding of the core concepts.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Scalability:** The current analysis assumes a finite and discrete set of contexts and actions. This assumption may not hold in applications with very large or continuous context spaces (e.g., where contexts are high-dimensional feature vectors). It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss how the proposed framework might be extended, for example, by incorporating parametric reward models (e.g., linear functions of the context) to allow for generalization across contexts.
*   **Conceptual Motivation:** In Section 4, the motivation for combining UCB with ALP could be more explicitly linked to established principles in reinforcement learning. The algorithm is an excellent example of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle, where one acts optimally with respect to an optimistic estimate of the world model. Drawing a parallel to how algorithms like UCRL2 update their policy based on optimistic MDP parameters would help situate this work within a broader conceptual framework.
*   **Lack of Experiments:** The work is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong enough to stand on its own, the paper would have a greater impact if accompanied by numerical experiments. Simulations could empirically verify the logarithmic regret of UCB-ALP and contrast it with the O(sqrt(T)) regret of alternative approaches, thereby demonstrating the practical advantage of the proposed algorithm. This could be valuable for an extended or journal version of this work.
*   **Related Work:** The authors have done a good job of positioning their work relative to prior art, including the recent preprints [21, 22] that appeared around the same time. The footnote discussing them is appreciated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides the first algorithm for constrained contextual bandits with a near-optimal O(log T) regret bound, a significant improvement over the state of the art.
2.  Introduces a novel and computationally efficient algorithmic framework (ALP and UCB-ALP) based on an elegant adaptive linear programming approximation.
3.  The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous and insightful analysis.
4.  It is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The assumptions of finite contexts and actions may limit immediate applicability to some large-scale problems.
2.  The lack of empirical validation leaves the practical performance of the algorithm unevaluated.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant theoretical advance for the important problem of constrained contextual bandits. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the approach and the improved regret bound, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is well-executed and clearly communicated. I recommend acceptance.
