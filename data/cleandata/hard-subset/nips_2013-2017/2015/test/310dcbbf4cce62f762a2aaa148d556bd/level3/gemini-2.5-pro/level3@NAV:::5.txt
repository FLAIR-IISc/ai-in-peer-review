This paper addresses the problem of budget-constrained contextual bandits with discrete contexts and actions, a challenging extension of the classic bandit problem where decisions are coupled over time due to resource constraints. The authors' primary contribution is the UCB-ALP algorithm, which cleverly combines an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle policy with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) approach for exploration. This method stands out by achieving a near-optimal O(log T) regret bound in most cases, a significant improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) regret common in prior work on resourceful bandits, such as Badanidiyuru et al. [17].

The paper's strengths are considerable. The theoretical contribution is the main highlight; achieving logarithmic regret with a computationally efficient algorithm is a noteworthy advance for this problem class. The key insight of using an adaptive LP relaxation to decouple decision-making from information acquisition is elegant and effective. Furthermore, the paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, making the sophisticated analysis accessible. The clear, logical progression from the known-statistics case (ALP) to the unknown-statistics case (UCB-ALP) provides a strong foundation for the results.

Despite its strengths, the paper could be improved in a few areas. The primary weakness is the absence of numerical experiments to complement the theoretical analysis. Including simulations would help validate the algorithm's practical performance and demonstrate the tightness of the regret bounds. For the final version, I suggest several minor revisions. First, the rationale in Section 4.1 for combining UCB with ALP could be made more explicit by directly referencing the "optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty" principle and its connection to policy updates in algorithms like UCRL2. Second, to address scalability when contexts are numerous, the authors might consider discussing the potential for extending their framework to parametric reward models. Finally, the authors should consider citing the related work by Agrawal et al. (arXiv:1506.03374), which also explores contextual bandits with global constraints.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that merits acceptance. It tackles an important problem with a novel and efficient approach, providing a significant theoretical improvement over existing methods. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed in a revised version.
