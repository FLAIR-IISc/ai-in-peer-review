- Studies constrained contextual bandits and proposes an adaptive linear-programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle policy.
- ALP attains a constant regret gap for unit-cost systems with known context distribution, except for measure-zero “boundary” budget ratios where regret is O(√T).
- UCB-ALP couples ALP with UCB to achieve O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases.
- Extensions address unknown context distributions (EALP/UCB-EALP with truncation for concentration) and heterogeneous costs (LP over context-action pairs with ε-first exploration).
- Analysis maps the adaptive budget process to “sampling without replacement,” using hypergeometric concentration for remaining budget and decomposes regret into within-context and cross-context errors.
- Advances prior work by handling random context arrivals, providing an instance-dependent oracle benchmark, and offering computationally efficient procedures with sharper (logarithmic) rates under discrete settings.
- Improves on Mixture Elimination and concave/knapsack approaches by achieving logarithmic regret under the stated assumptions.
- Technical soundness: LP relaxation, ALP thresholding, concentration arguments, and regret partitioning are well-motivated and appear correct.
- Completeness: Main results are fully proved for unit-cost known-context cases; extensions are sketched in the supplement and boundary cases are clearly identified.
- Limitations: requires finite discrete contexts/actions, iid contexts, known π for main results; boundary regimes still incur √T; scalability may be poor with many contexts.
- Clarity: paper is well written, ALP mechanism and proof sketches are clearly explained and easy to follow.
- Suggested clarifications: explicitly state the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle and why ALP tolerates rank errors; make per-round LPs and computational cost explicit; define and discuss boundary cases earlier.
- Originality: novel combination of adaptive LP with UCB and the use of hypergeometric concentration; surprising O(1) oracle gap for non-boundary settings.
- Significance: relevant for budgeted decision-making (clinical trials, recommendation, procurement); practical appeal due to simple, efficient algorithms achieving O(log T).
- Scalability suggestion: consider parametric models (e.g., linear/GLM) to share strength and reduce the O(JK log T) factor, e.g., replace per-context UCB with LinUCB-style methods.
- Empirical suggestion: add experiments to show finite-sample performance, robustness to π misspecification, sensitivity near boundaries, and comparisons to policy-class and knapsack baselines.
- Prior-work suggestion: clarify relation to Agrawal & Devanur (arXiv:1506.03374) and dynamic-context extensions, and precisely state differing assumptions that enable improved rates.
- Boundary-regime suggestion: consider small randomization or budget smoothing to avoid boundary behavior in practice and discuss theoretical prospects to improve √T there.
- Arguments for acceptance: clear, technically solid, novel ideas (adaptive LP + hypergeometric concentration), first log-regret guarantees for discrete iid contextual bandits, and computational efficiency.
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions limit generality, no empirical validation, and √T regret persists at boundary cases—practical impact unclear.
- Recommendation: Accept, with encouragement to add empirical results and clarify optimism rationale and connections to related contextual knapsack work.
