This paper studies the problem of budget-constrained contextual bandits with discrete contexts and actions. The authors propose a computationally efficient algorithm via linear programming (LP) approximation, which achieves a logarithmic regret bound. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it a pleasure to read.

The main contributions of this paper are two-fold. Firstly, the authors develop an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle algorithm for budget-constrained contextual bandits. The ALP algorithm is shown to achieve near-optimal performance, with a regret bound of O(1) except for certain boundary cases. Secondly, the authors combine the ALP algorithm with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method to propose a UCB-ALP algorithm for constrained contextual bandits with unknown expected rewards. The UCB-ALP algorithm is shown to achieve a logarithmic regret bound of O(log T) except for certain boundary cases.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem, the proposed algorithms, and the theoretical analysis. The technical proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which makes the paper easy to follow for non-experts.

One potential suggestion for improvement is to consider parametric reward models for scalability when contexts are numerous. While the authors assume a non-parametric model, incorporating parametric models could make the algorithm more scalable and efficient.

Another suggestion is to clarify the rationale for combining UCB with ALP in Section 4.1. The authors could explicitly state the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle and its relation to UCRL2 policy updates. This would provide a clearer understanding of the underlying principles behind the UCB-ALP algorithm.

Additionally, including numerical experiments in an extended version of the paper would be beneficial in demonstrating the practical performance of the proposed algorithms.

The authors could also consider referencing arXiv:1506.03374, which appears to be a relevant work in the field of contextual bandits.

In conclusion, this paper provides an efficient and improved algorithm for constrained contextual bandits with near-optimal regret. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem and the proposed algorithms. The technical analysis is sound, and the results are significant. Therefore, I recommend this paper for acceptance.
