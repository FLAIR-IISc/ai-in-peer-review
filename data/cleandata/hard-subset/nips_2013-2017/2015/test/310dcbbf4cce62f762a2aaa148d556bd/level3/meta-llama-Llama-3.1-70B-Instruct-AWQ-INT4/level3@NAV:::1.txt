**Paper Review: Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits**

**Summary:**

This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves near-optimal performance when the system statistics are known. They then combine ALP with the Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) method to propose a UCB-ALP algorithm for the case where the expected rewards are unknown. The authors show that UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors provide a clear and thorough analysis of the ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms. However, the proof of Theorem 2 seems to be incorrect, and the rate should be O(√(K T) + …) instead of O(√T + …).

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed algorithms, and the theoretical analysis. However, some parts of the paper, such as the proof of Theorem 1, are not easy to follow.

**Originality:**

The paper's contribution is not entirely new, as the authors build upon previous work on contextual bandits and budgeted bandits. However, the combination of ALP and UCB is novel, and the authors provide a new perspective on how to tackle the challenges introduced by budget constraints.

**Significance:**

The paper's results are significant, as they provide an efficient approach to dealing with the challenges introduced by budget constraints in contextual bandits. The authors' work has the potential to be applied in various applications, such as clinical trials, online recommendation, and online hiring in crowdsourcing.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

1. The paper provides a novel combination of ALP and UCB, which achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases.
2. The authors provide a clear and thorough analysis of the ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms.
3. The paper's results are significant, and the authors' work has the potential to be applied in various applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The proof of Theorem 2 seems to be incorrect, and the rate should be O(√(K T) + …) instead of O(√T + …).
2. The paper's contribution is not entirely new, as the authors build upon previous work on contextual bandits and budgeted bandits.
3. Some parts of the paper are not easy to follow, and the authors could improve the clarity of the presentation.

**Conclusion:**

I remain neutral about the paper's acceptance, as the authors provide a novel combination of ALP and UCB, but the proof of Theorem 2 seems to be incorrect. I recommend that the authors clarify the proof and provide a more accurate rate. Additionally, the authors could improve the clarity of the presentation to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.
