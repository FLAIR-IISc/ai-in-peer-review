- Tackles constrained contextual bandits, proposing algorithms that claim logarithmic regret.  
- Proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) for unit-cost systems to approximate the oracle and achieve near-optimality.  
- Extends to UCB-ALP for unknown rewards, claiming logarithmic regret except for “boundary cases.”  
- Generalizes methods to heterogeneous costs and unknown context distributions.  
- Problem is well-motivated and the algorithms are presented clearly and logically.  
- Properly connects to prior work on resource allocation and knapsack-like problems.  
- Novelty is limited: LP approximation plus UCB feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.  
- Boundary cases yield O(√T) regret, undermining the universality of the logarithmic-regret claim.  
- Theorem 2’s boundary-rate O(√T + ...) is suspect; a rate like O(√(KT) + ...) may be more appropriate.  
- Regret bounds are insufficiently expressive and lack problem-dependent arm-gap formulations; the “KJ” bound is not insightful.  
- No matching lower bounds are provided, limiting assessment of tightness in both problem-dependent and independent regimes.  
- Proof approach based on UCB ordering is standard and may not justify logarithmic regret under correlated context arrivals.  
- Missing analysis of context complexity and nontrivial lower bounds beyond classical bandit cases.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, clear exposition, covers known/unknown context scenarios.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, unsatisfactory handling of boundary cases, lack of tight lower bounds and insightful regret bounds.  
- Overall assessment: Neutral — authors should clarify novelty and address boundary cases, lower bounds, and regret bound expressiveness in rebuttal.
