This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle algorithm with linear programming (LP) and achieves near-optimality. They then combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to develop a UCB-ALP algorithm for the general case where the expected rewards are unknown a priori.

The main result of the paper is that the UCB-ALP algorithm achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases. This is a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits, as it provides a computationally efficient algorithm that can handle budget and time constraints.

However, there are several aspects of the paper that require clarification. Firstly, the authors claim that for large horizon, the coupling of contexts vanishes, so each context occurrence could be treated as a separate bandit instance. However, it is not clear why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as an independent bandit problem.

Secondly, the authors provide a problem-dependent upper bound expressed in terms of arm gaps, but it is not clear why this bound is not expressed in terms of sums of inverse arm gaps instead of a KJ factor. Additionally, the proof approach based on UCB ordering events seems to be overly complicated, and it is not clear why a simpler approach cannot be used.

Thirdly, the authors do not provide matching lower bounds in both problem-dependent and problem-independent settings. This is a significant omission, as lower bounds are essential for understanding the optimality of the proposed algorithm.

Finally, the authors conclude that the UCB-ALP algorithm achieves logarithmic regret, but this result is not entirely surprising. As the problem can be viewed as a time/budgeted bandit with JK arms and action constraints, it is not clear what the novelty of the proposed algorithm is.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, this paper meets the criteria for quality, as the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. However, the paper could be improved in terms of clarity, as some of the explanations are overly complicated and require significant effort to understand. Additionally, the paper could be improved in terms of originality, as the novelty of the proposed algorithm is not entirely clear.

Overall, I remain neutral on the acceptance of this paper, as while it makes a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits, it requires significant clarification and improvement in terms of clarity and originality.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits by providing a computationally efficient algorithm that can handle budget and time constraints.
* The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases, which is a significant improvement over existing algorithms.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper requires significant clarification on several aspects, including the treatment of context occurrences as separate bandit instances and the proof approach based on UCB ordering events.
* The paper does not provide matching lower bounds in both problem-dependent and problem-independent settings, which is a significant omission.
* The novelty of the proposed algorithm is not entirely clear, and it is not clear what the contribution of the paper is beyond existing work on contextual bandits.

Recommendations for Improvement:

* Clarify the treatment of context occurrences as separate bandit instances and provide a clear explanation for why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as an independent bandit problem.
* Simplify the proof approach based on UCB ordering events and provide a clear explanation for why this approach is necessary.
* Provide matching lower bounds in both problem-dependent and problem-independent settings to establish the optimality of the proposed algorithm.
* Clarify the novelty of the proposed algorithm and provide a clear explanation for what the contribution of the paper is beyond existing work on contextual bandits.
