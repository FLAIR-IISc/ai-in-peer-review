- Paper studies constrained contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B, a practically relevant and harder setting than standard bandits.  
- Main contribution: computationally efficient algorithms (ALP and UCB-ALP) that achieve logarithmic regret, improving over prior O(√T) results.  
- ALP: an adaptive linear program that approximates the intractable oracle by using current average remaining budget; shown near-optimal with O(1) regret except at boundary cases.  
- UCB-ALP: combines UCB estimation with ALP decisions to handle unknown rewards; achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases.  
- Extensions include handling unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.  
- Strength — Novelty/significance: first to show logarithmic regret is achievable in this constrained setting; advances theory substantially.  
- Strength — Elegant approach: two-stage design (ALP approximation then UCB learning) converts a complex sequential problem into tractable myopic LPs.  
- Strength — Strong baseline: performance measured against a sophisticated budget-managing oracle, demonstrating practical strength.  
- Strength — Clarity: paper is well-written and logically structured, building intuition via simpler cases first.  
- Weakness — Boundary-case intuition: need clearer explanation why ρ equal to cumulative context probabilities q_j causes regret to degrade (sensitivity/indifference issues).  
- Weakness — Practical impact of near-boundary ρ: unclear how regret constants behave as ρ approaches q_j and whether O(1) bounds deteriorate.  
- Evaluation: technically sound, clear, original, significant, and computationally efficient — merits acceptance.  
- Argument for acceptance: tackles important problem, provides first logarithmic-regret algorithm, elegant and efficient framework, and strong presentation.  
- Argument against acceptance: minor gap in intuitive explanation for performance degradation in boundary cases.
