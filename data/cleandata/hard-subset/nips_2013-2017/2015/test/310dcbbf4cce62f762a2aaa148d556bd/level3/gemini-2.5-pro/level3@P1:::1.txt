This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B). The process terminates when either resource is exhausted. The authors aim to design computationally efficient algorithms that achieve logarithmic or sublinear regret against the optimal oracle policy, which knows the problem parameters (context distribution, expected rewards).

The core of the proposed approach is to first develop an approximation for the intractable oracle policy. The authors formulate a Linear Program (LP) that relaxes the hard budget constraint into an average one. Based on this, they propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which at each step re-solves the LP using the ratio of remaining budget to remaining time (b_τ/τ). This ALP algorithm is shown to achieve O(1) regret in "non-boundary" cases (when the budget-to-time ratio ρ=B/T does not align with certain cumulative context probabilities) and O(sqrt(T)) regret otherwise.

Building on this near-optimal oracle approximation, the authors address the setting with unknown expected rewards by combining ALP with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T) + log T) in boundary cases. This is presented as the first logarithmic regret bound for this problem. The paper also discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The problem of contextual bandits with hard budget and time constraints is well-motivated and captures important practical scenarios not addressed by the standard contextual bandit model.
2.  **Oracle Approximation:** The development of the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm as a tractable and provably near-optimal approximation to the DP-based oracle is a key technical contribution. Showing that this simple, adaptive policy achieves O(1) regret (in non-boundary cases) is an elegant result and provides a strong foundation for the subsequent bandit algorithm.
3.  **Main Result:** Achieving a logarithmic regret bound is a significant goal in bandit theory. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to establish such a result for the constrained contextual bandit setting, which represents a clear advance in the state of the art.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

My primary concerns relate to the novelty of the problem framing and the tightness of the theoretical analysis.

1.  **Novelty and Problem Framing:** The paper argues that the budget constraint introduces complex coupling among contexts over time. However, with a finite number of contexts (J) and actions (K), this problem can be viewed as a multi-armed bandit problem with a total of JK arms. A context arriving at time t simply makes a specific subset of K arms available. This setup is closely related to the "bandits with knapsacks" or "bandits with budgets" literature. In that view, achieving logarithmic regret when T is large is not entirely surprising, as the learning problem for each of the JK arms should behave like a standard bandit. Could the authors clarify why the contextual nature of the problem is fundamentally different from a budgeted bandit problem with JK arms and stochastic availability (where arm (j,k) is only available when context j appears)? For large T, the law of large numbers suggests the allocation problem should stabilize, diminishing the "complex coupling" that motivates the work.

2.  **Quality of Regret Bounds:** The problem-dependent regret bound for UCB-ALP is stated as O(JK log T). This form is quite coarse. Standard analysis for UCB-style algorithms typically yields problem-dependent bounds that scale with the sum of inverse squared reward gaps (e.g., sum over suboptimal arms i of log(T)/Δ_i^2). A blanket factor of JK hides the true instance difficulty. Is this coarse bound an artifact of the proof technique, which relies on bounding the probability of "ordering events" (E_rank)? A more refined analysis would provide much greater insight. I encourage the authors to rework the bounds in the supplementary material to provide a clean, problem-dependent upper bound expressed in terms of the relevant reward and cost gaps.

3.  **Lack of Lower Bounds:** The paper provides upper bounds on regret but no corresponding lower bounds. To establish the significance of the results, it is crucial to show that the proposed algorithms are optimal or near-optimal. The authors should provide problem-dependent and problem-independent lower bounds that match their upper bounds. A non-trivial lower bound would need to incorporate the complexity arising from both the context arrival process and the budget constraint, going beyond simple reductions to classical bandit lower bounds.

4.  **Clarity on Regret Rate:** In Theorem 2, the regret for boundary cases is given as O(sqrt(T) + JK log T). Does the sqrt(T) term hide dependencies on problem parameters? For instance, in many bandit settings, this term would appear as O(sqrt(K T log T)) or similar. Please clarify the exact dependence.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical variant of the contextual bandit problem.
*   The ALP approximation of the oracle is clever, and its O(1) regret analysis is a strong theoretical result.
*   The paper is the first to establish a logarithmic regret bound for this problem setting.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   The novelty is questionable, as the problem can be reframed as a known "bandits with knapsacks" problem, making the O(log T) result less surprising.
*   The regret bounds are coarse and not in the standard problem-dependent form that depends on reward gaps.
*   The analysis lacks matching lower bounds to demonstrate the optimality of the proposed algorithms.

**Recommendation:**

I am currently neutral on this paper. The ALP approximation is a nice contribution, but I am not yet convinced of the overall originality and significance of the UCB-ALP result. The claim of achieving logarithmic regret seems to follow from the fact that with enough time, the problem decomposes. The analysis needs to be tightened, and the authors need to provide a compelling argument in their rebuttal for why this problem is not just a straightforward instance of bandits with knapsacks. I am willing to reconsider my score if the authors can satisfactorily address the concerns regarding novelty and provide a clearer picture of the tightness of their bounds, ideally by discussing the prospects for matching lower bounds.
