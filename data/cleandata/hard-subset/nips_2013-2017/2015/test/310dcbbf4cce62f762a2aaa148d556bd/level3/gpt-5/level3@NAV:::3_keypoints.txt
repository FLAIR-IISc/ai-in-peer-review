- Problem setup: contextual bandits with hard budget B and known horizon T, i.i.d. contexts, skip action, known costs c_{j,k}, stochastic rewards in [0,1].  
- Main idea: approximate DP oracle with an adaptive linear program (ALP) that replaces hard budget constraint by average remaining budget bτ/τ.  
- Special-case result: for known context distribution π and unit costs, UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret under a mild non-boundary condition (ρ not equal to certain cumulative context probabilities), and O(√T) in boundary cases.  
- Extensions: approach extended to unknown π (using empirical estimates) and heterogeneous costs, preserving similar regret guarantees.  
- Novelty: ALP surrogate coupled with UCB is simple, new, and yields the first O(log T) for budgeted contextual bandits (previous work gave O(√T)).  
- Technical strength: careful analysis using a “sampling without replacement” view to get concentration of remaining budget bτ/τ, enabling tight bounds.  
- Key dependency: results rely heavily on Lemma 1 (LP upper bound via static relaxation).  
- Clarity: paper is very clearly written; LP/ALP construction and regret decomposition (action- vs context-ranking errors) are crisply explained.  
- Suggested improvement: important proof idea (why average-constraint sequence is optimal) should be highlighted in the main text.  
- Limitation: O(log T) requires a non-boundary condition; boundary cases have an Ω(√T) lower bound.  
- Open questions: is computing the exact offline optimal action sequence NP-hard?  
- Open questions: can the method handle unknown horizon T (e.g., via doubling/anytime ALP that preserves bτ/τ behavior)?  
- Open questions: in unknown π case, does unknown π break assumed ordering u*1>u*2>…>u*J, or can that ordering be assumed w.l.o.g.?  
- Open questions: for heterogeneous costs, what is per-round LP complexity in J, K and can problem structure be exploited?  
- Pros: first O(log T) result, elegant and practical algorithm, clear writing, solid proofs, meaningful theoretical advance.  
- Cons: reliance on a mild non-boundary condition, heavy dependence on Lemma 1, and unknown-horizon setting not addressed.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept.
