This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon T and a budget B. This is a practical and important extension of the standard contextual bandit setting, where resource constraints introduce complex temporal dependencies, making the exploration-exploitation tradeoff significantly more challenging. The main contribution is a new algorithm, UCB-ALP, which is shown to be the first to achieve a logarithmic regret of O(log T) for this problem. This improves upon previous state-of-the-art results which achieved O(√T) regret (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., COLT 2014).

The core of the proposed method is an elegant two-step approach. First, for the oracle setting where all problem statistics are known, the authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. ALP approximates the intractable dynamic programming solution by repeatedly solving a simple linear program that adapts to the current ratio of remaining budget to remaining time. This approximation is shown to be remarkably effective, achieving O(1) regret in most cases. Second, for the practical setting with unknown rewards, this ALP policy is combined with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) estimation method. The resulting UCB-ALP algorithm carefully balances learning the unknown rewards with making near-optimal budget-aware decisions. The paper also discusses extensions to the more general cases of unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its main theoretical result: achieving O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits. This is a substantial improvement over the existing O(√T) bounds and represents a significant advance in our understanding of this problem. The work is highly original in its approach to decoupling the learning and planning aspects of the problem via the ALP approximation.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical approach is sound, novel, and insightful. The idea of using an adaptive linear program that only relies on the *ordering* of rewards is clever, as this property makes it robust to estimation errors and enables the combination with UCB. The analysis, which relies on showing the concentration of the remaining budget process, is non-trivial and well-executed.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and motivated. It builds the argument logically, starting from the oracle case with unit costs and progressively moving to more complex scenarios. This makes a potentially dense topic very accessible to the reader. The design of the ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms is simple and intuitive.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Conditional Guarantee:** The main limitation is that the O(log T) regret bound holds only in "non-boundary" cases, i.e., when the budget-to-horizon ratio `ρ = B/T` does not coincide with certain cumulative context probabilities. In the "boundary" cases, the regret bound degrades to O(√T). While the authors correctly argue that these boundary cases form a set of measure zero, this condition does weaken the result. A universal logarithmic regret bound would have been a much stronger contribution. It would be valuable for the authors to comment on whether this is a fundamental limitation of the problem or an artifact of their analytical technique.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel algorithm that is the first to achieve logarithmic regret for the constrained contextual bandit problem, a significant improvement over prior art.
*   The proposed method (ALP + UCB) is elegant, simple, and computationally efficient, contrasting with more complex prior approaches.
*   The paper is very clearly written and the analysis appears technically sound.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The logarithmic regret guarantee is conditional and does not hold universally for all problem parameters, reverting to the known O(√T) bound in boundary cases.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

*   The motivation for the LP approximation rests on the intractability of the optimal DP solution. Could you clarify if computing the optimal policy is known to be NP-hard? A formal statement on this would further strengthen the motivation for your approximation.
*   Lemma 1, which provides an upper bound on the oracle's performance, is a cornerstone of the analysis. While the proof is in the appendix, the claim itself is powerful. Adding a sentence or two of intuition in the main text about why this relaxation holds could be helpful for the reader.
*   In the discussion of unknown context distributions (Section 5), it is assumed for simplicity that `u*1 > u*2 > ... > u*J`. In the online setting where these rewards are unknown, this ordering is also unknown. I presume the algorithm sorts the contexts based on the current UCB estimates `u_hat*j(t)` at each step. Could you please confirm this and clarify it in the text?
*   The analysis assumes a known, fixed time-horizon T. Are there fundamental challenges to extending this framework to settings with an unknown or infinite horizon?

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on contextual bandits. The strengths—a novel O(log T) regret bound achieved via an elegant and simple algorithm—far outweigh the limitation of the non-boundary condition. I strongly recommend acceptance.
