This paper proposes a novel algorithm for constrained contextual bandits, a variant of the classic multi-armed bandit problem with budget and time constraints. The authors introduce an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves near-optimal performance when the system statistics are known. They then extend this algorithm to the case where the expected rewards are unknown by combining it with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method.

The paper makes several key contributions. Firstly, it provides a novel approach to solving the constrained contextual bandit problem by using linear programming to approximate the oracle algorithm. Secondly, it shows that the ALP algorithm achieves O(1) regret except for certain boundary cases, where it achieves O(√T) regret. Finally, it extends the ALP algorithm to the case where the expected rewards are unknown and shows that the UCB-ALP algorithm achieves O(log T) regret except for certain boundary cases.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, with a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with existing work and highlight the advantages of their approach.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to solving the constrained contextual bandit problem using linear programming
* Achieves O(1) regret except for certain boundary cases, where it achieves O(√T) regret
* Extends the ALP algorithm to the case where the expected rewards are unknown and achieves O(log T) regret except for certain boundary cases
* Well-motivated and clearly written paper with a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance

Weaknesses:

* The algorithm relies heavily on the assumption of known context distribution, which may not always be the case in practice
* The analysis of the algorithm's performance is complex and may be difficult to follow for non-experts
* The paper does not provide a clear comparison with other state-of-the-art algorithms for constrained contextual bandits

Overall, I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The authors have made significant contributions to the field of constrained contextual bandits, and their algorithm has the potential to be widely adopted in practice.
