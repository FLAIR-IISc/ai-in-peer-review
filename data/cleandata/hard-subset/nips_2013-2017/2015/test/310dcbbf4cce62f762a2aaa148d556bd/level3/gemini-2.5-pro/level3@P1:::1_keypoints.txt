- Problem studied: contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B, terminating when either is exhausted.
- Goal: design computationally efficient algorithms achieving logarithmic or sublinear regret versus an oracle that knows context distribution and expected rewards.
- Method: relax hard budget via an LP and propose Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) that re-solves LP using remaining budget-to-time ratio b_τ/τ.
- ALP theoretical behavior: O(1) regret in non-boundary cases (ρ = B/T not aligned with certain context probabilities) and O(√T) in boundary cases.
- Extension to learning: combine ALP with UCB to form UCB-ALP, achieving O(log T) regret (non-boundary) and O(√T + log T) (boundary).
- Additional extensions: discussions of unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.
- Strength: well-motivated practical problem formulation capturing constraints beyond standard contextual bandits.
- Strength: ALP is a tractable, provably near-optimal oracle approximation with an elegant O(1) regret result in non-boundary cases.
- Strength: first claimed logarithmic regret bound for this constrained contextual bandit setting.
- Concern: novelty—problem can be reframed as JK arms with stochastic availability (bandits with knapsacks), so log T may be unsurprising.
- Concern: coarse regret bound O(JK log T); reviewer asks for refined, gap-dependent bounds (e.g., scaling with 1/Δ_i^2).
- Concern: lack of lower bounds—no problem-dependent or problem-independent lower bounds to assess optimality.
- Concern: clarity on the √T term in Theorem 2—possible hidden dependencies on problem parameters (e.g., K) need clarification.
- Recommendation/status: reviewer neutral; requests tighter analysis, clearer novelty argument vs. bandits-with-knapsacks, and matching lower bounds before changing score.
