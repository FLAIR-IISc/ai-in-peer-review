This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget (B). The authors introduce a novel algorithmic framework that, for finite contexts and actions, achieves logarithmic regret in T, a significant improvement over the standard O(√T) regret seen in more general settings. The core of their approach is to first approximate the intractable dynamic programming solution of the oracle with an Adaptive Linear Program (ALP). This ALP approximation has the desirable property that it only requires the correct ordering of expected rewards. Building on this insight, the authors propose the UCB-ALP algorithm for the case with unknown rewards, which combines the ALP decision-making framework with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) estimation method. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in "non-boundary" cases (where the budget-to-time ratio is not equal to certain critical values) and O(√T) regret otherwise. The work is also extended to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Significance:** The problem of incorporating resource constraints (like budgets) into contextual bandits is of high practical importance in areas like computational advertising, clinical trials, and recommendation systems. This paper addresses a relevant and challenging open problem.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Result:** The primary contribution is an algorithm that achieves O(log T) regret. This is the first result of its kind for this problem setting and represents a substantial theoretical improvement over existing O(√T) bounds for related, more general problems.
3.  **Novel Algorithmic Approach:** The decomposition of the problem via the Adaptive Linear Program (ALP) is an elegant and powerful idea. It effectively decouples the complex, long-term planning aspect (handled by the LP) from the online learning aspect (handled by UCB), making both the algorithm design and its analysis more tractable.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
Despite the strengths, there are several points that need clarification to fully assess the paper's contribution, primarily concerning originality and the tightness of the theoretical analysis.

1.  **Novelty of the Problem Formulation:** The paper argues that the budget constraint creates complex coupling among contexts over time. However, for a large time horizon T and i.i.d. context arrivals, it is not entirely clear why this coupling does not vanish in expectation. Could the problem be viewed more simply as a standard multi-armed bandit problem with J*K arms, where at each time step, only the K arms corresponding to the observed context are available? This framing reduces the problem to a "bandits with knapsacks" or "budgeted bandits" problem with time-varying arm availability. The authors should explicitly clarify the key technical challenges and novelty that distinguish their problem from these well-established frameworks and explain why existing techniques are insufficient. Without this clarification, the originality of the contribution is difficult to ascertain.

2.  **Form of the Problem-Dependent Regret Bound:** The problem-dependent regret bound in Theorem 2 is given as O(JK log T). This form is rather coarse, as it scales with the total number of context-action pairs. Standard, more informative problem-dependent bounds for UCB-style algorithms are typically expressed as a sum over suboptimal arms involving their reward gaps (e.g., Σ (log T / Δ²)). The current proof technique, which bounds the number of times UCB ordering events are incorrect, may be the reason for this looser bound. A cleaner, gap-dependent bound would significantly strengthen the paper's claims. Can the analysis be refined to produce such a bound? The bound presented in the supplementary material for the heterogeneous cost case also seems to require reworking for clarity and tightness.

3.  **Lack of Lower Bounds:** The paper provides upper bounds on regret but offers no corresponding lower bounds. To demonstrate the optimality of the proposed algorithms, it is essential to provide matching lower bounds for both the problem-dependent (logarithmic) and problem-independent (square-root) cases. A non-trivial lower bound should specifically incorporate the complexity arising from the interaction between contexts and the global budget constraint, going beyond a simple reduction to the classical K-armed bandit lower bound.

4.  **Clarification on Regret Rate in Theorem 2:** For the boundary cases, Theorem 2 states a regret of O(√T + ...). Standard problem-independent regret bounds for K-armed bandits often scale as O(√(KT)). Could the authors clarify if the dependence on J and K is hidden in the O(·) notation? A rate that is truly independent of the number of arms seems surprising, and a more precise statement of this dependency is needed.

**Summary:**
This paper presents an elegant algorithmic solution for the constrained contextual bandit problem, achieving an impressive O(log T) regret bound. The use of an adaptive LP to approximate the oracle is a key strength. However, the review is tempered by concerns regarding the novelty of the problem formulation when compared to existing budgeted bandit literature. Furthermore, the analysis could be strengthened by providing tighter, gap-dependent regret bounds and establishing matching lower bounds to prove optimality.

Due to insufficient evidence of originality relative to the bandits with knapsacks literature, I am remaining neutral on this paper. I would be willing to raise my score if the authors can provide a convincing argument in their rebuttal that clarifies the novelty of their formulation and addresses the questions about the theoretical analysis.
