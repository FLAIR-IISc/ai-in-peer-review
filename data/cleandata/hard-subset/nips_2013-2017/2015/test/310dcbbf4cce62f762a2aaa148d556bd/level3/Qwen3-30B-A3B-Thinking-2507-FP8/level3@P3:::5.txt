

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints ("constrained contextual bandits"), a significant extension of standard contextual bandit problems that better models real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing. The authors consider a simplified model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which they justify as applicable to many practical scenarios. The key contribution is a computationally efficient algorithm (UCB-ALP) that achieves logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases), significantly improving upon prior work that achieved O(√T) regret for similar problems [17].

The paper makes several important technical contributions. First, it proposes an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle algorithm that achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases) by dynamically adjusting the budget constraint according to the remaining budget and time. This is a crucial insight that enables the subsequent algorithmic development. Second, the authors combine ALP with UCB to develop UCB-ALP, which achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases (where J is the number of contexts and K is the number of actions). This represents the first known result achieving logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under their model assumptions. The theoretical analysis is particularly strong, employing novel techniques to bound regret through careful handling of context ranking errors and remaining budget fluctuations.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the intuition behind each algorithmic component and a well-structured theoretical development. The authors effectively relate their work to previous NIPS contributions, particularly distinguishing their approach from [17] (Resourceful Contextual Bandits) and [20] (which achieves O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts).

The main strengths of the paper include its clean theoretical framework, rigorous analysis, practical relevance, and the elegant combination of LP approximation with bandit exploration. The paper makes a significant contribution to the constrained bandit literature by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting.

However, I have several suggestions for improvement. First, the authors should consider parametric reward models (e.g., linear models) to address scalability when contexts become numerous, as suggested in the key points. This would strengthen the paper's applicability to larger-scale problems. Second, Section 4.1 could be improved by explicitly stating how the optimism principle applies in the UCB-ALP algorithm and clarifying the relationship to UCRL2 policy updates, as this would help readers understand the algorithmic design more thoroughly. Third, the paper lacks numerical experiments to validate the theoretical results, which would be valuable in an extended version. Finally, the authors should cite arXiv:1506.03374 by Agrawal et al. (which addresses similar problems with linear contextual bandits) to properly situate their work within the broader literature.

The paper's quality is high, with technically sound claims supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The clarity of presentation is excellent, and the originality is clear through the novel LP-based approximation approach and the resulting logarithmic regret bound. The significance of the work is substantial, as it provides an efficient, near-optimal algorithm for a practically important problem that had previously only been solved with sublinear regret.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution to the contextual bandit literature by providing the first logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits under discrete contexts and actions. The algorithm is computationally efficient and well-motivated by real-world applications. The minor suggestions for improvement (parametric models, clearer rationale for UCB-ALP, numerical experiments, and additional citations) would further strengthen an already excellent paper.

I recommend acceptance, as this work provides an important improvement in the constrained bandit literature with near-optimal regret guarantees. The paper merits publication at NIPS as it makes a clear theoretical contribution with practical implications for resource-constrained online decision making.
