This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors introduce an adaptive linear programming approximation of the oracle and propose algorithms that achieve O(log T) regret. The paper is well-structured, clear, and well-documented, with explicit motivation and relevant citations.

The main idea of the paper is to tackle the challenges introduced by budget constraints in contextual bandits. The authors restrict their analysis to finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which is a reasonable simplification for many practical scenarios. They propose an adaptive linear programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle and achieves near-optimal performance. The ALP algorithm is then combined with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to handle the case where the expected rewards are unknown.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the regret of the proposed algorithms, including the ALP algorithm and the UCB-ALP algorithm. They show that the ALP algorithm achieves O(1) regret except for certain boundary cases, where it achieves O(√T) regret. The UCB-ALP algorithm is shown to achieve O(log T) regret except for certain boundary cases, where it achieves O(√T) regret.

The paper makes solid theoretical contributions, and the proofs are convincing. However, the practical aspects of the algorithms are not thoroughly treated. Sections 5 and 6, which discuss the cases with unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs, respectively, are brief and lack experimental results. The authors claim that the empirical distribution provides a maximum likelihood estimate of the context distribution, but a rigorous analysis of the empirical ALP (EALP) algorithm is challenging due to the dependency introduced by the empirical distribution.

The paper cites relevant work, including the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) model, which is a more general setting with possibly infinite contexts, random costs, and multiple budget constraints. However, the authors argue that their simplified model is justified in many scenarios, such as clinical trials and rate selection in wireless networks.

Overall, this is a good paper that uses appropriate simplifying assumptions to obtain strong theoretical results. The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their ideas, and the theoretical contributions are solid. However, the practical aspects of the algorithms could be further explored, and experimental results would be helpful in demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
