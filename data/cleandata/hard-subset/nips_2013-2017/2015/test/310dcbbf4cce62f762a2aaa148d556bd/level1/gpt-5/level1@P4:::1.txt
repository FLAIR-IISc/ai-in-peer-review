Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with both a finite time horizon T and a hard budget B, where each action under a context incurs a cost and yields a reward. The authors focus first on unit-cost, finite, i.i.d. contexts with known distribution, and then extend to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs. The core idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming (oracle) solution via an adaptive linear program (ALP) that enforces an average budget constraint equal to the current average remaining budget bτ/τ. The ALP solution leads to a simple threshold policy over contexts. A key technical insight is that, under ALP, the remaining budget evolves like sampling without replacement, enabling a hypergeometric concentration analysis. The ALP achieves O(1) regret (vs. the oracle) except at “boundary” values of the budget ratio ρ=B/T that coincide with cumulative context probabilities, where the regret is O(√T). For unknown rewards, the authors combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) to learn the required rankings; they prove O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They further discuss using empirical context frequencies (EALP/UCB-EALP) and a heterogeneous-cost extension (with an ε-first variant for unknown rewards).

This work relates to: (i) non-contextual “bandits with budgets/knapsacks” [11–16], which achieve sublinear regret but do not handle observable contexts; (ii) resourceful contextual bandits (RCB) [17], which allow more general resource models but achieve O(√T) regret vs. a finite policy comparator with higher computational complexity; and (iii) bandits with concave rewards and convex knapsacks [20–22], which yield O(√T) regret in static/dynamic contexts, often with infinite policy classes. In contrast, this paper achieves logarithmic regret under a more specialized but practically relevant setting with discrete contexts/actions and a single budget.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple algorithmic idea (adaptive LP relaxation) that is computationally efficient (threshold structure; only ordering of u∗j needed).
- Technically solid analysis: the mapping of budget evolution to sampling without replacement and the resulting concentration lemmas are elegant and central to the regret bounds.
- First logarithmic-regret guarantees (to the best of my knowledge) for constrained contextual bandits with stochastic contexts under discrete, finite settings.
- Thoughtful decomposition of regret into action-ranking and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation components; UCB-ALP analysis aligns with standard UCB machinery.
- Extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs, with clear identification of where analysis is harder (handled in the supplement).

Weaknesses:
- Scope relies on strong assumptions: finite i.i.d. contexts, known horizon, single budget, unit costs (first), uniqueness and strict ordering of best expected rewards across contexts. Practical implications of relaxing these (e.g., ties, near-ties) are only briefly discussed.
- Boundary-case behavior degrades to O(√T). While measure-zero in the limit, near-boundary regimes may be common in practice; robustness bounds as ρ approaches qj are not quantified beyond asymptotics.
- For unknown context distributions, the rigorous analysis uses a truncated empirical estimator; the gap between theory and the fully adaptive empirical version used in practice could be narrowed.
- Heterogeneous-cost, unknown-reward case resorts to ε-first rather than a fully adaptive UCB-like scheme; regret constants and practicality may be less attractive.
- No explicit instance-dependent constants or gap dependence are stated in the main text; “JK log T” hides potentially large dependence on reward gaps and the number of arms.
- Limited empirical evidence in the main text; experiments that compare ALP/UCB-ALP to RCB [17] and BwK-type baselines [12] would strengthen the case.

Quality:
- The methodology is technically sound and carefully argued. The hypergeometric concentration for bτ under ALP/UCB-ALP is convincing and seems correctly leveraged to bound regret.
- The regret decomposition and use of standard UCB concentration (Lemma 3) are appropriate. The proofs are sketched clearly in the main text, with details deferred to the supplement.
- Some assumptions (unique best action per context; strict ordering of u∗j) simplify exposition; the authors should clarify how ties or near-ties affect thresholds and regret constants.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and well-organized. The problem statement, benchmarks, and algorithmic descriptions are clear. The threshold policy and its intuition are easy to follow.
- Minor suggestions: explicitly state per-round computational complexity (sorting û∗j and computing p̂j); clarify behavior when b=0 before T; discuss tie-breaking and near-tie robustness; add a brief summary of dependence on gaps (Δ) in the JK log T term.

Originality:
- The adaptive LP relaxation tailored to constrained contextual bandits, and the analysis yielding O(1)/logarithmic regret in the discrete setting, are novel relative to [12,17,20–22].

Significance:
- The results advance theoretical understanding by showing that logarithmic regret is achievable under budget constraints with contexts, in contrast to prior O(√T) guarantees.
- The approach is computationally lightweight and potentially useful in applications with finite context sets (e.g., rate selection, clinical trials). Extensions to broader settings could be catalyzed by these techniques.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually simple, computationally efficient algorithms with strong regret guarantees.
- Novel analysis (sampling-without-replacement view) enabling tight bounds.
- Bridges a gap between contextual bandits and resource constraints with improved rates.
- Clear pathway to extensions; relevant to several application domains.

Cons for acceptance:
- Restricted setting; robustness near boundaries and under ties not fully quantified.
- Unknown context distribution and heterogeneous-cost cases have weaker/partial theory.
- Limited empirical validation presented in the main text.
- No lower bounds tailored to this model to certify optimality beyond order terms.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide experiments comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to RCB [17] and BwK [12], and sensitivity to ρ near qj.
- Clarify handling of ties and near-ties; give gap-dependent constants where possible.
- Discuss whether a fully adaptive UCB-style method can replace ε-first under heterogeneous costs, and the trade-offs.
- Tighten the analysis (or provide guidance) for the fully online empirical-context version without truncation.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear novelty and solid analysis in a well-motivated constrained contextual bandit setting.
