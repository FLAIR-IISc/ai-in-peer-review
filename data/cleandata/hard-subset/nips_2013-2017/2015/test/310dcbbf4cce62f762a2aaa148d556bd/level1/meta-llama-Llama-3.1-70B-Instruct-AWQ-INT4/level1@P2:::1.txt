**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits" studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle algorithm and achieves near-optimality with only the ordering of expected rewards. They then combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to propose a UCB-ALP algorithm for the general case where the expected rewards are unknown. The authors show that UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work on contextual bandits, including the epoch-greedy algorithm [1], contextual multi-armed bandits [2], and a survey on contextual multi-armed bandits [3]. The authors also relate their work to previous studies on budget-limited multi-armed bandits [11-16] and resourceful contextual bandits [17]. However, the authors note that their work is distinct from previous studies in that they consider a "hard" budget constraint and aim to achieve logarithmic regret.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Algorithm**: The authors propose a novel ALP algorithm that approximates the oracle algorithm and achieves near-optimality with only the ordering of expected rewards.
2. **Theoretical Guarantees**: The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the regret of ALP and UCB-ALP, showing that they achieve logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases.
3. **Practical Applications**: The authors demonstrate the practicality of their algorithms by applying them to real-world scenarios such as clinical trials and online hiring in crowdsourcing.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Simplifying Assumptions**: The authors make simplifying assumptions, such as finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which may not hold in all real-world scenarios.
2. **Boundary Cases**: The authors note that their algorithms achieve sublinear regret in boundary cases, which may be a limitation in certain applications.
3. **Computational Complexity**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their algorithms, which may be a concern in large-scale applications.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their ideas. The theoretical guarantees for the regret of ALP and UCB-ALP are rigorous and well-supported by mathematical analysis. However, the authors could provide more details on the computational complexity of their algorithms and the practical implications of their simplifying assumptions.
