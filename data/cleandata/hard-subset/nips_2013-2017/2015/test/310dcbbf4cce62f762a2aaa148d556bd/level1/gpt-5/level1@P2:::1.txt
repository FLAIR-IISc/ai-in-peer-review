Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with a single hard budget and a finite horizon, where contexts are i.i.d. from a finite set and costs are fixed (initially unit). The key difficulty is that, unlike standard contextual bandits, the optimal policy (oracle) must couple actions across time via the remaining budget/time, leading to an intractable DP benchmark.

The authors propose a linear programming (LP) relaxation that replaces the hard budget with an average budget constraint, yielding a simple, closed-form “static LP” upper bound on the oracle’s expected reward. Building on this, they design an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that, at each round, re-solves the LP with the average remaining budget bτ/τ and plays the best action for the current context with a context-dependent probability. A core technical insight is that under ALP, budget consumption is equivalent to sampling without replacement, so the remaining budget concentrates (hypergeometric tails). Consequently, ALP’s expected reward is within O(1) of the oracle except in boundary cases where ρ = B/T equals a cumulative context probability, in which case regret is O(√T).

For unknown rewards, ALP is combined with UCB (UCB-ALP): maintain UCB estimates of each context-action mean, take the current per-context argmax, and feed their UCBs into the ALP. The authors prove RUCB-ALP = O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They also discuss unknown context distributions (replace π with empirical estimates; rigorous bounds require truncating the estimate update) and heterogeneous costs (generalized LP across all (j, k); O(1) regret with known stats; for unknown stats an epsilon-first ALP is proposed).

This work connects to Bandits with Knapsacks (BwK) and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB). Prior non-contextual BwK and contextual RCB (e.g., Mixture Elimination) typically achieve O(√T) regret, often against a finite policy class and with high computational cost. Agrawal & Devanur’s primal-dual style algorithms for concave rewards/convex knapsacks achieve O(√T) and assume static contexts or handle dynamic contexts with O(√T) regret. In contrast, this paper achieves O(log T) regret (instance-dependent) in finite, stochastic-context settings by adaptively matching the remaining budget to context arrivals via LP, which appears novel.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The LP upper bound and ALP design are clean, and the regret proofs are grounded in a neat budget-concentration argument via hypergeometric tails. The UCB-ALP analysis carefully separates regret from within-context action misranking (O(log T)) and regret from context misranking and budget fluctuations (O(JK log T)). The boundary-case degradation to O(√T) is explicitly identified and justified.
- Completeness/honesty: Assumptions (finite contexts/actions, i.i.d. contexts, known costs, uniqueness and strict ordering for exposition) are stated. Unknown π requires a truncated analysis; heterogeneous costs with unknown stats rely on epsilon-first. These limitations are acknowledged.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The oracle approximation, ALP algorithm, and the mapping to sampling without replacement are clearly explained. The definition of boundaries qj and their role is clear. Suggestions: explicitly state how to handle ties in u* across contexts and actions; give pseudocode for ALP; include a brief geometric intuition for v(ρ) (piecewise-linear concave envelope); clarify computational cost per round (LP with J variables and a simple threshold solution).

Originality:
- The adaptive LP idea tailored to hard budget contextual bandits and the proof that ALP is within O(1) of the oracle (non-boundary) is a notable conceptual contribution. Achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits by combining ALP with UCB appears to be the first such result under these assumptions.

Significance:
- The results are practically relevant (budgeted recommendation, clinical trials) and likely to influence follow-up work on constrained contextual bandits. The decoupling of estimation from decision via a robust ALP that only needs ranks is insightful and potentially reusable. While assumptions are specialized (finite, i.i.d. contexts, single resource), this is a meaningful step toward computationally efficient algorithms with strong regret guarantees.

Strengths:
- Simple, computationally efficient algorithmic framework (thresholded LP with adaptive bτ/τ).
- Strong, instance-dependent regret guarantees: O(1) vs. oracle (known stats) and O(JK log T) (unknown stats) in non-boundary cases.
- Clean concentration argument for budget dynamics; clear separation of regret sources.
- Extensions to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs, with plausible techniques.

Weaknesses:
- Scope limitations: finite i.i.d. contexts, single resource, known costs (initially). Extensions to continuous/structured contexts (linear/similarity) are not addressed; dependence O(JK log T) is large if J, K are big.
- Boundary cases yield O(√T) and may be non-negligible in discrete systems; no mechanism to avoid or smooth the boundary (e.g., randomized tie-breaking of ρ or budget shaping).
- Unknown context distribution requires truncating empirical updates to prove concentration; the gap between the analyzed truncated variant and the natural continuously-updating algorithm should be discussed more.
- Heterogeneous costs with unknown stats revert to epsilon-first; no log-regret analog of UCB-ALP is provided.
- Empirical evaluation (if any) is not discussed here; comparisons to primal-dual BwK/RCB baselines would strengthen the case.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide experiments comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to Mixture Elimination and primal-dual BwK variants under finite contexts.
- Discuss practical handling of boundary/tie cases and whether slight budget randomization can recover log-regret.
- Explore structured settings (e.g., linear contextual rewards) to reduce the O(JK) dependence.
- Tighten the analysis for unknown π without truncation, or empirically validate that truncation is unnecessary.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually novel and elegant adaptive LP approach with strong theoretical guarantees.
- First logarithmic-regret result (to my knowledge) for constrained contextual bandits under finite stochastic contexts.
- Computationally practical and broadly applicable framework.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Restricted setting; boundary-case √T regret and lack of a remedy.
- Analysis for unknown π relies on a truncated estimator; heterogeneous-cost unknown case lacks UCB-style log-regret.
- Limited empirical validation in the main text.

Overall recommendation: Accept (borderline to clear accept). The paper advances the theory of constrained contextual bandits with a simple, well-justified algorithm and strong guarantees, and is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.
