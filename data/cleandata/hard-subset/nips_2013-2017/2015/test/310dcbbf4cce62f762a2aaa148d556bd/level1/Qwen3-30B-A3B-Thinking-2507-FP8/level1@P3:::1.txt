

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses the important problem of contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors make a significant theoretical contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases) in this setting, which represents a substantial advancement over prior work. Previous work on budget constraints (e.g., [11-16]) applies to non-contextual bandits, while [17] studies resourceful contextual bandits but achieves only O(√T) regret with a restricted benchmark. The authors' key insight is to approximate the oracle with Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP), which achieves near-optimal performance with O(1) regret (except for boundary cases), and then combine this with UCB to create UCB-ALP, which achieves O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, the ALP approximation is cleverly designed to relax the hard budget constraint to an average budget constraint, providing a clean theoretical framework that avoids the computational complexity of dynamic programming. The regret analysis for ALP is rigorous, showing that it achieves O(1) regret except for boundary cases where it achieves O(√T) regret. This is a substantial improvement over the O(√T) regret of prior work. Second, the authors successfully combine ALP with UCB to create UCB-ALP, which achieves logarithmic regret for non-boundary cases. The analysis of how ALP tolerates estimation errors and can be combined with UCB is particularly elegant and insightful. Third, the paper extends naturally to more general settings (unknown context distribution, heterogeneous costs), demonstrating the robustness of their approach.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to extensions. The key insights about approximating the oracle with ALP and the adaptation property of UCB-ALP are well-explained. The authors are careful to identify limitations, particularly the boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T), and they provide a thorough discussion of how their approach differs from prior work.

However, there are some important limitations to consider. First, the paper's assumption of known context distribution (until Section 5) is restrictive for many practical applications. While they address this in Section 5 with Empirical ALP (EALP), the analysis is more complex and the regret bounds are less clean than for the known distribution case. Second, the experimental validation is minimal (only mentioned in the supplementary material), which would help confirm the theoretical results. Third, the comparison with the recent work [21, 22] could be more thorough. The authors claim their approach achieves logarithmic regret while [21, 22] achieve O(√T) regret, but they don't fully explain why their approach achieves a better regret bound in the dynamic context case. This comparison is particularly important since [21, 22] explicitly extend their work to dynamic contexts.

The paper's significance is high. Constrained contextual bandits are relevant to many real-world applications, including clinical trials, crowdsourcing, and online recommendation systems. Achieving logarithmic regret represents a major theoretical advance over prior work, and the authors' approach provides a framework that could be extended to more general constrained contextual bandit problems. The paper identifies an important open problem (efficient algorithms for general constrained contextual bandits) and makes significant progress on it.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by detailed proofs in the supplementary material. The authors carefully address the challenges of the exploration-exploitation tradeoff under budget constraints, and their approach of decoupling information acquisition from decision making is particularly insightful.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution over prior work. The authors correctly identify that prior work on budget constraints does not apply to contextual bandits, and their ALP approach is novel. While the idea of approximating DP with LP has been studied before (e.g., [17, 19]), the authors' specific implementation and analysis for constrained contextual bandits is new.

For acceptance, the main strengths are the significant theoretical contribution (logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits), the elegant ALP approximation, and the clear analysis of how to combine it with UCB. The main weaknesses are the limited experimental validation, the restrictive assumption of known context distribution, and the need for a more thorough comparison with [21, 22].

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are important and advance the state of the art. While there are some limitations, they do not undermine the main contribution, which is a major step toward solving the open problem of efficient algorithms for constrained contextual bandits. I recommend acceptance.
