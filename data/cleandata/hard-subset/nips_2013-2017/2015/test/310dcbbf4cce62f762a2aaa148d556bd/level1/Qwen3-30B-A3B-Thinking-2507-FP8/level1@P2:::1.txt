

# Review of "Algorithms with Logarithmic or Sublinear Regret for Constrained Contextual Bandits"

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, a variant of the contextual bandit problem where both time and budget constraints limit the agent's actions. The authors propose a novel approach combining adaptive linear programming (ALP) with UCB to achieve logarithmic regret, which is a significant improvement over previous work that achieved O(√T) regret.

The paper builds upon prior work on contextual bandits (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 7]) and budget-constrained bandits (e.g., [11-16]), but identifies a critical gap: existing budget-constrained bandit results do not generalize to contextual settings. The authors note that [17] (Resourceful Contextual Bandits) achieves O(√T) regret but uses a restricted benchmark and lacks computational efficiency. The key contribution is showing that constrained contextual bandits can achieve logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases) with the proposed UCB-ALP algorithm.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper demonstrates strong technical rigor. The ALP approximation provides a near-optimal solution with O(1) regret (except for boundary cases), and the UCB-ALP algorithm achieves O(JK log T) regret. The analysis involves novel techniques for handling concentration properties under adaptive algorithms and bounding estimation errors under random contexts. The authors properly address the challenges of constrained contextual bandits, including the complex coupling between contexts over time, and provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis (with proofs in supplementary material).

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, key challenges, and proposed solutions are articulated with precision. The distinction between unit-cost and heterogeneous cost systems is clearly explained, and the authors carefully define all necessary notation. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

*Originality:* The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. As the authors correctly state, this is the first work to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. The ALP approach represents a novel way to approximate the oracle solution, and the UCB-ALP combination cleverly leverages the ordering property of ALP to tolerate estimation errors. The paper distinguishes itself from [17, 20-22] by addressing a more natural regret definition, achieving logarithmic regret, and providing computationally efficient algorithms.

*Significance:* The results have substantial practical significance for applications like clinical trials and online recommendation systems where both time and budget constraints are critical. The paper provides a computationally efficient approach to a challenging problem, demonstrating how to decouple information acquisition and decision making. The insights about adaptive linear relaxation could inform future work on more general constrained contextual bandits. The O(log T) regret bound represents a meaningful improvement over the O(√T) bounds of previous work.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks experimental validation of the proposed algorithms. The authors mention numerical simulations in the supplementary material but do not provide concrete results or comparisons with baseline methods in the main text. This weakens the practical validation of their claims.

*Clarity:* The paper's focus on unit-cost systems in the main text, with generalizations to heterogeneous costs in Section 6, may confuse readers about the practical applicability of the results. A clearer discussion of when unit-cost assumptions are reasonable versus when heterogeneous costs are necessary would improve the paper.

*Originality:* While the paper claims to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret, it would benefit from a more detailed comparison with [21, 22] (which the authors mention as extending [20] to dynamic contexts). The paper acknowledges these works but could better clarify how their results differ.

*Significance:* The paper's focus on boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T) is somewhat limiting. The authors acknowledge this but do not provide a compelling argument for why these cases are rare or how they might be addressed in practice. The significance would be strengthened by discussing practical implications of these boundary cases.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- First work to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits
- Novel ALP approach provides theoretical insights into the problem structure
- Rigorous analysis with carefully developed techniques
- Significant improvement over previous O(√T) regret bounds
- Well-written and clearly organized paper

*Con:*
- Limited experimental validation of the proposed algorithms
- Boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T) are not fully addressed
- Some technical details about heterogeneous costs are relegated to supplementary material
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the contextual bandit literature by demonstrating how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. The ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms are well-motivated, theoretically sound, and represent a meaningful advancement over previous work. While the lack of experimental validation is a minor weakness, the paper's theoretical contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The results provide valuable insights into the constrained contextual bandit problem and could guide future work on more general settings. The paper meets all NIPS criteria with strong quality, clarity, originality, and significance, and would be a valuable addition to the conference proceedings. I recommend acceptance.
