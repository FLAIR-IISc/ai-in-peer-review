This paper tackles the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, a significant extension of standard contextual bandits incorporating budget and time limitations. The authors make a strong case for the problem's relevance, citing applications in crowdsourcing and clinical trials. The core idea of approximating the oracle using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) and subsequently combining it with UCB for unknown reward scenarios is novel and well-motivated. The analysis appears rigorous, leveraging concentration inequalities and bounding estimation errors.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:** The paper builds upon the contextual bandit literature [1, 2, 3] and extends it to incorporate resource constraints. It acknowledges prior work on budget-constrained bandits [11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16] but correctly points out their inapplicability to the contextual setting. The work also positions itself relative to Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], highlighting the simplifications (finite contexts, fixed costs) that allow for more tractable algorithms and analysis. The combination of LP relaxation and UCB is related to [20, 21, 22], but distinguishes itself by considering random context arrivals and an adaptive budget constraint leading to logarithmic regret. The contribution of achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, a previously open problem, is substantial.

**Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears careful and the regret bounds are clearly stated. The use of LP relaxation and the subsequent ALP algorithm seem well-justified. The extension to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs demonstrates a good understanding of the problem's nuances. While the proofs are likely complex, the structure of the analysis is presented in a logical manner. The paper is largely a complete piece of work, though some of the extensions (Section 5 & 6) feel less fleshed out than the core result in Section 3 & 4, relying on supplementary material. The authors are reasonably honest about the limitations (boundary cases with √T regret).

**Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The notations are mostly consistent, and the algorithm descriptions are concise. However, the mathematical details, especially in the regret analysis, can be dense and challenging to follow without a strong background in bandit theory. Providing more intuition behind some of the key lemmas and theorems would improve readability. The flow between sections could also be smoother, particularly when transitioning from the known-statistics case to the unknown-statistics case.

**Originality (9/10):** The problem itself is an important extension of existing bandit frameworks. The ALP algorithm and its combination with UCB represent a novel approach to addressing the challenges posed by resource constraints. The adaptive budget constraint is a key innovation.  The authors appropriately cite related work and clearly distinguish their contributions.  The fact that this is the first work to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting solidifies its originality.

**Significance (9/10):** The results are significant. Achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits is a major step forward. The proposed algorithms are likely to be useful in real-world applications where resource limitations are prevalent. The insights gained from this work could inform the development of more efficient algorithms for more general constrained bandit problems. The emphasis on computational efficiency is also valuable. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a demonstrably better way than previous research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: Achieves logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits for the first time.
*   Technical Soundness: Rigorous analysis and well-motivated approach.
*   Significance: High potential impact on real-world applications and future research.
*   Clear Presentation:  Generally well-written and organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Density of Analysis: The mathematical details can be difficult to follow.
*   Extensions Less Developed: Sections 5 & 6 rely heavily on supplementary material.
*   Boundary Cases:  The √T regret in boundary cases is a limitation, though acknowledged.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of contextual bandits. The technical details are demanding, but the core ideas are well-presented and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance. A minor suggestion would be to expand on the intuition behind some of the key steps in the analysis and perhaps provide more details on the empirical performance of the algorithms.
