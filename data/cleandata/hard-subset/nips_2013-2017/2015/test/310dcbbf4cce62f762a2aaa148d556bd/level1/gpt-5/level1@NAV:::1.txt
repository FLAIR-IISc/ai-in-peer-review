This paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints, proposing an adaptive linear-programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle when statistics are known and a UCB-ALP algorithm when rewards are unknown. The key idea is to relax the hard knapsack constraint to an adaptive average-constraint LP driven by the remaining budget ratio bτ/τ, yielding an O(1) regret gap to the oracle in unit-cost systems except on measure-zero “boundary” parameters. Combining ALP with UCB produces O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases, and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. The paper further sketches extensions to unknown context distribution via empirical estimates (truncated EALP) and to heterogeneous costs via a generalized LP and an ε-first variant. Relative to Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], Bandits with Concave Rewards and Convex Knapsacks [20–22], and non-contextual budgeted bandits [11–16], the main novelty is achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts/actions with i.i.d. arrivals, via an adaptive LP that decouples estimation and control.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound. The LP upper bound benchmark, the hypergeometric concentration for remaining budget, and the regret decomposition are convincing. Assumptions (unique best action per context, strict ordering of u∗j, i.i.d. contexts, unit costs for core results) are clearly stated. Boundary-case analysis remains weaker (O(√T) with no matching lower bound), and several important proofs are deferred to the supplement. Empirical validation is minimal.

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized; Algorithm 1 is clear. Some notation is heavy and a short illustrative example (beyond J=2) would aid intuition. It would help to discuss per-round LP complexity and practical implementation details for large J,K.

Originality: High. Adaptive LP to achieve log regret under budget constraints in contextual bandits appears new, improving upon O(√T) results in [17,20–22].

Significance: Strong. The approach is computationally light and likely to influence practical designs under budgets. Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs broaden impact, though the ε-first strategy may be conservative.

Pros for acceptance:
- First logarithmic regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits (finite contexts/actions).
- Clean, modular analysis with broadly applicable insights (adaptive relaxation, concentration of bτ).
- Computationally efficient and conceptually simple.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong simplifying assumptions; boundary cases retain √T regret.
- Limited experiments; no comparison to [17,20–22] in practice.
- Extensions (unknown π, heterogeneous costs) are partial and rely on truncation/ε-first heuristics.

Overall, a solid theoretical contribution with clear novelty and potential impact.
