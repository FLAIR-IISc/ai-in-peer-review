This paper studies the contextual bandit problem with both a time horizon and a budget constraint, a practical setting where decisions are coupled over time due to resource limitations. The authors' main idea is to first approximate the intractable optimal oracle policy with an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approach. This ALP algorithm dynamically adjusts its decision-making based on the ratio of remaining budget to remaining time. The paper shows that this approximation is near-optimal for the known-statistics case. Building on this, the authors propose the UCB-ALP algorithm for the more general setting with unknown rewards. This work distinguishes itself from prior research on resourceful contextual bandits (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) by achieving logarithmic regret under a more restricted but still practical set of assumptions (finite contexts, single budget), whereas previous works typically achieve O(√T) regret in more general settings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The paper's primary contribution is providing the first algorithm to achieve logarithmic (O(log T)) regret for constrained contextual bandits. This is a significant theoretical advance for a well-motivated problem. The result demonstrably improves upon the state of the art for this problem class, moving beyond typical square-root regret bounds.
*   **Quality:** The technical approach is sound and elegant. Decomposing the problem by first developing a near-optimal, low-complexity approximation (ALP) for the oracle and then combining it with a standard learning method (UCB) is a powerful and insightful technique. The theoretical analysis, though sketched, appears rigorous.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It builds intuition by starting with the simplest case (unit cost, known statistics) before proceeding to the main UCB-ALP algorithm and its analysis. This structure makes the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The logarithmic regret bound holds under several key assumptions, including finite contexts and, crucially, for "non-boundary" cases where the budget-to-time ratio does not align perfectly with cumulative context probabilities. In "boundary" cases, the regret degrades to O(√T). While these boundary cases may be a set of measure zero, the sensitivity of the algorithm to being near such a boundary is not explored.
*   The analysis in the main paper focuses on the unit-cost setting with a known context distribution. The important extensions to unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs are discussed only briefly, with technical details deferred to the supplementary material. This makes it difficult to fully evaluate the generality of the approach from the main paper alone.

In summary, the paper presents a novel and computationally efficient algorithm with a strong, state-of-the-art theoretical guarantee. The strengths, particularly the achievement of logarithmic regret, substantially outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the scope of the main result. This is a high-quality contribution that will likely inspire further work in bandits with complex constraints.
