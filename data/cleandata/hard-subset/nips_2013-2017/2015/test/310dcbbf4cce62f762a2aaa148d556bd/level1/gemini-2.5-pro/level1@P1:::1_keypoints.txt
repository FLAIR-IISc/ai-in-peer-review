- Paper studies constrained contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B, addressing complex temporal dependencies.  
- Main contribution: UCB-ALP algorithm that is computationally efficient and achieves O(log T) regret.  
- Introduces Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) as an oracle approximation that solves a simple LP using remaining budget/time.  
- For known statistics, ALP attains O(1) regret in most cases, with O(sqrt(T)) only in well-characterized “boundary” cases where ρ=B/T aligns with context probabilities.  
- UCB-ALP plugs UCB reward estimates into ALP to handle unknown rewards, achieving O(log T) in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) in boundary cases.  
- Analysis extended to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs, showing generality of the approach.  
- Technical strengths: rigorous, elegant analysis; key Lemma on concentration of remaining budget; clean regret decomposition into action-ranking and context/budget errors.  
- Quality caveat: core analysis relies on simplifying assumptions (unit costs, known context distribution) presented in main text, with generalizations in supplement.  
- Major weakness: complete lack of experimental validation to assess practical performance, constants, and boundary-case behavior.  
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written and organized, with useful proof sketches and strong positioning against prior work.  
- Originality: novel adaptive combination of LP and UCB; first logarithmic-regret result for this problem class.  
- Significance: important theoretical advance with practical relevance (e.g., ads, trials), and computationally practical per-step LP.  
- Arguments for acceptance: foundational theoretical result, novel ALP approach, high clarity and practical promise.  
- Arguments against acceptance: no experiments and reliance on simplified assumptions in the main presentation.  
- Final recommendation: strong accept as a theory paper, with suggestion to add experimental results in the camera-ready version.
