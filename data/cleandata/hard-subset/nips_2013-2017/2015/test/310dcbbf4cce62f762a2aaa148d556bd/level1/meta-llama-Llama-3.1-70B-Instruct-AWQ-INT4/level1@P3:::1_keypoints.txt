- Studies constrained contextual bandits with budget B and horizon T, aiming to maximize expected reward under constraints.  
- Proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) that approximates an oracle and requires only ordering of expected rewards.  
- Introduces UCB-ALP combining ALP with UCB for unknown expected rewards.  
- Proves UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret in general, with sublinear regret in certain boundary cases.  
- Focus on constrained contextual bandits (budget and time constraints) is novel relative to prior contextual/MAB/RL work.  
- Strength: novel algorithm (UCB-ALP) achieving strong regret guarantees.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis and proof of regret bounds.  
- Strength: algorithm is computationally efficient and practical to implement.  
- Weakness: assumes known context distribution, which may be unrealistic.  
- Weakness: suboptimal (sublinear) regret in boundary cases could limit practical performance.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed empirical or comparative evaluation against existing constrained-bandit algorithms.  
- Quality: technically sound with clear, well-organized presentation and convincing proofs.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and easy to follow.  
- Originality: novel combination of adaptive linear programming and UCB for this problem.  
- Significance: results are important for applications with budget/time constraints (e.g., advertising, clinical trials).  
- For acceptance: novelty, theoretical rigor, and practical efficiency support acceptance.  
- Against acceptance: unrealistic context-distribution assumption, boundary-case regret, and insufficient comparisons argue for revision.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, with encouragement to address limitations and add more comparisons.
