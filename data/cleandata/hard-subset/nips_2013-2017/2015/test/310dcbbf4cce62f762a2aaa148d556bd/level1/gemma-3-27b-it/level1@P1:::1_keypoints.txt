- Addresses constrained contextual bandits with budget and time limits, a relevant and challenging extension of contextual bandits.  
- Main contribution: first algorithms (ALP and UCB-ALP) achieving logarithmic regret in this setting (except boundary cases).  
- ALP: adaptive linear-programming relaxation that approximates the optimal policy by dynamically adjusting budget constraints.  
- UCB-ALP: combines ALP with UCB to handle unknown rewards, yielding logarithmic regret (boundary cases excluded).  
- Builds on prior contextual bandit and constrained-bandit work, improving regret rates over recent dynamic-context extensions.  
- Technical soundness: detailed theoretical analysis with lemmas and theorems supporting regret bounds.  
- Analysis of boundary cases is provided but complex and results in √T regret for those cases.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written; algorithm descriptions and proofs are concise and logically organized.  
- Algorithms are computationally efficient and simpler than alternatives like Mixture Elimination.  
- Generalization: extends to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, showing robustness.  
- Weakness: boundary-case logarithmic guarantee limitation lacks analysis of practical prevalence.  
- Weakness: relies on finite discrete contexts and fixed costs; unclear extension to continuous contexts or dynamic costs.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical evaluation on synthetic or real-world datasets and comparisons to baselines.  
- Weakness: insufficient exploration of the connection between LP duality and algorithm performance.  
- Weakness: dense notation and some redundancy reduce readability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/significant contribution, rigorous analysis, efficient algorithms, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: boundary-case limitation, no empirical validation, simplified assumptions limiting generality.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: analyze/prevalence of boundary cases, discuss extensions to continuous/dynamic settings, add empirical results, and streamline notation.
