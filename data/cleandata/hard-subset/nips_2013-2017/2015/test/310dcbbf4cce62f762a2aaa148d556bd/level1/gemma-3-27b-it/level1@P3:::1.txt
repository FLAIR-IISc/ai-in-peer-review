This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, specifically with both budget and time constraints. The authors make a significant contribution by demonstrating how to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting, a result previously unknown. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and builds upon existing literature in a logical manner. The technical analysis appears rigorous, and the proposed UCB-ALP algorithm seems promising.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles a natural extension of contextual bandits, adding realistic constraints related to budget and time.  Existing work on contextual bandits (Langford & Zhang, Li et al., Zhou) focuses on unconstrained problems. Budget-constrained bandits *without* contexts have been studied (Tran-Thanh et al., Badanidiyuru et al., Jiang & Srikant, Slivkins), but these results do not directly translate to the contextual case.  The authors correctly position their work as filling a gap in the literature.  They draw inspiration from Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Badanidiyuru et al., 2017), but simplify the problem to achieve stronger results – specifically, logarithmic regret instead of the √T regret achieved in the more general RCB setting.  The core idea is to approximate the optimal policy using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP), which is then combined with the UCB algorithm to handle the unknown reward statistics.  The use of LP relaxation for approximating dynamic programming problems is a standard technique (Veatch, Badanidiyuru et al. 2017), but the adaptive nature of the budget constraint in ALP is a novel contribution.  The connection to related work is thoroughly discussed and appropriately cited.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The primary strength is achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. This is a significant theoretical advance.
* **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous, with a clear progression of ideas from the known statistics case to the unknown case. The use of concentration inequalities (Dubhashi & Panconesi) and the careful partitioning of regret sources are hallmarks of good technical work.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the mathematical notation is generally accessible.  The stepwise progression of ideas (unit cost, known rewards, unknown rewards, heterogeneous costs) makes the paper easier to follow.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The constrained contextual bandit problem is relevant to numerous real-world applications (clinical trials, advertising, crowdsourcing).
* **Computational Efficiency:** The algorithms proposed, particularly UCB-ALP, are relatively simple to implement compared to more general approaches.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper addresses generalizations to unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs, demonstrating the robustness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Boundary Cases:** While achieving logarithmic regret is a major result, the existence of boundary cases with √T regret is a limitation.  The paper acknowledges this, but further investigation into the size and characteristics of these boundary cases would be valuable. How frequently do these cases arise in practice?
* **Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper lacks empirical evaluation. While the theoretical analysis is strong, it would be beneficial to see experimental results demonstrating the performance of the UCB-ALP algorithm on synthetic or real-world datasets. This would help validate the theoretical findings and provide insights into the practical performance of the algorithm.
* **Constant Factors:** The logarithmic regret bounds likely hide large constant factors. Addressing these constants (even qualitatively) would improve the paper.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of a known context distribution (initially) is somewhat limiting, though the paper addresses this later.  Exploring algorithms that directly estimate the context distribution *within* the UCB-ALP framework could be a valuable extension.  Also, while simplifying to fixed costs is justified for initial analysis, the extension to variable costs appears somewhat brief (Section 6).




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper solves a significant open problem in the field of contextual bandits.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.
* The proposed algorithm is relatively simple and potentially practical.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
* The work pushes the state-of-the-art in constrained reinforcement learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The existence of boundary cases with suboptimal regret.
* The lack of empirical evaluation.
* The potentially large constant factors hidden in the logarithmic regret bounds.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on contextual bandits. While the boundary cases and lack of empirical evaluation are limitations, the novelty and theoretical soundness of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with a minor revision request to include some preliminary empirical results and a further discussion of the frequency and characteristics of the boundary cases.  Specifically, even simple simulations comparing UCB-ALP to existing baseline algorithms (e.g., a simple UCB variant that ignores the budget constraint) would greatly strengthen the paper.
