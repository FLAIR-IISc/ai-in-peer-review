- Problem: Studies constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a setting under-addressed by prior work.
- Main idea: Proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) to approximate the oracle by adaptively adjusting budget to average remaining budget.
- Known-statistics result: ALP achieves near-optimal performance with O(1) regret (except boundary cases).
- Unknown-statistics result: Combining ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) yields O(log T) regret, with boundary cases incurring O(√T) regret.
- Extensions: Methodology extended to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.
- Key insight: Decouples information acquisition and decision making via adaptive linear relaxation, enabling logarithmic regret bounds.
- Technical strength: Rigorous theoretical analysis and novel concentration techniques for adaptive budget evolution and ranking errors.
- Comparison to prior work: Improves on RCB [17] and [20] by achieving logarithmic (vs. O(√T)) regret and providing computationally efficient algorithms.
- Practical significance: Relevant to real-world applications (e.g., clinical trials, crowdsourcing) where budgets matter.
- Clarity: Well-structured, clearly written, with supplementary material for complex extensions.
- Weakness — boundary cases: For ρ = qj, regret degrades to O(√T) and the paper does not fully resolve these cases.
- Weakness — unit-cost assumption: Main analysis assumes unit costs; heterogeneous costs handled later but initial restriction may limit applicability.
- Weakness — technical truncation: Analysis for unknown context distribution relies on a truncated algorithm, a technical compromise.
- Recommendation: Strong candidate for acceptance due to novelty, rigor, practical relevance, and computational efficiency despite noted limitations.
