- Problem: Studies constrained contextual bandits with budget and time limits, a practically relevant but understudied setting.  
- Core contribution: Proposes UCB-ALP algorithm achieving logarithmic regret, improving over prior √T bounds.  
- Approach: Develops ALP (adaptive LP) from an oracle LP approximation then extends to unknown rewards with UCB-ALP.  
- Relation to prior work: Extends contextual bandits literature and differs from RCB [17] by using a simplified finite-context/fixed-cost setting to obtain efficient algorithms.  
- Novelty: First to demonstrate logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.  
- Technical soundness: Rigorous analysis with careful regret decomposition; key concentration lemmas (Lemmas 2 and 3) appear well-justified.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-structured and clearly motivates the problem and algorithms.  
- Completeness: Addresses known and unknown parameters and heterogeneous costs; discusses boundary cases.  
- Computational efficiency: Algorithm is computationally practical compared to prior work like [17].  
- Weakness — boundary cases: Regret degrades to √T in boundary cases; prevalence and mitigation are not deeply explored.  
- Weakness — empirical evaluation: Lacks simulations or experiments to validate practical performance and compare baselines.  
- Weakness — unknown context distribution: Analysis relies on truncating empirical context estimates; sensitivity and impact of truncation are not fully analyzed.  
- Weakness — assumptions: Known context distribution and finite-context/fixed-cost assumptions limit general applicability; extensions are not detailed.  
- Pro-accept: Significant theoretical advance, rigorous analysis, practical relevance, and computational efficiency support acceptance.  
- Con-accept: Boundary-case regret, absence of empirical results, and restrictive assumptions argue for rejection or revision.  
- Overall assessment: Strong theoretical contribution recommending acceptance, with a request to add empirical results and deeper treatment of boundary cases.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10
