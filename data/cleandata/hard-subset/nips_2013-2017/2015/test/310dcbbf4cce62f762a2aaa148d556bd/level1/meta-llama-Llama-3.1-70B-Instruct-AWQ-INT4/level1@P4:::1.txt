**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle algorithm, achieving near-optimality with known system statistics. When the expected rewards are unknown, the authors combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to propose a UCB-ALP algorithm. The paper shows that UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases, where it achieves sublinear regret.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work on contextual bandits, multi-armed bandits, and reinforcement learning. The authors cite relevant papers on these topics, including [1-8, 17]. The paper also relates to the work on bandits with knapsacks [11-16] and resourceful contextual bandits [17]. However, the authors argue that their work is distinct in that it achieves logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits, whereas previous work only achieves sublinear regret.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their ideas. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the authors provide proofs for their main results in the supplementary material. The paper also includes a clear explanation of the algorithms and their intuition. However, some of the proofs and technical details are relegated to the supplementary material, which may make it difficult for non-experts to follow.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their ideas. The notation is consistent, and the authors define all the variables and symbols used in the paper. The paper includes several figures and tables that help to illustrate the main ideas. However, some of the paragraphs are long and contain multiple ideas, which may make it difficult to follow.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel approach to constrained contextual bandits, combining ALP with UCB to achieve logarithmic regret. The authors argue that their work is distinct from previous work on bandits with knapsacks and resourceful contextual bandits. The paper also provides new insights into the design of computationally efficient algorithms for constrained contextual bandits.

**Significance**

The paper has significant implications for applications that involve personalized service requirements, such as clinical trials, online recommendation, and online hiring in crowdsourcing. The authors argue that their work provides an efficient approach to dealing with the challenges introduced by budget constraints. The paper also contributes to the theoretical understanding of contextual bandits and may inspire further research in this area.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

Pro:

* The paper presents a novel approach to constrained contextual bandits, achieving logarithmic regret.
* The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their ideas.

Con:
