Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies contextual bandits under a hard budget constraint and finite horizon, with i.i.d. finite contexts and bounded rewards. The main technical contribution is an adaptive linear programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle policy that relaxes the hard budget to an average budget, recomputed online using the remaining budget-to-time ratio bτ/τ. For unit-cost systems with known context distribution and known rewards, ALP attains O(1) regret relative to the oracle except on boundary cases (when ρ = B/T equals a cumulative context probability), where it incurs O(√T) regret.

Building on this, the authors propose UCB-ALP: maintain UCB estimates per context-action pair and run ALP using the estimated ordering of context values; this yields RUCB-ALP(T,B) = O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on boundaries. They further discuss unknown context distributions (empirical estimates, with a truncated variant enabling concentration) and heterogeneous costs (generalized LP over all context-action pairs and an epsilon-first variant). The paper argues this is the first to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.

This work is positioned against: (i) Bandits with knapsacks and budgeted MAB (non-contextual) achieving logarithmic/sublinear regret [11–16]; (ii) RCB [17], which attains O(√T) regret with possibly infinite contexts but against a finite policy class and with high computational cost; (iii) primal-dual UCB for concave rewards and convex constraints [20–22], which achieves O(√T) regret, often with static contexts or finite policy classes. The proposed approach is computationally simpler, instance-dependent, and uses the oracle LP structure to decouple learning and resource allocation.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The LP upper bound and the adaptive thresholding structure are well motivated. The key concentration argument maps remaining budget dynamics under ALP to sampling without replacement (hypergeometric), yielding tight control of bτ/τ. The regret partition for UCB-ALP into within-context action mistakes (O(log T)) and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation components is standard but nontrivial here; the event-based analysis around the critical threshold appears carefully done. The non-boundary O(1) gap of ALP to the dynamic oracle is a strong and useful result.
- Assumptions and limitations: The unit-cost, finite-context/action, i.i.d. contexts, and uniqueness/strict ordering assumptions (u∗1 > ··· > u∗J; unique best per context) simplify analysis; ties and small gaps may complicate practical performance. The “boundary cases” leading to O(√T) regret are measure-zero in ρ but could matter in practice since B and T are integers and ρ is commonly rational; the paper acknowledges optimality here is open. For unknown context distributions, theoretical guarantees rely on a truncated empirical variant; full details are in the supplement.
- Rigor and completeness: Many proofs are deferred. A minor concern is the statement that bτ under UCB-ALP has the same concentration as ALP—formally, action-ranking errors perturb consumption probabilities away from b/τ; the paper claims this impact is negligible and quantifies the number of such errors, but a clear coupling argument in the main text would strengthen confidence. There are minor notational/typo issues (e.g., Theorem 1’s “Θ(o)√T” with Θ(o) defined as a constant).

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized. The ALP intuition, threshold structure j̃(ρ), and the role of v(ρ) are clearly presented. The regret decomposition and event definitions (Erank,s) convey the key ideas. However:
- Some critical arguments (unknown π analysis via bounded differences, coupling for UCB-ALP budget concentration) are only sketched.
- A brief complexity discussion would help: ALP reduces to computing a threshold over J contexts per round; the heterogeneous-cost LP is larger (JK variables) but still tractable given structure.
- Fix small typos and make the boundary-case bounds explicit and consistently parameterized.

Originality:
Achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits with i.i.d. contexts, against the true oracle with a hard budget, appears novel. The adaptive LP relaxation and the insight that only the ordering of expected rewards is needed are elegant and exploited effectively. The mapping to sampling-without-replacement for budget dynamics is a neat analytical device.

Significance:
The results advance our understanding of resource-constrained contextual decision-making and provide a computationally efficient, practically plausible algorithm. The O(log T) regret (non-boundary) is a substantial improvement over O(√T) bounds common in related settings, and the approach could influence algorithms for broader constrained contextual problems. The heterogenous-cost generalization and unknown-π discussion suggest extensibility.

Pros:
- Clear, simple algorithmic idea (ALP/UCB-ALP) with strong theoretical guarantees.
- First logarithmic regret in this constrained contextual setting under natural assumptions.
- Computationally efficient and insightful analysis (budget concentration via hypergeometric).
- Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

Cons:
- Dependence on simplifying assumptions (finite, i.i.d. contexts; known costs; strict orderings).
- Boundary cases incur O(√T) regret; practical frequency of such cases not empirically assessed.
- Unknown-π guarantees hinge on truncated empirical estimates; full algorithmic details/guarantees deferred.
- Lack of empirical evaluation in the main text to illustrate constants, sensitivity to gaps, and comparison to [17,20–22].

Suggestions/questions:
- Provide a concise complexity analysis per round and discuss practical implementation for heterogeneous costs.
- Clarify and tighten the argument that budget consumption under UCB-ALP concentrates like ALP; include a short lemma in the main text or a clearer pointer.
- Discuss tie-handling and near-ties; how sensitive are results to small gaps?
- Include experiments comparing UCB-ALP to Mixture Elimination [17] and primal-dual BwK-style baselines on finite-context benchmarks, especially near boundary ρ.
- Can Thompson sampling or KL-UCB improve constants? Any lower bounds for boundary cases?

Recommendation: Accept (borderline strong). The paper offers a clean, insightful approach with strong theoretical guarantees and fills an important gap in constrained contextual bandits. Strengthening clarity and adding empirical support would further enhance impact.
