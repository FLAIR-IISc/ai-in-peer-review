**Summary**

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, where the agent is given a budget B and a time-horizon T, and needs to maximize the expected total reward subject to the budget and time constraints. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that approximates the oracle algorithm, which achieves near-optimality and only requires the ordering of expected rewards. They then combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to propose a UCB-ALP algorithm for constrained contextual bandits with unknown expected rewards. The authors show that UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases, where it achieves sublinear regret.

**Related Work**

The paper is related to previous work on contextual bandits, multi-armed bandits, and reinforcement learning. The authors cite several papers on these topics, including [1-17]. However, the paper's focus on constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints is novel and distinct from previous work.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel algorithm**: The paper proposes a new algorithm, UCB-ALP, which achieves logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. This is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Theoretical analysis**: The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the algorithm's regret, including a proof of the logarithmic regret bound.
3. **Practicality**: The algorithm is computationally efficient and can be easily implemented in practice.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Assumptions**: The paper assumes that the context distribution is known, which may not always be the case in practice.
2. **Boundary cases**: The algorithm's regret is sublinear in certain boundary cases, which may be a limitation in practice.
3. **Comparison to existing work**: While the paper cites several related papers, it does not provide a detailed comparison to existing algorithms for constrained contextual bandits.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their algorithm and theoretical analysis. The proof of the logarithmic regret bound is rigorous and convincing.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear introduction to the problem and their algorithm, and the technical sections are well-organized and easy to understand.

**Originality**

The paper proposes a novel algorithm for constrained contextual bandits, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors' use of adaptive linear programming and the upper-confidence-bound method is also original and innovative.

**Significance**

The paper's results are significant because they provide a new algorithm for constrained contextual bandits that achieves logarithmic regret. This has important implications for applications such as online advertising and clinical trials, where budget and time constraints are common.
