Review after rebuttal:
- I agree it is not exactly the same setting, but it still seems sufficiently close: if the horizon T is large enough, the coupling through the context effectively vanishes. Given that, I do not see why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as a separate instance of a bandit problem.
- This paper would be much stronger with a clean problem-dependent upper bound expressed in terms of arm gaps (the supplementary bound needs to be reworked), and ideally with matching lower bounds in both the problem-dependent and problem-independent regimes.

The authors study a budgeted, time-constrained contextual bandit. Their main result is that when the numbers of contexts and actions are finite and T is sufficiently large (relative to the instance), a regret of order log(T) is achievable—a new result for contextual bandits. The results seem thorough and the writing is precise. The findings are interesting but not very surprising: with finite numbers of contexts J and arms K, the problem can be cast as a time-constrained, budgeted bandit with JK arms, further action-constrained because at each round only the K arms associated with the current context are available. Since the total number of arms is finite, for large enough T the regret becomes log(T), as in action-constrained bandits. I am therefore unsure how innovative the contribution is; clarification would help.

Questions and comments:
- In Theorem 2, case (2) states a regret of O(√T + ...). Shouldn’t it be at least O(√(KT) + ...)?
- Still in Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ log T) or O(√T + KJ log T), depending on the arm configuration. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are more refined and involve sums of inverse gaps. Can a similar refinement be obtained here? I understand you obtain KJ (rather than a sum over gap terms) because the proof proceeds via events ensuring the correct UCB orderings rather than via concentration on the gaps. But is that approach optimal?
- Lower bounds would be valuable—both problem-dependent and problem-independent. The trivial lower bounds from classical bandits apply when the cost is zero and the context is constant. Can these be sharpened to reflect the complexity introduced by the contexts?

I remain unconvinced about the originality of the setting and therefore stay neutral on this paper. I still have the impression that the setting is very close to a budgeted bandit with action constraints, so I am not sure how innovative it is; clarification would be welcome.
