This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon T and a budget B. The authors focus on the setting with a finite number of contexts and actions. The core contribution is an algorithm, UCB-ALP, which is shown to achieve O(log T) regret, a significant improvement over prior work on related, more general problems that achieve O(sqrt(T)) regret. The approach is to first develop an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that serves as a near-optimal approximation to the oracle (the optimal policy with known statistics). This ALP policy has the desirable property that it only requires the relative ordering of expected rewards. This allows it to be combined with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) estimation method for the general case where expected rewards are unknown, leading to the main UCB-ALP algorithm.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem. Budget constraints are a common feature in many real-world applications of contextual bandits (e.g., online advertising, clinical trials), and this work provides a computationally efficient algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees.
*   **Technical Quality:** The development of the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation is a key technical insight. It provides an elegant way to handle the complex temporal dependencies introduced by the budget constraint and effectively decouples the complex planning problem from the reward estimation problem.
*   **Originality:** Achieving O(log T) regret is a novel and important result for this problem setting. It improves upon the O(sqrt(T)) regret bounds for more general resourceful contextual bandit frameworks and demonstrates what is possible under the finite-context assumption.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and structured. Building the argument from the known-statistics case with ALP to the unknown-statistics case with UCB-ALP provides valuable intuition and makes the overall approach easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Novelty of the Setting:** The main concern is regarding the conceptual novelty of the problem formulation. With a finite number of J contexts and K actions, the problem can be viewed as a standard multi-armed bandit problem with JK arms, where at each time step, a random subset of K arms (corresponding to the observed context) becomes available. This is closely related to the "bandits with sleeping arms" or action constraints setting. While the shared budget couples decisions across contexts, achieving logarithmic regret for a finite-armed bandit problem is not, in itself, surprising. The authors could strengthen the paper by more clearly articulating how the coupling induced by the budget across contexts presents challenges that are fundamentally different from prior work on budgeted bandits with action constraints.
*   **Refinement of Regret Bounds:** The regret bounds in Theorem 2 are expressed in terms of the product JK. For many bandit problems, it is possible to derive more refined, instance-dependent bounds that depend on the sum of inverse gaps between arm rewards. The current bound seems to be a result of the proof technique, which relies on identifying the correct ordering of the UCB estimates. Is it possible to obtain a tighter bound that more directly reflects the difficulty of the specific problem instance (i.e., the reward gaps)?
*   **Lack of Lower Bounds:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of regret lower bounds. While standard bandit lower bounds apply, it is not clear if they are tight in this constrained setting. Deriving lower bounds that explicitly account for the interaction between the budget, contexts, and time horizon would help to establish the optimality of the proposed algorithm and better frame its contribution.
*   **Clarity of the `sqrt(T)` Term:** For the boundary case in Theorem 2, the regret is stated as `O(sqrt(T) + JK log T)`. It would be helpful to clarify if the leading `O(sqrt(T))` term also has a dependency on J or K.
