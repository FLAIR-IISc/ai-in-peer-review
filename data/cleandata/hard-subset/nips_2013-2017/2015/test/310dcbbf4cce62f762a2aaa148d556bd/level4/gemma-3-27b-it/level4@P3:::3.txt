This paper tackles the challenging problem of contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a setting prevalent in many real-world applications. The authors present algorithms achieving logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over existing work, particularly [16], which provides a √T bound. The core idea of approximating the oracle using linear programming (ALP) and combining it with UCB is well-motivated and leads to a surprisingly clean and effective algorithm. The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically organized, making it easy to follow the complex arguments.

The paper begins by formally defining the constrained contextual bandit problem and highlighting its relevance. It then focuses on a simplified case with known context distributions and unit costs, developing the ALP algorithm and demonstrating its near-optimality. Building upon this foundation, the authors extend their approach to the more general scenarios of unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, providing corresponding algorithms and regret bounds. A key contribution is the identification of “boundary cases” where the regret degrades to √T, offering a nuanced understanding of the algorithm’s performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** This work appears to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, a substantial advance in the field.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithms are well-defined and the regret analyses appear rigorous, supported by carefully presented lemmas and theorems. The connection to linear programming provides a strong theoretical foundation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand, even for readers not intimately familiar with the field. The organization is logical and the explanations are clear.
*   **Significance:** Constrained contextual bandits are increasingly important in practice. The development of efficient algorithms with logarithmic regret has the potential to be highly impactful in applications like resource allocation, clinical trials, and online advertising.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The authors don't shy away from addressing the limitations of their approach, explicitly identifying and analyzing the “boundary cases” where the performance degrades. The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs further demonstrate the robustness of their framework.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Boundary Cases:** While the identification of boundary cases is commendable, the √T regret in these situations is a limitation. Further investigation into mitigating these cases or characterizing them more precisely would be valuable.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of known context distributions in some parts of the analysis simplifies the problem. While the extension to unknown distributions is presented, the analysis becomes more complex and less detailed. A more thorough analysis for the unknown distribution case would strengthen the results.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the algorithms are presented as computationally efficient, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost, especially concerning the LP solving, would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and practically relevant problem.
*   The proposed algorithms achieve state-of-the-art performance (logarithmic regret) in a challenging setting.
*   The technical analysis is rigorous and well-supported.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, making it accessible to a broad audience.
*   The authors acknowledge and address the limitations of their approach, demonstrating intellectual honesty.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The √T regret in boundary cases is a potential drawback.
*   The analysis of the unknown context distribution case could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of contextual bandits. The authors present a novel and effective approach to solving a challenging problem, supported by rigorous analysis and clear writing. While the boundary cases and the assumption of known context distributions represent minor limitations, they do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. I **strongly recommend acceptance** to NIPS 2017.
