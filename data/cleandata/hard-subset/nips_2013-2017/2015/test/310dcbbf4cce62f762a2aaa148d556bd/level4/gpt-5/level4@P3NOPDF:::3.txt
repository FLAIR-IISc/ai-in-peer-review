Summary
The paper studies a contextual bandit problem with a hard budget constraint B and a known time horizon T. At each round, the agent observes a context j drawn i.i.d. from a fixed distribution π over {1,…,J} and chooses an action k ∈ {0} ∪ {1,…,K}, where k = 0 denotes skipping. If k = 0, no reward is obtained and no cost is incurred. If k > 0, the agent receives a stochastic reward y ∈ [0,1] drawn from a distribution that depends on (j, k), and pays a known cost c_{j,k} > 0. The objective is to maximize cumulative reward subject to the cumulative cost never exceeding B.

The main technical focus is the special case with known π and unit costs c_{j,k} = 1, for which the paper proposes a simple algorithm with O(log T) regret under a mild condition. The approach is then extended to the general case with unknown π and arbitrary costs. Compared to prior work (e.g., [16]), which achieves O(√T) regret in a more general model, this appears to be the first result achieving O(log T) regret in the budgeted contextual bandit setting, albeit under the stated condition. The algorithm builds on an approximation routine for the oracle planning problem when the statistics are known.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and strong motivation: budget constraints are central in many practical bandit applications.
- A simple algorithm with a compelling O(log T) regret guarantee in the unit-cost/known-π case under a mild condition.
- Extension to unknown π and non-unit costs, broadening applicability.
- Potentially the first O(log T) regret in this budgeted contextual setting, making the contribution notable relative to [16].
- The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

Concerns and suggestions
- Scope of the O(log T) guarantee: The main result hinges on a mild condition. The contribution would be significantly strengthened by either (i) proving O(log T) regret without this condition, or (ii) establishing an Ω(√T) lower bound in the “boundary” cases where the condition fails, thereby delineating when logarithmic regret is fundamentally impossible.
- Central reliance on Lemma 1: Initially I was not fully convinced that the optimal action sequence always satisfies condition (2) for all trials and that the average constraint is a genuine relaxation of the hard budget constraint. After the rebuttal, I am convinced of Lemma 1’s correctness. I recommend adding a brief, self-contained proof sketch or intuitive explanation in the main text to guide readers, since many subsequent arguments depend on it.
- Practicality of the oracle/approximation step: Since the learning algorithm relies on an approximation to the optimal action sequence when statistics are known, it would help to more explicitly state the computational complexity and approximation guarantees, and to clarify how these propagate into regret bounds.

Questions for the authors
- Computational hardness of the oracle: Is computing the exact optimal action sequence under the hard budget constraint NP-hard? If so, please state this explicitly and cite relevant results. If not, clarify the precise complexity and whether the proposed approximation is necessary for tractability or only for analysis simplicity.
- Unknown horizon T: Can the algorithm and regret guarantees be adapted when T is unknown (e.g., via a doubling trick or anytime confidence bounds)? Please discuss any changes in constants or additional overhead.
- Ordering of contexts/utilities: In several places it appears convenient to index contexts by decreasing utility u*_1 > u*_2 > … > u*_J. Can this be assumed without loss of generality when π is unknown? If so, please clarify how the algorithm maintains or updates such an ordering during learning without biasing the estimates; if not, indicate where the analysis depends on this indexing and how to avoid it.
- Assumption clarity: Please clearly state the “mild condition” under which O(log T) regret is obtained in the main theorem statement, and discuss how often it holds in practice (e.g., margin or separability interpretations). Providing concrete examples where it fails would help contextualize the boundary behavior.
- Extension to arbitrary costs: In the general case with non-unit costs and unknown π, how do the constants in the regret bound scale with the range or heterogeneity of c_{j,k}? Are there sensitivity issues when costs vary widely?

Presentation suggestions
- Include a succinct intuition or proof sketch for Lemma 1 in the main text, as it underpins several results.
- Summarize the oracle approximation’s guarantee and its impact on regret in one place for readability.
- Provide a brief comparison table or paragraph contrasting assumptions and regret rates with [16], emphasizing the regimes where your improvements apply.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated and clearly written paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the study of budgeted contextual bandits. Achieving O(log T) regret under a natural condition, and extending to unknown context distributions and non-unit costs, represents noteworthy progress over prior work. While some guarantees rely on a mild condition and details around the oracle’s complexity could be sharpened, these do not detract from the core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
