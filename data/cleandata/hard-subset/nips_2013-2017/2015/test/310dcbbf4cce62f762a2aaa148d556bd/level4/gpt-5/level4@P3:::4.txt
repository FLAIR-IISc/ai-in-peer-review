Summary
This paper studies contextual bandits under both time and budget constraints (“constrained contextual bandits”). The key challenge is that the optimal oracle must balance current rewards with future opportunities given the remaining budget and time, which generally leads to an intractable dynamic program. The authors propose a linear-programming (LP) relaxation that replaces the hard budget with an average budget constraint. Building on this, they design an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that, at each round, solves the LP with the average remaining budget bτ/τ and acts accordingly. When expected rewards are known, ALP achieves O(1) regret except at certain boundary cases. For unknown rewards, they combine ALP with UCB to form UCB-ALP, and prove O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. They also discuss extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates) and heterogeneous costs.

Relation to prior work
- Non-contextual bandits with budgets/knapsacks achieve logarithmic or sublinear regret, but do not handle observable contexts directly.  
- Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] obtains O(√T) regret against a finite policy class via Mixture Elimination but at higher computational cost and with a different benchmark.  
- Bandits with concave rewards and convex knapsacks [20–22] achieve O(√T) regret and do not adapt the budget to remaining time as done here.  
This paper targets a simpler, practically motivated setting (finite contexts, fixed costs, single budget) and, to my knowledge, is the first to show logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits by leveraging an adaptive LP relaxation.

Quality
The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The LP upper bound Û(T,B) provides a transparent benchmark. The analysis of ALP via the “sampling without replacement” view and hypergeometric concentration is elegant and yields tight O(1) regret off the boundaries. The UCB-ALP analysis carefully separates regret from action-ranking errors (within-context) and context-ranking/threshold errors (across contexts), and controls both. The extension to unknown context distribution using empirical estimates is plausible; the truncated analysis via bounded differences and coupling is reasonable, though more detail in the main text would help. Heterogeneous costs are treated thoughtfully, with a natural normalized reward metric and a structured LP.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized. The intuition for why ALP needs only the ordering of u∗j is clear and important. A few places could be clearer:
- Precisely define and motivate the “boundary cases” earlier (see main comment below).  
- Algorithm 1: explicitly state behavior when b=0 (stop taking actions) and how ties in UCBs are broken.  
- Unify notation (e.g., consistently denote thresholds j̃(·), cumulative probabilities qj, and make the piecewise-linear structure of v(ρ) explicit in the main text).  
- For unknown context distribution, summarize the truncated EALP result and its assumptions in the main paper.

Originality
The adaptive LP-based control of budget via bτ/τ in contextual bandits is, to my knowledge, novel, and the regret guarantees (O(1) in known case, O(log T) with learning) appear to be the first of this kind for constrained contextual bandits with finite contexts/actions. The analytical techniques (hypergeometric concentration under adaptive control; careful decomposition of context-ranking errors) are also interesting.

Significance
The work advances our understanding of constrained contextual bandits and offers computationally simple, provably efficient algorithms that could be relevant to applications (crowdsourcing, clinical trials, wireless rate selection). The approach may inform broader designs in resource-constrained online learning.

Main question: Why are boundary cases special?
At ρ = qj (a cumulative context probability), the LP value per round v(ρ) is piecewise linear with a “kink” (the threshold j̃(ρ) changes). Under ALP/UCB-ALP, bτ/τ concentrates around ρ with standard deviation Θ(√(1/τ)), but when ρ lies exactly at a breakpoint, even tiny fluctuations of bτ/τ around ρ switch the optimal LP solution between j̃ = j and j̃ = j − 1 (or j + 1). Consequently, the event “wrong side of the threshold” occurs with constant probability per round (not exponentially small), and the cumulative regret scales with the variance of bτ, yielding Θ(√T) terms. In contrast, off the boundary there is a positive margin δ to the nearest breakpoint, and deviations across the threshold are exponentially unlikely, leading to O(1) regret for ALP and leaving only the O(log T) learning term for UCB-ALP. It would help readers if the paper adds this intuition and, if possible, discusses whether a lower bound precludes better than √T regret exactly at the boundary.

Pros
- Clear, principled reduction from a hard DP to an adaptive LP with a tight, analyzable benchmark.  
- First logarithmic regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits in a natural finite setting.  
- Computationally efficient and easy to implement.  
- Useful analytical tools (concentration under adaptive control, ranking-error decomposition).

Cons
- Results hinge on simplifying assumptions (finite contexts/actions, fixed costs, i.i.d. contexts, uniqueness and strict ordering u∗1 > … > u∗J). Sensitivity to ties or near-ties is not fully explored.  
- Boundary cases incur √T regret; the paper does not establish matching lower bounds at the boundary.  
- The unknown context distribution analysis is truncated and deferred; more main-text detail would improve self-containment.  
- The heterogeneous-costs case uses an ε-first approach in the unknown-reward setting; whether a fully adaptive UCB-style method can match the known-case guarantees is not shown.

Suggestions
- Add intuitive explanation and, if possible, lower-bound discussion for boundary cases.  
- Discuss robustness to ties (e.g., u∗j = u∗j+1 or multiple best actions within a context) and near-ties; specify how ALP/UCB-ALP handle these and how regret scales with small gaps.  
- Summarize the truncated EALP analysis in the main text, including assumptions and the effect on constants.  
- Comment on per-round computational complexity and practical implementation details (e.g., sorting û∗j and maintaining p̂j).  
- If space permits, include empirical results illustrating the boundary vs non-boundary regimes and the benefit of adaptively using bτ/τ.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper offers a compelling, simple, and theoretically solid approach to a timely problem, with clear improvements over prior art in both guarantees and practicality.
