Review after rebuttal:
- I agree it is not exactly the same, but it seems close enough: if the horizon is large, the coupling across contexts effectively vanishes; given that, I do not understand why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as a separate instance of a bandit problem.
- This paper would be much stronger with a clean problem-dependent upper bound in terms of arm gaps (the bound in the supplementary material needs to be reworked), and ideally with matching lower bounds in both the problem-dependent and problem-independent regimes.

The authors study a budgeted, time-constrained contextual bandit. Their main result is that when the numbers of contexts (J) and actions (K) are finite, and when the time horizon T is sufficiently large relative to the problem parameters, a regret of order log(T) is achievable. To my knowledge, this is new for contextual bandits. The results appear thorough and the writing is precise.

That said, the results are interesting but not very surprising. With finite J and K, the problem can be viewed as a time-constrained, budgeted bandit with JK arms, subject to an action constraint: at each round only the K arms corresponding to the current context are available. Since the total number of arms is finite, for large T one expects log(T) regret as in action-constrained bandit problems. This makes me unsure about the conceptual novelty. Clarification on how the setting or techniques differ fundamentally from such a reduction would help.

Questions and comments:
- Theorem 2, case (2): the stated regret is O(sqrt(T) + …). Is it really O(sqrt(T) + …)? Shouldn’t it be at least O(sqrt(K T) + …)?
- Still in Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ log T) or O(sqrt(T) + KJ log T), depending on the arm configuration. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are expressed more finely using sums of inverse gaps. Is it possible to derive such gap-dependent bounds here? I understand why KJ appears—your proof tracks events ensuring the correct ordering of UCB indices rather than exploiting concentration in terms of gaps—but is this approach optimal, or could a gap-based analysis yield sharper bounds?
- It would be valuable to have lower bounds for this setting, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. Trivial lower bounds follow from the classical bandit (zero cost, fixed context). Can these be refined to reflect the complexity introduced by multiple contexts?
- More broadly, I still feel this setting is very close to a budgeted bandit with action constraints. If there are inherent obstacles that prevent treating each context occurrence as an independent bandit instance (beyond finite-sample coupling), please make them explicit.

Overall, the paper is carefully written and the guarantees are solid, but I remain unconvinced about the originality of the setting and the extent to which the results go beyond what one would expect from the finite-J, finite-K reduction. I would be more positive with (i) a polished, truly gap-dependent upper bound (fixing the supplementary bound) and (ii) matching lower bounds in both regimes, together with a clearer explanation of why the contextual coupling fundamentally changes the problem compared to a budgeted, action-constrained bandit with JK arms. I therefore remain neutral.
