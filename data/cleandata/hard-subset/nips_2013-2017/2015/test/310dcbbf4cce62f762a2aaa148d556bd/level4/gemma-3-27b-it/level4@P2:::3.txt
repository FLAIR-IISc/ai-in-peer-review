This paper addresses the challenging problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints. The authors develop algorithms achieving logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over existing work, particularly [16], which provides a $\sqrt{T}$ regret bound. The central contribution lies in approximating the optimal policy using adaptive linear programming (ALP), and combining this with an upper confidence bound (UCB) approach to handle unknown reward distributions. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a novel and interesting approach.

The paper begins by framing the constrained contextual bandit problem, highlighting its relevance in scenarios like crowdsourcing and clinical trials. It then focuses on a simplified case with known context distributions and unit costs, developing an ALP algorithm with O(log T) regret under certain conditions.  This work is extended to the more general setting with unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, building upon the insights gained from the simpler model. The core idea is to leverage ALP’s desirable property of only requiring reward ordering, which allows for effective combination with estimation methods like UCB.

The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work, correctly positioning their contribution as a potentially first result achieving O(log T) regret for this problem.  The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm appears to be computationally efficient, addressing an open problem in the field. The writing is exceptionally clear, making the complex concepts accessible.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The achievement of logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits is a significant advance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The approach of using ALP to approximate the oracle and combining it with UCB appears theoretically justified.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is important and has applications in various real-world scenarios.
*   **Well-Motivated:** The introduction clearly establishes the problem’s importance and the gap in existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Boundary Cases:** The logarithmic regret guarantee does not hold in all cases, with a $\sqrt{T}$ regret bound applying to boundary cases. While acknowledged, establishing a more comprehensive understanding of these cases, or even proving a lower bound demonstrating their necessity, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Lemma 1:** The initial concern regarding the correctness of Lemma 1 was valid, but clarified by the rebuttal. This highlights the need for rigorous justification of key theoretical components.
*   **Generalizability:** While the paper extends to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, the analysis appears more complex and less detailed in these sections. A more thorough analysis, or at least a clear indication of the remaining challenges, would be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of a known context distribution, even in initial stages of analysis, could be relaxed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The achievement of logarithmic regret is a significant contribution to the field.
*   The writing is excellent and makes the complex concepts accessible.
*   The paper is well-motivated and clearly positions its contribution within the existing literature.
*   The potential practical impact of the proposed algorithms is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The logarithmic regret guarantee is not universally applicable, with a $\sqrt{T}$ bound in boundary cases.
*   The analysis for the general case (unknown context, heterogeneous costs) is less detailed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The clear writing, novel approach, and theoretical soundness outweigh the minor weaknesses. The boundary cases and the less detailed analysis of the general settings could be addressed in future work. Despite these limitations, the paper represents a substantial advance and I **strongly recommend acceptance**.
