- Introduces adaptive linear programming (ALP) combined with UCB to tackle constrained contextual bandits.
- Proposes approximating an oracle that strategically skips suboptimal rounds to conserve budget as a strong baseline.
- The UCB-ALP algorithm achieves logarithmic regret in most cases, claimed as the first such result for constrained contextual bandits.
- Handles both known and unknown context distributions and extends to heterogeneous action costs.
- Technical analysis is rigorous with novel techniques for adaptive algorithms and random contexts; notation and structure are generally clear.
- The core idea—approximate oracle via ALP then use UCB for unknown rewards—is compelling and well-motivated.
- Reviewer questions the special treatment of boundary cases (ρ = q_j), asking for a clearer intuitive justification for different regret behavior.
- Boundary-case analysis for j > 1 is not immediately convincing and needs more explanation.
- Analysis is dense and may be hard to follow for readers unfamiliar with the specific technical tools.
- Strength: novel approach combining ALP and UCB for a challenging constrained problem.
- Strength: meaningful baseline comparison against a skipping oracle.
- Strength: strong theoretical guarantee (logarithmic regret) and generality of extensions.
- Weakness: insufficiently motivated/justified boundary-case treatment.
- Weakness: complexity and density of technical analysis may limit accessibility.
- Recommendation: accept with a minor revision to provide a more intuitive explanation for boundary cases.
