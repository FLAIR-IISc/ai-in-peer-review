

Review after rebuttal: I acknowledge the distinction, but the setting appears sufficiently close to warrant reconsideration. Specifically, when the time horizon T is large relative to the problem, the context coupling vanishes—this seems inconsistent with the authors' claim that each context instance cannot be treated as a separate bandit problem. The core contribution appears to be the derivation of a log(T) regret bound under budgeted, time-constrained contextual bandits with finite contexts and arms. However, this result follows directly from viewing the problem as a bandit with JK arms (J contexts × K arms), where action constraints limit accessible arms to K per context. Since the arm count is finite, log(T) regret is standard for such action-constrained bandits, and I remain unconvinced of the novelty. The authors should clarify this in their revision.  

Key concerns:  
1. **Theorem 2 (Case 2)**: The regret bound is stated as O(√T + ...). This should be O(√(KT) + ...) to account for the K arms per context. The current form appears incorrect.  
2. **Problem-dependent bounds**: The authors present O(KJ log T) or O(√T + KJ log T) bounds. For contextual bandits, problem-dependent bounds typically refine this using the sum of inverse gaps (e.g., Σ 1/Δ_i). While the proof relies on UCB-order events rather than gap concentration, this approach yields a suboptimal dependence on KJ. A refined bound incorporating gaps would be preferable and more aligned with standard contextual bandit literature.  
3. **Lower bounds**: The paper lacks matching lower bounds. Trivial lower bounds (e.g., standard bandit bounds when cost=0 and context is static) are insufficient. A refined lower bound accounting for context complexity would strengthen the contribution. The supplementary material’s bound requires revision to address these gaps.  

The results are technically sound and well-written, but the novelty is questionable. The setting is nearly identical to a budgeted bandit with action constraints (i.e., a bandit over JK arms under per-context accessibility limits). Without a clear distinction from existing action-constrained bandit frameworks, the paper’s contribution appears marginal. I urge the authors to explicitly contrast their approach with the standard reduction to a JK-armed bandit and justify why their analysis offers new insights beyond known results. Without this, the work feels like a routine application of established bandit theory to a constrained setting.  

I remain neutral pending the authors’ clarification on novelty and their resolution of the technical points above. The paper would be significantly improved by: (i) a clean problem-dependent bound using arm gaps, (ii) matching lower bounds, and (iii) a precise justification of the novelty relative to the JK-armed reduction.
