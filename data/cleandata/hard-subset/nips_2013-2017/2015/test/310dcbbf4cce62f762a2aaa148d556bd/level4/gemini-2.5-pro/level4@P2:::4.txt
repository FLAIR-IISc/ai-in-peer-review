This paper addresses the problem of contextual bandits under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), a setting the authors term "constrained contextual bandits." The key challenge is that these constraints introduce a temporal dependency, making the optimal policy a complex dynamic program (DP). The authors' main contribution is a novel and computationally efficient approach that, for the first time, achieves logarithmic regret in this setting.

The core idea is to first approximate the intractable DP-based oracle with a tractable policy. For the case with known statistics, the authors propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. At each step, ALP solves a relaxed linear program where the hard budget constraint is replaced by an average budget constraint based on the *remaining* budget and time. This adaptive nature is crucial. The authors provide a strong theoretical result showing that this ALP policy achieves constant O(1) regret compared to the true oracle, except in specific "boundary cases" where the regret is O(√T).

Building on this insight, the paper presents the UCB-ALP algorithm for the more realistic setting where expected rewards are unknown. This algorithm combines the UCB principle for exploration (estimating rewards) with the ALP policy for exploitation (making decisions). The desirable property of ALP is that it primarily relies on the *ordering* of rewards, which UCB can learn efficiently. The main result of the paper is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) regret in boundary cases. This logarithmic regret bound is a significant improvement over the O(√T) regret achieved by prior work in related settings (e.g., [17]). The authors also discuss extensions to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper provides the first logarithmic regret bound for the constrained contextual bandit problem. This is a substantial theoretical advance over existing O(√T) results. The approach of decoupling the problem by using an adaptive LP to approximate the DP oracle is novel, insightful, and could be influential for related constrained sequential decision-making problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears solid. The paper successfully analyzes the complex interplay between the UCB estimation error, the ALP decision-making process, and the fluctuation of the remaining budget. The proof sketch, which maps the evolution of the remaining budget to a known stochastic process ("sampling without replacement") and analyzes the concentration properties, is elegant and convincing.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and generally well-written. It builds the argument logically, starting from the simplest case (known statistics, unit costs) and progressively adding complexity. This makes the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Intuition on Boundary Cases:** The analysis heavily relies on the distinction between "non-boundary" cases (where the budget-to-time ratio ρ ≠ qⱼ for any j) and "boundary" cases (ρ = qⱼ). While the mathematical necessity for this distinction is apparent in the proofs (it determines whether the threshold j̃(ρ) is sensitive to small fluctuations in the budget ratio), the paper could provide more intuition in the main text. Why are these cumulative probability points fundamentally more difficult? A brief explanation of why the average remaining budget bτ/τ is more likely to fluctuate across a decision threshold in these specific cases would strengthen the paper's clarity and impact. Is this a fundamental property of the problem, or an artifact of the ALP approximation and its analysis?

2.  **Framing of Assumptions:** The main logarithmic results are derived under simplifying assumptions, primarily known context distributions and unit costs. The authors are transparent about this and address these limitations in Sections 5 and 6. However, the presentation could more strongly emphasize that these later sections, which are crucial for practical relevance, present a much harder analytical challenge. Highlighting the difficulties introduced by estimating πⱼ or handling heterogeneous costs could better frame the full scope of the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper tackles an important and practical variant of the contextual bandit problem.
*   **Pro:** It introduces a novel and effective technique (ALP) for handling the complex coupling introduced by budget constraints.
*   **Pro:** The primary result—achieving O(log T) regret—is a significant theoretical breakthrough for this problem class.
*   **Pro:** The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is computationally efficient, enhancing its potential significance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The intuition behind the critical "boundary case" phenomenon could be better explained.
*   **Con:** The strongest results are established under simplifying assumptions, although the paper does make progress on relaxing them.

In summary, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the literature on bandit problems. The novelty of the approach and the strength of the theoretical results are impressive. The weaknesses are minor and related to presentation clarity, which can be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
