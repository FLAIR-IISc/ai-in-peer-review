Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with a hard budget B and horizon T, focusing on the proportional regime B/T = ρ. The core idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle via a linear program that relaxes the hard budget to an average one. This yields (i) a static LP upper bound Û(T,B) = T v(ρ) for benchmarking; and (ii) an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy that solves the LP with the instantaneous average remaining budget bτ/τ. With known statistics and unit costs, ALP achieves O(1) regret for non-boundary ρ (i.e., ρ not equal to cumulative context probabilities) and O(sqrt(T)) regret at boundary cases. When expected rewards are unknown, ALP is combined with UCB to form UCB-ALP, which achieves O(JK log T) regret off the boundary and O(sqrt(T) + JK log T) on the boundary. The paper sketches extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates) and to heterogeneous costs (via an LP over all context-action pairs and an epsilon-first variant for learning).

Relation to prior work
Prior resource/budgeted bandit formulations without context (e.g., knapsack/budgeted MAB) achieve logarithmic or sublinear regret. Resourceful contextual bandits (RCB) and work on bandits with concave rewards and convex knapsacks achieve O(sqrt(T)) regret in more general settings but with higher complexity or restricted policy classes. This paper’s contribution is an efficient, instance-dependent log(T) regret under finite discrete contexts and actions by leveraging an adaptive LP relaxation. The positioning relative to recent dynamic-context knapsack-style results is discussed, emphasizing finite context/action and the adaptive budget constraint.

Strengths
- Clear, principled approximation of the oracle via LP, yielding a clean benchmark and a simple adaptive policy (ALP).
- Strong instance-dependent results: O(1) regret for ALP off the boundary, and O(JK log T) for UCB-ALP, which is notable under constraints.
- Technically interesting analysis: mapping budget evolution under ALP/UCB-ALP to sampling without replacement (hypergeometric concentration) to control bτ/τ fluctuations.
- Computational efficiency: the ALP structure depends only on the ordering of context-wise best rewards, which UCB can learn quickly; per-round computation is lightweight.
- Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, with reasonable algorithmic adaptations.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality
- The boundary-case term in Theorem 2 is O(sqrt(T)) + JK log T. Is the sqrt(T) dependence independent of K because it arises solely from budget fluctuations (as in ALP), or should it scale like sqrt(KT) once learning is included? Please clarify the precise source of the sqrt(T) term and whether tighter dependence on K is possible.
- The regret bounds are primarily order-wise. A problem-dependent analysis (gap-based) would be highly valuable. Given that the key is consistent ordering of u* across contexts, can you provide gap-dependent bounds (e.g., sums over 1/Δ terms) both for action ranking within contexts and for context-ranking error events?
- A lower bound would strengthen the contribution. Trivial reductions to standard bandits yield known lower bounds, but can you derive lower bounds that capture the added complexity of context arrivals and budgeting (e.g., dependence on J, π, and margins between u* across contexts)?

Clarity
- The “measure zero boundary” statement could be made more precise (e.g., a margin assumption on ρ relative to qj). Present the boundary conditions explicitly in the main text, not only the supplement.
- The supplementary derivations (e.g., concentration and regret decompositions) are central. Please tighten and state the most important lemmas and constants in the main paper, and ensure the supplemental proofs are self-contained and streamlined.
- Explicitly state per-round computational complexity of ALP and UCB-ALP (e.g., sorting û*j and thresholding), to support the claim of computational efficiency.

Originality
- While the adaptive LP idea is well-motivated and the analysis is careful, the overall setting (finite J,K, random contexts, single budget) can be viewed as a knapsack bandit with action constraints. The paper would benefit from a sharper discussion of why prior reductions or known techniques for action-constrained budgeted bandits do not directly yield logarithmic regret here, and what is fundamentally new in the adaptive-LP analysis (beyond the concentration of bτ/τ). A nuanced comparison to RCB and dynamic-context knapsack bandits would clarify novelty claims.

Significance
- The results are practically appealing: simple algorithms with strong guarantees and low runtime in a commonly used finite-context setting.
- The adaptive relaxation and budget-concentration analysis may inform algorithms for more general resourceful contextual bandits. That said, the broader significance would be clearer with tighter gap-dependent bounds and matching lower bounds.

Suggestions
- Provide gap-dependent upper bounds for both within-context action ranking and cross-context ordering; consider margin assumptions to eliminate boundary behavior and obtain clean instance-dependent rates.
- Add problem-independent and problem-dependent lower bounds that account for context complexity (e.g., dependence on J, π, and margins between context-wise u*).
- Clarify the origin of the sqrt(T) term in Theorem 2 and whether it can be reduced under additional margin/anti-boundary assumptions.
- Strengthen the empirical section (if any): compare to baselines from bandits with knapsacks under dynamic contexts, and quantify constants and practical overhead.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, efficient algorithmic approach; new log(T) guarantee in a constrained contextual setting with finite J,K.
- Solid technical ideas (adaptive LP, concentration of remaining budget).
- Extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to action-constrained knapsack bandits; needs a sharper comparison and possibly lower bounds.
- Lack of gap-dependent bounds and tight constants.
- Boundary-case behavior and its practical impact are not fully clarified.

Post-rebuttal note
I appreciate the clarifications, but I still suspect that as T grows large the coupling across contexts diminishes, making the setting close to a budgeted bandit with action constraints. The paper would be significantly strengthened by (i) clean gap-dependent upper bounds (the supplementary bound needs tightening) and (ii) matching lower bounds in both problem-dependent and problem-independent regimes. I remain neutral pending these improvements.
