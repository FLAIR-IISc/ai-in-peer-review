**Review After Rebuttal:**

My concerns about the setting's similarity to an action-constrained bandit problem with JK arms persist after reading the rebuttal. While I acknowledge the settings are not identical, the distinction appears minor. For a sufficiently large time horizon, the coupling induced by the context is expected to diminish, making it unclear why each context-action pair cannot be treated as a separate instance within a larger bandit problem.

---

This paper considers the problem of a contextual bandit with both budget and time constraints. The authors' main finding is that for a finite number of contexts (J) and actions (K), and for a sufficiently large time horizon T, a logarithmic regret of O(log(T)) is achievable. This is a novel result in the contextual bandit setting.

The technical analysis appears thorough, and the paper is precisely written. However, while the results are interesting, they are not entirely surprising. Given that J and K are finite, this problem can be viewed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms. In this formulation, the context at each time step simply restricts the set of available arms, which is a form of action constraint. For action-constrained bandits with a finite number of arms, a logarithmic regret is the expected outcome when T is large. This raises questions about the fundamental novelty of the proposed setting, which the authors could clarify further.

I have the following questions and suggestions for improvement:

1.  In Theorem 2, the regret for case 2 is stated as `O(sqrt(T) + ...)`. Should this bound not depend on the number of arms, for instance, `O(sqrt(KT) + ...)`?

2.  Still regarding Theorem 2, the regret is given as either `O(KJ log(T))` or `O(sqrt(T) + KJ log(T))`. Problem-dependent bounds are typically expressed more granularly in terms of the sum of inverse gaps between arm rewards. Would it be possible to derive such a gap-dependent bound here? I understand the current proof is based on events characterizing the ordering of the UCB indices rather than on concentration bounds of the gaps themselves, but it is not clear if this approach is optimal.

3.  The paper would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of lower bounds, ideally for both the problem-dependent and problem-independent cases. While trivial lower bounds from the classic multi-armed bandit problem exist (i.e., a single context with zero costs), a more refined analysis demonstrating a lower bound that incorporates the complexity of the context structure would be more compelling.

In its current form, I am not convinced of the originality of the problem setting. A clean, problem-dependent upper bound in terms of arm gaps (the one in the supplementary material appears to need revision) and ideally, matching lower bounds would make this a much stronger contribution. Therefore, I will maintain a neutral stance on this paper.
