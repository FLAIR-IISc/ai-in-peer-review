This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves near-optimality and only requires the ordering of expected rewards. They then combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to develop a UCB-ALP algorithm for the general case where the expected rewards are unknown. The authors show that UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases.

The main idea of the paper is to approximate the oracle algorithm using linear programming (LP) that relaxes the hard budget constraint to an average budget constraint. The authors first study approximations of the oracle when the system statistics are known, and then extend the results to the case with unknown statistics by combining ALP with UCB.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed algorithms. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the authors provide a detailed proof of the regret bounds.

One of the key contributions of the paper is the development of the ALP algorithm, which achieves near-optimality and only requires the ordering of expected rewards. This is a significant improvement over existing algorithms, which often require accurate estimates of the expected rewards.

The combination of ALP with UCB is also a key contribution of the paper. The authors show that UCB-ALP achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases, which is a significant improvement over existing algorithms that achieve O( √ T ) regret.

The paper also provides a clear explanation of the boundary cases and why they need to be analyzed differently. The boundary cases occur when the ratio of the budget to the time horizon is equal to the cumulative probability of a certain context. In this case, the ALP algorithm may not be able to distinguish between the optimal and suboptimal actions, which can lead to a higher regret.
