This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves near-optimality and only requires the ordering of expected rewards. They then combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to handle the case where expected rewards are unknown. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases.

The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the problem, including the challenges introduced by budget constraints and the limitations of existing algorithms. The authors also provide a detailed comparison of their algorithm with other state-of-the-art algorithms.

The key insight of the paper is that the close interactions between information acquisition and decision making in constrained contextual bandits can be decoupled by adaptive linear relaxation. This allows the authors to design an efficient algorithm that achieves low regret.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and organization. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and their approach, and the rest of the paper is well-organized and easy to follow.

However, I have one question regarding the analysis of the algorithm. The authors analyze the boundary cases separately, which seems to be a special case where rho equals q_j. However, it is not clear why this case needs to be analyzed differently. In particular, it is not obvious why rho = q_j appears to be a special case, especially for j > 1.

Overall, this is a well-written and well-organized paper that provides a comprehensive analysis of constrained contextual bandits. The authors' approach is novel and efficient, and their results are impressive. I recommend accepting this paper for publication.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of constrained contextual bandits.
* The authors propose a novel and efficient algorithm that achieves low regret.
* The paper is well-written and well-organized.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The analysis of boundary cases is not clear, and it is not obvious why these cases need to be analyzed separately.

Rating: 8/10
