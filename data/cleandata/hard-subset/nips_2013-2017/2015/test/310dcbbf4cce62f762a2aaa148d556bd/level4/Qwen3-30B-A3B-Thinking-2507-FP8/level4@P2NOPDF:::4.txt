

The paper presents an interesting and novel framework for contextual bandits by evaluating against a stronger baseline that employs adaptive linear programming to strategically forgo a fraction of suboptimal arm selections (referred to as "budget" by the authors). However, a critical gap exists in the analysis of boundary cases. Specifically, the authors treat ρ = q_j as a distinct scenario requiring separate handling, yet this condition lacks justification for its special treatment, particularly when j > 1. The condition ρ = q_j appears arbitrary—there is no evident structural or algorithmic reason why this equality should trigger a different analytical approach, as it does not inherently introduce discontinuities or singularities in the problem setup. For instance, if ρ = q_j merely represents a continuous transition point without functional significance, its isolation as a boundary case undermines the paper’s generalizability. Clarifying why this boundary is meaningful (e.g., whether it stems from a technical constraint in the LP formulation or a design choice) would significantly strengthen the argument. Without this, the analysis risks appearing ad hoc, potentially weakening the paper’s core contribution. The baseline’s use of adaptive budgeting is a compelling innovation, but the unresolved boundary case issue demands attention to ensure the theoretical claims hold broadly. Overall, the work offers a promising direction, but addressing this point is essential for full validation.
