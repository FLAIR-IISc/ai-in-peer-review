

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, proving logarithmic regret under certain conditions. The authors demonstrate that when the number of contexts and actions is finite and the time horizon is sufficiently large, their proposed UCB-ALP algorithm achieves O(log T) regret except for boundary cases where it achieves O(√T) regret. The results appear technically sound with rigorous analysis, and the writing is clear and precise.

The main novelty of the paper lies in extending logarithmic regret guarantees to constrained contextual bandits, which differs from previous work on budgeted bandits. However, I question whether the problem setting is truly distinct from standard budgeted bandit problems with action constraints. Since the number of contexts J and arms K are finite, this problem can be viewed as a budgeted bandit with JK arms, where at each time step only K arms are accessible (corresponding to the current context). Given this perspective, the logarithmic regret result is somewhat expected for large T, as it aligns with known results for action-constrained bandit problems.

I have several concerns regarding the theoretical results:
1. In Theorem 2, the regret in case 2) is stated as O(√T + JK log T). Shouldn't this be O(√(KT) + JK log T) at minimum, considering the number of arms?
2. The problem-dependent regret bounds are expressed in terms of JK rather than the sum of inverse arm gaps, which is standard for contextual bandit literature. Could the authors provide a more refined bound using arm gaps?
3. The authors should provide lower bounds for this problem to establish tightness of their upper bounds. While trivial lower bounds exist from classical bandit problems, it would be valuable to see how the context complexity affects the lower bound.

The supplementary material's upper bound analysis requires reworking to be more rigorous. I believe the paper would be significantly strengthened by providing clean problem-dependent bounds with arm gaps, ideally accompanied by matching lower bounds. Without these refinements, the novelty of the approach remains questionable, as the problem appears closely related to existing budgeted bandit work with action constraints.
