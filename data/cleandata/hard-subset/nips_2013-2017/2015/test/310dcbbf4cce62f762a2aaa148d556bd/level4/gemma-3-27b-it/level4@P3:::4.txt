This paper addresses the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits with both time and budget limitations, a setting highly relevant to numerous real-world applications. The authors make a significant contribution by demonstrating how to achieve logarithmic regret in this complex setting, a feat not previously accomplished. The core idea of approximating the oracle with Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) and then combining it with the UCB method is clever and well-motivated. The paper is clearly written and logically organized, building intuition with the simpler unit-cost case before generalizing to more complex scenarios. The related work is thoroughly discussed, accurately positioning the contribution within the existing literature.

**Summary of the Approach:** The paper introduces a framework for constrained contextual bandits where actions consume both reward and budget. It first analyzes the case with known system statistics, proposing ALP, an algorithm that approximates the optimal policy by relaxing the hard budget constraint. The key insight is that ALP only requires knowing the *ordering* of expected rewards. This property is then exploited in the more general case with unknown rewards by combining ALP with UCB, resulting in the UCB-ALP algorithm. The authors provide regret bounds demonstrating logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases) and extend the framework to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits is a significant advance. Previous work, even recent extensions like [21, 22], typically achieve sublinear (√T) regret or rely on restrictive assumptions.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, including rigorous regret bounds and insightful justifications for the proposed algorithms. The use of LP relaxation and concentration inequalities is well-executed.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The considered constraints – budget and time – are common in many practical applications, increasing the impact of this work.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the algorithms and their theoretical underpinnings. The stepwise introduction of complexity (unit cost, known statistics, unknown statistics, heterogeneous costs) is effective.
*   **Insights into Algorithm Design:** The ALP approximation and its analysis provide valuable insights into designing efficient algorithms for constrained contextual bandit problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Boundary Cases:** The analysis of boundary cases (where ρ = qj) results in a √T regret term. The explanation provided – that a constant factor in the log T term can be large – is somewhat unsatisfying. A more intuitive explanation of *why* these cases require different analysis would be beneficial. The reviewer’s original question directly points to this: it isn’t immediately obvious why ρ = qj necessitates a separate treatment. While the authors acknowledge the challenge in the conclusion, further clarification would strengthen the paper.
*   **Complexity of Extension to Heterogeneous Costs:** While the authors outline the extension to heterogeneous costs, the details are relegated to the supplementary material.  A more concise discussion within the main body of the paper, perhaps highlighting the key adjustments required, would improve readability.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks empirical results. While the focus is theoretical, including simulations to validate the performance and illustrate the impact of the boundary cases would strengthen the paper and increase confidence in the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound solution to a challenging problem.
*   The logarithmic regret bound represents a significant improvement over existing approaches.
*   The clear writing and logical organization make the paper accessible and impactful.
*   The work has the potential to influence future research in constrained reinforcement learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The treatment of boundary cases lacks a fully satisfying explanation.
*   The lack of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of the algorithm.
*   The extension to heterogeneous costs is only briefly discussed in the main body.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the practical relevance of the problem outweigh the shortcomings.  I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors provide a more intuitive explanation for the behavior in boundary cases.  Adding some empirical results, even simple simulations, would further strengthen the paper.
