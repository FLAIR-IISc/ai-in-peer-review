### **Summary**

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem with a hard budget constraint `B` over a known time horizon `T`. In each round, an agent observes a context `j` (from a set of `J` contexts) drawn from a fixed distribution `π`. The agent then chooses an action `k` from a set of `K` actions, plus a "skip" action (denoted as 0). Choosing to skip incurs no cost and yields no reward. Taking an action `k > 0` in context `j` incurs a known cost `c_{j,k} > 0` and produces a stochastic reward drawn from a distribution on [0,1] specific to the context-action pair. The agent's objective is to maximize the total reward subject to the constraint that the cumulative cost never exceeds the budget `B`.

The core of the paper introduces an algorithm for a specialized version of this problem where the context distribution `π` is known and all costs `c_{j,k}` are unity. Under a mild technical condition, this algorithm is shown to achieve an `O(log T)` regret bound. The authors then extend this approach to the general case with unknown `π` and arbitrary costs.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is the `O(log T)` regret bound. This is a substantial improvement over prior work, such as [16], which addresses a more general setting but provides an algorithm with a much looser `O(sqrt T)` regret. To my knowledge, this is the first work to achieve a logarithmic regret bound for this problem, marking a significant theoretical advance.

2.  **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and clearly written. The problem is well-defined, and the exposition of the algorithm and its analysis is easy to follow.

3.  **Novel Algorithm Design:** The proposed algorithm, which is based on an approximation of the optimal policy for the offline version of the problem, is both simple and insightful. Its design is elegant and provides a strong foundation for the theoretical results.

### **Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The main condition for achieving the `O(log T)` regret is that the optimal policy does not fall on a "boundary." While the authors describe this as a mild condition, the paper would be more impactful if it explored the consequences when this condition is not met. For instance, can a lower bound of `Ω(sqrt T)` be proven for these boundary cases? A more thorough discussion of the necessity of this condition would provide a more complete understanding of the problem's complexity and better situate the paper's contribution.

### **Clarifications and Questions for the Authors**

A previous concern regarding the correctness of Lemma 1—specifically, the justification for relaxing the hard, per-trial budget constraint (2) to an average constraint—has been satisfactorily addressed by the author's rebuttal. I am now convinced of its validity.

I have a few remaining minor questions for the authors:

*   **Computational Complexity:** What is the computational complexity of finding the optimal policy in the offline setting (i.e., when all problem parameters are known)? Is this problem NP-hard? A brief comment on this would be helpful for context.
*   **Unknown Time Horizon:** Could the proposed algorithm and its analysis be extended to the case where the time horizon `T` is unknown?
*   **Utility Ordering:** The paper assumes an ordering of utilities (`u^1 > u^2 > ... > u^*_J`). While this can be assumed without loss of generality when the context distribution `π` is known, is it still justifiable when `π` is unknown and must be learned?

### **Recommendation**

In summary, this paper presents a novel and important contribution to the literature on budgeted multi-armed bandits. The achievement of an `O(log T)` regret bound is a significant theoretical advance, and the paper is well-structured and clearly written. Despite the minor points raised for clarification, the strengths of this work are substantial.

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
