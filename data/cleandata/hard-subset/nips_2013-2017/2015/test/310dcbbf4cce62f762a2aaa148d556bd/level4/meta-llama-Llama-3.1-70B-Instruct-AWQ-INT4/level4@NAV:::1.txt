Review after Rebuttal:

Upon re-examining the paper, I still believe that the proposed setting bears a strong resemblance to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints. Although the authors argue that the context coupling does not vanish when the horizon is large enough, I remain unconvinced. It seems plausible that each occurrence of a context could be treated as a separate instance of a bandit problem.

To strengthen this paper, I would recommend deriving a clean upper bound in terms of arm gaps for the problem-dependent case, as well as establishing matching lower bounds for both problem-dependent and independent cases. This would significantly enhance the paper's contributions.

The authors investigate the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit, demonstrating that a regret of order log(T) is achievable when the number of contexts and actions is finite, and the time constraint T is sufficiently large. While the results appear thorough, and the writing is precise, the findings, although interesting, do not strike me as particularly surprising.

Given that the number of contexts J and arms K are finite, this bandit problem can be viewed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms, which is also action-constrained. Since the number of arms is finite, the regret is log(T) for large enough T, similar to action-constrained bandit problems. I remain uncertain about the paper's innovativeness, and the authors' rebuttal did not fully alleviate my concerns.

Several questions regarding the paper remain:

- In Theorem 2, the regret bound appears to be O(√(T) + ...), but should it not be O(√(KT) + ...) at the very least?
- The regret bound in Theorem 2 is either O(KJ log(T)) or O(√(T) + KJ log(T)), depending on the arm configuration. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are expressed in terms of the sum of inverse arm gaps. Is it possible to refine the bound in a similar manner?
- It would be valuable to establish lower bounds for this problem, ideally problem-dependent and problem-independent. While trivial lower bounds exist, refining them to account for the complexity of the context would be beneficial.

I still have reservations about the originality of this setting, which makes me neutral about the paper.
