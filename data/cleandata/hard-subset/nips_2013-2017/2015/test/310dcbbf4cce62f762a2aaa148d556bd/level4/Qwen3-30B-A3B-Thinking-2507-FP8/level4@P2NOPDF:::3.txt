

This paper addresses contextual bandits under a budget constraint $B$ and known time horizon $T$. At each step, the agent observes a context $j$ drawn from a fixed distribution $\pi$ over $\{1,\dots,J\}$, then selects action $k \in \{0\} \cup \{1,\dots,K\}$ (where $k=0$ denotes "skip"). For $k>0$, the agent receives a reward $y$ (drawn from a fixed distribution over $[0,1]$ depending on $j,k$) and incurs cost $c_{j,k} > 0$; for $k=0$, both reward and cost are zero. The goal is to maximize cumulative reward subject to cumulative cost never exceeding $B$.

The core contribution focuses on a special case where $\pi$ is known and $c_{j,k}=1$ for all $j,k$. The authors propose an algorithm achieving $O(\log T)$ regret under a mild condition. This is extended to the general setting with unknown $\pi$ and arbitrary costs $c_{j,k}$. The work is significant as prior methods (e.g., [16]) achieve only $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret, making this the first to establish $O(\log T)$ regret for this problem. The algorithm leverages an approximation for the optimal action sequence under full statistical knowledge, and its simplicity is notable.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and technically sound. While the $O(\log T)$ bound currently requires a mild condition, the results would be even more compelling if the bound held universally—though the current condition is reasonable. The significance is strong, especially given the $O(\sqrt{T})$ lower bound in boundary cases.

The theoretical foundation relies heavily on Lemma 1. Initially, I questioned whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies Eq. (2) for all trials, and thus whether the average constraint truly relaxes the hard constraint. However, the rebuttal provided a convincing resolution, and I now fully endorse Lemma 1.

Minor suggestions for strengthening the paper:
1. Is computing the exact optimal action sequence NP-hard? Clarifying this would contextualize the algorithm’s approximation.
2. Can the results be extended to unknown $T$? This is a natural generalization.
3. In the unknown $\pi$ case, can the ordering $u^{(1)} > u^{(2)} > \dots > u^{(J)}$ be assumed without loss of generality? The authors should address this.

The paper makes a clear, valuable contribution to constrained bandit learning. The $O(\log T)$ regret bound under a mild condition represents a meaningful improvement over prior work. The writing is precise, the algorithm is elegant, and the technical arguments are well-structured. The minor concerns raised have been satisfactorily addressed. I strongly recommend acceptance.
