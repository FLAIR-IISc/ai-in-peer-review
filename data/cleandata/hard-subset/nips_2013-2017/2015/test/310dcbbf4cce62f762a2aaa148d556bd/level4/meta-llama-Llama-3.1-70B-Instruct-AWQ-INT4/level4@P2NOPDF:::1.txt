Review after rebuttal: 
I acknowledge the authors' clarification, but I still believe that the proposed setting is similar to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints, especially when the horizon is large enough. In this case, the coupling of the context becomes negligible. I am not convinced that each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as a separate instance of a bandit problem. 
To strengthen this paper, I suggest providing a clean upper bound in terms of arm gaps for the problem-dependent case, along with matching lower bounds for both problem-dependent and independent cases.

The authors investigate the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit. Their primary contribution is proving that, with a finite number of contexts and actions, and a sufficiently large time constraint T, a regret of order log(T) can be achieved, which is a novel result for contextual bandits.

The results appear thorough, and the writing is precise. However, the findings, although interesting, are not particularly surprising. Given the finite number of contexts J and arms K, this bandit problem can be viewed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms, which is also action-constrained since not all JK arms are accessible at each time. Since the number of arms is finite, for large enough T, the regret is log(T), similar to action-constrained bandit problems. I question the innovation of this paper, and I would appreciate further clarification from the authors.

I have several questions regarding this paper: 
1. In Theorem 2, the regret in case 2) is O(√T + ...). However, shouldn't it be O(√(KT) + ...) at least? 
2. Still in Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ log(T)) or O(√(T) + KJ log(T)), depending on the configuration of the arms. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are expressed in a more refined way using the sum of inverse arm gaps. Is it possible to achieve something similar in this context? I understand that the proof relies on events characterizing the correct orders of the UCB, rather than concentration bounds on their gaps. However, I wonder if this approach is optimal.
3. It would be valuable to establish lower bounds for this problem, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. The trivial lower bounds correspond to classical bandit problems with zero cost and a constant context. Can these bounds be refined to account for the complexity of the context? I remain neutral about this paper due to concerns about its originality.

The setting of this paper appears to be very close to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints. I would appreciate further clarification from the authors on the innovation of their proposed setting.
