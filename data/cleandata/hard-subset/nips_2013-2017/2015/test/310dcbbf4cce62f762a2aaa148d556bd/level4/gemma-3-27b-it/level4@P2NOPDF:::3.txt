This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under a budget constraint, with a known time horizon *T* and a budget *B*. In each round, an agent observes a context *j* drawn from a fixed distribution π over *J* possible contexts. The agent then selects an action *k* from the set {0, 1, ..., *K*}, where action 0 represents skipping a trial (incurring no reward or cost). Selecting *k* > 0 yields a reward *y* sampled from a distribution dependent on *j* and *k*, and incurs a known cost *c<sub>j,k</sub>* > 0. The objective is to maximize cumulative reward while ensuring the cumulative cost remains within the budget *B*.

The core of the paper focuses on a simplified scenario where the context distribution π is known and the costs *c<sub>j,k</sub>* are uniform (*c<sub>j,k</sub>* = 1 for all *j* and *k*). For this case, the authors present an algorithm with a regret bound of O(log *T*) under a stated condition. The work then extends this algorithm to the more general setting of unknown π and arbitrary costs *c<sub>j,k</sub>*.

While related work [16] addresses a similar general setting, it achieves a regret of O(√*T*). This paper potentially establishes the first O(log *T*) regret bound for this problem, although its applicability depends on satisfying the specified condition. The algorithm's foundation lies in an approximation technique for computing the optimal action sequence when all statistical parameters are known. This approach results in a simple yet insightful algorithm.

The paper is well-motivated and exceptionally clear in its presentation. Its significance would be further enhanced by demonstrating that the O(log *T*) regret bound holds universally, or by proving a corresponding Ω(√*T*) lower bound for cases where the condition is not met.

Initially, I had concerns regarding the correctness of Lemma 1, specifically whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies the condition expressed in equation (2), and consequently, whether averaging the constraint provided a valid relaxation of the original hard constraint. However, these concerns were addressed and clarified through the authors' rebuttal, which convincingly explains the lemma’s validity. 

Several minor questions remain. Is determining the exact optimal action sequence NP-hard? Can the results be generalized to scenarios where the time horizon *T* is unknown? Finally, in the case of an unknown context distribution, is the assumption *u<sup>1</sup>* > *u<sup>2</sup>* > ... > *u<sup>*</sup><sub>J</sub>* without loss of generality justifiable?

Despite these minor points, I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
