

Review after rebuttal: The authors have clarified the distinction between their setting and standard contextual bandits, but I remain unconvinced of the novelty. The core insight—that finite contexts (J) and actions (K) reduce the problem to an action-constrained bandit with JK arms—aligns with established theory. For sufficiently large T, the regret bound of O(log T) is expected in such settings (e.g., as in standard action-constrained bandits), not a new result. The paper should explicitly address why this reduction does not render the work redundant.  

Regarding technical contributions:  
1. **Theorem 2 (Regret Bound)**: The bound O(√T + ...) in Case 2 appears incorrect. Given K arms per context, the standard regret scaling for action-constrained bandits is O(√(KT)), not O(√T). The authors should verify this derivation, as the current form understates the dependence on K.  
2. **Problem-Dependent Bounds**: The current bounds (O(KJ log T) or O(√T + KJ log T)) lack refinement. Standard bandit literature expresses regret in terms of arm gaps (e.g., ∑ 1/Δ_i), which better captures problem difficulty. While the authors cite a proof strategy based on UCB order events rather than gap concentration, this approach appears suboptimal. A gap-dependent bound would significantly strengthen the paper and address a key gap in the analysis. The supplementary material’s bound requires reworking to achieve this refinement.  
3. **Lower Bounds**: The authors should provide matching lower bounds (problem-dependent and independent) to establish tightness. Trivial lower bounds (e.g., classical bandits with static context) are insufficient. A refined lower bound accounting for context complexity (e.g., J) would clarify the problem’s inherent difficulty and reinforce the upper bounds.  

The paper’s setting—budgeted, time-constrained contextual bandits with finite contexts—is closely related to budgeted bandits with action constraints (where only K arms are accessible per context). This overlap undermines the perceived novelty. The authors must clarify how their work advances beyond existing results for finite-armed bandits under constraints.  

**Overall Assessment**: The results are technically sound, and the writing is precise. However, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty: the problem reduces to a well-studied bandit scenario, and the bounds do not significantly improve upon standard approaches. The requested gap-dependent bounds and lower bounds are essential for a meaningful contribution. Without these, the paper’s impact is limited to a minor extension of known theory. I remain neutral pending substantial revisions addressing these points.
