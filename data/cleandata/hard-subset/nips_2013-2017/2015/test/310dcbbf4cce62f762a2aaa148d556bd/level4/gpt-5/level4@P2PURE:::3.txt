This paper studies a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint B and a known time horizon T. In each round, the agent observes a context j drawn from a fixed distribution π over {1, …, J} and selects an action k from {0} ∪ {1, …, K}, where k = 0 means “skip.” If k = 0, the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost. If k > 0, the agent receives a reward y and incurs a cost c{j,k}, where y is drawn from a fixed distribution on [0,1] that depends on j and k, and c{j,k} > 0 is known to the agent. The goal is to maximize cumulative reward subject to the cumulative cost never exceeding B.

The main contribution focuses on the special case where π is known and c{j,k} = 1 for all j and k. The paper proposes an algorithm that achieves O(log T) regret under a mild condition, and then extends the approach to the general setting where π is unknown and the c{j,k}’s are arbitrary.

Although a more general setting is examined in [16], that work achieves only O(√T) regret. Hence, this paper is likely the first to obtain O(log T) regret, subject to the mild condition. The method builds on an approximation algorithm for computing the optimal action sequence when all statistics are known. The algorithm is simple yet very interesting.

The paper is well motivated and clearly written. It would be even more compelling if O(log T) regret could be guaranteed universally; otherwise, establishing an Ω(√T) lower bound in boundary cases would be valuable.

All results appear to hinge on Lemma 1. I was initially not convinced of its correctness: I do not believe the optimal action sequence necessarily satisfies (2) in every round, and thus I did not see why the average constraint truly relaxes the hard constraint. After reading the rebuttal, I am now convinced by Lemma 1. Thank you.

Minor comments:
- Is it NP-hard to exactly compute the optimal action sequence?
- Can the results be extended to the case where T is unknown?
- Can we assume u^1 > u^2 > ... > u^*_J without loss of generality even when the context distribution is unknown?

I strongly recommend acceptance.
