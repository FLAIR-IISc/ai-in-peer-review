## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission

The authors address the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit. Their main result demonstrates that, under finite context and action spaces and sufficiently large time horizons *T*, a regret of O(log *T*) is achievable – a novel finding for contextual bandit problems. The results appear technically sound and the writing is generally precise.

However, while the result is correct, its innovativeness is questionable. The core observation is that a finite contextual bandit can be recast as a finite-armed, action-constrained bandit with *JK* arms (where *J* is the number of contexts and *K* is the number of actions). Since the time horizon *T* is assumed to be sufficiently large, the expected regret naturally approaches O(log *T*), mirroring known results for standard action-constrained bandit problems. The paper’s contribution, therefore, seems incremental, and the authors should clarify in their rebuttal precisely where the novelty lies.  I find the connection to existing work in action-constrained bandits not sufficiently discussed.

I have several specific questions and suggestions:

1. **Theorem 2 Regret Bound:** In Theorem 2, the regret in case 2 is presented as O(√*T* + …). This seems unusually high. Given the *K* available actions at each context, shouldn’t the regret be at least O(√*KT* + …)?

2. **Problem-Dependent Bounds:** Theorem 2 provides regret bounds of either O(*KJ* log *T*) or O(√*T* + *KJ* log *T*) depending on the problem configuration. Standard problem-dependent bounds for bandit problems are typically expressed using a more refined metric – the sum of inverse arm gaps. While I understand that the authors' proof relies on events characterizing the order of Upper Confidence Bounds (UCB) rather than direct concentration bounds on the gaps, I question whether this approach is optimal. Is it possible to express the regret in terms of arm gaps, even with the *KJ* factor?  A sharper bound in terms of arm gaps would significantly strengthen the paper.

3. **Lower Bounds:** The inclusion of lower bounds, particularly problem-dependent and problem-independent bounds, would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. The trivial lower bound, corresponding to a constant cost and a single context, seems weak. Can the lower bound be refined to account for the complexity introduced by the contextual information? Establishing tighter lower bounds would help delineate the theoretical limits of this problem setting and solidify the paper’s contribution.

**Post-Rebuttal:** I largely agree with the authors’ clarification that the setting is not *exactly* equivalent to a standard action-constrained bandit. However, the similarity remains strong enough that I question why each instance of a context cannot be treated as a separate bandit problem. Specifically, if the horizon is sufficiently large to allow exploration within each context, the coupling between contexts appears to diminish.  I still do not fully understand why this coupling prevents a more straightforward application of existing bandit algorithms.

Overall, while technically sound, this paper lacks a compelling argument for its novelty. A clean upper bound in terms of arm gaps for the problem-dependent case, and ideally matching lower bounds in both problem-dependent and problem-independent cases, would dramatically improve the paper. As it stands, I remain neutral.
