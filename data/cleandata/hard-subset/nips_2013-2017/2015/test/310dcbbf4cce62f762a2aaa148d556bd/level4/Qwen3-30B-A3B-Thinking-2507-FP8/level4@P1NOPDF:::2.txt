

The paper addresses contextual bandits under budget and time constraints, focusing on finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint. Though simplified, these assumptions remain reasonable in practice. Crucially, this setting enables algorithms achieving O(log T) regret through an adaptive linear programming approximation of the oracle.  

The paper is clearly written and well-documented, with thorough citations of relevant work and explicit motivation. It presents a solid theoretical contribution. However, practical considerations are only briefly addressed in Sections 5 and 6, and the work lacks experimental validation. That said, the authors make a significant advance: to the best of my knowledge, they are the first to establish O(log T) regret bounds for constrained contextual bandits. The proofs appear sound (though I did not verify every detail), and the simplifying assumptions are appropriate for achieving this result. This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in constrained bandit problems.  

The primary limitation lies in the minimal discussion of practical implementation. While the theoretical framework is compelling, the absence of empirical results or detailed analysis of computational overhead in real-world settings leaves a gap. Future work could strengthen the impact by demonstrating scalability or performance in applications, but this does not diminish the core theoretical novelty. Overall, the paper delivers a rigorous and well-motivated solution to an important problem, making a clear step toward efficient constrained decision-making under uncertainty.
