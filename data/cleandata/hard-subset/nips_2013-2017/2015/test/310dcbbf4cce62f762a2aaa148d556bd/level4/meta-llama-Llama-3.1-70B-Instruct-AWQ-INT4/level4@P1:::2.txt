The paper considers contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a problem that has garnered significant attention in recent years due to its relevance to various applications such as clinical trials, online recommendation, and online hiring in crowdsourcing. The authors restrict their analysis to finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which, although simplified, is still reasonable in practice and results in algorithms that achieve O(log T) regret.

The key contribution of the paper is the introduction of the adaptive linear programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle, which achieves near-optimal performance while tolerating certain estimation errors of system parameters. The authors then combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to propose a UCB-ALP algorithm for constrained contextual bandits with unknown expected rewards. The UCB-ALP algorithm achieves O(log T) regret except for certain boundary cases, where it achieves O(√T) regret.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their contributions. The relevant work is properly cited, and the motivation for the research is explicit. The proofs are convincing, although a more detailed analysis of the practical aspects of the algorithms would be beneficial.

The significance of the paper lies in its ability to obtain logarithmic regret in the case of constrained contextual bandits, which is a challenging problem. The authors' approach to decoupling the close interactions between information acquisition and decision making by adaptive linear relaxation is innovative and provides a new perspective on solving this problem.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical aspects of the algorithms, including experimental results. The authors briefly discuss the application of their algorithms to bandits with unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs, but a more thorough analysis of these scenarios would be valuable.
