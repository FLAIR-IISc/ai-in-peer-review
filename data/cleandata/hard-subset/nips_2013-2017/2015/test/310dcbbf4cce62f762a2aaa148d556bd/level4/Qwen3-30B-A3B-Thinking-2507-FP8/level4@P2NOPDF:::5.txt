

This paper addresses a budget-constrained contextual bandit (CB) problem with discrete contexts and actions, presenting a computationally efficient algorithm via linear programming (LP) approximation and establishing a logarithmic regret bound of O(log T). The work is technically sound, with a clear exposition that makes the core contributions accessible and easy to follow.  

The primary strengths lie in the algorithmic efficiency and the tight regret analysis, which represent meaningful progress over prior approaches. The LP-based approximation effectively handles the budget constraint while maintaining theoretical guarantees, and the O(log T) regret bound aligns with optimal performance in similar settings. The paper’s organization and writing are exceptional, contributing significantly to its readability.  

For improvement, the authors should consider incorporating parametric reward models in future work. This would be particularly valuable for scaling to large context spaces, where discrete representations become computationally prohibitive. In Section 4.1, the rationale for combining UCB with ALP requires clarification. The UCB-ALP algorithm appears to implement the optimism under uncertainty principle, which may structurally mirror the policy update mechanism in UCRL2. Explicitly stating this connection and justifying the design choice would strengthen the methodological foundation. Additionally, including numerical experiments in an extended version would provide empirical validation of the algorithm’s performance, complementing the theoretical results.  

The authors should also cite related work on constrained bandits, such as arXiv:1506.03374, which explores similar problem structures and could contextualize their contributions more precisely.  

Overall, this paper delivers a compelling solution to a well-motivated problem, offering both theoretical rigor and practical efficiency. The proposed method improves upon existing techniques in computational cost while maintaining near-optimal regret guarantees. Given its clarity, novelty, and significance, the work merits acceptance for publication at NIPS.
