- Paper studies constrained contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B, creating complex temporal dependencies.
- Main challenge: dual constraints make exploration-exploitation and optimal DP policy computationally intractable.
- Key idea: decouple decision-making from learning via an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation that relaxes the budget and adapts to remaining budget-to-time ratio.
- For known statistics, ALP is computationally efficient and near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret relative to an LP upper bound except in boundary cases.
- For unknown rewards, the UCB-ALP algorithm combines UCB exploration with ALP decision-making.
- UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) regret in boundary cases.
- Claim: first work to establish a logarithmic regret bound for this constrained setting, improving on prior O(√T) results.
- Paper also extends analysis to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.
- Strength: significant and original problem formulation with a novel, tractable ALP framework.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis using budget evolution mapped to sampling without replacement and concentration inequalities.
- Strength: clear writing and logical progression from oracle to UCB-ALP.
- Weakness: treatment of "boundary cases" (ρ = q_j) is unclear; need clearer intuition why these yield fundamentally different (O(√T)) regret.
- Suggestion: add a more detailed intuitive explanation of the knife-edge sensitivity when ρ equals a cumulative context probability.
