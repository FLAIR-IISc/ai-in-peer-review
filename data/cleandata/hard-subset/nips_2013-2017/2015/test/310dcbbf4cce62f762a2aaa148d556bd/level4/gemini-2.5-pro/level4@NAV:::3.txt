This work addresses a contextual bandit problem operating under a known time horizon `T` and a fixed budget `B`. In each round, an agent observes a context `j` drawn from a static probability distribution `π` over `J` possible contexts. The agent then selects an action `k` from a set that includes `K` active choices and a "skip" action (denoted as 0). Selecting the skip action yields zero reward and incurs no cost. Conversely, choosing an active action `k > 0` produces a stochastic reward `y` drawn from a distribution on [0,1] specific to the context-action pair `(j,k)`, and incurs a known, positive cost `c{j,k}`. The agent's objective is to maximize its total accumulated reward, subject to the hard constraint that the cumulative cost does not exceed the budget `B` at any point.

The paper's primary focus is on a specialized version of this problem where the context distribution `π` is known and all action costs are uniform, i.e., `c{j,k} = 1`. For this setting, the authors propose an algorithm that achieves an `O(log T)` regret bound, contingent on a mild assumption. This algorithm is then generalized to handle the broader case involving an unknown context distribution `π` and arbitrary costs `c{j,k}`.

In the context of existing literature, a more general problem formulation was explored in [16], which presented an algorithm with an `O(√T)` regret. The current submission is therefore notable as it appears to be the first to establish an `O(log T)` regret bound for this problem, albeit under a specific condition. The proposed method is built upon an approximation algorithm designed for the offline version of the problem where all statistics are known. The resulting algorithm is elegant in its simplicity and conceptually interesting.

The motivation for the work is strong and the exposition is exceptionally clear. The paper's impact could be strengthened if the `O(log T)` regret were shown to hold unconditionally, or alternatively, if the authors could establish an `Ω(√T)` lower bound for the boundary cases where their condition does not hold.

A central element of the analysis is Lemma 1. I initially had reservations about its proof, as it was not immediately clear why the optimal policy would necessarily satisfy condition (2) at every trial. This made it difficult to understand how the average constraint serves as a valid relaxation of the hard budget constraint. However, the authors' rebuttal has satisfactorily addressed this concern and clarified the validity of Lemma 1.

Minor comments:
*   Is the problem of computing the exact optimal action sequence in the offline setting known to be NP-hard?
*   Could the results be generalized to scenarios with an unknown time horizon `T`?
*   Can the assumption `u^1 > u^2 > ... > u^*_J` be made without loss of generality even when the context distribution is unknown?

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
