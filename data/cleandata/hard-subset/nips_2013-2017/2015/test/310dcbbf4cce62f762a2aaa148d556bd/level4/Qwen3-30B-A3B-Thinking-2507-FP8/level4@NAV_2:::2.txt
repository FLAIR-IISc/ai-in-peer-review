

This paper investigates contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a setting that extends traditional contextual bandit models by incorporating resource limitations. The authors focus on a simplified yet practically relevant model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which is justified in applications such as clinical trials and wireless networks. Their key contribution is the development of an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle, which enables the design of computationally efficient algorithms achieving O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases, representing the first work to establish logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality through rigorous analysis of the ALP approximation and its combination with UCB methods (UCB-ALP), showing near-optimal performance with O(1) regret for non-boundary cases and O(√T) for boundary cases. The technical arguments are well-structured and convincing, with appropriate references to related work including Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] and recent extensions [21, 22]. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from prior work, particularly noting that while [20] achieves O(√T) regret with static budget constraints, their UCB-ALP uses adaptive budget constraints to achieve logarithmic regret for random context arrivals.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-organized sections that progressively build the theoretical foundation and algorithmic design. The motivation is explicit, and the writing is accessible to experts in the field. The contributions are significant in advancing the state of the art for constrained contextual bandits, with the insights about decoupling information acquisition from decision making providing valuable theoretical guidance.

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the practical aspects receive limited attention, with only brief discussion in Sections 5 and 6 and no experimental validation. The absence of empirical results represents a minor weakness, though this is understandable given the paper's focus on theoretical guarantees. The paper's scope is well-aligned with NIPS topics, as it addresses a fundamental problem in online decision making with resource constraints.

Pro: First to establish logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits with a simplified model; clear theoretical development; rigorous analysis; well-motivated and properly contextualized within prior work; computationally efficient algorithms.

Con: Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations; no experimental results to validate the theoretical claims; the simplified assumptions, while reasonable, may limit applicability to more complex real-world settings.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances the field of constrained contextual bandits. The paper's simplified model is appropriate for achieving the logarithmic regret bound, and the authors provide clear justification for their modeling choices. The technical content is sound, the writing is clear, and the significance of the results is well-established. The minor limitations regarding practical implementation and experimental validation do not detract from the paper's substantial theoretical contributions.
