This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B). The main contribution is a computationally efficient algorithm that achieves logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over prior work on related problems which typically achieved sublinear regret of O(sqrt(T)).

The authors' approach is to first analyze the problem in a simplified setting where all system statistics (context distribution, expected rewards) are known. The key challenge in this setting is that the optimal policy, which can be formulated as a dynamic program (DP), is computationally intractable. To overcome this, the authors propose approximating the oracle by relaxing the hard budget constraint into an average one, solvable via a Linear Program (LP). More critically, they introduce the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which re-solves the LP at each step using the current ratio of remaining budget to remaining time. This adaptive policy is shown to be near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret in most cases ("non-boundary") and O(sqrt(T)) regret in specific "boundary" cases. The analysis relies on cleverly mapping the evolution of the budget to a sampling-without-replacement process, allowing the use of concentration bounds.

Building on this foundation, the paper addresses the more realistic setting where expected rewards are unknown. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm combines the UCB principle for exploration with the ALP framework for decision-making. At each step, UCB estimates are used in place of the true expected rewards within the ALP solver. The main result of the paper is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) regret in boundary cases. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first result demonstrating logarithmic regret for this problem. The paper also briefly discusses extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The problem of bandits with budget and time constraints is highly practical and important. Achieving logarithmic regret is a strong theoretical result that advances the state-of-the-art for this class of problems, moving beyond the O(sqrt(T)) bounds seen in related, more general settings (e.g., [17]).
*   **Technical Quality:** The core idea of using an adaptive linear program (ALP) to approximate the intractable DP is elegant and effective. The analysis of the ALP algorithm in the known-statistics case is a valuable contribution in its own right, providing a solid foundation for the main algorithm. The proof sketch appears sound, and the decomposition of the regret into components from action-ranking errors and context-ranking/budget-fluctuation errors is insightful.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The authors do a good job of building intuition by starting with the simpler known-statistics case before tackling the full problem with unknown rewards.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Originality and Relation to Action-Constrained Bandits:** A central question is how this problem setting fundamentally differs from a standard multi-armed bandit problem with JK arms where action availability is constrained by the context. One could view this problem as having JK total arms, but at each time step `t`, only the K arms corresponding to context `Xt` are available. In such action-constrained settings with a finite number of arms, logarithmic regret is generally the expected outcome for a sufficiently long time horizon. While the budget constraint clearly adds a crucial layer of complexity by coupling decisions over time, the paper would be stronger if it more explicitly articulated why existing analyses for action-constrained bandits are insufficient and how the proposed LP-based approach uniquely addresses the challenges introduced by the budget.
*   **Refinement of Regret Bounds:** The problem-dependent regret bounds are presented in the form of O(KJ log T). In standard bandit analysis, such bounds are typically refined to depend on the sum of the inverse of the reward gaps, which provides a more precise characterization of the problem's difficulty. The current form appears to be a consequence of the proof technique, which relies on bounding the number of times the UCB ordering is incorrect. Is it possible to derive a tighter, gap-dependent bound using the current analytical framework, or would this require a fundamentally different approach?
*   **Absence of Lower Bounds:** The paper establishes upper bounds on regret but does not provide corresponding lower bounds. While the O(log T) result is asymptotically optimal for non-boundary cases by analogy to the classic MAB problem, formal lower bounds for this specific constrained contextual setting would significantly strengthen the contribution. Such bounds would help to formally establish the optimality of the proposed algorithms and clarify how the problem difficulty depends on the context distribution, budget ratio `ρ`, and reward structure, especially for the boundary cases where O(sqrt(T)) regret is obtained.
*   **Clarity on `sqrt(T)` Term:** In Theorem 2, the regret for boundary cases is stated as O(sqrt(T) + JK log T). It is unclear if the `sqrt(T)` term is independent of the number of contexts and actions. For instance, in many bandit problems, sublinear regret terms scale with `sqrt(K)` or `sqrt(J)`. A clarification on the constants hidden in the O(sqrt(T)) notation would be helpful.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    1.  The paper addresses a practical and important extension of the contextual bandit problem.
    2.  It is the first work to establish a logarithmic regret bound for this setting, a significant theoretical improvement over existing results for related problems.
    3.  The proposed technical approach, centered on the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the optimal policy, is novel, well-motivated, and could be of broader interest.
    4.  The paper is clearly written and the analysis appears to be technically sound.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    1.  The conceptual novelty compared to action-constrained bandits is not fully elaborated, leaving questions about the magnitude of the contribution.
    2.  The regret analysis could be tightened to provide more standard and informative gap-dependent bounds.
    3.  The lack of problem-specific lower bounds makes it difficult to assess the tightness of the derived upper bounds, particularly in the sublinear regret regime.

Overall, this is a strong paper with an interesting result and a novel technical approach. My primary reservations concern the positioning of the contribution relative to existing bandit frameworks and the refinement of the theoretical bounds. I am currently neutral but would be willing to raise my score if the authors can convincingly address the weaknesses outlined above in their rebuttal, particularly regarding the conceptual originality of their setting.
