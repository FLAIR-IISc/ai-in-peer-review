

This paper studies a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint $B$ and known time horizon $T$. At each trial, the agent observes a context $j$ drawn from a fixed distribution $\pi$ over the context set $\{1, \dots, J\}$, and selects an action $k \in \{0\} \cup \{1, \dots, K\}$, where $k=0$ denotes "skip." If $k=0$, the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost. If $k > 0$, the agent receives a reward $y$ drawn from a fixed distribution over $[0,1]$ (depending on $j$ and $k$) and incurs cost $c_{j,k} > 0$, which is known to the agent. The agent aims to maximize cumulative reward while ensuring the cumulative cost never exceeds $B$.

The main contribution addresses the special case where $\pi$ is known and $c_{j,k} = 1$ for all $j,k$, presenting an algorithm achieving $O(\log T)$ regret under a mild condition. The approach is extended to the general case with unknown $\pi$ and arbitrary $c_{j,k}$. While a more general setting is considered in [16], that work achieves only $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret. Thus, this paper may be the first to achieve $O(\log T)$ regret, though the validity of the mild condition requires verification. The algorithm leverages an approximation for computing the optimal action sequence under known statistics, and is both simple and conceptually interesting.

The paper is well motivated and clearly written. Its significance would be substantially enhanced if $O(\log T)$ regret held universally, as otherwise the $\Omega(\sqrt{T})$ lower bound in boundary cases remains unaddressed.

All results rely on Lemma 1, which I initially questioned. Specifically, I doubted that the optimal action sequence always satisfies (2) for all trials, and thus was unclear why the average constraint constitutes a relaxation of the hard constraint. The authors' rebuttal has resolved this concern, and I now accept Lemma 1.

Minor comments:  
1. Is obtaining the exact optimal action sequence NP-hard?  
2. Can the results be extended to unknown $T$?  
3. Is the assumption $u^1 > u^2 > \dots > u^*_J$ without loss of generality even for unknown $\pi$?  

I strongly recommend acceptance.
