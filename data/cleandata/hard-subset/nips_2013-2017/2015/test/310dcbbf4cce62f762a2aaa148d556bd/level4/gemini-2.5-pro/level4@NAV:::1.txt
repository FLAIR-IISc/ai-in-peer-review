**Review After Rebuttal:**
While I acknowledge the distinction made by the authors, the setting still appears fundamentally similar to existing models. For a sufficiently large time horizon, the coupling effect of the contexts is expected to diminish. It is therefore unclear why each context occurrence cannot be treated as an independent bandit instance. The paper's contribution would be significantly strengthened by a refined upper bound for the problem-dependent case (the version in the supplementary material needs revision) and, ideally, matching problem-dependent and problem-independent lower bounds.

----------------------

This work addresses the contextual bandit problem under both budget and time constraints. The central finding is that for finite context and action spaces, and a sufficiently large time horizon T, an O(log(T)) regret is achievable. The authors position this as a novel result for the contextual bandit setting.

The analysis is presented with rigor, and the writing is clear. However, while the results are interesting, their novelty is questionable. With a finite number of J contexts and K actions, the problem can be reframed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with a total of JK arms. The contextual aspect simply acts as an action constraint, making only K arms available at each time step. In such a finite-arm setting, O(log(T)) regret for a large T is the expected outcome, aligning with established results in action-constrained bandit literature. This raises questions about the conceptual innovation of the paper, which I hope the authors can clarify in their rebuttal.

I have several specific questions:
- In Theorem 2, the regret for case 2 is given as O(sqrt(T) + ...). Should this bound not depend on the number of arms, perhaps scaling as O(sqrt(KT) + ...) at a minimum?
- Still concerning Theorem 2, the problem-dependent regret is expressed as O(KJ log(T)). Typically, problem-dependent bounds are characterized more precisely through the sum of inverse sub-optimality gaps. Is it possible to derive a similar, more granular bound here? I recognize that the current bound's form likely arises from a proof strategy centered on high-probability events on UCB ordering, rather than direct concentration on the gaps. However, it is worth questioning if this approach yields the tightest possible bound.
- The inclusion of lower bounds—both problem-dependent and independent—would be valuable. While trivial lower bounds can be inherited from the classic bandit setting (i.e., zero costs and a single context), a more insightful analysis would derive bounds that explicitly capture the complexity introduced by the contextual element.

My primary reservation is that the proposed setting seems very close to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints. Therefore, I am not convinced of the work's originality and remain neutral on the submission.
