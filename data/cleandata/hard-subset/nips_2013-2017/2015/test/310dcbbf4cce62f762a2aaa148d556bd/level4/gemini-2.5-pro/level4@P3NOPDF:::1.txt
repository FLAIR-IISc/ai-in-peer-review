### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

The authors investigate the contextual multi-armed bandit problem under both a budget and a time constraint. The main contribution is a theoretical analysis for the case with a finite number of contexts (J) and actions (K). They demonstrate that for a sufficiently large time horizon (T), it is possible to achieve a logarithmic regret of O(log T), a novel result for this specific setting. The paper is well-written, and the theoretical results appear to be thorough.

**Critique and Major Concerns**

While the results are technically sound, their originality and significance are questionable. The core issue is that the proposed setting seems closely related to a standard budgeted multi-armed bandit with action constraints. Specifically, a problem with J contexts and K arms can be viewed as a single bandit problem with JK arms, where at each time step, the available actions are constrained to the K arms corresponding to the observed context.

Given that logarithmic regret is a known result for standard bandit problems with a finite number of arms (and their action-constrained variants) when the time horizon is large, the O(log T) result presented here is interesting but not particularly surprising. The paper's primary weakness is its failure to sufficiently differentiate this problem from existing frameworks and to highlight what makes the "contextual" aspect fundamentally different in this finite setting. This central concern about the paper's innovativeness tempers my enthusiasm.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the authors address the following points:

1.  **Refined Regret Bounds in Theorem 2:**
    *   The problem-independent regret bound in Case 2 is stated as O(√T + ...). Could the authors clarify if there should be a dependency on the number of arms, K? A bound of at least O(√(KT)) might be expected here.
    *   The problem-dependent regret bound of O(KJ log T) is rather coarse. In bandit literature, it is standard to express such bounds more precisely in terms of the sum of inverse arm gaps (Δ). I understand that the current proof technique, which relies on events characterizing the correct ordering of UCB indices, may not easily yield such a bound. However, a more fine-grained analysis would provide deeper insight into the problem's structure. Is it possible to derive a gap-dependent bound, and if not, why is this problem fundamentally harder?

2.  **Inclusion of Lower Bounds:**
    The paper would be significantly more complete with the addition of lower bounds. Trivial lower bounds can be established by considering a single context (J=1) with no cost, which reduces to the classical bandit problem. However, a more insightful lower bound would explicitly account for the complexity introduced by the interplay of multiple contexts, the budget, and the time horizon. Establishing matching lower bounds for both the problem-dependent and problem-independent cases would clearly define the difficulty of this problem and solidify the paper's contribution.

### Comments After Rebuttal

I have read the authors' rebuttal, but I am not yet convinced of the paper's novelty. My primary concern remains.

The authors argue about the coupling of the context, but this argument is not compelling. For a sufficiently large time horizon, the influence of this coupling is expected to vanish. In this regime, I still do not see a fundamental reason why each context-action pair cannot be treated as an independent arm in a larger JK-armed bandit problem, subject to action constraints and a shared budget.

For this paper to be a strong contribution to the field, it needs more than the current analysis. My recommendations are as follows:

1.  **A Clean, Gap-Dependent Upper Bound:** The paper would be much improved with a rigorous upper bound for the problem-dependent case, expressed in terms of the arm gaps. The current bound in the supplementary material needs to be reworked to achieve this standard of precision.

2.  **Matching Lower Bounds:** As mentioned previously, the paper should ideally include matching lower bounds for both the problem-dependent and problem-independent cases to demonstrate that the proposed algorithms are optimal.

Without these significant additions, the paper's contribution feels incremental, as the setting and results are very close to what can be inferred from existing, more general bandit frameworks. Therefore, I will maintain my current score.
