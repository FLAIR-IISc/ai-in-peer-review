### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), a setting the authors term "constrained contextual bandits." The analysis is conducted under a set of simplifying yet practical assumptions: a finite set of discrete contexts, a single budget constraint, and fixed, known costs for actions.

The core contribution is a novel two-step approach to developing a low-regret algorithm. First, for the setting where reward statistics are known, the authors show that the optimal policy, which is an intractable dynamic program, can be closely approximated by a simple and efficient Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. This ALP policy re-solves a linear program at each step based on the remaining time and budget. Crucially, the authors prove that this approximation achieves near-optimal performance, with O(1) regret in most cases ("non-boundary" cases) and O(√T) regret in specific "boundary" cases.

Building on this insight, the authors then tackle the case with unknown rewards. They combine the ALP framework with the Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) method to create the UCB-ALP algorithm. The key insight enabling this combination is that the ALP policy primarily relies on the correct *ordering* of expected rewards, which UCB is designed to learn efficiently. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases, which, to the best of my knowledge, is the first logarithmic regret bound for this problem. The paper also discusses extensions to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a high-quality theoretical contribution. The technical approach is sound, elegant, and well-motivated. By first analyzing the known-statistics case to develop a strong benchmark and algorithmic primitive (ALP), the authors establish a solid foundation for tackling the more complex unknown-statistics problem. The proof sketches provided in the main text are insightful and convincing, particularly the analysis of the remaining budget's evolution via a "sampling without replacement" analogy and its resulting concentration properties. The theoretical results are strong and represent a clear advance.

The main weakness is the complete lack of empirical validation. While this is primarily a theoretical work, the claims would be significantly strengthened by experiments. For instance, simulations could illustrate the performance difference between boundary and non-boundary cases, demonstrate how the regret scales in practice, and compare UCB-ALP against existing algorithms for related problems (e.g., from [17] or [20]), even if those algorithms have weaker theoretical guarantees in this specific setting.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of guiding the reader from the high-level problem and its challenges to the specifics of their solution. The motivation for each step—approximating the DP with an LP, making the LP adaptive, and then combining it with UCB—is articulated clearly. The use of "Sketch of Proof" sections is particularly effective in conveying the main ideas of the analysis without getting lost in technical minutiae.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While LP relaxations of DPs are a known concept, the design and analysis of the *adaptive* LP for this bandit setting, and the proof of its O(1) regret, are novel. This constitutes a significant conceptual contribution. The subsequent combination with UCB, while a natural step, is executed non-trivially and leads to a novel algorithm for the problem. The result of O(log T) regret is a clear departure from previous work (e.g., [17]), which established O(√T) regret in more general settings but against a weaker benchmark.

**Significance:** The results are theoretically significant. Achieving the first logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits is a major milestone. The paper's core idea—decoupling the complex interactions between learning and resource management via the ALP approximation—provides a powerful and computationally efficient framework. This approach could be influential and potentially extendable to other online learning problems with complex constraints. The practical significance is currently limited by the simplifying assumptions and the lack of experiments, but the foundational ideas are strong and likely to inspire future applied work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and elegant method (ALP) for handling budget and time constraints in contextual bandits, leading to the first O(log T) regret bound for this problem. This is a significant theoretical advance.
2.  **Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a complex topic accessible and the contributions easy to understand.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The analysis is rigorous and the step-by-step development from a known-statistics model to the final UCB-ALP algorithm is methodologically sound and compelling.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The absence of any experimental results is a notable omission. It leaves open questions about the practical performance of the algorithm, the size of the constants in the regret bounds, and how it behaves in the O(√T) boundary cases.

**Conclusion**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on contextual bandits. The authors have successfully identified a tractable approach to a challenging problem, resulting in a new algorithm with a state-of-the-art logarithmic regret bound. While the paper would have been stronger with an experimental section to validate the theoretical findings, its theoretical merits are substantial. I recommend acceptance. A minor suggestion would be for the authors to add a discussion on the practical implications of the "boundary cases" and how frequently they might be expected to occur.
