- Problem: Constrained contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B induce temporal coupling, making the optimal policy a complex dynamic program.

- Main contribution: Novel, computationally efficient approach achieving logarithmic regret for this problem class for the first time.

- ALP algorithm (known statistics): Replaces hard budget constraint with an adaptive average-budget linear program using remaining budget/time, achieving O(1) regret except O(√T) in boundary cases.

- UCB-ALP algorithm (unknown rewards): Combines UCB for reward estimation with ALP for exploitation, achieving O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases.

- Key technical insight: ALP primarily depends on reward ordering, which UCB can learn efficiently; analysis maps remaining-budget evolution to sampling-without-replacement to obtain concentration bounds.

- Extensions: Authors discuss handling unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, addressing more realistic settings.

- Significance & originality: First log-regret result for constrained contextual bandits and novel decoupling via adaptive LP that could influence related constrained decision problems.

- Technical quality: Solid, elegant theoretical analysis that carefully handles UCB errors, ALP decisions, and budget fluctuations.

- Clarity: Well-structured exposition, building from simple to complex cases, making core ideas accessible.

- Weakness — boundary intuition: Paper lacks deeper intuition on why boundary cases (ρ = q_j) are fundamentally harder and more sensitive to budget fluctuations.

- Weakness — assumptions framing: Strong log-regret results rely on simplifying assumptions (known context distribution, unit costs); the practical difficulty of relaxing these could be emphasized more.

- Pros for acceptance: Tackles an important practical problem, introduces an effective new technique (ALP), achieves a major theoretical breakthrough (O(log T)), and is computationally efficient.

- Cons against acceptance: Insufficient intuition/explanation for boundary-case phenomenon and strongest results depend on simplifying assumptions.

- Recommendation: Strong paper with minor presentation issues; reviewer recommends acceptance.
