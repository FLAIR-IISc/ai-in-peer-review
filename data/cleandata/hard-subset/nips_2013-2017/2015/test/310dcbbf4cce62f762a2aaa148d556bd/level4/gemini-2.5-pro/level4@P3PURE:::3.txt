This paper addresses a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint B over a known time horizon T. In each trial, the agent observes a context `j` from a set `{1, ..., J}`, drawn from a fixed probability distribution `π`. The agent then chooses an action `k` from `{0, 1, ..., K}`, where action 0 is a "skip" option. If `k=0`, the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost. If `k>0`, the agent receives a stochastic reward `y` from a distribution on [0,1] (dependent on `j` and `k`) and incurs a known, positive cost `c_{j,k}`. The agent's goal is to maximize the cumulative reward while ensuring the cumulative cost never exceeds the budget B.

The main body of the paper focuses on a special case where the context distribution `π` is known and all costs `c_{j,k}` are unity. For this setting, the authors propose an algorithm and prove that it achieves an `O(log T)` regret bound under a mild condition. The paper then extends this algorithm to the general case with an unknown `π` and arbitrary costs `c_{j,k}`.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is well-motivated and very clearly written.
*   The key contribution is the achievement of an `O(log T)` regret bound, which is a significant improvement over the `O(sqrt(T))` regret of algorithms for more general settings, such as the one in [16]. This appears to be the first work to achieve logarithmic regret for this problem, albeit under a specific condition.
*   The proposed algorithm, which is based on an approximation for the optimal policy in the offline setting, is simple yet insightful.

**Critique and Questions:**
*   Initially, I had a question regarding the correctness of Lemma 1. I was not convinced that the optimal action sequence would satisfy condition (2) in all trials, and therefore did not see how the average cost constraint could be considered a relaxed version of the hard cumulative constraint. The authors' rebuttal has satisfactorily clarified this point.
*   The main limitation of the current result is that the `O(log T)` regret is contingent on a specific condition. The paper's impact would be greater if this logarithmic regret could be shown to hold more generally. Alternatively, proving an `Ω(sqrt(T))` lower bound for cases that fall outside this condition would also strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**
*   Is the problem of computing the exact optimal action sequence (assuming all distributions are known) NP-hard? A comment on the computational complexity would be insightful.
*   Could the proposed algorithm and its analysis be extended to the case of an unknown time horizon T?
*   Regarding the assumption `u^1 > u^2 > ... > u^*_J`, can this be made without loss of generality even when the context distribution `π` is unknown?

Overall, this is a strong paper with an interesting result. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
