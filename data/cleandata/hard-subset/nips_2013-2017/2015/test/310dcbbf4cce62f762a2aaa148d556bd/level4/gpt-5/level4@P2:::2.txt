This paper studies contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints (“constrained contextual bandits”). The authors focus on finite discrete contexts, fixed (possibly heterogeneous) costs, and a single budget. With known statistics and unit costs, they propose an adaptive linear programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle that relaxes the hard budget to an average one and adapts to the remaining budget/time ratio. ALP achieves O(1) regret (except at measure-zero boundary cases where it is O(√T)). When rewards are unknown, they combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) and prove O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases and O(√T + log T) at boundaries. They further discuss extensions to unknown context distribution (via empirical estimates, EALP/UCB-EALP) and heterogeneous costs, where related ALP-style algorithms retain similar regret guarantees. Relative to prior work on resourceful/contextual bandits (e.g., Mixture Elimination with O(√T) regret and high complexity, bounded to a finite policy set) and bandits with convex/concave constraints (typically O(√T) and static contexts), this work is, to my knowledge, the first to obtain logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under these assumptions while maintaining computational efficiency.

Quality: The technical development is solid. The LP relaxation provides a clean upper bound and an implementable policy, and the regret proofs are convincing (though I did not check every detail). The analysis carefully handles the coupling induced by budget and the randomness of contexts, leveraging concentration of the remaining budget and UCB ordering guarantees.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized, with appropriate references. The role of “boundary cases” and how they arise is explained, though additional intuition and guidance for detecting or mitigating them in practice would help. Complexity and constant factors could be discussed more explicitly.

Originality: The adaptive LP relaxation tailored to the remaining budget and its integration with UCB is novel in this setting. The work clearly differentiates itself from prior approaches that either assume static contexts, restrict to finite policy classes, or achieve only O(√T) regret.

Significance: Establishing logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits is an important advance. The algorithms are computationally light and the analysis techniques (e.g., mapping to sampling without replacement to control budget fluctuations) may inform future work on more general constraints.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Scope: Results rely on discrete contexts, a single budget, and known context distribution in the main analysis; the unknown-distribution extension requires truncation and is sketched. Clarify robustness to misspecified or drifting context distributions and discuss empirical estimation without truncation.
- Boundary cases: Provide practical diagnostics for being near boundaries and possible remedies (e.g., margin-based tie-breaking or conservative slack).
- Empirics: Theoretical results would be strengthened by simulations comparing ALP/UCB-ALP to Mixture Elimination and Knapsacks-style baselines, including sensitivity to model misspecification and heterogeneous costs.
- Practical guidance: Elaborate on implementation details (complexity per round, constants in regret), and give examples mapping real problems (e.g., clinical trials, ad allocation) to the model, including cost heterogeneity.

Reasons to accept:
- First logarithmic regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits under reasonable and clearly stated assumptions.
- Conceptually simple, computationally efficient algorithms with strong theoretical backing.
- Clean LP-based benchmark and analysis techniques that may generalize.

Reasons to reject:
- Lack of empirical validation and limited practical guidance.
- Dependence on simplified assumptions (finite contexts, single budget, known distribution in core results).
- O(√T) behavior at boundary cases without concrete mitigation strategies.

Overall assessment: A strong theoretical contribution with novel ideas and clear writing. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to add empirical evaluation and more discussion of boundary handling and robustness.
