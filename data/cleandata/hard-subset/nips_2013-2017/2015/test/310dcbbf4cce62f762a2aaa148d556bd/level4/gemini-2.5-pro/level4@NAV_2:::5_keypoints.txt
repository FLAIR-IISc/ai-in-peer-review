- Addresses contextual bandits with time and budget constraints, where dynamic programming is intractable.
- Proposes Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) that relaxes hard budget constraints and adapts to average remaining budget for known statistics.
- Shows ALP is near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret in most known-statistics scenarios.
- Combines ALP with UCB to form UCB-ALP for unknown expected rewards, leveraging that ALP depends only on reward ordering.
- Proves UCB-ALP attains O(log T) regret, improving over prior O(√T) results.
- Novel contribution: first logarithmic-regret result for budget-constrained contextual bandits and an ALP framework that decouples learning and planning.
- Technical strength: rigorous analysis using concentration of remaining budget and partitioning regret into action vs. context ranking errors.
- Clarity: well-written exposition that motivates algorithm design from known- to unknown-statistics cases.
- Computational efficiency: UCB-ALP requires solving a small LP per step, making it more practical than prior complex methods.
- Limitation: analysis restricted to discrete contexts/actions and initially assumes known context distribution.
- Limitation: purely theoretical work with no empirical evaluation to demonstrate practical performance or behavior in boundary cases.
- Suggestion: clarify design rationale of UCB-ALP and relate it to optimistic frameworks like UCRL2.
- Suggested related work: discuss concurrent Agrawal et al. (arXiv:1506.03374) addressing contextual bandits with global constraints.
- Overall assessment: significant and novel theoretical contribution that merits acceptance despite simplifications and missing experiments.
