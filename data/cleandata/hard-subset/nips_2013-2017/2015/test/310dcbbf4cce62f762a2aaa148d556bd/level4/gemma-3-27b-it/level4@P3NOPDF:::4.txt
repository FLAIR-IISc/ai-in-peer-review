This paper analyzes contextual bandit algorithms in a setting with a particularly strong and well-motivated baseline: an adaptive linear program that strategically sacrifices performance on a fraction of rounds (“budget”) to improve overall learning. This is an interesting and unique setup, and the authors provide compelling solutions.

My primary question concerns the handling of boundary cases in the analysis. Specifically, it’s unclear why these cases require separate treatment. The condition ρ = q<sub>j</sub> doesn’t appear inherently special, particularly for *j* > 1, and the justification for a distinct analytical approach isn’t immediately obvious. A more detailed explanation of *why* this boundary condition necessitates different handling would significantly strengthen the paper.

The motivation for considering a baseline with a budget constraint is well-articulated. Traditional contextual bandit analyses often compare against a fixed-action or random-action baseline. Introducing a strategically informed, adaptive baseline like the one proposed—which explicitly manages a tradeoff between exploration and exploitation by allowing suboptimal actions—is a significant contribution. It presents a substantially more challenging benchmark and forces a deeper understanding of the algorithm’s performance characteristics. The authors effectively demonstrate that achieving competitive performance against this baseline requires careful consideration of exploration strategies.

The technical contributions are sound. The analysis appears rigorous and the proofs are well-structured (though a thorough verification would require more detailed scrutiny). The derived regret bounds are informative and clearly illustrate the dependence on key parameters, such as the budget size and the problem’s complexity. I appreciate the clarity with which the authors present the algorithmic modifications needed to compete with the adaptive linear programming baseline. 

The experimental evaluation, while perhaps limited in scope, provides some evidence supporting the theoretical findings. Showing performance curves against the adaptive baseline would be beneficial. Currently, the presentation focuses primarily on theoretical regret bounds. Visualizing the practical performance differences would add valuable insight. Furthermore, details on the parameter tuning for both the proposed algorithm and the adaptive baseline would enhance reproducibility and understanding of the results.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the theory of contextual bandits. The introduction of a strategically adaptive baseline raises the bar for evaluating algorithm performance, and the authors’ work provides a solid foundation for further research in this direction. Addressing the question regarding the boundary case analysis would significantly improve the clarity and completeness of the paper. I believe that with minor revisions addressing this point and potentially expanding the empirical evaluation, this work would be a strong addition to the NIPS conference.
