- Addresses constrained contextual bandits with finite discrete contexts and actions under time horizon T and budget B.  
- Main contribution: UCB-ALP algorithm—first computationally efficient method achieving logarithmic regret for this problem class.  
- Introduces ALP (Adaptive Linear Programming) for the known-distribution case, approximating the intractable dynamic program via an LP solved using remaining budget/time.  
- ALP is near-optimal: achieves O(1) regret except for narrow boundary cases (O(√T)).  
- ALP only requires correct ordering of expected rewards, not their exact values.  
- UCB-ALP combines UCB estimates with ALP, using UCB to rank actions/contexts and ALP for decision-making.  
- UCB-ALP theoretical guarantee: O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases.  
- Analysis rigorously treats the evolution and concentration of the remaining budget.  
- Algorithm is computationally efficient, needing only a small LP per step rather than dynamic programming.  
- Paper is very clearly written with well-presented motivation and technical arguments.  
- Weakness: purely theoretical—no numerical experiments to validate regret bounds, constants, or compare baselines.  
- Weakness: assumes finite discrete contexts; extension to large/continuous or parameterized (e.g., linear) contexts would increase applicability.  
- Weakness: Section 4.2 could better motivate UCB-ALP by explicitly framing it as optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty and relating to methods like UCRL2.  
- Overall: strong, novel, and technically solid work that merits acceptance despite lacking empirical validation.
