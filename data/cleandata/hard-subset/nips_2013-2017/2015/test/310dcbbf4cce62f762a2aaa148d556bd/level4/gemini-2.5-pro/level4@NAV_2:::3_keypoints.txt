- Problem: studies contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B where actions incur costs and process stops at T or budget depletion.
- Main contribution: proposes an algorithm achieving O(log T) regret, improving over prior O(sqrt(T)) results under a structured model.
- Model scope: finite number of contexts and actions, focus first on unit-cost case with known context distribution, later extends to unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs.
- Key technique: introduces Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) to approximate the oracle policy and handle budget coupling without full dynamic programming.
- ALP property: near-optimal (O(1) regret) in non-boundary cases and depends only on ordering of expected rewards, enabling combination with UCB.
- Algorithmic result: UCB-ALP for unknown rewards is simple, computationally efficient, and comes with rigorous analysis.
- Regret guarantees: O(log T) in non-boundary cases, but degrades to O(sqrt(T)) in boundary cases where budget-to-time ratio aligns with context cumulative probabilities.
- Limitation: logarithmic regret is not universal due to boundary cases, tempering the main claim.
- Comparative context: contrasts with Resourceful Contextual Bandits work that achieves O(sqrt(T)) regret in more general settings.
- Strengths: novel ALP idea, clear exposition, and solid theoretical analysis.
- Open theoretical question: whether boundary-case O(sqrt(T)) is an artifact of analysis or a fundamental lower bound.
- Practical/assumption concerns: relies on finite contexts/actions and uses known T in UCB bonuses; extensions to unknown/infinite horizon need discussion.
- Clarification requested: whether the assumed sorted ordering of optimal rewards is maintained or learned in the unknown context-distribution extension.
- Suggested author tasks: clarify oracle computational complexity (is solving it NP-hard?), address unknown/infinite horizon modifications, and discuss boundary-case fundamentalness.
