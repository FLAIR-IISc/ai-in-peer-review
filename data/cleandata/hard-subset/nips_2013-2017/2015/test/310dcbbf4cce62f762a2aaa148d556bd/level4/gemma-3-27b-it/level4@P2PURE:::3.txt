This paper addresses a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint *B* and a known time horizon *T*. In each round, the agent observes a context *j* sampled from a fixed distribution π over a context set {1, …, *J*}, and selects an action *k* from {0} ∪ {1, …, *K*}, where *k* = 0 represents skipping the trial. Choosing *k* = 0 yields no reward or cost, while *k* > 0 results in a reward *y* (sampled from a distribution dependent on *j* and *k* and taking values in [0, 1]) and incurs a cost *c<sub>j,k</sub>*, where *c<sub>j,k</sub>* > 0 is a known parameter. The agent’s objective is to maximize cumulative reward subject to the constraint that cumulative cost does not exceed *B*.

The core of the paper focuses on a special case where π is known and *c<sub>j,k</sub>* = 1 for all *j* and *k*, presenting an algorithm with a regret bound of O(log *T*) under a certain condition. The work then extends this algorithm to the more general scenario of unknown π and arbitrary *c<sub>j,k</sub>* values.

While a related work [16] considers a similar setting, it achieves only an O(√*T*) regret bound. Thus, this paper potentially represents the first work to achieve O(log *T*) regret, contingent upon the satisfaction of the stated condition. The proposed algorithm is based on approximating the optimal action sequence when all statistics are known, offering a simple yet compelling approach.

The paper is well-motivated and exceptionally clearly written. Its impact would be further enhanced if the O(log *T*) regret held universally, or if a corresponding Ω(√*T*) lower bound were established for cases where the condition is not met. 

Initially, I had concerns regarding the correctness of Lemma 1, specifically questioning whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies condition (2) for all trials and thus whether averaging the constraint provides a valid relaxation of the hard constraint. However, these concerns were addressed by the rebuttal and I am now convinced of the lemma’s validity. 

Minor points for consideration include: Is it NP-hard to compute the optimal action sequence exactly? Can the results be generalized to scenarios with an unknown time horizon *T*? Finally, can we assume *u<sup>1</sup>* > *u<sup>2</sup>* > … > *u<sup>*</sup><sub>J</sub>* without loss of generality, even when the context distribution is unknown? 

Overall, I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
