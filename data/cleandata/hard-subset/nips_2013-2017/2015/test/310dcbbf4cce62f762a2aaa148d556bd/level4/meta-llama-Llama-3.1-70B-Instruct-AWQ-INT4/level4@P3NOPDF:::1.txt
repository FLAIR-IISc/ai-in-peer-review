Review after rebuttal:

Although I agree that the proposed setting is not identical to existing bandit problems, I still believe it bears a strong resemblance to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints, particularly when the horizon is large enough. In such cases, the coupling of the context appears to vanish, making me wonder why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as a separate instance of a bandit problem.

To strengthen this paper, I would like to see a clean, reworked upper bound in terms of arm gaps for the problem-dependent case, ideally accompanied by matching lower bounds for both problem-dependent and independent cases.

The authors investigate the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit. Their primary contribution is proving that, given a finite number of contexts and actions, and a sufficiently large time constraint T, a regret of order log(T) is achievable, a novel result for contextual bandits.

The results presented in the paper are thorough, and the writing is precise. However, the findings, although interesting, are not particularly surprising. Since the number of contexts J and arms K are finite, this bandit problem can be viewed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms, which is also action-constrained, as not all JK arms are accessible at each time. Given the finite number of arms, for large enough T, the regret is log(T), similar to action-constrained bandit problems. This raises questions about the paper's originality, and I would appreciate further clarification from the authors.

I have several questions regarding this paper:

1. In Theorem 2, the regret in case 2) is O(√T + ...). However, shouldn't it be O(√(KT) + ...) at the very least?

2. Still in Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ log(T)) or O(√(T) + KJ log(T)), depending on the configuration of the arms. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are expressed in a more refined way, using the sum of inverse arm gaps. Is it possible to achieve something similar in this context? Although the proof relies on events characterizing the correct orders of the UCB, rather than concentration bounds on their gaps, I am unsure if this approach is optimal.

3. It would be valuable to have lower bounds for this problem, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. While trivial lower bounds can be derived from classical bandit problems (with zero cost and a constant context), can these be refined to account for the complexity of the context? I remain unconvinced by the originality of this setting and, therefore, remain neutral about this paper.

Upon reevaluation, I am still under the impression that the setting of this paper closely resembles a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints. I would appreciate further clarification from the authors regarding the originality of their work.
