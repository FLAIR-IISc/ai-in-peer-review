This paper introduces an interesting and novel setup for analyzing contextual bandits, comparing their performance against a strong baseline employing adaptive linear programming to strategically abstain from acting in a fraction of rounds – a concept the authors term a “budget.” The core idea and proposed solutions are unique and potentially valuable.

However, a central aspect of the analysis remains unclear. The paper justifies analyzing boundary cases separately, but the rationale for this distinction is not fully convincing. Specifically, it’s unclear why the case where ρ = q<sub>j</sub> requires special consideration, particularly for j > 1. The mathematical presentation doesn't suggest any inherent difference or singularity associated with this condition; it doesn’t appear that ρ = q<sub>j</sub> possesses any particularly unique properties. 

I would appreciate clarification on the motivation behind the differentiated analysis of boundary cases. Understanding *why* these cases demand separate treatment is crucial for fully appreciating the technical contribution of the paper. Addressing this point would significantly strengthen the manuscript.
