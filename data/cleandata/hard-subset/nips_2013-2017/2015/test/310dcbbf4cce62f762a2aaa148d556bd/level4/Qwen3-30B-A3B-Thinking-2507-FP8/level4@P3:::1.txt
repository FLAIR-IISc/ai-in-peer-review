

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, where the agent faces both time and budget constraints. The authors develop algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret in this setting, which they claim is novel. The main contribution is the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm for known statistics and its combination with UCB (UCB-ALP) for unknown statistics, achieving O(log T) regret except for boundary cases where the regret is O(√T).

The paper relates well to prior work. It acknowledges that budget constraints have been studied in non-contextual bandits [11-16], but these results don't apply to contextual settings. The authors also reference Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], which achieves O(√T) regret using a different benchmark and algorithmic approach. The paper's simplified model (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint) is justified by applications like clinical trials and wireless networks, allowing for more efficient algorithms with better regret bounds.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The regret analysis is rigorous, with careful decomposition of regret sources and novel techniques for handling the complex interactions between budget constraints, context arrivals, and reward estimation. The proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 are thorough, though the supplementary material would benefit from a clearer presentation of the regret decomposition. The authors provide appropriate context by comparing their results with related work, including recent extensions [21, 22] that achieve O(√T) regret in more general settings.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem is well-defined, the algorithms are clearly described, and the writing is precise and accessible. The authors effectively use lemmas and theorems to build their argument, and the figures (not shown here) would presumably further aid understanding. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, though some parts of the regret analysis could be streamlined.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the constrained bandit literature. While the draft review questions whether the problem is truly novel, the authors correctly distinguish their work from previous budgeted bandit studies by emphasizing the contextual aspect. The key insight is that the contextual bandit problem with budget constraints can be decoupled through adaptive linear relaxation, which enables logarithmic regret. The ALP approximation is novel in its approach to handling the oracle algorithm's complexity, and the UCB-ALP combination effectively addresses the exploration-exploitation tradeoff under constraints.

The significance of this work is strong for applications with budget constraints and contextual information, such as clinical trials and online recommendation systems. The authors' algorithms are computationally efficient and provide a promising path toward solving the broader open problem of general constrained contextual bandits. The paper's theoretical results advance the state of the art by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in this constrained setting, which previous work could not.

The main concerns are related to the novelty argument and the regret bounds. The draft review correctly points out that for finite contexts and actions, the problem can be viewed as a budgeted bandit with action constraints (where only K of JK arms are available at each step). While this perspective is valid, the authors should more clearly articulate how their approach differs from a naive reduction to a budgeted bandit with JK arms. Specifically, the adaptive nature of their algorithm (adjusting the budget constraint based on remaining time and budget) is crucial to achieving logarithmic regret, whereas a naive reduction would likely yield worse bounds.

Regarding the regret bounds, the draft review's concern about the O(√T) term in Theorem 2 is valid. The authors should clarify whether the O(√T) bound is tight for boundary cases or if it could be improved. The draft review's suggestion to provide problem-dependent bounds in terms of arm gaps is well-founded; such bounds would strengthen the paper's contribution by providing a more refined characterization of the regret.

The paper would be significantly improved by including problem-dependent regret bounds expressed in terms of arm gaps, as well as a discussion of lower bounds for the problem. While the current O(JK log T) bound is technically correct, the draft review's suggestion to present bounds in the form of sums of inverse gaps (as in standard bandit literature) would make the results more comparable to existing work and better demonstrate the algorithm's efficiency.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the contextual bandit literature with clear technical merit. The paper addresses an important practical problem with a theoretically sound approach, and the algorithms are well-motivated and efficient. The main weakness is the insufficient discussion of how the problem differs from a naive reduction to a budgeted bandit, and the lack of problem-dependent regret bounds. With these clarifications, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, the paper makes an important contribution but needs to better articulate its novelty and provide more refined regret analysis to fully establish its significance.
