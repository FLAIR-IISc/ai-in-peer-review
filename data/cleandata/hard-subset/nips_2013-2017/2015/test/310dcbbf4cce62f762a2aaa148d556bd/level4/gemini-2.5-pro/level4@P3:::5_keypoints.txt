- Problem: Studies contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B, where decisions depend on remaining budget/time, complicating exploration-exploitation.

- Main contribution: Presents UCB-ALP, a computationally efficient algorithm achieving O(log T) regret in most cases — a major improvement over prior contextual-budget work.

- Core idea: Approximate the DP oracle with an Adaptive Linear Program (ALP) that uses remaining-budget/remaining-time ratio to decide actions.

- ALP (known stats): Solves an LP each step; achieves O(1) regret except for boundary cases where regret is O(sqrt(T)).

- UCB-ALP (unknown rewards): Uses UCB estimates to rank contexts/actions and plugs them into ALP; achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) in boundary cases.

- Extensions: Discusses handling unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

- Relation to prior work: Improves over Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) and other contextual approaches that achieved O(sqrt(T)) and were computationally heavier; aligns with non-contextual budget literature with logarithmic regret.

- Strength — Originality/Significance: First constrained contextual bandit algorithm with a logarithmic regret bound; adaptive LP as planner is novel and conceptually valuable.

- Strength — Quality: Theoretical analysis is rigorous and non-trivial, with insightful proof techniques (e.g., sampling-without-replacement analogy).

- Strength — Clarity: Paper is very well-written and pedagogically structured, moving from known-statistics ALP to full UCB-ALP.

- Weakness — Empirical validation: No experiments; practical performance, hidden constants, finite-T behavior, and boundary-case behavior are untested.

- Weakness — Simplifying assumptions: Results rely on finite discrete contexts, known context distribution, and unit costs in main analysis, limiting direct applicability; extensions need further work.

- Weakness — Presentation: The connection between UCB estimates and ALP could be framed more explicitly as optimism-driven planning to aid intuition.

- Recommendation: Strong accept — theoretical novelty and solid analysis outweigh limitations.

- Pro-accept arguments: First O(log T) result for constrained contextual bandits; novel, efficient ALP/UCB-ALP framework; clear exposition and solid proofs.

- Con-accept arguments: Reliance on simplifying assumptions and absence of empirical results constrain practical evaluation.
