

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, where agents face both budget and time constraints. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves near-optimal performance when statistics are known, and combine it with UCB to create UCB-ALP for unknown statistics. They show that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases, which represents the first logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits.

The paper is well-motivated by practical applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing, where budget constraints are critical. It clearly relates to previous work on contextual bandits [1-7] and Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17], noting that RCB achieves only O(√T) regret with a restricted benchmark. The authors justify their simplified model (finite contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint) as practical for many real-world scenarios while enabling efficient algorithms.

Key strengths include:
1. The novel ALP approximation, which provides a clean benchmark for regret analysis and achieves O(1) regret except for boundary cases.
2. The insightful connection between constrained contextual bandits and linear programming, which elegantly decouples exploration and exploitation.
3. The rigorous regret analysis, with careful handling of interactions between budget fluctuations and context ranking.
4. The clear presentation of UCB-ALP, which combines ALP's adaptivity with UCB's exploration.

The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the first logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits under natural assumptions. The authors provide valuable insights into designing computationally efficient algorithms for this challenging problem, with the UCB-ALP algorithm being both theoretically sound and practically implementable.

However, the novelty claim could be more precisely framed. While the problem can be viewed as a contextual bandit with JK arms (only K available per context), the authors' specific analysis and algorithm design are novel. The paper could better distinguish their approach from standard bandit results by emphasizing how the budget constraint fundamentally changes the exploration-exploitation tradeoff in contextual settings.

The boundary cases (where regret is O(√T)) are acknowledged as having zero Lebesgue measure, but the paper would benefit from more discussion of their practical relevance. The regret bound O(JK log T) in Theorem 2 for non-boundary cases is technically correct, but the authors could explore whether this can be refined using problem-dependent analysis with arm gaps, as is standard in bandit literature.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in contextual bandits. The supplementary material appears to contain the necessary technical details for reproducibility. The authors have made a clear contribution that advances the state of the art in constrained contextual bandits.

Pro:
- First logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits under natural assumptions
- Novel ALP approximation providing clean regret analysis
- Computationally efficient algorithms with rigorous theoretical guarantees
- Clear connection to practical applications

Con:
- Novelty claim could be more precisely framed
- Boundary cases need more practical discussion
- Regret bound could be refined with problem-dependent analysis

This is a strong contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The paper is technically sound, well-structured, and addresses a significant challenge in contextual bandits with constraints. I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
