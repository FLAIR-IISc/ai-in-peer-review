This paper considers a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint *B* and a known time horizon *T*. In each trial, the agent observes a context *j* drawn from a fixed probability distribution π over a context set {1, …, *J*}, and selects an action *k* from {0} ∪ {1, …, *K*}, where *k* = 0 represents skipping. Skipping yields no reward or cost, while *k* > 0 results in a reward *y* and a cost *c<sub>j,k</sub>*, where *y* is drawn from a fixed distribution on [0, 1] dependent on *j* and *k*, and *c<sub>j,k</sub>* > 0 is a known parameter. The agent’s objective is to maximize cumulative reward subject to the constraint that cumulative cost does not exceed the budget *B*.

The core of the paper focuses on a special case where π is known and *c<sub>j,k</sub>* = 1 for all *j* and *k*. The authors present an algorithm achieving a regret bound of O(log *T*) under a mild condition. They then extend this algorithm to the more general setting where π is unknown and the *c<sub>j,k</sub>* values are arbitrary. 

While a more general setting is considered in [16], that work yields an algorithm with O(√*T*) regret. Therefore, this paper potentially represents the first work to achieve O(log *T*) regret for this problem, although it is conditional on satisfying the aforementioned mild condition. The algorithm is based on an approximation approach for computing the optimal action sequence given complete knowledge of the statistics, and is both simple and insightful.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. Its significance would be enhanced if the O(log *T*) regret bound held unconditionally, or if a lower bound of Ω(√*T*) were established for the cases where the condition is not met. 

Initially, my understanding hinged on the correctness of Lemma 1, as the results appear to heavily rely on it. I was initially concerned that the optimal action sequence does not always satisfy condition (2) for all trials, and therefore questioned whether averaging the constraint provided a valid relaxation of the hard constraint. However, this concern was addressed and resolved by the rebuttal; I am now convinced of Lemma 1's validity. 

Minor comments and potential future work include: Is it NP-hard to exactly determine the optimal action sequence? Can the results be generalized to scenarios with an unknown time horizon *T*? Finally, can we assume *u<sup>1</sup>* > *u<sup>2</sup>* > … > *u<sup>*</sup><sub>J</sub>* without loss of generality, even in the case of an unknown context distribution?

Overall, I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
