

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint B and known time horizon T. At each round, the agent observes a context j drawn from a fixed distribution π over {1, ..., J}, selects an action k ∈ {0} ∪ {1, ..., K} (where k=0 denotes "skip"), and incurs cost c_{j,k} > 0 with reward y ∈ [0,1] (stochastically determined by j,k). The goal is to maximize cumulative reward subject to cumulative cost ≤ B. The main contribution focuses on a special case where π is known and c_{j,k} = 1 for all j,k, presenting an algorithm achieving O(log T) regret under a mild condition. The approach is extended to the general case (unknown π, arbitrary c_{j,k}), building on a known-statistics approximation algorithm for optimal action sequences.

The work is significant as prior methods (e.g., [16]) achieve only O(√T) regret. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first to establish O(log T) regret for this setting, though the mild condition (e.g., sufficient budget relative to T) must hold. The algorithm is elegant and leverages a key insight: relaxing the hard budget constraint to an average constraint (over T rounds) enables efficient optimization. This relaxation is valid under the mild condition, as the paper demonstrates via Lemma 1 (which the author clarified in the rebuttal, resolving my initial concern about the generality of the constraint relaxation). The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and makes a strong technical contribution.

The primary limitation is that the O(log T) bound relies on the mild condition. It would be significantly strengthened by either proving the bound holds unconditionally or establishing a Ω(√T) lower bound in the boundary cases where the condition fails. However, the current result already represents a notable improvement over prior work.

The technical core rests on Lemma 1, which asserts that the optimal action sequence satisfies a specific average constraint (equation (2)). While this initially seemed questionable (as the hard constraint might not align with the average over all rounds), the rebuttal provided a clear justification: the mild condition ensures that the optimal solution under the relaxed constraint never violates the hard budget constraint, making the relaxation valid for the regret analysis. I am now confident in Lemma 1's correctness.

Minor points for consideration:
1. Is the exact computation of the optimal action sequence (under known statistics) NP-hard? The paper mentions approximation but does not establish hardness.
2. Can the results be extended to unknown T? This is a natural direction for future work.
3. For the unknown π case, can the ordering assumption u_1 > u_2 > ... > u_J^* (used in Lemma 1) be made without loss of generality? The paper assumes it, but its necessity for the general case merits discussion.

The paper makes a clear, well-executed contribution to contextual bandits with budget constraints. The O(log T) regret bound is a substantial advance over existing work, and the algorithmic approach is both simple and insightful. The writing is excellent, and the technical arguments are sound (after the Lemma 1 clarification). While the mild condition is a caveat, it does not diminish the paper's novelty or significance. The minor questions raised do not detract from the core contribution. This work would be a valuable addition to NIPS 2015. I strongly recommend acceptance.
