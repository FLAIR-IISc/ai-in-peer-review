

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, where both budget and time constraints limit the agent's actions. The authors propose algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret in most cases (except boundary cases), which represents a significant advancement over previous work that achieved only O(√T) regret for similar problems. The key contribution is demonstrating that logarithmic regret is achievable in constrained contextual bandits—a problem not previously solved to this level of efficiency.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it introduces the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which approximates the oracle solution by relaxing the hard budget constraint to an average constraint. This approach achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary cases, providing a crucial stepping stone toward logarithmic regret. Second, the authors combine ALP with the UCB method to create the UCB-ALP algorithm, which achieves O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) for boundary cases. Third, they extend their results to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, demonstrating the robustness of their approach.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and thorough analysis. The authors provide clear definitions, carefully justify their assumptions, and offer detailed regret bounds for multiple scenarios. The writing is precise and well-organized, making complex concepts accessible to readers. The experimental insights (though not explicitly presented in the draft) appear to support the theoretical claims. The paper also properly situates its contribution within the literature, noting how it differs from previous work on Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17] and other budgeted bandit approaches [11-16].

The main strength of this work is its novel approach to handling the complex coupling between budget constraints and contextual decision-making. By leveraging linear programming approximations and adaptive budget allocation, the authors successfully decouple the challenging interactions between information acquisition and decision making that plague constrained bandit problems. This approach is both theoretically grounded and computationally efficient, making it valuable for practical applications.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, while the authors claim novelty in achieving logarithmic regret, the connection to standard bandit problems with action constraints could be clearer. The problem can be viewed as a bandit with JK arms (where each context-action pair is an arm), but with the constraint that only K arms are accessible at each time step. This perspective would strengthen the novelty argument and help readers better understand the contribution.

Second, the problem-dependent regret bounds could be more refined. The current bounds use O(JK log T) rather than expressions involving the inverse gaps between arm rewards, which is standard in bandit literature. While the authors acknowledge this is due to their approach using UCB ranking events rather than gap concentration, it would be valuable to explore whether such a refinement is possible.

Third, the paper lacks matching lower bounds to establish the tightness of their regret guarantees. While the authors reference the O(√T) bound for Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17], they do not provide lower bounds specific to their constrained contextual bandit setting that would confirm their upper bounds are optimal.

The paper's significance is clear for applications where budget constraints are critical, such as clinical trials and online recommendation systems. The proposed algorithms are computationally efficient, which is particularly valuable for real-world implementation. The paper's insights about approximating the oracle with linear programming could also inform future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Clarify the relationship between their problem and standard bandit problems with action constraints
2. Explore whether more refined problem-dependent regret bounds are possible
3. Provide lower bounds to establish tightness of their guarantees
4. Address the boundary cases more thoroughly, as the current analysis suggests these cases might be more frequent than implied

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the contextual bandit literature by demonstrating that logarithmic regret is achievable in constrained settings. While the novelty claim could be strengthened with clearer comparison to standard bandit formulations, the technical contributions are sound and the results are significant for both theory and practice. The paper advances the state of the art in constrained bandit problems and provides a promising direction for future research on more general settings.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors have addressed an important problem with a novel approach that yields theoretically sound and practically useful results. With minor revisions to clarify the novelty argument and refine the regret bounds, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
