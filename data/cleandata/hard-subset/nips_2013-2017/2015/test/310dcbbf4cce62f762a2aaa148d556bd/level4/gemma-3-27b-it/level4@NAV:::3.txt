This paper addresses a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint B and a known time horizon T. In each round, an agent observes a context *j* drawn from a distribution π over a set of *J* contexts, and selects an action *k* from {0, 1, ..., *K*}, where *k* = 0 represents skipping the trial (resulting in zero reward and cost).  For *k* > 0, the agent receives a reward *y* – drawn from a distribution dependent on *j* and *k* – and incurs a cost *c<sub>j,k</sub>*. The agent aims to maximize cumulative reward while ensuring the cumulative cost remains within the budget B.

The core of the paper focuses on the scenario where π is known and *c<sub>j,k</sub>* = 1 for all *j* and *k*, presenting an algorithm with an O(log T) regret bound under a specific condition. This is then extended to the more general case of unknown π and arbitrary *c<sub>j,k</sub>* values.

While related work [16] considers a similar setting, it achieves only an O(√T) regret bound. Thus, this paper potentially offers the first algorithm with O(log T) regret for this problem, contingent upon the stated condition. The algorithm leverages an approximation technique for determining the optimal action sequence given full statistical knowledge; it is a simple yet compelling approach.

The paper is well-motivated and exceptionally clearly written.  Its impact would be enhanced if the O(log T) regret guarantee were universally applicable, or if a corresponding Ω(√T) lower bound could be established for the boundary cases. 

Initially, the correctness of Lemma 1 was a concern, particularly regarding the validity of condition (2) and the justification for using an average constraint as a relaxation of the hard budget constraint. However, this has been clarified by the rebuttal and I am now satisfied with the lemma’s proof.

Minor points for consideration include: the computational complexity of finding the exact optimal action sequence (is it NP-hard?), the potential for generalization to scenarios with an unknown time horizon T, and whether the assumption *u<sup>1</sup>* > *u<sup>2</sup>* > ... > *u<sup>*</sup><sub>J</sub>* can be made without loss of generality even when the context distribution is unknown. 

Overall, I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper.
