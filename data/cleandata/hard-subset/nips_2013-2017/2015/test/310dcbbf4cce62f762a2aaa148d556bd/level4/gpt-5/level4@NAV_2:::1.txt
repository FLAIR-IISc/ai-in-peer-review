Review after rebuttal:
- I acknowledge it is not strictly the same, but it still seems sufficiently close: as the horizon grows, inter-context coupling effectively fades. Given that, I do not see why each arrival of a context cannot be handled as an independent bandit instance.
- In my opinion, the paper would be much stronger with a clean, problem-dependent upper bound (the supplementary bound needs to be reworked) expressed in terms of arm gaps, and ideally matching lower bounds in both instance-dependent and instance-independent regimes.

----------------------
The paper addresses contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints. The central contribution is that with a finite number of contexts and actions, and for sufficiently large time horizon T (relative to the instance), one can attain O(log T) regret—a new guarantee in contextual bandits.

The results appear thorough and the exposition is careful. The findings are interesting, though not particularly surprising. With finite J contexts and K actions, the setting can be viewed as a time- and budget-constrained bandit with JK arms and an action-access constraint (only the K arms associated with the realized context are available each round). For a finite arm set and large T, a O(log T) regret rate aligns with results for bandits with action constraints. I am therefore unsure about the extent of novelty—clarification would help.

Questions:
- In Theorem 2, case (2), the regret is stated as O(sqrt(T) + …). Is it truly O(sqrt(T) + …)? Shouldn’t it be at least O(sqrt(KT) + …)?
- Also in Theorem 2, the regret is O(KJ log T) or O(sqrt(T) + KJ log T) depending on the instance. Typically, instance-dependent bounds are given in a more refined form involving sums over inverse gap terms. Can a similar gap-dependent characterization be provided here? I understand KJ appears (instead of a sum over gaps) because the proof is organized around events ensuring correct UCB orderings rather than gap-based concentration, but is that approach optimal?
- It would be valuable to have lower bounds for this setting, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. Trivial reductions recover classical bandit lower bounds (e.g., zero cost and a single context). Can these be sharpened to reflect contextual complexity?

I still find the setup very close to budgeted bandits with action constraints, and hence I remain unconvinced about the originality. Consequently, my overall recommendation remains neutral.
