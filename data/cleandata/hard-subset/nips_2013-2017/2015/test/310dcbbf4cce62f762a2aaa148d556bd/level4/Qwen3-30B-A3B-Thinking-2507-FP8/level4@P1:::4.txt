

This paper makes a significant contribution to constrained contextual bandits by establishing logarithmic regret guarantees under specific conditions, which represents an important advancement over prior work achieving only sublinear regret. The authors propose a novel Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approach that approximates the optimal oracle solution while maintaining computational efficiency, and then combine this with UCB to handle unknown reward expectations.

The key insight is that ALP's ability to tolerate estimation errors in reward rankings enables a clean separation between information acquisition (handled by UCB) and decision making (handled by ALP). This decoupling is particularly elegant, as it allows the authors to achieve O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases, whereas previous approaches for constrained bandits [17, 20, 21] achieved only O(√T) regret. The paper's technical analysis is rigorous, with careful handling of the budget constraint through adaptive linear programming that dynamically adjusts to the remaining budget and time.

However, I have a substantial concern about the boundary case analysis. The paper distinguishes between "boundary cases" where ρ = q_j for some j and "non-boundary cases" where ρ ≠ q_j for all j, with the regret being O(√T) in boundary cases but O(log T) otherwise. The authors do not provide sufficient intuition for why these cases need special treatment, especially for j > 1. The condition ρ = q_j appears to be a technical artifact of the regret analysis rather than a fundamental property of the problem, and this distinction seems arbitrary without further explanation. The authors should clarify why these boundary cases arise and whether they represent a meaningful structural difference in the problem rather than a mathematical artifact of their analysis.

The paper properly situates itself against prior work, particularly [17] on Resourceful Contextual Bandits, and clearly demonstrates how their approach improves upon previous results. The technical contributions are well-motivated and the theoretical analysis is thorough, with the authors carefully addressing the challenges of the constrained contextual bandit problem. The paper also makes a compelling case for the practical relevance of their approach in applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing where budget constraints are critical.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with the mathematical exposition being precise and accessible to readers familiar with contextual bandits. The authors have effectively communicated their key insights about how to handle the complex interaction between exploration and exploitation under budget constraints.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field with clear theoretical advances and practical implications. The boundary case analysis is the only significant weakness, but it doesn't undermine the paper's overall importance. The paper's approach to decoupling information acquisition from decision making using ALP represents a valuable methodological contribution that could inform future work on constrained bandit problems. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the boundary case analysis.
