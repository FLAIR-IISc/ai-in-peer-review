Summary
This paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints. At each round, a context j is drawn i.i.d. from a finite set with known distribution π (in the main results), the agent may either act (k > 0) and incur a known cost cj,k > 0 while receiving a reward in [0,1] with mean uj,k, or skip (k = 0). The total cost must never exceed a fixed budget B over a known horizon T. The goal is to maximize expected cumulative reward.

The core contribution is an adaptive linear-programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle policy when statistics are known. In the unit-cost case (cj,k = 1), the ALP uses an LP relaxation with an average budget constraint and adapts the effective budget ratio to the remaining budget/time bτ/τ. ALP requires only the ordering of the best expected rewards per context u∗j and achieves O(1) regret except on boundary parameter values (where ρ = B/T coincides with certain cumulative context probabilities qj), in which case regret is O(√T). Building on this, the authors propose UCB-ALP for unknown rewards by combining ALP with UCB to learn action values. UCB-ALP attains O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on the boundary.

The paper further discusses extensions: (i) unknown context distribution via empirical estimation (EALP and UCB-EALP), showing similar behavior with a truncated update scheme and concentration arguments; and (ii) heterogeneous costs, where an LP over context–action pairs with a per-context single-action constraint yields ALP-type policies with analogous regret guarantees, and an exploration-first approach handles unknown rewards.

Relation to prior work
The setting connects to “resourceful contextual bandits” and bandits with knapsacks. Prior work such as Mixture Elimination [17] and the concave-reward/convex-knapsack approaches [20–22] yield O(√T) regret, often over finite policy sets or with static contexts. This paper appears to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts/actions and i.i.d. contexts, leveraging the adaptive LP relaxation.

Quality
The technical approach is sound and carefully motivated. The ALP analysis hinges on bounding deviations of the remaining budget via a “sampling without replacement” view and mapping to hypergeometric concentration; the extension to UCB-ALP cleanly separates regret from action-ranking errors and context-threshold errors. I initially questioned Lemma 1 (the LP-based upper bound), but the rebuttal clarified why the average constraint indeed relaxes the hard budget, and I am now convinced.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. A brief intuitive justification of Lemma 1 in the main text (not only the appendix) would further aid readers. It would also help to state the computational complexity of solving the small LPs per round and to summarize assumptions (unique best action per context, strict ordering u∗1 > … > u∗J) upfront and how ties could be handled.

Originality
The adaptive LP relaxation and the observation that ALP needs only a correct ordering of u∗j are novel and practically useful. Combining this with UCB to obtain O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases is original in this constrained, contextual setting.

Significance
Achieving logarithmic regret with a computationally simple method under budget constraints is impactful and likely to influence subsequent algorithm design for constrained contextual bandits. The extensions to unknown π and heterogeneous costs further increase relevance.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation of constrained contextual bandits.
- ALP provides a simple, computationally efficient approximation with O(1)/O(√T) regret.
- UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret (non-boundary) and is conceptually clean.
- Careful regret decomposition and concentration analysis.
- Extensions to unknown π and heterogeneous costs.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Boundary cases remain at O(√T); it would strengthen the paper to either (a) prove a matching lower bound in these cases or (b) propose refinements that improve the rate.
- The main results assume known T; discussion of unknown or adversarial horizons (e.g., doubling tricks or anytime variants) would improve practicality.
- Clarify whether computing the exact oracle is NP-hard or merely intractable due to DP’s curse of dimensionality; a formal complexity statement would be useful.
- Discuss whether the strict ordering assumptions (unique best action per context and u∗1 > … > u∗J) can be removed without changing rates, and how ties are handled in the algorithms and analysis.
- For the unknown π case, it would help to provide a concise, high-level statement of the regret bound for the truncated EALP/UCB-EALP in the main text.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- First O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits under standard finite assumptions.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with clear intuition.
- Strong, careful analysis; compelling empirical/analytical narrative.
Cons:
- Suboptimal rate in boundary cases without lower bounds.
- Some assumptions (known T, strict orderings) could be relaxed or discussed more fully.

Questions for the authors
- Is computing the exact oracle policy NP-hard? Can you provide a formal complexity result?
- How would you adapt ALP/UCB-ALP when T is unknown (e.g., anytime version or doubling trick), and would the regret guarantees persist?
- Can the strict ordering assumptions be removed cleanly, especially with unknown π, without changing the regret rates?

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper presents a clean and influential approach that achieves logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, with solid theory and practical appeal.
