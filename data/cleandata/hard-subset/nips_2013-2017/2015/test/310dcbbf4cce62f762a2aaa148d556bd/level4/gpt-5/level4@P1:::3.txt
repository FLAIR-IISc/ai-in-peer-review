This paper studies contextual bandits under both time and budget constraints. At each round t ≤ T, a context j ∈ {1,…,J} is drawn i.i.d. from a known or unknown distribution π, the learner may either skip (action 0) or choose an action k ∈ {1,…,K}, receives a reward in [0,1] with mean u_{j,k}, and incurs cost c_{j,k} > 0. The process stops when either the horizon T is reached or the total cost hits the budget B. The goal is to maximize expected cumulative reward subject to the hard budget constraint.

Main contributions:
- Unit-cost, known-π setting: The paper proposes an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) policy. It first introduces a static LP upper bound Û(T,B) by relaxing the hard budget constraint to an average budget ρ = B/T, then adapts the LP at each round to the current average remaining budget b_τ/τ. When expected rewards are known, ALP achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary cases (ρ ≠ ∑_{j′≤j} π_{j′}) and O(√T) in boundary cases.
- Unknown-reward setting: UCB-ALP combines ALP with UCB estimates to learn the action rankings. It achieves logarithmic regret O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases.
- Extensions: The approach is extended to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates) and to heterogeneous costs, where an ε-First ALP variant is proposed.

Relation to prior work: Budget constraints have been studied in non-contextual bandits and in more general resourceful contextual bandits; however, prior algorithms typically achieve O(√T) regret or target finite policy classes. By leveraging an adaptive LP relaxation and an instance-dependent analysis for finite context/action sets, this paper appears to be the first to obtain O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits (under non-boundary conditions).

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: Approximating the oracle with an adaptive LP that tracks the average remaining budget is simple, intuitive, and computationally efficient.
- Theoretical guarantees: The paper proves O(1) regret for ALP with known rewards (non-boundary) and O(JK log T) regret for UCB-ALP (non-boundary), with careful treatment of boundary cases.
- Technical novelties: The analysis connects ALP’s budget dynamics to sampling without replacement, yielding concentration of b_τ/τ, and decomposes regret due to action and context ranking errors under UCB.
- Practical relevance: The setting is motivated by realistic budgeted applications (e.g., clinical trials, crowdsourcing).

Weaknesses and questions:
- Boundary cases: The regret is O(√T) when ρ hits the context cumulative-probability boundaries. It would substantially strengthen the paper to either (i) obtain O(log T) regret in these cases or (ii) prove a matching Ω(√T) lower bound showing the boundary hardness.
- Dependence on strict ordering: The analysis assumes u*_1 > … > u*_J (best expected rewards per context strictly ordered). Please clarify whether this can be enforced without loss of generality (e.g., via deterministic or randomized tie-breaking), especially when π is unknown and estimated.
- Oracle tractability: The paper argues dynamic programming is computationally prohibitive. Is computing the exact oracle policy NP-hard in the general finite-context/action, hard-budget setting? A formal hardness statement (or a discussion referencing known hardness) would better justify the approximation route.
- Unknown horizon: Can the results be extended to unknown T (e.g., via doubling or anytime confidence bounds) without worsening regret guarantees?

Quality:
- Technical soundness appears strong. Many results hinge on Lemma 1 (the LP upper bound via average-budget relaxation). I was initially unconvinced that the oracle’s hard constraint implies feasibility of the average-constraint LP at all times; however, the rebuttal clarified this, and I am now convinced. I recommend adding a brief intuitive explanation in the main text to guide readers unfamiliar with this relaxation.
- The regret analyses for ALP and UCB-ALP are well structured, with clear separation of budget fluctuation and ranking-error contributions.

Clarity:
- The paper is well organized and clearly written. The intuition behind ALP and UCB-ALP is presented cleanly, and the boundary/non-boundary distinction is explicit. Including a short proof sketch or intuition for Lemma 1 in the main text would improve readability.

Originality:
- The use of an adaptive LP relaxation to decouple decision-making from learning in budgeted contextual bandits is novel and insightful. Achieving O(log T) regret in this setting (non-boundary) is, to my knowledge, new.

Significance:
- The results advance the state of the art for constrained contextual bandits and may inform algorithms for broader resource-constrained learning problems.

Pros for acceptance:
- First O(log T) regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits under reasonable assumptions.
- Simple, efficient algorithms with clear intuition and rigorous analysis.
- Extensions to unknown π and heterogeneous costs.

Cons for acceptance:
- O(√T) regret in boundary cases without a matching lower bound.
- Some assumptions (strict ordering) could be discussed more thoroughly.
- Lack of formal hardness results for the oracle.

Minor comments:
- Please clarify the computational complexity of the exact oracle (DP) and whether NP-hardness can be established.
- Discuss how to make the algorithms horizon-free (unknown T).
- Clarify whether assuming u*_1 > … > u*_J can be made without loss of generality when π is unknown and estimated.

Overall recommendation: Strong accept. The paper is well motivated, technically solid, and offers a clean and effective approach that yields state-of-the-art regret bounds in a practically important setting.
