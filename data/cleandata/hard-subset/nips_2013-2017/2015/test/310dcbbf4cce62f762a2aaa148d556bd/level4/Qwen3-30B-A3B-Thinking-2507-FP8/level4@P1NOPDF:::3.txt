

This paper addresses a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint $B$ and known time horizon $T$. At each trial, the agent observes a context $j$ drawn from a fixed distribution $\pi$ over $\{1, \dots, J\}$, then selects an action $k \in \{0\} \cup \{1, \dots, K\}$, where $k=0$ denotes "skip." When $k=0$, the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost. For $k>0$, the agent receives a reward $y \in [0,1]$ (drawn from a distribution depending on $j$ and $k$) and incurs a known cost $c_{j,k} > 0$. The goal is to maximize cumulative reward subject to the constraint that cumulative cost never exceeds $B$.

The core contribution focuses on the special case where $\pi$ is known and $c_{j,k} = 1$ for all $j,k$, presenting an algorithm achieving $O(\log T)$ regret under a mild condition. The paper extends this to the general case with unknown $\pi$ and arbitrary $c_{j,k}$. While a more general setting is studied in [16] (with $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret), this appears to be the first work achieving $O(\log T)$ regret, provided the mild condition holds. The algorithm leverages an approximation for the optimal action sequence under full statistical knowledge, resulting in a simple yet elegant approach.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. Its significance would be substantially enhanced if the $O(\log T)$ regret bound held universally, rather than conditionally, especially given the $\Omega(\sqrt{T})$ lower bound in boundary cases. 

All results rely critically on Lemma 1, which initially raised concerns regarding its correctness. Specifically, I questioned whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies (2) for all trials, and thus why the average constraint constitutes a relaxation of the hard constraint. I am now convinced of Lemma 1's validity following the authors' rebuttal; thank you for the clarification.

Minor points for consideration:  
1. Is computing the exact optimal action sequence NP-hard?  
2. Can the results be extended to unknown $T$?  
3. In the unknown $\pi$ case, can we assume $u_1 > u_2 > \dots > u_J^*$ without loss of generality?  

The work makes a strong contribution to contextual bandits under budget constraints, with a clear algorithm and rigorous analysis. I strongly recommend acceptance.
