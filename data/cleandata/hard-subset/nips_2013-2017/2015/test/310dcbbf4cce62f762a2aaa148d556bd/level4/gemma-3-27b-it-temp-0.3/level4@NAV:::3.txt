This paper addresses a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint *B* and a known time horizon *T*. In each round, an agent observes a context *j* sampled from a fixed distribution π over a set of *J* contexts, and selects an action *k* from {0, 1, ..., *K*}, where *k* = 0 represents skipping the trial (resulting in zero reward and cost).  For *k* > 0, the agent receives a reward *y* drawn from a distribution dependent on *j* and *k*, and incurs a cost *c<sub>j,k</sub>* > 0, which is known. The objective is to maximize cumulative reward while ensuring the cumulative cost remains within the budget *B*.

The core of the paper focuses on a simplified scenario where π is known and *c<sub>j,k</sub>* = 1 for all *j* and *k*, presenting an algorithm with a regret bound of O(log *T*) under a specified condition. This algorithm is then extended to handle the more general case of unknown π and arbitrary *c<sub>j,k</sub>* values.

While related work [16] considers a similar setting, it achieves a regret of O(√*T*).  Therefore, this paper potentially represents the first work to achieve O(log *T*) regret in this context, contingent upon the satisfaction of the stated condition. The proposed algorithm leverages an approximation technique for determining the optimal action sequence given complete statistical knowledge, and is notable for its simplicity and interesting approach.

The paper is well-motivated and exceptionally clear in its presentation.  Further strengthening the work would involve demonstrating that the O(log *T*) regret holds universally, or alternatively, proving a corresponding Ω(√*T*) lower bound for cases where the condition is not met. 

Initially, the correctness of Lemma 1 was a concern, specifically regarding whether the optimal action sequence consistently satisfies equation (2) and whether the averaged constraint accurately relaxes the hard constraint. However, this concern has been addressed and clarified by the authors’ rebuttal.

Minor points for consideration include: the computational complexity of exactly finding the optimal action sequence (specifically, whether it is NP-hard); the potential for generalizing the results to scenarios with an unknown time horizon *T*; and whether the assumption *u<sup>1</sup>* > *u<sup>2</sup>* > ... > *u<sup>*</sup><sub>J</sub>* can be made without loss of generality even when the context distribution is unknown.

Overall, I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
