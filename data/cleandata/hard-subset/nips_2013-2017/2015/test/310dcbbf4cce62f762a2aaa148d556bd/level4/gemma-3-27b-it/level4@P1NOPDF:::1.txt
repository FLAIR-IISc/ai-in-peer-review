The authors address the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit. Their main contribution is a proof that a regret of order \log(T) is achievable when the number of contexts and actions is finite, and the time horizon T is sufficiently large relative to the problem parameters. This is a novel result for contextual bandits. 

The results appear thorough and the writing is precise. However, the findings are not particularly surprising. Given a finite number of contexts J and arms K, this bandit problem can be viewed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms, subject to the action constraint that only K arms are accessible for each context. For a sufficiently large T, the regret should scale as \log(T), as is common in action-constrained bandit problems. Consequently, the paper’s novelty is unclear – I encourage the authors to address this point in their rebuttal.

I have several questions regarding the paper:

– In Theorem 2, the regret in case 2 is O(\sqrt{T} + …). Is this correct? It seems it should be at least O(\sqrt{KT} + …).
– Still regarding Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ \log(T)) or O(\sqrt{T} + KJ\log(T)), depending on the problem configuration. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are expressed with greater precision using the sum of inverse arm gaps. Is a similar approach possible here? I understand that the proof relies on events characterizing the correct ordering of UCB values, rather than concentration bounds on the gaps themselves, leading to the KJ term. However, is this approach optimal?
– It would be valuable to include lower bounds for this problem, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. Trivial lower bounds correspond to the classical bandit problem (cost = 0 and a constant context). Can this be refined to account for the complexity introduced by the context?

I remain unconvinced regarding the originality of this setting, and therefore maintain a neutral assessment of this paper. 

I have the impression that this setting is very similar to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints. Consequently, the degree of innovation is unclear – I would appreciate clarification from the authors in their rebuttal.
