

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, a significant extension of standard contextual bandit problems that captures important real-world limitations like limited resources in clinical trials or crowdsourcing. The authors make a clear contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting under simplified but practical assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint), which to the best of their knowledge is the first such result in the literature. They build upon prior work on resourceful contextual bandits [17] and non-contextual bandits with budget constraints [11-16], but overcome key limitations of these approaches by developing a computationally efficient algorithm that achieves optimal regret.

The paper's main technical contribution is the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which approximates the oracle solution by relaxing the hard budget constraint to an average budget constraint. The authors prove that ALP achieves O(1) regret (i.e., within a constant of the optimum) except for certain boundary cases. They then combine ALP with the UCB method to create the UCB-ALP algorithm, which achieves O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases (where J is the number of contexts and K the number of actions) and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. This represents a significant improvement over prior work like the Mixture Elimination algorithm [17], which achieves O(√T) regret, and over recent extensions [20-22] that also achieve O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear exposition that makes the technical content accessible. The authors effectively connect their approach to the literature by carefully distinguishing their contributions from prior work on contextual bandits [1,2,3,5,6,7] and budget-constrained bandits [11-16]. They demonstrate a thorough understanding of the field by discussing related work in detail and explaining how their approach differs.

The technical contributions are strong, with rigorous proofs for all key results. The ALP approximation is particularly clever, as it only requires the ordering of expected rewards rather than their exact values, making it highly compatible with UCB methods. The regret analysis is non-trivial, addressing the complex interactions between information acquisition and decision making that characterize constrained contextual bandits. The authors provide a clear sketch of the analysis, identifying the key challenges and how they overcome them.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of constrained contextual bandits by showing how to decouple the information acquisition and decision making processes through adaptive linear relaxation. This insight could prove useful for future work on more general constrained bandit settings.

Minor suggestions for improvement: 
1) In Section 4.1, the authors could more explicitly state that their approach follows the "optimism under uncertainty" principle, which is fundamental to UCB algorithms and makes the combination with ALP theoretically sound. The connection to UCRL2 [8] could be clarified, as the authors hint at it but do not develop the relationship.

2) The paper would be strengthened by including numerical experiments demonstrating the algorithm's performance, particularly in comparison to alternative approaches. The authors mention this as a future direction but it would be valuable to see at least preliminary results.

3) For large context spaces, using a parametric model for the reward function (as suggested in the draft review) would be a valuable extension, as the current approach scales with the number of contexts. The authors could briefly discuss how their framework might accommodate such models.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS, as it makes a clear technical contribution to a well-motivated problem, provides rigorous analysis, and offers practical algorithms with computational efficiency. The authors have clearly demonstrated how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under reasonable assumptions, which is a significant advancement over previous work. The regret bounds they achieve are optimal for non-boundary cases, and the boundary cases are handled gracefully with O(√T) regret, which is still better than previous approaches.

The paper merits acceptance as it provides a substantial improvement over existing work on constrained contextual bandits, with a clear technical contribution that advances the state of the art. The results are scientifically sound, the writing is excellent, and the contribution is significant for both theoretical understanding and practical applications in resource-constrained learning scenarios. The authors have successfully addressed a challenging problem in a novel and effective way, making their work a valuable addition to the NIPS literature.
