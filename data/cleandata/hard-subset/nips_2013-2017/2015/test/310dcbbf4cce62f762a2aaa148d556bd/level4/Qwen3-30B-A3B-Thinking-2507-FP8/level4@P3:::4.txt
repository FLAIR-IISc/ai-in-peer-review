

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, a variant of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approach to approximate the oracle policy and combine it with the Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) method to develop the UCB-ALP algorithm. The main contribution is showing that this algorithm achieves logarithmic regret (O(log T)) for most cases, with O(√T) regret in boundary cases, which represents a significant improvement over previous O(√T) regret bounds in the constrained bandit literature.

The paper builds upon recent work on contextual bandits [1, 2, 3, 7] and constrained bandits [11-16]. It specifically addresses the gap in the literature regarding contextual bandits with budget constraints, noting that previous work on budget constraints (e.g., [11-16]) applies only to non-contextual bandits and is inapplicable to the contextual setting. The authors correctly identify Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] as the most relevant prior work, which achieves O(√T) regret but with a restricted benchmark and high computational complexity. The paper also contrasts with [20], which achieves O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts but uses a fixed budget constraint rather than the adaptive constraint employed in this work.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The ALP approximation is rigorously analyzed, showing O(1) regret except for boundary cases. The UCB-ALP algorithm is well-motivated and the regret analysis for the unknown statistics case is thorough, addressing complex interactions between context ranking errors, remaining budget fluctuations, and random context arrivals. The authors provide a clear sketch of the regret analysis, demonstrating how they partition the regret into manageable components and bound each part. The paper also extends their results to unknown context distributions (EALP) and heterogeneous costs, showing the robustness of their approach.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem is well-defined, the key insights are clearly articulated, and the algorithms are presented with sufficient detail. The paper effectively explains the intuition behind ALP (using linear programming to approximate the oracle while adapting to the remaining budget) and how this enables the combination with UCB. The theoretical analysis is presented in a logical sequence that builds upon the unit-cost case before extending to more general settings.

The originality of the work is significant. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first paper to demonstrate logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. The ALP approximation, which only requires the ordering of expected rewards rather than their exact values, represents a novel insight that enables the combination with UCB. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly [17] and [20], by addressing the contextual setting with a more natural benchmark and achieving a better regret bound.

The significance of this work is substantial. Many real-world applications (clinical trials, online recommendation, crowdsourcing) involve both contextual information and budget constraints. The paper's results provide a theoretically sound foundation for addressing these practical challenges. The computational efficiency of the proposed algorithms (UCB-ALP) makes them particularly appealing for implementation, and the insights about decoupling information acquisition and decision making could influence future work on constrained bandits.

Strengths of the paper include:
- The novel ALP approach to approximate the oracle policy
- Rigorous regret analysis showing logarithmic regret for non-boundary cases
- Clear differentiation from prior work with precise technical comparisons
- Well-structured theoretical development with logical progression
- Practical relevance to important real-world applications

Weaknesses include:
- The analysis of boundary cases (where ρ = q_j for some j) is somewhat opaque. While the paper provides a detailed analysis, the reason why these cases require special treatment isn't intuitively clear from the main text. The authors could improve the exposition by more explicitly explaining why ρ = q_j represents a special case where the threshold changes discontinuously.
- The paper lacks experimental results to validate the theoretical findings, which would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

Arguments for acceptance:
- This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to contextual bandit literature by achieving logarithmic regret in constrained settings.
- The ALP approach is both theoretically sound and computationally efficient.
- The paper clearly demonstrates how it differs from and improves upon prior work.
- The problem is practically important, and the results could directly impact real-world applications.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The boundary cases analysis could be clarified for better understanding.
- The absence of experimental validation is a minor weakness for a NIPS paper that typically values both theoretical and empirical contributions.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the contextual bandit literature. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The only minor weakness is the lack of experimental validation, which is understandable given the paper's focus on theoretical analysis. The paper's contribution to achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits represents a meaningful advance over prior work, making it highly suitable for NIPS.
