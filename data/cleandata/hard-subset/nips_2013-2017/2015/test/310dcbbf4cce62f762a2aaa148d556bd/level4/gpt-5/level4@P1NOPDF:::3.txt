This paper studies a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint B and a known horizon T. On each round, a context j is drawn from a fixed distribution π over {1,…,J}. The agent then chooses an action k from {0} ∪ {1,…,K}, where k=0 means “skip.” If k=0, the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost. If k>0, the agent receives a reward y and incurs a cost c_{j,k}, where y is drawn from a fixed distribution on [0,1] that depends on (j,k), and c_{j,k}>0 is known to the agent. The goal is to maximize cumulative reward subject to the cumulative cost never exceeding B.

The main focus is a special case where π is known and c_{j,k}=1 for all j,k. For this case, the paper proposes an algorithm with O(log T) regret under a mild condition, and then extends the approach to the general case where π is unknown and the costs c_{j,k} are arbitrary. A more general setting is considered in [16], but that work achieves only O(√T) regret. Thus, this paper appears to be the first to obtain O(log T) regret (under the stated condition). The algorithm builds on an approximation algorithm for computing the optimal action sequence when all statistics are known; it is simple yet quite interesting.

The paper is well motivated and clearly written. It would be even more impactful if O(log T) regret could be shown to hold without the additional condition; otherwise, it would be valuable to establish an Ω(√T) lower bound in the boundary cases where the condition fails.

Much of the analysis seems to rest on Lemma 1. Initially, I was not convinced of its correctness: I did not believe the optimal action sequence necessarily satisfies condition (2) on every round, and therefore I did not see why the average constraint is a relaxation of the hard budget constraint. After reading the rebuttal, I am now convinced by Lemma 1. Thank you for the clarification.

Minor comments and questions:
- Is it NP-hard to compute the exact optimal action sequence?
- Can the results be extended to the setting with unknown T (e.g., via a doubling trick), and would the same regret rates be preserved?
- Can we assume u^1 > u^2 > … > u^*_J without loss of generality even when the context distribution π is unknown?

I strongly recommend acceptance.
