This is a short review, and my primary question concerns the justification for analyzing boundary cases differently. It’s not immediately clear why ρ = q<sub>j</sub> should be treated as special, particularly for j > 1. This paper analyzes contextual bandits against a strong baseline – one that employs adaptive linear programming to strategically abstain from acting on a certain percentage of suboptimal rounds (referred to by the authors as a “budget”). The setup is interesting, and the proposed solutions are unique. 

The motivation for introducing the budget constraint is well-explained, and the connection to adaptive linear programming provides a rigorous framework for comparison. The authors clearly articulate the trade-off between exploration and exploitation within this constrained setting. I appreciate the clarity with which they define the problem and establish the baseline.

However, the analysis of the proposed algorithm feels somewhat limited. While the theoretical results demonstrate performance guarantees, the constant factors hidden within the asymptotic notation are not discussed. A more detailed discussion of these constants would be beneficial, as they could significantly impact the practical applicability of the algorithm. Specifically, how does the required budget influence the observed regret in practice?

Furthermore, the empirical evaluation, while showing promising results, lacks a comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art contextual bandit algorithms *without* a budget constraint. Demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods, even when accounting for the budget, would strengthen the paper's contribution. It’s possible the budget introduces an unnecessary overhead, and this should be investigated.

The presentation is generally good, but there are a few instances where the notation could be clarified. For example, the meaning of certain symbols is not immediately obvious and requires referring back to earlier definitions. A more consistent use of terminology throughout the paper would also improve readability.

Overall, this is a solid paper with a novel approach to contextual bandit problems. The use of adaptive linear programming as a baseline is a particularly strong point. Addressing the questions regarding the practical impact of the constant factors and providing a more thorough empirical comparison would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. The question about the special treatment of boundary cases remains a key concern and should be addressed.
