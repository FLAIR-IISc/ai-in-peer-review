Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints (“constrained contextual bandits”), where taking an action incurs cost and the process stops when either time or budget is exhausted. The main idea is to approximate the dynamic-programming oracle with a linear program (LP) that enforces only an average budget constraint. Building on this, the authors propose Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP), which at each step solves the LP using the current average remaining budget. In unit-cost settings with known context distribution and known rewards, ALP achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary cases (and O(√T) on a measure-zero set of boundary parameters).

When rewards are unknown, ALP is combined with UCB to form UCB-ALP. Leveraging the fact that ALP needs only the ordering of expected rewards, UCB-ALP attains O(JK log T) regret for non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. The paper also sketches extensions to unknown context distributions (via empirical estimates) and to heterogeneous costs, where similar guarantees (O(1) or O(√T) in boundary cases) are obtained, and proposes a simple explore-then-ALP scheme when rewards are unknown. The approach is computationally efficient and contrasts with more general but heavier algorithms such as Mixture Elimination for resourceful contextual bandits.

Relation to prior work
- Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) (Badanidiyuru et al., COLT’14) achieve O(√T) regret with a finite policy set and high computational cost; here the authors obtain logarithmic regret (outside boundary cases) for finite contexts/actions with a direct comparison to the oracle.
- Bandits with budgets/knapsacks (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al.; Combes et al.) and concave rewards with convex knapsacks (Agrawal & Devanur) typically yield O(√T) regret; those results either lack observable context or use static/adaptive budget rules that do not exploit adaptive average-budget updates per round.
- The paper’s adaptive LP relaxation appears to be the key to improving regret to logarithmic in this constrained contextual setting.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clean LP relaxation that doubles as both an analyzable upper bound and a practical decision rule (ALP), with sharp regret guarantees. The mapping to “sampling without replacement” for budget dynamics is elegant and yields useful concentration.
- Novelty and significance: To my knowledge, this is the first demonstration of logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits with random contexts under finite contexts/actions.
- Computational efficiency: ALP/UCB-ALP are lightweight (solve a small LP or its closed-form each round), a clear improvement over prior general methods.
- Clarity: The paper is well organized and generally easy to follow. The decomposition of regret into action-ranking and context-ranking components is insightful.

Weaknesses and points to clarify
- Boundary cases: The regret degrades to O(√T). It would be helpful to discuss whether this is an inherent barrier (e.g., via lower bounds) or an artifact of analysis.
- Assumptions: Main results assume known context distribution (later relaxed with a truncated empirical variant) and finite discrete contexts/actions. The unknown-distribution analysis relies on stopping updates, which may be conservative in practice.
- Scalability in J and K: The regret scales as O(JK log T). For large context/action spaces, structure (e.g., linear or parametric models) could reduce this dependence.
- Algorithmic rationale: Section 4.1 could more explicitly tie UCB-ALP to optimism under uncertainty and clarify how the UCB ordering interacts with the ALP thresholding (the connection is reminiscent of UCRL2’s optimistic planning).
- Empirics: While the theory is strong, additional experiments (beyond what may be in the supplement) would help illustrate robustness to model misspecification, boundary cases, and the unknown-distribution setting.

Quality
- Soundness: The analyses—LP upper bound, concentration of remaining budget, and UCB-based ranking error bounds—are convincing, with careful handling of context and budget randomness.
- Completeness: Core settings are thoroughly covered; extensions are plausible but less detailed.
- Evaluation: Empirical validation could be strengthened to complement the theory.

Clarity
- The writing is clear and well structured. Improving the exposition in Section 4.1 (motivating UCB-ALP via optimism) and explicitly stating per-round computational cost would further help.

Originality
- The adaptive LP relaxation for constrained contextual bandits and its combination with UCB to achieve logarithmic regret (outside boundary cases) appear novel and impactful relative to prior constrained bandit literature.

Significance
- The work advances both theory and practice for constrained contextual bandits, offering an efficient and analyzable alternative likely to be used and extended.

Suggestions for improvement
- Consider parametric/structured reward models (e.g., linear or GLMs) to handle large J or continuous contexts while preserving adaptive budget control.
- Clarify the optimism principle in UCB-ALP and its relation to UCRL2-style updates; provide guidance on exploration constants and sensitivity.
- Discuss impossibility or lower bounds in boundary cases if known, or outline potential techniques to mitigate the √T term.
- Expand experiments to cover heterogeneous costs, unknown distribution (truncation vs. continuous updating), and sensitivity to mis-specified π.
- Please cite and discuss closely related work on contextual bandits with global constraints, e.g., Agrawal & Devanur (arXiv:1506.03374; 1507.06738), and contrast their regret and assumptions with your adaptive approach.

Pros
- First logarithmic-regret algorithm (outside boundary cases) for constrained contextual bandits with random contexts.
- Simple, computationally efficient methods with clear theoretical underpinnings.
- Useful analytical techniques (budget concentration via sampling-without-replacement).

Cons
- Degradation to O(√T) in boundary cases, with unclear tightness.
- Dependence on known π in the main result and on finite discrete spaces.
- Limited empirical evidence in the main text to validate practical performance.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper provides a clean, efficient, and theoretically strong approach to constrained contextual bandits, achieving near-optimal regret in most cases and improving over prior work in both regret and computational tractability.
