- Studies contextual bandits with time and budget constraints where contexts are i.i.d. and actions incur known costs and unknown rewards, aiming to maximize cumulative reward under budget B and horizon T.
- Core contribution: adaptive linear-programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle policy using an LP relaxation with an average budget constraint and adapting to remaining budget/time ratio.
- For unit-cost case, ALP needs only the ordering of best expected rewards per context and achieves O(1) regret except O(√T) on boundary parameter values.
- UCB-ALP combines ALP with UCB to learn rewards and attains O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on boundaries.
- Extensions: unknown context distribution handled via empirical estimation (EALP and UCB-EALP) with truncated updates and concentration arguments.
- Extensions: heterogeneous costs handled by an LP over context–action pairs and an exploration-first approach for unknown rewards, with analogous regret guarantees.
- Novelty: first to achieve logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits under finite contexts/actions and i.i.d. contexts using an adaptive LP relaxation.
- Technical approach is sound, with ALP analysis using a sampling-without-replacement view and hypergeometric concentration; regret decomposition cleanly separates error sources.
- Reviewer initially questioned Lemma 1 but was convinced by rebuttal justification for the average constraint relaxation.
- Clarity is good overall; suggests adding an intuitive justification of Lemma 1 in the main text and stating LP solve complexity and assumptions up front.
- Originality: ALP needing only ordering of u* and combining with UCB for O(log T) regret is novel and practically useful.
- Significance: achieving logarithmic regret with a simple, computationally light method is impactful and likely influential.
- Strengths: clear problem formulation, simple efficient ALP, strong UCB-ALP results, careful analysis, and relevant extensions.
- Weaknesses: boundary cases remain O(√T) without matching lower bounds or refinements to improve rate.
- Practicality concerns: main results assume known T; need discussion of anytime variants or doubling tricks.
- Complexity clarity needed: whether computing exact oracle is NP-hard or merely intractable due to DP curse of dimensionality.
- Assumption clarity needed: whether strict ordering and unique best-action assumptions can be relaxed and how ties are handled.
- For unknown π, recommend a concise high-level regret statement for truncated EALP/UCB-EALP in the main text.
- Pros for acceptance: first O(log T) result, simple implementable algorithms, strong analysis and narrative.
- Cons for acceptance: suboptimal boundary rates and some assumptions (known T, strict orderings) need fuller treatment.
- Key questions for authors: is computing the exact oracle NP-hard; how to adapt algorithms when T is unknown and would guarantees persist; can strict ordering assumptions be removed without changing rates.
- Recommendation: Strong accept — clean, influential approach with solid theory and practical appeal.
