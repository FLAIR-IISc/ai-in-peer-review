The authors consider the problem of a contextual bandit with both budget and time constraints. Their main result is that for a finite number of contexts and actions, a logarithmic regret on the order of O(log(T)) is achievable when the time horizon T is sufficiently large. The authors present this as a new result for contextual bandits.

While the paper is well-written and the results appear thorough, the central contribution seems less surprising than claimed. The setting with J finite contexts and K actions per context can be viewed as a standard budgeted bandit problem with JK arms. The fact that only K arms are available at any given time simply introduces an action constraint. For such action-constrained bandit problems with a finite number of arms, a logarithmic regret for large T is an expected result. This raises questions about the paper's novelty. Furthermore, with a sufficiently large time horizon, the coupling between contexts should diminish, making the problem closely resemble running independent bandit problems for each context. This reinforces the view that the problem might be reducible to existing frameworks.

I have several specific questions and suggestions regarding the theoretical analysis:

1.  In Theorem 2, case 2, the regret is given as O(sqrt(T) + ...). Given the K arms available at each step, should this bound not be at least O(sqrt(KT) + ...)?

2.  Still concerning Theorem 2, the problem-dependent regret is expressed as O(KJ log(T)). Typically, problem-dependent bounds are more refined, often involving the sum of inverse arm gaps. Is it possible to derive such a bound here? I understand that the KJ factor arises from the proof technique, which is based on events characterizing the ordering of the UCB indices rather than direct concentration bounds on the gaps. However, this raises the question of whether this proof strategy is optimal, or if a tighter, gap-dependent analysis is possible.

3.  The paper would benefit from a discussion of lower bounds. While trivial lower bounds can be derived from the classical bandit setting (i.e., with zero cost and a single, fixed context), can a more sophisticated lower bound be established that explicitly accounts for the complexity introduced by the multiple contexts?

In summary, my primary concern is the originality of the proposed setting, which appears very close to a special case of an action-constrained bandit problem. Due to this concern, I currently remain neutral on the paper. The work would be significantly strengthened by a clearer demonstration of its novelty. On the technical side, the paper would be much improved with a cleaner, problem-dependent upper bound expressed in terms of arm gaps (which may require reworking the bound from the supplementary material). Ideally, this would be accompanied by matching lower bounds for both the problem-dependent and problem-independent cases.
