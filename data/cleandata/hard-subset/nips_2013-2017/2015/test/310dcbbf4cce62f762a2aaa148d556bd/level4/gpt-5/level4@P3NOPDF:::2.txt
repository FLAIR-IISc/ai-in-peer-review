Summary
The paper studies contextual bandits under both time and budget constraints, focusing on the practically relevant yet analytically tractable case of finitely many discrete contexts, fixed per-action costs, and a single budget. The main contribution is an adaptive linear-programming (LP) approximation to the oracle that yields algorithms with O(log T) regret. The presentation is clear, the motivation is well articulated, and related work is appropriately cited. The theoretical development appears solid; while I did not verify every proof in full detail, the arguments are convincing overall.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and careful restriction to assumptions that are both reasonable in practice and enable sharp theoretical guarantees.
- Significant theoretical contribution: to the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under these assumptions.
- The adaptive LP–based approach is elegant and conceptually appealing, bridging oracle-based design with an implementable approximation while controlling regret.
- The paper is well written and well organized, making the technical ideas accessible.

Limitations and areas for improvement
- Practical validation is minimal. Sections 5 and 6 discuss applications only briefly, and there are no experiments. While NeurIPS welcomes theory-only work, some empirical evidence (even synthetic) would help illustrate the algorithm’s behavior, constants, and computational burden.
- Scope of assumptions: the analysis focuses on finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint. These are reasonable starting points, but the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how the results might extend to:
  - Multiple resource constraints (multi-dimensional budgets).
  - Stochastic or context-dependent costs.
  - Larger or continuous context spaces (e.g., via function approximation).
  - Unknown or random horizons, or nonstationary context distributions.
- Computational aspects are underexplored. Since the algorithm relies on repeatedly solving or updating an LP, readers would benefit from:
  - A complexity analysis of the LP (size as a function of contexts, actions, and time) and the frequency of re-solving.
  - Discussion of practical speed-ups (warm starts, incremental primal-dual updates) and whether per-round computation is feasible at scale.
  - Any trade-offs between the tightness of the LP’s approximation to the oracle and regret/performance.
- Positioning relative to existing literature on bandits with knapsacks (and contextual variants) could be sharpened. In particular:
  - Clarify precisely which assumptions enable O(log T) regret and how the result contrasts with known lower bounds or prior sublinear but non-logarithmic rates in related models.
  - Provide a direct comparison of regret rates and assumptions with prior constrained (contextual) bandit frameworks.
- Regret statements would be clearer with explicit dependence on problem parameters: number of contexts and actions, gap terms, budget magnitude, and minimum/maximum costs. Are the constants benign in realistic settings, or could they be large?
- Clarify algorithmic behavior near budget exhaustion and at different stages of the horizon:
  - How is exploration scheduled under tight budgets?
  - Does the method adaptively adjust exploration as the remaining budget/horizon changes?
  - Are results high-probability or in expectation? If the latter, can high-probability guarantees be obtained?

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Add a small experimental section on synthetic data to illustrate:
  - Empirical regret scaling versus T and versus relevant problem parameters.
  - Budget utilization efficiency and frequency of budget violations (if any).
  - Computational overhead of the adaptive LP (time per update, total runtime) and the impact of practical accelerations.
  - Robustness to mild departures from assumptions (e.g., slight noise in costs).
- Expand the discussion on generalizations:
  - Sketch how the analysis might extend to multiple budgets, stochastic costs, or larger context spaces.
  - Identify main technical obstacles (e.g., concentration under resource coupling, complexity of the oracle) and potential avenues (e.g., function approximation with confidence bounds).
- Provide more explicit comparisons with closely related constrained bandit frameworks, highlighting the exact assumption set that unlocks logarithmic regret and any accompanying trade-offs.
- If feasible, include a brief complexity analysis or implementation notes for the LP updates, including the size of the LP and how often it must be re-solved to achieve the stated guarantees.

Overall assessment
This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a clear and, to my knowledge, novel contribution by achieving O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits under well-motivated simplifying assumptions. The writing is clear, and the proofs appear sound. The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation and limited discussion of computational practicality and extensions. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors add a brief empirical illustration and expand the discussion of computational aspects and possible generalizations.
