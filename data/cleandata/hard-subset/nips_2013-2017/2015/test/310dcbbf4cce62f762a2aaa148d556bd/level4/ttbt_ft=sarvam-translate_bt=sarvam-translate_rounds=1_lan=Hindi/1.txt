The author considers a budgetary and time-bounded sequential bandit problem. The main conclusion of their paper is that if the number of contexts and tasks is limited, and if the time constraint T is large enough (in relation to the problem at hand), then they prove that an error of the order \log(T) can be obtained, which is a new result for sequential bandits.
The results seem profound, and the writing is precise. The results are interesting but not very surprising. In fact, since the number of contexts J and arms K are limited, this bandit problem can be viewed as a time-bounded and budgetary bandit problem with JK arms, which is also constrained by the action because, at each time, not all JK arms are accessible (only K arms corresponding to the context). Since the number of arms is limited, there is an error \log(T) for T large enough, as happens in action-constrained bandit problems. Therefore, I'm not sure how novel this paper is - perhaps the author can clarify this in their rebuttal.
I have some questions about this paper: - In theorem 2, the error inn case 2) O(\sqrt(T) + ...). But is it really O(\sqrt(T) + ...)? Shouldn't it be at least O(\sqrt(KT) + ...)? - Still in theorem 2, the error is either O(KJ \log(T)) or O(\sqrt(T) + KJ\log(T)), depending on the configuration of the arms. Generally, the problem-dependent bounds are expressed in a more sophisticated way with the sum of the arm gaps' reciprocals. Isn't it possible to do something similar here? I understand that you have KJ instead of a fixed sum of gaps because your proof is based on events characterized by the correct orders of UCB, and not on their concentration limits on gaps. But is this an optimal idea? - It would be interesting to have lower bounds for this problem, ideally problem-dependent and problem-independent. The trivial lower bounds are those of the classical bandit who has a cost equal to 0 and corresponds to the context which is always the same. Can this be refined to account for the complexity of the context?
