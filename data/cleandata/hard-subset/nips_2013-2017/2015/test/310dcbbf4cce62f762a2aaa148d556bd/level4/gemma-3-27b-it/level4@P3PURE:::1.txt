Review after rebuttal: I acknowledge the authors’ clarification that their setting is not identical to the budgeted bandit problem with action constraints, and I find their explanation reasonably convincing. However, I remain concerned that the coupling of contexts diminishes with a sufficiently large horizon, and struggle to understand why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as a separate instance of a bandit problem. Consequently, I am not fully convinced of the novelty of this approach. A clear upper bound on regret in terms of arm gaps for the problem-dependent case, and ideally matching lower bounds for both problem-dependent and independent cases, would significantly strengthen this paper. The authors address the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit. The main contribution of this paper is a proof that a regret of order \log(T) is achievable when the number of contexts and actions is finite, and the time constraint T is sufficiently large relative to the problem parameters. This is a novel result for contextual bandits.

The results appear thorough, and the writing is generally precise. While the findings are interesting, they are not entirely surprising. Given that the number of contexts, J, and arms, K, are finite, this problem can be framed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms, subject to the constraint that only K arms are accessible for each context. For sufficiently large T, the regret in such a finite-armed, constrained bandit problem is known to be \log(T). Therefore, the degree of innovation is unclear, and I request the authors address this point in their rebuttal. 

I have several questions regarding the paper: First, in Theorem 2, the regret in case 2 is stated as O(\sqrt(T) + ...). Is this correct? Shouldn’t it be O(\sqrt(KT) + ...) at a minimum? Second, also in Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ \log(T)) or O(\sqrt(T) + KJ\log(T)) depending on the specific configuration of the arms. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are expressed with greater precision using the sum of inverse arm gaps. Is a similar refinement possible here? I understand that the authors’ proof relies on events characterizing the correct ordering of UCB estimates rather than concentration bounds on the gaps themselves, which leads to the use of KJ. However, I question whether this is an optimal approach. Finally, it would be valuable to include lower bounds for this problem, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. The trivial lower bounds from classical bandit problems correspond to zero cost and a constant context. Could these be refined to account for the complexity introduced by the context? 

I remain somewhat unconvinced by the originality of this setting – and therefore, my assessment of this paper is neutral. I have the impression that this setting is closely related to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints. Consequently, I reiterate my concern regarding the paper’s novelty and welcome further clarification in the rebuttal.
