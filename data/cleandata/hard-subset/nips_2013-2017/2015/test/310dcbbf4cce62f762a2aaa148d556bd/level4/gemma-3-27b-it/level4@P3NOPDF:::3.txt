This paper addresses the problem of contextual bandits with a budget constraint, operating over a known time horizon *T*. In each trial, an agent observes a context *j* drawn from a fixed distribution π over *J* possible contexts, and selects an action *k* from the set {0, 1, ..., *K*}, where action 0 represents skipping. Selecting *k* > 0 yields a reward *y* (drawn from a distribution dependent on *j* and *k*) and incurs a known cost *c<sub>j,k</sub>*. The agent aims to maximize cumulative reward while ensuring the cumulative cost remains within a budget *B*.

The core of the paper focuses on a simplified scenario where the context distribution π is known and all costs *c<sub>j,k</sub>* are equal to 1.  Under a mild condition, the authors present an algorithm achieving a regret bound of O(log *T*). They then extend this algorithm to the more general case of unknown π and arbitrary *c<sub>j,k</sub>*.

This work is particularly noteworthy because it potentially achieves an O(log *T*) regret bound, a significant improvement over the O(√*T*) bound obtained by [16] in a more general setting. While this improvement is conditional on the aforementioned mild condition being satisfied, the result is compelling. The algorithm itself is both conceptually simple and demonstrates an intriguing connection to approximation algorithms for optimal action sequence computation when full statistics are known.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly motivated. A potential direction for future work would be to either demonstrate that the O(log *T*) regret bound holds universally, or to rigorously prove an Ω(√*T*) lower bound for the boundary cases where the condition for the O(log *T*) bound is not met. 

Initially, I had concerns regarding the correctness of Lemma 1, specifically whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies condition (2) as presented. This concern stemmed from a lack of clarity regarding why averaging the cost constraint constitutes a valid relaxation of the original hard constraint. However, these concerns were thoroughly addressed in the rebuttal, which provided a convincing explanation. 

I have several minor questions that might be addressed to further strengthen the paper: 

*   Is determining the optimal action sequence exactly NP-hard? Establishing this complexity would further justify the use of approximation algorithms.
*   Can the results be generalized to scenarios where the time horizon *T* is unknown?  Adapting the algorithm to handle unknown *T* would broaden its applicability.
*   In the case of an unknown context distribution, can we safely assume *u<sup>1</sup>* > *u<sup>2</sup>* > ... > *u<sup>*<sub>J</sub></sup>* without loss of generality? Clarifying this assumption’s validity would enhance the clarity of the analysis.

Despite these minor points, I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. The clear exposition, insightful algorithm, and potentially significant improvement in regret bounds make it a valuable contribution to the field.
