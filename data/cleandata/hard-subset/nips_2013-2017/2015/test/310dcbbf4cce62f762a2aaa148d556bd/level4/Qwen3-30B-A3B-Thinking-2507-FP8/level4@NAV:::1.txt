

Review after rebuttal: I agree it is not identical, but it appears sufficiently similar to me: if the horizon is large enough, the context coupling vanishes (and I do not understand why this precludes treating each context occurrence as a separate bandit instance). To me, this paper would be significantly strengthened by a clean problem-dependent upper bound in terms of arm gaps (the supplementary material's bound requires revision), and ideally matching lower bounds for both problem-dependent and problem-independent cases.  

The authors address a budgeted, time-constrained contextual bandit problem. Their key result is that with finite contexts and actions, and sufficiently large time constraint $T$, $\log(T)$ regret is achievable—a novel contribution for contextual bandits. The results appear thorough and the writing precise, though the findings are not highly surprising. Given finite $J$ contexts and $K$ actions, the problem reduces to a time-constrained, budgeted bandit with $JK$ arms (where only $K$ arms are accessible per context), which is action-constrained. For finite arms and large $T$, $\log(T)$ regret is standard in action-constrained bandits. I question the paper's novelty—authors should clarify this in the rebuttal.  

I have three specific concerns:  
1. In Theorem 2, case 2 regret is $O(\sqrt{T} + \cdots)$. Should it not be $O(\sqrt{KT} + \cdots)$ at minimum?  
2. Theorem 2 reports regret as $O(KJ \log T)$ or $O(\sqrt{T} + KJ \log T)$ depending on arm configuration. Problem-dependent bounds typically refine this via sums of inverse arm gaps. While the proof relies on UCB order events (not gap concentration), is this approach optimal?  
3. Lower bounds—ideally problem-dependent and problem-independent—are missing. Trivial lower bounds match classical bandits (cost=0, fixed context), but can they be refined to incorporate context complexity? I remain unconvinced of the setting's novelty and maintain a neutral stance.  

I believe the setting is closely aligned with a budgeted bandit under action constraints, further casting doubt on the paper's originality. Authors should address this in the rebuttal.
