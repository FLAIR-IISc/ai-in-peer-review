

This paper addresses contextual bandits under budget constraints and time limits, focusing on finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint. While these assumptions simplify the setting, they remain practically motivated. The key contribution lies in achieving O(log T) regret through an adaptive linear programming approximation to the oracle, a significant advancement. To the best of the reviewer's knowledge, this is the first work to establish logarithmic regret bounds in constrained contextual bandits, representing a notable theoretical contribution.

The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and thoroughly documented. The literature review is comprehensive, and the motivation for the problem formulation is explicit. The theoretical arguments are rigorous, with proofs appearing sound (though not verified in full detail). The approach appropriately leverages simplifying assumptions to derive strong regret guarantees, demonstrating clear technical merit.

However, the practical implications of the proposed algorithms are underdeveloped. Sections 5 and 6 briefly discuss implementation considerations and extensions but lack empirical validation. The absence of experimental results limits the assessment of real-world applicability and performance trade-offs. While the theoretical novelty is compelling, the paper would benefit significantly from empirical analysis to contextualize the practical relevance of the algorithms, particularly given the constraints on budget and time.

The work’s core strength is its theoretical breakthrough in achieving O(log T) regret under constraints—a result that advances the state of the art. The simplifications made (finite contexts, single budget, fixed costs) are justified by the context of many real-world applications (e.g., ad auctions with fixed campaign budgets), making the theoretical results directly applicable to specific, important scenarios. The adaptive LP approach is both elegant and effective for the constrained problem, providing a clear path to logarithmic regret where prior work often achieved only polynomial bounds.

The paper is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in constrained bandit literature. Its limitations—primarily the lack of experimental validation—are understandable given the focus on theory, but they represent a missed opportunity to strengthen the practical impact. Future work could explore empirical performance, scalability to larger contexts, or extensions to multiple budget constraints, which would further enhance the paper’s significance.

In summary, this is a well-executed theoretical paper that makes a clear and important advance in constrained contextual bandits. The assumptions are reasonable, the analysis is rigorous, and the regret bound is a substantial improvement over prior work. While the practical discussion requires expansion, the theoretical contribution stands as a valuable addition to the literature. The paper is suitable for acceptance with minor consideration given to the empirical gap.
