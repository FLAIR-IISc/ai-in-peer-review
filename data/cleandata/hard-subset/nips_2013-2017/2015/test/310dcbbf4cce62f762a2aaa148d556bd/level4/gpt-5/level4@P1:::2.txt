This paper studies contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, focusing on the practically motivated yet simplified setting of finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget. Under these assumptions, the authors introduce an adaptive linear programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle policy and show that it enables algorithms with O(log T) regret. The approach is to relax the hard budget constraint to an adaptive average budget constraint and, when rewards are unknown, to combine ALP with UCB.

The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation is explicit, the related work is appropriately cited, and the technical development is coherent. While the setting is simplified, the assumptions appear reasonable for applications such as clinical trials and crowdsourcing, and they enable strong theoretical guarantees. As far as I know, this is the first work to obtain logarithmic regret for constrained contextual bandits.

This is a solid theoretical contribution. The proofs appear convincing, although I did not verify every detail. The practical aspects are only briefly discussed (Sections 5 and 6), and there are no experimental results. While empirical evaluation is not strictly necessary given the theoretical focus, additional discussion of implementation considerations and practical trade-offs would strengthen the paper.

Pros:
- Clear and well-documented presentation with appropriate citations and motivation.
- Conceptually simple and elegant ALP approximation of the oracle that leads to tractable algorithms.
- Strong theoretical contribution: O(log T) regret in constrained contextual bandits under reasonable simplifying assumptions; to my knowledge, the first such result.

Cons:
- Practical aspects are discussed only briefly, and no experiments are provided to illustrate behavior in realistic settings or to assess computational overhead.
- The scope is limited to simplified assumptions; a more detailed discussion of how these assumptions impact practical deployment would be helpful.

Overall, this is a good paper. By making appropriate simplifications, it delivers algorithms with O(log T) regret and advances the theoretical understanding of constrained contextual bandits. Expanding the discussion of practical implications would further improve the work.
