

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, where the agent faces both budget and time constraints while selecting actions based on observable contexts. The authors propose a novel approach using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) combined with Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) methods to achieve logarithmic regret in most cases, representing a significant improvement over prior work.

The paper builds upon the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17], which achieved O(√T) regret but suffered from high computational complexity. The authors make three key contributions: (1) they establish a near-optimal LP-based approximation (ALP) for the oracle when statistics are known, achieving O(1) regret; (2) they combine ALP with UCB to create UCB-ALP, which achieves O(log T) regret for most parameter settings; and (3) they extend their approach to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with rigorous regret analysis that carefully accounts for multiple interacting factors including action/context ranking errors and budget fluctuations. The authors properly acknowledge boundary cases where regret increases to O(√T), demonstrating appropriate honesty about limitations. The proof techniques, particularly their novel methods for analyzing concentration properties under adaptive algorithms, represent a significant methodological contribution.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The writing is precise and well-organized, with clear explanations of the ALP approximation and its integration with UCB. The key insight—that ALP tolerates certain estimation errors while maintaining near-optimal performance—shines through clearly. The paper effectively builds from simpler unit-cost systems to more general cases, with the supplementary material providing necessary technical depth.

The originality is strong. While prior work [17, 20] studied constrained bandits, this is the first to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits with discrete contexts and actions. The authors carefully distinguish their work from related papers, including Agrawal et al. (2015) [21] which focuses on infinite contexts and achieves O(√T) regret. The ALP approximation technique differs substantially from prior LP approaches in the literature [17, 19], particularly in how it dynamically adapts to remaining budget and time.

The significance is substantial. Constrained contextual bandits model important real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing where both time and budget constraints matter. The authors' efficient algorithm provides a practical solution that could be directly applied in these settings, while their approach offers valuable insights for addressing more general constrained bandit problems. The work advances the state of the art by demonstrating how to achieve logarithmic regret where previous methods could only achieve sublinear regret.

The paper has minor weaknesses worth noting. First, while the authors acknowledge the boundary cases where regret increases to O(√T), a more detailed discussion of these cases would strengthen the analysis. Second, the paper could better clarify the connection between their optimism under uncertainty approach and existing algorithms like UCRL2 [8]. Third, while the authors mention numerical experiments in their suggestions, including even a limited simulation would provide valuable empirical validation of their theoretical results.

The authors effectively address related work. They correctly position their contributions relative to [17] (which achieves O(√T) regret with high complexity), [20] (which achieves O(√T) regret for static contexts), and Agrawal et al. (2015) [21] (which extends [20] to dynamic contexts but still achieves O(√T) regret). The paper makes a compelling case for its novelty and significance.

The paper merits acceptance for its strong technical contribution, clear writing, and practical significance. It represents a meaningful advance in constrained contextual bandits that should interest both theoretical and applied researchers. The authors have carefully considered the limitations of their approach and have provided a theoretically grounded solution to a challenging problem.

Pro: Logarithmic regret bound in most cases, novel LP-based approximation technique, clear writing, strong theoretical foundation, practical significance for real-world applications.
Con: Boundary cases where regret increases to O(√T), limited empirical validation, some minor gaps in connecting to existing algorithms like UCRL2.

This paper is an excellent contribution to the field, providing a computationally efficient algorithm with near-optimal regret for constrained contextual bandits. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the problem's challenges and have made a significant step toward solving the open problem of general constrained contextual bandits. I recommend acceptance.
