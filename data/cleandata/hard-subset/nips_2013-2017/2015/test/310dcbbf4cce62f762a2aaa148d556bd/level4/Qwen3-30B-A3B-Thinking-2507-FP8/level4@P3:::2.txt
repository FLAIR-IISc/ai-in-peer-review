

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, where the agent must maximize reward subject to both time and budget constraints. The authors focus on a simplified model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint—assumptions justified in applications like clinical trials and wireless networks. They introduce Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) to approximate the oracle solution, which achieves near-optimal performance with O(1) regret (except for boundary cases), and combine it with UCB to develop UCB-ALP, achieving O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution. Prior work on contextual bandits (e.g., [1-7]) generally ignores budget constraints, while budgeted bandit studies (e.g., [11-16]) are limited to non-contextual settings. The authors correctly note that existing approaches for Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] achieve only O(√T) regret due to restricted benchmarks and computational complexity. By making reasonable simplifications, they demonstrate how to achieve logarithmic regret—a major advancement in the field.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The authors provide clear motivation, precise problem formulation, and rigorous theoretical analysis. The ALP approximation is novel and insightful, leveraging linear programming to overcome the computational challenges of dynamic programming in constrained settings. The regret analysis for ALP is particularly impressive, with the authors carefully addressing the complex interactions between budget fluctuations, context arrivals, and ranking errors. The UCB-ALP extension is well-motivated, and the regret bound of O(JK log T) for non-boundary cases is order-optimal given the problem structure. The proofs appear solid, though the supplementary material would benefit from more explicit discussion of the concentration arguments.

Clarity is a strength of the paper. The writing is precise, the organization is logical, and the key concepts (e.g., adaptive linear programming, context ranking errors) are well-explained. The authors effectively use the unit-cost system as a stepping stone to more complex cases, building intuition before presenting generalizations. The related work is thoroughly cited, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from previous contributions.

The originality is substantial. While the idea of approximating DP with LP has appeared elsewhere (e.g., [17, 19]), the authors' specific implementation for constrained contextual bandits and their regret analysis are novel. The paper is the first to show logarithmic regret in this setting, as the authors claim, and this constitutes a meaningful advance over the O(√T) results in [17] and [21].

The significance is high. This work addresses a critical gap in contextual bandit theory with practical implications for applications like clinical trials and online recommendation systems. The authors' insights about decoupling information acquisition and decision making through adaptive linear relaxation could guide future work on more general constrained bandit problems. The paper's focus on computationally efficient algorithms (UCB-ALP is straightforward to implement) further enhances its practical relevance.

Minor weaknesses include the absence of experimental validation, which would strengthen the claims about practical performance. The paper mentions that empirical results are available in supplementary material but does not include them in the main text. The discussion of boundary cases where regret is O(√T) is somewhat brief, and the authors might elaborate on how common these cases are in practice. While the assumption of known context distribution is reasonable for the main analysis, the extension to unknown distributions (Section 5) lacks sufficient detail about implementation challenges.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First paper to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits
- Significant theoretical contribution addressing a meaningful gap in the literature
- Well-motivated and technically sound analysis
- Clear exposition and strong organization

Arguments against acceptance:
- No experimental results to validate theoretical claims
- Practical implementation details for the general case are underdeveloped
- Boundary cases where regret is higher are not fully explored

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in contextual bandits. The assumptions are reasonable for the problem addressed, and the results are compelling. The lack of experiments is a limitation, but this is a theoretical paper that makes a clear, significant contribution to the field. The paper satisfies all four NIPS criteria: it is of high quality, clearly written, original, and significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to expand on the boundary cases and provide more details about the practical implementation of the algorithms.
