

This paper addresses a significant gap in the contextual bandit literature by studying contextual bandits with budget and time constraints (constrained contextual bandits), where the agent must maximize expected total reward subject to a hard budget constraint. The authors consider a setting where the agent observes a context before selecting an action, and each action incurs both a reward and a cost. This problem is particularly relevant to applications like clinical trials and online recommendation systems, where resources are limited.

The paper makes a novel contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, which is a significant improvement over previous work. For example, [17] (Resourceful Contextual Bandits) achieves O(√T) regret for a more general setting, but the benchmark used in [17] is restricted to a finite policy set. The authors' approach is novel in that they approximate the oracle solution using adaptive linear programming (ALP), which achieves near-optimal performance with O(1) regret in non-boundary cases. They then combine ALP with UCB to handle unknown expected rewards, resulting in an algorithm (UCB-ALP) that achieves O(log T) regret except for certain boundary cases where it achieves O(√T) regret.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem definition through theoretical analysis to practical algorithms. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison with related work, highlighting how their approach differs from previous methods in both the problem setting and the regret bounds achieved. For instance, they correctly note that while [20] extends to dynamic contexts, it achieves O(√T) regret due to using a fixed budget constraint, whereas their UCB-ALP algorithm adapts the budget constraint to achieve logarithmic regret.

The technical contributions are strong, with rigorous proofs for the regret bounds. The key insight that ALP only requires the ordering of expected rewards (not their exact values) enables the combination with UCB, which is a clever and elegant approach. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the boundary cases where the regret is O(√T), showing that these cases are rare (of zero Lebesgue measure) and that the logarithmic regret bound holds for the vast majority of parameter settings.

The main limitation of the paper is that the logarithmic regret bound holds only for non-boundary cases, with the regret becoming O(√T) in boundary cases. However, the authors carefully characterize these boundary cases, and the results still represent a significant improvement over previous work. The paper also demonstrates that the approach extends to more general settings, including unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, though these extensions are more challenging and require additional technical work.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear exposition of the key ideas. The technical arguments are sound, and the authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The supplementary materials are comprehensive and address the more challenging generalizations to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

The significance of this work is high, as it provides a computationally efficient algorithm for a practically important problem, and it advances the state of the art by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. The results also shed light on the design of algorithms for more general constrained contextual bandit problems, which is an open problem in the field.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of bandit algorithms, with clear theoretical advances and practical relevance. The authors have carefully addressed the challenges of constrained contextual bandits and provided a solution that achieves logarithmic regret for most parameter settings. The only minor limitation is the boundary cases where the regret is not logarithmic, but this is a reasonable trade-off given the complexity of the problem, and the authors have thoroughly characterized these cases.
