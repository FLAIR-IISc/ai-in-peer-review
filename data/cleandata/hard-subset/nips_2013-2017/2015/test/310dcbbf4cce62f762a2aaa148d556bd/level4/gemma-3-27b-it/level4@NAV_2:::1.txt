The authors study the problem of contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints, a relevant and challenging extension of the classical contextual bandit setting. The key contribution is demonstrating that logarithmic regret is achievable under finite action and context spaces, a result not previously established for this constrained setting. The paper presents a novel algorithm, UCB-ALP, combining adaptive linear programming with upper confidence bounds, and provides a detailed analysis of its performance.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The authors clearly articulate the problem, their approach, and the theoretical results. The development of the ALP approximation and its subsequent use in the UCB-ALP algorithm is logically presented and justified. The analysis appears rigorous, with careful consideration given to the complexities introduced by the constraints and the unknown reward structure. The connection to related work, including resource-constrained bandits and previous approaches to contextual bandits, is appropriately discussed. 

A strength of this work is the insightful use of linear programming to approximate the oracle solution, even with limited information. The adaptive nature of the ALP algorithm, adjusting to the remaining budget and time, is a key element of its success. Furthermore, the authors address both the case of known and unknown context distributions, extending the applicability of their results. The detailed regret analysis, even accounting for boundary cases, is commendable.

However, while the result is valuable, the claim of novelty could be strengthened. The connection to budgeted bandits with action constraints, as the authors acknowledge, is significant. While the contextual aspect adds complexity, the paper doesn't fully delineate how the constraints fundamentally alter the regret landscape compared to existing results in constrained bandit problems. The regret bounds, particularly in the boundary cases (O(√T + log T)), could be improved. While the authors acknowledge the potential for tighter bounds using arm-gap based analysis, pursuing this direction would significantly strengthen the paper. Additionally, the lack of lower bounds for this problem is a limitation; establishing such bounds would further solidify the optimality of the proposed algorithm. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of adaptive linear programming to constrained contextual bandits.
*   Achieves logarithmic regret, a significant result for this setting.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and clear presentation.
*   Addresses both known and unknown context distributions.
*   Algorithm is computationally efficient.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Connection to existing work on budgeted bandits with action constraints is strong, potentially diminishing novelty.
*   Regret bounds could be tighter, particularly in boundary cases.
*   Absence of lower bounds leaves the optimality of the algorithm unconfirmed.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of contextual bandits. While the novelty is somewhat tempered by connections to existing literature, the technical achievements and clear presentation justify acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the tightness of the bounds and the inclusion of lower bound results in a potential rebuttal or future work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
