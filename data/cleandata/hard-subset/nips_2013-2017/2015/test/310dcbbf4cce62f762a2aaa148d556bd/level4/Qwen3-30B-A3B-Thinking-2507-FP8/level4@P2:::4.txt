

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, a variant of contextual bandits with additional budget constraints, which is an important extension for real-world applications like clinical trials and online recommendation systems. The authors propose a novel approach using Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) to achieve logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over previous works that only achieved sublinear regret.

The paper begins by establishing the problem setting and relating it to prior work. It correctly notes that while budget constraints have been studied in non-contextual bandits (e.g., [11-16]), these results are inapplicable to contextual bandit settings where context is observable. The authors also properly reference the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17], noting its limitations (O(√T) regret, restricted benchmark, high computational complexity) and justifying their simplified model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and single budget constraint. This simplification is well-motivated by practical applications like clinical trials [10] and wireless networks [18].

The key contribution is the ALP algorithm, which approximates the oracle solution using linear programming with adaptive budget constraints. The paper demonstrates that ALP achieves O(1) regret (except for boundary cases) when statistics are known, a significant step toward achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits. The authors then extend this to the unknown statistics case with UCB-ALP, which combines ALP with upper-confidence-bound methods to achieve O(log T) regret (except for boundary cases). This appears to be the first work to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, making it a substantial contribution to the field.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous analysis. The authors carefully analyze the regret bounds for both ALP and UCB-ALP, addressing the complex interactions between information acquisition and decision making. The proof techniques are novel, including the use of concentration properties under adaptive algorithms and bounding estimation errors under random contexts. The paper also appropriately addresses generalizations to unknown context distributions (EALP and UCB-EALP) and heterogeneous costs.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem formulation, algorithms, and analysis are well-structured and clearly presented. The key insights about how ALP decouples information acquisition from decision making are particularly well-explained. The paper also provides a thoughtful discussion of how their approach differs from prior work, especially [20-22] which achieved O(√T) regret for dynamic contexts.

The originality of the work is strong. While linear programming has been used to approximate dynamic programming solutions in related problems [17,19], the authors' specific design and analysis of ALP for constrained contextual bandits is novel. The paper clearly demonstrates how their approach differs from previous work and establishes a significant improvement in regret bounds.

The significance of the work is substantial. Constrained contextual bandits are important for many practical applications where resources are limited, and the paper provides a computationally efficient algorithm with logarithmic regret. This represents a meaningful advance over previous approaches, which either had higher regret or were computationally intractable.

The paper has one notable weakness: the treatment of boundary cases (where ρ = q_j for some j) seems somewhat abrupt. The draft review correctly questions why these cases require special analysis, as it's not immediately clear why ρ = q_j appears "special" for j > 1. The paper states that for boundary cases, ALP achieves O(√T) regret rather than O(1), but the intuition for this distinction is not thoroughly explained. A more detailed discussion of why these boundary cases behave differently would strengthen the paper, as the boundary cases appear to be a potential weakness in the analysis.

The paper also could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to the recent works [21,22] that extend [20] to dynamic contexts. While the paper mentions these works, it could more explicitly discuss how their approach differs in achieving logarithmic regret versus the O(√T) regret of [21,22].

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the contextual bandit literature. The technical quality is high, the approach is novel and well-motivated, and the results are of considerable significance for practitioners dealing with resource constraints in bandit problems. The only minor weakness is the somewhat unclear treatment of boundary cases, which could be addressed with a bit more explanation.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound with rigorous regret analysis
- Clarity: Well-structured and clearly written
- Originality: Novel approach to constrained contextual bandits
- Significance: Important contribution with practical applications

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to clarify the boundary case analysis. The paper represents a significant advance in the field and would be valuable to the NIPS community.
