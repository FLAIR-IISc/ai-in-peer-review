This paper addresses a budget-constrained contextual bandit (CB) problem with discrete contexts and actions, presenting a relatively simple yet effective framework. The primary contribution of the paper lies in the computational efficiency of the proposed algorithm, achieved through linear programming (LP) approximation, and its ability to attain a log(T) regret bound.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, making it a pleasure to read. The authors have done an excellent job in presenting their ideas, making the content accessible to a broad audience.

A potential direction for future work could be the incorporation of a parametric model for the reward. This approach would be particularly useful when dealing with a large number of contexts, as it would provide a more efficient and scalable solution.

In Section 4.1, the authors combine UCB with their proposed algorithm, ALP. To further enhance the clarity of this section, it would be beneficial to provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the UCB-ALP algorithm. Specifically, it appears that the authors are employing the optimism in the face of uncertainty principle, which is similar to the policy update in UCRL2. A more explicit discussion of this connection would strengthen the paper.

Including numerical experiments in an extended version of the paper would also be valuable, as it would provide empirical evidence to support the theoretical results.

Additionally, the authors may find the following paper relevant to their work: http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.03374. This paper provides a useful perspective on the topic and could be used to further situate the current work within the broader literature.

Overall, this paper merits acceptance due to its efficient algorithm for constrained CB, which achieves optimal regret in most cases. The work is interesting, and it indeed represents an improvement over existing research in the field.
