This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B). The key challenge in this setting is that the constraints create complex dependencies across time, making the standard exploration-exploitation tradeoff significantly more difficult. The authors' main contribution is a computationally efficient algorithm, UCB-ALP, that achieves a near-optimal logarithmic regret bound in most cases.

The core idea is to first develop an approximation for the oracle policy (which assumes known reward statistics). The optimal oracle policy is computationally intractable, likely requiring a dynamic programming solution. The authors cleverly approximate this by relaxing the hard budget constraint into an average one, solvable via a Linear Program (LP). Building on this, they propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that dynamically adjusts the LP's average budget constraint based on the ratio of remaining budget to remaining time. This ALP policy is shown to be near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret against the true oracle in most settings ("non-boundary cases").

For the more realistic setting where expected rewards are unknown, the paper combines the ALP decision-making framework with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle for exploration. The resulting UCB-ALP algorithm uses optimistic estimates of the rewards within the ALP framework. The main theoretical result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) regret in boundary cases. This is a significant improvement over prior work on resourceful contextual bandits, such as [17], which established O(sqrt(T)) regret under more general but computationally demanding settings. The paper also discusses extensions to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** This work appears to be the first to achieve a logarithmic, instance-dependent regret bound for the constrained contextual bandit problem. This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art. The problem itself is of high practical importance in areas like online advertising, clinical trials, and crowdsourcing, where resources are finite. The proposed method of decoupling exploration from a near-optimal constrained planning policy (ALP) is insightful and could be influential for related problems.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed. The paper provides clear proof sketches that build intuition, such as mapping the evolution of the remaining budget under the ALP policy to a hypergeometric distribution to demonstrate its concentration properties. This is a key step that enables the regret analysis and is a nice technical result in its own right.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building the reader's intuition by first analyzing the simplified setting with known statistics (ALP) before tackling the full problem with unknown statistics (UCB-ALP). This layered presentation makes the complex analysis much easier to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The primary weakness is the absence of any numerical experiments. While the theoretical results are strong, experiments would be invaluable for several reasons: 1) to validate the asymptotic results in finite-horizon settings; 2) to understand the practical impact of the constants hidden by the Big-O notation; and 3) to compare the performance and computational time of UCB-ALP against existing baselines, such as the algorithm from [17]. An extended version of the paper would be significantly strengthened by an experimental section.
*   **Simplifying Assumptions:** The core analysis relies on a number of simplifying assumptions: a finite and discrete set of contexts and actions, known context distribution, and (for most of the paper) unit costs. While these assumptions are necessary to achieve the strong results, they limit the direct applicability of the algorithm. In future work, it would be valuable to consider a parametric model for the rewards (e.g., linear), which is a common approach for handling very large or continuous context spaces.
*   **Clarification of UCB Rationale:** In Section 4.2, the paper introduces the UCB-ALP algorithm. While the mechanism is clear, the rationale could be stated more explicitly. It would be helpful to connect it directly to the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle, clarifying that the algorithm proceeds at each step by solving the planning problem (ALP) as if the optimistic UCBs were the true expected rewards. This is analogous to the planning step in algorithms like UCRL2 for reinforcement learning, and explicitly drawing this connection would be beneficial.
*   **Related Work Integration:** I note that a relevant recent work [21] (Agrawal et al., 2015) is cited in a footnote. Given its direct relevance to contextual bandits with global constraints, integrating the comparison with this work into the main related work section, rather than a footnote, would strengthen the paper's positioning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel and computationally efficient algorithm for an important and challenging problem.
*   **Pro:** It provides the first logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits, which is a significant theoretical advance.
*   **Pro:** The paper is technically sound, very clearly written, and the analytical approach is insightful.
*   **Con:** The work is purely theoretical, lacking any empirical validation to support the analysis.
*   **Con:** The main results are derived under a simplified model (e.g., discrete contexts, known context distribution).

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on contextual bandits. The theoretical results are novel and important, and the proposed algorithm is both elegant and computationally efficient. Despite the lack of experiments, the strength of the theoretical contribution is sufficient for acceptance. The paper is well-written, interesting, and represents a clear improvement over existing work. I recommend acceptance.
