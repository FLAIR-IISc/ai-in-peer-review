

This paper addresses a well-motivated variant of the contextual bandit (CB) problem under budget constraints, with discrete contexts and actions. The primary contribution lies in proposing a computationally efficient algorithm leveraging linear programming (LP) approximation, achieving a logarithmic regret bound. The work is significant for its practical efficiency and theoretical guarantees in a constrained setting where prior methods may struggle.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured, making the technical content accessible without sacrificing rigor. The problem formulation, algorithm design, and analysis are presented with precision, enabling easy comprehension of the key innovations. The theoretical results are sound, and the regret bound aligns with optimal performance in standard CB settings.

Regarding suggestions for improvement:  
1. **Parametric Reward Models**: Future work should explore parametric reward models (e.g., linear or neural networks) to handle large context spaces efficiently. While the current discrete approach is valid for small contexts, scalability to high-dimensional settings is a critical practical concern.  
2. **Algorithmic Rationale**: In Section 4.1, the integration of UCB with ALP (Approximate Linear Programming) requires clearer justification. The UCB-ALP algorithm appears to implement the *optimism principle* (optimistic exploration under uncertainty), which is conceptually related to UCRL2’s policy updates. Explicitly stating this connection would strengthen the methodological foundation and help readers contextualize the approach within existing literature.  
3. **Empirical Validation**: Including numerical experiments in an extended version would significantly bolster the claims. Simulations demonstrating the algorithm’s performance against baselines (e.g., standard UCB or budget-constrained methods) would provide concrete evidence of computational efficiency and regret behavior in practice.  

The paper builds effectively on prior work, particularly in constrained CB settings. We recommend considering the related work in [arXiv:1506.03374](http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.03374), which addresses similar budget constraints and offers complementary insights into policy optimization under resource limitations.  

In summary, this work delivers a compelling solution to a non-trivial problem with strong theoretical guarantees and computational practicality. The algorithm’s efficiency and logarithmic regret bound represent a meaningful advancement over existing approaches, particularly for problems where budget constraints are non-negotiable. The clarity of presentation and sound technical contributions make this a valuable addition to the literature.  

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper merits publication for its elegant algorithmic design, optimal regret analysis, and relevance to real-world constrained decision-making scenarios. The suggested improvements are minor and would enhance an already strong contribution.
