Post-rebuttal: I appreciate the clarifications, and I agree the setting is not exactly the same as a budgeted bandit with action constraints. However, it still appears sufficiently close that, for a large enough horizon T, the coupling induced by the context effectively vanishes. Given that, I do not understand why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as a separate instance of a bandit problem. In addition, the paper would be much stronger with a clean, gap-dependent upper bound (the current bound in the supplementary material needs to be reworked) and, ideally, matching lower bounds in both the problem-dependent and problem-independent regimes.

Summary and assessment:
- The paper studies a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit. The main result is that when the numbers of contexts J and actions K are finite, and T is sufficiently large (relative to the problem), a regret of order log T is achievable—a result the authors present as new for contextual bandits.
- The results seem thorough and the writing is precise. The findings are interesting but, in my view, not very surprising. With finite J and K, the problem can be seen as a time- and budget-constrained bandit with JK arms and action constraints (only the K arms associated with the current context are available at each round). Because the total number of arms is finite, for large T one expects log T regret, as in action-constrained bandit problems. This makes me uncertain about the degree of novelty; the paper would benefit from a clearer positioning relative to this perspective.

Questions and suggestions:
1) Theorem 2, case (2): The regret is stated as O(sqrt(T) + ...). Is it truly O(sqrt(T) + ...)? Shouldn’t it be at least O(sqrt(K T) + ...), reflecting the dependence on the number of actions?
2) Still in Theorem 2: The regret is either O(KJ log T) or O(sqrt(T) + KJ log T), depending on the arm configuration. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are more refined and involve sums of inverse gaps. Can you derive a similar gap-dependent characterization here? I understand that you obtain KJ rather than a sum over inverses of gaps because your proof proceeds via events ensuring the correct UCB orderings, rather than concentration on the gaps themselves. But is that approach optimal, or can it be strengthened to yield gap-dependent terms?
3) Lower bounds: It would be valuable to have lower bounds, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. The trivial lower bounds are those of classical bandits (e.g., zero cost and a single, always-present context). Can these be refined to incorporate the complexity arising from multiple contexts?
4) Supplementary bound: The bound in the supplementary material appears to need revision; a clean, transparent gap-dependent upper bound would substantially improve the paper.

Overall, I remain unconvinced about the originality of the setting, given its apparent proximity to a budgeted bandit with action constraints, and therefore my evaluation remains neutral.
