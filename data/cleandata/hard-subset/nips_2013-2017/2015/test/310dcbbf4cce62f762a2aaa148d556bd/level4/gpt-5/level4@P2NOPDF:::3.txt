This paper studies a contextual bandit with a budget B and known horizon T. At each round, the agent observes a context j drawn from a fixed distribution π over {1,…,J} and chooses an action k from {0} ∪ {1,…,K}, where k=0 means “skip.” If k=0, the agent receives no reward and pays no cost. If k>0, the agent receives a reward y drawn from a fixed distribution on [0,1] that depends on (j,k) and incurs a known cost c_{j,k}>0. The objective is to maximize cumulative reward subject to never exceeding the total budget B.

The main part of the paper focuses on the case where π is known and c_{j,k}=1 for all j,k, and proposes an algorithm with O(log T) regret under a mild condition. The paper then extends the approach to unknown π and arbitrary positive costs c_{j,k}. Although a more general setting is examined in [16], the algorithm there attains O(√T) regret. To my knowledge, this work is the first to achieve O(log T) regret in this budgeted contextual bandit setting (under the stated mild condition). The algorithm leverages an approximation routine for the offline problem of computing the optimal action sequence when all statistics are known. The method is simple yet insightful.

The paper is well motivated and clearly written. The contribution would be even stronger if the O(log T) regret could be shown to hold without the mild condition, or if an Ω(√T) lower bound were established for the boundary cases where the condition fails.

Most results hinge on Lemma 1. Initially, I was not convinced the optimal action sequence satisfies condition (2) at every trial, and thus I did not see why the average constraint is a relaxation of the hard budget constraint. The rebuttal clarified these points, and I am now convinced of Lemma 1. Thank you.

Minor comments and questions:
- Is computing the exact optimal action sequence (in the fully known offline problem) NP-hard?
- Can the results be extended to the case of unknown or unbounded T, e.g., via an anytime or doubling-trick variant, without weakening the regret guarantees?
- Can we, without loss of generality, relabel so that u^1 > u^2 > … > u^*_J even when the context distribution is unknown?

Overall, I strongly recommend acceptance.
