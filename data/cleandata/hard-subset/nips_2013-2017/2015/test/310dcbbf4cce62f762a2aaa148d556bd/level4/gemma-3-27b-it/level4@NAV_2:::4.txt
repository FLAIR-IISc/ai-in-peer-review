This paper addresses the challenging problem of constrained contextual bandits, introducing a novel approach based on adaptive linear programming (ALP) to manage the trade-off between exploration and exploitation under budget and time constraints. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and makes a significant contribution by establishing logarithmic regret bounds – a first for this problem setting. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature on contextual bandits, resource allocation, and dynamic programming, highlighting the limitations of prior approaches and the novelty of their solution.

The core idea of approximating the oracle with ALP, and then combining it with UCB for the unknown reward case, is insightful. The analysis appears rigorous, employing techniques like concentration inequalities and careful partitioning of regret sources. The extension to heterogeneous costs and unknown context distributions demonstrates the adaptability of the proposed framework. The paper is well-organized, with a clear progression of ideas from the idealized known-statistics case to more realistic scenarios. 

However, the discussion regarding the “boundary cases” where the regret becomes O(√T) feels somewhat unsatisfying. While the authors acknowledge these cases, the explanation for *why* they arise – and why ρ = q<sub>j</sub> should be particularly problematic – isn't entirely convincing. It isn't immediately clear why these specific parameter values lead to a degradation in performance beyond the logarithmic bound, and a more intuitive explanation would be beneficial. A more detailed exploration of the impact of these boundary conditions and their practical implications would strengthen the paper.  

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the first work to demonstrate logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithms and their analysis appear rigorous and well-justified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the complex ideas accessible.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to be impactful in applications where resource constraints are critical.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The algorithms are relatively easy to implement, offering a practical solution to a challenging problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Boundary Case Explanation:** The justification for the √T regret in boundary cases lacks sufficient intuition.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the paper focuses on theoretical analysis, some empirical validation would strengthen the claims.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant open problem in reinforcement learning.
*   The proposed solution is novel, technically sound, and theoretically well-supported.
*   The clarity of presentation makes the work accessible to a broad audience.
*   The results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of intuitive explanation for the boundary cases weakens the analysis.
*   The absence of empirical results leaves some questions about the practical performance of the algorithms.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits. Despite the minor weakness regarding the boundary case explanation, the novelty, technical soundness, and potential impact of the work outweigh the concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the discussion of boundary cases.
