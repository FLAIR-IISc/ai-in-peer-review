Review after rebuttal:

Upon reevaluation, I still believe that the setting presented in this paper bears a strong resemblance to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints. While I acknowledge the authors' assertion that the two are not identical, I think they are similar enough, particularly when the horizon is large. In such cases, the coupling of the context appears to vanish, leading me to question why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as a separate instance of a bandit problem.

To strengthen this paper, I would recommend providing a clean upper bound in terms of arm gaps for the problem-dependent case, as well as deriving matching lower bounds for both problem-dependent and problem-independent cases. The supplementary material's bound would need to be reworked to achieve this.

The authors investigate the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit, yielding the primary finding that a regret of order log(T) is achievable when the number of contexts and actions is finite, and the time constraint T is sufficiently large with respect to the problem at hand. Although the results appear thorough and the writing is precise, I find them somewhat unsurprising.

Given that the number of contexts J and arms K are finite, this bandit problem can be viewed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms, which is also action-constrained since not all JK arms are accessible at each time. Since the number of arms is finite, for T large enough, the regret is log(T), similar to action-constrained bandit problems. I remain uncertain about the innovativeness of this paper and would appreciate further clarification from the authors.

I have several questions regarding this paper:

1. In Theorem 2, the regret in case 2) is O(√(T) + ...). However, should it not be O(√(KT) + ...) at the very least?
2. Still in Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ log(T)) or O(√(T) + KJ log(T)) depending on the configuration of the arms. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are expressed in a more refined way using the sum of inverse arm gaps. Is it possible to achieve something similar in this context? While I understand that the proof is based on events characterizing the correct orders of the UCB, rather than concentration bounds on their gaps, I question whether this approach is optimal.
3. It would be interesting to establish lower bounds for this problem, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. Trivial lower bounds correspond to classical bandit problems with cost equals 0 and context always the same. Can these bounds be refined to account for the complexity of the context?

I remain unconvinced by the originality of this setting and therefore remain neutral about this paper.
