This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem with a known time horizon T and a budget constraint B. At each trial, the agent observes a context `j` (from a set {1, ..., J}) drawn from a fixed probability distribution `π` and selects an action `k` from a set {0, 1, ..., K}. Action 0 is a "skip" action, which yields no reward and incurs no cost. For any other action (k>0), the agent receives a stochastic reward (from a distribution on [0,1] dependent on `j` and `k`) and incurs a known, positive cost `c_{j,k}`. The agent's goal is to maximize the cumulative reward subject to the constraint that the total incurred cost never exceeds the budget B.

The main body of the paper focuses on a special case where the context distribution `π` is known and all costs `c_{j,k}` are equal to 1. For this simplified setting, the authors propose an algorithm that achieves an `O(log T)` regret bound, provided a mild condition is met. This algorithm is then extended to the general case with an unknown `π` and arbitrary costs.

The proposed algorithm is based on an approximation method for computing the optimal policy when all problem parameters are known. The approach is simple yet theoretically interesting. While a more general setting was considered in [16], the algorithm proposed therein achieved a higher `O(sqrt(T))` regret. Therefore, this paper appears to be the first to achieve `O(log T)` regret for this problem, albeit under the aforementioned condition.

The paper is well-motivated and very clearly written. Its significance would be enhanced if the `O(log T)` regret bound were shown to hold universally, or alternatively, if an `Ω(sqrt(T))` lower bound were established for the boundary cases not covered by the current analysis.

The main results hinge on Lemma 1. I was initially not convinced of its correctness, as it was unclear why the optimal action sequence must satisfy condition (2) for all trials, which is key to relaxing the hard budget constraint into an average one. However, the authors' rebuttal has satisfactorily addressed this concern and clarified the proof.

Minor Comments:
*   Is computing the exact optimal action sequence NP-hard, assuming all statistics are known?
*   Can these results be generalized to the case where the time horizon T is unknown?
*   Is it possible to assume `u_1 > u_2 > ... > u_J` without loss of generality, even when the context distribution is unknown?

This is a strong paper presenting a novel and important result. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
