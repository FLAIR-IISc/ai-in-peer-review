This paper studies the contextual bandit problem under a hard budget constraint B and a known time horizon T. In each round, an agent observes a context drawn from a fixed distribution, and then chooses an action from a fixed set. Taking an action yields a random reward and incurs a deterministic cost, both dependent on the context and action. The agent can also choose to "skip" a context, which yields zero reward and incurs no cost. The goal is to maximize the cumulative reward, with the constraint that the total cost incurred never exceeds the budget B.

The core of the paper focuses on the special case of unit costs and a known context distribution. For this setting, the authors propose the UCB-ALP algorithm and show that it achieves an O(log T) regret bound, except for a set of "boundary cases" where the regret is O(sqrt(T)). This approach is then extended to the more general settings of unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses an important and practical extension of the contextual bandit framework. While more general settings have been considered (e.g., in [17]), previous work achieved O(sqrt(T)) regret. To my knowledge, this is the first work to achieve O(log T) regret for constrained contextual bandits. This is a significant theoretical improvement, even if it holds only for non-boundary cases.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed algorithm is based on an elegant and insightful approximation of the optimal policy (the oracle). The key idea is to replace the intractable dynamic programming problem with an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) relaxation. This approximation is not only computationally efficient but also robust enough to be combined with a UCB-style exploration strategy.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-motivated and very clearly written, progressively building the solution from the known-statistics case to the more complex unknown-statistics case.

**Weaknesses and Questions:**

The main limitation is that the logarithmic regret bound is not universal. The significance of the work would be further strengthened if the O(log T) regret held for all cases, or if a matching Omega(sqrt(T)) lower bound were proven for the boundary cases, which would confirm that the algorithm is optimal in those instances as well.

A key step in the analysis is Lemma 1, which establishes an upper bound on the oracle's performance using an LP with an *average* budget constraint. I found the justification for this lemma unclear. The optimal policy is subject to a *hard* budget constraint across the entire time horizon, and it is not immediately obvious why the solution to this LP relaxation provides a valid upper bound. A more detailed explanation would be helpful.

**Minor Questions:**

1.  Is computing the exact optimal policy (the oracle) known to be computationally intractable (e.g., NP-hard)? A brief comment on this would help motivate the need for an approximation.
2.  Can the proposed methods be generalized to the case where the time horizon T is unknown?
3.  For the analysis, you assume that u*_1 > u*_2 > ... > u*_J. Is it possible to make this assumption without loss of generality, particularly in the setting where rewards and the context distribution are unknown? The agent would not know this ordering a priori.

Despite these questions, the paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to an important problem, achieving a significant improvement in the state-of-the-art regret bounds. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
