

The paper presents an interesting setup for contextual bandits, with a novel approach to handling adaptive decision-making. However, a key point requires clarification: why are boundary cases—specifically when ρ = q_j for j > 1—analyzed separately? This condition does not appear inherently special, as ρ = q_j arises naturally in the problem formulation without apparent distinction for j > 1. The authors’ treatment of these cases lacks justification, and it remains unclear why they necessitate distinct analysis from other scenarios.  

The paper evaluates contextual bandits against a stronger baseline: one that employs adaptive linear programming to strategically skip a fraction of suboptimal rounds (referred to as the "budget"). This comparison is well-motivated and adds rigor to the evaluation. The proposed solution leverages this baseline effectively, resulting in a compelling framework with practical implications. The technical contributions, particularly the integration of adaptive budgeting within contextual bandit settings, represent a meaningful advancement.  

While the core ideas are sound and the experimental validation is thorough, the unresolved ambiguity around the boundary case analysis weakens the theoretical foundation. Addressing this point would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution. Overall, the work offers a valuable perspective on contextual bandit optimization with a clear, novel methodology.
