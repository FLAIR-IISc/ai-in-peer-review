This paper addresses the problem of contextual bandits under budget and time constraints, a practically relevant extension to the standard contextual bandit setting. The authors focus on the case of finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint. While these assumptions represent a simplification of more general scenarios, they are reasonable and allow for a rigorous theoretical analysis. The key contribution of this work is the development of algorithms that achieve O(log T) regret, achieved through an adaptive linear programming approximation of the oracle solution.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for studying constrained contextual bandits is explicitly stated, and the related work is appropriately cited, providing sufficient context for the reader. The theoretical development is sound, and the authors present a convincing argument for the performance of their algorithms. While a complete verification of all proofs was not possible within the scope of this review, the presented arguments appear logically consistent and well-supported. 

This work represents a significant advancement in the field. To the best of my knowledge, the authors are the first to achieve logarithmic regret bounds for constrained contextual bandits. This is a notable accomplishment, and the proposed approach—leveraging adaptive linear programming—appears to be a promising direction for tackling this challenging problem. 

The main weakness of the paper lies in its limited discussion of practical considerations and the complete absence of experimental validation. Sections 5 and 6 touch upon potential practical implications, but these are brief and high-level.  Demonstrating the performance of the algorithms on synthetic or real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights into their practicality. Further discussion on the computational cost of the adaptive linear programming approximation, particularly as the number of contexts grows, would also be beneficial. How does the performance scale with problem size and what are the practical limitations regarding implementation?

Despite the lack of experimental results, the theoretical contributions are substantial and justify acceptance. The authors have successfully navigated the complexities of constrained contextual bandits by making appropriate simplifying assumptions, resulting in a theoretically strong and potentially impactful algorithm. 

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   Significant theoretical contribution: First logarithmic regret bounds for constrained contextual bandits.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Solid theoretical analysis with convincing arguments.
*   Appropriate simplification of the problem to enable rigorous analysis.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of experimental validation.
*   Limited discussion of practical considerations and computational complexity.

**Recommendation:** Accept. While the paper would be further strengthened by experimental results, the theoretical contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance to NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to consider incorporating experimental validation and a more detailed analysis of practical considerations in future work.
