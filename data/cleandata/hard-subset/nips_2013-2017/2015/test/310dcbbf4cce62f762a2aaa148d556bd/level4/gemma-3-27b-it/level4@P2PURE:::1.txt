The authors address the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit. Their primary contribution is a proof demonstrating that a regret of order \log(T) is achievable when the number of contexts and actions is finite, and the time horizon *T* is sufficiently large relative to the problem parameters. This represents a novel result within the context of contextual bandits. 

The results appear comprehensive, and the writing is precise. While the findings are interesting, they are not entirely unexpected. Given the finite number of contexts *J* and actions *K*, the problem can be framed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with *JK* arms, subject to the constraint that only *K* arms are accessible for each context. Consequently, for a sufficiently large *T*, a regret of \log(T) is expected, consistent with results from action-constrained bandit problems. This leads me to question the degree of innovation presented – a clarification from the authors in their rebuttal would be valuable.

I have several questions regarding the paper: First, in Theorem 2, the regret in case 2 is stated as O(\sqrt{T} + …). Is this accurate? Shouldn't it be at least O(\sqrt{KT} + …)? Second, also in Theorem 2, the regret is expressed as either O(KJ \log(T)) or O(\sqrt{T} + KJ\log(T)), depending on the problem configuration. Problem-dependent bounds are typically expressed with greater refinement, utilizing the sum of inverse arm gaps. Is a similar approach feasible here? I understand that the *KJ* term arises from the proof's reliance on events characterizing the correct ordering of UCB values, rather than concentration bounds on the gaps themselves. However, it is worth considering whether this approach is optimal. Finally, it would be valuable to have lower bounds for this problem, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. While trivial lower bounds exist based on the classical bandit setting (zero cost and constant context), can these be refined to account for the complexity introduced by the context? 

I remain unconvinced of the originality of this setting and therefore maintain a neutral assessment of the paper. 

I reiterate my impression that the problem setting is closely related to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints. This further fuels my uncertainty regarding the innovative nature of this work, and I encourage the authors to address this point in their rebuttal.
