This paper addresses the problem of constrained contextual bandit (CB) learning with a budget constraint, specifically focusing on discrete context and action spaces. The primary contribution lies in the development of an algorithm that leverages linear programming (LP) relaxation to achieve computational efficiency while maintaining a strong theoretical guarantee of O(log(T)) regret.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and its theoretical underpinnings. The authors effectively articulate the problem setting and motivate their solution. The technical details are presented with sufficient rigor without being overly dense. 

The proposed algorithm offers a significant practical advantage through its computational tractability, particularly important in constrained CB settings where many existing algorithms suffer from scalability issues. The achieved regret bound is also a valuable theoretical result, demonstrating near-optimality in many scenarios. Compared to existing work in constrained CB, this paper presents a demonstrable improvement in terms of both efficiency and theoretical performance. The use of LP relaxation is a clever technique for managing the complexity inherent in the constrained optimization problem.

I have a few suggestions that could further strengthen the paper. Firstly, the authors might consider exploring the use of a parametric model for the reward function in future work. This would be particularly beneficial when dealing with a large number of contexts, as it could lead to improved generalization and sample efficiency. 

Secondly, in Section 4.1, the rationale behind combining Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) with the Approximate Linear Program (ALP) could be clarified. While the connection to optimism in the face of uncertainty is intuitive, explicitly stating this connection – and perhaps drawing a parallel to the policy update rule in UCRL2 – would enhance the reader's understanding of the algorithm’s design choices. A more detailed explanation of *why* this particular combination is effective would be appreciated.

Finally, the inclusion of numerical experiments in an extended version of the paper would significantly bolster the practical impact of the work. Demonstrating the algorithm’s performance on synthetic or real-world datasets would provide concrete evidence of its effectiveness and allow for a more thorough comparison with existing approaches. 

I also recommend the authors consider the related work presented in [http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.03374]. 

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits. It offers an efficient algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees and is an improvement over existing techniques. I recommend acceptance.
