### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), a setting the authors term "constrained contextual bandits." The key challenge is that these constraints create complex temporal dependencies, making the standard exploration-exploitation trade-off significantly more difficult. The optimal policy (oracle), which would require dynamic programming, is computationally intractable and provides no clear benchmark for regret analysis.

The authors' main contribution is to develop a tractable and near-optimal approach by first approximating the oracle. For the case with known statistics, they propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm. ALP works by relaxing the hard budget constraint into an average one that is dynamically updated based on the remaining budget and time. This simple and efficient approximation is shown to achieve O(1) regret, i.e., it is within a constant gap of the true oracle, except for a specific set of "boundary cases" where regret is O(√T).

Building on this insight, the paper tackles the more realistic setting where expected rewards are unknown. They propose the UCB-ALP algorithm, which combines the ALP decision-making framework with an Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) estimation method. This combination is effective because ALP only requires the correct *ordering* of expected rewards, which UCB can learn efficiently. The main result of the paper is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) regret in boundary cases. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to establish a logarithmic regret bound for this problem setting. The authors also sketch extensions of their approach to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper provides the first logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits. This is a substantial theoretical improvement over previous O(√T) results in related, more general settings (e.g., [17]). The problem is well-motivated and has practical relevance in applications like crowdsourcing and clinical trials.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The core idea of using an adaptive linear programming relaxation to decouple decision-making from the complex state evolution is elegant and powerful. The analysis, particularly showing that the remaining budget concentrates around its mean (Lemma 2) even under an adaptive policy, is non-trivial and forms the foundation for the main regret bounds. This ALP approximation provides a principled way to manage a shared resource over time in a learning setting.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors build their argument methodically, starting with the simpler case of known statistics to introduce the ALP algorithm and build intuition, before moving to the full UCB-ALP algorithm for the unknown-reward setting. This progression makes the core ideas easy to follow.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for improvement):**

1.  **Motivation for Boundary Cases:** The distinction between "boundary" and "non-boundary" cases is central to the main results, but the reason for the performance degradation at the boundary is not sufficiently explained in the main text. From the LP solution in Eq. (4), it appears that when `ρ = q_j`, the system is at a knife-edge point where the optimal policy is indifferent between two deterministic thresholding rules. Small fluctuations in the empirical average budget `bτ/τ` would cause the policy to switch frequently between these rules, potentially increasing variance and regret. A more detailed and intuitive explanation of this phenomenon in the main paper would significantly improve clarity and strengthen the reader's understanding of the results. Is this analysis an artifact of the specific LP relaxation, or does it reflect a fundamental property of the problem?

2.  **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on several simplifying assumptions, such as a finite set of discrete contexts and, for the main UCB-ALP analysis, a known context distribution. While these assumptions are reasonable for an initial theoretical result, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the challenges involved in relaxing them. For example, how might the thresholding policy of ALP be generalized to settings with a large or infinite number of contexts, where estimating individual context probabilities `πj` becomes infeasible?

3.  **Details of Extensions:** The extensions to unknown context distributions (Section 5) and heterogeneous costs (Section 6) are interesting and important. However, they are treated very briefly in the main paper, with most details deferred to the supplement. While this is understandable given space constraints, providing a little more intuition in the main text—for instance, on how the structure of the LP changes for heterogeneous costs, or how the coupling argument for the EALP analysis works—would make the paper feel more complete.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on contextual bandits. The development of the ALP approximation as a tractable benchmark and a practical decision-making tool is a novel and important step. The subsequent UCB-ALP algorithm and its O(log T) regret bound represent a new state-of-the-art result for this problem. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity and the scope of the assumptions, and they do not detract from the core technical contribution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem. I recommend acceptance.
