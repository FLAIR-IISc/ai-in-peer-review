- Problem: studies contextual bandits with both time horizon T and budget B, where constraints induce complex temporal dependencies.  
- Main contribution: proposes UCB-ALP, a computationally efficient algorithm achieving near-optimal performance.  
- Oracle approximation: relaxes hard budget constraint to an average constraint solved by an LP to approximate the intractable oracle.  
- ALP policy: Adaptive LP dynamically updates the average-budget constraint using remaining budget/time ratio.  
- ALP guarantee: shown near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret vs. the oracle in non-boundary cases.  
- UCB-ALP: combines ALP planning with UCB optimistic reward estimates to handle unknown expected rewards.  
- Regret bounds: UCB-ALP attains O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(√T) in boundary cases.  
- Improvement over prior work: significantly better instance-dependent bounds than prior O(√T) results, while being computationally efficient.  
- Extensions: discusses handling unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.  
- Originality/significance: first to obtain an instance-dependent logarithmic regret bound for constrained contextual bandits.  
- Technical quality: rigorous analysis with key concentration argument via mapping remaining budget evolution to a hypergeometric distribution.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and structured, with a layered exposition (known-statistics ALP before unknown-statistics UCB-ALP).  
- Weakness — experiments: lacks any empirical evaluation to validate finite-sample behavior, constants, and runtime comparisons.  
- Weakness — assumptions: relies on simplifying assumptions (finite/discrete contexts and actions, known context distribution, unit costs) limiting applicability.  
- Weakness — exposition: should more explicitly state the UCB rationale as "optimism in the face of uncertainty" and analogize to planning in RL.  
- Weakness — related work: relevant prior work (Agrawal et al., 2015) is relegated to a footnote and should be integrated into the main related-work discussion.  
- Decision rationale: technically strong, novel, and well-presented paper; acceptance recommended despite lack of experiments and simplifying assumptions.
