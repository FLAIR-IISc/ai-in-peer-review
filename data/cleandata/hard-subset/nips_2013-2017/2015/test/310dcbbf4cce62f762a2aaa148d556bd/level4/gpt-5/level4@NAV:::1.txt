- I acknowledge it is not exactly the same, but it appears sufficiently close: when the horizon is large, the coupling due to context essentially disappears. Under that assumption, I do not see why each occurrence of a context could not be handled as a separate bandit instance.
- The paper would be significantly stronger with a clean problem-dependent upper bound stated in terms of arm gaps (the bound in the supplement needs revision), and ideally matching lower bounds in both problem-dependent and problem-independent regimes.
----------------------
The authors study a contextual bandit with both budget and time constraints. Their main result is that when the numbers of contexts and actions are finite, and when the time horizon T is sufficiently large (relative to the instance), a regret of order log(T) is achievable—claimed as new for contextual bandits.

The results appear thorough and the writing is precise. The findings are interesting but not very surprising. With finite contexts J and arms K, this can be viewed as a time- and budget-constrained bandit with JK arms, subject to action-availability constraints since only the K arms associated with the current context are available at each round. With finitely many arms, for large T, the regret scales as log(T), as in action-constrained bandit problems. Thus, I am unsure about the level of novelty—perhaps the authors can clarify this in their rebuttal.

I have a few questions:
- In Theorem 2, case (2), the regret is stated as O(√T + ...). Is it truly O(√T + ...)? Shouldn’t it be at least O(√(KT) + ...)?
- Still in Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ log T) or O(√T + KJ log T), depending on the arm configuration. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are more refined, e.g., expressed as sums of inverse arm gaps. Is a similar refinement possible here? I understand you obtain KJ instead of a sum over gaps because your proof is based on events about the correct ordering of UCB indices rather than concentration on the gaps; is this the optimal approach?
- Lower bounds would be valuable, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. Trivial lower bounds come from the classical bandit case with zero cost and a single, fixed context. Can these be sharpened to capture the complexity of the context?

I remain unconvinced about the originality of the setting and therefore stay neutral about this paper.
