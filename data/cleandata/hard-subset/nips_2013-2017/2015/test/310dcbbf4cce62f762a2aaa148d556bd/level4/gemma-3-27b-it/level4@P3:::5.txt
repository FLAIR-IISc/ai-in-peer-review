This paper considers a contextual bandit problem with both budget and time constraints, a setting that introduces significant challenges to the exploration-exploitation trade-off. The primary contribution lies in the development of an algorithm, UCB-ALP, that achieves logarithmic regret (except for boundary cases) and benefits from computational efficiency through the use of linear programming (LP) approximations. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas.

The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature on contextual bandits [1, 2, 3] and multi-armed bandits [4], highlighting the gap in addressing budget constraints, particularly in the presence of observable contexts. They correctly identify the limitations of prior work on constrained bandits [11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17] when applied to the contextual setting. The introduction provides sufficient motivation for the problem and clearly outlines the contributions of the paper. The use of LP to approximate the oracle is a clever approach, and the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm is well-motivated. Combining ALP with UCB to handle unknown rewards appears sound and leads to the UCB-ALP algorithm. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, with clear statements of theorems and lemmas. The discussion of boundary cases and the analysis under unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs further strengthen the paper.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper tackles a challenging problem – constrained contextual bandits – and provides the first logarithmic regret bound for this setting.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The use of LP approximations enables a computationally efficient algorithm, addressing a significant concern in practical applications.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis is thorough, with well-defined theorems and supporting lemmas.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making it easy to understand the proposed methods and their analysis.
*   **Generalization:** The authors extend their framework to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs, demonstrating the versatility of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Simplifying Assumptions:** The assumption of discrete contexts and actions, while simplifying the analysis, may limit the applicability of the algorithm to certain real-world scenarios.
*   **Boundary Cases:** The regret bound of O(√T) in boundary cases is a potential limitation. Understanding the prevalence of these cases and exploring ways to mitigate their impact would be valuable.
*   **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The absence of numerical experiments is a notable omission. Demonstrating the performance of UCB-ALP on synthetic or real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Related Work Overlap:** The paper could benefit from explicitly comparing its performance to more recent work, such as [21, 22], which were published concurrently and also address dynamic context cases (as noted by the authors).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of constrained contextual bandits.
*   The proposed algorithm is computationally efficient and achieves optimal regret bounds in most cases.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
*   The extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs demonstrate the generality of the approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The simplifying assumptions may limit the applicability of the algorithm.
*   The lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of UCB-ALP.
*   The boundary case regret bound is suboptimal, and the frequency of these cases is unclear.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are significant, and the clarity of the presentation is commendable. The lack of empirical evaluation is a concern, but I believe the theoretical rigor and potential impact of the work justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Minor Suggestions:**

*   Consider explicitly acknowledging and comparing to [21, 22] in the introduction or related work section.
*   The rationale for combining UCB and ALP in Section 4.1 could be more explicitly stated, linking it to the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle or UCRL2.
*   Including numerical experiments in a future version of the paper would significantly strengthen the results.
*   Discuss the practical implications of the boundary cases and potential strategies to address them.
