This paper introduces a compelling framework for contextual bandits that incorporates a budget, allowing the algorithm to strategically pass on rounds deemed suboptimal. The authors propose a novel solution that utilizes adaptive linear programming to manage this budget and analyze its performance against a stronger, more practical baseline than is typically considered.

The problem formulation is both interesting and well-motivated. By enabling an algorithm to skip rounds, the paper addresses a practical scenario often encountered in real-world applications where committing to any action can incur a significant cost. The proposed solution is unique and technically sound, and the analysis against an adaptive baseline represents a valuable contribution that moves beyond standard regret minimization frameworks.

My primary question for the authors concerns the technical analysis. In the proofs, the boundary cases are handled separately, but the motivation for this distinction is not immediately clear. Specifically, I would appreciate clarification on why the case where ρ = q_j requires a different line of analysis, particularly for j > 1. It does not seem, on the surface, that this condition presents a unique mathematical challenge that would necessitate a separate treatment from the general case. A brief explanation of the technical necessity for this distinction would significantly improve the paper's clarity.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel problem setting and an elegant solution. It is a valuable contribution to the bandits literature, and I would be happy to see it accepted pending a clear response to the question above.
