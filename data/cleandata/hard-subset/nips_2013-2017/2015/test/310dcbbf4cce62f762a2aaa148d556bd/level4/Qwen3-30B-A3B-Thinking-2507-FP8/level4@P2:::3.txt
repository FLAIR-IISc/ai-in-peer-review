

This paper addresses the constrained contextual bandit problem with budget and time constraints, where the agent must maximize reward while respecting a budget constraint on resource consumption. The authors propose algorithms that achieve logarithmic regret (O(log T)) for most cases, which is a significant improvement over prior work that achieved O(√T) regret. The key contribution is the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which approximates the oracle solution by relaxing the hard budget constraint to an average constraint, and the UCB-ALP algorithm that combines ALP with upper-confidence-bound methods for unknown statistics.

The paper builds on and extends previous work in contextual bandits [1,2,3,7] and constrained bandits [11-16,17]. While Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) [17] achieved O(√T) regret with a finite policy set benchmark, the current work provides a more natural regret definition and achieves logarithmic regret under a simplified model (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint). The authors demonstrate that their approach is computationally efficient while achieving superior regret bounds.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The problem formulation is precise, the key ideas (ALP, UCB-ALP) are well-motivated, and the technical development is logically structured. The authors effectively use linear programming as a tool to approximate the oracle solution, which is particularly elegant given the complexity of dynamic programming approaches. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance, with detailed regret bounds for both known and unknown statistics cases.

Regarding quality, the theoretical contributions are sound. The authors provide rigorous proofs for their regret bounds, including novel techniques for handling the interactions between information acquisition and decision making. The paper correctly identifies and addresses the main challenges in constrained contextual bandits: the complex coupling between contexts over time, the difficulty of implementing dynamic programming, and the challenge of extending to unknown statistics. The boundary cases where regret is O(√T) are clearly identified and analyzed, which demonstrates careful attention to the problem's nuances.

The originality of the work is strong. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first paper to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under the given assumptions. The ALP approximation technique is novel and provides important insights into how to handle budget constraints in contextual bandit settings. The connection between ALP and UCB is particularly clever, as it leverages the desirable property that ALP only requires the ordering of expected rewards rather than their exact values.

The significance of this work is high for both theoretical and practical reasons. Budget constraints are common in real-world applications like clinical trials and crowdsourcing, where resource limitations are critical. The proposed algorithms are computationally efficient and could be directly applied to these settings. The paper also provides a framework for addressing more general constrained contextual bandit problems, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

The main limitation is the boundary cases where the regret becomes O(√T) rather than O(log T). While the authors correctly identify that these cases occur for a set of parameters of zero Lebesgue measure, it would be valuable to discuss how common these boundary cases might be in practical applications. The paper could also better address how the results generalize to the case where T is unknown, which is a common practical constraint.

The paper's main strength is its elegant solution to a challenging problem that has received limited attention in the literature. The authors successfully bridge the gap between theoretical analysis and practical implementation, and their approach is both theoretically sound and computationally feasible. The writing is clear, the proofs are well-structured, and the contributions are properly contextualized within the existing literature.

Strengths:
- First to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits
- Clear, well-structured presentation of complex ideas
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with novel techniques
- Practical algorithms with computational efficiency
- Strong connection between theory and real-world applications

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of practical frequency of boundary cases
- Could better address the unknown T case
- The paper assumes u*1 > u*2 > ... > u*J without thorough justification for its necessity

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the contextual bandit literature by providing the first logarithmic regret solution for constrained contextual bandits. The theoretical results are well-supported, the algorithms are practical, and the writing is clear. While the boundary cases where regret becomes O(√T) are acknowledged, the paper could provide more insight into their practical relevance. Given the importance of the problem and the novelty of the solution, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field that will likely influence future research on constrained bandit problems.
