

This paper presents a well-motivated approach to contextual bandits by introducing a novel comparison against a sophisticated baseline that employs adaptive linear programming to strategically forgo a fraction of suboptimal rounds (termed "budget"). The setup is interesting, and the authors' solution addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. However, a critical clarification is needed regarding the treatment of boundary cases.  

Specifically, the paper analyzes cases where ρ = q_j separately for j > 1, yet the rationale for this distinction remains unclear. The condition ρ = q_j does not appear to introduce any unique structural properties or computational challenges for j > 1 that would necessitate specialized analysis. For instance, when j > 1, q_j typically represents a non-boundary quantile in the underlying distribution, and the equality ρ = q_j would not inherently alter the problem's dynamics compared to interior points. The authors should provide a concrete justification for this segmentation—either by demonstrating a mathematical discontinuity, a computational artifact, or a specific constraint in the algorithm that only manifests at these points. Without this, the boundary analysis risks appearing arbitrary, potentially undermining the paper’s technical rigor.  

The paper’s core contribution—leveraging adaptive LP to manage the "budget" of suboptimal rounds—remains compelling. This approach effectively models a more realistic learning scenario where agents can strategically skip rounds rather than always committing to a decision. The authors’ theoretical guarantees and empirical validation for this setup are strong, but the unresolved ambiguity around boundary cases requires attention. If the boundary treatment is indeed necessary, the paper must explicitly link it to the algorithm’s mechanics or the problem’s constraints; otherwise, the analysis may be overcomplicated.  

Minor suggestions to enhance clarity:  
1. Define "budget" more precisely in the introduction (e.g., "a user-specified fraction of rounds to be skipped") to avoid confusion with standard bandit "budgets."  
2. Consider whether the boundary analysis for j > 1 could be subsumed under a unified framework, which would simplify the exposition and strengthen the argument.  

Overall, this work offers a valuable extension to contextual bandit literature with a practical baseline. Addressing the boundary case justification would significantly elevate its technical contribution. The paper is suitable for acceptance pending clarification of this point.
