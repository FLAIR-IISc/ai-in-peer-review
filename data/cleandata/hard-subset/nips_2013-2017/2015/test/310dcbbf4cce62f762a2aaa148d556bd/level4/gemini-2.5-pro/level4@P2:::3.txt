This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), a practical and important extension of the standard setting. The agent's goal is to maximize the total reward before either the time horizon is reached or the budget is depleted. The key challenge identified by the authors is that these constraints introduce complex temporal dependencies, making the trade-off between exploration and exploitation significantly harder than in unconstrained settings.

The main contribution is a computationally efficient algorithm, UCB-ALP, that achieves a logarithmic regret bound of O(log T) for this problem, a significant improvement over the O(sqrt(T)) regret from prior work on related problems (e.g., [17]). The analysis is presented in a clear, bottom-up fashion. First, the authors consider a simplified unit-cost system with known statistics. For this setting, they propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, an elegant approximation of the computationally intractable oracle. ALP works by solving a linear program at each step that relaxes the hard budget constraint into an average one, adapted to the current remaining budget and time. The authors show that this approximation is remarkably effective, achieving O(1) regret in "non-boundary" cases. Building on this insight, they develop the UCB-ALP algorithm for the more realistic setting where expected rewards are unknown. This algorithm combines the UCB principle for exploration with the ALP framework for decision-making. The paper also discusses extensions to cases with unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by being the first, to my knowledge, to achieve logarithmic regret for a contextual bandit problem with hard budget constraints. The improvement from the state-of-the-art O(sqrt(T)) regret is substantial. The core idea of using an adaptive LP relaxation to decouple the complex planning problem from the learning problem is novel and insightful. This approach could prove influential for tackling other bandit problems with complex constraints.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of building intuition by starting with the simplified known-statistics case to introduce the ALP algorithm before moving to the full UCB-ALP algorithm. The problem is well-motivated with practical examples, and the analytical approach is clearly laid out.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed algorithm is simple and computationally efficient. The analysis appears solid and relies on a clever mapping of the budget's evolution to a "sampling without replacement" process, which allows the authors to leverage concentration inequalities (Lemma 2) to control the behavior of the system. This analytical technique is a key strength of the paper.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

1.  **Justification of the LP Upper Bound (Lemma 1):** The entire regret analysis hinges on Lemma 1, which states that the value of a static LP with an average budget constraint provides an upper bound on the oracle's performance. The oracle is bound by a hard budget constraint that must hold for every realization, whereas the LP considers an average constraint. It is not immediately clear why the expected reward under the hard constraint cannot exceed the optimal value of the relaxed LP. A more detailed justification or a proof sketch in the main paper (rather than only in the appendix) would greatly strengthen this foundational part of the argument.

2.  **Impact of Boundary Cases:** The main result of O(log T) regret holds for "non-boundary" cases, where the budget-to-time ratio `ρ` does not exactly equal a cumulative context probability `q_j`. While the authors note these boundary cases form a set of measure zero, their practical impact is worth discussing. How does the algorithm's performance degrade as `ρ` approaches a boundary? More importantly, is the O(sqrt(T)) regret achieved in these cases optimal? A discussion on whether a matching lower bound might exist for these specific instances would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's performance.

3.  **Assumption on Context Ordering:** For simplicity of exposition, the authors assume the contexts are ordered by their optimal expected rewards (u*1 > u*2 > ... > u*J). This is a benign assumption when these rewards are known. However, it is less clear how this applies to the unknown reward setting. Does the algorithm require *a priori* knowledge of this ordering, or does it learn the ordering of contexts as well as the ordering of actions within a context? If it's the latter, the analysis should account for potential errors in learning this higher-level ordering.

**Minor Comments:**

*   It would be interesting if the authors could comment on the computational complexity of the true oracle problem. Is it known to be NP-hard? This would further motivate the need for the ALP approximation.
*   Could the analysis be extended to scenarios where the time horizon T is not known in advance?

**Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant advance in the study of constrained contextual bandits. The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is simple and effective, and the O(log T) regret bound is a clear improvement over prior work. The approach of using an adaptive LP relaxation is both novel and powerful. While there are a few points that would benefit from further clarification, the core contributions are strong and impactful. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
