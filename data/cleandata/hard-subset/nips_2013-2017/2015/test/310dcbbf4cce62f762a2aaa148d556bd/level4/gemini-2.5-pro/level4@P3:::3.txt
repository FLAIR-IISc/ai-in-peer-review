### **Summary**

This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), a setting referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The core challenge is that the budget couples decisions across time, making the exploration-exploitation tradeoff significantly more complex than in the unconstrained setting. The authors' main contribution is a computationally efficient algorithm, UCB-ALP, that achieves logarithmic (`O(log T)`) regret, a significant improvement over prior `O(sqrt T)` bounds for related problems.

The authors build their approach methodically. First, they analyze the simplified setting where all system statistics (context distribution, expected rewards) are known. They propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) method to approximate the intractable dynamic programming oracle. This ALP algorithm is shown to be near-optimal, achieving `O(1)` regret except in specific "boundary cases". A key feature of ALP is that it only requires the relative ordering of expected rewards, not their exact values. This property enables the second step: for the more realistic setting with unknown rewards, the authors combine ALP with an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) estimation approach. The resulting UCB-ALP algorithm successfully learns the reward structure while managing the budget, achieving `O(log T)` regret in non-boundary cases and `O(sqrt T)` in boundary cases. The paper also discusses extensions to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs per action.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** This work appears to be the first to achieve logarithmic regret for a constrained contextual bandit problem. This is a substantial improvement over the `O(sqrt T)` regret achieved in the more general setting of Resourceful Contextual Bandits [17]. The problem is well-motivated and has direct applications in areas like crowdsourcing and clinical trials where resources are finite.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The core technical idea of approximating the DP-based oracle with an adaptive linear program is both elegant and effective. The analysis, which connects the evolution of the remaining budget to sampling without replacement and leverages concentration inequalities to bound the regret, is novel and insightful. The way the authors decouple the regret from action-ranking errors and context-ranking errors is clever and leads to a clean analysis.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors build their case logically, starting from the oracle with known statistics and progressively moving to the more complex bandit setting. This structure makes the key ideas—particularly the intuition behind the ALP approximation—easy to follow and appreciate.

4.  **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is simple to implement and computationally efficient, relying on solving a small LP at each step. This makes it a practical solution for the problem it addresses.

### **Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Boundary Cases:** The primary limitation of the result is the distinction between non-boundary and boundary cases. The logarithmic regret holds only when the budget-to-time ratio (`ρ`) does not precisely equal the cumulative probability of a subset of the best contexts (`q_j`). In the boundary cases, the regret degrades to `O(sqrt T)`. While the authors correctly state that these boundary points have zero Lebesgue measure, it would be valuable to discuss their practical impact. For instance, how sensitive is the performance to being *near* a boundary? Furthermore, the paper would be even stronger if it could either provide a matching `Ω(sqrt T)` lower bound for these specific cases, which would establish the tightness of the analysis, or discuss potential algorithmic modifications to handle them more gracefully.

2.  **Justification for LP Relaxation:** The central argument for using an LP approximation is that the optimal DP solution is intractable. While this is intuitively true, a brief formal statement regarding the computational hardness of the oracle problem (e.g., is it NP-hard?) would better motivate the need for the approximation.

3.  **Clarity of Lemma 1:** The analysis hinges on Lemma 1, which states that the static LP solution provides an upper bound on the oracle's performance. The proof is deferred to the appendix. Given the lemma's importance, a short, intuitive explanation in the main text of why relaxing the hard, path-dependent budget constraint to an average budget constraint yields a valid upper bound would improve the paper's self-containment and readability.

### **Questions for Authors**

*   Is the assumption `u^*_1 > u^*_2 > ... > u^*_J` made without loss of generality? In the known-statistics case, one can simply re-index the contexts. However, in the bandit setting, this ordering is unknown and must be learned. Does the analysis hold irrespective of the true underlying (and unknown) ordering? A brief clarification would be helpful.
*   Could the proposed framework be extended to handle an unknown or random time horizon T?

### **Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to an important and practical problem. The proposed algorithm is simple and intuitive, yet the analysis is technically sophisticated. The achievement of logarithmic regret is a clear advance for constrained bandit problems. Despite the minor weakness concerning the boundary cases, the paper's strengths in significance, technical quality, and clarity are overwhelming. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
