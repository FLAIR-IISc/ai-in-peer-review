This paper studies contextual bandits with both time and budget constraints and proposes an adaptive linear programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle when the statistics are known, showing O(1) regret except on measure-zero “boundary” instances. Building on this, the authors combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP) to learn the ordering of expected rewards and obtain O(log T) regret for non-boundary cases (and O(√T) on the boundary). The work contrasts with prior resourceful contextual bandits results that provide O(√T) regret under more general settings and higher complexity; here the focus on finite contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint yields computationally simple algorithms and stronger, instance-dependent guarantees.

My (single) question is about the boundary cases: why must they be analyzed separately, and what makes ρ = qj intrinsically special, especially for j > 1? From the presentation, it is not obvious why exact alignment of the average budget ρ with a cumulative context probability threshold qj should fundamentally change the regret order. Some intuition for the source of the √T term would help: for instance, is the issue that small stochastic fluctuations of bτ/τ around the threshold cause frequent switches in the optimal LP solution, creating unavoidable “thrashing”? If so, can this effect be mitigated by a smoothing or tie-breaking scheme (e.g., adding a margin, randomized thresholding, or biasing toward one side) without sacrificing correctness in non-boundary cases? More broadly, are there lower bounds showing that √T regret is unavoidable on these boundary instances for any policy that only relies on reward ordering (rather than exact values), or is the rate an artifact of the analysis?

Overall, the setup is interesting and the solutions are distinctive: using an adaptive LP as a decision backbone is a clean idea, and combining it with UCB to learn orderings is appealing and computationally efficient.
