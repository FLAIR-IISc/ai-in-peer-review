

This paper considers a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint $B$ and a known time horizon $T$. At each trial, the agent observes a context $j$ drawn from a fixed distribution $\pi$ over a context set $\{1, \dots, J\}$ and selects an action $k \in \{0\} \cup \{1, \dots, K\}$, where $k=0$ denotes "skip." If $k=0$, the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost. If $k > 0$, the agent receives a reward $y$ drawn from a fixed distribution over $[0,1]$ (depending on $j$ and $k$) and incurs a cost $c_{j,k} > 0$, which is known to the agent. The goal is to maximize cumulative reward subject to the constraint that cumulative cost never exceeds $B$.

The main contribution focuses on the special case where $\pi$ is known and $c_{j,k} = 1$ for all $j,k$, presenting an algorithm with an $O(\log T)$ regret bound under a mild condition. The algorithm is extended to the general case where $\pi$ is unknown and $c_{j,k}$ are arbitrary. While a more general setting is addressed in [16] with an $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret bound, this paper appears to be the first achieving $O(\log T)$ regret, provided the mild condition holds. The algorithm leverages an approximation for computing the optimal action sequence under full statistical knowledge and is simple yet insightful.

The paper is well motivated and clearly written. Its significance would be enhanced if the $O(\log T)$ regret bound held universally, as an $\Omega(\sqrt{T})$ lower bound exists in boundary cases. All results rely on Lemma 1, but its correctness was initially unclear to me: I questioned whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies Equation (2) for all trials and thus why the average constraint is a relaxed version of the hard constraint. After the rebuttal, I am convinced of Lemma 1's validity. Thanks.

Minor comments:  
1. Is obtaining the optimal action sequence exactly NP-hard?  
2. Can the results be generalized to unknown $T$?  
3. Without loss of generality, can we assume $u^1 > u^2 > \dots > u^*_J$ even for unknown $\pi$?  

I strongly recommend acceptance.
