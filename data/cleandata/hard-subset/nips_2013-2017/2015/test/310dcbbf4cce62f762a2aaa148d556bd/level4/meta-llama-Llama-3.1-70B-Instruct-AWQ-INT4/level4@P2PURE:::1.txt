Review after rebuttal:
Upon reevaluation, I still believe that the setting presented in this paper is sufficiently similar to a standard bandit problem, especially when the horizon is large enough, causing the coupling of the context to vanish. I remain unclear as to why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as a separate instance of a bandit problem.

To significantly strengthen this paper, I would recommend providing a clean upper bound in terms of arm gaps for the problem-dependent case, ideally accompanied by matching lower bounds for both problem-dependent and problem-independent cases. The supplementary material's bound requires reworking to achieve this.

The authors investigate the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit. Their primary contribution is proving that, for a finite number of contexts and actions, and a sufficiently large time constraint T, a regret of order log(T) is achievable, which is a novel result for contextual bandits.

The results appear thorough, and the writing is precise. However, the findings, although interesting, are not particularly surprising. Given that the number of contexts J and arms K are finite, this bandit problem can be viewed as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms, which is also action-constrained since not all JK arms are accessible at each time. Since the number of arms is finite, for T large enough, the regret is log(T), similar to action-constrained bandit problems. This raises questions about the paper's innovation, and the authors' clarification in their rebuttal would be beneficial.

I have several questions regarding this paper:

1. In Theorem 2, the regret in case 2) is O(sqrt(T) + ...). However, shouldn't it be O(sqrt(KT) + ...) at the very least?

2. Still in Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ log(T)) or O(sqrt(T) + KJ log(T)), depending on the configuration of the arms. Typically, problem-dependent bounds are expressed in a more refined manner, using the sum of inverse arm gaps. Is it possible to achieve something similar in this context? Although the proof relies on events characterizing the correct orders of the UCB, rather than concentration bounds on their gaps, I wonder if this approach is optimal.

3. It would be valuable to establish lower bounds for this problem, ideally both problem-dependent and problem-independent. While trivial lower bounds exist, corresponding to classical bandit problems with zero cost and a constant context, refining these bounds to account for the complexity of the context would be beneficial.

I remain unconvinced by the originality of this setting, which appears very close to a budgeted bandit problem with action constraints, and therefore remain neutral about this paper.
