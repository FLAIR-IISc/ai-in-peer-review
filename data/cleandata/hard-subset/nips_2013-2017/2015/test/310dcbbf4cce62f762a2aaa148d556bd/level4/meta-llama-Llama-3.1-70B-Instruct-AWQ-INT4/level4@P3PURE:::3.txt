This paper considers a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint B and a known time horizon T. For each trial, the agent observes a context j chosen according to a fixed probability distribution π over a context set {1, ..., J} and takes an action k from {0} ∪ {1, ..., K}, where 0 means "skip". If k = 0, then the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost. If k > 0, then the agent receives a reward y and incurs a cost c{j, k}, where y is chosen according to a fixed probability distribution over [0, 1] that depends on j and k, and c{j, k} > 0 is a parameter known to the agent. The goal of the agent is to maximize the cumulative reward under the condition that the cumulative cost never exceeds the budget B.

The main body of the paper focuses on a special case where π is known and c{j, k} = 1 for all j and k, and presents an algorithm with a regret bound of O(log T) under a mild condition. The paper then extends the algorithm to the general case where π is unknown and c{j, k}'s are arbitrary. This is an improvement over the work in [16], which considers a more general setting but achieves a regret bound of O(√T). Therefore, this paper may be the first to achieve a regret bound of O(log T), although this result relies on the satisfaction of a mild condition. The algorithm is based on an approximation algorithm for computing the optimal action sequence when all the statistics are known. While the algorithm is simple, it is also very interesting.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. However, it would be even more interesting and significant if the O(log T) regret bound held without the need for the mild condition. Alternatively, proving an Ω(√T) lower bound for the boundary cases would strengthen the paper's results.

Initially, I was not convinced of the correctness of Lemma 1, as I did not believe that the optimal action sequence always satisfies equation (2) for all trials. Consequently, I did not understand why the average constraint was a relaxed version of the hard constraint. However, after reviewing the rebuttal, I am now convinced of Lemma 1's correctness.

Some minor comments and suggestions for future work: Is it NP-hard to exactly obtain the optimal action sequence? Can the results be generalized to the case where T is unknown? Can we assume u^1 > u^2 > ... > u^*_J without loss of generality, even for the unknown context distribution case? I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
