

This paper studies constrained contextual bandits, where the agent faces both budget and time constraints while making decisions based on observable contexts. The authors consider a setting with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which is justified by practical applications like clinical trials and wireless network rate selection. The main contribution is an algorithm (UCB-ALP) that achieves O(log T) regret under non-boundary conditions, which is a significant improvement over previous works that achieve O(√T) regret for similar problems.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature. Previous work on contextual bandits (e.g., [5, 6, 7]) focuses on maximizing rewards without budget constraints, while budget-constrained bandits (e.g., [11-16]) typically assume non-contextual settings. The only related NIPS work on contextual bandits with constraints is [17], which achieves O(√T) regret for a more general setting. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach is the first to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, making a clear contribution to the field.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The quality is excellent: the authors provide rigorous theoretical analysis, including novel techniques for handling the interactions between exploration and exploitation under budget constraints. Their ALP approximation of the oracle algorithm is particularly elegant, achieving near-optimal performance with O(1) regret (except for boundary cases) while only requiring the ordering of expected rewards rather than their exact values. This property enables the combination with UCB for unknown statistics, leading to the O(log T) regret bound for UCB-ALP.

The clarity of the paper is outstanding. The problem formulation is precise, the ALP algorithm is well-motivated and clearly explained, and the regret analysis is structured logically. The authors effectively use mathematical notation to convey complex ideas without unnecessary complexity. The paper is well-organized, with clear transitions between sections, and provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

The originality is strong. While the idea of using linear programming to approximate dynamic programming solutions has appeared elsewhere (e.g., [17, 19]), the specific design and analysis of ALP for constrained contextual bandits is novel. The paper makes a clear distinction from prior work, particularly in how they handle the adaptive budget constraint, which enables the logarithmic regret bound. The authors also properly contextualize their work against recent NIPS papers [21, 22] that extend [20] to dynamic contexts but still achieve O(√T) regret.

The significance of the work is high. Constrained contextual bandits model important real-world applications where resources are limited (e.g., clinical trials with budget constraints, online recommendation systems with cost per impression). The O(log T) regret bound represents a substantial improvement over previous approaches, and the computational efficiency of the proposed algorithms makes them practical for large-scale applications. The insights gained about decoupling information acquisition from decision making could inform future work on more general constrained bandit problems.

The main weakness is the boundary cases where the regret becomes O(√T). While the authors acknowledge this limitation and provide a thorough analysis, it would be stronger if they could address why these boundary cases are unavoidable or provide additional intuition about their prevalence. The paper assumes unit costs for the main results, which is a simplification that might limit applicability to certain real-world settings, though the authors do extend to heterogeneous costs in Section 6.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-justified. The draft review's concern about Lemma 1 was addressed in the rebuttal, and the authors' explanation of how the average budget constraint serves as a valid relaxation of the hard budget constraint is convincing. The paper's contribution is significant, with clear connections to both theoretical and practical aspects of contextual bandits.

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field with a novel algorithm that achieves logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, a problem that has received limited attention in the literature. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the writing is clear, and the results have significant practical implications. While the boundary cases with O(√T) regret are a limitation, they are well-characterized and represent a small fraction of the problem space, making the overall contribution highly valuable for the NIPS community. The paper's insights into the interplay between information acquisition and decision making under constraints represent a meaningful advancement in the field.
