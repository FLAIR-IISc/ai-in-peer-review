

This paper investigates constrained contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, where the agent must maximize cumulative reward subject to a hard budget constraint. The authors propose a novel approach that achieves logarithmic regret in non-boundary cases, representing a significant improvement over prior work that achieved O(√T) regret in similar settings. Previous work on constrained bandits [17, 20] either considered simpler settings or achieved suboptimal regret bounds, while the current paper provides the first demonstration of logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under reasonable assumptions.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) developing an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation of the oracle algorithm for known statistics that achieves O(1) regret in non-boundary cases; (2) combining ALP with UCB to create UCB-ALP, which achieves O(log T) regret for unknown expected rewards; and (3) extending these results to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs. The authors provide rigorous theoretical analysis supported by novel concentration techniques and careful regret decomposition.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical results are thoroughly analyzed, with careful attention to boundary cases. The authors properly acknowledge limitations (e.g., logarithmic regret only holds in non-boundary cases) and provide a clear characterization of when the results apply. The experimental validation, while limited to theoretical analysis in the main paper, is supported by detailed proofs in the supplementary material.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation, key insights, and algorithm design are clearly explained. The authors effectively use the unit-cost setting as a stepping stone to more general cases, making the complex ideas accessible. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results, with clear definitions of all key concepts and algorithms.

Originality: This work makes a substantial original contribution by achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, a setting where previous approaches only achieved O(√T) regret. The ALP approximation technique is novel in this context, and the authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work, particularly from [17] (Resourceful Contextual Bandits) and [20] (Bandits with concave rewards and convex constraints). The paper also provides valuable insights into how to decouple information acquisition and decision making in constrained bandit problems.

Significance: The results have clear practical implications for applications with budget constraints, such as clinical trials and online recommendation systems. The work advances the state of the art in contextual bandits by providing the first logarithmic regret guarantees for constrained contextual bandits. The insights about using linear programming approximations and adaptive budget constraints could inform future work on constrained bandits and reinforcement learning.

Strengths: The paper makes a technically sophisticated contribution that is well-motivated and clearly presented. The ALP approximation provides a clever way to handle the complex budget constraints while maintaining computational efficiency. The regret analysis is particularly impressive, with careful handling of multiple interacting factors including context ranking errors and budget fluctuations.

Weaknesses: The logarithmic regret guarantee holds only for non-boundary cases, with O(√T) regret in boundary cases. While the authors properly acknowledge this limitation, it would be even stronger to see a more detailed discussion of when these boundary cases might occur in practice and whether they represent significant practical limitations. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with the recent extensions [21, 22] that also address dynamic contexts.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to a fundamental problem in machine learning with clear connections to real-world applications. The results are novel, technically sound, and well-presented, making this a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to further clarify the boundary cases and their practical implications.
