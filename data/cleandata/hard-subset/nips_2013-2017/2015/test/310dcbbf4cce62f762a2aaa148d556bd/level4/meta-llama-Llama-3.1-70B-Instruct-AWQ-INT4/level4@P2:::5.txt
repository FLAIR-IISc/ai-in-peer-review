This paper considers a budget-constrained contextual bandit (cb) problem with discrete contexts and actions. The main contribution is the computational efficiency of the proposed algorithm, which utilizes a linear programming (LP) approximation to achieve a logarithmic regret bound.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it a pleasure to read. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their algorithm and its theoretical analysis.

The key insight of this paper is the use of LP approximation to tackle the budget-constrained cb problem. By relaxing the hard budget constraint to an average budget constraint, the authors are able to design an efficient algorithm that achieves near-optimal performance. The proposed Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm is simple yet effective, and its combination with the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method leads to a logarithmic regret bound.

The theoretical analysis of the paper is rigorous and well-done. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the regret of their algorithm and show that it achieves logarithmic regret in most cases. The use of concentration inequalities and the study of the evolution of the remaining budget are particularly noteworthy.

One potential area for improvement is the consideration of a parametric model for the reward. This could be useful when the number of contexts is large, as it would allow for a more compact representation of the reward function.

In Section 4.1, the authors combine UCB with ALP, but the rationale behind this combination could be more clearly explained. It appears that the authors are following the principle of "optimism in the face of uncertainty," which is equivalent to the policy update in UCRL2. A clearer explanation of this connection would be helpful.

Additionally, including some numerical experiments in an extended version of the paper would be beneficial. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's performance in practice.

Overall, this paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of budget-constrained contextual bandits. The proposed algorithm is efficient and achieves optimal regret in most cases, making it a valuable addition to the literature.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel and efficient algorithm for budget-constrained contextual bandits.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-done, providing a thorough understanding of the algorithm's performance.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper considers a relatively simple budget-constrained cb problem with discrete contexts and actions. It may be beneficial to consider more complex scenarios or extensions to the algorithm.
* The paper could benefit from the inclusion of numerical experiments to demonstrate the algorithm's performance in practice.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
