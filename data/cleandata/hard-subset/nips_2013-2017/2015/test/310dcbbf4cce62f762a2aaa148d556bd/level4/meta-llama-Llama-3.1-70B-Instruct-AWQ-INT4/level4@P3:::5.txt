This paper considers a budget-constrained contextual bandit (cb) problem with discrete contexts and actions. The main contribution is the computational efficiency of the algorithm, achieved through linear programming (LP) approximation, and the logarithmic regret bound it achieves.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it a pleasure to read. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, and the technical sections are well-organized and easy to understand.

The paper's main strength is its contribution to the field of constrained cb. The proposed algorithm, UCB-ALP, achieves logarithmic regret in most cases, which is a significant improvement over existing work. The use of LP approximation is a clever idea, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance.

One potential area for improvement is the consideration of more complex models for the reward function. As the number of contexts increases, a parametric model may be more useful. The authors may want to explore this direction in future work.

In Section 4.1, when combining UCB with ALP, the authors could provide more clarity on the rationale behind the UCB-ALP algorithm. Specifically, they seem to be following the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle, which is equivalent to the policy update in UCRL2. A brief discussion on this point would be helpful.

Additionally, including some numerical experiments in an extended version of the paper would be beneficial. This would provide further evidence of the algorithm's performance and help to illustrate the theoretical results.

The authors may also want to consider citing the paper "Contextual bandits with global constraints and objective" (arXiv:1506.03374), which appears to be related to their work.

Overall, this paper merits acceptance due to its efficient algorithm for constrained cb with optimal regret in most cases. It is an interesting and significant improvement over existing work, and the authors have made a valuable contribution to the field.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel and efficient algorithm for constrained cb with logarithmic regret in most cases.
* The use of LP approximation is a clever idea, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper considers a relatively simple model for the reward function, and may not be applicable to more complex scenarios.
* The authors could provide more clarity on the rationale behind the UCB-ALP algorithm.
* The paper lacks numerical experiments to illustrate the theoretical results.
