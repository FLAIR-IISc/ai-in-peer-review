This paper considers a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint B and a known time horizon T. In each trial, the agent observes a context *j* chosen according to a fixed probability distribution π over a context set {1,..,J} and takes an action *k* from {0} ∪ {1,..,K}, where 0 represents “skip”. If *k* = 0, the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost. If *k* > 0, the agent receives a reward *y* and incurs a cost *c<sub>j,k</sub>*, where *y* is drawn from a fixed distribution over [0,1] dependent on *j* and *k*, and *c<sub>j,k</sub>* > 0 is a known parameter. The agent’s goal is to maximize cumulative reward subject to a cumulative cost not exceeding the budget B.

The core of the paper focuses on a special case where π is known and *c<sub>j,k</sub>* = 1 for all *j* and *k*, providing an algorithm with a regret bound of O(log T) under certain conditions. The paper then extends this algorithm to the more general case where π is unknown and the *c<sub>j,k</sub>* values are arbitrary. 

A related work [16] considers a more general setting, but achieves a regret of O(√T). Therefore, this paper potentially represents the first work to achieve O(log T) regret, contingent on the specified conditions being met. The proposed algorithm is based on an approximation of the optimal action sequence when all statistics are known and is presented as simple yet interesting.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. It would be strengthened by demonstrating that O(log T) regret *always* holds, or by proving a corresponding Ω(√T) lower bound for the boundary cases.

The results rely heavily on Lemma 1, which initially raised concerns regarding its correctness. Specifically, it wasn’t immediately clear why the average constraint accurately relaxes the hard constraint, and whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies the conditions described. However, the rebuttal clarified this point, and I am now convinced of Lemma 1’s validity.

Minor comments: Is finding the exact optimal action sequence NP-hard? Could the results be generalized to scenarios where T is unknown? Can we assume *u<sup>1</sup>* > *u<sup>2</sup>* > ... > *u<sup>*</sup><sub>J</sub>* without loss of generality, even when the context distribution is unknown?

Overall, I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
