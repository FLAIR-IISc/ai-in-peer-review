- Problem: studies contextual bandits with time horizon T and budget B, introducing coupling across time; proposes UCB-ALP achieving O(log T) regret, improving over prior O(√T) results.

- Approach (known stats): approximate intractable dynamic program with an Adaptive Linear Program (ALP) that adapts to remaining budget/time and attains O(1) or O(√T) regret vs. oracle.

- Approach (unknown rewards): combine ALP with UCB to form UCB-ALP, yielding O(log T) regret except for certain boundary cases where regret is O(√T).

- Extensions: discusses handling unknown context distributions and heterogeneous action costs.

- Strength — relevance: tackles a practically important problem (advertising, trials, crowdsourcing) with finite resources.

- Strength — methodology: ALP is a novel, simple, and computationally efficient approximation that only needs reward ordering, fitting naturally with UCB exploration.

- Strength — theory: main result of O(log T) regret is a significant theoretical advance for constrained contextual bandits.

- Strength — presentation: paper is well-written, building intuition via simpler known-statistics analysis before addressing unknown rewards.

- Weakness — originality: finite-context setting may reduce to a standard budgeted multi-armed bandit (JK arms); authors should clarify unique technical challenges from dynamic context arrivals.

- Weakness — coarse bounds: Theorem 2 gives O(JK log T) regret; reviewers request refined, gap-dependent bounds (e.g., Σ 1/Δ) instead of this coarse form.

- Weakness — missing lower bounds: no matching lower bounds are provided, so optimality of O(log T) and necessity of O(√T) in boundary cases are unclear.

- Weakness — boundary-case clarity: unclear whether the O(√T) term hides dependencies on J and K (e.g., O(√(JKT))); authors should clarify scaling.
