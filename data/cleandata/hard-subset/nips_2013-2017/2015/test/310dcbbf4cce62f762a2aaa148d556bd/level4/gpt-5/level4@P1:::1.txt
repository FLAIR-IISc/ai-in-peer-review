Review after rebuttal:
I agree the setting is not exactly the same as a budgeted bandit with action constraints, but it still seems quite close. If the horizon is large enough, the coupling induced by random contexts appears to vanish, which raises the question of why each context occurrence cannot be treated as a separate bandit instance. My assessment remains that the novelty needs to be clarified. I also continue to believe the problem-dependent upper bound in the supplement should be reworked to be cleaner, ideally in terms of arm gaps.

Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies contextual bandits with both budget and time constraints (“constrained contextual bandits”). With finite context and action sets, and assuming unit costs initially, the authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) approximation to the oracle policy by relaxing the hard budget to an average budget and adapting the LP to the remaining budget/time ratio. With known rewards, ALP achieves O(1) regret except on boundary cases (where O(√T) holds). For unknown rewards, they combine ALP with UCB (UCB-ALP), proving RUCB-ALP(T,B) = O(JK log T) in non-boundary cases and O(√T + JK log T) on boundaries. They extend the approach to unknown context distributions via empirical estimation (EALP/UCB-EALP) and to heterogeneous costs (with an ε-first variant). They position the work against Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Mixture Elimination, O(√T) regret within a finite policy set and higher complexity) and recent work on bandits with global constraints (typically O(√T) in dynamic contexts). The authors claim this is the first work to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits under these assumptions.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation and thorough analysis under a practically relevant simplification (finite contexts/actions, fixed/known costs initially).
- The ALP relaxation is elegant and yields a tractable benchmark and algorithm with strong performance (O(1) non-boundary regret with known rewards).
- UCB-ALP is simple and computationally efficient, with clean O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases.
- Useful technical insights, especially concentration of the remaining budget via a sampling-without-replacement view, enabling clean regret decomposition.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Novelty/significance: With finite J and K, the setting seems close to a budgeted, action-constrained bandit with JK arms, where at each round only K arms are available (those matching the current context). For large T, a logarithmic regret rate is not surprising. The paper would benefit from a sharper discussion of what fundamentally prevents a reduction to a standard (action-constrained) budgeted bandit, and where the coupling across contexts truly matters asymptotically.
- Theorem 2 (boundary cases): The stated regret is O(√T + JK log T). Is the √T term genuinely tight here? Shouldn’t one expect at least O(√(K T)) in these boundary regimes, given K actions are available under a context? Please clarify the dependence on K in the sublinear term and whether O(√T) versus O(√(KT)) is correct.
- Problem-dependent bounds: The bounds in Theorem 2 are problem-independent (up to factors JK). In finite-armed bandits, problem-dependent bounds typically scale with inverse gap sums. Can you derive a gap-dependent regret bound here (both within-context action gaps and, if relevant, across-context threshold gaps)? The current proof structure based on UCB ranking events leads to JK log T; a gap-based analysis could be more refined and informative. I also found the supplementary bound insufficiently clean; a reworked, gap-based upper bound would strengthen the contribution.
- Lower bounds: It would be valuable to provide matching or near-matching lower bounds, both problem-dependent and problem-independent, tailored to the constrained contextual setting (beyond trivial reductions: zero cost, single context). Can the lower bounds incorporate the complexity induced by context variability and the budget/time coupling?
- Clarity on boundary cases: The boundary regime drives the √T term. It would help to more explicitly characterize when these cases occur (measure-zero statements aside) and how sensitive the regret is to small perturbations of ρ around boundaries.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound and the analysis appears careful. The decomposition of regret and the concentration arguments are well-executed. However, the current regret guarantees could be strengthened by:
- A clean, problem-dependent (gap-based) upper bound.
- Clarification and, if possible, tightening of the √T term’s dependence on K in boundary cases.
- Context-sensitive lower bounds.

Clarity:
The writing is precise and generally easy to follow. The supplementary material is useful but some bounds should be streamlined and presented in a more standard gap-dependent form. A clearer comparison to budgeted bandits with action constraints and to bandits with knapsacks would help situate the novelty.

Originality:
The adaptive LP idea in this constrained contextual setting is appealing, but the overall rate improvements with finite J and K feel unsurprising. The work would be more compelling with explicit arguments showing why reductions to standard budgeted/action-constrained bandits fail (or are suboptimal) and where the LP-based adaptivity provides a genuine advantage.

Significance:
Achieving logarithmic regret with simple, efficient algorithms under constraints is potentially useful. The practical impact depends on the tightness of the bounds (especially gap-dependent ones), lower bounds, and clarity about when the boundary regime matters.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-structured analysis with strong non-boundary guarantees.
- Simple, implementable algorithms (ALP, UCB-ALP).
- Insightful techniques (budget concentration, regret decomposition).

Cons for acceptance:
- Novelty relative to budgeted/action-constrained bandits is not fully convincing.
- Lack of gap-dependent bounds and tailored lower bounds.
- Ambiguity in the √T term’s dependence on K in boundary cases.
- Supplementary bounds would benefit from reworking.

Overall:
Interesting and carefully executed, but I am not fully convinced about the originality and tightness of the results in light of their proximity to budgeted/action-constrained bandits with finite arms. I remain neutral and would be swayed by clearer novelty claims, gap-dependent upper bounds, and appropriate lower bounds.
