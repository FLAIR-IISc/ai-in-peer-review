This paper addresses a contextual bandit problem where an agent is subject to a budget constraint B and a known time horizon T. In each trial, the agent observes a context j drawn from a fixed probability distribution π over a set of contexts {1,..,J} and selects an action k from the set {0} ∪ {1,..,K}, where 0 denotes "skip". If k = 0, the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost; otherwise, the agent receives a reward y, drawn from a fixed probability distribution over [0,1] that depends on j and k, and incurs a cost c{j,k}, which is a known positive parameter. The agent's objective is to maximize the cumulative reward while ensuring that the cumulative cost does not exceed the budget B.

The majority of the paper focuses on a special case where π is known and c{j,k} = 1 for all j and k, presenting an algorithm with a regret bound of O(log T) under a mild condition. The paper further extends this algorithm to the general case where π is unknown and c{j,k} values are arbitrary.

Notably, a more general setting is explored in [16], but it yields an algorithm with O(√T) regret, making this paper potentially the first to achieve O(log T) regret, contingent upon satisfying the mild condition. The algorithm relies on an approximation method for computing the optimal action sequence when all statistics are known. Although simple, the algorithm is highly intriguing.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. Its significance would be enhanced if the O(log T) regret bound held universally; otherwise, proving an Ω(√T) lower bound for boundary cases would be valuable.

The paper's results appear to hinge on Lemma 1, but I initially questioned its validity, specifically whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies equation (2) for all trials, and thus whether the average constraint is a relaxed version of the hard constraint. However, the rebuttal has alleviated my concerns regarding Lemma 1.

Minor suggestions include investigating whether exactly obtaining the optimal action sequence is NP-hard, whether the results can be generalized to the case where T is unknown, and whether assuming u^1 > u^2 > ... > u^*_J without loss of generality is feasible even for the unknown context distribution case. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
