This paper considers a contextual bandit problem with a budget constraint B and a known time horizon T. In each trial, the agent observes a context j drawn from a fixed probability distribution π over a context set {1, ..., J} and selects an action k from {0} ∪ {1, ..., K}, where 0 represents "skip". If k = 0, the agent receives no reward and incurs no cost. If k > 0, the agent receives a reward y and incurs a cost c{j,k}, where y is chosen according to a fixed probability distribution over [0,1] that depends on j and k, and c{j,k} > 0 is a known parameter. The agent's objective is to maximize the cumulative reward while ensuring that the cumulative cost never exceeds the budget B.

The main body of the paper focuses on a special case where π is known and c{j,k} = 1 for all j and k. In this case, the paper presents an algorithm with a regret bound of O(log T) under a mild condition. The paper then extends this algorithm to the general case where π is unknown and c{j,k} can take arbitrary values.

A more general setting is considered in [16], but the algorithm proposed in that work has a regret bound of O(√T). Therefore, this paper may be the first to achieve a regret bound of O(log T), although this result relies on the satisfaction of a mild condition. The algorithm is based on an approximation algorithm for computing the optimal action sequence when all statistics are known. While the algorithm is simple, it is also very interesting.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. However, it would be even more significant if the O(log T) regret bound held without the need for the mild condition. Otherwise, proving an Ω(√T) lower bound for the boundary cases would be of great interest.

All the results appear to rely on Lemma 1, but I initially had concerns about its correctness. Specifically, I was unsure whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies equation (2) for all trials, and thus I did not understand why the average constraint is a relaxed version of the hard constraint. However, after reading the rebuttal, I am now convinced of the validity of Lemma 1.

Minor comments: Is it NP-hard to exactly compute the optimal action sequence? Can the results be generalized to the case where T is unknown? Can we assume u^1 > u^2 > ... > u^*_J without loss of generality, even for the unknown context distribution case? I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
