This paper studies contextual bandits with budget and time constraints, referred to as constrained contextual bandits. The authors propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm that achieves near-optimality and only requires the ordering of expected rewards. They then combine ALP with the upper-confidence-bound (UCB) method to develop a UCB-ALP algorithm for the general case where expected rewards are unknown. The UCB-ALP algorithm achieves logarithmic regret except for certain boundary cases.

The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the regret bounds for both ALP and UCB-ALP algorithms. The authors also discuss the generalization of their algorithms to systems with unknown context distribution and heterogeneous costs.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the problem and their proposed solutions. The analysis of the regret bounds is thorough and well-presented.

One potential drawback of the paper is that it assumes finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint. It would be interesting to see how the authors' approach can be extended to more general settings.

The paper is a significant contribution to the field of contextual bandits and has the potential to impact various applications such as clinical trials, online recommendation, and online hiring in crowdsourcing.
