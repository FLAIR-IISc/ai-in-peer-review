This paper studies contextual bandits evaluated against a strengthened baseline that can abstain on a budgeted fraction of rounds. The abstention policy is computed via an adaptive linear program that selectively “passes” on rounds likely to be suboptimal, subject to a budget constraint. The setup is interesting, and the LP-based perspective is novel and potentially useful for applications where deferral is permitted.

Main question (boundary cases): The paper treats boundary cases (e.g., ρ = q_j) separately. Why is this necessary? ρ = q_j does not appear intrinsically special, especially for j > 1. Please provide clear intuition and a concise, self-contained argument for the boundary handling. In particular:
- What failure in the main proof necessitates separate treatment? Is it the loss of strict inequalities required for concentration, identifiability, or sensitivity of the LP solution?
- Does ρ = q_j induce degeneracy in the LP (e.g., multiple optimal solutions or a change in the active constraint set) that invalidates a stability or Lipschitz argument used elsewhere?
- Are these boundary events measure-zero under your distributional assumptions (e.g., continuous context/reward noise), in which case they could be handled via a generic perturbation/tie-breaking argument? If so, why not unify the proof by adding an ε-perturbation (or randomized tie-breaking), analyzing the perturbed problem, and letting ε → 0?
- If the analysis distinguishes j = 1 from j > 1, what structurally changes across j? If the quantile/threshold index j only enumerates identical constraints, the asymmetry is surprising and should be explained or removed with a unified argument.

Strengths
- Clear, practically motivated baseline: Allowing an algorithm to forgo a budgeted fraction of rounds captures realistic deferral/abstention settings (e.g., triage, risk management).
- Novel use of adaptive LP: Framing the abstention decision as an LP that adapts to evolving estimates is appealing and enables crisp comparisons against a strengthened benchmark.
- Conceptual contribution: Evaluating regret against a baseline that can abstain is stricter than standard contextual bandits and aligns better with some application requirements.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Baseline definition and implementability: “Passing on a certain percent of suboptimal rounds” is ambiguous. Operationally, what does “suboptimal” mean at decision time? The LP cannot know ground-truth suboptimality; it must rely on estimates. Please:
  - Specify precisely what information the baseline has and when (estimated gaps, confidence intervals, prior knowledge).
  - Clarify whether the baseline is an oracle (uses true suboptimality) or a realizable/learned proxy. If oracle-like, justify this as a fair comparator and discuss how regret should be interpreted.
- Regret notion and trade-offs: Formalize regret with respect to the abstaining baseline and show how it reduces to standard regret when the budget is zero. Characterize how the regret bound scales with the abstention budget. Is there a point at which the baseline becomes too strong (e.g., trivial if the budget is large), and how do your guarantees reflect that?
- Near-boundary robustness: Even if exact boundaries are rare, behavior near ρ ≈ q_j can be numerically unstable. Do the algorithm and analysis yield continuity or Hölder-type stability of the value function and policies near the boundary? If not, what practical mitigations (smoothing, regularization, tie-breaking) are recommended?
- Computational aspects: Provide complexity details for the adaptive LP (variables/constraints as a function of actions, contexts, and time). How often is the LP solved, and is warm-starting or constraint screening used? Is the approach scalable to large context or action spaces?
- Empirical evaluation (if included): Please ensure comparisons against standard contextual bandit baselines without abstention and, where possible, constrained/knapsack-style baselines. Include sensitivity to the budget parameter, ablations for LP components (e.g., tie-breaking, regularization), and stress tests near boundary conditions.
- Assumptions: List all statistical assumptions explicitly (realizability, noise model, boundedness). If any are only needed for boundary handling, say so and consider whether they can be weakened with a unified argument.

Presentation and notation
- Define ρ and q_j at first use and maintain consistent indexing. If q_j are quantiles or thresholds derived from the LP, add a figure illustrating the feasible region and which constraints become active when ρ crosses q_j.
- Summarize the boundary-case argument in the main text with a high-level lemma and intuition, even if full details remain in the appendix.
- Provide pseudo-code or a clear procedural description of the adaptive LP step, including how estimates and confidence radii feed into the LP, and the policy derived from its solution.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses a timely and practically relevant variant of contextual bandits and proposes a distinctive LP-based framework to evaluate algorithms against a stronger, abstaining baseline. I find the setup and direction promising. The primary technical concern is the separate treatment of boundary cases, which currently feels ad hoc; a unified, perturbation-based proof or a crisp justification of why degeneracy forces case splits would significantly strengthen the work. Clarifying the exact capabilities of the baseline, tightening the regret definition, and discussing computational and near-boundary robustness would further improve the paper’s clarity and impact.

With these clarifications—especially a transparent, intuitive explanation for the boundary analysis—I would be inclined to view the contribution favorably.
