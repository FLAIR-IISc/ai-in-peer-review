This paper addresses the problem of contextual bandits under both a time horizon (T) and a budget constraint (B), a setting the authors term "constrained contextual bandits." The key challenge identified is that these dual constraints create complex temporal dependencies, making the standard exploration-exploitation tradeoff difficult and rendering the optimal policy, solvable in principle by dynamic programming, computationally intractable.

The authors' main contribution is a novel and computationally efficient approach that decouples decision-making from learning. To achieve this, they first analyze the simplified setting where problem statistics are known. They propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, which approximates the intractable oracle by relaxing the hard budget constraint and adaptively solving a linear program at each step based on the remaining budget-to-time ratio. This approach is shown to be near-optimal, achieving O(1) regret relative to a strong LP-based upper bound, except for specific "boundary cases."

Building on this insight, the paper then tackles the more general case of unknown expected rewards. The authors propose the UCB-ALP algorithm, which combines the Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) method for exploration with the ALP framework for decision-making. The central result is that UCB-ALP achieves O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases and O(sqrt(T)) regret in boundary cases. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to establish a logarithmic regret bound for this problem, a significant improvement over prior O(sqrt(T)) results for related problems like Resourceful Contextual Bandits. The paper further extends the analysis to handle unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a practical and important extension of the contextual bandit problem. The introduction of the ALP framework as a tractable approximation to the optimal policy is a novel and powerful idea. Achieving a logarithmic regret bound is a major theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous. The paper provides a clear path from the known-statistics case to the unknown-statistics case, building intuition along the way. The technique of analyzing the budget's evolution by mapping it to sampling without replacement and leveraging concentration inequalities is elegant.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and structured. The motivation for the problem is clear, and the progression of ideas from the oracle approximation to the final UCB-ALP algorithm is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Clarification:**
*   The primary point needing clarification is the special treatment of "boundary cases," where the average budget ratio `ρ` equals a cumulative context probability `q_j`. While the proof sketch suggests this is where the policy's threshold `j̃(bτ/τ)` becomes unstable, the intuition for why this leads to a fundamentally different regret order (O(sqrt(T)) instead of O(log T)) could be explained more clearly in the main text. Does `ρ = q_j` represent a "knife-edge" scenario where the optimal policy is highly sensitive to small fluctuations in the remaining budget, an effect that is smoothed out in non-boundary cases? A more detailed intuitive explanation would strengthen the paper's presentation.

In summary, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the literature on bandits with constraints. The proposed ALP framework and the resulting UCB-ALP algorithm provide both a novel theoretical approach and a computationally efficient solution that achieves state-of-the-art regret bounds.
