This paper considers a constrained contextual bandit problem with budget and time limitations, focusing on discrete contexts and actions. The central contribution lies in the computational efficiency of the proposed algorithm, achieved through a linear programming (LP) approximation, and the resulting logarithmic regret bound.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the technical details and understand the key ideas. The authors effectively situate their work within the existing literature on contextual bandits and resource allocation, and clearly articulate the challenges of incorporating budget constraints. The theoretical analysis appears sound and the proposed UCB-ALP algorithm is a reasonable approach to tackling this problem. The discussion of generalizations to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs is also valuable, although less detailed.

Strengths: The primary strength of this work is its development of a computationally efficient algorithm with provable logarithmic regret bounds for a challenging problem. The LP approximation technique is a clever way to address the complexity of the constrained optimization. The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation and clearly outlines the key assumptions and limitations. The discussion of both known and unknown system parameters adds to the practical relevance of the work.

Weaknesses: While the theoretical results are strong, the lack of empirical validation is a notable limitation. Including numerical experiments to demonstrate the performance of the UCB-ALP algorithm in practice would significantly strengthen the paper. The connection between the LP approximation and dynamic programming, while mentioned, could be elaborated further. Specifically, detailing why a direct DP approach is intractable beyond the computational complexity concerns would be helpful.  The rationale for combining UCB with ALP in Section 4.1 could be made more explicit – highlighting the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle and drawing parallels to algorithms like UCRL2 would be beneficial.  The paper does not extensively discuss how sensitive the performance is to the accuracy of the context distribution estimate when using the Empirical ALP approach (Section 5).

Related Work: The authors should be aware of the work in [http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.03374], which explores related ideas.

Arguments for acceptance:
*   Novel and relevant problem: Constrained contextual bandits are increasingly important in real-world applications.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees: The logarithmic regret bound is a significant result.
*   Computational efficiency: The LP approximation makes the algorithm practical.
*   Clear and well-written presentation: The paper is easy to understand and follow.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   Lack of empirical validation: The absence of experiments weakens the practical impact of the work.
*   Limited discussion of sensitivity analysis: The performance of the empirical approach with inaccurate context estimates is not thoroughly addressed.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors include numerical experiments and address the comments above in a revised version. The paper presents a significant improvement over existing work and is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in the field.
