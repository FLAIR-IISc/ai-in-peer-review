Post-rebuttal update:
- I agree the setting is not exactly identical to a budgeted bandit with action constraints, but it still seems very close: as the horizon grows, coupling across contexts effectively vanishes. Given that, I do not see why each occurrence of a context cannot be treated as an independent bandit instance.
- The paper would be much stronger with a clean, problem-dependent upper bound in terms of arm gaps (the current supplementary bound needs revision), and ideally matching lower bounds in both problem-dependent and problem-independent regimes.

Summary and assessment:
The paper studies contextual bandits with both a budget and a time constraint. The central result is that, with finitely many contexts J and actions K, and for sufficiently large T (relative to the instance), one can achieve logarithmic regret in T, which is new in this contextual setting. The analysis seems thorough and the writing is precise. However, the result is not very surprising: with finite J and K, the problem can be viewed as a time- and budget-constrained bandit with JK composite arms, under an action-availability constraint (only the K arms compatible with the current context are available at each round). For large T and a finite arm set, O(log T) regret matches known behavior in action-constrained bandits. Consequently, the extent of the conceptual novelty remains unclear.

Questions and comments:
- Theorem 2, case (2): the regret is stated as O(sqrt(T) + …). Shouldn’t the dependence be at least O(sqrt(KT) + …)? Please clarify how K enters the rate.
- Still in Theorem 2, the bounds are either O(KJ log T) or O(sqrt(T) + KJ log T), depending on the arm configuration. Standard problem-dependent bounds are typically refined via sums over inverse gaps (e.g., sum over suboptimal arms of 1/Δ). Can you obtain analogous gap-dependent expressions here? I understand the KJ factor may arise because the proof is driven by events ensuring correct UCB orderings rather than concentration on the gaps themselves, but is that approach optimal? Could a gap-based analysis reduce the KJ dependence?
- Lower bounds: it would be valuable to include both problem-independent and problem-dependent lower bounds. Trivial reductions recover classical bandit lower bounds when costs are zero and the context is fixed. Can you sharpen these to reflect context complexity and the budget constraint (e.g., via instance-dependent quantities that mix gaps with context frequencies or budget tightness)?
- Supplementary material: the current bound appears to require significant reworking for correctness and clarity. Providing a polished, gap-dependent upper bound would greatly improve the paper.

Overall evaluation:
The paper is well written and the results are technically sound, but I am not fully convinced about the novelty relative to budgeted/action-constrained bandits with finite arm sets. Clearer separation from that literature, along with refined gap-dependent upper bounds and meaningful lower bounds (both problem-dependent and -independent), would significantly strengthen the contribution. Pending these clarifications and improvements, I remain neutral.
