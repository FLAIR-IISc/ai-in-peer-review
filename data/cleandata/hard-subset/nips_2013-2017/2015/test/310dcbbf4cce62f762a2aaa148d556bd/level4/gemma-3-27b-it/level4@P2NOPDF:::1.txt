This paper addresses the problem of contextual bandits under budget and time constraints. The authors demonstrate that, when the number of contexts and actions is finite and the time horizon *T* is sufficiently large, a regret of O(log *T*) is achievable – a novel result within the contextual bandit framework. The analysis appears rigorous and the writing is clear.

However, the contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The setting, with a finite number of contexts *J* and actions *K*, effectively transforms the problem into a time and budget-constrained bandit problem with *JK* arms, subject to action constraints dictated by the current context. Given this reduction, the established O(log *T*) regret bound for finite-armed, constrained bandit problems seems a natural consequence for sufficiently large *T*. Therefore, the paper’s originality warrants further clarification from the authors.

Several specific points require attention. First, concerning Theorem 2, the reported regret of O(√*T* + …) in case 2) appears potentially suboptimal. A bound of O(√(*K*T) + …) would seem more appropriate. 

Second, Theorem 2 presents two distinct regret bounds – O(*KJ* log *T*) or O(√*T* + *KJ* log *T*) – depending on the problem instance. While understandable given the proof's reliance on UCB ordering events rather than direct concentration bounds on arm gaps, it would strengthen the paper to express the bounds more precisely using a sum of inverse arm gaps, as is common in problem-dependent contextual bandit analyses. Investigating whether such a refinement is possible would be valuable.

Finally, the inclusion of lower bounds, both problem-dependent and problem-independent, would significantly enhance the paper. While trivial lower bounds exist by considering the case of zero cost and a uniform context, exploring bounds that incorporate the complexity introduced by the context would be highly desirable.

Following the rebuttal, I acknowledge the authors’ explanation regarding the relationship to standard bandit problems; while not identical, the similarities are considerable. However, my concern persists that the core contribution is a somewhat direct application of existing results in constrained bandit problems to a contextual setting. A tighter upper bound, expressed in terms of arm gaps – ideally accompanied by matching lower bounds – would substantially strengthen the paper. As it stands, the supplementary material's current bound requires revision. 

Currently, I remain neutral in my assessment due to lingering questions regarding the novelty of the setting and the desire for more refined bounds.
