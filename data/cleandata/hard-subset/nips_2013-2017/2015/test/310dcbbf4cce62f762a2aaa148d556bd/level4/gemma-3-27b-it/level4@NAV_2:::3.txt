This paper considers a contextual bandit problem with budget constraint B and known time horizon T. For each trial, the agent observes a context j chosen according to a fixed probability distribution π over a context set {1,...,J} and takes an action k from {0} ∪ {1,...,K}, where 0 means "skip". If k = 0, then the agent gets no reward and incurs no cost. If k > 0, then the agent gets reward y and incurs cost c_{j,k}, where y is chosen according to a fixed probability distribution over [0,1] which depends on j and k, and c_{j,k} > 0 is a parameter known to the agent. The goal of the agent is to maximize the cumulative reward under the condition that the cumulative cost never exceeds the budget B. 

The main body of the paper is devoted to a special case where π is known and c_{j,k} = 1 for all j and k, and the paper gives an algorithm whose regret bound is O(log T) under a mild condition. The paper extends the algorithm to the general case where π is unknown and c_{j,k}'s are arbitrary. A more general setting is considered in [17], but it gives an algorithm with O(√T) regret. So the paper is potentially the first work with O(log T) regret, though this depends on satisfying the stated mild condition. The algorithm is based on an approximation algorithm for computing the optimal action sequence when all the statistics are known. The algorithm is simple but very interesting.

The paper is well motivated and very clearly written. It would be more impactful if O(log T) regret always held, or if a corresponding Ω(√T) lower bound were proven for the boundary cases. 

All results seem to be based on Lemma 1, and initially the correctness was not clear. The reasoning behind the average constraint being a relaxation of the hard constraint was initially unclear, specifically whether the optimal action sequence always satisfies (2) for all trials. However, the authors clarified this point and I am now convinced of Lemma 1. 

Minor comments: Is it NP-hard to exactly obtain the optimal action sequence? Can the results be generalized to the case where T is unknown? Can we assume u^*_1 > u^*_2 > ... > u^*_J without loss of generality even for the unknown context distribution case? 

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a potentially significant contribution by achieving logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, a problem where prior work (e.g., [17]) achieved sublinear but not logarithmic regret.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the problem, proposed algorithm, and theoretical results.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The core technical ideas – approximating the oracle with linear programming and combining this with UCB – appear sound, and the analysis demonstrates a good understanding of the interplay between exploration and exploitation under budget constraints.
*   **Significance:** The problem of constrained contextual bandits is highly relevant to many real-world applications, such as clinical trials and online advertising. An algorithm with logarithmic regret has the potential to be impactful in these domains.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Boundary Cases:** The logarithmic regret guarantee does not hold for all parameter settings, with boundary cases resulting in a √T regret. It would be stronger if this limitation was addressed with a lower bound proof, or an algorithm that avoids these cases.
*   **Assumptions:** The reliance on known context distributions in some parts of the analysis (and subsequent extension to the unknown case) might limit the practical applicability of the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem.
*   The proposed algorithm is novel and achieves a significantly improved regret bound compared to existing work.
*   The paper is technically sound and well-written.
*   The potential impact of the work is high.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The logarithmic regret guarantee is not universal and the √T regret in boundary cases is a limitation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance to the conference.
