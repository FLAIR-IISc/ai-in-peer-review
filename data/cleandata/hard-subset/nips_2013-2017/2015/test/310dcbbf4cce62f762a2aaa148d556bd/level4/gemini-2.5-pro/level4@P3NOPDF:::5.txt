This paper addresses the problem of budget-constrained contextual bandits in a setting with discrete contexts and actions. The primary contribution is a computationally efficient algorithm, achieved through an approximate linear programming (LP) approach, that attains a near-optimal regret bound of O(log T). The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear problem formulation and a logical flow that makes the technical contributions easy to follow.

**Strengths:**

The work represents a significant and interesting improvement over existing methods for budget-constrained bandits. By providing an algorithm that is not only computationally efficient but also achieves a strong theoretical regret bound, the authors make a valuable contribution to the field. The clarity of the presentation is a major strength, making the core ideas highly accessible.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, a few minor revisions could further enhance its quality and impact.

1.  **Clarification of UCB-ALP Rationale (Section 4.1):** The exposition in Section 4.1 regarding the UCB-ALP algorithm could be strengthened. Specifically, the rationale for combining the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) principle with the Approximate Linear Program (ALP) could be made more explicit. It appears the algorithm is designed around the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" (OFU) principle, where the UCB estimates are used to construct an optimistic model that is then solved. Clarifying this connection would be beneficial. Furthermore, it might be valuable to discuss the relationship between this policy update step and similar approaches in the literature, such as the value iteration or policy iteration steps in algorithms like UCRL2. This would help situate the work more broadly within the reinforcement learning literature.

2.  **Extension to Parametric Models (Future Work):** A natural and impactful extension for future work would be to consider parametric models for the reward function. The current tabular setting can be limiting when the number of contexts is large or continuous. Incorporating function approximation (e.g., linear reward models) would significantly broaden the applicability of the proposed framework, and it would be useful to acknowledge this as a direction for future research.

3.  **Numerical Experiments:** The paper's contributions are primarily theoretical. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the inclusion of numerical experiments would substantially enhance the paper's impact. Empirical validation of the algorithm's performance, perhaps comparing it against relevant baselines, would provide valuable insights into its practical behavior and complement the theoretical analysis. If space is a concern, these experiments could be included in an appendix or an extended version of the paper.

4.  **Additional Relevant Work:** The authors may find the following related work relevant to their discussion: http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.03374.

**Recommendation:**

This paper merits acceptance. It presents a well-motivated and technically sound contribution by providing an efficient algorithm for constrained contextual bandits with optimal regret in most cases. The work is a clear advancement over the state of the art, and the suggestions above are intended to further strengthen an already excellent paper.
