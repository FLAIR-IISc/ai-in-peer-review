Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies contextual bandits with both a time horizon T and a hard budget B, where each action under a context incurs a known positive cost and yields a stochastic reward in [0,1]. The goal is to maximize cumulative reward subject to the budget constraint. The work focuses first on unit-cost systems with a known context distribution and then extends to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.

The key contribution is an adaptive linear program (ALP) that approximates the oracle (optimal policy with known statistics) by replacing the hard constraint with an average budget constraint and adapting it over time using the remaining budget-to-time ratio. In unit-cost systems with known context distribution and known expected rewards, ALP achieves O(1) regret (relative to the oracle) except on “boundary” parameter values, where it achieves O(√T). Building on this, the authors propose UCB-ALP, which combines ALP with UCB estimates of expected rewards and attains O(JK log T) regret in non-boundary cases, and O(√T + JK log T) in boundary cases. For unknown context distributions, they propose empirical ALP (EALP) and show (via a truncated update scheme and concentration arguments) analogous performance. They also treat heterogeneous costs by generalizing the LP and propose an ε-First ALP variant for learning in that setting.

This work complements and advances the literature on budgeted or resource-constrained bandits. Compared to Bandits with Knapsacks and related works that achieve O(√T) regret in non-contextual or static-context settings, this paper achieves logarithmic regret for discrete, stochastic contextual arrivals under a single budget constraint. It also relates to Resourceful Contextual Bandits (Mixture Elimination), which attains O(√T) regret relative to a finite policy class with higher computational cost; here the benchmark is the true oracle and the algorithms are computationally light. The analysis also clarifies connections to UCRL-style RL approaches in Markovian settings and to concave-reward/convex-constraint frameworks, which typically yield O(√T) regret under dynamic contexts.

Quality:
The technical development is sound and carefully argued. The LP upper bound (Lemma 1) is the backbone of the regret analysis; I was initially unsure whether the average constraint truly relaxes the hard budget, but the rebuttal clarified this and I am convinced. I recommend adding a brief, intuitive justification in the main text explaining why the static LP’s value upper-bounds the oracle’s expected reward. The analysis of ALP via a “sampling without replacement” view of the remaining budget, yielding hypergeometric concentration, is neat and effective. The UCB-ALP decomposition into action-ranking and context-ranking components is clear, and the resulting bounds are plausible and instance-dependent. The boundary-case behavior is explicitly characterized.

Clarity:
The paper is generally very well written and organized. Notation is heavy but consistent. Two suggestions for clarity:
- Provide a short, self-contained explanation (in the main paper) of why the LP relaxation gives an upper bound and why adapting the average budget bτ/τ leads to tight performance except at boundaries.
- Include a small running example (e.g., J=3) to illustrate the threshold structure j̃(ρ), how ALP acts, and why boundary cases cause O(√T) fluctuations.

Originality:
The use of an adaptive LP relaxation to approximate the oracle under a hard budget, coupled with a regret analysis that isolates the cost of learning the ordering, is novel for constrained contextual bandits. Achieving O(log T) regret (outside measure-zero boundary cases) appears to be the first such result in this setting with stochastic context arrivals.

Significance:
The results are important for practical, resource-constrained online decision-making (clinical trials, recommendations, crowdsourcing), where simple and efficient algorithms are needed. The approach is computationally light (threshold-based decisions) and should be applicable beyond the specific model analyzed. The paper will likely stimulate further work on sharper bounds for boundary cases and more general constraints.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear conceptual framework: adaptive LP as a near-oracle decision rule.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: O(1) for known statistics, O(log T) with learning, with careful treatment of boundary cases.
- Computational efficiency and practical implementability.
- Meaningful extensions to unknown context distributions and heterogeneous costs.
- Good positioning relative to Bandits with Knapsacks and RCB; advances the state of the art.

Cons / questions:
- Boundary cases remain at O(√T); it would be valuable to discuss whether this is intrinsic. Is there a lower bound showing Ω(√T) regret near boundaries?
- The analysis for unknown context distributions relies on truncating the empirical updates. Can the authors provide guidance or empirical evidence on the non-truncated EALP/UCB-EALP performance and steps toward a full analysis?
- Empirical evaluation is only briefly mentioned; more experiments exploring sensitivity to ρ near boundaries, mis-specified π, and heterogeneous costs would strengthen the paper.

Specific comments and suggestions:
- Complexity of the oracle: Is computing the exact optimal policy NP-hard even in unit-cost settings with finite contexts? A formal hardness statement or citation would contextualize the need for ALP.
- Unknown horizon: Can the approach be made anytime? A doubling trick in T or using b/(t_remaining) with an adaptive estimate of remaining time could be discussed.
- Assumptions on ordering: The paper often assumes strict ordering u∗1 > … > u∗J (and unique best action per context) to simplify exposition. Please clarify how ties are handled. For the unknown-context-distribution case, can the same “without loss of generality via tie-breaking” argument be used, and what is the impact on constants?
- Practical tuning: Comment on the choice of the UCB exploration coefficient (the paper uses a smaller constant); a brief note on its effect on regret constants and empirical behavior would help practitioners.
- Upper-bound benchmark: Since Û(T,B) = T v(ρ) is central, including a short proof sketch of Lemma 1 in the main text (or an intuitive coupling argument) would improve readability.

Overall recommendation:
I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper is well motivated, technically solid, original, and significant. It provides the first logarithmic-regret algorithms for constrained contextual bandits under natural assumptions, with clear analysis and practical algorithms. The authors’ rebuttal satisfactorily addressed my concern about the LP upper bound; incorporating that explanation will further strengthen the paper.
