

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, a problem where agents must maximize rewards under both time and budget constraints, with the budget constraint significantly complicating the exploration-exploitation tradeoff. The authors focus on a simplified model with finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, and a single budget constraint, which is justified in applications like clinical trials and wireless networks. They propose an Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm for the case with known statistics, which achieves near-optimal performance (O(1) regret) except for certain boundary cases, and combine it with UCB to create the UCB-ALP algorithm for unknown statistics, achieving O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases.

The paper makes a significant contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret in constrained contextual bandits, which is the first such result to the best of their knowledge. This extends prior work on resourceful contextual bandits (Badanidiyuru et al., COLT 2013) that achieved O(√T) regret with a different regret benchmark and high computational complexity. While Agrawal et al. (2015) have studied contextual bandits with concave rewards and convex constraints, their approach assumes static contexts and uses a fixed budget constraint, resulting in O(√T) regret in our setting. The authors' key insight is that ALP's requirement for only reward orderings rather than exact values enables combining it with UCB for efficient exploration.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical exposition is precise, with each technical contribution building logically on the previous one. The authors provide detailed proofs for their main theorems and carefully explain the intuition behind their approach. The writing style is professional and accessible to a broad audience in machine learning, making the paper particularly valuable for practitioners.

Regarding quality, the technical contributions are sound and well-supported. The regret analysis is rigorous, with careful consideration of boundary cases and multiple interacting factors such as action/context ranking errors and budget fluctuations. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison to related work and demonstrate how their approach differs from existing methods. The paper also properly addresses the limitations of their simplified model and clearly states the regime where their results apply (B and T growing proportionally).

The originality of the work is strong. While the idea of approximating dynamic programming with linear programming has been studied (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., 2013), the authors' specific design and analysis of ALP for constrained contextual bandits is novel. Their approach to combining this approximation with UCB to handle unknown statistics is particularly innovative, and their regret bounds represent a significant improvement over prior work.

The significance of the results is substantial. Constrained contextual bandits arise in many important applications, including clinical trials and crowdsourcing, where budget limitations are crucial. The authors' algorithm achieves logarithmic regret in most cases, which is optimal, and is computationally efficient compared to previous approaches. Their work provides a clear path toward addressing the open problem of general constrained contextual bandits, as they note in their conclusion.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed. The authors could strengthen their discussion of the relationship between their UCB-ALP algorithm and the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle. While they mention this principle, they do not explicitly connect it to their algorithm design. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion of how their approach might extend to cases where the context distribution is unknown, as this is a natural extension of their current work (though they do touch on this in Section 5).

The draft review mentions a reference to Agrawal et al. (2015) - "Contextual bandits with global constraints and objective" (arXiv:1506.03374) - which is highly relevant. This paper studies contextual bandits with budget constraints but assumes static contexts and achieves O(√T) regret, making the current paper's results for dynamic contexts a clear advancement.

The paper merits acceptance for the following reasons: it provides a computationally efficient algorithm for constrained contextual bandits that achieves optimal regret in most cases, it makes a significant theoretical contribution by showing how to achieve logarithmic regret where previous work could only achieve sublinear regret, and it is exceptionally well-written with a clear exposition of both the intuition and technical details. The work is a meaningful advancement over previous approaches to constrained contextual bandits, and the insights provided could guide future research on more general settings. The authors have also appropriately acknowledged related work and clearly articulated how their contribution differs from it.

The only suggestion for future work is to consider the case of continuous contexts (where a parametric model might be useful) or to include numerical experiments to demonstrate the algorithm's performance in practice, though the theoretical contribution is already strong enough for acceptance. The paper's results are sufficiently general for NIPS, as they address a fundamental problem in online learning with important applications.
