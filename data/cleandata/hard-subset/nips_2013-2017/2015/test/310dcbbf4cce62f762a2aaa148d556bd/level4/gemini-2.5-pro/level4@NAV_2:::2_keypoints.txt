- Addresses contextual bandits with time and budget constraints under a simplified model of finite discrete contexts, fixed action costs, and a single budget.  
- Identifies complexity from temporal dependencies that make the optimal dynamic program intractable.  
- Main contribution: Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP), an LP-relaxation oracle approximation that adaptively updates the budget-to-time ratio.  
- ALP achieves near-optimal performance: O(1) regret in most cases and O(√T) regret in boundary cases.  
- Extends to unknown rewards via UCB-ALP, combining ALP with UCB learning to obtain O(log T) regret in non-boundary cases.  
- Claims significant improvement over prior O(√T) results (e.g., Resourceful Contextual Bandits).  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous, with convincing concentration arguments using sampling-without-replacement mapping.  
- Paper is very well-written and clearly organized, easing comprehension of core ideas and progression.  
- Major weakness: no empirical validation or simulations to assess practical performance and finite-T behavior.  
- Analysis relies on simplifying assumptions (discrete contexts, known context distribution in main results, fixed costs), with limited development of extensions in the main text.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical advance (first O(log T) result), elegant ALP framework, and strong presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: entirely theoretical with no experiments and analysis restricted to a simplified model.
