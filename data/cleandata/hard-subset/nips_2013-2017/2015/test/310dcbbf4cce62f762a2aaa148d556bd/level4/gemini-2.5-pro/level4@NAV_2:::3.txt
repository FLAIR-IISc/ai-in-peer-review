This paper addresses the contextual bandit problem under both a time horizon T and a budget B. In this setting, an agent sequentially observes a context, chooses an action (or chooses to skip), and receives a reward while also incurring a cost. The process terminates when either the time horizon is reached or the budget is depleted. The paper's primary contribution is an algorithm that achieves logarithmic regret, a significant improvement over prior work.

The work is well-situated within the existing literature on bandits with budget constraints. While more general settings, such as in the Resourceful Contextual Bandits (RCB) framework [17], have been studied, the algorithms proposed typically achieve O(sqrt(T)) regret. This paper distinguishes itself by demonstrating that O(log T) regret is attainable, albeit under a more structured model with a finite number of contexts and actions. The core of the paper focuses on the unit-cost case with a known context distribution, later extending the ideas to handle unknown distributions and heterogeneous costs.

The main strength of this work lies in its novel and elegant approach to a challenging problem. The key technical contribution is the introduction of the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) method to approximate the oracle's policy. This is an insightful way to handle the complex coupling between decisions over time introduced by the budget constraint, sidestepping the intractability of a full dynamic programming solution. This ALP approximation is not only shown to be near-optimal (achieving O(1) regret in non-boundary cases) when statistics are known, but it also possesses a crucial property: it relies only on the ordering of expected rewards. This feature enables its effective combination with an Upper-Confidence-Bound (UCB) approach, leading to the proposed UCB-ALP algorithm for the case where rewards are unknown. The resulting algorithm is simple, computationally efficient, and its analysis is rigorous. The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured, building intuition by first analyzing the simpler known-statistics case before tackling the full problem.

Despite the significant contributions, there are some limitations. The primary one is that the O(log T) regret bound only applies to "non-boundary" cases, where the budget-to-time ratio does not coincide with specific cumulative probabilities of the contexts. In the "boundary" cases, the regret degrades to O(sqrt(T)). While the authors acknowledge this, it tempers the main claim of achieving logarithmic regret. It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether these boundary cases are artifacts of the analysis or fundamental to the problem, and whether an O(sqrt(T)) lower bound could be established for these specific instances.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant advance in the study of constrained contextual bandits. The proposed ALP framework is a compelling idea that could inspire further research.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   It is one of the first works to achieve O(log T) regret for a constrained contextual bandit problem, which is a substantial theoretical improvement over the existing O(sqrt(T)) bounds.
*   The core idea of using an Adaptive Linear Program (ALP) to approximate the oracle is novel, insightful, and provides a computationally efficient way to handle the budget constraint.
*   The paper is very clearly written, well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis appears sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The main result of O(log T) regret is not universal and breaks down to O(sqrt(T)) in "boundary cases," which slightly limits the impact of the contribution.

**Questions and suggestions for the authors:**
1.  The paper motivates the ALP approximation by stating that the exact oracle solution is computationally intractable. Could you comment on the precise computational complexity of the oracle problem (e.g., is it NP-hard)?
2.  The analysis assumes a known time horizon T, which is used in the UCB bonus terms. Could you discuss the potential challenges and modifications required to extend the UCB-ALP approach to settings with an unknown or infinite horizon?
3.  For simplicity, the paper assumes a sorted order of optimal rewards (u*1 > ... > u*J) in the known-statistics setting. For the extension to an unknown context distribution (Section 5), is this simplifying assumption still made, or is the ordering of contexts learned simultaneously with their probabilities and rewards? A clarification would be helpful.
