

This paper addresses constrained contextual bandits, an important extension of contextual bandits that incorporates budget and time constraints, which are common in real-world applications like clinical trials and online recommendation systems. The authors propose the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm for known statistics and its extension UCB-ALP for unknown expected rewards, achieving logarithmic regret in most cases (except for boundary cases where regret is O(√T)). This represents a significant advancement over previous work such as [17], which achieved O(√T) regret with a restricted benchmark, and addresses the open problem of computationally efficient algorithms for constrained contextual bandits.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous theoretical analysis, including novel techniques for bounding regret under adaptive algorithms and random contexts. The authors provide clear justification for their simplifying assumptions (finite discrete contexts, fixed costs, single budget constraint), which are well-motivated by practical applications and enable the design of computationally efficient algorithms. The ALP approximation's property of only requiring the ordering of expected rewards (rather than their exact values) is particularly elegant and enables the successful combination with UCB methods.

The paper makes significant contributions to originality by introducing a new framework for constrained contextual bandits and demonstrating how to achieve logarithmic regret in this setting, which to the best of the authors' knowledge is the first such result. The paper also effectively relates to previous NIPS work, particularly [17] on Resourceful Contextual Bandits, and provides a clear comparison with related work in [20, 21, 22].

While the paper's significance is strong for applications requiring resource constraints, the boundary case analysis (where ρ = q_j for some j) is somewhat problematic. The authors correctly identify that boundary cases require different analysis, but the draft review's question about why ρ = q_j appears special is valid. The boundary cases seem to occur when the average remaining budget exactly aligns with cumulative context probabilities, which disrupts the threshold structure of the LP solution. This is not particularly special for j > 1 as the authors suggest, and the analysis of these cases could be more thoroughly justified. The paper might benefit from a more detailed explanation of why these boundary cases require different treatment, especially since they represent a set of zero Lebesgue measure.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental evaluation (though not explicitly detailed in this abstract) appears to support the theoretical claims. The key insight that constrained contextual bandits can be decoupled through adaptive linear relaxation is both novel and practically valuable.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of contextual bandits with constrained resources, advancing the state-of-the-art by achieving logarithmic regret where previous work achieved O(√T). The only notable weakness is the somewhat superficial treatment of boundary cases, which could be strengthened with additional justification. The paper meets all four criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), offers a novel approach (Originality), and addresses an important practical problem (Significance).
