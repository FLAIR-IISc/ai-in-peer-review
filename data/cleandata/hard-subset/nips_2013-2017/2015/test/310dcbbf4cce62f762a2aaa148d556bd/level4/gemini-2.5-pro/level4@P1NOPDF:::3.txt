This paper addresses a contextual bandit problem with a known time horizon T and a fixed budget B. At each time step, the agent observes a context j, drawn from a distribution π over a set of J contexts. The agent then selects an action k from a set of K actions, plus a "skip" action (k=0). Selecting the skip action yields zero reward and incurs zero cost. Choosing an action k > 0 results in a stochastic reward drawn from a distribution specific to the context-action pair (j,k) and a deterministic, known cost c_{j,k} > 0. The agent's objective is to maximize the cumulative reward, subject to the hard constraint that the cumulative cost never exceeds the budget B.

The paper first focuses on a simplified setting with a known context distribution (π) and unit costs (c_{j,k} = 1). For this case, the authors propose an algorithm that achieves an O(log T) regret bound, subject to a mild technical condition. This algorithm is then extended to the more general case with an unknown context distribution and arbitrary known costs. The core of the proposed method is an elegant and simple approximation algorithm for the optimal offline policy where all problem parameters are known.

The contribution is significant. While a more general setting was considered in [16], the algorithm presented there only achieved O(√T) regret. To my knowledge, this work is the first to achieve logarithmic regret for this problem, making it a substantial improvement. The proposed algorithm is based on an interesting and surprisingly simple approximation for the optimal policy in the offline setting.

The paper is well-motivated and very clearly written. The significance of the work would be further strengthened if the O(log T) regret could be shown to hold universally, or alternatively, if an Ω(√T) lower bound were established for the cases not covered by their condition.

A central part of my initial review concerned the correctness of Lemma 1. I was initially unconvinced that the optimal action sequence would always satisfy the property in equation (2), and I did not see how the average cost constraint served as a valid relaxation of the hard, path-dependent budget constraint. The author rebuttal has fully clarified this point and I am now convinced of the lemma's correctness. Thank you for the explanation.

Minor Comments:
*   Is computing the optimal offline policy NP-hard? A brief discussion of the computational complexity of the oracle problem would be helpful for context.
*   Could the results be extended to the case of an unknown time horizon T?
*   Regarding the assumption u¹ > u² > ... > u*_J: is it possible to make this assumption without loss of generality when the context distribution π is unknown? It seems this ordering would depend on the unknown π.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel algorithm with a significantly improved regret bound for an important problem. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.
