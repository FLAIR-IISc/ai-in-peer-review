This paper addresses the problem of constrained contextual bandits with both budget and time limitations, focusing on computational efficiency and achieving logarithmic regret. The setting considers discrete contexts and actions, and the core contribution lies in utilizing linear programming (LP) to approximate the oracle policy, leading to an algorithm with provable performance guarantees.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the problem setup, proposed methodology, and theoretical analysis. The authors effectively motivate the problem, connecting it to real-world applications like crowdsourcing and clinical trials. They clearly articulate the challenges introduced by the constraints and how their approach tackles them. The step-by-step development of the Adaptive Linear Programming (ALP) algorithm, culminating in the UCB-ALP algorithm for unknown rewards, is logical and well-explained. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the regret bounds are clearly stated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is remarkably well-written and easy to understand, even for those not intimately familiar with the constrained bandit literature.
*   **Novelty:** Achieving logarithmic regret in the constrained contextual bandit setting is a significant contribution, particularly with the focus on computational efficiency. This work appears to be the first to demonstrate this result.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The use of LP relaxation and the resulting algorithms are designed to be computationally tractable, a crucial aspect for practical application.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The theoretical analysis appears sound, providing strong guarantees on the performance of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Well-Motivated Problem:** The authors effectively demonstrate the relevance of constrained contextual bandits to real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Simplified Setting:** The assumption of discrete contexts and actions, while simplifying the analysis, limits the immediate applicability of the results to more complex, continuous-state problems.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The absence of numerical experiments is a drawback. While the theoretical analysis is strong, empirical validation would bolster the claims and provide insights into practical performance.
*   **Connection to UCRL2:** The rationale behind combining UCB with ALP (optimism in the face of uncertainty) could be more explicitly linked to existing algorithms like UCRL2, potentially providing additional context and clarity.

**Related Work:**

The authors appropriately cite relevant work in contextual bandits, multi-armed bandits, and constrained optimization. However, they should be aware of the work by Agrawal et al. (2015) [http://arxiv.org/abs/1506.03374], which explores related concepts of bandits with concave rewards and convex constraints.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound solution to a challenging problem in reinforcement learning.
*   The focus on computational efficiency is particularly valuable, making the proposed algorithms potentially applicable in practice.
*   The clear and well-organized presentation makes the paper accessible to a broad audience.
*   The work represents a significant improvement over existing approaches for constrained contextual bandits.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The simplified setting may limit the immediate impact of the results.
*   The lack of empirical validation weakens the evidence supporting the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of contextual bandits. The authors have successfully developed computationally efficient algorithms with provable performance guarantees for a challenging problem. While the simplified setting and lack of empirical evaluation are minor drawbacks, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** of this paper to NIPS 2015.

**Suggestions for Future Work:**

*   Explore extensions of the proposed algorithms to handle continuous contexts and actions.
*   Investigate the use of parametric models for the reward function, particularly in high-dimensional context spaces.
*   Conduct extensive numerical experiments to validate the theoretical results and assess the practical performance of the algorithms.
*   Further explore the connections between UCB-ALP and existing algorithms like UCRL2.
