The authors consider the problem of a budgeted and time-constrained contextual bandit. The main finding of their paper is that if the number of context and actions is finite, and if the time constraint T is sufficiently large (with respect to the problem at hand), then they prove that a regret of order log(T) is achievable, which is a new result for contextual bandits.

The results seem thorough, and the writing is precise. The results are interesting but not very surprising. Indeed, since the number of contexts J and arms K are finite, this bandit problem can be seen as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms, which is also action-constrained since at each time, not all JK arms are accessible (only the K arms corresponding to the context). Since the number of arms is finite, for T large enough, the regret is log(T) as in action-constrained bandit problems. So I am not sure how innovative this paper is - maybe the authors can clarify this in their rebuttal.

I have a few questions regarding this paper: 

1. In Theorem 2, the regret in case 2) is O(√T + ...). But is it really O(√T + ...)? Shouldn't it be O(√(KT) + ...) at least? 

2. Still in Theorem 2, the regret is either O(KJ log(T)) or O(√T + KJ log(T)) depending on the configuration of the arms. In general, problem-dependent bounds are expressed in a more refined way with the sum of inverse of arm gaps. Isn't it possible to do something similar here? I understand that you have KJ instead of a certain sum of gaps because your proof is based on events characterizing the correct orders of the UCB, and not concentration bounds on their gaps. But is it an optimal idea?

3. It would be interesting to have lower bounds for this problem, ideally problem-dependent and problem-independent. Trivial lower bounds are the ones of classical bandit which correspond to cost equals 0 and context which is always the same. Can it be refined to take into account the complexity of the context?

I am still not convinced by the originality of this setting - and therefore remain neutral about this paper.

Strengths:
- The paper presents a new result for contextual bandits, achieving a regret of order log(T) under certain conditions.
- The results seem thorough, and the writing is precise.

Weaknesses:
- The paper's originality is questionable, as the problem can be seen as a time-constrained and budgeted bandit problem with JK arms.
- The regret bounds in Theorem 2 could be refined to take into account the arm gaps.
- Lower bounds for the problem are not provided.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the originality of the paper in the rebuttal.
- Consider refining the regret bounds in Theorem 2 to take into account the arm gaps.
- Provide lower bounds for the problem, ideally problem-dependent and problem-independent.
