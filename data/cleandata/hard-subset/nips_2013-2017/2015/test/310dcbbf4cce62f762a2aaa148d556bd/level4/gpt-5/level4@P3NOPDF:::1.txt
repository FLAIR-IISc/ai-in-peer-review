Review after rebuttal:
I appreciate the authors’ clarifications that their setting is not identical to standard budgeted bandits with action constraints. However, I remain unconvinced that the difference is substantive in the large-horizon regime: when T is sufficiently large, the coupling induced by the context process appears to vanish, and it is not clear why each context occurrence cannot be treated as a separate bandit instance (up to the shared budget). A precise explanation—or a counterexample—showing when and why this reduction fails would help.

Summary:
The paper studies contextual bandits with both a budget constraint and a time horizon. With a finite number of contexts J and actions K, the main result is that, for sufficiently large T (relative to problem parameters), one can achieve O(log T) regret. This is presented as a new result for contextual bandits. The paper is clearly written and the results are thorough.

Novelty and significance:
While the results are technically solid, their conceptual novelty is not entirely clear. With finite J and K, one can view the problem as a time- and budget-constrained bandit with JK “composite” arms, where only the K arms corresponding to the realized context are available at each round. In classical budgeted/action-constrained bandits with finitely many arms, O(log T) regret is achievable for large enough T. Thus the core guarantee here feels unsurprising. The paper would benefit from a sharper delineation of what is genuinely new: for instance, where the contextual structure fundamentally changes the attainable regret, the algorithms needed, or the lower bounds, relative to a JK-arm reduction.

Technical questions and requests:
- Theorem 2 (case 2): The stated regret is O(sqrt(T) + …). Should this not be O(sqrt(KT) + …), at least up to constants, given K arms per context? Please clarify the dependence on K and J throughout.
- Problem-dependent bounds: The current guarantees are either O(KJ log T) or O(sqrt(T) + KJ log T), depending on arm configurations. In classical bandits, refined problem-dependent bounds scale with sums over inverse gaps (e.g., Σ_a Δ_a^{-1} log T). Is it possible to obtain analogous gap-dependent bounds here (e.g., per-context or per “composite arm” sums over gaps), rather than a coarse KJ factor? I understand your proof monitors events about the order of UCBs rather than directly exploiting gap-dependent concentration, but that choice seems to leave statistical efficiency on the table. A “clean” gap-dependent upper bound would substantially strengthen the paper.
- Supplementary material: The gap-dependent bound there needs reworking for correctness and clarity. Please provide a precise statement with explicit constants, dependence on J, K, budget, and all relevant gaps.
- Lower bounds: The paper would be much stronger with lower bounds, ideally both problem-independent (e.g., Ω(√T) where appropriate) and problem-dependent (e.g., matching Σ Δ^{-1} log T terms). Trivial reductions from classical bandits apply when costs are zero and the context is constant; can you refine these to capture the complexity introduced by multiple contexts and the shared budget? For example, can you show regimes where the budget induces coupling that precludes a naïve JK reduction and leads to strictly larger minimax or gap-dependent lower bounds?
- Assumption clarity: Please make explicit the conditions under which O(log T) is attainable, including how “sufficiently large T” depends on J, K, the budget, the context process, and the arm gaps. If the result hinges on known context frequencies or independence assumptions, state them prominently.

Overall assessment:
- Strengths: Clear writing; thorough analysis; a practically relevant setting (budgets plus time with context); and favorable O(log T) rates under finite J, K.
- Weaknesses: The novelty relative to a JK-arm, action-constrained, budgeted reduction is not convincingly articulated; the problem-dependent analysis is coarse; the supplementary gap-dependent bound needs revision; and the absence of lower bounds leaves open whether the rates are tight.

Recommendation:
I remain neutral. If the authors can:
1) provide a corrected and refined gap-dependent upper bound (with explicit dependence on gaps rather than a blanket KJ factor),
2) add lower bounds (problem-independent and problem-dependent) that illuminate when contextual coupling fundamentally matters, and
3) clearly delineate the regimes where the contextual structure prevents a straightforward JK reduction (ideally with a formal separation or counterexample),
then the contribution would be significantly strengthened and its novelty made clear.
