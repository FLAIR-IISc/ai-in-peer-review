- Studies MLE in exponential families constrained to convex fast-mixing parameter sets Θ instead of low-treewidth graphs.  
- Assumes an MCMC kernel mixes geometrically uniformly over Θ: ∥M^v_θ q − p_θ∥TV ≤ C α^v.  
- Analyzes projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, giving high-probability guarantees to approximate the MLE restricted to Θ.  
- Treats two regimes: convex likelihood (guarantees in objective value) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) likelihood (guarantees in parameter distance).  
- Derives a Lipschitz constant for the gradient and decomposes MCMC gradient error into bias (mixing) and sampling noise.  
- Provides explicit schedules for iterations K, per-iteration samples M, and chain length v, with companion lower bounds.  
- Proves FPRAS sampling complexity: O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)) for objective accuracy (convex) and O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) for parameter accuracy (strongly convex).  
- Lower bounds show near-optimality of the schedules up to constants and log factors.  
- Framework broadly applies to exponential families and instantiates Θ via known fast-mixing criteria (e.g., Gibbs bounds for Ising/Potts, spectral/induced norm constraints).  
- Includes a small Ising example illustrating the schedules and behavior.  
- Contrasts with treewidth-based tractability and offers an alternative fast-mixing perspective.  
- Fills a gap in prior MCMC-MLE/stochastic approximation work by providing finite-sample nonasymptotic guarantees under biased gradients.  
- Clarifies conditions under which CD-like and persistent-sampling heuristics can approximate MLE with sufficient burn-in and projection.  
- Builds on prior projection-to-mixing-set work but preserves the original stationary distribution (unlike restart-based methods).  
- Strength: clear, principled analysis of biased stochastic gradients with high-probability convergence.  
- Strength: general treatment for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not tied to a single graph class.  
- Strength: interpretable explicit schedules and credible Θ constructions tied to known mixing criteria.  
- Weakness: requires knowledge of global mixing constants (C, α) and an efficient projection oracle, which may not exist beyond pairwise binary MRFs.  
- Weakness: mixing-constant bounds are conservative, potentially implying impractically large v and M; empirical validation is limited.  
- Weakness: bounded sufficient-statistics assumption excludes many continuous exponential families and unbounded-energy models.  
- Weakness: analysis assumes fresh independent samples each iteration and does not cover persistent-chain practice.  
- Weakness: algorithmic guidance on step-size, projection for non-Ising models, and estimating/adapting C and α is limited.  
- Theoretical development appears careful and correct, using TV-based Lipschitz bounds, error decomposition, concentration inequalities, and inexact-gradient results.  
- Presentation is generally clear with explicit assumptions, but notation and Algorithm 1/projection descriptions could be tightened and expanded for non-Ising cases.  
- Originality: novel FPRAS for MLE under fast-mixing constraints with nonasymptotic biased-gradient analysis.  
- Significance: provides a rigorous foundation for MCMC-based learning under fast-mixing constraints; practical impact depends on constructing Θ with usable constants.  
- Suggestions: give practical guidance for estimating C, α and adapting v, add empirical results beyond toy Ising (e.g., spectral-norm projection MRF), discuss bounded-statistics scope, approximate projections, and step-size selection.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid, novel theory with clear proofs, near-optimal complexity, and broad conceptual relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy reliance on global mixing bounds/projection oracles, conservative constants and limited experiments, and omission of persistent-chain analyses.  
- Overall: meaningful theoretical advance that would benefit from additional experiments and practical guidance on constructing Θ and choosing constants.
