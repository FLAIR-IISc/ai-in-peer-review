This paper addresses the problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in undirected graphical models and, more generally, in exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics. The core challenge is the intractability of computing the gradient of the log-likelihood, which requires calculating expectations over the model's distribution. This is often infeasible for models with high treewidth.

The authors propose constraining the model parameters `θ` to a "fast-mixing" set `Θ`, defined as a set where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to converge to the stationary distribution at a geometric rate. This provides a notion of tractability that is fundamentally different from the common approach of restricting the graph structure (e.g., to trees). The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the widely used practical strategy of performing projected gradient descent where the gradient is approximated using MCMC samples. The paper proves that this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood estimate within the constrained set `Θ`. Separate analyses and convergence guarantees are provided for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, bounding the total computational effort required to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its significant theoretical contribution. While using MCMC to approximate gradients for MLE in graphical models is a common heuristic (e.g., in training Restricted Boltzmann Machines), it has largely lacked formal guarantees. The gradient estimates are not just noisy but also biased due to the finite number of MCMC steps, which invalidates standard stochastic gradient descent analyses. This paper provides the first rigorous proof, to my knowledge, that this procedure can converge to an arbitrarily accurate approximation of the constrained MLE solution in polynomial time. The framing of this result as an FPRAS is strong and precise.

2.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The idea of defining a tractable class of models based on the mixing time of MCMC, rather than on structural properties like treewidth, is novel and insightful. It connects the literature on MCMC theory and mixing times directly to the problem of tractable learning. This perspective could open up new avenues for designing and analyzing learning algorithms for complex probabilistic models.

3.  **Technical Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound and very well-written. The problem setup, assumptions, and main results are stated clearly. The authors build their analysis upon established results in convex optimization for inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011) and concentration inequalities, making the proofs comprehensible. The inclusion of proof sketches in the main body provides valuable intuition for the reader before diving into the appendix. The separate treatment of the convex and strongly convex cases is thorough and highlights the different convergence behaviors.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The analysis hinges on several strong assumptions. The foremost is the requirement of knowing a convex, fast-mixing set `Θ` and, crucially, an efficient algorithm to project onto it. While the paper provides examples for the Ising model, identifying such sets and their corresponding projection operators for more general or complex MRFs is a major challenge in itself and is not addressed here. Furthermore, the convergence rates depend on the mixing parameters `C` and `α`, which are rarely known in practice, and whose theoretical bounds are often extremely loose. This makes the explicit schedules for setting the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC steps (K, M, v) more of a theoretical tool than a practical guideline.

2.  **Potential Looseness of Bounds:** The example in Section 6 suggests that the derived theoretical bounds may be quite conservative. The authors note that a much smaller Lipschitz constant worked better in practice, and the final solution was more accurate than the target `εθ`. While this does not invalidate the correctness of the analysis, it does raise questions about the tightness of the bounds and their utility in guiding the parameterization of the algorithm in real-world scenarios.

3.  **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper motivates the approach by highlighting the flaws of methods like pseudolikelihood, particularly in the misspecified case where they do not converge to the M-projection. However, the proposed method is also limited, as it only finds the M-projection *within the constrained set Θ*. If the true M-projection lies outside this set, the solution is inherently approximate. A more direct empirical comparison showing how the quality of the solution (e.g., in terms of KL divergence to the data-generating distribution) from this method compares to that from pseudolikelihood would be valuable for understanding the practical trade-offs.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

*   It would strengthen the paper to include a more detailed discussion on the challenges of identifying fast-mixing sets `Θ` and their projectors for models beyond the well-studied Ising model. Are there general principles or promising research directions for constructing such sets?
*   The authors acknowledge that their analysis does not cover common heuristics like persistent chains (as used in Persistent Contrastive Divergence). It would be useful to comment on what key difficulties prevent the current proof technique (which relies on sample independence at each iteration) from being applied to that setting, and whether it could be a fruitful direction for future work.
*   In the example, could the authors show the result of running the algorithm with the theoretically derived Lipschitz constant `L=97`? This would help illustrate the degree of conservatism in the bound. A brief comment on why the theoretical Lipschitz bound might be so loose for this model class could also be insightful.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pros:**
    *   Provides a novel and significant theoretical guarantee (FPRAS) for the widely-used MCMC-based MLE procedure.
    *   Introduces an interesting new perspective on tractability based on fast mixing rather than graph structure.
    *   The paper is technically solid, clear, and well-written.

*   **Cons:**
    *   Relies on strong assumptions about knowing the fast-mixing set `Θ` and having an efficient projection operator, which may limit practical applicability.
    *   The derived theoretical bounds appear conservative, potentially limiting their utility for practical algorithm tuning.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that rigorously analyzes a popular and important learning algorithm. It fills a crucial gap in our understanding and introduces a valuable new perspective on tractable learning. Despite the practical limitations of its assumptions, the theoretical contribution is significant and of high quality, making it a clear fit for NIPS.
