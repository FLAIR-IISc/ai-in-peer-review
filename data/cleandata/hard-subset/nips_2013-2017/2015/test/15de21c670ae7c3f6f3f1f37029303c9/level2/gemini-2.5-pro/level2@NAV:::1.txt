This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models with high treewidth, where exact inference is intractable. The authors propose a novel approach: instead of restricting the model structure (e.g., to low treewidth), they restrict the model parameters to a convex set where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is provably fast-mixing. The central claim is that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, performing projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients within such a fast-mixing parameter set constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood problem. This work provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for a common, yet largely heuristic, practical approach.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of defining a tractable model class via fast-mixing parameters, rather than structural properties, is highly original and provides a new lens through which to view tractable learning. The paper's most significant contribution is providing the first formal convergence guarantees for the widely used method of MCMC-based stochastic gradient descent for learning graphical models. This bridges an important gap between theory and practice. The results are general and apply broadly to exponential families.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper appears technically sound, providing detailed theoretical analysis for both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) optimization cases. The authors derive explicit schedules and complexity bounds for the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and chain lengths required to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the contribution within existing literature, and lays out the theoretical results in a logical and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

*   **Practical Implications:** The authors candidly state that the paper makes "no significant practical contribution," and the derived bounds appear quite conservative in the provided example. The analysis also assumes the existence of an efficient projection operator onto the fast-mixing set, the complexity of which is not included in the main analysis and could be a practical bottleneck.
*   **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis does not cover common practical heuristics used with MCMC-based learning, such as initializing chains from a persistent "pool" of samples or using strategies like contrastive divergence. While the authors acknowledge this, incorporating these aspects would broaden the impact.

Overall, this is an excellent theoretical paper. The weaknesses are minor and relate primarily to immediate practical applicability, which is not the paper's main goal. The novelty of the approach and the significance of providing the first formal guarantees for this class of algorithms are substantial. The work provides a solid foundation that future research can build upon. I strongly recommend acceptance.
