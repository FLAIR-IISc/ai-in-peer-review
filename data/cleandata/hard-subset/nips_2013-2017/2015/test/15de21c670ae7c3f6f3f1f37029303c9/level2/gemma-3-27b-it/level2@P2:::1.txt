This paper explores learning in graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, rather than the traditional tree-structured parameters. The core contribution is proving a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within such a set, leveraging gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for both unregularized and ridge-regularized cases, establishing polynomial sample complexity bounds. The work builds upon previous research in graphical model learning (e.g., Chow-Liu, pseudolikelihood, score matching) by offering a different notion of tractability and a corresponding theoretical framework. It also relates to the existing body of work on MCMC convergence and its application to statistical estimation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of utilizing “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable alternative to tree-structured parameters is a genuinely novel contribution. It broadens the scope of tractable learning beyond traditional graphical models.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including FPRAS guarantees, convergence rates, and bounds on sample complexity. The separation of convex and strongly convex cases is appropriate and leads to insightful results.
*   **Generality:** The results are stated within the broader framework of exponential families, making them applicable beyond graphical models specifically, enhancing the impact of the work.
*   **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The key concepts, notations, and assumptions are clearly defined. The presentation of the theorems and their sketches provides a good level of detail for understanding the main results.
*   **Example:** The inclusion of a small-scale empirical example, while not extensive, helps to illustrate the practical implications of the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Relevance:** The primary limitation is the lack of a compelling demonstration of practical advantage. The theoretical results are valuable, but the paper doesn't convincingly show how easily or efficiently one can *find* fast-mixing parameter sets for real-world problems. The example uses a simple Ising model, and its limited scope doesn't address potential challenges in more complex scenarios.
*   **Projection Complexity:** The paper acknowledges the cost of projecting onto the fast-mixing set but doesn’t delve deeply into its computational complexity for various models. This is a critical factor in determining overall efficiency.  The discussion of projection algorithms (e.g., for Ising models) feels somewhat disjointed from the main analysis.
*   **Tightness of Bounds:** The authors mention conservatism in their theoretical bounds. While acknowledging this is good scientific practice, a more rigorous analysis of the tightness of the bounds, or a discussion of scenarios where the bounds are loose, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Comparison to Existing Methods:** While the paper mentions limitations of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, etc.), it doesn't present a comprehensive empirical or theoretical comparison. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between this approach and established methods would be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions:** The requirement of knowing constants (C, α) that determine mixing rates is a significant assumption. In practice, these can be difficult to estimate accurately, impacting the reliability of the theoretical guarantees.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to learning in graphical models and exponential families.
*   The FPRAS guarantees and polynomial sample complexity bounds are significant theoretical contributions.
*   The generality of the results beyond graphical models is a strength.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical relevance of the proposed method remains unclear, and the paper lacks a compelling demonstration of its advantages in real-world scenarios.
*   The computational cost of projection onto the fast-mixing set could be a bottleneck.
*   The assumptions regarding mixing rate constants may be difficult to satisfy in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical paper with a novel idea. While the practical implications are not fully demonstrated, the rigorous analysis and general applicability of the results are commendable. I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding practical relevance and providing a more detailed discussion of the projection complexity and the limitations of the assumptions. A stronger justification of why finding fast-mixing sets is a reasonable task, and perhaps some exploration of algorithms for *finding* such sets, would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.



**Rating:** 7 (Strong Accept with Minor Revisions)
