

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by focusing on "fast-mixing parameter sets" rather than the conventional approach of restricting to tree-structured models. The main contribution is a theoretical guarantee that when parameters are constrained to a set where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) mixes rapidly, gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation will converge to the maximum likelihood solution inside the set with high probability. The authors establish both convex and strongly convex convergence rates, showing that the total computational effort required is polynomial in 1/ε (where ε is the desired accuracy).

The work is situated within the broader context of tractable learning in graphical models, building on prior approaches like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and tree-structured parameter constraints. The key novelty lies in shifting the focus from treewidth to mixing time as the criterion for tractability, which provides a more general framework applicable to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.

Quality is strong, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by detailed proofs in the appendix. The paper carefully distinguishes between convex and strongly convex cases, providing precise convergence guarantees for both. The main results (Theorems 2, 4, 5 for convex case and Theorems 6, 8, 9 for strongly convex case) are well-motivated and logically developed. The analysis of gradient Lipschitz continuity (Theorem 1) provides a solid foundation for the subsequent convergence results. The example in Section 6 demonstrates the practical applicability of the theory with a concrete Ising model example, though the small training set (5 samples) slightly limits its demonstration of practical utility.

Clarity is excellent. The paper is well-organized, with clear notation and logical progression from background to results. The main ideas are presented accessibly, with proof sketches provided for the more complex results. The example helps ground the theoretical analysis in a concrete setting. The paper adequately informs the reader about the necessary background on mixing times and exponential families.

Originality is a significant strength. The concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" as a tractability criterion represents a meaningful departure from the standard treewidth-based approach. While the paper references prior work on mixing times (Jerrum and Sinclair, Domke and Liu), it extends these results to provide a theoretical foundation for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning, which is a novel contribution to the literature. The generalization to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, rather than restricting to graphical models, further enhances the originality.

Significance is substantial. The work provides theoretical justification for a common practice in machine learning (using MCMC to approximate gradients in maximum likelihood learning) that previously lacked guarantees. This addresses a gap between theoretical understanding and common practice. The results offer practitioners concrete guidance on how to set parameters for convergence, which could improve the reliability of MCMC-based learning methods. The theoretical framework could potentially be applied to a wide range of models beyond graphical models, extending its impact.

A minor weakness is the limited discussion of how to determine fast-mixing parameter sets for practical models beyond the simple Ising model example. While the paper references projection techniques for specific models, a more detailed discussion of how to identify or construct such sets for general models would strengthen the practical relevance. The example with a 4×4 grid Ising model and only 5 training samples, while theoretically sound, doesn't fully demonstrate the approach's utility for more realistic learning scenarios.

The paper could be improved by including a more comprehensive comparison with existing MCMC-based learning methods like contrastive divergence, explaining how the proposed theoretical framework relates to and potentially improves upon these methods. A discussion of computational complexity beyond the total sampling effort, such as the cost of the projection step, would also be valuable.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in understanding when MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning is guaranteed to work. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and makes a novel contribution to the field of tractable learning in graphical models and exponential families. The theoretical guarantees provide a solid foundation for practitioners using MCMC-based learning methods, and the general framework could inspire future work on extending these ideas to more complex models. The work is well-suited for NIPS, as it advances theoretical understanding of a fundamental machine learning problem with clear implications for practice.
