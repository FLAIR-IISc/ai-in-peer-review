## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models with high treewidth. The authors propose a novel approach: instead of restricting parameters to tractable sets like tree-structured parameters, they focus on “fast-mixing parameters” – those allowing for rapid convergence of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The core contribution lies in proving a Fully Polynomial Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within such a set, using gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. This is a significant result, extending beyond graphical models to general exponential families.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing approaches (pseudolikelihood, composite likelihoods) and the potential benefits of fast-mixing parameter sets. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing both convex and strongly convex convergence guarantees, along with explicit bounds on the computational effort required to achieve a desired accuracy. The authors also acknowledge and address potential practical concerns, such as the complexity of the projection step and the impact of sample initialization.  The inclusion of an illustrative example, even with caveats about the Lipschitz constant, helps ground the theory.

However, the paper’s strength in theoretical depth also presents a weakness in practical focus. While demonstrating an FPRAS is a valuable theoretical contribution, the paper doesn't delve deeply into *how* to identify or construct such “fast-mixing” parameter sets in practice for complex models. The discussion relies heavily on existing mixing time bounds, which are often difficult to compute or apply. The analysis assumes knowledge of parameters like the Lipschitz constant (L) and mixing rate constants (C, α), which may be hard to determine in real-world scenarios.  Furthermore, the practical example shows a discrepancy between the theoretical and observed convergence, suggesting a degree of conservatism in the derived bounds.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The theoretical results are strong and represent a novel approach to tackling a fundamental problem in graphical model learning. The FPRAS guarantees are a significant advance. The work is mathematically sound and well-presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The practical implications are somewhat limited by the difficulty of identifying and working with fast-mixing parameter sets. The sensitivity of the results to parameters like L and α needs further investigation. 

**Overall:**  This is a strong theoretical paper with the potential to inspire further research. While practical application may be challenging, the results are significant and contribute valuable insights to the field of statistical inference.  I recommend acceptance, with a suggestion to the authors to discuss potential strategies for identifying fast-mixing parameter sets in more detail in future work.
