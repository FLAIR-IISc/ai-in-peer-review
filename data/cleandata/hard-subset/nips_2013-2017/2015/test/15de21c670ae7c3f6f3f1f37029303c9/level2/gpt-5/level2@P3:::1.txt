Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a tractability notion based on fast-mixing parameter sets rather than low treewidth. The key setup constrains parameters to a convex set Θ for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast, uniformly over Θ: ∥Mvθq − pθ∥TV ≤ Cαv. Under bounded sufficient statistics, the paper analyzes projected gradient descent where likelihood gradients are approximated by MCMC samples, yielding high-probability guarantees that the algorithm approximates the MLE restricted to Θ. Two regimes are treated: convex (possibly unregularized) likelihood with guarantees in function value, and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) likelihood with guarantees in parameter distance. The work derives Lipschitz constants for the gradient, establishes error decompositions for biased/noisy MCMC gradients, provides explicit schedules for iteration count K, per-iteration sample size M, and chain length v, and proves that the total sampling effort KMv gives an FPRAS: O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)) for ε-accuracy in objective (convex case) and O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) for ε-accuracy in parameters (strongly convex). Lower bounds show near-optimality up to constants and log factors. The framework applies broadly to exponential families and instantiates Θ through known fast-mixing conditions (e.g., Gibbs mixing bounds for Ising/Potts with bounded interactions; spectral/induced norm constraints [Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum 2004; Liu–Domke NIPS’14]). A small Ising example illustrates the schedules.

Relation to prior work
- Treewidth-based tractability (e.g., Chow–Liu 1968; high-girth learning [Heinemann–Globerson ICML’14]) contrasts with the fast-mixing perspective here.
- MCMC-MLE and stochastic approximation (e.g., Geyer 1991; Gu–Zhu 2001) lack nonasymptotic guarantees under biased gradients; this work provides finite-sample high-probability bounds assuming uniform mixing.
- Contrastive divergence and persistent sampling (Tieleman 2008; Hinton 2010; Carreira-Perpiñán & Hinton 2005) are widely used but typically heuristic; this work clarifies when CD-like procedures with sufficient burn-in and projection can approximate MLE inside Θ.
- Projection to fast-mixing sets has been studied (Domke & Liu NIPS’13; Liu & Domke NIPS’14). This paper leverages such sets to deliver end-to-end learning guarantees and explicit complexity schedules.
- Restart-based fast-mixing learning (Steinhardt & Liang ICML’15) ensures mixing by design; the present work maintains the original stationary distribution but constrains parameters to ensure mixing.

Strengths
- Clear, principled analysis of biased stochastic gradients arising from finite-time MCMC, with high-probability convergence guarantees.
- General exponential-family treatment with bounded sufficient statistics; not limited to a specific graphical structure.
- Explicit, practically interpretable schedules for K, M, v and companion lower bounds that demonstrate near-optimality (up to logs).
- Concrete connections to known fast-mixing criteria and convex projection formulations for Ising/MRFs (e.g., spectral/induced norm balls), making the Θ construction credible in important cases.
- Useful separation of convex vs. strongly convex regimes, with a Lipschitz gradient bound and reliance on established inexact/proximal gradient results [Schmidt et al. NIPS’11].

Weaknesses and limitations
- Practical scope depends critically on possessing a convex Θ with known global mixing constants (C, α) and an efficient projection oracle. For many models beyond pairwise binary MRFs (e.g., RBMs with hidden units, higher-order factors), constructing Θ and tractable projection is nontrivial or unknown.
- Mixing constants and bounds are often extremely conservative, potentially resulting in very large v and M. The small Ising example suggests conservatism, but broader empirical evidence is limited.
- The bounded sufficient-statistics assumption excludes common continuous exponential families (e.g., Gaussians without compact support). The applicability to typical energy-based models with unbounded statistics is restricted.
- The analysis assumes fresh, independent sampling each iteration. Popular and effective practice uses persistent chains or sample pools; the paper notes this but does not provide an adaptation of the proofs or bounds.
- Algorithmic presentation could more fully specify step-size selection and projection details for non-Ising families; guidance on estimating C, α, or adapting v online is limited.

Quality
The theoretical development appears careful and correct at a high level: Lipschitz bounds are derived using TV distance; gradient error is decomposed into sampling noise and bias due to finite mixing; concentration tools (Efron–Stein, Bernstein, Hoeffding) are used appropriately; and inexact gradient descent bounds [Schmidt et al.] are applied correctly in both convex and strongly convex regimes. The lower/upper work bounds are consistent with the schedules and assumptions. Some constants are likely loose, but the results are internally coherent and well-motivated.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with helpful summaries and proof sketches. Assumptions are stated explicitly. Notation could be tightened (e.g., consistent use of R2 vs. R, clearer statement of boundedness assumptions). A more explicit description of Algorithm 1 and a self-contained presentation of projection procedures for at least one non-Ising model would improve accessibility.

Originality
The core contribution—a general FPRAS for MLE under fast-mixing parameter constraints using MCMC-based gradient approximations, with explicit complexity and lower bounds—appears novel and distinct from prior treewidth-based or restart-chain approaches. The combination of projection onto mixing sets with nonasymptotic convergence analysis for biased gradients is an original and useful perspective.

Significance
The work provides a rigorous foundation for a widely used but poorly understood practice—MCMC-based gradient ascent—under a realistic and increasingly studied constraint (fast mixing). The practical impact depends on how broadly suitable Θ and its projection can be constructed with usable constants. Even so, the conceptual advance and unifying analysis are likely to influence subsequent theoretical and algorithmic work on learning in high-treewidth models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide guidance for estimating or upper-bounding C and α in practice, and discuss adaptive selection of v (e.g., via coupling or spectral-gap estimates), including the robustness of guarantees if only loose upper bounds are available.
- Include at least one additional experimental demonstration beyond the toy Ising case, ideally on a pairwise MRF with spectral-norm projection [Liu & Domke 2014], to illustrate the projection, schedules, and empirical constants.
- Clarify the scope of bounded sufficient statistics and discuss extensions or limitations for common continuous families or models with hidden variables.
- Discuss approximate projections and their effect on convergence (e.g., additive projection error integrated into the inexact gradient framework).
- Elaborate on step-size selection and whether backtracking/line search can be incorporated without violating the analysis.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, novel theoretical contribution with clear proofs and near-optimal complexity bounds.
- Broad applicability within exponential families and a compelling alternative to treewidth-based tractability.
- Timely connection between mixing-time theory and learning guarantees.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on global mixing bounds and projection oracles that may be unavailable beyond Ising-like models.
- Limited empirical validation; constants may be too conservative for practical guidance.
- Excludes persistent-chain practice that is common in large-scale learning.

Overall, the work makes a meaningful theoretical advance with careful analysis; impact would be strengthened by modest additional experiments and practical guidance on constructing Θ and choosing constants.
