Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies maximum likelihood learning for exponential families by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ where MCMC mixes geometrically fast. It proves that projected gradient descent using MCMC-based gradient estimates yields, with high probability, approximate MLE within Θ, establishing an FPRAS: O(ε−3) total sampling effort for ε-accurate log-likelihood in the convex (unregularized) case and O(ε−2) for ε-accurate parameter error in the ridge-regularized, strongly convex case (up to logarithmic factors). The analysis includes a Lipschitz gradient bound, explicit schedules for iterations/samples/mixing steps, and near-matching lower bounds. This connects to NIPS work on projection to fast-mixing sets for Ising/MRFs (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), inexact proximal-gradient convergence (Schmidt et al.), and learning fast-mixing models (Steinhardt & Liang), while contrasting with tree-structured learning (Chow–Liu) and composite/pseudo-likelihood and score matching.

Strengths
- Clear, general theoretical framework beyond graphical models (any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics).
- High-probability end-to-end guarantees for biased MCMC gradients, with explicit complexity and scheduling and accompanying lower bounds.
- Concrete linkage to mixing-time literature and projection-based definitions of Θ; candid discussion of limitations and scope.
- Potentially unifies practical MCMC-based learning with principled guarantees, offering a contrastive-divergence-like view with theory.

Weaknesses
- Practicality hinges on access to a convex fast-mixing set Θ and a projection oracle; for many families, constructing Θ and estimating C, α is nontrivial and problem-dependent.
- Mixing-time constants are notoriously loose, so prescribed work may be prohibitive; empirical section is minimal and toy.
- No empirical comparison to pseudo/composite likelihood or score matching under mis-specification; no treatment of persistent chains or hidden variables.
- Some clarity issues (e.g., consistent notation for bounds on sufficient statistics, explicit algorithmic detail for projection in general families).

Quality: Technically sound with plausible proofs (sketched in main text, deferred to appendix) leveraging standard concentration and inexact gradient descent analyses.

Clarity: Generally well organized; could benefit from a clearer catalog of known Θ/projection methods and assumptions upfront, and a summary table of constants and their roles.

Originality: Novel end-to-end FPRAS for MLE over fast-mixing sets in general exponential families with near-optimal bounds.

Significance: Advances theoretical understanding of when MCMC-based MLE is computationally tractable; impact depends on practicality of defining/projecting onto Θ.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, general theory with clear novelty and careful bounds.
- Bridges a widely used practice (MCMC gradients) with guarantees.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation; practical applicability uncertain due to projection and mixing-constant requirements.

Constructive suggestions
- Add experiments on moderate-sized MRFs comparing to pseudo/composite likelihood and score matching.
- Provide guidance or algorithms for constructing Θ and estimating C, α for common models; discuss adaptive schedules, persistent chains, and variance reduction.
- Clarify notation and present a concise algorithm box with all assumptions and parameter settings.
