**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of maximum likelihood learning in exponential family models, particularly high-treewidth graphical models where exact inference is intractable. The central idea is to constrain the model parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, a convex set where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to converge rapidly to the stationary distribution. The work provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of projected gradient descent where the gradient is approximated using MCMC samples. The main result is a proof that this common heuristic, when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood estimate within that set. The analysis is presented for both the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, deriving explicit bounds on the total computational effort (number of MCMC transitions) required to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. This work provides a formal theoretical foundation for a widely used class of practical learning algorithms.

**Relation to Prior Work**

The paper correctly positions its contribution relative to two main streams of research for learning intractable models. First, it contrasts with methods like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods, noting their potential shortcomings in the mis-specified model setting where they are not guaranteed to find the M-projection (the minimum KL-divergence solution). Second, it differentiates itself from approaches that restrict the model's graphical structure to ensure tractability (e.g., the Chow-Liu algorithm for trees). The key novelty lies in defining tractability through parameter constraints that guarantee fast MCMC mixing, rather than through structural simplicity.

This work builds directly upon a long line of research that uses MCMC to approximate the likelihood gradient, a popular approach for models like Markov Random Fields and Boltzmann Machines. However, while most prior work in this area is heuristic, this paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees and complexity analysis. The analysis leverages existing results on MCMC mixing times for specific models (e.g., Ising models) and on the convergence of projected gradient methods with inexact gradients, such as those by Schmidt et al. [2011].

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs (with intuitive sketches in the main text). The analysis is rigorous, carefully accounting for the two sources of error in the gradient estimate: the finite-sample Monte Carlo error and the bias from a finite number of MCMC transitions. The derivation of an FPRAS is a strong theoretical result. The separate, detailed treatment of the convex and strongly convex optimization settings adds to the completeness of the work. The assumptions, such as the existence of a convex fast-mixing set with an efficient projection operator, are clearly stated.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the problem is formally defined, and the main results are stated precisely before being proven. The notation is consistent and a helpful table is provided for reference. The use of proof sketches in the main body effectively conveys the core ideas behind the theorems without overwhelming the reader, while deferring the full technical details to the appendix. The illustrative example in Section 6, while simple, helps to ground the theoretical results and demonstrate their application.

**Originality**

The primary contribution—defining a tractable parameter set via fast MCMC mixing and proving that MCMC-based gradient descent can provably find the ML solution within this set—is highly original. While practitioners have implicitly hoped for such behavior, this work is the first to formalize it. It reframes the concept of a "tractable model" from one where exact inference is possible to one where approximate inference is provably efficient. This is a novel and powerful perspective that connects the theory of MCMC mixing times directly to the theory of learning.

**Significance**

The results presented are highly significant. They provide a much-needed theoretical justification for a class of algorithms that are widely used in practice but have lacked formal guarantees. This could encourage more principled approaches to learning intractable models, for example, by explicitly designing regularizers or constraints that promote fast mixing. Researchers working on both learning theory and MCMC methods are likely to build on these ideas. The general framework, applicable to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, broadens the potential impact beyond graphical models.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve it.

1.  **Scope of Fast-Mixing Sets:** The analysis hinges on the ability to identify a convex, fast-mixing set Θ and an efficient projection operator onto it. The paper gives good examples for the Ising model. However, a more extensive discussion on the applicability of this assumption to other models of interest in machine learning (e.g., models with latent variables, higher-order potentials, or continuous state spaces) would strengthen the paper. It would be helpful to better characterize the classes of models for which such sets are known to exist or are likely to be found.

2.  **Complexity of Projection:** The complexity analysis focuses on the total number of MCMC transitions, assuming the cost of projection is negligible. While often true, for some sets (e.g., the spectral norm ball for a matrix of parameters), the projection can be computationally non-trivial. A brief comment on the complexity of the projection operators for the cited examples would add to the completeness of the analysis.

3.  **Conservatism of Bounds:** As acknowledged in the example, the theoretical bounds on the required number of samples (M) and MCMC steps (v) can be quite conservative in practice. This is typical of high-probability bounds. A short discussion in the conclusion could note this gap and perhaps speculate on whether problem-dependent aspects (e.g., initializing the chain from the empirical data distribution, as in contrastive divergence) might lead to tighter, albeit more complex, analyses in future work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution by providing the first FPRAS for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning.
*   Provides a rigorous foundation for a widely used but previously heuristic class of algorithms.
*   The work is technically sound, well-written, and clearly presented.
*   The general framework for exponential families gives the results broad applicability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical utility is constrained by the assumption that a suitable fast-mixing set and an efficient projection operator are known.
*   The empirical validation is a small-scale demonstration rather than a comprehensive comparison with alternative methods.

In summary, the strengths of this paper, particularly its originality and theoretical significance, far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a fundamental contribution to the understanding of learning in intractable probabilistic models.
