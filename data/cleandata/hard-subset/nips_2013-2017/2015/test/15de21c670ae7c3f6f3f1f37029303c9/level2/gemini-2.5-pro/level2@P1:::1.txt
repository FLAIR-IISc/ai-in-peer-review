This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in undirected graphical models with high treewidth. The standard approach to this problem is often to restrict the model structure (e.g., to a tree) to ensure tractable inference. This work proposes a compelling alternative: instead of restricting the model's structure, it restricts the model's parameters to a convex set `Θ` where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is provably fast-mixing.

The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a common learning algorithm: projected gradient descent where the gradient of the log-likelihood is approximated using MCMC samples. The authors prove that, under the assumption of a "fast-mixing" parameter set, this procedure is a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the MLE within the set `Θ`. The analysis is general, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. The paper provides two key results: for the general convex case (e.g., unregularized MLE), achieving an `ε`-accurate solution in log-likelihood requires `O(1/ε^3)` total work. For the strongly convex case (e.g., with ridge regularization), an `ε`-accurate solution in parameter distance requires `O(1/ε^2)` work. This work provides a valuable theoretical foundation for a widely used class of heuristic methods, formally connecting the theory of MCMC mixing times with optimization guarantees for machine learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets, rather than structural properties like treewidth, is novel and insightful. This provides a new and potentially powerful perspective on learning complex graphical models. The paper tackles a fundamental problem in the field, and providing the first formal convergence guarantees (an FPRAS) for this MCMC-based learning scheme is a significant contribution. This work could inspire a new line of research into designing models and learning algorithms around the principle of guaranteed-efficient inference.

*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears to be sound and rigorous. The proofs clearly build upon established results in optimization with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011) and concentration inequalities, applying them carefully to the specific setting of biased and noisy gradients from finite-length MCMC runs. The assumptions of the analysis (existence of a convex fast-mixing set, an efficient projection operator, bounded sufficient statistics) are explicitly stated.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation and a good overview of related work, positioning the contribution effectively. The problem setup and main theorems are stated precisely. The inclusion of informal proof sketches in the main body provides valuable intuition for the reader, and the notation is consistent and well-defined.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

*   **Practicality of Assumptions:** The analysis relies on knowing a convex set `Θ` for which a uniform geometric mixing rate is guaranteed, along with an efficient algorithm to project onto `Θ`. While the paper provides compelling examples for Ising models based on prior work (e.g., bounding the spectral norm of the interaction matrix), the availability of such sets and projectors for more general and complex MRFs (e.g., with higher-order potentials or non-binary variables) is less clear. This could be a limitation for the method's immediate practical application. A brief discussion on the challenges or prospects of identifying such sets for other model families would be welcome.

*   **Conservatism of the Bounds:** As is common with worst-case theoretical analyses, the derived schedules for the number of iterations (`K`), samples (`M`), and MCMC steps (`v`) seem quite conservative. The authors acknowledge this in the experimental section, where they use a much smaller Lipschitz constant than the theoretical one to achieve faster convergence. While the polynomial complexity is the key result, it would be useful to discuss which parts of the analysis contribute most to this conservatism and whether tighter bounds might be achievable under slightly stronger assumptions.

*   **Scope of the Analyzed Algorithm:** The analysis covers a specific version of MCMC-based gradient descent where each iteration starts a new chain from an arbitrary distribution. In practice, more sophisticated schemes are often used, such as persistent contrastive divergence (PCD), where a "pool" of particles is maintained across iterations to provide a warm start. The paper correctly notes in the conclusion that its analysis, which relies on sample independence at each iteration, does not apply to such methods. While analyzing PCD is a much harder problem, acknowledging this limitation more prominently and perhaps speculating on the challenges involved would be helpful for the reader.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and significant conceptual contribution by defining a tractable class of models based on fast-mixing parameters rather than graph structure.
2.  It provides the first rigorous proof of convergence (an FPRAS) for a widely used but largely heuristic MCMC-based MLE algorithm.
3.  The theoretical work is high quality, and the paper is very clearly written and well-argued.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The practical applicability may be limited by the strong assumptions on the existence of, and ability to project onto, a known fast-mixing parameter set.
2.  The derived complexity bounds, while polynomial, are likely too loose for direct practical guidance.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant and original contribution to our understanding of learning in intractable graphical models. While the immediate practical impact may be tempered by the strength of its assumptions, the conceptual shift it proposes—from structural tractability to inferential tractability—is important and thought-provoking. The weaknesses are minor in the context of a theoretical contribution and do not detract from the quality of the results. I recommend acceptance.
