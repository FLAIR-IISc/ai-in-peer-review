

This paper presents a theoretically rigorous analysis of maximum likelihood learning via gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, under the novel constraint that parameters lie in a "fast-mixing set" where MCMC converges rapidly. The core contribution is establishing that for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, such constraints yield a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning. The analysis distinguishes between convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, deriving precise computational complexity bounds (e.g., cubic in 1/ε for convex, quadratic for strongly convex).

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical framework is sound and well-motivated. The paper correctly identifies the key gap in existing MCMC-based learning methods (lack of convergence guarantees) and addresses it by formalizing the fast-mixing assumption (Equation 2). The proofs for Lipschitz gradient (Theorem 1) and convergence guarantees (Theorems 2, 4, 6, 8) are technically solid, with clear proof sketches that capture the essential arguments. The distinction between convex and strongly convex cases is well-executed, and the computational complexity analysis (Theorems 3, 5, 7, 9) is thorough. The connection to prior work on treewidth constraints (Chow-Liu, Heinemann & Globerson) is appropriately contextualized, highlighting the novelty of shifting from structural tractability to mixing-time tractability.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Critical Implementation Gap:** The paper assumes the existence of an efficient projection operator Π_Θ[φ] onto the fast-mixing set Θ but provides no practical algorithm for computing it beyond trivial Ising model truncation (Section 3.1). For non-trivial exponential families (e.g., higher-order MRFs), the projection complexity is unaddressed. While the authors note this in Section 7, they fail to quantify its impact on the overall FPRAS claim. A concrete example (e.g., projection for pairwise MRFs via Domke & Liu [2013]) would strengthen the practical relevance.  

2. **Underdeveloped Practical Significance:** The empirical example (Section 6) is limited to a 4×4 Ising model with synthetic data and does not compare to existing methods (e.g., pseudolikelihood, contrastive divergence). Without such a comparison, it is unclear whether the theoretical guarantees translate to practical efficiency gains. The claim that "the algorithm finds a solution tighter than the specified ε_θ" is unconvincing without benchmarking against baselines.  

3. **Missing Key Related Work:** The paper overlooks Steinhardt & Liang (ICML 2014) who also study fast-mixing for learning, and Domke & Liu (NIPS 2013) who developed projection methods for Ising models. These works are directly relevant to the projection operator and fast-mixing sets, and their omission weakens the originality claim. The discussion should explicitly contrast their approach with the current paper’s.  

4. **Overstated Significance:** The conclusion claims the method "provides a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme," but this assumes the projection step is computationally negligible. As noted in Section 7, projection complexity may dominate for large models, which the paper does not quantify. This undermines the practical significance of the FPRAS claim.  

**Constructive Suggestions:**  
- **Address Projection Complexity:** Add a subsection on projection algorithms (referencing Domke & Liu, Liu & Domke) and analyze their complexity relative to sampling. For instance, if projection is O(N^2) and sampling is O(N^3), the FPRAS holds; if projection is O(2^N), it does not.  
- **Add Empirical Comparison:** Include a small-scale experiment comparing the proposed method to contrastive divergence (or pseudolikelihood) on a synthetic model, measuring both convergence speed and final likelihood.  
- **Clarify Scope:** Explicitly state that the FPRAS holds *only if* projection is efficient. Modify the abstract/conclusion to reflect this caveat.  
- **Cite Key Related Work:** Add Steinhardt & Liang (2014) and Domke & Liu (2013) to the background, contrasting their focus on specific models (Ising) with the paper’s general exponential family framework.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by rigorously establishing convergence guarantees for MCMC-based learning under fast-mixing constraints. The mathematical analysis is impeccable and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. However, the practical relevance is undermined by the unaddressed projection complexity and lack of empirical validation. The missing related work further dilutes the novelty claim. While the theoretical results are valuable, the paper falls short of demonstrating *why* this approach is preferable to existing methods in practice.  

**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept** (borderline). The core theory is novel and correct, but the lack of practical implementation details and empirical evidence prevents it from being a strong contribution for NIPS. With the suggested revisions (especially adding projection complexity analysis and a minimal empirical comparison), this could be a significant contribution. As-is, it risks being perceived as a "theory-only" paper without clear pathways to application.
