This paper proposes a framework for maximum likelihood learning in graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, where MCMC inference converges quickly. It proves a fully polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within such sets, analyzing gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The work builds on existing approaches like Chow-Liu and pseudolikelihood, but offers theoretical guarantees lacking in those methods.

The strengths of this work are significant. The problem addressed – efficient ML learning in high-treewidth models – is a long-standing challenge. The theoretical results establishing an FPRAS are strong and provide a valuable contribution, particularly the analysis of gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The generalization beyond graphical models to arbitrary exponential families is also a notable advantage. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with careful notation and a gradual introduction of concepts. The inclusion of a concrete example (Ising model) helps illustrate the applicability of the results. The extensive related work section provides good context. The analysis of both regularized and unregularized cases is a plus.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications are less clear. The constant factors hidden within the polynomial time complexity (e.g., the dependence on the Lipschitz constant L and the mixing rate parameters C and α) could be large, potentially limiting the method’s usefulness in practice. The example provides some reassurance, but a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, perhaps on more complex graphical models, would strengthen the claims. The discussion acknowledges this limitation, but a deeper exploration of potential strategies for mitigating the constant factors would be beneficial. The reliance on the ability to project onto the fast-mixing set Θ is also a potential bottleneck – while algorithms for some specific cases (Ising model) are mentioned, the general case might be computationally expensive. The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the implications of choosing different fast-mixing sets or how to identify suitable sets for specific problems. Further, the work builds heavily on existing results regarding mixing times (e.g., for the Ising model). The dependence of the runtime on these mixing time bounds, which themselves can be difficult to compute, is not fully discussed.

Arguments for acceptance:

*   Strong theoretical guarantees (FPRAS) for a challenging problem.
*   Novel approach using fast-mixing parameter sets.
*   Generalization to exponential families beyond graphical models.
*   Clear presentation and thorough related work.

Arguments against acceptance:

*   Potential for large constant factors limiting practical applicability.
*   Limited empirical evaluation.
*   Computational cost of projection onto the fast-mixing set.
*   Dependence on potentially difficult-to-compute mixing time bounds.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution with potential for impact. While the practical limitations should be acknowledged, the strength of the theoretical results and the novelty of the approach justify acceptance. Revisions addressing the empirical evaluation and a more detailed discussion of the constant factors and projection complexity would significantly enhance the paper. It’s a valuable contribution to the theory of learning in graphical models and related exponential families.
