

This paper proposes a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by introducing the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" where MCMC inference converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The authors prove that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to such a set, gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation provides a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning. The main theoretical contributions include bounds on computational complexity for achieving ε-accuracy: cubic in 1/ε for the unregularized case and quadratic in 1/ε for the ridge-regularized case.

The paper is well-motivated by the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth models, and the authors effectively contrast their approach with existing tractable methods (e.g., tree-structured models, pseudolikelihood). They make a valuable contribution by generalizing beyond graphical models to the exponential family framework, which broadens the applicability of their results. The connection to recent work on fast-mixing models (Steinhardt and Liang [28]) is well-handled, and the paper demonstrates how their theoretical framework could guide practical algorithm design.

The technical quality is strong. The authors provide rigorous proofs for both convex and strongly convex cases, with clear statement of theorems and helpful proof sketches. The mixing time analysis is appropriately grounded in the literature (e.g., using the Convergence Theorem [19, Theorem 4.9]), and the bounds on computational complexity are explicit and well-justified. The Ising model example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the theoretical results with concrete parameter choices, though the small scale (4×4 grid) limits practical insights.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good notation and logical flow. The background on mixing times in Section 3.1 is particularly helpful for readers unfamiliar with MCMC convergence guarantees. The discussion of limitations (Section 7) is honest and thoughtful, acknowledging that the analysis ignores projection complexity and doesn't encompass common strategies like maintaining a sample pool across iterations.

Regarding originality, the paper presents a genuinely novel perspective on tractable learning by shifting focus from treewidth to mixing time guarantees. This represents a meaningful advance beyond the standard tree-structured approach (Chow-Liu) and related methods that assume well-specified models. The exponential family framework generalization is a significant strength that broadens the scope beyond graphical models.

The significance lies primarily in the theoretical contribution of establishing a FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning under fast-mixing constraints. The cubic vs. quadratic complexity bounds provide valuable insights into the computational trade-offs for different accuracy requirements. While the authors acknowledge the paper has no significant practical contribution, the theoretical framework could inform future algorithm design, particularly for models where MCMC mixing can be guaranteed.

A few specific points for improvement: The paper would benefit from more discussion of how to identify fast-mixing sets for non-trivial model classes beyond the Ising example. The projection operation onto the fast-mixing set is mentioned as "trivial" in some cases (e.g., setting θij = β for |θij| > β), but for more complex models, this could be computationally expensive. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, a brief discussion of the computational complexity of projection would strengthen the analysis. Additionally, the paper would be more compelling with a larger-scale example demonstrating the practical feasibility of the approach, though the authors appropriately note this as beyond the scope of their theoretical contribution.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical models with a theoretically sound solution that advances the state of the art. The results are significant for theoretical machine learning, providing rigorous guarantees where previous methods lacked them. The authors have carefully considered the relationship to prior work, with appropriate citations to relevant NIPS proceedings (e.g., Domke and Liu [2013], Steinhardt and Liang [2014]).

The main weakness is the limited discussion of practical implementation challenges. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed exploration of how to identify fast-mixing sets for common model classes would strengthen the paper's contribution. The example with the Ising model, while helpful, is quite small-scale and doesn't demonstrate the approach's potential for larger models.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution with clear significance for the field. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a novel contribution to the literature on tractable learning. The theoretical guarantees provide a solid foundation for future practical algorithms, and the exponential family generalization broadens the applicability beyond graphical models. The minor weaknesses are primarily in the limited discussion of practical implementation challenges, but these don't detract from the paper's theoretical value.

I recommend acceptance as a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of tractable maximum likelihood learning. The paper's rigorous analysis and clear presentation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
