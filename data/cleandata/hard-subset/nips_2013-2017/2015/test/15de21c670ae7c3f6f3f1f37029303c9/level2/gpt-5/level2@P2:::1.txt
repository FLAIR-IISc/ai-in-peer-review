Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting to low-treewidth structures (e.g., trees), the authors constrain parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ: a convex subset for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast with known constants (C, α). They analyze projected (proximal) gradient descent where the likelihood gradients are approximated by MCMC sampling. Under bounded sufficient statistics, they prove: (i) the negative log-likelihood has an L-Lipschitz gradient; (ii) in the convex (unregularized) case, the averaged iterate achieves εf-suboptimality with total MCMC work KMv scaling as Õ(1/ε3); (iii) in the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case, the final iterate achieves ∥θK − θ∗∥ ≤ εθ with total work Õ(1/ε2). They give explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain steps v, and argue these are within logarithmic factors of lower bounds. The results apply beyond graphical models to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics. Examples of Θ come from known mixing-time conditions for Gibbs or Swendsen–Wang on Ising/Potts models (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral-norm bounds, bounded couplings). A small Ising example illustrates the schedules.

Relation to prior work
This builds on classical MCMC-MLE (Geyer; Gu & Zhu), but provides high-probability, end-to-end optimization guarantees under explicit mixing assumptions. It complements structure-restricted learning (Chow–Liu trees; high-girth correlation decay) and consistent surrogates (pseudolikelihood, score matching), by targeting MLE within a tractable fast-mixing set. It extends Domke & Liu (NIPS’13) and Liu & Domke (NIPS’14), who studied projecting parameters for fast mixing, by providing a full FPRAS for MLE inside Θ with explicit computational schedules. It is distinct from Steinhardt & Liang (ICML’14), which enforces fast-mixing via restarts, by handling general exponential families and projecting to mixing-certified sets. The work is also conceptually connected to FPRAS for the Ising partition function (Jerrum–Sinclair) but focuses on learning rather than partition estimation.

Strengths
- Clear, general theoretical contribution: high-probability convergence of projected gradient descent with biased, MCMC-based gradients under explicit mixing assumptions; covers both convex and strongly convex cases.
- Explicit schedules for K, M, v with matching (up to logs) lower bounds; complexity statements are transparent and interpretable.
- Applicability beyond graphical models: framed for general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Useful synthesis of mixing-time literature to define convex, projectable Θ (e.g., bounded couplings; spectral/induced-norm constraints), and discussion of projection algorithms.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality of assumptions: the FPRAS schedules require known constants (C, α) for geometric mixing. For most models/graphs, only loose, highly conservative bounds are available; for some chains (e.g., Swendsen–Wang, subgraphs-world) usable constants are rarely known. This limits the operational relevance of the schedules.
- Independence across iterations: the analysis assumes fresh, independently run chains each iteration; common practice (persistent chains/sample pools) is not covered and could dramatically reduce cost. The paper acknowledges this but offers no partial theory.
- Limited empirical validation: a single 4×4 Ising example with hand-tuned L offers little sense of tightness of bounds, practical runtime, or benefits of the Θ constraint relative to pseudolikelihood/CD or unconstrained MCMC-MLE on realistic sizes.
- Clarity/notation: the bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2R2∥θ − φ∥2 is used without a detailed proof; the constants around L = 4R2 2 + λ rely on R2 as a bound on ∥t(x)∥2, but the notation “R2” is easy to confuse with R squared. Some theorems rely on Algorithm 1, which is not shown here; step-size policy (1/L?) should be stated unambiguously.
- Scope of Θ: while ferromagnetic Ising with subgraphs-world is mentioned, projection and computation of α for that chain are not developed; convexity of Θ and availability of efficient projection should be clarified for each example Θ.
- Computational model: the complexity accounts for KMv but omits projection cost; for spectral-norm constraints, repeated SVDs could dominate for large models. The paper notes this but does not quantify regimes where projection is negligible.

Quality
The main arguments are plausible and build on standard tools (Efron–Stein, Bernstein/Hoeffding, Schmidt et al. on inexact gradients). The scheduling and lower bounds are carefully derived. However, some steps (Lipschitz and TV bounds, constants) would benefit from fuller proofs or precise citations. The empirical section does not substantiate practical impact.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with helpful intuition following the formal statements. Nonetheless, tightening notation (e.g., avoid overloading R2), explicitly stating the algorithm (projection step, step size), and consolidating assumptions (bounded t(x), convex Θ, known C, α, oracle for projection) would improve readability.

Originality
The notion of optimizing MLE over a fast-mixing parameter set with provable convergence for MCMC-based gradients appears novel, especially the FPRAS characterization and general exponential-family scope.

Significance
As a theory contribution, this is a clean and useful framework that connects learning, mixing-time analysis, and inexact optimization. The practical impact is limited by strong/unknown constants and the independence assumption, but the conceptual message—restrict to fast-mixing Θ and get principled guarantees for a ubiquitous procedure—is valuable and likely to influence follow-up work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concise algorithm box with the projection, step size (e.g., 1/L), and how M, v, K are set from the theorems.
- Discuss how C, α can be obtained or estimated in practice (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral-norm bounds), and whether adaptive v (based on TV or coupling diagnostics) could preserve guarantees.
- Extend analysis to sample reuse/persistent chains (perhaps via mixing plus perturbation arguments), or at least provide partial results.
- Add experiments on larger grids/graphs, comparing against pseudolikelihood/score matching/CD and unconstrained MCMC-MLE, reporting optimization error vs runtime and how the Θ constraint affects inference tractability.
- Clarify convexity/projection for all Θ examples, and quantify projection cost.
- Clean up references (missing years/venues) and typos (e.g., “Inferning” → “Inference”).

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled theoretical framework with clear, general results.
- Solid connection to mixing-time literature and inexact gradient methods.
- Potential to influence future robust learning procedures under mixing-time constraints.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions with unknown constants; limited practical guidance.
- Minimal empirical validation; unclear real-world benefits.
- Does not cover common persistent-chain practice.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept for a theory-focused contribution. If additional empirical evidence and clarification of assumptions/constants can be added, it would strengthen the case.
