- Problem: MLE in undirected graphical models is intractable because computing log-likelihood gradients requires expectations over high-treewidth distributions.
- Approach: Constrain parameters θ to a convex "fast-mixing" set Θ where MCMC converges geometrically, and run projected gradient descent with gradients approximated by MCMC.
- Main theoretical contribution: Proves this MCMC-based projected gradient procedure is an FPRAS for finding the constrained MLE, with separate guarantees for convex and ridge-regularized cases.
- Analysis: Provides bounds on total computation (iterations, samples, MCMC steps) needed to reach desired accuracy with high probability.
- Novelty: Introduces tractability defined by MCMC mixing time rather than graph structure, linking MCMC theory to learning tractability.
- Significance: First rigorous proof that biased, finite-step MCMC gradient estimates can yield polynomial-time convergence to constrained MLE.
- Technical quality: Analysis is sound, well-written, uses established inexact-gradient and concentration results, with helpful proof sketches.
- Assumption concern: Requires knowing a convex fast-mixing set Θ and an efficient projection operator, which is nontrivial beyond special cases like Ising models.
- Practicality issue: Convergence rates depend on mixing parameters C and α that are rarely known and often loosely bounded, limiting practical scheduling of K, M, v.
- Loose bounds: Empirical example suggests theoretical bounds (e.g., Lipschitz constant) are conservative and may not guide real-world tuning well.
- Limited comparisons: Paper lacks direct empirical comparison to alternatives like pseudolikelihood in terms of solution quality (e.g., KL divergence).
- Missing coverage: Does not analyze common heuristics like persistent chains (Persistent Contrastive Divergence) and depends on sample independence across iterations.
- Suggested additions: Discuss methods or principles for identifying Θ and projectors beyond Ising models and comment on extending proofs to persistent chains.
- Suggested experiments: Run the algorithm using the theoretical Lipschitz constant (L=97) and comment on why the theoretical bound is so loose.
- Overall assessment: Strong, novel theoretical paper filling an important gap with rigorous FPRAS results, though practical applicability is limited by strong assumptions and conservative bounds.
