This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, tackling the intractability of inference at high treewidth. Instead of restricting parameters to tractable sets like tree-structured parameters, the authors propose using “fast-mixing parameters,” where MCMC inference converges quickly. They prove a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within such sets, analyzing gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients for both unregularized and ridge-regularized cases. The work builds on existing approaches like pseudolikelihood and composite likelihoods but provides theoretical guarantees lacking in those methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of using fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable constraint for ML learning is a compelling alternative to traditional approaches like tree-structured parameters.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees, establishing an FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning under the proposed constraints. The analysis is thorough, covering both convex and strongly convex cases with different convergence rates.
* **Generality:** The results extend beyond graphical models, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. This broadens the applicability of the findings.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear outline of the problem, proposed solution, and theoretical results. The inclusion of a cartoon in Figure 1 helps visualize the algorithm.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors do a good job of situating their work within the existing literature on maximum likelihood learning and graphical models, acknowledging the limitations of existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Relevance:** While theoretically sound, the practical implications of the work are not fully explored. The example provided (Section 6) feels somewhat limited and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the benefits of this approach over existing methods in realistic scenarios. The conservative Lipschitz constant highlights this issue.
* **Projection Complexity:** The paper acknowledges that the complexity of projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set is not fully addressed. This is a crucial practical consideration, as projection can be computationally expensive, potentially negating the benefits of fast mixing. While the example suggests it’s manageable in some cases, a more detailed discussion of projection complexity for different types of fast-mixing sets would be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of bounded sufficient statistics, while reasonable, might not hold for all applications. The sensitivity of the results to violations of this assumption should be discussed.
* **Mixing Rate Dependence:** The runtime complexity heavily depends on the mixing rate (C and α). Obtaining accurate estimates of these parameters for real-world models can be challenging, and the impact of inaccurate estimates on the performance of the algorithm should be addressed.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** A more extensive empirical evaluation with diverse datasets and model structures would strengthen the paper. Comparing the performance to established methods like pseudolikelihood or composite likelihood would be particularly helpful.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to maximum likelihood learning in intractable graphical models.
* The FPRAS guarantee is a significant theoretical contribution.
* The generality of the results beyond graphical models is a strength.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The practical relevance of the work is not fully demonstrated.
* The complexity of projection onto the fast-mixing parameter set is a potential bottleneck.
* The empirical evaluation is limited.
* The assumptions made (bounded sufficient statistics) may not always hold in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field. While the practical implications are not fully explored, the theoretical results are significant, and the approach offers a promising alternative to existing methods. I recommend **acceptance**, but with the caveat that the authors should address the concerns regarding practical relevance, projection complexity, and empirical evaluation in future work. Specifically, expanding the experimental section to showcase more realistic scenarios and a thorough analysis of projection costs would be valuable.
