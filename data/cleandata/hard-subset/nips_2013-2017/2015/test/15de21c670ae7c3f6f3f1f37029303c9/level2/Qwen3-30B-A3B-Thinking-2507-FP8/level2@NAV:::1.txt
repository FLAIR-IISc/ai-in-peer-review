

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing sets" where MCMC inference converges quickly. The main claim is that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients will approximate the maximum likelihood solution within a fast-mixing parameter set with high probability, providing fully-polynomial time randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) for both unregularized and ridge-regularized cases.

The paper's strengths lie in its strong theoretical foundation. The authors provide rigorous proofs for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases, establishing precise computational complexity bounds: cubic in 1/ε for unregularized and quadratic in 1/ε for regularized settings. The analysis appropriately connects mixing time theory with optimization, using well-established tools like Bernstein's inequality and Schmidt et al.'s results on gradient descent with errors. The paper demonstrates excellent field knowledge, properly contextualizing its work against previous approaches (Chow-Liu, pseudolikelihood, and fast-mixing methods like Steinhardt & Liang), while making clear contributions to the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning.

The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation beyond a simple theoretical example. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the paper makes no practical claims, leaving readers uncertain about real-world applicability. The theoretical bounds also appear somewhat conservative (as noted in the example section), though this is expected for worst-case analysis.

The novelty is significant: instead of restricting to tree-structured parameters (the conventional tractable set), the authors propose fast-mixing parameters as a more flexible alternative that maintains computational tractability. This represents a meaningful theoretical contribution to the field.

The paper is clearly written with well-organized mathematical exposition, though the absence of experimental results limits its practical significance. The theoretical results are sufficiently complete for reproducibility, with detailed proofs in the appendix.

For acceptance: This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of when MCMC-based learning can be guaranteed to converge. While lacking empirical validation, the paper makes a strong theoretical case for a novel approach to tractable maximum likelihood learning. The significance is moderate for the field, as it provides theoretical grounding for a common practice rather than introducing a new algorithm. The work is clearly within NIPS scope as it makes a substantial theoretical contribution to machine learning.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, novel perspective on tractable learning, rigorous proofs
Con: No empirical validation, conservative theoretical bounds, limited practical significance
