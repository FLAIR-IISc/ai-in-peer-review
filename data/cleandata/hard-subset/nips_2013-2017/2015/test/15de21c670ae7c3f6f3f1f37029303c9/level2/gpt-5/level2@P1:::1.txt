Summary
This paper studies maximum likelihood (ML) learning for high-treewidth undirected models by constraining parameters to sets where Markov chains are guaranteed to mix rapidly (“fast-mixing sets”), rather than to low-treewidth structures. Within the general exponential family setting with bounded sufficient statistics, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated via MCMC. Under uniform geometric mixing over a convex parameter set Θ and assuming a projection oracle onto Θ, they prove high-probability convergence to the ML solution within Θ. They derive explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and Markov steps v that yield an FPRAS: in the unregularized (convex) case, an ε-accurate objective value with total sampling effort ~ (1/ε)^3 up to logs; in the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case, an ε-accurate parameter estimate with effort ~ (1/ε^2) up to logs. The analysis hinges on (i) a Lipschitz gradient bound for the regularized negative log-likelihood, (ii) concentration bounds controlling stochastic and bias errors from finite-length MCMC, and (iii) convergence results for projected gradient with inexact gradients [26]. The paper relates these guarantees to concrete fast-mixing sets from the literature for Ising/MRFs (e.g., bounded |θij|, norm bounds on R(θ), ferromagnetic conditions with alternative chains), and includes a small illustrative experiment.

Relation to prior work: Prior approaches to tractability enforce tree structures (Chow–Liu) or use composite/pseudolikelihood/score matching [3,16,20,30,22], or rely on approximate inference. Domke & Liu [8,20] proposed projecting parameters to fast-mixing sets to enable efficient inference; Steinhardt & Liang [28] ensured mixing by adding restarts. Classic MCMC-ML methods (e.g., Geyer; Gu & Zhu) use samples to approximate likelihood gradients but lack end-to-end complexity guarantees. This paper unifies these lines by showing that, given a fast-mixing parameter set and a projection oracle, MCMC-based gradient descent yields an FPRAS for ML within Θ, with explicit complexity in ε, mixing constants, and dimension-related quantities.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: reframes tractability for ML around fast-mixing sets rather than graph structure, and does so in general exponential families with bounded statistics.
- Theoretical completeness: precise high-probability convergence bounds, explicit schedules for K, M, v, and near-matching lower bounds (up to constants/logs) in both convex and strongly convex settings.
- Broad applicability: results hold for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, and accommodate various chains provided uniform mixing over Θ.
- Clear connection to known fast-mixing parameterizations (e.g., ∥R(θ)∥ constraints, ferromagnets) and to inexact gradient theory [26].
- Careful discussion of limitations (projection cost, persistent chains, initialization, contrastive divergence).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strength of assumptions: The existence of a convex Θ with known uniform geometric mixing constants (C, α) and an efficient projection ΠΘ is nontrivial in practice. Many available mixing-time bounds are conservative; estimating C, α for a given model is itself difficult, and projection may be costly (e.g., spectral-norm constraints for general MRFs).
- Practicality: The algorithm restarts chains from an arbitrary distribution each iteration/sample and requires relatively large v, differing from common persistent-CD heuristics that greatly reduce wall-clock time. The toy 4×4 example, while illustrative, does not probe scalability or practical tightness of the schedules.
- Bound looseness: The cubic dependence on 1/ε for the unregularized case may be pessimistic; constants depending on R, L, C, α can be large. While the authors are upfront about conservatism, some guidance on typical magnitudes would help.
- Notational clarity: The bound on sufficient statistics alternates between Ra and R2, and Theorem 1 uses L = 4R22 + λ; it would help to standardize notation (e.g., denote a single scalar bound R and avoid R2 both as a symbol and as a square).
- Dependence on problem size: While the authors state polynomial dependence on “other parameters,” it would be useful to spell out dimension/dependence on N, degree Δ, and graph size in the final work/complexity expressions for representative Θ (e.g., ∥R(θ)∥ constraints).
- Missing comparison baselines: No empirical comparison against pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood or against approximate inference-based ML is provided to illustrate when the proposed approach is preferable.

Quality
The technical development appears sound: the decomposition of gradient error into sampling noise and bias from finite mixing is standard; the use of Efron–Stein, Bernstein, and vector Hoeffding is appropriate; the Lipschitz gradient proof sketch is plausible under bounded statistics and TV continuity. The lower/upper bound pairing on total effort is a strong point. Some proofs are deferred; given space limits, this is acceptable if the appendix is complete. Clarifying the TV Lipschitz bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ c∥θ − φ∥ (constant and assumptions) would strengthen Theorem 1.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful intuition and explicit schedules. Improving notation consistency (Ra vs R2, subscript conventions) and summarizing the dependence of K, M, v on problem size for a few canonical Θ would aid readability. A brief algorithm box with precise step sizes and projection details would be helpful.

Originality
The combination of fast-mixing parameter sets, projection, and an FPRAS-style complexity analysis for ML with MCMC gradients in general exponential families is, to my knowledge, novel. Prior work addressed either inference-time projection or specific fast-mixing chains; this paper provides the missing learning-time guarantees.

Significance
The work advances theoretical understanding of when MCMC-based ML learning can be made provably tractable by design. While the practical scope is limited by the need for a known fast-mixing Θ and efficient projection, the results are likely to influence future work on principled constraints/regularizers that enforce mixing and on adaptive estimation of mixing rates.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a precise lemma and reference for the TV Lipschitz bound between pθ and pφ used in Theorem 1, including constants in terms of the bound on t(x)?
- How do your total work bounds scale with model size N for the Ising examples under ∥R(θ)∥∞ or spectral-norm constraints? A compact table would help.
- Do your results extend to persistent chains or sample pools (initializing from previous samples), perhaps via coupling/mixing-from-warm-start bounds?
- In practice, how would one choose/estimate C and α, and can the schedules be made adaptive (e.g., doubling v until a coupling test passes)?
- Beyond Ising/Potts, what examples of Θ and projection oracles exist for higher-order or continuous exponential families?

Suggestions
- Unify notation for the sufficient-statistics bound and check uses of R, R2, Ra throughout.
- Add a short empirical comparison to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood and to a persistent MCMC-ML baseline on modest-sized grids to contextualize practicality.
- Discuss projection complexity more concretely for the cited Θ (e.g., spectral-norm projection via SVD) and its contribution to total runtime.
- Consider a brief extension sketch for warm-start mixing guarantees to capture common practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually fresh and general theoretical framework; clear contribution.
- Solid technical results with explicit, nearly tight complexity bounds.
- Bridges a gap between fast-mixing parameterization and learning-time guarantees.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong assumptions (uniform mixing constants, projection oracle) limit practical applicability.
- Limited empirical validation; unclear real-world impact at present.
- Some notational/clarity issues and reliance on conservative constants.

Overall, this is a thoughtful, well-motivated theoretical paper that would make a useful addition to NIPS, provided the authors improve clarity around assumptions/constants and better articulate practical implications.
