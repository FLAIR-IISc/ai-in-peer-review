This paper addresses the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in Markov networks, where exact gradient computation is intractable, necessitating the use of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). The authors make a significant theoretical contribution by proving that, under the condition of fast MCMC mixing, the resulting MCMC-based weight learning algorithm achieves a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). They provide separate theoretical bounds for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (non-convex) cases.

The work builds upon previous approaches to tackling intractable maximum likelihood learning, such as pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22], and composite likelihoods [20, 30]. However, it distinguishes itself by focusing on a novel notion of tractability: fast-mixing parameter sets, rather than restricting to tree-structured parameters as in the Chow-Liu algorithm [1968] or high-girth models [Heinemann & Globerson, 2014]. This is a conceptually appealing shift, and the generality to exponential families beyond graphical models is a strength. The paper appropriately cites relevant work on mixing times [19, 12, 9] and acknowledges the limitations of existing bounds requiring adaptation to a learning setting. 

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is well-defined, and the proof sketches offer valuable intuition. Minor typos are present, but do not detract significantly from understanding. The theoretical analysis appears plausible, though a complete verification of the proofs is beyond the scope of this review. The careful separation of analyses for convex and strongly-convex cases is commendable, leading to nuanced results regarding the computational effort required for different levels of accuracy.

However, a major weakness lies in the practical relevance of the core assumption: fast-mixing parameters. The paper acknowledges that the parameter constraints required for the theorems are unlikely to hold in practice, severely limiting the applicability of the results. While the example Ising model section provides *some* evidence of practical viability, it relies on a relatively simple setup and a modified Lipschitz constant, raising concerns about its generalizability. The authors' own discussion highlights this limitation, suggesting that enforcing the mixing condition artificially (through regularization) might be beneficial, but also cautions against over-regularization when combined with ridge regression. This raises a difficult trade-off that remains unexplored.

The open question regarding artificially enforcing the mixing condition is a pertinent criticism. Successfully doing so would represent a substantial step towards making the theoretical results practical, but is likely to be a non-trivial research undertaking. It’s unclear how to effectively regularize *for* fast mixing without introducing undesirable biases or computational costs. The discussion of initializing samples with contrastive divergence [5] is interesting, but leaves open the question of how the analysis would need to be adjusted to account for this.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous formalization of the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning yields a FPRAS.
* **Novelty:** The concept of fast-mixing parameter sets as a notion of tractability is a valuable contribution.
* **Generality:** The results extend beyond graphical models to the broader class of exponential families.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making the complex theoretical analysis accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited practical applicability:** The requirement of fast-mixing parameters is likely unrealistic in many practical scenarios.
* **Lack of concrete guidance for enforcement:** The paper doesn’t offer specific strategies for enforcing the mixing condition in practice.
* **Potential for over-regularization:** Combining mixing-enforcing regularization with existing methods like ridge regression could lead to suboptimal results.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a solid theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of MCMC-based weight learning. However, the substantial gap between the theoretical results and practical applicability raises serious concerns. While the authors are forthright about this limitation, addressing it will require significant non-trivial research.  I recommend accepting the paper with the caveat that future work is needed to bridge the gap between theory and practice. A "weak accept" seems appropriate given the quality of the theoretical work and the clear acknowledgement of its limitations.
