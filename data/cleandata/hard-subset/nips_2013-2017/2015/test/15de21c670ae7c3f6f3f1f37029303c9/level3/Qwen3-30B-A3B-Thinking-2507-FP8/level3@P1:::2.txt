

This paper introduces a novel approach to maximum likelihood estimation in exponential families by leveraging fast-mixing Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods, providing theoretical guarantees where previous methods lack them. The authors propose constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing set" where MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution, rather than restricting to small-treewidth graphical models. They prove that gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within this set, with complexity bounds of O(1/ε³) for unregularized cases and O(1/ε²) for ridge-regularized cases.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by addressing a gap in the literature: while MCMC-based likelihood approximation is widely used in practice (e.g., contrastive divergence), it lacks theoretical guarantees. The authors bridge this gap by combining fast-mixing assumptions with proximal gradient descent analysis from Schmidt et al. (2011), providing rigorous convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases. The theoretical framework is well-motivated and generalizes beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.

The work is original in its approach to tractable learning - instead of restricting to tree-structured models (Chow-Liu), it focuses on parameter sets where inference is tractable due to fast mixing. This represents a meaningful alternative to existing methods, though the paper could better clarify how it differs from recent work on fast-mixing models (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang, 2014). The authors demonstrate the approach with a simple Ising model example that confirms their theoretical bounds, though they correctly note the practical limitations of the method.

Quality is strong: the proofs are technically sound, with appropriate use of concentration inequalities and mixing time bounds. The analysis of both convex and strongly convex cases is comprehensive. However, the paper contains several important omissions that weaken its technical quality. Most significantly, it fails to explicitly state that the log-likelihood is convex (a crucial assumption for the analysis), which follows from the convexity of A(θ) (the log-partition function). The paper also omits a critical assumption: the constraint set Θ must be convex for the projection operator to be well-defined and for the analysis to hold. These omissions undermine the paper's technical rigor.

Clarity is generally good, but the missing convexity statement and unmentioned convexity assumption of Θ are serious gaps. The paper would benefit from a more explicit summary of all assumptions at the beginning. There are also minor typographical errors (lines 185, 192, 207, 281, 311-312) that could be easily fixed.

Originality is high: the fast-mixing parameter set concept represents a new theoretical framework for tractable MLE learning, distinct from existing approaches like tree-structured models or pseudolikelihood. The paper thoughtfully situates itself against previous work, acknowledging limitations of existing methods and positioning its contribution as a meaningful alternative.

Significance is moderate. While the theoretical contribution is substantial, the practical impact is limited because proving fast-mixing for MCMC remains challenging in practice. As the authors note, this "replaces one difficult problem with another." The paper's theoretical framework is valuable for understanding the behavior of MCMC-based learning algorithms, but it doesn't provide a practical method for finding fast-mixing parameter sets. The authors acknowledge this limitation candidly, discussing how their analysis relates to contrastive divergence (though they clarify their algorithm isn't exactly CD), which is commendable.

The paper's strengths include its novel theoretical contribution, solid technical analysis, and honest discussion of limitations. Weaknesses include the missing convexity statement, the unmentioned convexity of Θ, and limited practical significance. The work is well-positioned as a theoretical advance that complements rather than replaces existing methods.

Pro: Novel theoretical framework for tractable MLE learning using fast-mixing parameter sets; rigorous convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases; clear connection to practical MCMC-based learning algorithms; thorough discussion of limitations.

Con: Missing key technical assumptions (convexity of log-likelihood, convexity of Θ) that undermine the paper's technical quality; practical impact limited by difficulty of proving fast-mixing; the approach may not be directly applicable to most real-world learning problems.

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCMC-based maximum likelihood estimation in exponential families. While the practical utility is limited by the difficulty of identifying fast-mixing parameter sets, the theoretical framework provides valuable insights into the behavior of existing MCMC-based learning algorithms. The paper is well-written, technically sound where assumptions are properly stated, and makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of tractable learning. The authors' candid discussion of limitations strengthens the paper's credibility. Given NIPS' focus on theoretical contributions, this paper should be accepted for its novel theoretical perspective, even if its practical impact is limited.
