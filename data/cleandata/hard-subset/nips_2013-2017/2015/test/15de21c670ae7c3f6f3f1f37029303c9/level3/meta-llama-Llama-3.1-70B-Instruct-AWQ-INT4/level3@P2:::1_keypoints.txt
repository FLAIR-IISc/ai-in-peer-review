- Proposes restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set so MCMC inference converges quickly for ML in high-treewidth undirected graphical models.  
- Proves that for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, gradient descent with sampling-approximated gradients converges to the ML solution within the fast-mixing set with high probability.  
- Paper is well-motivated and explains the problem and proposed solution clearly.  
- Fails to adequately acknowledge prior literature, notably connections to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics.  
- Algorithm is closely related to existing methods and should be compared empirically/theoretically to prior work on optimizing intractable likelihoods.  
- Makes strong/unnecessary assumptions, e.g., requiring uniform ergodicity of Markov chains and reinitialization from a fixed distribution r.  
- Reinitializing from the previous sample would be preferable and has been analyzed in prior work; current choice lacks justification.  
- Presentation of assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 is unclear and should be stated explicitly near the theorems.  
- Results are not particularly novel; existing theory already covers similar and more general scenarios, so novelty is marginal.  
- Strong assumptions (e.g., in Theorem 6) are unnecessary; should cite Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret for more general results.  
- Writing is clear and background is well explained, but the organization is uneven and some sections feel disconnected.  
- Clarity could be improved by more concise presentation of assumptions and theorems.  
- Reviewer scores: Quality 6/10, Originality 4/10, Significance 5/10, Clarity 7/10; recommendation is to reject in current form.  
- Suggested revisions: acknowledge prior literature, relax/justify assumptions, improve presentation of theorems, and provide more novel or significant results.  
- Acceptance arguments: good motivation and structure; Rejection arguments: lack of novelty, poor literature acknowledgment, presentation issues, and limited impact.
