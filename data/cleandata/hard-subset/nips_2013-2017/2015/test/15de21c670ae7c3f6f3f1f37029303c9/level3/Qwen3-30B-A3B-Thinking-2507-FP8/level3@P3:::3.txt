

# Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the problem of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models. The authors propose an alternative to the standard approach of restricting to tree-structured models (e.g., Chow-Liu algorithm) by instead considering "fast-mixing parameter sets" where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The core theoretical result establishes that when parameters are constrained to such a set, MCMC-based gradient descent becomes a Fully Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning. The paper proves separate bounds for unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, showing cubic and quadratic dependence on 1/ε respectively for the total computational effort.

The work builds on previous mixing time theory (e.g., Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Liu & Domke, 2014) but applies it to the learning problem in a novel way. While previous work has focused on either tractable inference (tree-structured models) or approximate inference methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching), this paper provides a unified theoretical framework for when MCMC-based learning can be guaranteed to converge to the maximum likelihood solution.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with clear proofs. The main theorems establish rigorous bounds on the computational complexity of MCMC-based learning under the fast-mixing assumption. The analysis for convex and strongly convex cases is well-separated and thorough. The proofs appear plausible though not fully verified by the reviewer. The paper acknowledges limitations of the approach (e.g., ignoring projection complexity) and provides concrete examples.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and generally well-written with clear notation. The introduction effectively frames the problem and relates it to previous work. The main results are presented with clear proof sketches that convey the intuition behind the technical arguments. Minor typos are present but do not impede understanding.

**Originality:** This work represents a significant theoretical contribution that is distinct from previous NIPS papers. While previous work has examined mixing times for MCMC (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Domke & Liu, 2013), this paper is the first to establish a formal connection between mixing times and the convergence guarantees of MCMC-based learning algorithms. The generalization to exponential families beyond graphical models is a notable theoretical extension.

**Significance:** The theoretical insights are valuable for understanding the conditions under which MCMC-based learning can be trusted. However, the practical significance is limited by the stringent parameter constraints required for the theorems to hold. As the authors themselves note, "the parameter constraints required for the theorems are unlikely to be satisfied in practice," which significantly limits applicability. The paper does not address how to enforce the mixing condition in practice, though it raises the important open question of whether regularization could be used for this purpose.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution that precisely formalizes when MCMC-based learning yields a FPRAS. The separation of analysis for convex and strongly convex cases is elegant. The results provide a clear roadmap for when MCMC-based learning can be trusted.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant weakness is the disconnect between theory and practice. The parameter constraints required for the theorems are likely too restrictive for real-world applications, making the results primarily of theoretical interest. The paper does not address how to enforce the mixing condition in practice, and the open question about combining artificial mixing enforcement with ridge regression is not resolved.

**Overall assessment:** This is a solid theoretical paper that makes an important contribution to understanding the conditions under which MCMC-based learning can be guaranteed to work. The results are technically sound and well-presented. However, the practical relevance is limited due to the stringent assumptions required for the theorems to hold. While the paper provides valuable theoretical insights, significant non-trivial research would be needed to make these results practically useful.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of MCMC-based learning. While the practical applicability is currently limited, the theoretical insights are valuable for the field. The paper would be strengthened by more discussion of how to enforce the mixing condition in practice, but as is, it represents a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of learning with MCMC.
