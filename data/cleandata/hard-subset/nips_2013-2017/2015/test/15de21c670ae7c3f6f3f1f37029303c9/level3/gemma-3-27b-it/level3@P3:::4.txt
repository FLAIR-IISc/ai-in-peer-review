This paper introduces a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in intractable graphical models by focusing on "fast-mixing" parameter sets, offering convergence rate guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The framing of the problem and the theoretical results are original and represent a significant contribution to the field, particularly given the challenges of learning with intractable models. While the presentation is clear and the core ideas are well-articulated, the paper’s significance feels somewhat muted, and the discussion of lower bounds requires substantial expansion.

The paper skillfully connects to prior work, establishing the limitations of traditional approaches like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods, which often suffer from inferior sample complexity or fail to guarantee tractable inference post-learning. It correctly positions itself as an alternative to restricting parameters to tractable sets like tree-structured distributions (Chow-Liu algorithm) and acknowledges related work on mixing times, specifically citing Jerrum and Sinclair’s work on the Ising model’s computational complexity. The paper also appropriately references recent advances in MCMC-based learning, such as Steinhardt and Liang’s work on fast-mixing chains with restart probabilities.

**Strengths:**

* **Originality:** The concept of “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable class is novel and provides a different perspective on addressing intractability in graphical models.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper delivers concrete convergence rate results for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, a common practical technique lacking theoretical foundation. Providing fully-polynomial time randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) is a valuable achievement.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the proof sketches offer intuition for the key results.
* **Generality:** Extending beyond graphical models to the broader exponential family framework is a notable strength, increasing the applicability of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lower Bound Interpretation:** Theorem 7, concerning lower bounds on computational effort, is difficult to interpret and lacks sufficient discussion. The connection between the theoretical lower bound and practical implications isn’t made clear. Expanding this section with examples and a broader treatment of lower bounds would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Significance:** While the paper addresses an important problem and fills a gap in the literature, the overall significance is unclear. The practical impact of learning with fast-mixing parameter sets versus other approaches isn't fully explored.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** While an example is provided, the paper largely focuses on theoretical results. A more in-depth discussion of practical challenges, such as projection algorithm complexity and initialization strategies (e.g., contrastive divergence) would be beneficial.
* **Conservative Bounds:** The example provided suggests a degree of conservatism in the theoretical bounds, which should be acknowledged and potentially investigated further.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and mathematically rigorous approach to a challenging problem in machine learning.
* The theoretical results are significant, providing guarantees for a commonly used but previously unanalyzed learning procedure.
* The work fills a gap in the literature on learning intractable models using MCMC.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The unclear interpretation of the lower bound and limited discussion of practical considerations weaken the overall impact of the paper.
* The lack of a compelling demonstration of practical advantage compared to existing methods raises questions about the paper's ultimate significance.

**Recommendations:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically:

1. **Expand the discussion of Theorem 7:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of the lower bound, illustrate its implications with examples, and broaden the treatment of lower bounds in relation to the problem.
2. **Add a section on potential impact and future extensions:** Discuss the potential applications of this work, possible follow-on research directions (e.g., exploring different projection algorithms, adaptive step size strategies, and connections to variational inference), and the limitations of the current approach.
3. **Address the conservatism of the bounds:** Acknowledge the observed conservatism in the example and discuss potential reasons for it. Investigating methods to tighten the bounds would be valuable.



In conclusion, this paper presents a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of learning in intractable models. Addressing the identified weaknesses will further enhance its impact and solidify its position as a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
