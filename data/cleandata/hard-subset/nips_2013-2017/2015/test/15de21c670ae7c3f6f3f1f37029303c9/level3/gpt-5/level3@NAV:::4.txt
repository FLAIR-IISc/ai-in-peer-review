This paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected models by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set and analyzing projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated by MCMC. For exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, the authors derive nonasymptotic, high-probability convergence rates that scale with the mixing rate (C, α): cubic effort in 1/ε for the unregularized (convex) case and quadratic in 1/ε for the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case, yielding FPRAS-style guarantees. The work connects to NIPS/NeurIPS lines on tractable restrictions (e.g., Chow–Liu trees), surrogate estimators (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood), and learning with MCMC (e.g., Geyer; contrastive divergence), while dovetailing with fast-mixing projections (Domke & Liu) and fast-mixing learning (Steinhardt & Liang). The framing of “tractability via fast mixing” and the explicit schedules for K, M, v are original and valuable.

Quality: The analysis is technically sound and careful, with clear use of Lipschitz and strong convexity arguments, concentration inequalities, and inexact gradient descent bounds. The rates are meaningful and interpretable in terms of mixing times. One concern is the interpretability of the lower bound (Theorem 7): the statement is hard to parse, the role of γ and the logarithmic factors is not well contextualized, and its tightness relative to Theorem 8 deserves deeper discussion and comparison to the convex lower bound (Theorem 3).

Clarity: The manuscript is well organized and clearly written. The example helps, though more discussion of how to obtain or estimate C, α in practice would help readers deploy the results.

Originality: High. The “fast-mixing parameter class” abstraction and convergence-rate results for MCMC-based ML over arbitrary treewidth exponential families appear novel and fill a notable theoretical gap.

Significance: Potentially significant, but underdeveloped in the paper. The approach offers a principled alternative to heuristic CD and mis-specified surrogates, yet the practical impact depends on availability/efficiency of projection operators and realistic mixing constants. A broader discussion of domains (e.g., vision MRFs, structured prediction), comparisons to pseudolikelihood/score matching in sample and computational complexity, and follow-on opportunities (adaptive/learned fast-mixing constraints; other chains) would strengthen the case.

Pros for acceptance:
- Original and technically solid convergence guarantees tied to mixing times
- General exponential-family scope, with explicit schedules and FPRAS characterization
- Clear presentation and connection to prior NIPS work

Cons:
- Theorem 7 is difficult to interpret; needs expanded treatment and empirical intuition
- Practical significance and impact are not convincingly articulated
- Limited guidance on projecting to Θ beyond Ising and on estimating C, α

Recommendation: Accept, contingent on clarifying Theorem 7 and adding a discussion of impact, extensions, and practical guidance.
