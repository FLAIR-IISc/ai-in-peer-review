

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper addresses maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to "fast-mixing" sets where MCMC inference converges rapidly. The authors prove that with this constraint, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients can find an approximate maximum likelihood solution within the constrained set. They provide theoretical guarantees for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, showing that the total computational effort scales polynomially with the desired accuracy.

The paper's main contribution is the theoretical analysis of using MCMC-approximated gradients for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families, when parameters are constrained to fast-mixing sets. While the problem setting is relevant to graphical models, the authors claim their results apply more generally to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a clear theoretical framework for understanding the convergence properties of gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The analysis of the computational complexity in terms of the desired accuracy is well-structured, and the example in Section 6 provides a concrete illustration of the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper suffers from significant shortcomings in its literature review and theoretical foundations. The authors appear unaware of substantial prior work on stochastic optimization with Markovian dynamics. Their approach is closely related to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics, which have been extensively studied in the literature (e.g., [26] cited in the paper is exactly on this topic). The authors should have explicitly compared their work to these established methods.

The paper's theoretical contributions are not novel. The results on convergence of gradient descent with noisy gradients are covered by the general theory of stochastic approximation, particularly the work of Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret [26], which provides much more general convergence results for stochastic approximation algorithms with Markovian noise. The authors' assumptions about Markov chain mixing are unnecessarily restrictive; Markov chains need not be uniformly ergodic, and reinitializing at a fixed distribution r is not required (reinitializing with previous samples is actually preferable and has been extensively analyzed in the literature on contrastive divergence).

The presentation of key assumptions is poor. The assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 are not clearly stated near the theorems themselves, making it difficult for readers to assess the conditions under which the results hold. Theorem 6 makes strong assumptions that are unnecessary; the authors should have cited the more general results by Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret to justify their approach.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its analysis but relies on unnecessary assumptions and fails to properly situate its contributions within existing literature. The authors do not adequately address the limitations of their approach or compare with existing methods for optimizing intractable likelihoods. The theoretical results are not substantially stronger than what is already known in the stochastic approximation literature.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from poor presentation of assumptions and results. The connection to prior work is weak, and key assumptions are not clearly stated near the theorems where they are used. The discussion of related work is insufficient, particularly regarding the extensive literature on stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics.

**Originality:** The paper's novelty is marginal. The core idea of using MCMC to approximate gradients in maximum likelihood learning is well-established, and the theoretical analysis does not provide significant new insights beyond what is already covered in the stochastic approximation literature. The paper fails to clearly articulate how its approach differs from and improves upon existing methods.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is limited because the results are not substantially stronger than existing theory, and the practical contribution is unclear. The authors do not demonstrate that their approach outperforms existing methods for optimizing intractable likelihoods in practice, and the theoretical bounds appear to be conservative.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper provides a clear theoretical framework for understanding the convergence properties of gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The example in Section 6 illustrates the theoretical results with a concrete implementation.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The paper suffers from a major lack of awareness of prior literature, presents unnecessary assumptions, fails to properly compare with existing work, and the theoretical contributions are not novel. The presentation of key assumptions is poor, and the novelty of the work is marginal.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. The paper's contributions are not sufficiently novel, and the theoretical results are not substantially stronger than what is already known in the stochastic approximation literature. The authors should thoroughly review the literature on stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics before considering publication. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite that properly situates the work within the existing literature, removes unnecessary assumptions, and clearly articulates the novel contributions.
