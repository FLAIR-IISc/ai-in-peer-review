

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper introduces an elegant theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by leveraging fast-mixing Markov chains as a tractability condition, offering an alternative to traditional small-treewidth assumptions. The authors propose that if parameters are constrained to a set where MCMC mixing is guaranteed (a "fast-mixing parameter set"), then gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients provides a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes theoretical guarantees for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, showing that the total computational effort scales as O(1/ε³) for likelihood error and O(1/ε²) for parameter error. Second, it provides a clear connection between MCMC mixing time and learning guarantees, demonstrating that fast-mixing parameter sets can replace treewidth assumptions for tractability. Third, the authors candidly discuss limitations, including the practical difficulty of verifying fast-mixing (which replaces one hard problem with another), the need for convex constraint sets, and the conservative nature of theoretical bounds.

The paper's theoretical foundation is strong, building upon Schmidt et al. (2011) for gradient descent with errors and existing mixing time results. However, several important clarifications are missing. The paper never explicitly states that the log-likelihood is convex (a consequence of A(θ) being convex, with Hessian equal to the covariance of sufficient statistics), which is crucial for the convex analysis. Additionally, the requirement that the parameter set Θ must be convex is not stated in the main text, though it's mentioned in the appendix. These omissions could confuse readers about the conditions under which the results hold.

The authors correctly acknowledge that the practical impact of their work is limited because verifying fast-mixing for a given parameter set is generally as hard as the original problem. The theoretical guarantees require knowing the mixing parameters C and α, which are typically unknown in practice. While the reformulation may still be useful for theoretical analysis, it doesn't provide a practical alternative to existing methods for high-treewidth models.

The paper includes a simple but well-executed experiment on an Ising model showing the algorithm converges faster than theoretically predicted, demonstrating the practical viability of the approach. The authors also provide valuable insights into contrastive divergence and its relationship to their framework, noting that their analysis doesn't cover common variants using sample pools between iterations.

Strengths of the paper include:
- Clear theoretical contributions combining existing techniques in a novel way
- Comprehensive treatment of both convex and strongly convex cases
- Honest discussion of limitations and future directions
- Well-motivated alternative to treewidth assumptions
- Good experimental validation

Weaknesses include:
- Missing key assumptions (convexity of Θ, convexity of log-likelihood)
- Overly pessimistic theoretical guarantees (practical performance better than bounds)
- Limited practical impact due to difficulty of verifying fast-mixing
- Minor typographical errors (noted at lines 185, 192, 207, 281, 311-312)

The paper is well-written and technically sound, making a substantial theoretical contribution to the understanding of MLE with MCMC approximation. It advances the field by providing a rigorous framework for when MCMC-based learning can be guaranteed to work, even for high-treewidth models. However, its practical value is limited by the difficulty of verifying the fast-mixing condition, which remains a significant challenge.

In terms of NIPS criteria:
- Quality: High (technically sound, well-supported analysis)
- Clarity: Good (with minor omissions in key assumptions)
- Originality: High (novel combination of mixing time theory and optimization)
- Significance: Moderate (theoretically important but limited practical impact)

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that expands our understanding of when MCMC-based learning can be trusted, but it doesn't provide a practical solution for most real-world applications where verifying fast-mixing is infeasible. The authors are appropriately modest about this limitation.

Overall, this is an important theoretical paper that should be accepted for NIPS for its originality and technical contribution, though its practical significance is limited. The authors' candid discussion of limitations and future work strengthens their contribution. The paper is well-positioned to inspire further research on the relationship between mixing time guarantees and learning guarantees, even if it doesn't immediately solve practical learning problems.
