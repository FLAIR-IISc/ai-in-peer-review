Summary: The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to “fast-mixing” sets and running projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated via MCMC. Under bounded sufficient statistics and uniform geometric mixing across the constraint set, the authors derive high-probability convergence rates and interpret them as FPRAS-type guarantees: O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)) work for the convex (unregularized) case, and O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) work for the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case. The work is positioned as an alternative to treewidth-based tractability, and relates to practical strategies such as MCMC-based gradient estimates.

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic template (projected gradient with sampling) and clean, interpretable rates.
- The focus on parameter sets guaranteeing fast mixing is interesting and relevant to recent NIPS work on learning fast-mixing models.
- The analysis is careful in tracking MCMC bias and variance and leverages standard inexact gradient descent results.

Weaknesses and needed revisions:
- Related work: The paper shows a major lack of awareness of rich prior theory on stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics and Monte Carlo maximum likelihood. Classical results (Benveniste, Métivier and Priouret, 1990; Kushner & Yin, 2003; Meyn & Tweedie, 1993) and modern variants (Andrieu, Moulines, Priouret, 2005; Fort & Moulines; Delyon, Lavielle & Moulines, 1999 SAEM; Geyer, 1991 MCMCML; Gu & Zhu, 2001) already establish convergence and rates for SA/EM with Markov noise under far more general conditions. Please compare explicitly, clarify what is genuinely new (if anything) beyond packaging these tools as an FPRAS, and situate relative to existing approaches for optimizing intractable likelihoods (MCEM/SAEM, MCMCML, contrastive divergence/persistent CD).
- Assumptions: Uniform geometric ergodicity with fixed (C, α) over Θ and reinitializing each iteration from a fixed r are unnecessary and in places incorrect. Standard SA theory only requires drift/minorization (geometric or even subgeometric ergodicity) and allows state-dependent noise. Reinitializing from the previous sample (“persistent” initialization) is typically preferable and extensively analyzed (e.g., Younes; Andrieu–Moulines–Priouret). Markov chains need not be uniformly ergodic; uniformity over Θ is a strong restriction that should be relaxed or justified.
- Theory reuse vs novelty: Results such as Theorem 6 can be derived under weaker conditions by invoking the above SA literature; please either relax assumptions or clearly acknowledge that existing theory covers your setting and more.
- Clarity/presentation: Assumptions for Theorems 1–2 should be stated precisely next to the statements (bounded t, projection properties, exact definition of Θ, uniformity of mixing). The dependence on constants (C, α, R, L) and whether they are computable should be clarified.

Evaluation:
- Quality: Technically competent but relies on strong, unnecessary assumptions; misses key literature.
- Clarity: Presentation can be substantially improved.
- Originality: Marginal; overlap with established SA/EM/MCMCML theory.
- Significance: Limited without clearer novelty and comparisons.

Pros for acceptance:
- Timely topic; neat unification and explicit work bounds; potential to influence practice if assumptions are relaxed.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete related work; overstated novelty; avoidable assumptions; unclear theorem assumptions.

Recommendation: Reject, but could become a solid contribution with thorough literature integration, weaker assumptions, and stronger comparisons.
