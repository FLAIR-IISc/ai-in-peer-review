This paper addresses the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models, where direct gradient computation is often intractable. The authors propose a novel approach: leveraging the theoretical guarantees of fast-mixing Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) to establish a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for learning within a constrained parameter space.  Rather than restricting parameters to tree-structured forms (like Chow-Liu), they focus on sets where MCMC exhibits rapid convergence, offering a broader framework for tractable learning.  The key contribution lies in proving that gradient descent, utilizing MCMC-approximated gradients, will approximate the maximum likelihood solution with high probability when parameters are restricted to such "fast-mixing" sets.  They analyze both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, deriving distinct convergence rates.

The work builds upon existing approaches to intractable learning in graphical models. Traditional methods like pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22], and composite likelihoods [20, 30] are acknowledged as useful but often less sample efficient than maximum likelihood or limited to well-specified models.  More recent approaches like Mizrahi et al. [2014] and Abbeel et al. [2006] are also noted, but suffer from similar drawbacks. The paper distinguishes itself by focusing on the convergence *rate* of MCMC as the core criterion for tractability, shifting the focus from the model structure itself to the computational properties of inference.  It correctly points out the #P-completeness of even partition function evaluation [19] and the implications for obtaining a true FPRAS.  The connection to work on rapidly mixing chains for the Ising model, such as Jerrum and Sinclair [1993] and Hayes [2012], is appropriately referenced, although the authors clarify that their framework applies more generally to exponential families.

The strengths of the paper are substantial.  The theoretical results are rigorous, providing formal guarantees for the convergence of MCMC-based learning under specific conditions.  The analysis of both convex and strongly convex cases offers a nuanced understanding of the algorithm’s behavior, and the derived effort bounds (cubic in 1/ε for unregularized, quadratic for ridge-regularized) are significant.  The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the mathematical framework and key results. The inclusion of a proof sketch and a concrete example (Section 6) help to illustrate the theory and its potential application. The discussion of related work is comprehensive, correctly situating the contribution within the broader landscape of graphical model learning.

However, the paper also has notable weaknesses. The primary concern is the practical applicability of the "fast-mixing" constraint. The conditions required for guaranteeing rapid MCMC convergence – ensuring that the parameter space adheres to a strict mixing rate bound (Equation 3) – are often difficult to verify or enforce in real-world scenarios. As the authors themselves acknowledge, the constraints necessary to guarantee rapid mixing may be overly restrictive.  This raises a critical question: can this mixing condition be *artificially* enforced through regularization techniques without negating the benefits of learning?  The authors wisely caution against over-regularization when combining their approach with ridge regression, but further investigation into this interaction is needed.  Another point is the dependence on Lipschitz constant L and sufficient statistic norm bound Ra, which are potentially difficult to estimate accurately in practice. While the theoretical results are valuable, a more detailed discussion of how these parameters impact practical performance would be beneficial. Furthermore, the analysis assumes independent samples in each MCMC iteration, neglecting the potential benefits of maintaining a sample pool, a common practice in related algorithms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novel theoretical contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding the convergence properties of MCMC-based learning in graphical models.
* **FPRAS guarantee:** Establishing a FPRAS for a broad class of models under reasonable assumptions is a significant accomplishment.
* **Clear and well-written:** The paper is generally easy to follow, with a well-defined mathematical framework.
* **Relevant and impactful problem:** Addressing the intractability of maximum likelihood learning is a core challenge in the field of graphical models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Practical limitations:** The fast-mixing constraint is difficult to satisfy in practice, limiting the immediate applicability of the results.
* **Parameter estimation:** The reliance on accurate estimation of parameters like the Lipschitz constant and sufficient statistic norm could pose challenges.
* **Simplifying assumptions:** The assumption of independent samples may not reflect common practice, potentially affecting the accuracy of the theoretical analysis.



**Overall:** This is a solid theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of MCMC-based learning. However, the practical challenges associated with enforcing the fast-mixing condition raise concerns about its immediate impact. While the current work may not offer a directly deployable algorithm, it lays a valuable foundation for future research aimed at bridging the gap between theory and practice. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically encouraging the authors to explore techniques for enforcing the mixing condition through regularization and to provide a more detailed discussion of parameter estimation in real-world scenarios.
