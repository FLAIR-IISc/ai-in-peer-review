This paper proposes learning exponential-family models by constraining parameters to a set in which a chosen MCMC kernel provably mixes rapidly (“fast-mixing parameter sets”), and then running projected (proximal) gradient descent where the likelihood gradient is approximated by MCMC sampling. The central contribution is a finite-time analysis yielding an FPRAS for maximum-likelihood within the constrained set Θ: cubic effort in 1/ε for the convex (unregularized) case to achieve ε suboptimality in the objective, and quadratic effort in 1/ε for the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case to achieve ε parameter error. The results apply to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not only graphical models. Technically, the paper establishes Lipschitz smoothness of the regularized negative log-likelihood, uses total-variation bias control via mixing-time bounds, and leverages Schmidt et al. (NIPS’11) for inexact projected gradient descent. Schedules for number of iterations, samples per iteration, and chain length ensure high-probability guarantees, and an Ising toy example illustrates the theory.

Relation to prior work: In contrast to the common “tractable by structure” strategy (e.g., Chow–Liu trees; learning high-girth models with correlation decay; variational surrogates), this work is “tractable by rapid mixing,” aligning with Domke & Liu (NIPS’13; projection onto fast-mixing sets) and Steinhardt & Liang (ICML’15; restart-augmented fast mixing), but targets exact MLE within Θ with explicit runtime guarantees. It formalizes the long-standing heuristic of MCMC-based gradient estimation for MLE (Geyer’91; Gu & Zhu’01; Tieleman’08; Salakhutdinov’10) by quantifying both bias (finite-time mixing) and variance (finite samples). It complements pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihood (Besag’75; Hyvärinen’05; Varin et al.’11), which can be statistically or computationally attractive but optimize different criteria and may not recover the M-projection under misspecification.

Strengths
- Clear and principled framing: restricting to fast-mixing Θ makes MCMC-based MLE algorithmically analyzable and yields an FPRAS under standard bounded-statistics assumptions.
- Generality: analysis in the exponential-family setting, not limited to specific graphical structures or samplers.
- Solid technical development: Lipschitz gradient bound; careful bias/variance control using TV distance and concentration; provable schedules for K, M, v in both convex and strongly convex cases; near-matching lower bounds (up to log factors).
- Honest discussion of limitations and connections to CD/persistent-CD practice; useful guidance on projection operators for Ising/MRFs.

Weaknesses
- Practical impact is limited by the difficulty of certifying fast-mixing and computing explicit (C, α) for realistic models/samplers; in many regimes, verifying fast mixing may be as hard as inference.
- The utility of the FPRAS is conditioned on an efficient projection onto Θ, which can be nontrivial beyond special norms (e.g., spectral-norm constraints).
- Empirical section is minimal; no comparison to pseudolikelihood/score matching/CD or to persistent sample pools commonly used in practice.
- Some assumptions and prerequisites are scattered; clearer upfront statement would improve readability (e.g., convexity of Θ; bounded sufficient statistics; access to a mixing-time bound and projection oracle; independence across iterations).
- Minor clarity/typo issues (e.g., lines l185, l192, l207, l281, l311–312).

Quality
- Technically sound with appropriate use of concentration inequalities and inexact gradient descent. Proof sketches are reasonable; full proofs (appendix) should be checked. Assumptions are standard for the exponential family, but the availability of (C, α) and a projection oracle is a strong precondition that constrains applicability.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Suggested improvements:
  - Early, explicit summary of all assumptions: bounded sufficient stats; convex Θ; existence of fast-mixing bounds for the chosen kernel uniformly over Θ; ability to project onto Θ; independence of MCMC runs across iterations; step-size choice and use of L.
  - Early statement that A(θ) is convex, with Hessian equal to Covpθ[t(X)], hence f is convex (and λ-strongly convex when λ > 0).
  - Brief guidance on how (C, α) are obtained in practice for common kernels (Gibbs/Swendsen–Wang) and models, including dependence on degree, norms, and graph size.

Originality
- The “optimize within fast-mixing set Θ + FPRAS for MLE” package is novel. While the components (mixing-time bounds, projected gradient with inexact gradients) are known, their synthesis to deliver end-to-end complexity guarantees for MLE in exponential families is original and useful.

Significance
- Conceptually important: reframes tractability from structural sparsity to dynamical properties of the sampler. Theoretical guarantees help justify widely used MCMC-approximate MLE procedures. However, the reliance on certifiable fast mixing and efficient projection may limit immediate practical uptake.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide additional examples where Θ and projection are efficient beyond Ising with spectral/induced norms (e.g., Potts, higher-order factors)?
- Discuss how sensitive the bounds are to misspecification of (C, α), and whether adaptive schemes could estimate effective mixing rates on the fly with guarantees.
- Compare experimentally with pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, and CD/persistent-CD in small/medium problems, reporting wall-clock vs accuracy and whether the “fast-mixing” constraint aids downstream inference.
- Clarify the step-size choice (1/L) and whether backtracking or adaptive steps could tighten constants in practice.
- Explore whether the “sample pool” strategy can be analyzed by coupling or pathwise stability to relax the independence assumption across iterations.

Minor issues
- Please correct minor typos/wording (l185, l192, l207, l281, l311–312).
- Define all symbols at first use in the main text; ensure Algorithm 1 is self-contained.
- Make explicit that Θ must be convex for the projection and mixing assumptions used.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, theoretically solid contribution with novel perspective and FPRAS guarantees.
- Broad applicability to exponential families; connects and clarifies practice around MCMC-based MLE.
- High relevance to NeurIPS theory/inference communities.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical verification of fast mixing and projection may be challenging; limited empirical validation.
- Contributions largely theoretical synthesis rather than new algorithms with demonstrated impact.

Recommendation: Weak Accept.
