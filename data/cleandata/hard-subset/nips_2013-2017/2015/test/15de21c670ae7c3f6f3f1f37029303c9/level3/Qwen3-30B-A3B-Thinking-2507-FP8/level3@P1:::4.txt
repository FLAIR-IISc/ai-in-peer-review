

This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by focusing on "fast-mixing parameter sets" where MCMC inference can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution. The authors establish that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, gradient descent with gradients approximated by MCMC sampling will approximate the maximum likelihood solution inside a fast-mixing set with high probability. The key theoretical contribution is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning under this constraint, with convergence rates that are cubic in 1/ε for unregularized learning and quadratic for ridge-regularized cases.

The work builds on previous NIPS contributions such as Steinhardt and Liang (ICML 2014) who proposed learning using fast-mixing chains, and extends the framework to provide rigorous convergence guarantees for the standard MCMC-based learning approach widely used in practice. While previous methods for tractable learning typically focused on tree-structured parameters (Chow-Liu, Heinemann and Globerson 2014), this paper introduces a fundamentally different notion of tractability based on mixing times rather than treewidth.

The paper excels in quality and technical rigor. The main results (Theorems 2-6) provide precise convergence guarantees with explicit bounds on the number of Markov chain transitions, gradient descent iterations, and samples required. The Lipschitz gradient result (Theorem 1) is particularly important for establishing the theoretical foundation. The analysis carefully distinguishes between convex and strongly convex cases, providing different convergence rates for each scenario. The example in Section 6 demonstrates practical applicability and shows the theoretical bounds are reasonably conservative.

The clarity of presentation is excellent. The paper clearly defines all necessary notation and establishes the background on mixing times in a way that makes the technical contributions accessible. The proof sketches provide sufficient intuition for the main results without overwhelming the reader with excessive detail.

The originality of the work is strong. The paper introduces a novel class of tractable models based on fast-mixing properties rather than structural constraints like tree-structured parameters. The theoretical results establish convergence rates for MCMC-based learning that were previously lacking, filling a significant gap in the literature. This represents a meaningful advance beyond previous work that either assumed tractable inference (like tree-structured models) or provided no theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based learning.

The significance of the work, however, is somewhat unclear in the current form. While the paper convincingly demonstrates the theoretical soundness of the approach, it lacks sufficient discussion of potential applications and impact. The authors should elaborate on how this framework might enable learning in previously intractable models (beyond the Ising model example), potential computational savings compared to other approaches, and concrete problem domains where fast-mixing parameter sets might naturally arise.

A specific weakness is the treatment of the lower bound (Theorem 7). The bound is mathematically correct but difficult to interpret, as it involves multiple parameters with unclear relationships. The authors should expand the discussion to better explain the practical implications of this bound and how it relates to the upper bounds established in Theorems 4 and 8. A comparison to known lower bounds for other learning approaches would strengthen this section.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of potential future extensions. For instance, the authors could explore how their framework might integrate with modern MCMC techniques, or how it might be applied to more complex models like those with latent variables. The limitations section (Section 7) touches on some of these but could be expanded to provide a more comprehensive roadmap for future work.

The main strength of this paper is its theoretical contribution: providing rigorous convergence guarantees for a widely used practical approach to learning intractable models. The results are technically sound, novel, and address a gap in the literature. While the significance section could be strengthened with more concrete discussion of impact, the core contribution is substantial and should be of interest to the NIPS community.

The paper's main weakness is the somewhat limited discussion of significance and practical impact, but this is a minor issue given the strength of the theoretical contribution. The lower bound treatment could also be improved with clearer interpretation.

In summary, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for MCMC-based learning in exponential families under fast-mixing parameter sets. The results are original, technically sound, and provide meaningful convergence rates. While the significance discussion could be expanded, the theoretical contribution is substantial and represents a valuable advance in understanding the theoretical properties of widely used learning algorithms. The paper should be accepted.
