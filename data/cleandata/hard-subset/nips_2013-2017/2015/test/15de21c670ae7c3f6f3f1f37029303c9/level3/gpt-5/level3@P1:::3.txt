This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families when exact inference is intractable, and gives conditions under which the common “MCMC-in-the-loop” gradient method admits formal runtime guarantees. Rather than restricting to structurally tractable models (e.g., trees), the authors define a tractable parameter set Θ consisting of “fast-mixing” parameters for a chosen MCMC operator Mθ, i.e., parameters for which the chain contracts in total-variation at a geometric rate: ∥Mθ
v q − pθ∥TV ≤ C α^v for all q, with α < 1. Under bounded sufficient statistics, a projection onto Θ, and known mixing constants, they prove that projected gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates yields a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood within Θ. The analysis is given for general exponential families (not only graphical models).

Key technical ingredients: (i) the negative log-likelihood (optionally with L2 regularization) has L-Lipschitz gradient with L = 4 R2^2 + λ; (ii) a convex-case guarantee bounding suboptimality of the averaged iterate in terms of K gradient steps, M samples per step, and chain length v, controlling both sampling noise and MCMC bias; (iii) a strongly-convex (ridge-regularized) case that yields parameter error guarantees, leveraging uniform concentration per-iteration and the Schmidt–Roux–Bach bounds for inexact (proximal) gradient methods; and (iv) near-matching lower bounds and explicit schedules for K, M, v yielding total work KMv scaling as O(ε_f^{-3} polylog(1/ε_f)) for convex (accuracy in function value) and O(ε_θ^{-2} polylog(1/ε_θ)) for strongly convex (accuracy in parameter distance). The paper also surveys concrete Θ and projector constructions for Ising/MRFs via coupling-strength bounds and matrix norms (e.g., spectral norm constraints on R(θ)), relating to classical mixing-time results (Glauber dynamics, Swendsen–Wang, Jerrum–Sinclair).

Relation to prior work: The paper contrasts its approach with surrogates like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihood (Besag; Hyvärinen; Lindsay/Varin), and structural restrictions (Chow–Liu trees; high-girth models). It connects to Domke & Liu’s projection schemes for fast-mixing Ising/MRFs, and Steinhardt & Liang’s restart-based fast-mixing chains. It situates the mixing assumptions within the classic literature (Levin–Peres–Wilmer; Hayes; Jerrum–Sinclair) and uses inexact gradient analysis from Schmidt et al. The novelty is a general FPRAS-type global guarantee for MCMC-based ML optimization when parameters are constrained to a provably fast-mixing set.

Quality: The technical development is careful, with plausible proofs and standard concentration tools (Efron–Stein, Bernstein, Hoeffding) and clear use of inexact gradient convergence results. The convex vs strongly convex separation is appropriate: the former controls function error, the latter parameter error via strong convexity. Assumptions are explicit. However, several practical quantities are taken as known: a projector onto Θ, and concrete constants (C, α, R2, L). The guarantees depend critically on geometric mixing for the chosen kernel. Independence of samples across iterations is assumed; common heuristics like persistent chains or sample pools are not covered. The example is illustrative but limited.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The statement of assumptions (bounded sufficient statistics, mixing definition, existence/complexity of projection) is clear. Minor typos aside, the presentation of K/M/v schedules and lower bounds is helpful. Suggestions: (1) consolidate all assumptions (including knowledge of C and α) at the beginning of Section 2/3; (2) more explicit guidance on Θ choices and projection algorithms beyond Ising; (3) discuss how to proceed when C, α are unknown (diagnostics, adaptive schedules); (4) clarify the impact of misspecification beyond “M-projection within Θ.”

Originality: Establishing an FPRAS for ML via MCMC under fast-mixing constraints, with explicit complexity in K/M/v and near-matching lower bounds, appears novel and goes beyond prior consistency or asymptotic results for surrogates and earlier fast-mixing projections. The generality to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics is a strength.

Significance: Theoretical significance is solid: it formalizes when a pervasive heuristic (MCMC-based gradient descent) can be made polynomial-time. Practical impact is currently limited by the restrictiveness of Θ and the need to know or conservatively bound mixing rates. Projection can be nontrivial in general families, and known mixing bounds can be loose, potentially leading to pessimistic schedules.

Pros:
- Clear, general framework showing an FPRAS for ML within fast-mixing parameter sets.
- Separation of convex vs strongly convex regimes with explicit complexity and near-matching lower bounds.
- Broadly applicable to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Concrete connections to mixing-time literature and known Θ/projection constructions.

Cons:
- Practical applicability limited: fast-mixing Θ may exclude regimes of interest; mixing constants and projection operators may be unknown or expensive.
- Assumes independent fresh samples per iteration; excludes widely used persistent/sample-pool strategies.
- Empirical section minimal; no demonstration on realistic datasets or comparison to strong baselines.
- Dependence on conservative mixing bounds may lead to overly large K/M/v.

Questions and suggestions:
- Can the mixing condition be enforced via an explicit regularizer (e.g., spectral-norm penalty on R(θ)), with guarantees for the penalized objective? How to trade off with ridge; risk of over-regularization?
- Can one learn/adjust Θ adaptively (e.g., homotopy on constraint radius) while retaining guarantees?
- What if C and α are unknown? Can adaptive stopping, coupling diagnostics, or sequential tests yield high-probability guarantees with data-driven v?
- Can the analysis be extended to persistent chains/sample reuse, perhaps via martingale or mixing-within-iteration arguments?
- Beyond Gibbs, do analogous results hold for alternative kernels (Swendsen–Wang, block Gibbs) under their mixing analyses?
- Any path to latent-variable models or continuous-state families where bounded-statistics and projection are more delicate?

Overall assessment: A solid and well-executed theoretical contribution that clarifies when MCMC-based ML is provably efficient under fast-mixing constraints. While the practicality is constrained by strong assumptions about Θ and mixing information, the work is conceptually valuable and likely to spur follow-up on regularization schemes that promote mixing and on adaptive, diagnosable schedules.

Recommendation: Borderline accept for its theoretical value and clarity; stronger if accompanied by empirical demonstrations of mixing-regularized learning on modern graphical models.
