- Proposes learning exponential-family models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ and analyzes projected gradient descent with short MCMC chains to yield FPRAS-style guarantees.
- Provides explicit schedules for iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain length v, with total sampling effort scaling O(ε_f^-3 polylog) (convex) and O(ε_θ^-2 polylog) (strongly convex).
- Proves a Lipschitz-gradient bound (Theorem 1) that underpins the convergence rates.
- Framework is general for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics and discusses projection operators for Ising and pairwise MRFs.
- Connects to prior work on biased estimators, MCMC-based MLE without finite-time guarantees, mixing-via-restarts, and projections into fast-mixing sets; main novelty is end-to-end convergence-rate analysis.
- Strength: clear theoretical contribution with explicit, interpretable convergence rates that scale with mixing-time parameters (C, α).
- Strength: original tractability framing via MCMC-approximability (fast mixing) rather than low treewidth or variational surrogates.
- Strength: general results that subsume many graphical models.
- Strength: well-organized presentation with precise statements and helpful proof sketches.
- Major weakness: Theorem 7 (lower bound) is hard to interpret; dependence on γ, α, δ and multiple logs obscures tightness versus upper bounds.
- Suggestion: simplify lower-bound scaling, relate γ to λ/L, clarify whether lower bound is algorithmic or information-theoretic, and give concrete instantiations.
- Practical-significance concern: constants (C, α, R2, L) and exact projections onto Θ may be hard to obtain or estimate in large models.
- Suggestion: provide guidance for estimating/upper-bounding C and α, adapting v online, and discuss robustness to inexact projection.
- Scope limitation: analysis assumes independent samples per iteration and does not cover persistent chains or CD with very short chains common in practice.
- Assumption concern: membership/projection onto Θ can be computationally nontrivial (e.g., spectral-norm projections via SVD); need to quantify projection cost relative to sampling.
- Quality: technically sound with careful concentration-inequality use and convincing proof sketches; lower bound exposition needs improvement.
- Clarity: paper is very clearly written, well-structured, and consistent in notation; schedules are easy to follow.
- Originality: high—novel class definition and fresh theoretical guarantees for MCMC-approximate MLE.
- Significance: likely to influence theory and practice for learning in high-treewidth models, but practical uptake depends on estimating/controlling mixing and projections.
- Suggestions for extensions: adaptive schedules from online diagnostics, other samplers (Swendsen-Wang, SGLD), handling hidden variables/persistent chains, variance reduction, and practical projection recipes.
- Pros for acceptance: novel rigorous convergence-rate analysis, compelling tractable model class, clear presentation, fills a theoretical gap.
- Cons for acceptance: lower bound hard to interpret, practical significance and projection costs unclear, limited empirical validation.
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on expanded discussion of lower bounds and clearer articulation of practical impact and future extensions.
