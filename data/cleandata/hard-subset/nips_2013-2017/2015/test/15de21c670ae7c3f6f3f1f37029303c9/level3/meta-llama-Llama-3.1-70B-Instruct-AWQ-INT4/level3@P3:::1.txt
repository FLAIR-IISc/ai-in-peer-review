This paper explores the concept of "fast-mixing parameters" in the context of maximum likelihood learning for exponential families. The authors propose a method that restricts parameters to a set where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution. The main contribution of this work is a theoretical guarantee that, under certain conditions, gradient descent with gradients approximated by sampling will approximate the maximum likelihood solution inside the set of fast-mixing parameters with high probability.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they are trying to solve. However, the presentation is poor in some areas, with unclear assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2. Additionally, the authors make unnecessary assumptions, such as requiring Markov chains to be uniformly ergodic and reinitializing at a fixed distribution r. These assumptions are not only unnecessary but also incorrect, as reinitializing with the previous sample may be preferable and has been extensively analyzed previously.

Furthermore, the authors fail to acknowledge prior literature on related algorithms and theory. The proposed algorithm is closely related to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics. The authors should compare their method to existing work on optimizing intractable likelihoods and provide a more thorough discussion of the related literature.

The results presented in the paper are not particularly novel, and existing theory already covers the authors' results and more general scenarios. The authors rely on strong assumptions to derive their results, such as Theorem 6, which can be improved by citing more general results from Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret.

Despite these criticisms, the paper does provide some useful insights into the problem of maximum likelihood learning for exponential families. The authors' use of a convex set of parameters to guarantee fast mixing is an interesting approach, and their analysis of the convergence of gradient descent with errors in estimated gradients is thorough.

However, the paper's overall novelty is marginal, and the authors' failure to acknowledge prior literature and provide a clear presentation of their assumptions and results detracts from the paper's impact.
