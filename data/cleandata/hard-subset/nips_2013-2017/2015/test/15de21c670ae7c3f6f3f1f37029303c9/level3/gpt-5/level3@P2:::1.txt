Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families when exact inference is intractable. The central idea is to constrain parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ under which an MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast, and then run projected gradient descent where each gradient is approximated by MCMC samples. The authors prove non-asymptotic convergence guarantees: for convex (unregularized) objectives they obtain an ε-accurate solution in objective value with total sampling effort scaling as ~1/ε^3 (up to logs), and for strongly convex (ridge-regularized) objectives they obtain ε-accuracy in parameter distance with effort ~1/ε^2 (up to logs). They present Lipschitz gradient bounds based on bounded sufficient statistics, and use standard results on inexact projected gradient methods to translate control of bias/variance from finite-time mixing into optimization error. They position this as a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for MLE restricted to Θ, provided projection onto Θ is tractable.

Related work on optimizing intractable likelihoods is extensive. Longstanding Monte Carlo maximum likelihood (MCMLE; Geyer, 1991; Gu and Zhu, 2001) and Monte Carlo EM/SAEM (Wei and Tanner, 1990; Delyon, Lavielle and Moulines, 1999) treat MLE with Monte Carlo approximations, using Markov chains whose kernels depend on the current parameter. Convergence of stochastic approximation with Markovian (iterate-dependent) noise is well developed (Benveniste, Métivier and Priouret, 1990; Kushner and Yin, 2003; Andrieu, Moulines and Priouret, 2005; Atchadé, Fort and Moulines, 2011; Younes, 1999, for MRFs). In machine learning practice, persistent chains (e.g., persistent contrastive divergence; Tieleman, 2008, and earlier variants) carry samples across iterations rather than restarting, and this strategy has been analyzed in the SA literature under controlled Markov chains. Compared with these, the present paper imposes a uniform fast-mixing constraint and resets chains from a fixed distribution, which simplifies obtaining explicit finite-time rates but is stronger than necessary and departs from common practice.

Strengths:
- Clear optimization framework that connects finite-time MCMC bias/variance control to inexact projected gradient descent.
- Non-asymptotic complexity bounds and an FPRAS statement for the restricted problem; explicit schedules for iterations, chain lengths, and sample sizes.
- General exponential-family setting with bounded sufficient statistics, not limited to specific graphical model structures.
- Useful synthesis of mixing-time bounds with optimization error propagation.

Weaknesses:
- Limited awareness of and comparison to extensive prior literature on MLE with Monte Carlo approximations and SA with Markovian noise. Many general results (e.g., Benveniste–Métivier–Priouret, Kushner–Yin, Andrieu–Moulines–Priouret) provide convergence under weaker, more realistic conditions (iterate-dependent kernels, no need for uniform ergodicity on a constrained set, and no forced restarts).
- The imposed assumptions are unnecessarily strong or misaligned with standard practice: (i) uniform geometric ergodicity across Θ with known (C, α), (ii) reinitialization from a fixed distribution at every iteration, and (iii) independence across iterations. Reusing previous samples (persistent chains) is often preferable and has been extensively analyzed within controlled Markov SA frameworks.
- The novelty is marginal relative to established SA/MCMLE/MCEM/SAEM literature; the main contribution is packaging assumptions to yield explicit finite-time bounds/FPRAS, but the price is strong constraints whose practicality is unclear.
- Presentation issues: assumptions for key results (e.g., Theorems 1–2) are not stated crisply next to the theorems; the algorithmic details (step sizes, projection properties, verification of mixing constants) should be made explicit where used.
- Empirical section is minimal; no comparison with MCMLE/MCEM/SAEM or persistent-chain methods that target the same problem.

Quality:
- The proofs appear to follow standard inexact-gradient descent analyses once TV mixing and bounded statistics are assumed. However, the need for uniform (C, α) over Θ and per-iteration restarts is a substantial limitation, and the paper does not justify how these constants are obtained or verifiable in practice beyond special cases.
- The Lipschitz gradient bound is correct under bounded statistics but is quite loose; the paper may overstate practical implications of L.
- The FPRAS characterization is precise for the restricted setting, but the broader claim to “learning with arbitrary treewidth” is conditional on projections that may be costly or restrictive.

Clarity:
- Organization can be improved. State all assumptions immediately with each theorem (bounded sufficient statistics, convexity and diameter of Θ, uniform mixing over Θ, projection properties, step-size selection). Clearly distinguish assumptions that hold uniformly in θ versus at the current iterate.
- Clarify sampling protocol: number of chains, reuse versus restart, independence assumptions, and how bias and variance decompositions are derived.
- Discuss computational burden of projection in more detail, including complexity for the spectral norm constraint.

Originality:
- The conceptual pipeline (fast-mixing set + projected GD + MCMC gradient oracle) is not new in spirit; SA with controlled Markov chains already covers iterate-dependent kernels and persistent chains. The main novelty is a finite-time FPRAS under strong uniform mixing assumptions. Given prior theory that covers more general dynamics (and often without projection/restarts), the incremental novelty is limited.

Significance:
- The results are of theoretical interest but may have limited practical impact due to strong and hard-to-verify assumptions. Without empirical comparisons to established Monte Carlo MLE/EM and persistent-chain methods, it is hard to assess benefits.
- If reframed as finite-time complexity for a restricted, uniformly fast-mixing class with explicit projection, the contribution is clearer but narrower.

Constructive suggestions:
- Situate the work within the SA/MCMLE/MCEM/SAEM literature. Cite and contrast with: Benveniste, Métivier and Priouret (1990); Kushner and Yin (2003); Andrieu, Moulines and Priouret (2005); Atchadé, Fort and Moulines (2011); Younes (1999); Wei and Tanner (1990); Delyon, Lavielle and Moulines (1999). Also discuss persistent chains (Tieleman, 2008) and analyze reusing samples across iterations.
- Relax assumptions where possible. Uniform ergodicity over Θ and fixed restarts are not required by SA theory; drift/minorization conditions and iterate-dependent kernels can yield convergence (and sometimes rates) with diminishing step sizes. Discuss how your bounds might extend to controlled Markov chains without restarts.
- Make assumptions explicit next to each theorem and detail verifiability of (C, α). Provide conditions under which these constants can be bounded for common models and kernels.
- Compare empirically against MCMLE/MCEM/SAEM and persistent-chain approaches on synthetic and moderate-size problems to evaluate efficiency/accuracy trade-offs and the cost of projection.
- Clarify algorithmic choices: step sizes (fixed vs diminishing), projection complexity, and how to set M, v, and K in practice without access to true (C, α).
- Temper claims: emphasize that the FPRAS is for the constrained problem and discuss when such constraints are desirable.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides explicit, finite-time complexity bounds for MLE within uniformly fast-mixing parameter sets, connecting mixing-time control to optimization error.
- General exponential-family formulation; clean analysis and schedules.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Overlooks substantial prior work that already covers more general scenarios (iterate-dependent kernels, persistent chains), with unnecessary/incorrect assumptions (uniform ergodicity, fixed restarts).
- Marginal novelty; main ideas are known in SA/MCMLE literature; contribution is primarily packaging assumptions to obtain FPRAS.
- Weak empirical support and limited practical relevance given strong assumptions.
- Presentation needs improvement to state assumptions precisely and to position contributions within existing theory.
