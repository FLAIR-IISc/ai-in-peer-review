Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting to low-treewidth models (e.g., trees via Chow–Liu), the authors propose constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ, i.e., a subset for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically (in total variation) with known constants C and α. Within this set, they analyze projected gradient descent that uses MCMC samples to approximate gradients. They show that, for bounded sufficient statistics, this procedure converges with high probability to the MLE constrained to Θ, yielding fully-polynomial randomized approximation schemes (FPRASes): cubic effort in 1/ε for the unregularized (convex) case and quadratic in 1/ε for ridge-regularized (strongly convex) objectives, up to log factors. They also give lower bounds and explicit schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and chain steps.

This framing complements earlier tractable learning via structure or surrogate losses (pseudolikelihood [Besag], score matching [Hyvärinen], composite likelihood [Lindsay; Varin et al.], high-girth/correlation decay [Heinemann & Globerson], and tree learning [Chow–Liu]), as well as MCMC-based MLE strategies [Geyer; Descombes et al.; Salakhutdinov] and contrastive divergence [Hinton; Tieleman], which typically lack end-to-end guarantees. It builds on fast-mixing parameter projections for Ising/MRFs [Domke & Liu, NIPS 2013; Liu & Domke, NIPS 2014] and is related to learning fast-mixing models via restart chains [Steinhardt & Liang, ICML 2015]. The technical novelty is to translate mixing-time control into provable convergence rates for biased MCMC gradient estimates in general exponential families.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The assumptions (bounded statistics, known geometric mixing on Θ, and ability to project onto Θ) are clearly stated and leveraged. The Lipschitz gradient bound is standard under bounded statistics. The convergence analyses carefully use concentration inequalities and inexact gradient descent results [Schmidt et al.]. The schedules and complexity claims are consistent with the upper bounds.
- Proof coverage: Proof sketches are plausible; complete proofs are deferred. The dependence on C, α, R, L, λ, and δ is transparent in the statements.
- Limitations: The lower bound in Theorem 7, while valuable, is difficult to interpret; a more extensive discussion, intuition, and comparison to the upper bounds would improve readability. Practical acquisition of tight C, α, and the cost of projection (especially with spectral-norm constraints) can dominate in some regimes; the discussion acknowledges this but could be expanded. The algorithm assumes fresh independent MCMC runs per iteration; common “persistent/pool” sampling practices fall outside the analysis. Empirically, only a small Ising example is shown; while the paper claims no practical contribution, one or two sanity checks that the theory tracks practice (e.g., scaling with v, M) would strengthen confidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well organized, and careful about assumptions. Minor suggestions:
- Make Theorem 7 interpretable: extract leading-order dependence, state regimes where it is tight, and compare side-by-side with Theorem 8 (quantifying gaps beyond log factors).
- Clarify notation consistency for bounds on sufficient statistics (Ra vs R2) and ensure constants are defined once and reused.
- Provide a short table or paragraph summarizing known projection operators for Θ across models (Ising with box constraints, spectral-norm constraints, general pairwise MRFs) and their computational costs.

Originality
Framing tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets and proving FPRAS-type guarantees for MLE with biased MCMC gradients in general exponential families is novel. Prior work studied projecting to fast-mixing sets or designing inherently fast-mixing chains, but not end-to-end sample/iteration/chain-complexity for MLE with explicit rates in both convex and strongly convex regimes. The lower bounds and explicit schedules are also new to my knowledge.

Significance
The work fills an important theoretical gap: it provides principled guarantees for a widely used but heuristically justified practice (MCMC-based gradient estimation for likelihood learning). It could influence how practitioners select constraints or regularizers that ensure mixing, and it offers a clean alternative to treewidth-based tractability. While the immediate empirical impact is limited, the conceptual contribution and rates are likely to be used and extended (e.g., to other chains, adaptive projections, persistent sampling).

Pros
- Clear, principled framework connecting mixing-time control to optimization guarantees.
- General applicability to exponential families with bounded statistics.
- Meaningful rates with explicit schedules; FPRAS claims are well supported.
- Original and timely relative to NIPS literature on learning in high-treewidth models and mixing-time methods.
- Thoughtful discussion of limitations and connections to CD.

Cons
- The lower bound (Thm. 7) is hard to parse; needs intuition, simplification, and broader treatment.
- Practical guidance on estimating or bounding C and α in real models is limited.
- Projection costs are largely abstracted away; a more systematic analysis would help.
- Empirical section is minimal; a few additional diagnostics would improve the paper.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Could you provide practical recipes or references for estimating usable C and α (even conservative) for common models beyond Ising, and discuss the effect of misspecification of these constants on schedules?
- Can the analysis be extended to “persistent” sampling across iterations or to restart chains à la Steinhardt & Liang, potentially tightening v?
- For Theorem 7, please include a simplified corollary highlighting dominant terms, compare to Theorem 8 beyond log factors, and discuss tightness.
- Add a brief complexity discussion for common projections (box constraints vs spectral-norm constraints) and when projection may dominate sampling.
- Consider a small empirical ablation varying v, M, and K to illustrate the predicted trade-offs.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper offers an original and technically solid contribution that advances our theoretical understanding of likelihood learning under MCMC approximation, with clear connections to ongoing NIPS research on mixing, inference, and learning in graphical models. Strengthening the discussion around the lower bounds and practical constants would further improve the impact.
