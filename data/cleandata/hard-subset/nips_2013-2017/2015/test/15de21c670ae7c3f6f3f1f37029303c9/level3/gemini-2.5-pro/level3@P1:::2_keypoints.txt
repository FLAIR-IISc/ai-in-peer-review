- Addresses MLE in high-treewidth undirected graphical models where exact inference is intractable.
- Proposes constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing" convex set Θ so MCMC sampling converges rapidly.
- Develops projected gradient descent using MCMC-based gradient approximations for MLE within Θ.
- Proves this algorithm is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained problem.
- Analysis applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Provides separate guarantees for the unregularized (log-likelihood convergence) and ridge-regularized (parameter L2 error) cases.
- Derives total computational effort (MCMC transitions) needed to reach a desired accuracy with high probability.
- Strength: original conceptual shift — tractability via fast MCMC mixing rather than graphical structure.
- Strength: first formal convergence guarantees for gradient methods with MCMC gradient estimates; technically sound combination of optimization and MCMC theory.
- Strength: authors transparently discuss limitations (projection cost, initialization, relation to Contrastive Divergence).
- Weakness: assumes a known convex fast-mixing set Θ and an efficient projection operator, which is often hard or infeasible to obtain.
- Weakness: effectively replaces intractable inference with the equally hard problem of characterizing/projecting onto Θ; limited practical applicability beyond examples like the Ising model.
- Clarity issue: convexity of negative log-likelihood / log-partition function not explicitly stated.
- Clarity issue: convexity of Θ (needed for projection) is only implicit and should be stated upfront.
- Clarity issue: a concise summary of all assumptions (bounded stats, known geometric mixing rate, efficient projection) is missing and would aid readability.
- Minor typos and phrasing fixes noted (several line-specific corrections suggested).
- Overall summary: substantial, original theoretical contribution with limited immediate practical impact but likely to inspire future work.
- Acceptance arguments: originality, significance as first FPRAS guarantee for this class, rigorous and sound analysis.
- Rejection arguments: strong/unrealistic assumptions about Θ and omitted explicit statements of key assumptions reducing clarity.
