## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a framework for maximum likelihood learning in graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, guaranteeing rapid convergence of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors prove fully-polynomial time randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within this set, offering convergence guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients.

The core idea of focusing on “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a notion of tractability is interesting, attempting to move beyond the standard tree-structured parameter constraint. However, the paper significantly lacks awareness of prior work in related areas. It frames its approach as novel without adequately acknowledging its close relationship to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics – well-established techniques in statistical learning. The authors should directly compare their method to existing work on optimizing intractable likelihoods, such as score matching, pseudolikelihood, and composite likelihoods, discussing the trade-offs. 

A major weakness is the insufficient contextualization within existing theory. The results presented are largely covered by existing work, especially concerning the convergence of stochastic gradient descent with noisy estimates. For example, Theorem 6 makes strong assumptions that are unnecessarily restrictive; the authors should cite Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret (1990) for far more general results on stochastic approximation. Furthermore, the suggestion of reinitializing at a fixed distribution is unnecessary; reinitializing with the previous sample has been extensively analyzed and often performs better. 

The presentation also suffers from clarity issues. Assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 are not explicitly stated near the theorems themselves, hindering readability. Moreover, the paper's claims of novelty appear marginal given the existing literature. While the combination of ideas might be fresh, the underlying techniques and theoretical results are well-trodden ground. The practical example, while helpful, doesn’t strongly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The idea of leveraging fast-mixing parameters for tractable learning is conceptually appealing.
*   The paper provides theoretical guarantees (though not necessarily novel) for the proposed approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Major lack of awareness of relevant prior work.
*   Results largely covered by existing theory.
*   Unnecessary/incorrect assumptions and weak novelty.
*   Poor presentation and clarity.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection**. The paper presents a reasonable, but not particularly innovative, approach with significant shortcomings in its engagement with the existing literature and clarity of presentation. It needs substantial revision to address these issues and convincingly demonstrate its contribution to the field.
