### Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in exponential families where inference is intractable, a common problem in high-treewidth graphical models. Instead of restricting the model structure (e.g., to low treewidth), the authors propose a novel approach: constraining the model parameters to a "fast-mixing set" Θ, where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to converge quickly. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of a common practical heuristic—projected gradient descent using MCMC-approximated gradients. By combining analysis for proximal gradient methods with errors (Schmidt et al., 2011) with MCMC mixing time bounds, the authors prove that this procedure constitutes a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the MLE within the set Θ. They provide distinct convergence guarantees for the convex (unregularized) case, bounding the error in the log-likelihood, and for the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case, bounding the L2 error in the parameters. The theory is supported by a simple experiment on an Ising model.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets, rather than structural properties like treewidth, is a novel and important conceptual contribution. It reframes the problem of approximate MLE in a way that directly connects the learning objective with the efficiency of a widely used inference technique (MCMC). This provides a theoretically grounded alternative to methods that rely on structural assumptions (e.g., Chow-Liu) or make different trade-offs (e.g., pseudolikelihood).
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a solid theoretical analysis that formally connects MCMC mixing rates to the convergence of a gradient-based optimization algorithm. While building on existing results from optimization and MCMC theory, the combination is non-trivial and provides rigorous guarantees for a popular but often poorly understood heuristic. The derivation of an FPRAS is a strong theoretical result.
*   **Honesty and Self-Assessment:** The authors are commendably candid about the limitations of their work. They acknowledge that the theoretical bounds can be conservative in practice (as shown in their experiment) and discuss how their analysis relates to, but does not perfectly capture, other practical algorithms like contrastive divergence or methods that use a "pool" of samples. This thoughtful discussion of weaknesses and future directions adds to the paper's quality.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem setup, algorithm, and main theoretical results are presented clearly. The informal proof sketches in the main text are helpful for building intuition before the detailed proofs in the appendix.

### Weaknesses

*   **Practical Impact:** The primary limitation of this work is its practical applicability. The core assumption is the existence of a known fast-mixing parameter set Θ, complete with constants C and α governing the mixing rate, and an efficient projection operator onto Θ. For most interesting models, proving fast mixing and determining these constants is an extremely difficult problem in itself. In many ways, the framework replaces one hard problem (intractable inference for MLE) with another (proving fast mixing). While the reformulation is theoretically interesting, it may not immediately lead to new practical learning algorithms for complex models where mixing times are unknown.
*   **Clarity Omissions and Missing Assumptions:** The clarity of the paper could be improved in a few key areas:
    1.  The paper assumes but never explicitly states that the negative log-likelihood function `f(θ)` is convex. This is a crucial property for the entire analysis. A brief mention that the Hessian of `A(θ)` is the covariance matrix of the sufficient statistics, and thus positive semi-definite, would establish this fundamental point for the reader.
    2.  A critical assumption for the proposed projected gradient algorithm is that the constraint set Θ must be a **convex set**. This is necessary for the projection step `ΠΘ` to be well-defined and computationally tractable. This assumption is not stated in the main text but is essential for the validity of the algorithm and analysis.
    3.  For the reader's convenience, it would be highly beneficial to have a single, consolidated list of all assumptions made throughout the paper (e.g., bounded sufficient statistics, geometric mixing rate, convexity of Θ, etc.).
*   **Minor Issues:** I noted a few minor typos and awkward phrases that could be corrected:
    *   l185: "for more reference" -> "for reference"
    *   l192: "The projection operator is defined by..." This is standard notation and the definition is perhaps not needed here, but it's not wrong.
    *   l207: "bounds its variance" -> "bound its variance"
    *   l281: "where, rather than directly bounding" -> "where one could, rather than directly bounding"
    *   l311-12: "the strongly-convex analysis shows that ϵf optimality in the log-likelihood can be achieved with an amount of work only linear in 1/ϵf." This sentence is slightly confusing. It means work is `O(1/ϵf)` vs `O(1/ϵf^1.5)` from the convex analysis, which is a significant improvement. Rewording could make this clearer.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents an original, technically sound, and substantial contribution to the theory of learning in intractable models. The core idea of linking MLE tractability to MCMC mixing speed is insightful and opens a new avenue for research. The primary weakness is the strong assumption of a known fast-mixing set, which may limit the immediate practical impact of the results. However, as a theoretical work that provides the first formal guarantees for a widely used class of algorithms, its value is significant. The clarity omissions are easily addressable. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it advances our theoretical understanding of a fundamental machine learning problem.
