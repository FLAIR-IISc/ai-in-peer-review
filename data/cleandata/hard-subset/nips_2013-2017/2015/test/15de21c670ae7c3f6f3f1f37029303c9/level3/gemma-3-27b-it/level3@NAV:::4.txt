This paper introduces a novel framework for maximum likelihood learning in intractable models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, where MCMC inference converges rapidly. This elegantly sidesteps the intractability of traditional approaches by guaranteeing efficient learning within this constrained parameter space. The authors demonstrate a fully polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation under these conditions, with convergence rates analyzed for both regularized and unregularized cases. This work builds upon prior research addressing intractable learning, such as pseudolikelihood and score matching, by offering a more theoretically grounded approach with convergence guarantees lacking in those heuristics. It also connects to work on tractable model classes like tree-structured parameters, offering a compelling alternative based on mixing properties rather than graph structure.

The presentation is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and the technical details are carefully presented. The authors appropriately situate their work within the existing literature on graphical models and MCMC methods, referencing key papers like Jerrum & Sinclair (1993) and Chow & Liu (1968). The strengths lie in the originality of the “fast-mixing” parameter set concept and the resulting FPRAS guarantees. The theoretical analysis appears sound, with explicit convergence rates provided, making the results practically meaningful.

However, the significance of this work feels somewhat understated. While the paper fills a clear gap in understanding learning with MCMC, the reviewer struggles to grasp the breadth of its potential impact. The discussion feels overly focused on the theoretical results and lacks concrete examples of where this framework might be particularly advantageous beyond the Ising model example. The lower bound analysis (Theorem 7) is particularly difficult to interpret and its connection to practical implications isn’t clearly articulated. Expanding this discussion, and perhaps illustrating the limitations of the fast-mixing constraint in specific scenarios, would significantly strengthen the paper. 

Arguments for acceptance: introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to learning intractable models; provides valuable convergence rate guarantees; clear and well-written presentation. Arguments against acceptance: unclear overall significance and impact; limited discussion of practical considerations and limitations.

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors expanding the discussion of potential impact, future extensions (e.g., exploring different MCMC algorithms or adaptive mixing rate settings), and providing a more intuitive explanation of the lower bound results.
