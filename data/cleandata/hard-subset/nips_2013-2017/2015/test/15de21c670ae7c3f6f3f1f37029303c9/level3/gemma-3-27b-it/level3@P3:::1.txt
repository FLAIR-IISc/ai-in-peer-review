This paper proposes a framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, guaranteeing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). While the idea of leveraging fast-mixing Markov chains for learning is intuitive, the paper suffers from a significant lack of awareness of related work and presents results that are largely subsumed by existing theory.

The core idea – approximating the likelihood gradient using MCMC samples – is a common practice (e.g., [7, 10, 24, 25, 27, 29]). The novelty claimed by the authors lies in providing theoretical guarantees for this process when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set. However, the paper fails to adequately position this contribution within the broader context of optimization with stochastic gradients, particularly in relation to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics, which have a long history (e.g., [26]). The connection to existing work on optimizing intractable likelihoods (e.g., score matching [16], composite likelihoods [20, 30], pseudolikelihood [3]) is also superficially addressed. A thorough comparison to these methods, specifically analyzing their sample complexity and convergence properties within the fast-mixing framework, is missing.

Crucially, the theoretical results presented are not novel. The authors prove a FPRAS under certain conditions. However, existing theory, such as the work of Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret [26] on stochastic approximation, already covers these results and extends them to more general scenarios. For example, the authors’ assumptions regarding ergodicity (specifically, the requirement for uniform ergodicity and re-initialization at a fixed distribution *r*) are unnecessarily strong. Re-initializing with the previous sample, a commonly used and extensively analyzed technique (e.g., contrastive divergence [5]), may be preferable and would fall within the scope of existing theoretical analyses.  The assertion that a projection onto Θ is necessary is also a simplification; techniques like maintaining a pool of samples can mitigate the need for explicit projection.

The presentation of the paper is also problematic. The assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 are not clearly stated near the theorems themselves, hindering readability and understanding. Furthermore, the strong assumptions used for Theorem 6 seem unwarranted, given the broader theoretical landscape. The discussion of related work in Section 3.1, while extensive in terms of listing papers, often lacks critical analysis of how these works relate to the authors' contribution. The example provided in Section 6, while helpful for illustration, acknowledges conservatism in the theoretical bounds, hinting at potential limitations in the analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper identifies an important problem – learning with intractable likelihoods – and proposes a conceptually sound approach based on fast-mixing parameters.
*   The analysis provides theoretical guarantees (albeit not novel) for the proposed algorithm.
*   The example in Section 6 provides a practical illustration of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Major lack of awareness of prior literature on related algorithms and theory (Monte Carlo EM, stochastic approximation).
*   Results are largely subsumed by existing theory (Benveniste, Métivier, Priouret [26]).
*   Unnecessary/incorrect assumptions regarding ergodicity and re-initialization.
*   Poor presentation: unclear statement of assumptions, overly strong assumptions in Theorem 6.
*   Limited novelty: the approach represents an incremental improvement rather than a significant breakthrough.
*   Insufficient comparison to alternative methods for intractable likelihood optimization.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in machine learning.
*   The theoretical analysis, while not novel, is rigorous.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of awareness of related work is a significant flaw.
*   The novelty of the contribution is marginal.
*   The assumptions and presentation could be improved.
*   The paper does not adequately demonstrate its impact on the field.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper, as it stands, is not suitable for acceptance to NIPS. While the ideas are reasonable, the lack of engagement with existing literature and the incremental nature of the contribution significantly detract from its value. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a more thorough comparison to related work and a relaxation of the unnecessary assumptions, would be required for reconsideration. The current manuscript reads more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and compelling scientific contribution.
