

**Review for "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"**

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in learning undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is intractable in high-treewidth models due to the computational difficulty of inference. The authors propose a novel framework for tractable learning by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution. This approach differs from previous tractable learning methods (e.g., tree-structured models [Chow and Liu, 1968], pseudolikelihood [Besag, 1974], score matching [Hyvärinen, 2005]) that either have inferior sample complexity or require well-specified models.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by proving that for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients will converge to the maximum likelihood solution inside the set with high probability. The authors provide explicit bounds on computational complexity: for the unregularized case, achieving ε-accuracy in log-likelihood requires effort cubic in 1/ε, while for the ridge-regularized case (strongly convex), the effort is quadratic in 1/ε. These results establish a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning in this class of models.

**Quality**: The theoretical results are technically sound, with rigorous proofs for the main theorems. The paper carefully distinguishes between the convex and strongly convex cases, providing appropriate analysis for each. A notable weakness is the treatment of Theorem 7 (the lower bound), which is difficult to interpret and lacks sufficient discussion. The authors should expand on why this lower bound is meaningful and how it relates to the upper bounds. The example in Section 6 provides a useful empirical validation of the theoretical results, demonstrating that the algorithm can achieve solutions tighter than the specified accuracy.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The key concepts (fast-mixing parameter sets, mixing times, etc.) are clearly defined, and the main results are presented in a logical progression. The notation is consistent throughout, and the figures effectively illustrate the theoretical findings. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the results, but the core presentation is excellent.

**Originality**: The paper introduces a novel concept of tractable models based on fast-mixing properties, which significantly extends the understanding of tractable learning beyond traditional treewidth-based approaches. This is a clear departure from previous work on tractable learning at NIPS (e.g., [Heinemann and Globerson, 2014] on high-girth models, [Mizrahi et al., 2014] on local cluster learning). The paper's focus on the exponential family framework rather than just graphical models also broadens its applicability.

**Significance**: The work fills an important theoretical gap in understanding MCMC-based learning methods, which are widely used in practice but lack theoretical guarantees. While the paper does not claim significant practical contributions, it provides a theoretically grounded approach to learning intractable models. The significance would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of potential applications, future extensions, and how this work might influence future research in graphical models and MCMC-based learning. The authors should elaborate on how their approach might be applied to specific domains or combined with other techniques.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance**:
*Pro*: Novel theoretical framework for tractable learning; rigorous convergence guarantees with meaningful bounds; clear presentation; addresses an important gap in the literature; establishes a FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning in fast-mixing parameter sets.

*Con*: The significance section could be expanded to better articulate the practical impact; the lower bound (Theorem 7) requires more interpretation; the paper doesn't discuss practical implementation details or potential limitations in real-world applications.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of tractable learning for intractable models. The paper's main strength is its novel framework for identifying tractable learning classes and providing rigorous convergence guarantees. While the significance section could be strengthened, the theoretical results are significant and well-supported.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in maximum likelihood learning for undirected graphical models. The results are novel, technically sound, and well-presented. With minor revisions to address the interpretation of the lower bound and expand the discussion of significance, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
