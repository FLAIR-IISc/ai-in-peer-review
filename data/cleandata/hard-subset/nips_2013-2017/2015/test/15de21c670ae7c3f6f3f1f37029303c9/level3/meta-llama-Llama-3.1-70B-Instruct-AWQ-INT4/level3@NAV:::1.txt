This paper proposes a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution. The authors provide a theoretical guarantee that gradient descent with gradients approximated by sampling will approximate the maximum likelihood solution inside this set with high probability.

The paper's main contributions are:

1.  A new notion of tractability for exponential families, which is based on the mixing time of the Markov chain.
2.  A proof that gradient descent with MCMC sampling can approximate the maximum likelihood solution inside the fast-mixing set with high probability.
3.  A bound on the total computational effort required to achieve a certain level of accuracy in the log-likelihood.

However, there are several issues with the paper:

1.  **Lack of awareness of prior literature**: The authors do not adequately acknowledge or compare their work to existing research on related algorithms and theory, such as Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics.
2.  **Unnecessary assumptions**: The authors assume that Markov chains need to be uniformly ergodic and that reinitializing at a fixed distribution r is required. However, these assumptions are not necessary, and reinitializing with the previous sample may be preferable and has been extensively analyzed previously.
3.  **Poor presentation**: The assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 are unclear and should be stated explicitly near the theorems.
4.  **Marginal novelty**: The paper's results are not significantly different from existing work, and the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach advances the state of the art.

In terms of the conference guidelines, the paper's quality, clarity, originality, and significance are all somewhat lacking. While the paper is technically sound, the claims are not well-supported by theoretical analysis or experimental results. The paper is not clearly written, and the organization could be improved. The authors do not adequately compare their work to existing research, and the paper's novelty is marginal.

**Arguments for acceptance**:

*   The paper proposes a new approach to maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models.

**Arguments against acceptance**:
