

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in maximum likelihood learning for undirected graphical models: the intractability of computing gradients when the model has high treewidth. While previous work has focused on restricting parameters to tractable sets like tree-structured models (Chow-Liu algorithm), the authors propose a fundamentally different notion of tractability: constraining parameters to sets where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) mixing times are guaranteed to be fast. The authors prove that for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to such a "fast-mixing" set, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients will yield a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution.

This work builds on the rich literature of mixing times for MCMC (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Domke & Liu, 2013; Liu & Domke, 2014), but applies it to the specific context of maximum likelihood learning. The authors make a compelling theoretical argument that has been lacking in the machine learning community, where MCMC-based gradient estimation is widely used without theoretical guarantees.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical contribution is solid and well-motivated, providing a rigorous justification for a common practice. The authors carefully distinguish between the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, deriving separate convergence bounds for each. The paper is generally well-written with clear notation, and the proofs, while complex, appear logically sound. The authors are appropriately careful to discuss limitations, such as the practical difficulty of satisfying the fast-mixing condition.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses that significantly impact its significance and practical relevance. The key limitation is that the parameter constraints required for the fast-mixing guarantee (e.g., |θ_ij| ≤ β for Ising models) are unlikely to be satisfied in practice. As the authors note, these constraints effectively restrict the model to a very small set of parameters, making the results of limited practical use. The authors acknowledge this but do not provide a solution for enforcing the mixing condition in practice, which is a significant gap. The paper also raises the concern that combining this with ridge regression might lead to over-regularization, though this is not fully explored.

The significance of the paper is limited by these practical constraints. While the theoretical contribution is important for understanding when MCMC-based learning is justified, the results don't provide a practical method for implementing such learning in general cases. The authors correctly note that this is a "solid theoretical formalization" but "significant non-trivial research is needed to make the results practically useful."

In terms of the four criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with plausible proofs. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and weaknesses.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written with clear notation, though some proofs are complex.
- Originality: The notion of fast-mixing parameter sets is novel compared to existing tractable sets (like tree-structured parameters).
- Significance: The theoretical contribution is important, but practical applicability is limited by the restrictive parameter constraints.

Arguments for acceptance: Provides a theoretically rigorous justification for a widely used practice (MCMC-based gradient estimation), makes a significant contribution to theoretical understanding of when MCMC can be used for learning, and the results apply generally to exponential families.

Arguments against acceptance: The parameter constraints required for the fast-mixing guarantee are unrealistic in practice, the paper does not provide a practical method for enforcing these constraints, and the results are limited to cases where the model is already constrained to satisfy the mixing condition.

Overall, this paper makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding when MCMC-based learning is justified. However, the practical limitations of the results significantly reduce their significance for the machine learning community. While the theoretical framework is sound, the lack of practical applicability means the paper is more of a stepping stone than a complete solution to the problem. Given NIPS's emphasis on impactful contributions that advance the field, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from addressing how to enforce the mixing condition in practice or demonstrating that the constraints can be satisfied in meaningful applications.
