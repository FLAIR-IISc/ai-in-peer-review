**Summary**

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models with high treewidth, where computing the exact gradient of the log-likelihood is intractable. The standard approach of approximating the gradient using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is widely used in practice but generally lacks theoretical guarantees. The authors' main contribution is to provide a formal theoretical justification for this practice under specific conditions. They propose constraining the model parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where MCMC is guaranteed to converge to the stationary distribution at a geometric rate. The central result of the paper is a proof that, for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within this constrained set. The paper provides separate, rigorous analyses for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) optimization problems, deriving explicit bounds on the total computational effort required to achieve a desired accuracy. This work connects the theoretical literature on MCMC mixing times to the practical problem of parameter learning, formalizing the intuition that fast mixing is a key ingredient for reliable MCMC-based learning.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides a novel and significant theoretical contribution. While using MCMC to approximate gradients is a common heuristic (e.g., in training Restricted Boltzmann Machines), this work is, to my knowledge, the first to prove that this procedure constitutes an FPRAS under well-defined conditions. It formalizes a long-held intuition within the community and provides a solid theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms. This is a valuable bridge between the theory of MCMC and the practice of machine learning.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs that build upon established results in convex optimization and concentration inequalities. The analysis carefully distinguishes between the convex and strongly convex cases, providing appropriate convergence guarantees for each. This represents a complete and solid piece of theoretical work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the problem is formally stated, and the main results are presented in an accessible manner. The authors do a good job of providing intuition for their proofs and relating their theoretical framework to specific examples like the Ising model.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Practical Relevance and Assumptions:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the strong assumptions required, which may limit its direct practical applicability. The analysis hinges on the ability to define and project onto a convex set of parameters Θ that guarantees fast mixing. For the Ising model examples given, this set corresponds to placing bounds on the interaction strengths. In many practical applications, the optimal parameters may lie outside such a restrictive set, and constraining them a priori could lead to a poorly fitting model. The path from this theoretical result to a broadly useful, practical learning algorithm is not yet clear and would require significant further research.
*   **Enforcing Fast Mixing:** The paper assumes the fast-mixing parameter set Θ is given. A potentially more practical approach would be to encourage fast mixing via regularization, rather than enforcing it as a hard constraint. Could the authors comment on whether their analysis could be extended to such a setting? For instance, could one design a regularizer based on the conditions for rapid mixing (e.g., based on the spectral norm of the interaction matrix)? A potential concern with such an approach would be how to balance this new regularizer with standard ones like L2 ridge regularization, as the combination could lead to over-regularization.
*   **Complexity of Projection:** The analysis of the total computational effort focuses on the number of MCMC samples and gradient steps, largely ignoring the cost of projecting onto the set Θ at each iteration. While the authors argue this cost is often small, for more complex definitions of fast-mixing sets, the projection step itself could be computationally expensive and dominate the overall runtime. A more complete analysis would account for this projection cost.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
1.  Provides the first rigorous proof that the common practice of MCMC-based gradient learning can be a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS).
2.  The theoretical contribution is significant and builds a valuable bridge between the theory of randomized algorithms and practical machine learning.
3.  The paper is technically solid, well-written, and clearly presents a novel conceptual framework for tractable learning in intractable models.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
1.  The strong assumptions—particularly the existence of a known, convex, and easily projectable fast-mixing parameter set—severely limit the immediate practical utility of the results.
2.  The work feels more like a theoretical formalization of an existing heuristic rather than a proposal for a new, practical algorithm.

In conclusion, this is a strong theoretical paper that rigorously formalizes the conditions under which a popular learning heuristic is provably efficient. While the practical limitations are significant, the novelty and importance of the theoretical contribution are substantial. It provides a solid foundation that future work can build upon to develop more practical algorithms. I believe its contributions are of high interest to the NIPS community.
