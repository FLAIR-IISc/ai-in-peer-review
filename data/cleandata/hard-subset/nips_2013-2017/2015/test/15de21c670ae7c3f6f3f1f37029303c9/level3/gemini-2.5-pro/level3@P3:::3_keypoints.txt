- Problem: ML learning in high-treewidth Markov networks is intractable because gradient computation requires #P-hard expectations.
- Practical heuristic: MCMC sampling is commonly used to approximate gradients but historically lacked theoretical guarantees due to bias from approximate sampling.
- Main contribution: Introduces "fast-mixing parameter set" and proves projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients is an FPRAS when restricted to this set.
- Theoretical impact: Connects MCMC mixing guarantees to correctness and efficiency of learning, providing the first FPRAS for this learning paradigm.
- Technical analysis: Rigorous use of inexact-gradient descent results and concentration inequalities, with separate proofs for unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases.
- Explicit bounds: Derives schedules for gradient steps, number of MCMC samples, and chain lengths to achieve desired accuracy with high probability.
- Clarity and exposition: Well-written, organized, and places contributions clearly in context relative to structural or pseudolikelihood methods.
- Originality/significance: Novel tractability notion based on dynamic mixing behavior rather than structural constraints, opening new research directions.
- Practical relevance concern: Requires constraining parameters to a known fast-mixing set Θ, which current known sets are restrictive and may not model complex real-world data.
- Open question: Suggests softening hard projection via a "mixing-time regularizer" (e.g., penalize spectral norm) but raises concerns about interaction with standard L2 regularization and potential over-regularization.
- Acceptance arguments: Novel significant theoretical result, original tractability notion, and technically rigorous analysis.
- Rejection argument: Limited immediate practical applicability due to restrictive fast-mixing parameter assumptions.
- Overall assessment: High-quality foundational theoretical work that formalizes intuition linking efficient MCMC to efficient learning, but practical impact is presently limited.
