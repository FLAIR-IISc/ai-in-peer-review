## Review of “Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, specifically addressing the intractability of gradient computation for high-treewidth models. The authors propose a novel approach: restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, where MCMC inference converges rapidly. Their central contribution is a proof demonstrating that, under this constraint, MCMC-based weight learning achieves a fully polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). The analysis is presented separately for unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (non-convex) cases, providing theoretical bounds on the approximation accuracy and computational effort.

The work builds upon existing approaches to tractable parameter sets, like tree-structured parameters (Chow-Liu algorithm), but offers a fundamentally different criterion based on MCMC mixing rates. This is a valuable shift in perspective. The paper correctly identifies the lack of theoretical guarantees for commonly used MCMC-based gradient estimation and provides a rigorous framework for analyzing its performance. The authors also appropriately situate their work within the broader context of alternative learning methods like pseudolikelihood and composite likelihoods, highlighting the potential advantages of their approach when a well-specified model is assumed. 

The paper is generally well-written and the proofs, while not fully verified by this reviewer due to their complexity, appear plausible. The inclusion of proof sketches is helpful for understanding the key ideas. However, a significant weakness lies in the practical relevance of the theoretical results. The parameter constraints required for the theorems – guaranteeing rapid MCMC mixing – are likely difficult to satisfy in real-world applications. This severely limits the immediate applicability of the proposed method. 

A crucial open question raised by the authors themselves is whether the mixing condition can be artificially enforced through regularization. This is a promising direction for future research, but they rightly caution that combining this with ridge regression could lead to over-regularization. Further investigation into the interplay between mixing-time regularization and standard regularization techniques is warranted.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation for understanding the conditions under which MCMC-based learning yields a FPRAS.
*   Novel approach to defining a tractable parameter set based on MCMC mixing rates.
*   Clear presentation and well-structured analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited practical applicability due to the stringent requirements for fast mixing.
*   The theoretical results, while elegant, require significant further research to become practically useful.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong theoretical contribution that formalizes a crucial aspect of MCMC-based learning. However, the significant gap between theory and practice prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance at this time. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the practical limitations and explore the potential for enforcing the mixing condition through regularization in future work.
