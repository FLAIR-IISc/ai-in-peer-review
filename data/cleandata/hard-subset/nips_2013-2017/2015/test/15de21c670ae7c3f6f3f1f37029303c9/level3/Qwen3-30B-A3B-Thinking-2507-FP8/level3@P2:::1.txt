

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where MCMC sampling converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The authors claim to provide a theoretical guarantee for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, showing convergence to the maximum likelihood solution inside the constraint set with high probability. While the paper is technically sound in its analysis, it suffers from significant shortcomings in context, novelty, and presentation.

The main contribution appears to be the theoretical analysis of a standard MCMC-based optimization approach for maximum likelihood learning. The authors claim their work is novel, but it is closely related to well-established methods including Monte Carlo EM (Tanner & Wong, 1987) and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics (Benveniste et al., 1987). In fact, the core theoretical results are largely subsumed by existing stochastic approximation theory for non-uniformly ergodic Markov chains, which has been extensively studied for decades (e.g., Benveniste et al., 1987; Roberts & Rosenthal, 1998). The paper's assumption of uniformly ergodic Markov chains is unnecessarily restrictive compared to the more general theory already available.

The paper's main weakness is its lack of awareness of prior literature. The authors fail to cite key works that establish the convergence of similar methods. For example, the paper's Theorem 2 essentially replicates results from Benveniste et al. (1987) for stochastic approximation with Markovian noise, but without proper attribution. The authors also incorrectly claim that "such procedures lack theoretical guarantees" when in fact extensive theoretical work already exists on this topic.

The presentation of assumptions is poor, with critical conditions for the theorems not clearly stated near where they're used. For instance, the assumptions for Theorem 1 (Lipschitz gradient) and Theorem 2 (convex convergence) are buried in the text rather than explicitly stated at the beginning of the theorems. This makes the technical contributions difficult to evaluate.

The paper also makes unnecessary assumptions, such as requiring reinitialization of MCMC chains at a fixed distribution r at each iteration. In practice, it's common to initialize with the previous sample (as in contrastive divergence, Hinton, 2002), which can significantly reduce mixing time. This approach has been extensively analyzed in the literature (e.g., Tieleman, 2008; Salakhutdinov & Hinton, 2009), but the paper fails to acknowledge or discuss it.

The significance of the work is limited. While the paper provides theoretical bounds, these are not novel and are subsumed by existing theory. The practical contribution is minimal (as the authors themselves note in Section 6), and the paper doesn't provide any new algorithms or empirical results that would demonstrate the method's utility.

**Strengths:**
- The paper correctly identifies the computational challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth models
- The theoretical analysis is technically sound within its restricted framework
- The paper provides clear bounds on computational complexity for the proposed method

**Weaknesses:**
- Major lack of awareness of prior literature on stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics
- Unnecessary assumptions (uniformly ergodic Markov chains, fixed reinitialization)
- Marginal novelty compared to existing theory
- Poor presentation of assumptions and conditions
- Inadequate comparison to existing work on optimizing intractable likelihoods

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper correctly identifies an important problem in graphical models
- The technical analysis is rigorous within its framework
- The bounds provided could be useful for theoretical understanding

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The core theoretical results are not novel and are subsumed by existing literature
- The paper fails to properly contextualize its work within the broader literature
- The assumptions made are unnecessarily restrictive
- The practical contribution is minimal
- The presentation is unclear and lacks proper attribution

This paper would be significantly improved by properly contextualizing its results within the existing literature on stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics, removing unnecessary assumptions, and clarifying the presentation of technical conditions. As it stands, the novelty is marginal, and the paper would be better suited for a more specialized venue rather than NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**
