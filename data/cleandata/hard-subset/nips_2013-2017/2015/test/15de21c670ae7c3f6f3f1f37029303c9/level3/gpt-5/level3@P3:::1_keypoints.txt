- Paper studies ML in exponential families constrained to a “fast-mixing” parameter set Θ, using projected gradient descent with MCMC approximated expectations and gives high-probability suboptimality/parameter-error bounds and K,M,v schedules.

- Work is closely related to extensive prior literature (MC-MLE, MCEM/SAEM, stochastic approximation with Markovian noise) and needs explicit engagement with these sources.

- Strength: clear goal of enforcing tractability via fast-mixing constraints and analyzing end-to-end optimization complexity.

- Strength: general exponential-family setup with separate treatment of convex and strongly convex cases.

- Strength: provides concrete schedules linking desired accuracy to number of iterations K, samples M, and chain length v, appealing to readers who want worst-case guarantees.

- Strength: useful discussion connecting mixing-time conditions (Dobrushin/spectral-norm) to projection operators.

- Major concern: assumption of uniform geometric ergodicity with TV contraction and uniform constants (C, α) over Θ is very strong and not justified; weaker drift/minorization conditions suffice in SA theory.

- Major concern: restarting the chain from a fixed distribution each iteration is unnecessary and suboptimal—persistent chains or sample pools are standard and theoretically supported.

- Major concern: bounded sufficient statistics assumption is stronger than needed; SA analyses handle unbounded statistics under moment/ Lyapunov conditions.

- Novelty is limited: main guarantees follow from standard inexact/proximal gradient arguments given strong uniform-mixing and independence assumptions.

- FPRAS-style claims rely on knowing global mixing constants and efficient projection, which are often unrealistic and give pessimistic bounds.

- Theorem 6 and related results use strong/uniform assumptions (e.g., Hoeffding bounds) that can be weakened by invoking classic SA results.

- Presentation issue: key assumptions (bounded t(x), compact Θ, independence, uniform mixing constants) are scattered and should be stated precisely with each theorem.

- Technical correction: Section 3.1 conflates irreducibility/aperiodicity with existence of geometric rates; cite Meyn & Tweedie and clarify conditions for geometric ergodicity.

- The paper should include a focused comparison (theoretical and empirical) to MC-MLE, MCEM/SAEM, SA with Markov noise, and PCD on standard Gibbs-field benchmarks.

- Mathematical correctness holds under the stated strong assumptions, but proofs rely on independence and uniform mixing that existing theory can relax.

- Readability is generally fine, but clarity suffers because assumptions and which constants are uniform are not made explicit near results.

- Originality is marginal without deeper comparison to classic SA/MCEM/MCML literature or relaxation of restrictive assumptions.

- Significance is limited by strong/unrealistic assumptions and minimal empirical validation, though the mixing-time perspective is potentially useful.

- Suggested improvements: reframe analysis under stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics using drift/minorization, permit persistent chains, and cite Benveniste–Métivier–Priouret, Kushner–Yin, Andrieu–Moulines, Fort & Moulines.

- Suggested improvements: add thorough related-work and empirical comparisons to MC-MLE/SAEM/CD/PCD on Ising/Potts benchmarks.

- Suggested improvements: state all assumptions explicitly next to each theorem, correct mixing-time statements, and discuss practicality of estimating (C, α) and projection costs; temper FPRAS claims.

- Pros for acceptance: clear, self-contained optimization bounds and an interesting bridge between mixing-time literature and learning objectives.

- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty relative to established SA/MCEM theory, strong/unnecessary assumptions, exclusion of persistent-chain practice, and minimal empirical validation.

- Overall recommendation: leaning reject in current form; revise to engage SA-with-Markov-noise literature, relax assumptions, and add substantive comparisons to be a useful contribution.
