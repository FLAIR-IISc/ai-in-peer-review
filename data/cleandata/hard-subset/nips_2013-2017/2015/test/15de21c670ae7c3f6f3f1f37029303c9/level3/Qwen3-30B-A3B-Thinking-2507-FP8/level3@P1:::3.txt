

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in learning undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is intractable due to the computational difficulty of computing partition functions. The authors propose a novel approach by considering parameter sets where MCMC inference is guaranteed to mix rapidly, rather than restricting to tree-structured parameters as in previous work. The key contribution is proving that when parameters lie in a "fast-mixing" set, gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning.

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. First, it establishes that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, gradient descent with MCMC approximated gradients converges to the maximum likelihood solution inside the set with high probability. The authors derive separate theoretical bounds for the unregularized (convex) case requiring effort cubic in 1/ε and for the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case requiring effort quadratic in 1/ε. This generalizes previous work on tractable learning approaches, which typically restrict to tree-structured models or assume well-specified models. The paper builds on seminal work by Jerrum and Sinclair (1993) on partition function computation and extends the theoretical foundation for MCMC-based learning methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem statement to theoretical results. The authors provide sufficient technical detail for experts to understand the proofs, though some minor typos were noted. The theoretical results are sound, with careful analysis of mixing times and gradient errors. The authors correctly acknowledge related work in graphical models, including the Chow-Liu algorithm for tree-structured models and previous methods for fast-mixing parameter sets (e.g., Domke and Liu 2013, Liu and Domke 2014).

The primary strength of this work is its theoretical rigor in establishing conditions under which MCMC-based learning is guaranteed to converge to a good solution. This addresses a significant gap in the literature where practitioners commonly use MCMC-based gradient approximation without theoretical guarantees. The paper's generalization to exponential families beyond graphical models is also noteworthy.

However, the practical relevance of the results is limited by the difficulty of enforcing the fast-mixing condition in real applications. The parameter constraints required for the theorems (e.g., bounding interaction strengths in Ising models) are unlikely to be satisfied in typical learning scenarios without artificial regularization. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide a convincing argument for how to enforce the mixing condition in practice. The paper's example section demonstrates the algorithm's performance on a small Ising model, but this does not address the fundamental challenge of making the fast-mixing condition practically enforceable.

A particularly important concern is whether the authors can enforce the mixing condition through artificial regularization without over-regularizing the model. The paper suggests combining the fast-mixing constraint with ridge regularization, but the authors do not analyze how this might interact with the existing regularization. This is a critical issue because combining two forms of regularization could lead to excessive smoothing of the learned parameters, reducing the model's expressiveness.

The significance of the work is primarily theoretical rather than practical. While the paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding when MCMC-based learning methods are theoretically sound, the practical applicability is limited without addressing how to enforce the fast-mixing condition. The authors correctly note that their work is not a practical contribution but rather a theoretical foundation for future work.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with plausible proofs, though some details in the proofs could be strengthened. The experimental results are minimal and primarily serve to illustrate the theoretical bounds rather than demonstrate practical utility. The paper correctly acknowledges the limitations of its approach.

The originality of the work is strong, as it introduces a fundamentally different notion of tractability based on fast mixing rather than tree-structured models. This represents a significant departure from previous approaches to tractable learning in graphical models.

In summary, this paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to understanding the conditions under which MCMC-based learning algorithms are guaranteed to converge. The results are rigorous and well-motivated, and the paper is well-written. However, the practical relevance is limited by the difficulty of enforcing the fast-mixing condition in real applications. The authors do not provide a clear path to addressing this limitation, which is a significant barrier to practical adoption.

The main strengths are the theoretical novelty and rigor, while the primary weaknesses are the limited practical applicability and lack of a clear strategy for enforcing the fast-mixing condition. The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing either a practical method for enforcing fast mixing or a more thorough analysis of the tradeoffs involved in combining the fast-mixing constraint with other regularization techniques.

Given the high theoretical quality and novelty, I recommend acceptance, but with the understanding that the practical relevance requires further work to bridge the gap between theory and application. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that will inform future research on tractable learning methods for graphical models.
