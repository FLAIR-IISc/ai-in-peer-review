Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families (including undirected graphical models) when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting to tree-structured models (e.g., Chow–Liu) or using surrogate objectives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood), the paper proposes constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ where an associated MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast in total variation. Under bounded sufficient statistics, the authors prove that projected gradient descent with gradients approximated by MCMC samples constitutes an FPRAS for the MLE restricted to Θ. They provide convergence and complexity guarantees both for the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case. The unregularized case achieves ε-accuracy in objective value with total sampling effort scaling as Õ(ε−3), while the ridge-regularized case achieves ε-accuracy in parameter distance with effort Õ(ε−2). The analysis leverages standard tools: Lipschitz continuity of the gradient, concentration inequalities (Efron–Stein/Bernstein; Hoeffding-type), fast mixing bounds for MCMC (e.g., Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes; Jerrum–Sinclair), and convergence results for inexact projected gradient (Schmidt et al.). The results generalize beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, provided one can project onto Θ and the mixing rate admits explicit constants (C, α).

The work connects to and extends: tree-based learning (Chow–Liu), high-girth/correlation-decay (Heinemann–Globerson), surrogate objectives (Besag; Hyvärinen; Lindsay/Varin), MCMC-based MLE (Geyer; Gu & Zhu; Tieleman; Salakhutdinov), fast-mixing model design (Domke–Liu, NIPS 2013; Liu–Domke, NIPS 2014), and restart-based fast-mixing learning (Steinhardt–Liang, ICML 2015). The novelty is a unified optimization-and-mixing analysis that yields an FPRAS for restricted-MLE in general exponential families.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization of a widely used heuristic (MCMC-approximated gradients) with explicit high-probability convergence guarantees under fast mixing.
- General setting (arbitrary exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics), not limited to specific graphical structures.
- Transparent separation of stochastic error and bias due to finite-time MCMC; uses well-justified concentration and inexact-gradient results.
- Explicit, near-optimal schedules for iterations K, samples M, and chain length v; clear dependence on mixing constants (C, α), Lipschitz constant L, and regularization λ.
- Helpful discussion contrasting convex vs strongly convex regimes; toy example illustrates the quantities.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical applicability: Fast-mixing parameter sets Θ that admit explicit (C, α) and efficient projection are known mainly for limited families (e.g., Ising with bounded degree or spectral-norm constraints; certain ferromagnetic regimes). Realistic models often violate these constraints or lack usable constants, so the guaranteed regime may be small.
- Projection complexity and feasibility: For many exponential families, efficient projection onto Θ is unclear; even when possible (e.g., spectral norm constraints), it can be nontrivial. The analysis largely treats projection cost as negligible relative to sampling.
- Conservatism of constants: The bounds rely on worst-case L, C, α, leading to large K, M, v (as also seen in the example). This limits guidance for practical hyperparameter selection.
- Algorithmic mismatch with common practice: The theory assumes fresh i.i.d. MCMC samples per iteration from arbitrary initializations. Popular strategies (persistent chains/pools, contrastive divergence with very short chains) are not covered; warm starts are discussed but not analyzed.
- Scope limitations: Hidden variables, continuous variables with unbounded statistics, non-reversible samplers, or unknown mixing rates are out of scope.
- Minor writing issues: A few typos and notational choices (e.g., consistent use/definition of the bound R) could be clarified. It would also help to emphasize that ridge regularization makes the problem strongly convex (not non-convex).

Quality
The technical development appears sound and builds on standard, appropriate tools. Proof sketches are plausible; full proofs are deferred to the appendix. Assumptions are stated clearly (bounded sufficient statistics; existence of Θ with geometric mixing and known constants; ability to project). The most significant caveat is external: obtaining/estimating tight mixing constants and projections in practice.

Clarity
Generally well written and well organized. The paper motivates the setting, defines the algorithmic components, and states provable schedules and complexities. Suggestions:
- Clarify notation for the bound on sufficient statistics (use a single symbol consistently).
- Provide pseudo-code for the projected MCMC-based gradient method and explicitly state the stepsize used.
- Highlight the strong convexity in the ridge-regularized case.
- Summarize known Θ and projection methods beyond the Ising examples, or explicitly delimit where Θ is known.

Originality
The FPRAS result for restricted MLE via fast-mixing sets, with general exponential-family scope and explicit schedules, is novel to my knowledge. It builds on prior fast-mixing parameter-projection work and restart-mixing ideas but delivers a new learning complexity guarantee.

Significance
Theoretical significance is moderate: it gives a clean, general framework for when MCMC-based likelihood learning is provably efficient. Practical impact is currently limited by the scarcity of useful Θ with known mixing constants and efficient projections for complex models, and by the conservatism of bounds.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Beyond Ising, what concrete Θ and projection operators are available (e.g., for Potts, general pairwise MRFs with larger alphabets, conditional models)?
- Can you give practical procedures to estimate or upper-bound C and α (possibly data-dependent) and discuss robustness to misspecification?
- Would penalizing deviations from Θ (a “fast-mixing regularizer”) yield similar guarantees with approximate projections? Any analysis of penalty vs projection trade-offs and potential over-regularization when combined with ridge?
- Can the analysis be extended to warm starts or persistent chains, perhaps via coupling or path-stability arguments?
- Do the results extend to hidden-variable models or to samplers with restarts (as in Steinhardt–Liang)?
- Empirically, could you add experiments on larger graphs and compare practical schedules (smaller v, warm starts) to the theory?

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and general theory linking MCMC mixing, optimization with biased gradients, and MLE efficiency.
- Novel FPRAS result under clear assumptions; careful treatment of convex vs strongly convex regimes.
- Timely contribution at the intersection of learning and MCMC theory.

Arguments against acceptance
- Applicability hinges on strong, often unmet conditions (known fast-mixing set and projection); limited guidance for typical high-treewidth models.
- Very conservative constants; small-scale empirical validation.
- Does not cover prevalent practical variants (persistent chains, short-run CD), limiting immediate impact.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution with clear novelty, but the current applicability is narrow and empirical support is minimal. I encourage the authors to strengthen the practical side (broader classes with explicit Θ/projections, estimation of mixing constants, and empirical validation) and to extend the analysis to warm starts/persistent chains in a revision.
