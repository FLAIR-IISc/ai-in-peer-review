This paper explores a novel approach to learning in intractable graphical models by focusing on “fast-mixing parameters” – parameters for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference converges quickly. Instead of restricting parameters to tree-structured models (as in Chow-Liu), the authors demonstrate that constraining parameters to sets guaranteeing rapid MCMC mixing allows for a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning. The core result proves that gradient descent, using gradients approximated by MCMC sampling, achieves approximate maximum likelihood solutions within the constrained set with high probability.  They derive convergence rates both for regularized and unregularized cases, showing cubic (1/ε) effort for the former and quadratic (1/ε) effort for the latter. This work builds on prior efforts addressing the intractability of maximum likelihood estimation in graphical models, such as pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22], and composite likelihoods [20, 30]. However, these methods often suffer from inferior sample complexity or lack guarantees when the model is mis-specified.  The presented approach extends beyond graphical models, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. 

The paper is technically sound and presents a clear, well-organized argument. The authors are careful to provide both theoretical analysis and concrete examples to support their claims. The notation is introduced clearly, and the important symbols are conveniently summarized at the beginning of Section 2. The proofs, while detailed (and largely deferred to the appendix), are accompanied by insightful sketches, providing intuition for the results. The inclusion of Theorem 7, establishing a lower bound on computational effort, is commendable, though as the authors acknowledge, its interpretation requires further elaboration. The paper meticulously addresses the challenges associated with MCMC-based gradient estimation, acknowledging the potential for bias and noise and providing rigorous guarantees despite these issues. The thorough exploration of both convex and strongly convex settings further strengthens the robustness of the findings. The presentation is well-written and accessible, facilitating understanding of the complex theoretical concepts.

The originality of this work lies in its reframing of tractability, shifting the focus from the structure of the graph to the mixing properties of the MCMC algorithm used for inference. This provides a new lens for analyzing learning problems in intractable models.  The results are not merely a theoretical curiosity; they offer concrete guarantees on the convergence of a widely used learning strategy (gradient descent with MCMC gradients).  The applicability of the results to general exponential families expands the potential impact beyond graphical models.  While previous work explored learning algorithms applicable to specific tractable graph structures (e.g., tree-structured models [6], high-girth models [14]), this paper offers a more general and theoretically grounded approach. The consideration of both unregularized and regularized cases, and the corresponding convergence rate analysis, adds depth to the contribution.

The significance of the paper is potentially substantial, though it’s not immediately obvious how readily the results will translate into practical improvements. The problem of learning in intractable models is a fundamental challenge in many areas of machine learning and statistics. The authors convincingly argue that their approach offers a theoretical framework for designing and analyzing learning algorithms for these models.  The demonstrated FPRAS guarantees provide a strong foundation for future research. However, the paper would benefit from a more extended discussion of potential applications and impact.  For example, how might these results be used to improve learning in specific domains where intractable models are commonly used (e.g., computer vision, natural language processing)?  Furthermore, exploring the limitations of the approach (e.g., the sensitivity to the choice of mixing time parameters) would enhance the paper's value.  A discussion of potential avenues for future research, such as extending the analysis to more sophisticated sampling algorithms or addressing the computational cost of projection onto the fast-mixing set, would be welcome. The inclusion of an example, though brief, is a helpful illustration.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to learning in intractable models.
*   Provides rigorous convergence guarantees for a widely used learning strategy (gradient descent with MCMC gradients).
*   Applicability extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families.
*   Well-written, clear, and organized presentation.
*   Addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning and statistics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The significance and practical impact are not fully articulated.
*   The lower bound (Theorem 7) is difficult to interpret and could benefit from further discussion.
*   Limited discussion of potential limitations and future research directions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of learning in intractable models. While the practical implications could be more thoroughly discussed, the originality, technical soundness, and potential impact of the work justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS 2015.
