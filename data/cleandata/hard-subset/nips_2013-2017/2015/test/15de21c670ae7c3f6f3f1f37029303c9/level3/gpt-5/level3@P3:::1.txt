Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a constraint that parameters lie in a “fast-mixing” set Θ, i.e., a set where a chosen MCMC kernel mixes rapidly. The algorithm is projected gradient descent on the (regularized) negative log-likelihood; at each iterate, the model expectation is approximated by MCMC samples after v steps from a fixed initial distribution r. The main results give high-probability bounds on suboptimality in the convex case (Theorems 2–5) and on parameter error in the strongly convex case (Theorems 6–9), yielding schedules for K, M, v that imply an FPRAS-type total effort.

The idea of optimizing intractable likelihoods via MCMC-based gradient (or EM) approximations is well known: MC-MLE (Geyer, 1991/1992), stochastic approximation for Gibbs random fields (e.g., Younes, 1989), MCEM/SAEM (Delyon, Lavielle, Moulines, 1999), and the broad stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics literature (Benveniste, Métivier, Priouret, 1990; Kushner & Yin, 2003; Andrieu, Moulines, Priouret, 2005; Fort & Moulines, 2003/2011). Within machine learning, there are also pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, score matching, noise-contrastive estimation, and CD/PCD. The contribution here is the explicit packaging of “fast-mixing parameter sets” with projected optimization and worst-case complexity schedules.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal: enforce tractability by constraining to a fast-mixing set and analyze the end-to-end optimization complexity.
- General exponential-family framing (bounded statistics) and separation of convex vs. strongly convex cases.
- Concrete schedules linking accuracy to K, M, v and mixing constants, which may interest readers who value worst-case guarantees.
- Useful discussion connecting mixing-time bounds (Dobrushin-type/spectral-norm conditions) to projection operators.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Awareness of prior literature and positioning
- The approach and analyses are closely related to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics. Classic results (Benveniste–Métivier–Priouret, Kushner–Yin; Andrieu–Moulines–Priouret “On the stability of stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics,” Ann. Appl. Probab., 2005; Fort & Moulines) cover convergence and rates for SA when the noise arises from a geometrically ergodic Markov chain, often under weaker conditions than assumed here. Please engage with this literature and explain precisely what is new beyond these results.
- MC-MLE (Geyer, 1991/1992), Younes (1989), Gu & Zhu (2001), and later works on Gibbs random fields provide theory and practice for optimizing intractable likelihoods via MCMC gradients. A substantive comparison—conceptually and experimentally—would situate the work.

Assumptions: necessity and correctness
- Uniform geometric ergodicity with TV contraction ∥Mvθ q − pθ∥TV ≤ C α^v for all initial q and with constants (C, α) uniform over Θ is very strong. It is not implied by mere irreducibility/aperiodicity and is not needed by the SA theory; drift/minorization or Dobrushin-type conditions suffice, with convergence in a weighted V-norm (Meyn & Tweedie, 2009). Please fix the statement around “Convergence Theorem” and clearly state when uniform constants across Θ are assumed versus per-θ.
- Reinitializing each iteration from a fixed r is unnecessary and can be harmful. Persisting the chain from the previous iterate (or maintaining a sample pool) is standard (Younes, 1989; Tieleman, 2008; many SA-with-Markov-noise analyses) and can dramatically reduce burn-in. The existing theory allows dependent samples across iterations and delivers almost-sure convergence and asymptotic normality under mild conditions. The current analysis excludes this better practice.
- Bounded sufficient statistics are not essential for convergence; SA analyses accommodate unbounded statistics under moment/drift conditions. Please clarify what really needs to be bounded and consider relaxing with appropriate Lyapunov conditions.

Results and novelty
- The main convergence guarantees and complexity schedules follow standard inexact/proximal gradient arguments once you assume uniform mixing and independence across iterations. The new aspect is the explicit “fast-mixing set” projection and the FPRAS wording. However, the latter relies on knowing global mixing constants (C, α) and being able to project efficiently—assumptions that are often unrealistic and lead to very pessimistic bounds. Existing SA results already cover more general and practical scenarios (no uniform ergodicity, persistent chains, decreasing step sizes).
- Theorem 6 uses strong assumptions (uniform tail bounds via Hoeffding with bounded stats, uniform mixing) that can be weakened; please cite and leverage Benveniste–Métivier–Priouret, Kushner–Yin, and Andrieu–Moulines–Priouret for more general results.

Presentation and clarity
- The assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 (bounded t(x), compactness of Θ, projection well-defined, independence of samples within/ across iterations, uniform mixing constants) should be stated precisely with the theorems. As written, it is difficult to check applicability.
- Section 3.1 conflates existence of geometric rates with irreducibility/aperiodicity; please correct and cite appropriate sources (e.g., Meyn & Tweedie).
- The paper would benefit from a comparison section discussing MC-MLE, MCEM/SAEM, SA with Markov noise, and PCD, both theoretically (assumptions and rates) and, if possible, empirically on Ising/Potts benchmarks.

Quality
- Mathematically correct under the stated (strong) assumptions, but the proofs rely on independence and uniform mixing that can be relaxed by existing theory. Experimental support is minimal.

Clarity
- Generally readable but key assumptions are scattered. State them adjacent to each theorem; clarify which constants are uniform over Θ; fix the mixing-time discussion.

Originality
- Conceptually related to classic SA/MCEM/MCML; novelty is marginal without a deeper comparison or relaxation of assumptions.

Significance
- Potentially useful perspective linking mixing-time constraints to learning, but impact is limited by strong assumptions and lack of empirical comparison to established methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Reframe the analysis under stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics using drift/minorization, allowing persistent chains; cite Benveniste–Métivier–Priouret (1990), Kushner–Yin (2003), Andrieu–Moulines–Priouret (2005), Fort & Moulines.
- Provide a thorough related-work section and empirical comparison to MC-MLE, SAEM, CD/PCD, and composite likelihood on standard Gibbs random field benchmarks.
- Make all assumptions explicit near each theorem; correct statements about mixing.
- Discuss practicality of obtaining (C, α) and projection cost; temper FPRAS claims accordingly.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, self-contained optimization bounds; interesting angle via fast-mixing sets.
- Bridges mixing-time literature with learning objectives.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established SA/MCEM/MCML theory.
- Strong/unnecessary assumptions; incomplete and partly inaccurate treatment of mixing.
- Excludes standard persistent-chain practice that is theoretically covered elsewhere.
- Minimal empirical validation; weak comparison to existing methods.

Overall recommendation
Leaning reject in current form due to limited novelty, strong assumptions, and presentation gaps. With a revision that engages the SA-with-Markov-noise literature, relaxes assumptions, and adds substantive comparisons, this could be a useful contribution.
