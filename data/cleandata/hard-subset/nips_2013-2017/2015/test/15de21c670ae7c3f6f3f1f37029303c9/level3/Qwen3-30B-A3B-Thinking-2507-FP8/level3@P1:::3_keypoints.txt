- Addresses intractability of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models due to partition function computation.
- Proposes restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set so MCMC inference mixes rapidly instead of restricting to tree structures.
- Main theoretical claim: when parameters lie in the fast-mixing set, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients gives an FPRAS for maximum likelihood.
- Proves convergence for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics when parameters are constrained to the fast-mixing set.
- Provides separate complexity bounds: unregularized (convex) case scales cubicly in 1/ε; ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case scales quadratically in 1/ε.
- Generalizes prior tractable learning work (beyond tree-structured or well-specified models).
- Builds on and extends foundational results (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair 1993) regarding partition function computation.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient technical detail for experts.
- Theoretical analysis carefully treats mixing times and gradient approximation errors, with mostly sound proofs.
- Related work is acknowledged appropriately (Chow-Liu, Domke & Liu, etc.).
- Primary strength is theoretical rigor and providing guarantees for commonly used MCMC-based learning procedures.
- Generalization to arbitrary exponential families (not just graphical models) is a noteworthy contribution.
- Practical relevance is limited because enforcing the fast-mixing condition in real applications is difficult.
- Experimental validation is limited (small Ising model example) and does not show how to enforce mixing in practice.
- Concern that enforcing fast-mixing via artificial regularization may over-regularize and reduce model expressiveness.
- Interaction between fast-mixing constraints and existing regularization (e.g., ridge) is not analyzed and is a critical open issue.
- Overall significance is primarily theoretical; the paper is not yet practically applicable without methods to enforce mixing.
- Quality is high but some proof details could be strengthened and experiments expanded.
- Originality is strong—introduces a new tractability notion based on fast mixing rather than graph structure.
- Recommendation: accept for theoretical novelty and rigor, but further work needed to address practical enforcement of fast-mixing constraints.
