- Paper studies maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to “fast-mixing” sets so MCMC converges rapidly.  
- Authors prove gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients finds approximate ML solutions within the constrained set, with polynomial computational scaling in desired accuracy.  
- Results cover both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases.  
- Strength: Provides a clear theoretical framework for convergence of gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients.  
- Strength: Computational complexity analysis relative to accuracy is well-structured.  
- Strength: Section 6 gives a concrete example illustrating the theoretical results.  
- Major weakness: Poor literature review and lack of awareness of prior work on stochastic optimization with Markovian dynamics.  
- Major weakness: Approach closely related to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics; prior results (e.g., Benveniste, Métivier, Priouret) are not adequately cited or compared.  
- Major weakness: Theoretical contributions are not novel—the convergence results are covered by general stochastic approximation theory.  
- Major weakness: Assumptions about Markov chain mixing (e.g., uniform ergodicity, reinitialization from fixed distribution r) are unnecessarily restrictive and not justified.  
- Major weakness: Reinitializing with previous samples is preferable and already analyzed in literature (contrastive divergence), but not considered.  
- Presentation weakness: Key assumptions for Theorems 1, 2, and 6 are not clearly stated near the theorems, making conditions hard to assess.  
- Theorem 6 uses strong assumptions that appear unnecessary compared to existing general results.  
- Quality: Analysis is technically sound but relies on unnecessary assumptions and fails to situate contributions within existing literature.  
- Clarity: Presentation of assumptions and relation to prior work is poor.  
- Originality: Novelty is marginal; core idea and analyses are largely already known.  
- Significance: Limited — results not substantially stronger than existing theory and practical benefit is unclear; bounds seem conservative.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Clear theoretical framework and a concrete illustrative example.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Major lack of literature awareness, unnecessary assumptions, weak novelty, poor presentation of assumptions.  
- Recommendation: Reject; authors should review stochastic approximation literature, remove unnecessary assumptions, and rewrite to clarify novel contributions.
