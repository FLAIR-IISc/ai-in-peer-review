- Tackles ML learning for high-treewidth undirected models by proving MCMC-based projected gradient descent gives an FPRAS for the MLE when parameters lie in a fast-mixing set Θ.  
- Handles two regimes: unregularized (convex) with objective ε-accuracy in ~ (1/ε)³ transitions and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) with parameter ε-accuracy in ~ (1/ε)² transitions (up to logs).  
- Assumes fast geometric mixing for all θ ∈ Θ (TV distance ≤ Cα^v) and derives explicit schedules (K, M, v) and near-optimal lower bounds.  
- Provides Lipschitz gradient bounds in terms of bounded sufficient statistics and discusses projection onto fast-mixing parameter sets (e.g., Ising spectral-norm constraints).  
- Proof structure: control bias/variance of MCMC gradient estimates via mixing and concentration inequalities, then apply inexact gradient descent results.  
- Theorems are clearly stated and technically plausible, with explicit dependence on C, α, and R, though not all inequalities were fully verified by the reviewer.  
- Analysis is comprehensive: covers convex and strongly convex cases, includes lower bounds, constructive schedules, and practical caveats.  
- Major assumption/limitation: requires knowing/ensuring a fast-mixing Θ and efficiently projecting onto it, which is hard or yields restrictive models in practice.  
- Practical constants (notably dependence on 1/(1−α)) can be enormous, potentially making guarantees vacuous in realistic regimes.  
- Assumes fresh independent MCMC chains per iteration and does not cover persistent or pool-based sampling used in practice.  
- Example provided is illustrative but small; no empirical study of projection vs. optimization trade-offs.  
- Novelty: first to formalize constrained MLE via fast-mixing parameter sets into an FPRAS for bounded-stat exponential families at this level of generality.  
- Significance: conceptually clean connection between optimization guarantees and mixing-time theory, but limited immediate practical impact due to stringent/opaque constraints.  
- Strengths: clear theoretical development, explicit rates and near-matching lower bounds, unifies MCMC mixing and inexact gradient descent ideas.  
- Weaknesses: constructing certified Θ with tractable projection is often infeasible; conservative published mixing bounds; heavy dependence on mixing constants; omission of persistent sampling methods.  
- Open questions for authors: can mixing be enforced via explicit regularizers/constraints and how to tune them vs. fit?  
- Open questions: combined effect of mixing-regularization and ridge regularization—does it over-regularize and how to jointly tune or trade off errors?  
- Open questions: sensitivity to rough upper bounds on C and α and whether adaptive schemes can preserve guarantees.  
- Open questions: can the analysis be extended to persistent chains or sample-pool methods?  
- Minor comments: fix small typos/notation mismatches and explicitly state early that ridge regularization yields strong convexity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid, novel theoretical contribution formalizing when MCMC-based MLE is an FPRAS with constructive schedules and lower bounds.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical applicability, potentially loose constants, and no substantial empirical validation; ignores common persistent MCMC practices.  
- Overall assessment: valuable theoretical step clarifying conditions for MCMC-based learning to be an FPRAS, but requires further work to make it broadly usable in practice.
