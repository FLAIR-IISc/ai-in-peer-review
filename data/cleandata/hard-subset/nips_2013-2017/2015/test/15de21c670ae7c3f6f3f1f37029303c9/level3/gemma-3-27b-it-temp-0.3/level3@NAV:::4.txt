## Review of “Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets”

This paper introduces a novel framework for maximum likelihood learning in graphical models by shifting focus from traditional tractable parameter sets (like tree-structured parameters) to “fast-mixing” parameter sets, where MCMC inference converges rapidly. The core contribution lies in proving fully-polynomial time randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within these fast-mixing sets, utilizing gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The work elegantly extends beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. 

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, building logically from problem formulation to theoretical results. The notation is introduced carefully, and the proof sketches provide valuable intuition. The framing of the problem as learning within a “fast-mixing” model class is original and offers a fresh perspective on tackling intractable inference. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature on maximum likelihood learning, contrasting it with methods like pseudolikelihood and composite likelihoods, and appropriately referencing relevant work (e.g., Jerrum & Sinclair, Chow-Liu). 

The strengths of the paper are undeniable: technical soundness, clarity, and originality. The convergence rate results are meaningful and useful, providing theoretical guarantees for a commonly used practical approach. However, the significance of these results remains somewhat unclear. While the paper fills a gap in the theoretical understanding of learning intractable models using MCMC, the practical impact isn't fully articulated. The example provided is helpful, but a more extensive discussion of potential applications and the conditions under which this approach would be advantageous would strengthen the paper. 

A major point of concern is the interpretation of Theorem 7, which provides a lower bound on the computational effort. This bound is difficult to grasp without a more detailed explanation and broader treatment of lower bounds in this context. Expanding on this aspect, perhaps by comparing it to existing lower bounds for related problems, would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a discussion of potential future extensions, such as exploring different MCMC algorithms or adapting the framework to non-exponential families. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel class of tractable models, strong theoretical results (FPRAS guarantees), clear presentation.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Unclear significance, difficulty interpreting Theorem 7.

**Overall:** Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong theoretical contribution that should be accepted. Addressing the concerns regarding significance and Theorem 7 would further enhance the paper's impact. I recommend the authors add a section discussing potential impact, future work, and follow-on opportunities.
