

This paper introduces a novel framework for maximum likelihood learning in intractable models by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing sets" where MCMC convergence is guaranteed. The authors establish convergence rates for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, showing that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, learning within such a set yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme. The key contribution lies in formalizing the notion of MCMC-approximability of expectations and providing convergence guarantees that bridge the gap between theoretical learning and practical MCMC-based inference.

The paper excels in technical quality, with rigorous proofs establishing meaningful convergence rates in terms of mixing times. The results are original and extend beyond graphical models to general exponential families, making a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning. The presentation is clear and well-organized, with sufficient mathematical detail to enable reproduction.

The main weakness lies in the underdeveloped discussion of significance. While the paper fills an important gap in theoretically justifying common MCMC-based learning practices, it fails to adequately articulate the practical impact, potential applications, or how this framework enables new research directions. The lower bound in Theorem 7 requires substantial expansion to clarify its interpretation and relevance to the main results. The current presentation of the lower bound is difficult to interpret, making it challenging to assess the tightness of the upper bounds.

The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of potential applications in real-world learning problems, how this approach compares to existing methods in terms of computational efficiency, and specific future directions for research. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper's significance argument.

Overall, the theoretical contribution is strong, the results are meaningful, and the framework is novel. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical foundations of learning with intractable models. With minor revisions to clarify the lower bound and expand the discussion of significance, this paper would make an excellent contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with the suggested revisions.
