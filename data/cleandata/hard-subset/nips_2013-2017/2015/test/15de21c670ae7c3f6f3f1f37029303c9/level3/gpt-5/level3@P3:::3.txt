Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning for high-treewidth undirected models (more generally, exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics) when exact gradient computation is intractable. It analyzes the widely used practice of approximating likelihood gradients via MCMC samples and shows that, under a fast-mixing parameter constraint set Θ (i.e., for all θ ∈ Θ, TV distance to stationarity decays geometrically with constants C, α), projected gradient descent with MCMC-based gradients yields a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) to the MLE restricted to Θ. Two regimes are treated separately: the unregularized case (convex objective) and the ridge-regularized case, which makes the objective strongly convex (the paper’s analysis leverages strong convexity; calling this case “non-convex” would be inaccurate). The work builds on classical MCMC-ML (e.g., Geyer; Gu & Zhu), contrasts with surrogate objectives such as pseudolikelihood/score matching/composite likelihood (Besag; Hyvärinen; Lindsay/Varin/Reid/Firth), and relates to structure/parameter constraints for tractability (Chow-Liu trees; high-girth models) and to fast-mixing constructions (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang’s restart chains, spectral/single-site mixing bounds for Ising/Potts, Swendsen-Wang). It formalizes convergence rates by combining Lipschitz gradient bounds, concentration inequalities (Hoeffding, Efron–Stein, Bernstein), standard inexact gradient descent results (Schmidt et al.), and mixing-time assumptions.

Main technical contributions
- Proves that if parameters are constrained to a set Θ on which the chosen Markov chain mixes rapidly (∥Mθ v q − pθ∥TV ≤ Cαv for all q), then MCMC-based projected gradient descent approximates the MLE over Θ with high probability and overall computational effort polynomial in problem constants and 1/ε; hence an FPRAS.
- Provides separate bounds and schedules:
  - Unregularized (convex) case: to reach ε-accuracy in objective value, total transitions scale as ~ (1/ε)³ up to logs.
  - Ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case: to reach ε-accuracy in parameter distance, total transitions scale as ~ (1/ε²) up to logs; mapping between distance and objective suboptimality follows standard strong-convexity/Lipschitz relations.
- Gives Lipschitz constant for the (regularized) gradient in terms of the bound on sufficient statistics, and explicit parameter schedules K, M, v achieving the target accuracy, along with lower bounds indicating near-optimality (up to logarithmic factors).
- Discusses projection onto fast-mixing parameter sets (e.g., Ising with spectral-norm bounds on interaction magnitudes).

Quality
- Technical soundness: Theorems are clearly stated, with proof sketches that follow a standard and plausible template: bias and variance control of gradient errors via mixing and concentration, then inexact gradient descent analysis. The dependence on C, α, and R is explicit. I did not fully verify all inequalities, but the arguments appear consistent.
- Completeness: The analysis covers both convex and strongly convex regimes, includes lower bounds and constructive schedules, and discusses practical caveats (projection cost, sample initialization, non-covered “persistent/pool” sampling strategies).
- Assumptions: The central limitation is the requirement to know/ensure a fast-mixing Θ and to project onto it efficiently. For general models, obtaining useful C, α and a non-trivial Θ with tractable projection is difficult; published bounds can be very conservative.

Clarity
- Generally well written and well organized, with helpful summaries and an illustrative example. Minor typos and occasional notation inconsistencies exist but do not impede understanding. Suggestions: explicitly collect all assumptions at the start of each theorem; clarify which chains (Gibbs vs. subgraph/Swendsen–Wang) are admissible per Θ and how projection depends on the chosen chain.

Originality
- The idea of restricting to a fast-mixing parameter set and then proving an FPRAS for MLE under MCMC-based gradients is, to my knowledge, novel at this level of generality for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics. Prior work either altered the objective (pseudo/composite likelihood), altered the chain to ensure mixing (restart chains), or provided mixing bounds for fixed models. This paper closes a theoretical gap by turning the ubiquitous “run MCMC for gradients” heuristic into a formal FPRAS under explicit mixing constraints.

Significance
- The theoretical statement is clean and could guide future algorithm design by tying statistical optimization guarantees to mixing-time theory. However, practical impact is currently limited by stringent and hard-to-verify constraints on Θ (e.g., small spectral norm of couplings in dense graphs), unknown or loose mixing constants, and the need for efficient projection in general models. As such, the work is an important conceptual step, but further research is needed to make it broadly usable.

Strengths
- Clear, careful theoretical development with explicit rates and near-matching lower bounds.
- Unifies disparate ideas (MCMC mixing, inexact gradient descent) into an FPRAS for constrained MLE.
- Applicability beyond graphical models to bounded-stat exponential families.

Weaknesses and practical concerns
- Realistic construction of Θ with certified C, α and tractable projection is often infeasible or yields overly restrictive parameter sets that harm modeling fidelity.
- Dependence on 1/(1−α) can be enormous in regimes of interest; constants may render the guarantees vacuous.
- Assumes independent fresh chains per iteration; does not cover widely-used persistent or sample-pool schemes.
- The example is illustrative but small; no empirical study of the trade-off between projection-induced bias and optimization accuracy.

Questions for the authors
1) Can the mixing condition be enforced via an explicit regularizer or constraint during learning (e.g., penalizing spectral norms of interaction matrices or contractive Dobrushin-like coefficients), thereby “artificially” inducing fast mixing? If so, how would you choose the penalty strength to balance model fit versus mixing guarantees?
2) If such mixing-regularization is combined with ridge regularization, do you expect over-regularization in practice? Can you provide guidance on joint tuning or a theory that trades off statistical error, optimization error, and mixing?
3) How sensitive are your schedules to rough upper bounds on C and α? Could adaptive schemes (increasing v or M based on diagnostics) preserve guarantees?
4) Can your analysis be extended to persistent chains or sample-pool methods (which are prevalent in practice)?

Minor comments/typos
- A few minor typos/subscript mismatches and occasional missing constants; a careful proofread would help.
- Please emphasize early that ridge regularization yields strong convexity; a couple of places read as if the regularized case might be non-convex.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and novel theoretical contribution that formalizes when MCMC-based MLE is an FPRAS.
- Clean separation of convex vs. strongly convex regimes, with constructive parameter schedules and near-matching lower bounds.
- Broad conceptual relevance to learning with intractable inference.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical applicability is limited: constructing/proving fast-mixing Θ with tractable projection is hard and often unrealistic.
- Constants may be too loose for guidance in real systems; no substantive empirical validation beyond a toy example.
- Does not address common persistent MCMC practices.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-written theoretical paper that clarifies the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning yields an FPRAS. It advances our understanding of learning under intractable inference, but realizing practical impact will require non-trivial follow-up work to design usable fast-mixing constraints/regularizers and to relax the independence and projection assumptions. I view it as a valuable theoretical step with limited immediate practical utility.
