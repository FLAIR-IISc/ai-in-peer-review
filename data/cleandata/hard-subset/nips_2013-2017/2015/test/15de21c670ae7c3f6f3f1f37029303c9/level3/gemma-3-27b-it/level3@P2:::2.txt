This paper proposes a novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in exponential families. The core idea is to restrict parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, guaranteeing rapid convergence of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference, and then leverage this property within a proximal gradient descent framework, ultimately yielding a fully polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). This provides a compelling alternative to traditional methods relying on small-treewidth assumptions for graphical models.

The paper is well-motivated, presenting a clear need for methods that can handle intractable inference during MLE. The authors expertly situate their work within the existing landscape of approaches – pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods, and tree-structured parameter restrictions – and convincingly argue for the benefits of their approach, particularly in scenarios where model specification may be questionable. The theoretical analysis appears solid, though largely represents a nontrivial and insightful combination of established techniques, most notably drawing upon Schmidt et al. (2011) for convergence guarantees. Separate analyses are presented for both convex and strongly convex cases, resulting in different error bounds and computational complexities, showcasing a thorough investigation. The inclusion of a simple empirical demonstration, while not extensive, provides a preliminary validation of the theoretical results.

A significant strength of the paper lies in the authors’ candid discussion of limitations and directions for future work. They acknowledge the challenge of proving fast-mixing for MCMC, effectively shifting the computational burden but not entirely eliminating it. They also thoughtfully consider the connection to contrastive divergence (CD) type algorithms, offering valuable insight into potential extensions and improvements. The discussion regarding the computational cost of projection, and the potential for further optimization, demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the practical implications of their work.

However, the paper suffers from several clarity omissions that, while not fatal, detract from its overall impact. Crucially, the convexity of the log-likelihood is never explicitly stated. While likely obvious to experts, explicitly noting that the Hessian is positive definite (covariance) would significantly improve readability and completeness. Similarly, a concise summary of all key assumptions, ideally in a dedicated section or as a list, would enhance clarity. A significant, though potentially minor, missing assumption is that the parameter set Θ must be convex; this is implicitly used in the analysis but not explicitly stated in the main text. There are also minor typos and awkward phrasing throughout the manuscript (e.g., lines 185, 192, 207, 281, 311-312) that warrant careful attention during the revision process.

The practical impact of this work is somewhat limited. Proving fast-mixing for MCMC remains a notoriously difficult problem, effectively replacing one computational challenge with another. While the reformulation of the problem may provide a useful lens for analyzing existing methods or designing new algorithms, the immediate applicability may be restricted to scenarios where fast-mixing can be demonstrably guaranteed.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** Technically sound and rigorous. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis, and the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature. While largely building upon existing results, the combination and application to this specific problem are novel and insightful.
*   **Clarity:** Generally well-written, but suffers from minor clarity omissions regarding key assumptions and the convexity of the objective function. More explicit statements of assumptions and a concise summary would significantly improve readability.
*   **Originality:** The approach of leveraging fast-mixing parameters and combining it with gradient descent is original. The paper doesn't present entirely new theoretical tools, but provides a novel application and insightful analysis of existing techniques.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in statistical inference – efficient MLE in intractable models. While the practical impact may be limited by the difficulty of verifying fast-mixing, the theoretical contribution is substantial and could potentially inspire further research in this area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous analysis.
*   Novel and insightful approach to approximate MLE.
*   Well-motivated and clearly articulated problem statement.
*   Candid discussion of limitations and future directions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Practical applicability may be limited by the difficulty of verifying fast-mixing.
*   Minor clarity omissions regarding key assumptions and convexity.
*   Relatively limited empirical evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and original contribution to the field. While the practical impact may be somewhat limited, the theoretical results are significant and could potentially inspire further research. The paper is well-written and thoughtfully presented, with a candid discussion of its limitations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity issues mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should explicitly state the convexity of the log-likelihood and provide a concise summary of all key assumptions. Addressing the minor typos would further improve the quality of the manuscript.
