This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth graphical models, where exact inference is intractable. While prior work often restricts models to tractable structures (e.g., trees via the Chow-Liu algorithm) or uses estimators like pseudolikelihood that lack certain guarantees under model misspecification, this work introduces a novel notion of tractability. The authors define a class of "fast-mixing" parameter sets, where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is guaranteed to converge quickly. The main contribution is a formal proof that, for any exponential family constrained to such a set, stochastic gradient descent using MCMC-approximated gradients provides a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution. This provides crucial theoretical grounding for a widely used heuristic.

The paper has several significant strengths. The framing of tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets is an original and elegant conceptual contribution. The theoretical results are technically sound, providing meaningful convergence rates that connect learning guarantees to the mixing time of the underlying MCMC sampler. The presentation is clear and well-organized, making the complex analysis accessible.

The primary weakness lies in the interpretation and discussion of the theoretical bounds. Specifically, the lower bound presented in Theorem 7 is difficult to parse, and its implications are not fully explored. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an expanded discussion on this result, perhaps placing it in the broader context of lower bounds for this class of problems. Additionally, while the paper fills an important theoretical gap, its broader significance and potential impact are not fully articulated. The authors are encouraged to add a discussion on future extensions and follow-on research opportunities that this theoretical framework enables for practitioners and researchers.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel class of tractable models and provides the first formal convergence-rate guarantees for learning intractable models using MCMC-based gradient approximation. Despite the need for a clearer discussion on the lower bounds and potential impact, the originality and technical quality of the contributions are high. This work is a valuable addition to the literature and should be accepted.
