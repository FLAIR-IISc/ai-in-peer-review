This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families (with a focus on high-treewidth Markov networks) when exact gradients are intractable and MCMC-based gradient estimates are used. The main idea is to restrict parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ for which the Markov chain mixes geometrically, and to analyze projected gradient descent with MCMC-based gradients. The authors prove that under bounded sufficient statistics and known mixing constants (C, α), the procedure constitutes an FPRAS for ML within Θ: in the unregularized (convex) case they obtain ε-optimality in log-likelihood with total sampling effort cubic in 1/ε (up to logs), and in the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case they obtain ε-accuracy in parameter distance with quadratic effort. This connects to prior lines of work at NIPS and elsewhere: fast-mixing parameter sets for Ising/Potts (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral conditions; Domke & Liu), MCMC-ML (Geyer; Descombes et al.), contrastive divergence heuristics (Hinton; Tieleman), restart-based fast-mixing learning (Steinhardt & Liang), and convergence of inexact/proximal gradient methods (Schmidt et al.). The framing as an FPRAS under mixing constraints is novel and valuable.

Quality: The technical development appears sound, with plausible use of concentration and inexact gradient descent bounds; I did not verify every proof detail. The dependence on C, α, R, and projection quality is stated clearly. Minor typos exist but do not impede understanding.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized. It would help to explicitly separate assumptions (bounded statistics, projection oracle, known mixing bounds) and to discuss how C and α are obtained in practice for common models.

Originality: Formalizing MCMC-based ML as an FPRAS under fast-mixing constraints is a clean and, to my knowledge, new synthesis relative to prior projection-to-mixing and inexact gradient results.

Significance: The theoretical contribution is solid, but practical impact seems limited: fast-mixing constraints (and their known sufficient conditions) are typically very conservative, and projecting to Θ with nontrivial geometry may itself be costly or model-specific.

Questions and suggestions:
- Can mixing be enforced via an explicit regularizer (e.g., penalties on Dobrushin/spectral norms or restart probabilities), and what is the trade-off when combined with ridge? Risk of over-regularization seems real.
- Can the analysis be extended to persistent-sample “pool” methods used in practice?
- Please clarify how to estimate or adapt to unknown C, α; can schedules be made adaptive?

Pros:
- Clear theoretical contribution and FPRAS guarantees
- Careful treatment of convex vs. strongly convex cases
- Connections to established MCMC and optimization theory

Cons:
- Strong, likely impractical parameter constraints
- Reliance on projection oracles and known mixing rates
- Limited empirical grounding beyond a toy example

Overall: A rigorous and insightful theoretical paper; acceptance depends on committee appetite for primarily conceptual advances with limited near-term applicability.
