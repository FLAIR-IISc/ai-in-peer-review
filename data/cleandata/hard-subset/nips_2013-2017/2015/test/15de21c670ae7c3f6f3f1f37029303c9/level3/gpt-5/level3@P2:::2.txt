Summary
This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a tractability notion based on fast-mixing parameter sets for MCMC, rather than low treewidth or variational surrogates. The authors consider projected (proximal) gradient descent where the intractable model expectation is approximated by sampling from a Markov chain that mixes geometrically fast for all θ in a convex constraint set Θ. Under bounded sufficient statistics and the ability to project onto Θ, they prove high-probability convergence guarantees even with biased, MCMC-based gradients. For the unregularized (merely convex) case, they obtain ε-accuracy in log-likelihood with total MCMC effort O((1/ε)3 up to logs), and for ridge-regularized (λ-strongly convex) objectives, they obtain ε-accuracy in parameter distance with total effort O((1/ε)2 up to logs). These results yield an FPRAS for ML constrained to Θ. The analysis leverages Lipschitz continuity of the gradient, classical fast-mixing bounds (e.g., for Ising with spectral/induced norm constraints), and inexact projected gradient descent bounds from Schmidt et al. (NIPS’11). The paper also connects to prior ideas of learning with fast-mixing models (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang, ICML’15) and projection to fast-mixing sets (Domke & Liu, NIPS’13/’14), and contrasts with pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods, and Chow–Liu trees. A small Ising example illustrates the theory.

Relation to prior work
- Learning by MCMC-approximated ML has a long history (Geyer; Gu & Zhu; Salakhutdinov; Tieleman; Carreira-Perpiñán & Hinton), but typically without nonasymptotic guarantees due to gradient bias. This work gives explicit high-probability guarantees under mixing assumptions.
- It builds on known fast-mixing conditions and projections for Ising/MRFs (Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes; Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke).
- It complements Steinhardt & Liang (fast-mixing via restarts) by treating general exponential families and providing explicit complexity schedules for K, M, v with an FPRAS characterization.
- Compared to tree-structured learning (Chow–Liu) and pseudolikelihood/score matching/composite likelihood, it optimizes the true likelihood within Θ and provides runtime–accuracy tradeoffs.

Strengths
- Clear, principled notion of tractable learning via fast-mixing sets, applicable to general exponential families with bounded statistics.
- Solid technical development with careful use of mixing bounds, concentration, and inexact projected gradient theory to obtain explicit sample/iteration/mixing schedules and FPRAS guarantees.
- Treats both convex and strongly convex cases, with interpretable complexity (cubic vs quadratic in 1/ε).
- Honest discussion of limitations and connections to contrastive divergence and practical sampling strategies.
- Nicely situates the work among alternative tractability assumptions (treewidth vs mixing).

Weaknesses
- Practical impact is limited by the difficulty of certifying/designing Θ: proving fast mixing with known constants C, α and providing efficient projection operators is challenging beyond specific families (e.g., Ising with norm constraints).
- The constants can be pessimistic; the single toy example does not validate tightness or competitiveness versus strong baselines (e.g., pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood) on realistic problems.
- Some assumptions and requirements are scattered; the paper would benefit from a concise “Assumptions” box (bounded t(x), convex Θ, projection oracle, known mixing rate C, α, independence conditions for samples across iterations).
- Minor clarity issues and typos; a few missing or implicit statements (e.g., early, explicit statement of convexity/strong convexity and required convexity of Θ).

Quality
Overall technically sound and careful. The main theorems are plausible consequences of standard tools assembled in a nontrivial way. The reliance on known C, α and availability of projection is a strong assumption; the paper is transparent about this. Proof sketches are helpful; detailed proofs are promised in the appendix.

Clarity
Well written and organized, with a helpful symbol list. Clarity could be improved by:
- Stating early that f(θ) is convex (Hessian of A is the covariance) and λ-strongly convex when λ>0.
- Explicitly stating all assumptions in one place (Θ convex, projection oracle, bounded sufficient statistics, mixing inequality ∥Mvθq − pθ∥TV ≤ Cαv, independence of samples between iterations).
- Briefly discussing how to estimate or upper bound C, α in practice.
- Consistent notation for norms and constants; a small table mapping mixing-time statements to C, α would help.

Originality
The core novelty is in formulating maximum-likelihood learning with biased MCMC gradients under a fast-mixing constraint and proving an FPRAS with explicit complexity schedules for general exponential families. While built from known ingredients, the combination and the guarantees are, to my knowledge, new and conceptually clean.

Significance
Conceptually significant for our understanding of the algorithmic landscape of learning graphical models/exponential families: it provides an alternative tractability axis and a blueprint for end-to-end guarantees when approximate MCMC is used inside ML. Practical significance is more limited until certifiable fast-mixing sets and scalable projections are available for richer models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear theoretical contribution with explicit complexity bounds and high-probability guarantees.
- Broad applicability to exponential families; connects multiple strands of literature.
- Timely and relevant to NIPS (learning, probabilistic modeling, optimization).
- Transparent discussion of limitations; seeds future work on designing certifiable fast-mixing Θ.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical applicability is constrained; verifying mixing and projecting onto Θ is hard in general.
- Experimental validation is minimal; no comparison to standard estimators or study of constants.
- Some clarity/assumption presentation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a concise “Assumptions” section; explicitly require Θ to be convex and projection well-defined.
- State convexity/strong convexity properties up front; clarify Lipschitz constant choices and their practical estimation.
- Provide more empirical illustration: medium-scale Ising/Potts, projection costs, comparisons to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood and CD with fast-mixing regularization.
- Discuss strategies to estimate or upper-bound C, α, and sensitivity to misspecification of mixing rates.
- Clarify independence requirements for samples across iterations; comment on sample-reuse pool strategies and whether variants of the analysis could handle them.

Minor issues and typos
- Minor typos/wording around lines ~185, 192, 207, 281, 311–312.
- Check consistency of constants in Theorems 2/5/6 and symbol list (e.g., R2 vs R^2).
- In the example, justify using L=10 vs theoretical 97, or separate “used vs bound” values.

Overall recommendation
Weak Accept. Conceptually original and technically solid with clear relevance to NIPS, but with limited practical validation and strong assumptions about fast mixing and projection. My confidence: medium.
