

This paper proposes a method for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing set" where MCMC sampling converges quickly. The authors claim to establish theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation, showing polynomial-time complexity for achieving ε-accuracy.

While the problem of learning with intractable likelihoods is important, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings in literature awareness and theoretical novelty. The core ideas are closely related to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics, which have been thoroughly studied (e.g., Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret, 1990). The authors fail to adequately compare their approach with existing methods for optimizing intractable likelihoods, such as contrastive divergence.

The theoretical contributions appear to be largely covered by existing work. The mixing time assumption (Equation 2) is unnecessarily restrictive—Markov chains need not be uniformly ergodic, and reinitializing from a fixed distribution r is not required (reinitializing with previous samples has been extensively analyzed in the literature). The presentation of assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 is unclear, and they should be stated explicitly near the theorems. The strong assumptions in Theorem 6 are unnecessary; more general results exist that do not require the restrictive conditions used.

The paper's novelty is marginal as the results essentially follow from known theory with minor modifications. The authors do not properly acknowledge related work, which is a significant oversight. While the paper provides a clear framework for analyzing MCMC-based learning, the technical contributions are not sufficiently novel for NIPS.

Quality: The technical content is sound but builds on existing theory without significant advances. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical grounding and more comprehensive literature review.

Clarity: The paper would be improved by stating assumptions explicitly near relevant theorems and clarifying the relationship to prior work.

Originality: The novelty is marginal. The approach is a straightforward application of known theory rather than a novel contribution.

Significance: The significance is limited due to the marginal novelty and lack of practical contribution.

Overall, this paper does not meet the bar for NIPS as it fails to demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods and shows insufficient awareness of relevant prior literature.
