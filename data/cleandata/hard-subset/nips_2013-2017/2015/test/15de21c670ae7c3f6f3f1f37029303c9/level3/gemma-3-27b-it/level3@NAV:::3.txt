## Review of “Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, particularly when inference is intractable due to high treewidth. The authors move beyond restricting parameters to tractable sets like trees and instead focus on “fast-mixing parameters” – those for which MCMC converges rapidly. The core contribution is proving that, under this fast-mixing condition, MCMC-based gradient descent constitutes a Fully Polynomial Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation. The analysis is provided for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (non-convex) cases.

The work builds upon established challenges in ML learning, specifically the NP-hardness of exact gradient computation and the common reliance on MCMC for approximation. It effectively positions itself against alternatives like pseudolikelihood and composite likelihoods, highlighting their potential shortcomings in terms of sample complexity and inference tractability post-learning. The paper clearly articulates the connection to existing work on mixing times and provides examples of models where fast mixing can be guaranteed, such as the Ising model.

The paper's strength lies in its rigorous theoretical formalization of when MCMC-based learning yields a FPRAS. The separate analyses for convex and non-convex cases demonstrate careful consideration of different scenarios. The writing is generally clear, and the proofs, while not fully verified by this reviewer, appear plausible. 

However, a significant weakness is the practical relevance of the theoretical results. The parameter constraints required for the theorems – guaranteeing rapid MCMC mixing – are likely difficult to satisfy in many real-world applications. This severely limits the immediate applicability of the findings. The discussion acknowledges this, but the proposed direction of artificially enforcing the mixing condition through regularization raises concerns about potential over-regularization when combined with ridge regression. The authors also don't delve into the computational cost of *verifying* the mixing condition itself. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Solid theoretical contribution establishing a FPRAS under specific conditions.
* Clear presentation and well-defined problem statement.
* Rigorous analysis with separate results for convex and non-convex settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Limited practical applicability due to the stringent mixing condition.
* Unclear path towards making the results usable in real-world scenarios.
* Projection complexity not thoroughly addressed. 

**Overall:** This is a theoretically sound paper that provides a valuable, albeit currently limited, contribution to the field. Significant further research is needed to bridge the gap between theory and practice, potentially exploring methods for enforcing the mixing condition without excessive regularization, and addressing the computational cost of checking the mixing conditions in the first place. I lean towards **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised in the discussion, perhaps in a response to reviewers.
