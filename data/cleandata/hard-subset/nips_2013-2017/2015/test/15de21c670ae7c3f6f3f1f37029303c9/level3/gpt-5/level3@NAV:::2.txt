This paper proposes a principled, computationally efficient approach to approximate maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in exponential families by constraining parameters to a fast-mixing set for MCMC and performing projected proximal gradient descent with inexact (MCMC) gradients. Building on Schmidt et al. (2011) for inexact proximal gradients, the authors obtain an FPRAS: O(ε^-3 polylog) effort to achieve ε suboptimality in likelihood for the convex case, and O(ε^-2 polylog) to achieve ε parameter error under ridge regularization (strong convexity). The analysis applies to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics and includes a simple Ising-grid experiment illustrating the guarantees. The work is a well-motivated alternative to small-treewidth restrictions and relates to fast-mixing parameter projections (e.g., Domke & Liu) and recent work on learning fast-mixing models (Steinhardt & Liang), as well as classical MCMC-MLE and CD-type methods.

Quality: The theoretical development appears careful and technically sound, combining mixing-time bounds, concentration, and proximal gradient analyses in a nontrivial way. Assumptions are somewhat pessimistic but are discussed candidly. The experiment is illustrative but minimal.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized, but there are important omissions. The paper should explicitly state that the negative log-likelihood is convex (e.g., A(θ) is convex since its Hessian is a covariance). All assumptions should be summarized in one place: bounded statistics, convex Θ, projection oracle, known uniform mixing constants (C, α) over Θ, step-size/schedule choices, and independence of samples per iteration. The main text should also state the convexity of Θ (currently missing). Minor typos/wording: l185, l192, l207, l281, l311–12.

Originality: The FPRAS framing for constrained MLE under fast mixing is novel and insightful, though the proof strategy is largely a sophisticated synthesis of existing techniques. The connection to CD-type algorithms is interesting even if the analyzed algorithm is not exactly CD.

Significance: The conceptual contribution is substantial for theory-minded audiences. Practical impact is limited: certifying fast mixing for realistic models is difficult, arguably trading one hard problem for another, although the reformulation may still guide algorithm design and regularization.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, rigorous theory with explicit rates in both convex and strongly convex regimes
- General exponential-family scope; connects multiple literatures
- Honest discussion of limitations; potential to inform CD-like methods

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited practical utility absent verifiable fast-mixing certificates
- Experimental validation minimal
- Missing clarity on core assumptions and convexity statements

Overall: Original and substantial theoretical contribution; I lean toward a weak accept for a theory track, contingent on addressing clarity issues.
