This paper proposes a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where inference is typically intractable. The authors introduce the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" and show that if Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution, then a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) exists for the maximum likelihood solution.

The main contribution of this paper is the proof that, under certain conditions, MCMC-based weight learning algorithms can be considered a FPRAS. The authors derive separate theoretical bounds for the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized (non-convex) case. The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear plausible, although they have not been fully verified by this reviewer.

The paper's results are significant because they provide a theoretical foundation for the use of MCMC-based methods in weight learning. However, there are concerns about the practical relevance of the results. The parameter constraints required for the theorems are unlikely to be satisfied in practice, which limits the applicability of the results.

One open question raised by this paper is whether it is possible to enforce the mixing condition artificially, effectively using it as a form of regularization. This could potentially be combined with ridge regression, but the authors warn that this might over-regularize the model.

In conclusion, this paper provides a solid theoretical formalization of when MCMC-based weight learning yields a FPRAS. However, significant non-trivial research is needed to make the results practically useful. The authors' contribution is a valuable step towards understanding the theoretical foundations of MCMC-based methods, but further work is required to address the practical concerns and limitations of the approach.
