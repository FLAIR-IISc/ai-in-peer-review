- Proposes learning exponential-family models by restricting parameters to “fast-mixing” sets and running projected (proximal) gradient descent with MCMC-approximated likelihood gradients.  
- Main theoretical contribution: finite-time analysis giving an FPRAS for MLE within constrained Θ — cubic 1/ε effort for convex case and quadratic 1/ε effort for strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case.  
- Results hold for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models.  
- Key technical steps: show Lipschitz smoothness of regularized negative log-likelihood, control MCMC bias via total-variation mixing-time bounds, and apply inexact projected gradient descent analysis (Schmidt et al. NIPS’11).  
- Provide concrete schedules for iterations, samples per iteration, and chain length to ensure high-probability guarantees; illustrate theory with an Ising toy example.  
- Contrasts with “tractable by structure” approaches; frames tractability via rapid mixing, aligning with Domke & Liu (NIPS’13) and Steinhardt & Liang (ICML’15) but targeting exact MLE with runtime guarantees.  
- Formalizes MCMC-based gradient estimation heuristics by quantifying both finite-time bias and finite-sample variance.  
- Complements other estimators (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood) which optimize different criteria and may not recover the M-projection.  
- Strength: clear, principled framing that makes MCMC-based MLE algorithmically analyzable and yields an FPRAS under standard assumptions.  
- Strength: general analysis across exponential families and samplers, not limited to specific graph structures.  
- Strength: solid technical development with Lipschitz bounds, bias/variance control, provable schedules, and near-matching lower bounds (up to logs).  
- Strength: honest discussion of limitations and practical guidance on projection operators for Ising/MRFs.  
- Weakness: practical impact limited by difficulty of certifying fast mixing and computing explicit mixing constants (C, α) for realistic models/samplers.  
- Weakness: FPRAS utility depends on having an efficient projection onto Θ, which can be hard beyond special norms.  
- Weakness: minimal empirical evaluation; no comparisons to pseudolikelihood, score matching, CD, or persistent sample pools.  
- Weakness: assumptions and prerequisites are somewhat scattered; clearer upfront statement would help readability.  
- Weakness: minor clarity/typo issues throughout the manuscript.  
- Quality: technically sound with appropriate concentration inequalities; full proofs in appendix need checking; availability of (C, α) and projection oracle is a strong precondition.  
- Clarity: generally well written; suggests early explicit summary of assumptions and more guidance on obtaining (C, α) in practice.  
- Originality: novel synthesis of optimizing within fast-mixing sets plus FPRAS guarantees, despite using known components.  
- Significance: conceptually important reframing of tractability from structure to sampler dynamics, but practical uptake may be limited by certifiability and projection issues.  
- Suggested experiments: compare to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood/CD/persistent-CD on small/medium problems reporting wall-clock vs accuracy.  
- Suggested extensions: provide more examples where Θ and projection are efficient beyond Ising; analyze sensitivity to misspecified (C, α) and adaptive schemes to estimate mixing rates.  
- Suggested algorithmic clarifications: clarify step-size choice (1/L) and discuss backtracking/adaptive steps; analyze sample-pool strategies to relax independence across iterations.  
- Minor fixes: correct listed typos, define symbols at first use, make Algorithm 1 self-contained, and state convexity of Θ explicitly.  
- Pros for acceptance: theoretically solid, novel perspective with FPRAS guarantees, broad applicability, and relevance to theory/inference communities.  
- Cons for acceptance: challenges in verifying fast mixing and projection in practice, limited empirical validation, and primarily theoretical synthesis rather than new impactful algorithms.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept.
