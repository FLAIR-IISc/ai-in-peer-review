- Paper studies ML learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ where MCMC mixes geometrically.  
- Analyzes projected gradient descent using MCMC-estimated gradients and proves convergence to the MLE restricted to Θ with high probability.  
- For convex (unregularized) case: ε-accuracy in objective with total MCMC effort ≈ O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)).  
- For strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case: ε-accuracy in parameter distance with effort ≈ O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)).  
- Establishes Lipschitz continuity of the log-likelihood gradient under bounded sufficient statistics.  
- Provides explicit schedules for iterations (K), chain length (v), and sample size (M), and FPRAS-type guarantees.  
- Proves complementary lower bounds (Theorems 3 and 7) to frame near-optimality; Theorem 7 is dense and needs clearer intuition.  
- Assumptions are clear: bounded t(x), convex fast-mixing Θ with known projection, and uniform geometric mixing ∥Mv_θ q − pθ∥TV ≤ Cα^v.  
- Analysis carefully separates bias (finite v) and variance (finite M) of gradient estimates and uses standard concentration inequalities.  
- Results are general to exponential families, not tied to specific graphical-model tractable classes.  
- Novelty: frames tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets and gives end-to-end MCMC-based ML guarantees with explicit schedules and lower bounds.  
- Significance: fills theoretical gap for MCMC-based ML by providing a principled recipe to obtain provable convergence despite biased gradients.  
- Main limitations: independence of fresh samples per iteration (not persistent chains), cost/availability of projections, and practicality of constants C and α.  
- Empirical validation is minimal; practical impact depends on constructing Θ and estimating mixing constants for real models.  
- Suggested clarity improvements: expand intuition for Theorem 7, summarize constant dependencies (R, C, α, L, λ) in a table, and show typical scales for common models.  
- Suggested extensions: discuss constructing Θ/projections for non-binary or continuous families, guidance for estimating C and α, and analysis for persistent chains or sample pools.  
- Additional useful directions: adaptive schedules based on diagnostics, comparison to surrogate estimators in sample vs computational complexity, and discussion of trade-offs between Θ restrictiveness and modeling capacity.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — solid, original theoretical contribution with practical caveats and requested clarifications to strengthen impact.
