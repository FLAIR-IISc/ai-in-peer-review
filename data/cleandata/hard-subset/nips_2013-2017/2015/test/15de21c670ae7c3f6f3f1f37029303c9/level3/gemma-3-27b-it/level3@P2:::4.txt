This paper introduces a novel framework for learning in intractable graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, guaranteeing rapid convergence of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with gradients approximated by sampling within this set, establishing fully-polynomial time randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and technically sound, offering a significant contribution to the field of statistical learning.

The core idea – leveraging fast mixing properties as a notion of tractability – is original and fills a gap in the existing literature. Traditional approaches often focus on tree-structured parameters or other simplifying assumptions. This work provides an alternative, more general framework applicable to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models. The theoretical results, particularly the FPRAS guarantees, are strong and well-justified. The paper successfully connects concepts from statistical physics (mixing times) with learning theory (convergence rates), demonstrating a sophisticated understanding of both areas. The connection to existing work – such as pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods – is clearly articulated, highlighting the advantages of the proposed approach.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The clarity of the presentation is commendable. Notation is introduced systematically, and the mathematical derivations appear rigorous. The inclusion of a concrete example (Ising model on a 4x4 grid) helps to illustrate the practical implications of the theoretical results, even acknowledging a degree of conservatism in the theoretical bounds. The authors are careful to discuss the limitations of their analysis, such as the assumption of independent samples and the potential complexity of the projection step. They also address potential avenues for future work, including exploring the use of sample pools and the impact of initialization strategies.

However, there is a notable weakness concerning the lower bound presented in Theorem 7. The reviewer found this result difficult to interpret and its implications somewhat opaque. While the authors attempt to contextualize it, a more in-depth discussion of its significance, and perhaps a more intuitive reformulation, would be beneficial. A broader treatment of lower bounds in this context, perhaps contrasting it with existing lower bounds for other learning algorithms, would strengthen the paper. Specifically, relating this lower bound to the inherent difficulty of the problem – and explaining *why* this computational cost is unavoidable – would be very valuable.

Regarding the four NIPS review criteria:

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The proofs appear rigorous, and the assumptions are clearly stated. It’s a complete piece of work that honestly evaluates both strengths and weaknesses.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the derivations are easy to follow. The example provided further enhances the clarity.

**Originality:** The problem formulation and the proposed approach are novel. While building on existing techniques, the authors offer a significant contribution by introducing the concept of “fast-mixing parameter sets” and establishing associated convergence guarantees. The work appears distinct from previous NIPS proceedings.

**Significance:** The significance of the results is somewhat unclear, but appears potentially high. The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in learning with intractable models. While the immediate practical impact might not be obvious, the theoretical framework and the FPRAS guarantees suggest that this work could pave the way for more efficient learning algorithms in a variety of applications. Expanding the discussion on potential impact, future extensions, and follow-on opportunities would further highlight the significance of this work.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and original approach to learning in intractable models.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees (FPRAS) for convergence.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a fundamental challenge in statistical learning.
*   The framework is general and applies to a wide range of exponential families.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lower bound (Theorem 7) is difficult to interpret and could benefit from further discussion and clarification.
*   The discussion of practical impact could be expanded.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weakness concerning the lower bound, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The originality of the approach, the strength of the theoretical results, and the clarity of the presentation all strongly support acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2015.
