This paper addresses the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, where exact computation is intractable. The authors focus on the scenario where MCMC is used to approximate the likelihood gradient, a common practice lacking strong theoretical guarantees. Their key contribution is a proof demonstrating that, under the condition of “fast-mixing parameters” – parameters for which MCMC converges quickly – gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients provides a fully polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within the constrained parameter space. They analyze both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, deriving corresponding convergence rates.

The paper builds on existing work in several areas. It acknowledges the intractability results of Jerrum and Sinclair [1993] regarding the Ising model and the #P-completeness of partition function evaluation. It contrasts its approach with alternative methods like pseudolikelihood [3, 16, 22], composite likelihoods [20, 30], and parallel learning [2014, 2006], highlighting their limitations in sample complexity or applicability to mis-specified models. The work positions itself within the broader context of restricting parameters to tractable sets, like tree-structured parameters (Chow-Liu [1968]), and extends this notion to the more general condition of fast mixing. The analysis draws heavily on results related to MCMC convergence times [19] and stochastic gradient descent [26].

The strengths of this paper are significant. The theoretical result establishing a FPRAS for MCMC-based learning under fast-mixing conditions is a valuable contribution to the field. The rigorous analysis of both convex and strongly convex cases provides a nuanced understanding of the convergence properties. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents its core ideas. The inclusion of an example (though limited) helps to illustrate the concepts. The thorough referencing of related work demonstrates a strong grasp of the literature. The authors also appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach in the discussion section and propose potential avenues for future work.

However, the paper also has notable weaknesses. The central assumption of “fast-mixing parameters” is a major practical concern. The conditions required for this assumption to hold, as discussed in Section 3.1 and illustrated by the example, are often restrictive and unlikely to be satisfied in real-world applications. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper doesn’t offer concrete guidance on *how* to enforce this condition – beyond simply constraining parameter values. The concluding discussion briefly mentions artificially enforcing the mixing condition as a regularization strategy but doesn’t explore this in detail. The potential for over-regularization when combined with ridge regression is also a valid concern.

Furthermore, while the proofs are sketched and appear plausible, a full, detailed verification is challenging without substantial effort. The paper relies on existing results regarding mixing times, which themselves can be complex to apply in practice. The analysis focuses heavily on the theoretical guarantees and doesn’t provide much insight into the practical performance of the algorithm. The example provided, while helpful, is relatively simple and doesn’t showcase the scalability or robustness of the approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical result: Establishes a FPRAS for MCMC-based learning under a well-defined condition.
*   Rigorous analysis: Provides a detailed analysis of convergence rates for both convex and strongly convex cases.
*   Clear presentation: The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   Addresses an important problem: Tackles the challenging problem of learning in intractable graphical models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong assumption: The fast-mixing parameter condition is likely impractical in many real-world scenarios.
*   Limited practical guidance: The paper doesn’t offer concrete methods for enforcing the fast-mixing condition.
*   Proof complexity: Detailed verification of the proofs is challenging.
*   Lack of empirical validation: Limited empirical evaluation of the algorithm's performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of graphical model learning. However, its practical relevance is questionable due to the restrictive nature of the core assumption. While the result is mathematically interesting, significant further research is needed to bridge the gap between theory and practice. I would rate this paper as a **weak accept**. If the authors can address the concerns regarding the fast-mixing condition – for example, by proposing a practical regularization strategy to enforce it – the paper would be significantly strengthened. As it stands, the contribution is more of a theoretical curiosity than a readily applicable algorithm. I encourage the authors to explore these avenues in future work.
