This paper presents a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by leveraging the concept of fast-mixing parameter sets. The authors prove that when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, gradient descent with gradients approximated by Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling can approximate the maximum likelihood solution inside the set with high probability.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The proofs are plausible, although not fully verified by this reviewer. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, and their notation is well-defined and easy to understand.

The main contribution of this paper is the proof that MCMC-based weight learning is a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set. This result is significant, as it provides a theoretical guarantee for the convergence of MCMC-based weight learning algorithms.

The authors also derive separate theoretical bounds for the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized (non-convex) case, which is a nice touch. The analysis is thorough, and the authors provide a clear discussion of the implications of their results.

However, there are some concerns regarding the practical relevance of the results. The parameter constraints required for the theorems are unlikely to be satisfied in practice, which limits the applicability of the results. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear answer to the question of whether it is possible to enforce the mixing condition artificially, which is an important open question.

Overall, this paper presents a solid theoretical formalization of when MCMC-based weight learning yields a FPRAS. While the results are significant, they require significant non-trivial research to make them practically useful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence of MCMC-based weight learning algorithms.
3. The results are significant and provide a theoretical guarantee for the convergence of MCMC-based weight learning algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The parameter constraints required for the theorems are unlikely to be satisfied in practice, which limits the applicability of the results.
2. The authors do not provide a clear answer to the question of whether it is possible to enforce the mixing condition artificially.
3. The results require significant non-trivial research to make them practically useful.
