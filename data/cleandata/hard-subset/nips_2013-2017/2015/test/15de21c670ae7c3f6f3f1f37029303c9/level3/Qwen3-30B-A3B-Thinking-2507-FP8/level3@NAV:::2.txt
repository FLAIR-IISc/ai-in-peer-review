

This paper presents a novel approach to maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in exponential families by leveraging fast-mixing Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. The authors propose an alternative to traditional small-treewidth assumptions, instead constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing set" where MCMC converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. By combining proximal gradient descent with Schmidt et al. (2011), they establish a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for MLE within this parameter set. The theoretical contributions include convergence guarantees for both convex (log-likelihood error) and strongly convex (parameter error) cases, with complexity bounds of O((1/ε)³) and O((1/ε)²) respectively.

The paper's strengths are significant: the theoretical analysis is technically sound, well-motivated, and represents a nontrivial combination of existing techniques. The authors demonstrate clear awareness of limitations, candidly discussing overly pessimistic assumptions and practical challenges. The simple experiment successfully validates the theoretical claims.

However, several important weaknesses undermine the paper's practical impact. Crucially, the paper fails to explicitly state that the log-likelihood is convex (a key property proven via the Hessian = covariance), and omits the necessary assumption that Θ must be convex. While the approach is theoretically elegant, the practical utility is limited because proving fast-mixing for MCMC remains challenging in practice, effectively replacing one difficult problem with another. The paper also contains minor but distracting typos and wording errors.

The contribution is original in its theoretical framework, extending beyond graphical models to general exponential families. The analysis builds upon Schmidt et al. (2011) and mixing time literature, but the combination represents a meaningful contribution. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the practical implementation challenges. The paper would benefit from clearer exposition of convexity and the convexity assumption for Θ.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper with substantial theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of MLE approximation. While the practical value may be limited due to the difficulty of verifying fast-mixing conditions, the theoretical framework provides valuable insights for future work. The paper is well-written and makes a solid contribution to the literature, though it would be strengthened by addressing the clarity issues noted.
