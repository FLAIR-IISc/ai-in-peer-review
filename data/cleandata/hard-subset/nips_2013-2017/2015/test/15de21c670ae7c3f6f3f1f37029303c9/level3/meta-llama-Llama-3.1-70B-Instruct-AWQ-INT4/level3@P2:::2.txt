This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in exponential families by assuming that Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is fast-mixing. The authors combine proximal gradient descent with the technique of Schmidt et al. (2011) to obtain a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). The paper provides guarantees for both convex (likelihood error) and strongly convex (parameter error) cases, and includes a simple experiment demonstrating the theory.

The paper is well-motivated, as it provides an alternative to the common assumption of small treewidth in graphical models. The theoretical analyses appear solid, although they are largely a nontrivial combination of existing techniques. The authors candidly discuss the limitations of their approach, including overly pessimistic theoretical assumptions, and provide directions for future work. The paper also offers insight into contrastive divergence (CD)-type algorithms, although the algorithm analyzed is not exactly CD.

However, there are some important omissions in the paper. For example, the authors never explicitly state that the log-likelihood is convex, which is a crucial assumption in their analysis. They should note that the log-partition function A(θ) is convex, which can be shown via its Hessian being equal to the covariance of the sufficient statistics. Additionally, the authors should summarize all their assumptions, including the assumption that the parameter set Θ must be convex, which is not stated in the main text.

The practical impact of the paper is limited because proving fast-mixing for MCMC is generally hard, effectively replacing one difficult problem with another. However, the reformulation of the problem may still be useful in certain contexts. There are also some minor typos and wording errors noted in the paper (lines l185, l192, l207, l281, l311-12).

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors have made a clear effort to provide a thorough analysis of their approach. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of their results, as well as a more thorough comparison with existing methods.
