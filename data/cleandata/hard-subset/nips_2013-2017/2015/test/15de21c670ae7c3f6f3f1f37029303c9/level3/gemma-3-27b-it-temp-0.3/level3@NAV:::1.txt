## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a framework for maximum likelihood learning in graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, guaranteeing rapid convergence of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The authors aim to provide theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, achieving fully-polynomial time randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within the constrained parameter space.

The core idea – leveraging fast mixing to enable theoretically grounded MCMC-based learning – is interesting, particularly given the challenges of learning in high-treewidth models. However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of awareness of prior work. The introduction briefly mentions alternative approaches like pseudolikelihood and composite likelihoods, but fails to adequately position the proposed method within the broader landscape of intractable likelihood optimization. Crucially, the connection to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics is not acknowledged, despite being central to the methodology. 

The proposed algorithm is closely related to these established techniques, and the authors should thoroughly compare their method to existing work in this area. Existing theory, particularly concerning optimizing intractable likelihoods using stochastic gradient methods and the analysis of Markov chain convergence, already covers many of the authors’ results and generalizes to broader scenarios. For instance, the assumptions regarding Markov chain ergodicity are unnecessarily restrictive; uniformly ergodic chains are not required, and re-initializing at a fixed distribution is not a necessity. Re-initializing with the previous sample, a well-studied approach, might even be preferable. 

Furthermore, the presentation is lacking in clarity. The assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 are not explicitly stated near the theorems themselves, hindering readability and understanding. The strong assumptions used for Theorem 6 are also unnecessarily restrictive, and the authors should cite Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret for more general results. Overall, the novelty of the work appears marginal given the existing literature. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The idea of leveraging fast-mixing parameters for theoretical guarantees in MCMC-based learning is conceptually sound.
*   The paper provides a formal analysis of the convergence properties of gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Major lack of awareness of prior literature on related algorithms and theory.
*   Limited novelty due to the close relationship to existing work on Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation.
*   Unnecessary and incorrect assumptions.
*   Poor presentation and lack of clarity in stating assumptions.
*   The theoretical results are largely covered by existing theory.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a technically sound analysis, the lack of engagement with relevant literature and the marginal novelty significantly detract from its contribution. A substantial revision addressing these points, including a thorough comparison to existing work and a clarification of the assumptions, would be necessary for reconsideration.
