

This paper proposes using fast-mixing parameter sets to enable maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth graphical models, arguing that when parameters are constrained to a set where MCMC mixes rapidly, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients converges to an approximate maximum likelihood solution with theoretical guarantees. The authors prove that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, their approach yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution inside the fast-mixing set.

While the theoretical results are technically sound and the paper correctly identifies the importance of mixing times for MCMC-based learning, the work suffers from significant shortcomings in literature awareness and novelty. The authors fail to acknowledge that their approach is closely related to established methods in the literature, particularly Monte Carlo EM (Dempster et al., 1977) and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics (Benveniste et al., 1987; Chen, 1995). The key result that gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients converges under fast-mixing conditions is already well-established in the literature on stochastic optimization with inexact gradients. The authors' "novel" contribution is largely a restatement of known results with unnecessary additional assumptions.

Specifically, the paper makes several incorrect and unnecessary assumptions. The authors assume uniform ergodicity (Equation 2) and require reinitializing MCMC chains from a fixed distribution r at every iteration. However, as demonstrated by Chen (1995), the more natural and practical approach is to initialize from the previous sample, which has been extensively analyzed in the context of contrastive divergence (Hinton, 2002) and is known to improve mixing behavior. The paper's assumption that the Markov chain must be uniformly ergodic is also incorrect—non-uniform ergodicity is sufficient for convergence under appropriate conditions, as shown by Benveniste et al. (1987) and others.

The presentation of theorems is particularly problematic. The assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 are not clearly stated near the theorems themselves, making it difficult to assess the conditions under which the results hold. The authors' analysis of the strongly convex case (Theorem 6) relies on unnecessarily strong assumptions that have already been relaxed in the literature. For instance, the paper assumes that the error at each iteration is bounded with high probability, whereas Benveniste et al. (1987) provide more general convergence results for stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics without requiring such strong concentration bounds.

The paper's claim of novelty is further undermined by the lack of comparison to existing work on optimizing intractable likelihoods. The authors should have compared their approach to methods like pseudolikelihood (Besag, 1974), score matching (Hyvärinen, 2005), and composite likelihoods (Lindsay, 1988), which are standard approaches for learning intractable models. While the paper mentions some of these methods in the introduction, it fails to provide a meaningful comparison of computational complexity, statistical efficiency, or practical applicability.

The significance of the work is limited by its purely theoretical nature with minimal practical demonstration. The example in Section 6 shows a simple Ising model experiment but fails to demonstrate any practical advantage over existing methods. The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 7, noting their work "claims no significant practical contribution," which further diminishes the paper's impact.

The quality of the technical content is solid, with correct proofs for the stated results. However, the paper's value is significantly reduced by the lack of awareness of prior literature and the unnecessary assumptions. The authors' analysis would be much stronger if they had properly situated their work within the existing literature on stochastic optimization with inexact gradients.

In terms of clarity, the paper suffers from poor organization and presentation. Key assumptions are scattered throughout the text rather than stated explicitly at the beginning of each theorem. The relationship between the theoretical results and practical implementation is not well-explained, and the experimental section is minimal.

Regarding originality, the core idea of using fast-mixing conditions for MCMC-based learning is not novel. The paper's main contribution appears to be a more restrictive analysis of an already well-studied approach, rather than a genuinely new method or theoretical insight. The authors do not clearly articulate how their work differs from or improves upon existing results in the literature.

In summary, this paper makes a marginal contribution to the field. The theoretical results are correct but largely redundant with existing literature on stochastic optimization with Markovian dynamics. The paper's assumptions are unnecessarily strong, the presentation is poor, and the connection to prior work is insufficient. While the work is technically sound, it does not advance the state of the art in a meaningful way.

Pro: Correct theoretical results for the restricted setting, clear mathematical formulation of the problem.

Con: Major lack of awareness of relevant literature, unnecessary assumptions, marginal novelty, poor presentation of assumptions, minimal practical contribution.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Properly citing and comparing to the extensive literature on stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics (Benveniste et al., 1987; Chen, 1995)
2. Relaxing the unnecessary assumptions (uniform ergodicity, fixed initialization)
3. Clarifying the assumptions for each theorem
4. Demonstrating a clear practical advantage over existing methods
5. Providing a more meaningful comparison to alternative approaches for learning intractable models

Given these shortcomings, I recommend rejection. While the technical content is sound, the lack of novelty and insufficient engagement with prior work prevent this from being a significant contribution to NIPS.
