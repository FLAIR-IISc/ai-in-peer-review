This paper addresses the problem of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models where exact inference is intractable due to high treewidth. The standard approach to this problem is to either restrict the model parameters to a structurally simple set (e.g., tree-structured models, as in the Chow-Liu algorithm) or to use proxy objective functions (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching) that are easier to optimize but may not converge to the M-projection under model misspecification.

The authors propose a fundamentally different notion of a tractable parameter set: a set of "fast-mixing" parameters where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are guaranteed to converge to the stationary distribution at a geometric rate. The main contribution of the paper is to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the widely used, but often heuristic, practice of performing gradient descent where the gradient (which depends on intractable expectations) is approximated using samples from an MCMC chain. The paper proves that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, constraining the parameters to a fast-mixing set allows for a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within that set. The analysis provides explicit convergence rates and schedules for setting the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and MCMC transitions to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. Separate, rigorous results are provided for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) optimization problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The core idea of defining tractability through the lens of MCMC mixing time, rather than graphical structure like treewidth, is novel and insightful. This provides a new perspective on how to constrain complex models to make learning feasible. The theoretical results that formalize the convergence of MCMC-based gradient descent in this setting are, to my knowledge, the first of their kind and represent a significant conceptual contribution.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The authors provide detailed proofs and analyses for their claims, culminating in concrete convergence guarantees (Theorems 5 and 9). The results are meaningful; they connect the optimization error to the key parameters of the problem: the mixing rate of the chain (C, α), the number of gradient steps (K), samples (M), and transitions (v). The careful separation of the analysis into convex and strongly convex cases demonstrates a thorough treatment of the problem.

*   **Significance:** This work fills an important theoretical gap. MCMC-based gradient approximations are ubiquitous in the training of energy-based models and Markov Random Fields, yet they often lack formal guarantees. This paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for this practice, showing under what conditions and with what schedule of hyperparameters convergence can be guaranteed. This could guide practitioners in developing more principled versions of algorithms like contrastive divergence and provides a framework for future theoretical analysis of related methods.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem setup, assumptions, and main results are presented clearly. The inclusion of a background section on mixing times is helpful for readers not deeply familiar with that literature.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The primary weakness of the paper is in the presentation and discussion of the lower bound for the strongly convex case.

*   **Lower Bound Interpretation (Theorem 7):** The lower bound on computational effort in Theorem 7 is difficult to interpret as presented. The expression is complex, and the paper does not provide sufficient discussion to help the reader understand its implications. For example, how tight is this bound compared to the upper bound derived in Theorem 8? What does it tell us about the fundamental complexity of the problem? A more detailed discussion, perhaps with a simplified version under certain assumptions or a qualitative explanation of the dependencies on key parameters (ε, λ, etc.), would greatly enhance the paper's contribution. A broader treatment of how this lower bound relates to known lower bounds for stochastic optimization would also be valuable context.

*   **Discussion on Impact:** While the work appears significant, the paper would be stronger if it included a more detailed discussion of its potential impact and avenues for future work. For example, the authors could elaborate on how these theoretical results might influence the design of practical learning algorithms. Could this framework be extended to analyze more complex MCMC sampling strategies, such as those that reuse samples between iterations (a common heuristic)? Explicitly outlining promising follow-on research opportunities would help the community build upon this foundational work.

In summary:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel and valuable concept of a "fast-mixing" tractable model class.
2.  Provides the first rigorous convergence guarantees for maximum likelihood learning using MCMC-based gradient descent in this setting.
3.  The results are technically sound, complete, and fill a significant gap in the literature.
4.  The paper is clearly written and well-structured.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The lower bound in Theorem 7 is not well-explained, making it difficult to assess its tightness and implications.
2.  The paper could do more to articulate the broader significance and potential practical impact of its findings.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, technical quality, and the importance of the problem it addresses—far outweigh its weaknesses. The suggested improvements are addressable in a revision and would further strengthen an already excellent paper. I recommend acceptance.
