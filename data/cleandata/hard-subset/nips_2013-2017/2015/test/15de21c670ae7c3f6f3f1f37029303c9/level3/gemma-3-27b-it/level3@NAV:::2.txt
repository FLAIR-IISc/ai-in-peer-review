## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximating Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) in exponential families. The core idea is to constrain parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, allowing for theoretical guarantees on gradient descent using MCMC-approximated gradients. This offers an alternative to traditional methods relying on low-treewidth assumptions, a significant motivation given the computational intractability of MLE in high-treewidth graphical models. The authors establish Fully Polynomial Time Randomized Approximation Schemes (FPRAS) for both convex and strongly convex cases, supported by a simple experimental demonstration.

The work is well-motivated and demonstrates a solid understanding of the existing literature on MLE, composite likelihoods, and MCMC methods. The approach of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets is a conceptually interesting alternative, and the connection to projected gradient descent with error analysis is clearly articulated, largely building upon existing results from Schmidt et al. (2011). The authors are commendably upfront about limitations, acknowledging pessimistic theoretical assumptions and outlining potential future research directions, including the connection to Contrastive Divergence (CD) type algorithms. 

However, the practical impact of the work appears limited. While theoretically sound, the reliance on proving fast-mixing for MCMC is a substantial hurdle. This essentially shifts the computational difficulty from MLE to characterizing the mixing properties of the chosen parameter set, which is often as challenging. Furthermore, several clarity issues detract from the presentation. The paper never explicitly states the convexity of the log-likelihood (implicitly relying on the Hessian being positive definite via the covariance), and a concise summary of all assumptions is missing. A critical assumption—the convexity of the parameter set Θ—is not stated in the main text. 

Several minor typos and wording errors are present (e.g., lines l185, l192, l207, l281, l311-12), indicating a need for careful proofreading. While the experimental results are encouraging, they are relatively basic and don’t showcase the potential benefits across a wider range of problems.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Original and substantial theoretical contribution, well-written (despite minor flaws), offers a new perspective on MLE.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited practical value due to the difficulty of proving fast-mixing, clarity issues with assumptions and convexity, experiments lack breadth.

**Overall:** I assess this paper as an original and theoretically valuable contribution, but with limited practical relevance in its current form. While a solid piece of work, its impact hinges on progress in efficiently verifying the fast-mixing property for a broader range of parameter sets. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and a request for more comprehensive experimental evaluation if possible.
