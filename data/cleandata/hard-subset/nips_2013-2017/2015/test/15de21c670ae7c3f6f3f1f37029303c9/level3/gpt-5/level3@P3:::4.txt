Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes learning exponential-family models by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ for which an associated MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast. Within this set, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated using short MCMC chains, and provide end-to-end convergence guarantees (FPRAS-style) to the maximum-likelihood solution inside Θ. They treat both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) settings, deriving explicit schedules for the number of optimization iterations K, number of samples per iteration M, and chain length v, as functions of the desired accuracy and mixing-time constants. The resulting total sampling effort scales as O(εf^-3 polylog) for log-likelihood suboptimality in the convex case (Theorems 2, 4, 5) and O(εθ^-2 polylog) for parameter error in the strongly convex case (Theorems 6, 8, Corollary 9). A Lipschitz-gradient bound (Theorem 1) facilitates these rates. The framework is general (bounded sufficient statistics, not restricted to graphical models), and the paper discusses projection operators for Ising and pairwise MRFs, connecting to known fast-mixing conditions.

The work connects to: (i) efficient but biased estimators such as pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, score matching, and related learning algorithms (Besag; Lindsay; Varin et al.; Hyvärinen; Marlin & de Freitas); (ii) MCMC-based maximum-likelihood methods without finite-time guarantees (Geyer; Gu & Zhu; Descombes et al.); (iii) learning with guaranteed mixing via restarts (Steinhardt & Liang); and (iv) projections into fast-mixing parameter sets (Domke & Liu 2013; Liu & Domke 2014). The main novelty is the end-to-end convergence-rate analysis of MLE using biased MCMC gradients under mixing constraints, yielding an FPRAS in a broad exponential-family setting.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: meaningful, explicit convergence rates that scale with the mixing-time parameters (C, α) and are near-matched by lower bounds up to logs. The schedules for K, M, v are particularly useful for reasoning about effort allocation.
- Original framing: learning in a novel tractable class defined by MCMC-approximability (fast mixing), rather than low treewidth or variational surrogates. This fills a gap in theory for widely used MCMC-based learning.
- Generality: results apply to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, subsuming many graphical models.
- Presentation: well-organized, precise statements, and helpful proof sketches and discussion of projection operators.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Lower bounds (major concern): Theorem 7 is difficult to interpret. The dependence on γ, α, δ, and multiple logs obscures the main message, and it is not clear how tight this is relative to the upper bounds for typical regimes. A more expansive discussion comparing Theorem 7 to Theorem 8 (and to the convex case) would help, ideally:
  - Simplify to dominant-order scaling (e.g., Ω(ε^-2) times log factors) and explicitly relate γ to λ/L.
  - Clarify whether the lower bound is algorithmic (for the analyzed scheme) or information-theoretic within the model class.
  - Provide intuition for the log log factor and when it matters in practice.
  - Give a concrete instantiation (e.g., Ising with Gibbs) to show magnitudes.
- Practical significance: While theoretically clean, the practical impact is somewhat unclear because the constants (C, α, R2, L) and exact projections onto Θ may be hard to obtain in large, real models. The paper would benefit from:
  - Guidance on estimating/upper-bounding C and α in practice or adapting v online.
  - Discussion of robustness to inexact projection and how projection error affects guarantees.
  - A brief empirical illustration beyond the toy Ising example, or at least a more detailed case study underscoring when the fast-mixing constraint is nontrivial yet useful.
- Scope limitations: The analysis assumes independent samples per iteration and does not cover “persistent” sample pools or CD with very short chains, common in practice. The Discussion acknowledges this; it would help to sketch the main technical barrier and potential avenues (e.g., mixing-aware coupling or martingale analyses).
- Assumption check: membership and projection onto Θ may be computationally nontrivial (spectral-norm projections rely on SVD-based convex programs). It would be useful to better quantify when projection cost is negligible relative to sampling.

Quality
- Technically sound with careful use of concentration inequalities and known proximal-gradient results. Proof sketches are convincing; full proofs in the appendix are appropriate.
- The lower bound (Theorem 7) needs clearer exposition and positioning.

Clarity
- Very clearly written and well-structured. Notation is consistent; the setup and assumptions are explicit. The schedules for K, M, v are easy to follow.

Originality
- High. Defining tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets and proving convergence rates for MCMC-approximate MLE in exponential families is a fresh contribution relative to prior NIPS work on treewidth-based tractability or surrogate objectives.

Significance
- The results are likely to influence theory and practice on learning in high-treewidth models where exact inference is infeasible. They provide the first clear guarantees for a widely used paradigm (MCMC-based likelihood optimization). That said, practical uptake hinges on estimating/controlling mixing and projections in large-scale settings; the paper should articulate this impact more explicitly.

Suggestions for the authors
- Expand the lower-bound section: interpret Theorem 7 in simplified regimes, compare to Theorem 8, and discuss tightness. Include a small table/figure contrasting upper/lower bounds across settings.
- Discuss potential impact and future extensions:
  - Adaptive schedules that tune v and M based on online mixing diagnostics or coupling.
  - Extensions to other samplers (e.g., Swendsen-Wang, SGLD/Langevin for continuous models) and corresponding fast-mixing sets.
  - Handling hidden variables and persistent chains; variance-reduction techniques.
  - Practical recipes for checking/projection into Θ in common discrete models; approximate projections and their effect on guarantees.
- Provide more guidance on choosing λ and relating γ = 1 − λ/L to accuracy–work tradeoffs.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and rigorous convergence-rate analysis tied to mixing times; bounds are meaningful and useful.
- Introduces a compelling class of tractable models (fast-mixing/MCMC-approximability).
- Clear presentation and careful positioning with prior work.
- Fills a theoretical gap on learning in intractable models with MCMC.

Cons for acceptance
- Lower bound (Theorem 7) is hard to interpret; broader treatment of lower bounds is needed.
- Practical significance remains somewhat unclear; constants and projections may be hard in practice.
- Limited empirical illustration.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes an original and technically solid contribution by providing convergence guarantees and rates for MLE via MCMC within fast-mixing parameter sets. With an expanded discussion of lower bounds (Theorem 7) and a clearer articulation of potential impact and future extensions, it will be a strong addition to the program.
