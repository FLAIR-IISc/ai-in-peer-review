This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where exact computation of the likelihood gradient is intractable. A common practical approach is to approximate the gradient using samples from a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) procedure. While widely used, this approach lacks theoretical guarantees, as the MCMC samples only approximate the true stationary distribution, introducing bias into the gradient estimate. The central contribution of this work is to provide a formal proof that this MCMC-based gradient descent procedure is, in fact, a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood parameters, under the key assumption that the parameters are constrained to a convex set for which the MCMC chain is provably "fast-mixing". This result connects a popular heuristic to a rigorous theoretical foundation.

The authors ground their work in the context of existing methods. They correctly point out that alternatives like pseudolikelihood or score matching often assume a well-specified model and do not find the M-projection in the misspecified case, a guarantee that maximum likelihood provides. Another approach is to restrict the model structure, for example to trees via the Chow-Liu algorithm, but this can be overly restrictive. This paper proposes a different notion of a tractable set: one defined by rapid MCMC convergence rather than graphical structure. The analysis provides explicit schedules and complexity bounds for achieving a desired accuracy, considering both the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound and rigorous theoretical analysis. The claims are well-supported by theorems and proof sketches, which appear plausible (though not verified in full detail). The distinction between the analysis for the convex and strongly convex cases is handled carefully and demonstrates technical depth. The work is a complete theoretical piece, establishing not just convergence but also providing explicit bounds on the total computational effort (in terms of MCMC transitions) required to reach an ε-accurate solution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of setting up the problem, defining the notation, and stating their assumptions and main results. The inclusion of a small illustrative example in Section 6 is helpful for grounding the otherwise abstract theoretical results.

**Originality:** The core idea of proving an FPRAS guarantee for MCMC-based maximum likelihood estimation by constraining parameters to a fast-mixing set is novel and significant. While using MCMC to approximate gradients is standard practice, this work is the first, to my knowledge, to formalize the conditions under which this heuristic is provably efficient and accurate. It provides a new perspective on defining "tractable" parameter sets for graphical models, moving beyond purely structural constraints like treewidth.

**Significance:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the practical significance and applicability of its results. The entire framework rests on the assumption that the parameters `θ` are constrained to a predefined, convex, fast-mixing set `Θ`. There are two major issues with this assumption:
1.  The optimal unconstrained parameters `θ*` for a given dataset may lie far outside of `Θ`. In this case, the algorithm finds the best solution *within* the restricted set, but this constrained optimum might be a poor model of the data.
2.  While the paper gives examples of such sets for Ising models (e.g., bounding the magnitude of potentials or the spectral norm of the interaction matrix), defining and projecting onto such sets for more general models is a significant challenge in itself. The analysis largely overlooks the computational cost of the projection step, which might not be trivial.

This strong assumption limits the paper's immediate practical impact. However, its theoretical contribution is undeniable. It provides a solid foundation and a clear target for future research: developing methods to learn models that are both expressive and guaranteed to have fast-mixing MCMC inference.

A further question for the authors is whether the hard constraint of `θ ∈ Θ` could be relaxed. Could the fast-mixing condition be encouraged via a regularization term instead of a hard projection? This would allow the optimization to trade off likelihood against mixing speed. Of course, designing such a regularizer is a non-trivial research problem, and combining it with standard L2 regularization could lead to complex interactions and potential over-regularization.

**Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first formal proof that the common heuristic of using MCMC for gradient approximation in likelihood maximization can be an FPRAS. This is a significant theoretical result.
2.  **Rigorous Analysis:** The work is technically sound, providing detailed convergence rates and complexity bounds for both convex and strongly convex settings.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
4.  **New Perspective:** It introduces and formalizes a notion of tractability based on MCMC mixing time, which is a valuable alternative to traditional structure-based constraints.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Limited Practical Applicability:** The core assumption of restricting parameters to a known fast-mixing set is very strong and may not hold in practice. The solution found may be a poor approximation if the true optimal parameters lie outside this set.
2.  **Unaddressed Complexity:** The analysis does not fully account for the complexity of projecting onto the fast-mixing set `Θ`, which could be a computational bottleneck.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong theoretical paper that formalizes an important and widely used learning procedure. While the assumptions required for the guarantees are currently too restrictive for direct practical application, the work lays a crucial foundation for future research on learning tractable high-treewidth models. It answers an important theoretical question about a common heuristic. The paper's strengths in originality and technical quality outweigh its current practical limitations, making it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
