This paper explores the problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models with high treewidth, proposing an approach based on restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set and utilizing gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. While the problem addressed is important and the authors provide a theoretical analysis of convergence rates, the paper suffers from a significant lack of awareness of closely related prior work, leading to a presentation of results that are largely known or easily derivable from existing theory.

The core idea – approximating the likelihood gradient using MCMC samples and applying gradient descent – is a common strategy, deeply connected to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics. However, the paper largely frames this as a novel approach without adequately positioning it within the broader landscape of existing research. Specifically, the authors fail to engage with the extensive literature on stochastic approximation algorithms for maximizing likelihoods, particularly those dealing with noisy gradients arising from MCMC sampling (e.g., Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret, 1990). The theoretical guarantees presented, specifically the fully-polynomial time randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) for both unregularized and ridge-regularized cases, are ultimately consequences of well-established results in stochastic optimization.

The authors acknowledge related work like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods, but primarily focus on their drawbacks compared to the proposed method. A more thorough comparison to methods explicitly designed for optimizing intractable likelihoods using MCMC, such as those based on stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics (SGLD) and related techniques, is missing. The paper also does not adequately address how this approach compares to existing variational inference methods designed to approximate intractable posteriors and likelihoods.

Several assumptions appear unnecessarily strong or are not clearly justified. The requirement for “fast-mixing” parameter sets, defined with constants C and α, while reasonable, could benefit from a broader discussion of ergodicity and the limitations of assuming uniform ergodicity. The authors’ assertion that reinitializing the Markov chain from an arbitrary distribution *r* is necessary also lacks justification; maintaining samples from the previous iteration, as commonly done in contrastive divergence and related algorithms, may be preferable and has been extensively analyzed (e.g., Carreira-Peripiñán & Hinton, 2005).

The presentation also suffers from clarity issues. Assumptions used within Theorems 1 and 2 are not explicitly stated near the theorems themselves, making it difficult to follow the logical flow of the argument. For example, the bound on sufficient statistics norm (R2) is introduced without clear context within the theorem statements. Furthermore, the discussion regarding the Lipschitz constant (L) in the example section appears ad-hoc; using a smaller value empirically is not a justification for a theoretically derived bound.

The novelty of the work is therefore marginal. While the authors provide a unified framework for analyzing gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients within an exponential family, the core results do not represent a significant advancement beyond existing theory. The extensive references in the field (e.g., Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Koller & Friedman, 2009) demonstrate the maturity of the area, and the presented contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.

**Strengths:**

*   The problem addressed – learning in high-treewidth graphical models – is important and practically relevant.
*   The authors provide a theoretical analysis of convergence rates for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients.
*   The formulation within the exponential family provides a degree of generality.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Major lack of awareness of closely related prior work in stochastic approximation and Monte Carlo EM.
*   Results are largely known or easily derivable from existing theory.
*   Unnecessary/incorrect assumptions regarding Markov chain ergodicity and initialization.
*   Poor presentation: unclear statement of assumptions and ad-hoc justifications.
*   Marginal novelty.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a relatively self-contained analysis of a common practice (MCMC-based gradient descent).
*   The theoretical results, while not novel, are clearly stated and proven.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of engagement with related literature significantly weakens the contribution.
*   The assumptions are often unnecessarily strong or not well-justified.
*   The novelty is marginal, and the paper does not offer a substantial advancement over existing work.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **Reject**. While the paper is technically sound and presents a coherent analysis, its lack of novelty and failure to adequately acknowledge and build upon existing research make it unsuitable for acceptance at NIPS. The paper would benefit significantly from a more comprehensive literature review, a more nuanced discussion of assumptions, and a clearer articulation of its contribution relative to existing work.
