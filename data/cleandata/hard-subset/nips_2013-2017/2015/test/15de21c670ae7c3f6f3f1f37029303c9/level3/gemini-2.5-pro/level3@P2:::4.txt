### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models where exact inference is intractable. While prior work often restricts the model class to structurally simple ones (e.g., trees via the Chow-Liu algorithm), this paper proposes a fundamentally different notion of a tractable set: a "fast-mixing parameter set," where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are guaranteed to converge rapidly. The authors analyze the widely used but theoretically under-explored practice of performing gradient descent where the gradient (which depends on intractable expectations) is approximated using samples from an MCMC chain.

The main contribution is a formal proof that this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within a given convex, fast-mixing set. The result applies generally to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics. The paper provides rigorous convergence guarantees, detailing how the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and chain length should be set to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. Separate analyses and complexity bounds are derived for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, showing convergence in terms of log-likelihood and parameter distance, respectively.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Contribution:** The central idea of defining tractability through the lens of MCMC mixing time, rather than graphical structure (like treewidth), is both novel and insightful. This reframing connects a common practical heuristic (using MCMC for learning) to rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing a new and potentially fruitful class of models for study. This work appears to be the first to provide such guarantees for this general MCMC-based learning setting.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper provides a complete and technically sound analysis. The convergence rate bounds are meaningful, explicitly characterizing the trade-offs between computational effort (gradient steps, MCMC samples, burn-in) and approximation accuracy. By analyzing the accumulation of biased and noisy gradient estimates and leveraging results on inexact gradient descent, the authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for a widely used method that has largely lacked such guarantees.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem setup is clear, the assumptions are stated explicitly, and the main theorems are presented logically. The inclusion of proof sketches in the main body of the paper effectively conveys the intuition behind the formal results, making the technical contributions more accessible.

4.  **Significance:** This paper fills an important gap in our understanding of learning intractable probabilistic models. Practitioners frequently rely on MCMC-based approximations for training models like Boltzmann machines and other MRFs, often with heuristic choices for learning rates and sampling schedules. This work provides a principled framework for analyzing these methods, potentially guiding the development of more robust and efficient learning algorithms. The results are general and could spur further research into characterizing fast-mixing parameter sets for various models and MCMC samplers.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Interpretation of the Lower Bound:** A key area for improvement is the discussion surrounding the lower bound presented in Theorem 7. In its current form, the expression is quite complex, and its interpretation is not immediately obvious. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an expanded discussion that:
    *   Provides more intuition for what the bound implies about the fundamental difficulty of the problem.
    *   Compares the derived upper bounds (from Theorem 8) to this lower bound to better understand their tightness.
    *   Contextualizes this result by relating it to other known lower bounds in stochastic optimization or statistical learning.

2.  **Discussion of Broader Impact:** While the work is theoretically significant, the "Discussion" and "Conclusions" sections could be expanded to better articulate the potential impact and avenues for future work. For instance, the authors could elaborate on:
    *   What are the most promising practical applications where this framework could be directly useful?
    *   How might these theoretical results inform the design of practical MCMC-based learning algorithms, even when the fast-mixing assumptions are not strictly met?
    *   What are the key open questions? For example, exploring the development of efficient projection operators for more complex fast-mixing sets or analyzing adaptive MCMC sampling schedules.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** The paper introduces a novel and important concept of a "fast-mixing" tractable model class, moving beyond traditional structural constraints.
*   **Significance:** It provides the first formal FPRAS guarantee for the common practice of learning intractable models with MCMC-approximated gradients, a long-standing open issue.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical results are rigorous, and the paper is a complete and well-executed piece of research.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The primary weakness is the insufficient explanation and contextualization of the lower bound in Theorem 7, which makes it difficult to assess its full implications.
*   The discussion on the broader significance and future directions could be more developed to help readers appreciate the potential impact of the work.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the theory of learning in intractable graphical models. The strengths—particularly the originality of the problem framing and the rigorous theoretical guarantees—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to presentation and could be addressed in a revised version. The paper formalizes and validates a widely used practical approach, bridging a critical gap between theory and practice. I recommend acceptance.
