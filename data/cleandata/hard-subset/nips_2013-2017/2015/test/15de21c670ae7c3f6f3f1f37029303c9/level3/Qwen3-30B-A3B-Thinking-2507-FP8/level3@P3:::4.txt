

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper introduces a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set where MCMC inference is guaranteed to converge quickly. The authors frame the problem through a new class of tractable models defined by fast-mixing parameters, rather than the traditional tree-structured approach. The key contribution is proving that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients will converge to the maximum likelihood solution inside the set with high probability. The paper establishes convergence rates showing that to achieve ε-accuracy in log-likelihood requires cubic effort in 1/ε for the unregularized case, and quadratic effort for the ridge-regularized case.

This work builds on the well-known challenge of intractable maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth graphical models, which has motivated approaches like pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22], and composite likelihoods [20, 30]. While these methods address specific limitations, they typically assume well-specified models and often yield distributions with intractable inference. The authors' approach differs fundamentally by shifting the tractability criterion from treewidth to mixing times, which represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical results are original and significant, introducing a novel class of tractable models (MCMC-approximable expectations) with meaningful convergence rate bounds.
2. The presentation is clear, well-organized, and the paper effectively connects the abstract theory to practical considerations.
3. The convergence rates are meaningful and useful, providing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme for the learning problem.
4. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by providing theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based learning strategies that are widely used in practice.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The major weakness is the lower bound (Theorem 7), which is difficult to interpret and requires significant expansion. The current discussion lacks sufficient context for why this lower bound matters, how it compares to other bounds in the literature, and what its practical implications are.
2. The significance of the work is unclear to the reviewer. While the paper fills a gap in learning intractable models using MCMC, it would benefit from a more thorough discussion of potential impact, future extensions, and follow-on opportunities.
3. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison with related work on mixing times in learning contexts, particularly recent developments in the NIPS proceedings.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*For Acceptance:*
- Introduces a fundamentally new notion of tractability (fast-mixing parameters) that generalizes beyond graphical models to exponential families
- Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for a widely used practical approach (MCMC-based gradient descent)
- Establishes meaningful convergence rates with explicit computational complexity bounds
- The results are technically sound and well-supported by the theoretical analysis

*Against Acceptance:*
- The lower bound (Theorem 7) requires substantial expansion for proper interpretation
- The significance and broader impact of the work are not sufficiently articulated
- The paper would benefit from more discussion of practical implications and potential applications

**Evaluation:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the bounds are meaningful and useful. The main weakness is the underdeveloped lower bound discussion, which would benefit from more thorough treatment.

*Clarity:* The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and effectively communicates complex theoretical concepts. The presentation of results is logical and accessible to the target audience.

*Originality:* The framing of the problem through a "fast-mixing" model class represents a significant conceptual advance beyond traditional treewidth-based approaches. The theoretical results are novel, and the paper clearly distinguishes its contributions from previous work.

*Significance:* While the paper addresses an important gap in the literature, the significance is not fully articulated. The authors should expand their discussion to better highlight the potential impact of their results, including how this approach might enable new applications or lead to more efficient learning algorithms in practice. The paper appears significant but needs stronger justification of its importance.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCMC-based learning in graphical models. The main weakness is the insufficient discussion of the lower bound and the significance of the work. I recommend acceptance with the following revisions: 1) substantially expand the discussion of Theorem 7 to make it interpretable and meaningful, 2) add a detailed discussion of potential impact, future extensions, and follow-on opportunities, and 3) strengthen the significance argument by better connecting the theoretical results to practical applications and broader implications for the field.

The paper's novel theoretical framework and meaningful convergence rates make it a strong contribution to the field, and the proposed revisions would significantly enhance its impact and clarity.
