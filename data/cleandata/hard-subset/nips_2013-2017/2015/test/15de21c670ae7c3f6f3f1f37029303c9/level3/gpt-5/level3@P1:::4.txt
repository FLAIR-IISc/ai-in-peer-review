This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ—i.e., a set for which an MCMC kernel mixes to the model’s stationary distribution at a geometric rate. Within this set, the authors analyze projected gradient descent that uses MCMC to approximate the likelihood gradient. They prove that for bounded sufficient statistics, the method converges to the MLE restricted to Θ with high probability. In the unregularized (convex) case, they obtain ε-accuracy in objective value with total MCMC effort roughly O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)); with ridge regularization (strongly convex case), they obtain ε-accuracy in parameter distance with O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) effort. They establish Lipschitz continuity of the gradient, provide explicit schedules for steps/chain length/sample size (K, v, M), and complementary lower bounds. Although motivated by graphical models (e.g., Ising/Potts), the results hold for general exponential families. Relative to prior NIPS and related work on tractable structures (trees/Chow–Liu), correlation decay/high girth, and surrogate estimators (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood), this paper proposes a distinct tractability notion—fast mixing—and supplies end-to-end optimization guarantees for MCMC-based learning, building on mixing-time theory and inexact gradient descent results (e.g., Schmidt et al.).

Quality: The theoretical development appears sound. The assumptions are clear: bounded sufficient statistics, a convex fast-mixing set Θ with known projection, and uniform geometric mixing ∥Mv

The Lipschitz gradient bound is plausible under bounded t(x), and the convergence analyses carefully separate convex vs. strongly convex regimes, controlling both bias (finite v) and variance (finite M) of gradient estimates. The schedules for K, M, v, and the FPRAS statements are valuable. The lower bounds (Theorems 3 and 7) help frame optimality, though Theorem 7 is particularly dense (see below). The empirical illustration is intentionally minimal; the contribution is primarily theoretical. Limitations are candidly discussed: independence of samples across iterations, cost/availability of projections, and the gap to persistent-chain schemes commonly used in practice.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized. The setup cleanly states notation and assumptions; proofs are sketched with references to standard inequalities (Bernstein, Hoeffding/Efron-Stein). Figures and the toy example help intuition. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- Expand the discussion of Theorem 7: provide intuition for the role of the log terms, compare to the constructive schedule in Theorem 8, and isolate the minimal per-factor dependencies on α, γ, and δ.
- Summarize constant dependencies in a table (R, C, α, L, λ) and indicate typical scales for common models (e.g., Ising on grids vs. dense graphs).
- Clarify how one might estimate or bound C and α in practice when they are not analytically known.

Originality: Framing tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets, and proving FPRAS-type convergence guarantees for MLE with MCMC-approximated gradients across general exponential families, is novel. While prior NIPS work projected parameters to induce fast mixing (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), this paper connects such sets directly to optimization guarantees for maximum likelihood, with explicit schedules and lower bounds. The use of inexact projected gradient results in this context is also well thought out.

Significance: The results fill a notable theoretical gap: MCMC-based maximum-likelihood learning is widely used but often lacks guarantees due to biased gradients. The paper offers a clean recipe—constrain to a fast-mixing Θ and project each step—to obtain provable convergence with explicit complexity. The broader impact depends on how widely fast-mixing sets with efficient projections can be constructed beyond Ising-like models, and on the practicality of the constants. Even with these caveats, this provides a principled alternative to tree-based tractable classes and surrogate objectives, and offers guidance for designing regularizers that enforce fast mixing.

Pros:
- Clear, original theoretical framework linking fast mixing to learnability with guarantees.
- General applicability to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Explicit rates, schedules (K, M, v), and matching lower bounds up to logs.
- Careful discussion of assumptions and limitations; constructive bridge to practice.

Cons:
- Theorem 7’s lower bound is hard to parse; needs intuition and tighter connection to upper bounds.
- Practical significance uncertain without more empirical exploration and guidance on obtaining Θ, C, α in real models.
- Assumption of independent fresh samples per iteration diverges from common “persistent” MCMC practices; analysis does not cover sample pools.
- Projection cost and availability outside specific cases (e.g., Ising with spectral-norm constraints) are underexplored.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Provide more intuition and a simplified corollary for Theorem 7 (e.g., suppressing polylogs) and compare quantitatively to Theorem 8’s schedule.
- Discuss strategies to construct Θ and perform projection for non-binary, higher-order, or continuous exponential families; include at least one additional concrete example beyond Ising.
- Offer guidance on choosing/estimating C and α (e.g., via path coupling, Dobrushin conditions, spectral gaps), and how misspecification of these affects guarantees.
- Comment on extending the analysis to persistent chains or sample pools; could coupling arguments control bias across iterations?
- Explore adaptive schedules for K, M, v based on online diagnostics (autocorrelation, R̂).
- Add a brief comparison to surrogate estimators (pseudo-/composite likelihood, score matching) in terms of sample complexity vs. computational complexity when the model is misspecified.
- Clarify how restrictive Θ must be in practice to ensure useful modeling capacity and whether adaptive/learned regularizers can trade off mixing speed and fit.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper introduces a compelling tractable model class (fast-mixing sets) and delivers solid, general convergence-rate guarantees for MCMC-based maximum-likelihood learning, addressing a recognized gap. With expanded discussion of the lower bounds and practical instantiation of Θ, this work can have meaningful impact.
