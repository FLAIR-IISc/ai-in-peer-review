This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where exact inference is generally intractable. The authors propose a novel approach that departs from common strategies like restricting model structure to be tree-like (e.g., Chow-Liu) or using surrogate likelihood objectives (e.g., pseudolikelihood). Instead, they propose constraining the model parameters to a "fast-mixing" set Θ, within which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to converge rapidly to the stationary distribution.

The core contribution is a theoretical analysis of projected gradient descent for MLE, where the gradient is approximated using MCMC samples. The authors prove that this widely used heuristic constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within the constrained set Θ. The analysis is general, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. Two cases are considered: the unregularized (convex) case, where the analysis provides convergence guarantees on the log-likelihood value, and the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case, where guarantees are provided on the parameter error (L2 distance to the optimum). The paper derives the total computational effort (in terms of MCMC transitions) required to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The primary strength of this paper is its originality and theoretical contribution. The idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing MCMC rather than graphical structure (e.g., treewidth) is a compelling and elegant alternative. This work provides the first formal convergence guarantees for a common class of algorithms that combine gradient-based optimization with MCMC-based gradient approximations, a topic of significant practical and theoretical interest. The analysis, which builds upon the work of Schmidt et al. (2011) for inexact proximal methods, appears technically sound and is a non-trivial combination of existing results from optimization and MCMC theory.

Furthermore, the authors are commendably candid about the limitations of their work. The discussion section thoughtfully addresses the cost of projection, sample initialization strategies (drawing a connection to Contrastive Divergence), and the inability of the current proof technique to handle sample "pools" used in practice. This honesty strengthens the paper. While the authors state the work has no significant practical contribution, the theoretical insight it provides for understanding algorithms like Contrastive Divergence is valuable.

The main weakness of the paper lies in its practical significance. The central assumption is the existence of a known convex set of fast-mixing parameters Θ onto which one can efficiently project. For many interesting models, characterizing such a set is extremely difficult, and proving that a given set ensures fast mixing is a notoriously hard problem in itself. In essence, the work replaces one hard problem (intractable inference) with another potentially equally hard one (characterizing and projecting onto a fast-mixing set). While the paper provides examples for the Ising model where such sets are known, the general applicability remains a major question.

Another area for improvement is clarity. Several key assumptions are either understated or omitted from the main text.
1.  The analysis relies on the convexity of the negative log-likelihood, but this is never explicitly stated. A brief mention that the log-partition function A(θ) is convex (its Hessian is the covariance matrix of the sufficient statistics) would immediately justify the use of convex optimization machinery.
2.  The set of parameters Θ is assumed to be convex for the projection step to be well-defined and efficient, but this is only mentioned implicitly through the use of projection operators for convex sets. This assumption should be stated upfront.
3.  A concise summary of all assumptions (bounded sufficient statistics, known geometric mixing rate on a convex set Θ, efficient projection operator for Θ) would significantly improve readability for the reader.

Finally, there are a few minor typos and phrasing issues that could be corrected:
-   Line 185: "at long as" -> "as long as"
-   Line 192: "bounds its variance" -> "bound its variance"
-   Line 207: "is on the order of ϵ" -> "is of the order of ϵ"
-   Line 281: "needs to be done K times, this time will often" -> "needs to be done only K times, this cost will often"
-   Lines 311-312: The final sentence of Corollary 9 is very long and could be restructured for clarity.

### Summary of Contribution

Overall, this is a well-written paper that makes a substantial and original theoretical contribution. It provides a novel framework for tractable learning in complex models and delivers rigorous convergence guarantees for a popular class of algorithms. While the strong assumptions limit its immediate practical impact, the conceptual shift and theoretical insights are significant and likely to inspire future work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Originality:** Proposes a novel and interesting definition of a "tractable set" for MLE based on fast MCMC mixing, as an alternative to structural constraints like low treewidth.
2.  **Significance:** Provides the first FPRAS guarantee for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates, offering valuable theoretical insight into widely used but poorly understood heuristics (e.g., variants of Contrastive Divergence).
3.  **Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and sound, correctly identifying and addressing the challenge of biased gradient estimates.

**Arguments for rejection:**
1.  **Practical Impact:** The assumption that one can easily define and project onto a set of fast-mixing parameters is very strong and may not hold for many models of interest, limiting the method's applicability.
2.  **Clarity:** The paper omits explicit statements of key assumptions (e.g., convexity of the objective and the constraint set), which are crucial for the analysis.
