This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in Markov networks with high treewidth. The core problem is that computing the exact gradient of the log-likelihood is NP-hard, forcing practitioners to rely on approximations, most commonly via Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. While this approach is widely used in practice (e.g., in training Restricted Boltzmann Machines), it has lacked rigorous theoretical guarantees. The main contribution of this work is to formalize the conditions under which this heuristic is provably efficient. The authors prove that if the learning algorithm is restricted to a set of parameters for which the corresponding MCMC chain is guaranteed to mix rapidly, then MCMC-based gradient descent acts as a Fully Polynomial-time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within that set.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** This work provides a valuable and novel theoretical bridge between a popular heuristic and computational learning theory. Formalizing when MCMC-based learning is efficient is a significant contribution that could inspire more rigorous approaches to training complex graphical models.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is thorough, carefully deriving separate complexity bounds for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) optimization problems. The proofs appear plausible, and the formalization is sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the problem, the proposed theoretical framework, and the main results.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Practical Relevance:** The primary weakness is the strong assumption that one can constrain the parameters to a set where rapid mixing is guaranteed. The parameter constraints required to satisfy the theorems (e.g., small interaction strengths) are unlikely to be met in many practical applications, which limits the immediate applicability of these results. The authors are honest about this, but it remains a major limitation.

**Constructive Feedback:**
The analysis relies on a pre-defined fast-mixing set Θ. An interesting question for future work is whether this condition could be enforced algorithmically, perhaps as a form of regularization that penalizes slow-mixing parameters. However, care would be needed, as combining such a penalty with standard ridge regularization could lead to an over-regularized and poorly performing model. The paper would also benefit from a final proofreading pass to correct minor typos.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid theoretical paper that provides the first formal guarantees for a widely used learning algorithm. While its strong assumptions limit its current practical utility, it lays an important foundation for future research aimed at closing the gap between theory and practice in learning intractable models. Significant further research is needed to relax these assumptions, but the paper presents a clear and important first step.
