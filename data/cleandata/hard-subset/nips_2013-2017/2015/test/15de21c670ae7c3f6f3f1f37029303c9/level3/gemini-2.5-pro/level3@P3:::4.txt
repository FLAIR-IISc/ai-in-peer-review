### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in intractable probabilistic models, such as undirected graphical models with high treewidth. While standard approaches restrict the model class structurally (e.g., to trees) or use proxy objectives (e.g., pseudolikelihood), this work proposes a fundamentally different notion of tractability. The authors define a tractable parameter set as one where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are guaranteed to mix rapidly. This "fast-mixing" constraint ensures that expectations needed for the likelihood gradient can be efficiently approximated.

The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of projected gradient descent where the gradient is estimated using MCMC samples. The paper proves that this common, yet theoretically unsupported, heuristic converges to the maximum likelihood solution within the fast-mixing set. It establishes explicit convergence rates, showing that the procedure is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). For the convex (unregularized) case, achieving an ε-accurate solution in log-likelihood requires a total computational effort cubic in 1/ε. For the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case, an ε-accurate solution in parameter distance is achieved with effort quadratic in 1/ε. The analysis provides a clear schedule for setting the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and MCMC transitions to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Framing:** The central idea of defining a tractable model class via MCMC mixing rates, rather than structural properties like treewidth, is highly original and insightful. This reframes a classic problem in a novel way that directly connects the learning procedure to the tractability of inference, which is the core computational bottleneck. This conceptual contribution is a significant strength.

2.  **Theoretical Quality and Significance:** The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for the widely used practice of approximating the likelihood gradient with MCMC. This is a crucial result that fills an important theoretical gap in the literature. The derived bounds are meaningful, non-asymptotic, and useful, as they explicitly quantify the trade-offs between the number of gradient iterations, MCMC samples per iteration, and MCMC chain length. The result that this procedure constitutes an FPRAS is a strong theoretical statement.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the problem setup is precise, and the theoretical results are stated formally and followed by intuitive proof sketches. The authors do an excellent job of relating their work to a broad set of prior literature, from MCMC mixing time analysis to optimization with noisy gradients.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Interpretability of the Lower Bound:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the presentation and discussion of the lower bound for the strongly convex case (Theorem 7). The expression for the bound is highly complex and difficult to parse, making its interpretation challenging. It is not immediately clear how this lower bound compares to the upper bound derived in Theorem 8, or what the key takeaways from it are. The paper would be substantially improved if the authors could:
    *   Provide a dedicated discussion interpreting Theorem 7. For instance, an asymptotic analysis (e.g., for small ε) could simplify the expression and highlight the dominant terms.
    *   Directly compare the lower bound to their upper bound to give the reader a sense of its tightness.
    *   Situate this result within the broader context of computational complexity for this problem. Is this bound novel? How does it relate to other known lower bounds in stochastic optimization or statistical learning?

2.  **Discussion of Significance and Impact:** The paper is perhaps too modest about its potential impact, stating it claims "no significant practical contribution." While the work is primarily theoretical, its implications are significant. The conclusion and discussion sections could be strengthened by elaborating on the following points:
    *   **Potential Impact:** Beyond the Ising model example, which specific families of models or application areas could benefit most from this framework? For example, could this apply to learning deep energy-based models where mixing is a known challenge?
    *   **Future Extensions and Follow-on Research:** The paper opens up many interesting research questions. A discussion of these would be valuable. For instance, could the analysis be extended to more sophisticated MCMC samplers (e.g., Hamiltonian Monte Carlo) or to learning algorithms that adaptively set parameters like the number of MCMC steps? Does this work motivate new research into algorithms for projecting onto complex fast-mixing sets?

**Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the theory of learning intractable probabilistic models. By framing tractability in terms of fast MCMC mixing and providing the first formal convergence guarantees for a widely used MCMC-based learning algorithm, the authors fill an important gap in the literature. The work is technically sound, original, and clearly presented.

While the discussion of the lower bound needs significant expansion to be useful, and the paper could better articulate its potential impact, these are addressable issues. The core contributions are solid and of high quality.

**Final Verdict: Accept.** The strengths of originality and theoretical significance far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors revise the paper to improve the clarity and interpretation of the lower bound and expand the discussion on future work and potential impact.
