This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for approximating Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) in exponential families, particularly motivated by the challenge of intractable inference in high-treewidth graphical models. Instead of relying on common structural assumptions like low treewidth, the authors propose constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where MCMC inference is guaranteed to converge quickly. By combining this assumption with a proximal gradient descent algorithm, leveraging analysis from Schmidt et al. (2011), the paper establishes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained MLE problem. This work offers a conceptually new alternative to methods like the Chow-Liu algorithm or pseudo-likelihood, which have different limitations.

The paper has several significant strengths. The primary contribution is a solid theoretical analysis providing high-probability guarantees for both the convex case (bounding likelihood error) and the strongly convex regularized case (bounding parameter error). This is a non-trivial combination of existing techniques applied to a popular but often theoretically ungrounded heuristic (using MCMC for gradient steps). The authors are commendably transparent about the work's limitations, including the pessimistic nature of the theoretical bounds and directions for future research. The writing is generally clear and the simple experiment effectively demonstrates the theory.

However, the paper also has weaknesses that temper its impact. The most significant is the practical limitation of the core assumption. Proving that a set of parameters is fast-mixing for a given MCMC method is often as difficult as the original inference problem, effectively replacing one hard problem with another. This limits the immediate applicability of the method. On the clarity front, the paper should explicitly state that the negative log-likelihood is convex (a standard result, but important for context) and should summarize all assumptions in one place for the reader's convenience. The assumption that the parameter set Θ must be convex is crucial but not stated in the main text. Finally, several minor typos were noted (e.g., l185, l192, l207, l281, l311-12).

In summary, this paper makes an original and substantial theoretical contribution by providing guarantees for a widely used heuristic under a new set of assumptions. While well-written and technically sound, its practical value appears limited by the difficulty of verifying its core fast-mixing assumption.
