This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where exact inference is intractable. The standard approach to this problem is to restrict the model parameters to a structurally simple set, such as tree-structured models, for which efficient algorithms like Chow-Liu exist. This work proposes a fundamentally different notion of a tractable parameter set: a set where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing," meaning it converges rapidly to the stationary distribution.

The main contribution is a theoretical analysis of a common learning heuristic: performing gradient descent on the log-likelihood where the intractable gradient is approximated using samples from MCMC. The paper proves that if the parameters are constrained to a convex, fast-mixing set, this procedure constitutes a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within that set. The analysis, which builds on results for inexact proximal-gradient methods by Schmidt et al. (2011), provides explicit convergence guarantees and complexity bounds for both the unregularized (convex) case, converging in log-likelihood, and the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case, converging in parameter distance.

### **Strengths**

**Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its novel conceptual contribution. Defining tractability in terms of the dynamic property of fast mixing, rather than the static property of low treewidth, is an original and well-motivated idea. It provides a new theoretical framework for understanding tractable learning in complex models. Furthermore, by providing rigorous guarantees for MCMC-based gradient approximation, the work offers valuable theoretical insight into a class of widely used but poorly understood algorithms, including variants of contrastive divergence.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is the core of the paper and appears to be solid. The authors provide detailed proofs for both the convex and strongly convex settings, establishing explicit rates and computational complexities. This is a substantial technical contribution. The paper is also scientifically honest; the authors candidly discuss the limitations of their analysis, such as the potentially pessimistic nature of the theoretical bounds (as seen in the experiment), the unaddressed complexity of projecting onto the fast-mixing set, and the ways in which their analyzed algorithm differs from practical heuristics like contrastive divergence with sample pools.

### **Weaknesses**

**Practical Impact:** The most significant weakness is the limited practical applicability of the framework. The paper replaces one difficult problem (intractable inference) with another: defining a fast-mixing parameter set Θ and an efficient projection operator Π_Θ. Proving fast-mixing bounds for MCMC is a notoriously difficult problem in statistical physics and computer science, and often requires strong parameter constraints. The examples given (e.g., bounding interaction strengths) are tractable but might be too restrictive for many applications. Therefore, while theoretically elegant, the framework's direct utility may be limited until more is known about identifying and projecting onto richer fast-mixing sets.

**Clarity:** While generally well-written, the paper could be improved by making its core assumptions more explicit and accessible. For instance:
1.  The convexity of the negative log-likelihood is fundamental to the analysis but is only briefly mentioned in a proof sketch (Theorem 2). This fact, arising from the log-partition function A(θ) having the covariance of the sufficient statistics as its Hessian, should be stated clearly in the main setup.
2.  The requirement that the constraint set Θ be convex is crucial for the projected gradient descent method but is not stated in the main text until Section 3.1.
A consolidated list or paragraph summarizing all key assumptions (bounded sufficient statistics, geometric mixing rate, convex parameter set Θ, etc.) would greatly enhance readability.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   The paper introduces a novel and thought-provoking perspective on tractable learning in graphical models.
    *   It provides the first formal FPRAS guarantee for the widely used heuristic of learning with MCMC-approximated gradients.
    *   The theoretical contribution is substantial and technically sound.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The practical value is questionable, as the approach relies on solving the equally hard problem of characterizing fast-mixing parameter sets.
    *   The contribution is primarily a non-trivial combination and application of existing theoretical tools from optimization and MCMC theory.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

The paper is a valuable and original theoretical contribution that formalizes an important learning heuristic. Despite the practical challenges, the conceptual shift is significant and could inspire future work. I recommend acceptance.
