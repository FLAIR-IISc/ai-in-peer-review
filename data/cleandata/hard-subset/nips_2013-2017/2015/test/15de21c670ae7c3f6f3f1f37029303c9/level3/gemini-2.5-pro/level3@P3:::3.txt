This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth Markov networks. The core difficulty lies in computing the gradient of the log-likelihood, which requires calculating expectations over the model's distribution, a #P-hard problem. A common practical heuristic is to approximate these expectations using samples from a Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm. However, this approach has historically lacked theoretical guarantees, as the MCMC sampler only produces samples from an approximation of the true distribution, introducing bias into the gradient estimate.

The main contribution of this work is to provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for this heuristic. The authors introduce the concept of a "fast-mixing parameter set"—a set of model parameters for which an MCMC sampler is guaranteed to converge geometrically to its stationary distribution. The central result proves that if the learning algorithm is restricted to such a set, then projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients constitutes a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). This is a significant result, as it formally connects the efficiency of MCMC inference to the correctness and efficiency of the learning procedure. The paper provides separate, detailed analyses for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) learning problems, deriving explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and MCMC chain lengths required to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.

### **Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel formulation of tractability. Instead of relying on structural properties like low treewidth, it defines tractability based on the dynamic property of fast MCMC mixing. This is an elegant and powerful idea. Providing the first FPRAS guarantee for this widely used learning paradigm is a significant theoretical contribution that bridges a long-standing gap between practice and theory. It lays a solid foundation for future research into learning intractable models.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The authors leverage results on gradient descent with inexact gradients and concentration inequalities to build their argument carefully. The analysis is thorough, covering both the convex and strongly convex cases with distinct proofs and deriving explicit complexity bounds (total number of Markov transitions) for each. The proofs presented in the appendix appear plausible and well-reasoned.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the problem is formally defined, and the main theorems are stated precisely. The authors do an excellent job of placing their work in the context of related literature, distinguishing it from methods that rely on structural assumptions (e.g., Chow-Liu) or model-specification assumptions (e.g., pseudolikelihood).

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

*   **Practical Relevance:** The main weakness of the work is the significant gap between the theoretical requirements and practical application. The theorems rely on the ability to constrain parameters to a known fast-mixing set `Θ`. However, as the authors acknowledge, the currently known fast-mixing sets are quite restrictive (e.g., Ising models with weak interactions or ferromagnetic constraints). It is unclear whether these sets are expressive enough to model complex, real-world data distributions. This concern limits the immediate practical impact of the results, framing the work as a foundational theoretical piece rather than a ready-to-use algorithm.

*   **Open Question on Regularization:** The use of a hard projection onto the set `Θ` seems rigid. A natural question is whether this constraint could be softened into a form of regularization. For instance, instead of projecting, could one add a penalty term to the objective function that encourages fast mixing (e.g., a penalty on the spectral norm of the interaction matrix)? This might allow the algorithm to explore a richer parameter space. I would be interested in the authors' thoughts on this direction. A potential pitfall is that combining such a "mixing-time regularizer" with standard ridge (L2) regularization could lead to an over-regularized model, where the two penalties might work against each other or unduly shrink the parameters. Addressing this interaction would be a non-trivial but valuable research direction.

### **Summary**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel and significant theoretical result: the first FPRAS guarantee for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning.
2.  The core idea of defining tractability via MCMC mixing time is highly original and opens up a new perspective on learning intractable models.
3.  The analysis is technically rigorous, complete, and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The practical applicability is limited by the restrictive nature of known fast-mixing parameter sets.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to our understanding of learning in intractable graphical models. While the immediate practical utility is limited by the strong assumptions on the parameter space, the originality and significance of the theoretical results are undeniable. The paper formalizes the long-held intuition that efficient MCMC sampling should lead to efficient learning and provides a solid base upon which future, more practical algorithms may be built. The work is a perfect fit for NIPS and represents a solid step forward in the field.
