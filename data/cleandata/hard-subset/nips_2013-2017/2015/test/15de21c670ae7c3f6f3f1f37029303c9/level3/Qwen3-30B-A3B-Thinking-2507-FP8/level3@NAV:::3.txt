

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in maximum likelihood learning for Markov networks: the NP-hardness of exact gradient computation necessitates MCMC approximation, which lacks theoretical guarantees. The authors make a significant theoretical contribution by proving that when parameters lie in a "fast-mixing" set (where MCMC converges rapidly), MCMC-based weight learning becomes a Fully Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). They provide separate analyses for unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, establishing complexity bounds of O(1/ε³) and O(1/ε²) respectively for ε-accurate solutions.

The paper is well-motivated and builds upon prior work on tractable learning (Chow-Liu, tree-structured models) and mixing time analysis (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Domke & Liu, 2013), but offers a novel perspective by focusing on fast-mixing parameter sets rather than structural constraints. The theoretical framework is sound, with plausible proofs that would benefit from more rigorous verification. The writing is clear and well-organized, though minor typos exist.

The primary strength is the elegant theoretical unification of MCMC-based learning with FPRAS guarantees across exponential families. However, the practical relevance is severely limited by the stringent parameter constraints required for the mixing conditions (e.g., uniform bounds on interaction strengths), which are unlikely to hold in real-world applications. The authors' suggestion to artificially enforce mixing conditions via regularization is promising but risks over-regularization when combined with ridge regression, a concern not adequately addressed.

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution but falls short on practical applicability. While the results advance the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning, the disconnect between the required parameter constraints and real-world scenarios significantly diminishes the paper's significance for the machine learning community. The authors would need to demonstrate how to practically enforce fast-mixing conditions or provide empirical evidence that their bounds are not overly conservative in realistic settings.

In summary, this is a technically sound theoretical contribution with clear presentation (Quality: strong, Clarity: good), but its significance is limited by the impracticality of the assumptions. The originality is high within the theoretical framework, but the field would benefit more from work bridging theory and practice. I recommend rejection due to limited practical relevance despite strong theoretical foundations.
