The paper proposes learning exponential-family models by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing set” Θ for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically at a uniform rate, and then performing projected gradient descent where the model expectation in the gradient is approximated by MCMC samples. Under bounded sufficient statistics and uniform mixing over Θ, the authors derive nonasymptotic, high-probability error bounds: (i) for the convex (unregularized) case, an ε-accurate solution in log-likelihood with total work KMv scaling roughly as 1/ε3 up to logs; (ii) for the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case, an ε-accurate solution in parameter distance with total work scaling as 1/ε2 up to logs. This yields an FPRAS inside Θ. Results are framed generally for exponential families and are illustrated on a small Ising example. The paper relates in part to NIPS work on pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, and CD, and to mixing-time bounds for spin systems.

Relation to prior work: The algorithmic template is closely related to long-standing approaches for optimizing intractable likelihoods with MCMC-based gradient surrogates, including MCML (Geyer, 1991), MCEM (Wei & Tanner, 1990), and SAEM (Delyon, Lavielle & Moulines, 1999). Theoretical analyses of stochastic approximation with Markovian noise (Benveniste, Métiivier & Priouret, 1990; Kushner & Yin, 2003; Andrieu, Moulines & Priouret, 2005) provide convergence guarantees under geometric ergodicity without requiring uniform mixing constants or reinitialization at each iteration, and cover persistent sampling schemes. Persistent Contrastive Divergence (Tieleman, 2008) and analyses of CD-type updates (e.g., Yuille, 2005; Carreira-Perpiñán & Hinton, 2005) are also directly relevant for reusing previous samples. There is also a substantial literature on nonasymptotic error bounds for MCMC averages under mixing conditions (e.g., regeneration, spectral gap, or mixing-time-based inequalities), which can be brought to bear without uniform ergodicity assumptions.

Strengths
- Clear high-probability, finite-time bounds linking optimization error to MCMC mixing properties; explicit schedules for K, M, v.
- General exponential-family formulation; decouples model structure from the sampler via a fast-mixing parameter set.
- Useful conceptual connection between projection onto Θ (mixing-aware modeling) and learnability guarantees.
- The regularized case leverages strong convexity to obtain improved complexity.

Weaknesses
- Limited awareness and positioning relative to extensive prior work on MCML/MCEM/SAEM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics; many results appear to be rediscoveries under stronger assumptions.
- Uniform geometric ergodicity over Θ and reinitialization from a fixed distribution r at every iteration are unnecessary and restrictive. Persistent chains initialized from previous states have been extensively analyzed and often perform better.
- The FPRAS claim critically assumes that projection onto Θ is efficiently computable; its complexity is not accounted for in end-to-end guarantees.
- Presentation gaps: key assumptions for Theorems 1–2 are scattered and should be stated explicitly at the theorems; the dependence of constants on dimension N, bounds R, and mixing parameters (C, α) needs tightening.
- Empirical validation is minimal; no comparison to MCEM/SAEM/MCML, pseudolikelihood/score matching, or persistent-CD variants.

Quality
- The analyses appear sound under the stated assumptions, but several are stronger than needed. Established SA theory (Benveniste et al., Kushner & Yin; Andrieu et al.) can yield convergence without uniform mixing and without reinitialization, and can handle Markovian noise directly. Nonasymptotic concentration for Markov chains (e.g., spectral-gap or regeneration techniques) could replace the TV-based uniform bounds to broaden applicability.
- The Lipschitz gradient result follows standard exponential-family properties under bounded sufficient statistics; please state the boundedness assumption and constants with the theorem.

Clarity
- Define all assumptions (bounded t, uniform mixing over Θ, projection oracle) right before the relevant theorems; collect them as Assumptions A1–A4.
- Provide pseudocode with the sampling and projection steps, and specify whether chains are independent across steps or allowed to be persistent.
- Discuss the computational complexity of projection operators for the examples considered (e.g., spectral-norm projection for Ising models).

Originality
- Framing learning with projection onto fast-mixing sets is a nice perspective, but the optimization-with-MCMC methodology and convergence arguments have strong overlap with existing SA/MCEM/MCML theory. The main novelty is the specific nonasymptotic scheduling in terms of mixing-time parameters and the FPRAS framing inside Θ.

Significance
- Potentially useful for researchers seeking explicit finite-time guarantees tied to mixing-time constants; however, practical impact is limited unless the theory is extended to more realistic (non-uniformly ergodic, persistent) regimes and accompanied by substantive empirical comparisons.

Suggestions to improve
- Situate the work with respect to MCEM (Wei & Tanner, 1990), SAEM (Delyon et al., 1999), MCML (Geyer, 1991; Gu & Zhu, 2001), and SA with Markovian noise (Benveniste et al., 1990; Kushner & Yin, 2003; Andrieu et al., 2005). Clarify what is new beyond these and why the stronger assumptions are justified.
- Replace the uniform mixing assumption by geometric ergodicity with parameter-dependent rates, and analyze persistent chains initialized from θk−1 samples; cite analyses of persistent CD (Tieleman, 2008) and CD convergence results (Yuille, 2005; Carreira-Perpiñán & Hinton, 2005).
- Provide end-to-end complexity including projection cost; discuss feasibility for common Θ (e.g., induced norm constraints, ferromagnetic cones).
- Empirically compare against MCEM/SAEM, MCML, pseudolikelihood/score matching, and persistent CD on moderate-size Ising/Potts models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, finite-time, high-probability guarantees for a widely used heuristic (MCMC-approximate gradients).
- General exponential-family setting with explicit schedules and dependence on mixing-time parameters.
- Conceptual contribution tying learnability to fast-mixing parameter projections.

Arguments against acceptance
- Marginal novelty relative to established SA/MCEM/MCML literature with Markovian dynamics.
- Strong, sometimes unnecessary assumptions (uniform ergodicity; reinitialization) limit applicability.
- Insufficient engagement with related work; missing comparisons and practical validation.
- Presentation gaps and incomplete accounting of projection complexity.

Overall: borderline leaning to reject. With a thorough literature integration, relaxation of assumptions to standard Markovian SA settings (persistent chains, geometric ergodicity), clearer statements of assumptions, and stronger empirical comparisons, the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
