

# Review for "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing" set where MCMC sampling converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The authors present a theoretical guarantee that gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation provides a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within such parameter sets. This represents a fundamentally different approach from the common practice of restricting to tree-structured parameters (small treewidth), which has been explored in works like Chow-Liu (1968) and Heinemann and Globerson (2014).

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) it establishes theoretical guarantees for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases of the maximum likelihood problem, (2) it demonstrates that the total computational effort required is polynomial in 1/ε for ε-accuracy, and (3) it provides a clear connection between theoretical guarantees and practical implementation through a simple Ising model example.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors carefully analyze the convergence properties of gradient descent with approximate gradients, building on Schmidt et al.'s work on gradient descent with errors. The theoretical results are well-motivated and the proofs, while not fully detailed in the main text, appear sound. The authors are notably candid about limitations, including the assumption that Θ must be convex (which is missing from the main text but clarified in the appendix), the difficulty of verifying fast-mixing conditions in practice, and the conservatism of their theoretical bounds. The example experiment effectively demonstrates the feasibility of the approach, though it's limited in scope.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. However, there are several important omissions that reduce clarity. The paper never explicitly states that the log-likelihood is convex (which follows from the convexity of A(θ), the log-partition function), and the key assumption that Θ must be convex is missing from the main text (only appearing in the appendix). Several minor typos and wording inconsistencies (noted in lines 185, 192, 207, 281, 311-312) also detract from the presentation. A clearer summary of all assumptions at the beginning would significantly improve readability.

**Originality**: This work represents a substantial theoretical contribution with clear novelty. While MCMC-based learning is common in practice, the authors provide the first theoretical guarantees for this approach under a fast-mixing assumption. The alternative tractability notion (fast-mixing parameters instead of small treewidth) is fundamentally different from previous work, as demonstrated by the authors' careful comparison to tree-structured approaches. The work is clearly distinct from recent NIPS papers on graphical model learning, including those by Abbeel et al. (2006), Mizrahi et al. (2014), and Steinhardt and Liang (2014).

**Significance**: The theoretical significance is high, as the paper establishes a rigorous connection between MCMC mixing time and the convergence of gradient-based learning. However, the practical significance is limited. The authors acknowledge that verifying fast-mixing conditions is generally difficult, effectively replacing one challenging problem (small-treewidth learning) with another (fast-mixing verification). While the framework provides valuable insights into contrastive divergence-like algorithms (which the authors clarify is not exactly what they analyze), it doesn't offer a practical new learning algorithm. The approach could be useful for theoretical analysis of existing methods but has limited direct application.

**Strengths**: 
- Novel theoretical framework for understanding MCMC-based learning
- Rigorous guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases
- Clear connection between theoretical analysis and practical implementation
- Candid discussion of limitations and future work
- Well-executed example demonstrating the approach

**Weaknesses**: 
- Critical assumption (Θ must be convex) missing from main text
- Limited practical impact due to difficulty of verifying fast-mixing conditions
- Theoretical bounds are somewhat conservative (as acknowledged by authors)
- Minor typographical errors throughout

**Recommendation**: This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of MCMC-based learning. While the practical impact is limited, the theoretical insights are valuable for the field. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission, though the limited practical significance should be acknowledged. The authors have made an important contribution to the theoretical understanding of a widely used learning strategy.

The paper should be accepted as it represents a substantial theoretical advance with clear novelty, sound technical quality, and significant theoretical implications, even if its immediate practical utility is limited. The authors have provided a valuable framework for understanding MCMC-based learning that will inform future work in the area.
