This paper presents a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models with high treewidth, where exact inference is intractable. The authors propose an alternative notion of a tractable set, namely a set of "fast-mixing parameters" where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution. They prove that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, gradient descent with gradients approximated by sampling will approximate the maximum likelihood solution inside the set with high-probability.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear plausible, although not fully verified by this reviewer. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, and their approach is well-motivated by the limitations of existing methods. The main results are presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner, and the authors provide a useful discussion of the implications of their findings.

The key contribution of this paper is the proof that MCMC-based weight learning is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) when the parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set. This is a significant theoretical formalization of the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning yields a FPRAS. The authors also derive separate theoretical bounds for the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized (non-convex) case, which provides a more comprehensive understanding of the problem.

However, there are some concerns regarding the practical relevance of the results. The parameter constraints required for the theorems are unlikely to be satisfied in practice, which limits the applicability of the results. Furthermore, the authors do not provide any empirical evidence to support their claims, which would be useful in demonstrating the practical utility of their approach.

One open question that arises from this work is whether it is possible to enforce the mixing condition artificially, for example, through some form of regularization. This could potentially provide a more practical solution to the problem, but it is unclear how this would interact with the ridge regularization used in the paper. The authors warn that combining these two forms of regularization might over-regularize the model, which could have negative consequences for the accuracy of the learned model.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based weight learning. However, the practical relevance of the results is limited by the restrictive assumptions required for the theorems. Therefore, this reviewer would rate the quality of the paper as follows:

* Quality: 8/10
* Clarity: 9/10
* Originality: 8/10
* Significance: 7/10

Overall, this is a solid theoretical paper that provides a significant contribution to the field, but significant non-trivial research is needed to make the results practically useful.
