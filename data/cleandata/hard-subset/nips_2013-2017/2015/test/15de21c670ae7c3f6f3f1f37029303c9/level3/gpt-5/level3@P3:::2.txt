Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a new tractable regime for maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families: instead of restricting to small treewidth (e.g., trees/low-girth), it constrains parameters to a fast-mixing set Θ for which an MCMC kernel mixes geometrically in total variation. Under bounded sufficient statistics and the ability to project onto Θ, the authors analyze projected gradient descent that uses MCMC-based, biased gradient estimates, and—by combining concentration bounds with the inexact proximal-gradient framework of Schmidt et al. (NIPS 2011)—prove end-to-end guarantees. They obtain an FPRAS: for the unregularized (convex) case they achieve ε-accuracy in log-likelihood with total sampling effort Õ(ε−3), and for the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case they achieve ε-accuracy in parameter distance with Õ(ε−2) sampling effort. The analysis covers general exponential families, not just graphical models; the Ising model serves as a canonical example with known fast-mixing sets and projections (e.g., entrywise clipping or spectral-norm constraints, building on Hayes; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Domke–Liu; Liu–Domke).

This work complements a long line of tractable learning alternatives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, high-girth models, local/parallel methods), and relates to MCMC-MLE and CD-type procedures (Geyer; Tieleman; Hinton; Steinhardt–Liang’s restart chains). It also connects, conceptually, to classical results on FPRAS for Ising partition functions in special regimes (Jerrum–Sinclair), but here the goal is approximate MLE subject to a fast-mixing constraint, with explicit finite-sample optimization schedules for iterations, sample sizes, and chain lengths.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated alternative to treewidth-based tractability; framed broadly for exponential families with bounded statistics.
- Solid, careful theory yielding FPRAS-style guarantees: convex case (likelihood error on averaged iterates) and strongly convex case (parameter error for the final iterate), with explicit schedules for K, M, v and near-matching lower bounds up to logs/constant factors.
- Nontrivial synthesis of tools: mixing-time bounds, concentration (Hoeffding, Bernstein, Efron–Stein), and inexact projected gradient descent (Schmidt et al., 2011).
- Honest discussion of limitations and connections to CD-type algorithms; useful conceptual guidance even if the analyzed algorithm is not exactly CD.
- A simple experiment that illustrates the setting and the conservatism of the bounds.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Practical impact is limited: certifying fast mixing (and obtaining usable C, α) is hard in general, so the approach often swaps one intractable step (exact inference) for another (proving mixing). The analysis is still valuable as a clean reformulation that could guide algorithm design.
- Assumptions and convexity not summarized prominently. It would help to explicitly state early that the (negative) log-likelihood f(θ) = A(θ) − θ·t̄ + (λ/2)∥θ∥2 is convex because A is convex with Hessian equal to Covθ[t(X)] (and strongly convex when λ > 0). Also clearly list all assumptions in one place, including bounded statistics, known mixing constants, independence of samples across iterations, and projection feasibility.
- Convexity of Θ: the main text should clearly require Θ to be convex (needed for projection and the analysis). While convexity is used later, it should be emphasized upfront and in the problem setup.
- Dependence on unknown constants and pessimistic bounds: L = 4R2^2 + λ and TV mixing constants can be overly conservative; discuss how to set/estimate them in practice and the sensitivity of schedules to misspecification.
- Limited empirical validation: only a small Ising example; no comparisons to pseudolikelihood, CD, or “pool”-based MCMC-MLE.
- Some algorithmic choices common in practice (persistent chains/pools) are out of scope of the proof technique; note this more clearly in the experimental section.
- Minor typos/wording: l185, l192, l207, l281, l311–312.

Quality
Technically sound overall; proofs are careful and leverage appropriate tools. Guarantees are precise, with matching lower bounds that clarify optimality up to logs. The authors candidly discuss limitations and scope. The empirical section is minimal but consistent with the theoretical focus.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with helpful proof sketches and intuition. Clarity would improve by:
- Stating convexity of A(θ) and f(θ) up front (via Hessian = covariance) and summarizing all assumptions in a dedicated box/table.
- Highlighting differences between guarantees: averaged iterate f-suboptimality (convex) vs. last iterate parameter error (strongly convex).
- Explaining projection operators for common Θ beyond Ising (or citing practical solvers).

Originality
The core idea—tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets with end-to-end approximate MLE guarantees—is new and interesting. The analysis is a thoughtful integration of known techniques applied in a new way.

Significance
Conceptually significant but practically constrained by the challenge of certifying fast mixing and estimating constants. Nonetheless, the perspective may inspire new regularizers/constraints that promote mixing and inform analyses of CD-like methods.

Detailed suggestions for the authors
- State explicitly: (i) A is convex with Hessian = Covθ[t(X)]; hence f is convex and λ-strongly convex when λ > 0. (ii) Θ is assumed convex and projectable; summarize all assumptions (bounded t, known C, α, geometric mixing for all θ ∈ Θ, independence of samples across iterations).
- Discuss how to choose and project onto Θ in general exponential families (beyond pairwise Ising), with practical algorithms and complexity.
- Provide guidance on estimating L, C, α (e.g., empirical diagnostics, conservative surrogates), and how schedules degrade if they are misspecified.
- Add small-scale empirical comparisons to pseudolikelihood and CD; include a “persistent pool” variant discussion with pointers to where analysis breaks.
- Fix minor typos and consider consolidating notation (e.g., R2 vs. R).

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and well-argued tractability notion; clean FPRAS guarantees.
- Technically solid; near-optimal schedules and bounds.
- Broad applicability to exponential families; insightful discussion of CD-type methods.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited practical impact due to difficulty of certifying fast mixing and unknown constants.
- Sparse empirical validation; some clarity omissions and assumption summaries missing.

Overall verdict
An original and substantial theoretical contribution that is clearly written and carefully analyzed, but with limited immediate practical value. I lean toward a weak accept for its conceptual novelty and solid theory, provided the clarity issues and assumption summaries are addressed.
