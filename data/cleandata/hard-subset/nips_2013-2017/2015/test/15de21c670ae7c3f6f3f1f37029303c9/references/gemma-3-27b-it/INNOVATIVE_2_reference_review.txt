This paper tackles a fundamental problem in graphical model learning: maximum likelihood estimation when exact computation is intractable. The core idea – leveraging fast-mixing Markov chains to enable a FPRAS for ML estimation within a constrained parameter space – is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from existing approaches like pseudolikelihood or score matching. The authors correctly identify the limitations of these alternatives, particularly their statistical inefficiency and lack of guarantees when the model is misspecified. Framing the problem within the broader exponential family context adds to the generality and potential impact. The theoretical results, establishing a FPRAS under fast-mixing conditions, are substantial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of connecting fast-mixing MCMC with a FPRAS for constrained ML estimation is highly original. This isn’t simply incremental improvement; it’s a fundamentally different way to approach the problem.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for its claims, including Lipschitz gradient results and convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases. The bounds on computational complexity (KMv) are well-defined and insightful.
*   **Generality:**  Extending beyond graphical models to the broader exponential family framework increases the applicability of the results.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the shortcomings of existing methods and motivates the need for a new approach.
*   **Detailed Analysis:** The extensive background section on mixing times and related results demonstrates a deep understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality Concerns:** While theoretically sound, the practical implications are somewhat unclear. The constant factors hidden within the complexity bounds (e.g., involving *R*, *L*, and mixing rates) could be substantial, potentially rendering the method computationally expensive in practice. The example provided, while illustrative, doesn't fully address this concern.
*   **Fast-Mixing Assumption:** The reliance on a pre-existing guarantee of fast mixing within the constraint set Θ is a critical assumption. Finding such a set, and demonstrating its fast-mixing properties, is often a non-trivial task in itself. The paper provides examples (Ising model), but the applicability to more complex graphical models isn't fully explored.  The projection step, while acknowledged, isn't thoroughly analyzed for computational cost.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The single example is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the approach. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation on a variety of graphical models would be beneficial.
*   **Connection to Existing Work on Contrastive Divergence:** The discussion of contrastive divergence feels somewhat cursory. A deeper engagement with how this approach compares to, or can improve upon, existing CD-based methods would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to ML estimation in constrained parameter spaces.
*   The theoretical results are strong and well-supported.
*   The generality of the framework is a significant advantage.
*   The work opens up new avenues for research in constrained ML learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Practical concerns regarding computational cost and the fast-mixing assumption.
*   Limited empirical evaluation.
*   The potential for the constant factors in the complexity bounds to be prohibitive in practice.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty and theoretical rigor of the approach are compelling. While the practical implications remain somewhat uncertain, the potential benefits are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. I'm willing to overlook the limited empirical evaluation and the potential for high constant factors, given the boldness of the idea and the strength of the theoretical analysis.  This work shifts the conversation around ML estimation in intractable models and deserves to be presented at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I encourage the authors to address the practical concerns and expand the empirical evaluation in future work. A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of projection would also be helpful.
