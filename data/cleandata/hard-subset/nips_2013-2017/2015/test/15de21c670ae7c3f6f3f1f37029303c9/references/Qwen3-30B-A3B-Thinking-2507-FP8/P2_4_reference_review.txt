

# Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary
This paper addresses the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by proposing a novel approach based on restricting parameters to "fast-mixing" sets where MCMC sampling converges rapidly. The authors establish theoretical guarantees for gradient descent using MCMC to approximate gradients, showing that with appropriate settings of iterations, samples, and Markov transitions, the algorithm converges to a near-optimal solution with high probability. The work extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families and provides explicit bounds on computational complexity for both convex and strongly convex cases.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors carefully establish the Lipschitz property of the gradient (Theorem 1) and provide thorough convergence guarantees for both convex (Theorem 2-5) and strongly convex (Theorem 6-9) cases. The proof sketches are well-structured and provide sufficient intuition for the main results. The paper acknowledges limitations such as the assumption of efficient projection (Section 7) and the conservative nature of theoretical bounds (Section 6), demonstrating appropriate scholarly rigor.

The theoretical bounds are properly derived from mixing time assumptions and the properties of gradient descent with noisy gradients. The authors correctly cite relevant prior work (Jerrum and Sinclair [1993], Domke and Liu [2013], Steinhardt and Liang [2014]) and position their contribution within the broader literature on tractable graphical models.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Key notation is consistently defined early, and the main results are presented with precise statements. The example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the theoretical bounds with concrete numbers for an Ising model, making the abstract results more tangible. The proof sketches provide useful intuition without overwhelming the reader with technical details.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how to project onto the fast-mixing parameter set for general exponential families beyond the Ising model example. While the Ising model example is helpful, a brief discussion of projection complexity for other models would strengthen the practical relevance of the approach.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by introducing the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" as a new notion of tractability for maximum likelihood learning. While previous work has focused on tree-structured models (Chow-Liu) or bounded treewidth (Heinemann and Globerson), this paper takes a fundamentally different approach based on MCMC mixing times. The connection between fast mixing and efficient learning is novel and extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families.

The paper's theoretical framework for analyzing gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximations represents a significant conceptual advance over existing approaches that lack theoretical guarantees. The explicit computational complexity bounds (KMv) for achieving a given accuracy represent a concrete contribution to understanding the trade-offs in MCMC-based learning.

## Significance
The significance of this work lies in providing the first theoretical guarantees for a widely used practical approach to maximum likelihood learning in intractable models. Many practitioners use MCMC-based gradient estimation without theoretical justification, and this paper establishes when and how such approaches can be efficient.

The computational complexity bounds (polynomial in 1/ϵ) are significant as they show that MCMC-based learning can be efficient under appropriate conditions. The paper demonstrates that for strongly convex cases, the work required for ϵθ-optimality is only linear in 1/ϵf (compared to cubic for convex), which is an important practical improvement.

The work provides a valuable framework for understanding and improving existing MCMC-based learning algorithms, including variants of contrastive divergence. While the paper acknowledges it makes no practical contribution, the theoretical foundation it provides could guide future practical implementations.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis of MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning
- Novel "fast-mixing parameter sets" framework for tractability
- Explicit computational complexity bounds for both convex and strongly convex cases
- Well-motivated example with concrete numbers for an Ising model
- Careful acknowledgment of limitations and future directions

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited empirical validation beyond a single example
- Projection complexity is assumed efficient but not thoroughly analyzed for general cases
- The Ising model example uses a very small grid (4x4), which may not reflect real-world challenges
- No comparison with existing methods in terms of practical performance

## Recommendation
This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of when and how MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning can be efficient. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a novel contribution to the field. While the lack of empirical validation is a limitation, the theoretical results are sufficiently strong and novel to merit publication at NIPS.

The paper clearly addresses a significant problem in graphical models with a theoretically rigorous approach that extends beyond traditional tractability notions. The explicit bounds on computational complexity provide valuable guidance for practitioners and future theoretical work.

I recommend acceptance. The paper represents a significant theoretical advance that could influence both the theory and practice of maximum likelihood learning in intractable models. The authors have made a clear contribution to the field with well-justified results and thoughtful analysis of the limitations of their approach.
