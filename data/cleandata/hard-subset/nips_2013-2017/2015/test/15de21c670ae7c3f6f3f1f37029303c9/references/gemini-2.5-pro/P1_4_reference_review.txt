### Summary

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in general undirected graphical models. Instead of restricting the model structure to be tractable (e.g., low treewidth), the authors propose a novel approach: restricting the model parameters to a convex set Θ where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to be fast-mixing. The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a projected gradient descent algorithm where the gradient is estimated using MCMC samples. The authors prove that this common heuristic is, in fact, a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within the constrained set Θ. They provide explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples (M), and chain transitions (v) required to achieve a desired accuracy ε with high probability. The analysis is performed separately for the general convex case (unregularized MLE) and the strongly convex case (ridge-regularized MLE), yielding different convergence rates and complexity bounds. This work provides a formal theoretical foundation for a widely used but often poorly understood class of learning algorithms.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Important Theoretical Contribution:** The core idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets rather than structural properties like treewidth is a significant conceptual contribution. It formalizes a popular and powerful heuristic (using MCMC to approximate gradients for MLE) and provides the first, to my knowledge, rigorous end-to-end convergence guarantee for this procedure. This bridges the gap between the theory of MCMC mixing times and the theory of convex optimization with inexact gradients.

2.  **Rigorous and Complete Analysis:** The paper is technically sound. The authors carefully decompose the error in the gradient estimate into a bias term (from finite MCMC transitions `v`) and a variance term (from a finite number of samples `M`). They leverage appropriate concentration inequalities and existing results on the convergence of projected gradient descent with errors (Schmidt et al. [26]) to derive explicit complexity bounds. The separate, detailed treatment of the convex and strongly convex cases is thorough and insightful.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the setup and notation are defined precisely, and the main theorems are stated upfront with accompanying proof sketches that provide excellent intuition. The connection to existing work on mixing times for models like the Ising model is well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Strong Assumptions for Practical Application:** The analysis relies on knowing the convex set Θ, the mixing constants C and α, and the existence of an efficient projection operator Π_Θ. While the paper provides concrete examples, for many complex models of interest these quantities are unknown or the known bounds are extremely loose. This makes the derived schedules (Theorems 5 and Corollary 9) potentially too conservative for practical use, as acknowledged by the authors' own experiment where a much smaller Lipschitz constant worked better. The practicality of the *theory* is high, but the practicality of the resulting *schedules* is limited.

2.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a single, small-scale experiment on a 4x4 Ising model. While this serves as a useful sanity check and visualization, it does not demonstrate the practical utility of the approach compared to alternatives. A comparison against methods like pseudolikelihood or score matching, especially in a model-misspecified setting where this method is guaranteed to find the M-projection, would have significantly strengthened the paper.

3.  **Complexity of Projection:** The analysis focuses on the sampling complexity (KMv) and largely sidesteps the computational cost of the projection step. For some of the fast-mixing sets mentioned (e.g., based on the spectral norm of the interaction matrix), the projection can be computationally expensive, involving operations like SVD. This cost, which is incurred at each of the K iterations, could be significant and should be acknowledged more formally in the complexity analysis.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proofs appear sound, and the claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis. It is a complete piece of work that formally establishes a significant result. The authors are honest about the limitations, noting that the work is primarily theoretical and that the schedules may be conservative.

**Clarity:** The paper is written with outstanding clarity. The organization is logical, flowing from motivation to setup, background, main results, and discussion. The mathematical arguments in the proof sketches are intuitive and easy to grasp. The paper provides enough information for an expert to understand the theoretical contributions deeply.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While MCMC-based gradient estimation is not new (e.g., in training RBMs), the formal analysis framing it as an FPRAS for MLE within a fast-mixing set is novel. It connects disparate fields (mixing time analysis, convex optimization) in a new and meaningful way, providing a new perspective on tractability in graphical models that moves beyond graph structure. It is significantly different from prior work.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the machine learning theory community. This paper provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for a class of algorithms that are used in practice but have lacked formal guarantees. It opens up new avenues for research, such as finding broader classes of fast-mixing parameter sets or developing tighter, more practical convergence bounds. Researchers working on probabilistic modeling, MCMC, and optimization theory are likely to build on these ideas.

### Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Pros:**
*   Provides the first rigorous convergence guarantee for MLE via MCMC-approximated gradients.
*   Introduces a novel and powerful way of thinking about tractability in graphical models based on mixing time rather than treewidth.
*   The theoretical analysis is technically deep, sound, and complete.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.

**Cons:**
*   The practical applicability of the specific hyperparameter schedules is limited by strong assumptions.
*   The empirical validation is minimal and serves only as a proof of concept.
*   The complexity of the projection step is not fully incorporated into the analysis.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to our understanding of learning in intractable graphical models. Despite the practical limitations of the derived schedules, the conceptual framework and the rigorous convergence proof are of high scientific value. The paper is well-written, original, and significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.
