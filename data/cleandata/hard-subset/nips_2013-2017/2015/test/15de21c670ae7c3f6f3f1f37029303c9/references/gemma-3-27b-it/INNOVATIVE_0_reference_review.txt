This paper tackles a fundamental challenge in graphical model learning – maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) when exact computation is intractable. Instead of striving for exactness, the authors cleverly sidestep the intractability by focusing on *tractable* parameter sets defined not by structure (like tree-structured models) but by fast mixing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) convergence. This is a bold and conceptually elegant idea. The core result – a Fast Polynomial Random Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for MLE within such sets – is significant, providing a theoretical grounding for a widely practiced but often heuristically justified approach. 

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear motivation and outlining the limitations of existing approximate MLE methods. The mathematical setup is concise and the notation, while dense, is consistently used. The proofs, while relegated to the appendix, are accompanied by sufficient sketches to build intuition.  The connection to existing work on mixing times is well-established, and the examples (Ising model) are helpful in illustrating the applicability of the framework. The inclusion of both convex and strongly-convex analyses, and the resulting careful analysis of parameter settings (Theorems 3-5) demonstrate a rigorous approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of defining tractability through MCMC mixing is genuinely novel and offers a new perspective on learning in intractable models. It’s a shift away from focusing on structural constraints and towards algorithmic properties.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:**  The FPRAS result is a strong theoretical contribution, grounding a commonly used practice. The detailed analysis of convergence rates and the explicit schedules for parameters are impressive.
*   **Generality:** The framework extends beyond graphical models, applicable to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Despite the complexity, the paper is well-organized and explains the key ideas clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Impact – Remains Unclear:** While theoretically sound, the paper doesn’t fully address the practical challenges of *finding* suitable "fast-mixing" parameter sets Θ in real-world scenarios.  The examples provided are relatively simple. Identifying such sets for complex models could be difficult and computationally expensive itself.
*   **Mixing Rate Assumption:** The assumption of known constants C and α that govern mixing (Equation 2) is somewhat strong. In practice, estimating these parameters can be challenging.  The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t fully explore practical methods for estimating them.
*  **Computational Cost:** While the theoretical scaling is presented, the constants hidden within the scaling can be substantial, potentially limiting practical applicability for high-dimensional problems.
*   **Projection Complexity:** The paper briefly notes the potential cost of projection onto Θ. For some models, projection could be a bottleneck.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The paper introduces a truly novel concept and provides a solid theoretical foundation for a widely used technique.
*   The FPRAS result is significant and could open up new avenues for research in approximate inference and learning.
*   The rigorous mathematical analysis is commendable.

**Con:**

*   The practical applicability remains somewhat uncertain. More discussion of the challenges of finding suitable Θ in real-world scenarios would be beneficial.
*   The dependence on known mixing rate parameters is a potential limitation.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some practical limitations, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution. The novelty and theoretical rigor outweigh the weaknesses. The paper's exploration of tractability through MCMC mixing is a valuable contribution that shifts the paradigm on how we approach learning in intractable models. The authors haven't solved the practical problems of deploying this approach, but they've laid a critical theoretical foundation. I recommend **acceptance** with encouragement for future work to address the practical challenges. A minor suggestion would be to expand the discussion section to further acknowledge limitations and potential avenues for future research.
