Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a convex set Θ on which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes rapidly (in total variation at a geometric rate C α^v uniformly over Θ). Within this “fast-mixing parameter set,” the authors analyze projected gradient descent where the intractable gradient is approximated using finite-length MCMC, yielding biased, noisy estimates. They provide nonasymptotic guarantees for both the convex (unregularized) and λ-strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases: explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain length v that ensure an additive optimization error in objective (convex) or parameter distance (strongly convex) with high probability. They derive work bounds KMv that are polynomial in 1/ε (up to logs) and dependence on mixing constants, and they present lower bounds to argue near-optimality. They conclude this yields an FPRAS for MLE restricted to Θ. An illustrative Ising-model example is provided.

This connects several strands: (i) learning under tractable structure such as trees (Chow–Liu), high-girth/decay (Heinemann & Globerson), and projections to fast-mixing sets (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke); (ii) widely used but theoretically under-justified MCMC-MLE procedures (Geyer; Gu & Zhu) and contrastive divergence variants (Tieleman; Hinton et al.); and (iii) recent work that enforces fast mixing by construction (Steinhardt & Liang). The main novelty is a unified nonasymptotic analysis showing polynomial-time optimization under explicit mixing assumptions, with explicit schedules and lower bounds.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing: restrict to a tractable Θ defined by mixing-time guarantees; analyze the standard “MCMC-in-the-loop” gradient method.
- Generality: results stated for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics; the analysis does not rely on treewidth.
- Concrete, nonasymptotic guarantees: convex and strongly convex regimes, explicit dependence on K, M, v, and α, including constructive schedules and matching lower bounds (up to constants/logs).
- Good integration with mixing-time literature: shows how standard bounds (Dobrushin-type, spectral norm constraints) yield candidate Θ and even allow projections (e.g., Ising spectral-norm projection).
- Honest discussion of limitations (projection cost, initialization, pool-based sampling) and a small illustrative example.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality (technical soundness)
- The FPRAS claim needs tightening. Classically, an FPRAS provides a randomized algorithm whose runtime is polynomial in input size and 1/ε and returns a (1±ε)-relative approximation with high probability. Here guarantees are additive in objective or parameter distance. If you intend “polynomial-time approximation scheme” in an additive sense, please state that explicitly and formalize the runtime as polynomial in problem size (N, D, alphabet size) and 1/ε. As written, complexity is measured mainly in total Markov steps KMv; translating this to bit complexity and input size requires assumptions on per-step costs and on how α, C scale with N.
- Assumptions on known uniform mixing constants C, α over Θ are strong and typically conservative. In practice, α often degrades with N (e.g., α = 1 − Θ(1/N) for single-site Gibbs under Dobrushin). Please make explicit the dependence of K, M, v, and hence runtime, on graph size, degree, and alphabet. This is crucial to justify “polynomial time.”
- The Lipschitz-gradient bound (Theorem 1) seems conservative and its derivation via TV may be more complicated than necessary. Since ∇^2A(θ) = Cov[t(X)], one can bound the spectral norm by E||t||^2 ≤ R2^2 directly, giving a simpler and tighter L ≲ R2^2 + λ. If you keep the TV route, please clarify the step bounding ||pθ − pφ||TV ≤ 2R2||θ − φ||, which is nontrivial and may fail without additional assumptions.
- The guarantees rely on exact projection onto Θ. For the general pairwise case, the spectral-norm projection is convex but may be expensive; for multi-label or higher-order factors, construction of Θ and projection are less clear. The overall runtime should account for projection costs.

Clarity
- The presentation is generally clear, but several definitions and assumptions are implicit. Please: (i) precisely define the FPRAS notion used; (ii) list the per-iteration computational costs (sampling transition cost, projection, and gradient updates); (iii) include Algorithm 1 pseudocode; and (iv) gather all assumptions (bounded stats, known mixing constants, projection oracle) in one place.
- The example could report empirical scaling of error versus K, M, v and compare with the predicted rates to calibrate constants.

Originality
- The “fast mixing set + projection” idea has appeared (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), and using MCMC-in-the-loop for MLE is standard. The novelty here lies in a clean, nonasymptotic optimization analysis with explicit schedules and lower bounds, which seems incremental but meaningful.

Significance
- The theoretical contribution clarifies when the popular but biased MCMC gradient approach can be made provably reliable, given verifiable mixing assumptions. However, the practical reach is bounded by the restrictiveness of Θ (often small couplings) and by the difficulty of certifying/estimating C, α. The empirical section is too limited to inform practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, self-contained nonasymptotic analysis with constructive schedules and matching lower bounds.
- General exponential-family treatment and explicit links to mixing-time conditions and projections.
- Bridges an important gap in theory for MCMC-based MLE under realistic bias.

Cons for acceptance
- FPRAS claim is imprecise; dependence on problem size and cost model is not fully spelled out.
- Strong assumptions (known uniform mixing constants and exact projection); limited guidance on verifying them.
- Very modest empirical validation; limited practical impact without more evidence.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the approximation notion (additive vs relative) and provide a full runtime bound polynomial in N, D, alphabet size, 1/ε, and log(1/δ), explicitly substituting standard α(N) from Dobrushin/spectral-norm regimes.
- Replace or complement Theorem 1’s proof with a direct Hessian bound via Cov[t(X)], yielding tighter L and simplified presentation.
- Provide guidance (or oracles) for constructing Θ and performing projection for multi-label/higher-order models; discuss computational complexity of projection and its effect on overall runtime.
- Add experiments beyond a 4×4 grid: include larger graphs, varying degrees/couplings; compare to pseudolikelihood and CD in mis-specified regimes; empirically test scaling with ε and sensitivity to α mis-specification.
- Discuss extensions beyond bounded t(x), e.g., sub-Gaussian sufficient statistics, and whether similar rates hold with appropriate concentration tools.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The theoretical development is interesting and well-structured, but the FPRAS claim and runtime dependence on problem size need to be formalized, and the empirical section is too thin. With a clearer definition of the approximation scheme, explicit scaling in N, and stronger empirical evidence, this could be a strong resubmission.
