Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under an alternative notion of tractability: restricting parameters to a convex set Θ that guarantees fast mixing of a designated Markov chain (e.g., Gibbs). Within this set, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated by MCMC samples. Under bounded sufficient statistics and a geometric mixing assumption ∥Mvθq − pθ∥TV ≤ Cαv, they show high-probability convergence guarantees: (i) for the convex case (λ = 0), suboptimality in function value decreases with total work KMv roughly O(ε−3 log(1/ε)); (ii) for the strongly convex case (λ > 0), parameter error decreases with work O(ε−2 up to logs). They also provide lower bounds showing near-optimality (up to logarithms and constant factors) of their schedules; a Lipschitz bound for the gradient; and an illustrative Ising example. The results imply an FPRAS-style guarantee for maximum likelihood over Θ, assuming one can project onto Θ and that the chain mixes fast on Θ.

The work builds on: (a) fast-mixing parameter sets for Ising/MRFs (e.g., Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Hayes; Domke & Liu NIPS’13; Liu & Domke NIPS’14), including projections under spectral/induced norms; (b) MCMC-based maximum likelihood and stochastic approximation (e.g., Geyer ’91; Gu & Zhu ’01); (c) convergence of inexact/proximal gradient methods (Schmidt et al. NIPS’11); and (d) alternatives to likelihood such as pseudolikelihood, score/ratio matching, composite likelihood, and contrastive divergence (Hyvärinen; Marlin & de Freitas; Varin et al.; Hinton/Tieleman). The work is complementary to Steinhardt & Liang (ICML’15) who enforce fast mixing via restarts.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level arguments are standard but carefully assembled: (i) TV mixing controls bias in the Monte Carlo gradient via ∥Eqt[t(X)] − Epθ[t(X)]∥ ≤ 2R2 ∥q − pθ∥TV; (ii) concentration (Bernstein/Efron–Stein; Hoeffding) controls sampling noise; (iii) inexact projected gradient yields rates. The convex and strongly convex analyses are appropriate, and the lower bounds are informative. Assumptions (bounded statistics; projection onto Θ; geometric mixing with known C, α) are clearly stated.
- Caveats: The constants can be very loose (e.g., using worst-case mixing bounds), and the FPRAS claim rests on knowing/using C and α, which are rarely available tightly in practice. The independence across iterations (no persistent chains) is required by the proofs and can be costly. The notion of “FPRAS for maximum likelihood” is presented in additive-error optimization terms; this should be clarified since FPRAS is usually defined for relative error on function evaluation.

Clarity
- The writing is generally clear and well organized, with useful proof sketches. The setup and notation are mostly consistent. However:
  - R2 denotes a bound on ∥t(x)∥2, so expressions like R22 and 4R22 are potentially confusing; consider renaming the bound R or B and writing B2 explicitly.
  - Some theorem statements could be tightened for units/consistency (e.g., Theorem 2’s constants; define all symbols locally).
  - Algorithm 1 is referenced but not included in the excerpt; ensure the pseudocode appears and matches the analysis (averaging vs last iterate, step sizes, projection).

Originality
- The central idea—optimize ML within a fast-mixing parameter set and analyze MCMC-biased gradients under mixing assumptions—has appeared in pieces, but this paper gives a unified convergence analysis with explicit schedules and near-matching lower bounds for both convex and strongly convex cases. The “projection to fast mixing + inexact gradient descent” pipeline and its complexity quantification are a useful synthesis. That said, the algorithmic components (projection, MCMCML, inexact GD) are known; the novelty is primarily in the particular combination and the quantitative analysis.

Significance
- Conceptually valuable: It formalizes when the widely used “MCMC-in-the-loop” gradient method provably approximates ML, provided one restricts to a fast-mixing Θ and can project onto it.
- Practical impact may be limited: (i) Availability and tightness of C, α and projection operators for general exponential families; (ii) fast-mixing sets often enforce weak couplings that may degrade fit; (iii) independence across iterations is expensive compared to persistent chains; and (iv) only a toy example is provided, with no empirical study of performance vs pseudolikelihood/CD, or of the trade-off between projection tightness and fit.

Pros
- Clear, rigorous convergence analysis for ML with MCMC-biased gradients under fast-mixing constraints.
- General exponential-family treatment with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Explicit parameter schedules and complementary lower bounds (near-tight up to logs).
- Connects mixing-time literature to learning guarantees; leverages prior projection-to-mixing results.

Cons
- Strong assumptions: known geometric mixing constants; ability to project onto Θ; independent samples per iteration.
- Practical constants and total work likely very conservative; no empirical validation beyond a small Ising example.
- Limited algorithmic novelty; largely a unification of existing components.
- FPRAS terminology needs clarification (additive vs relative error; optimization vs evaluation).
- Minor clarity/notation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the FPRAS claim (precise definition: additive vs relative, and for optimization vs partition function evaluation).
- Provide empirical comparisons on synthetic and real datasets: (i) ML within Θ vs pseudolikelihood/score matching/composite likelihood; (ii) persistent vs non-persistent chains; (iii) different Θ (entrywise vs spectral norm).
- Discuss how to select Θ and estimate or avoid explicit C, α (e.g., adaptive v via autocorrelation or coupling diagnostics).
- Include the projection algorithms for general exponential families beyond pairwise discrete models, or scope the claims accordingly.
- Tighten constants (e.g., refine Lipschitz constant using model structure) and discuss step-size choices.
- Cite additional related work on MCMC with biased/noisy gradients and stochastic approximation with controlled Markov chains (e.g., Younes ’99; Andrieu, Moulines & Priouret 2005).

Recommendation: Weak reject
- The paper is technically solid and conceptually interesting, but the current version lacks empirical validation and relies on assumptions that limit practical significance. With stronger experiments, clarification of the FPRAS claim, and tighter discussion of constants/assumptions, it could be a valuable contribution.
