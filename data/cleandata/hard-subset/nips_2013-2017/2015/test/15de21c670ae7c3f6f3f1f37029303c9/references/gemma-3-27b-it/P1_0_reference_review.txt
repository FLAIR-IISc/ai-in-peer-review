## NIPS Review: Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, which is generally intractable. Instead of focusing on directly approximating the likelihood or resorting to suboptimal alternatives like pseudolikelihood, the authors propose a novel approach based on restricting the parameter space to a set Θ where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling mixes quickly. They demonstrate that if such a set exists and projection onto it is feasible, a Fast Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within Θ is guaranteed.  The work establishes theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation, providing bounds on the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC transitions required to achieve a desired level of accuracy. The analysis extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the broader landscape of approaches to address intractable maximum likelihood learning. It contrasts with methods like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods which, while offering computational efficiency, often suffer from statistical drawbacks and lack guarantees when the model is misspecified. It also distinguishes itself from methods aiming for tractable parameterizations (e.g., Chow-Liu algorithm), by focusing on *tractability through mixing*, rather than structural constraints. The work builds upon established results in MCMC convergence theory (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993) and stochastic gradient descent (Schmidt et al., 2013), providing a novel connection between fast mixing times and the feasibility of FPRAS for ML estimation. The authors correctly cite and contextualize prior work on mixing times for Ising models and related graphical models.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets to enable efficient ML learning is a significant contribution. It offers a fresh perspective on tackling intractable learning problems.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed approach, establishing an FPRAS and deriving bounds on convergence rates.  The rigorous analysis of gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation is commendable.
* **Generality:**  The framework extends beyond graphical models to encompass a broader class of exponential families, increasing its potential applicability.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and largely clearly written, with important notation defined upfront. The 'cartoon' illustration of the gradient descent process (Figure 1) is helpful.
* **Practical Relevance (Demonstrated):** The example with the Ising model, though limited in scope, provides some indication that the theoretical bounds are not overly conservative and demonstrates feasibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Projection Complexity:** The paper acknowledges the potential computational cost of projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ. While examples like the Ising model have established projection algorithms, the complexity of projection can vary significantly depending on the chosen Θ and may be a bottleneck in practice for more complex models. This point could be strengthened with a more detailed discussion.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** The theoretical bounds, while rigorous, appear to be quite loose, especially the dependence on the Lipschitz constant L. As acknowledged by the authors in the example section, using a smaller, empirically-derived L resulted in faster convergence, suggesting potential for tighter bounds.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The paper assumes an arbitrary starting distribution for MCMC. The impact of initialization on convergence speed and accuracy is not thoroughly investigated. As mentioned in the discussion, strategies like contrastive divergence could potentially improve initialization but are not formally analyzed.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical example is relatively simple and serves more as a proof-of-concept than a comprehensive evaluation. A more extensive experimental evaluation on diverse models and datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Discussion of Existing Constraints:** While the paper positions itself against simpler alternatives, it doesn't delve deeply into the tradeoffs between its approach (fast-mixing constraint) and other parameter space restrictions (e.g., low-treewidth models). A more nuanced comparison would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in statistical learning.
* The theoretical guarantees provide a strong foundation for future research in this area.
* The generality of the framework and potential applicability to a wide range of models are promising.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical bounds are potentially loose, and the computational cost of projection could be a significant practical limitation.
* The empirical evaluation is limited and doesn't fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
* Further investigation of initialization strategies and comparison with alternative parameter space constraints are needed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. While some practical considerations and limitations remain, the novelty and rigor of the work justify acceptance. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference and has the potential to inspire further research in the area of tractable maximum likelihood learning.

**Rating:** Accept (7/10)

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of projection onto different types of fast-mixing parameter sets.
* Investigate tighter bounds on the convergence rates and explore the impact of different choices for the Lipschitz constant.
* Conduct a more extensive empirical evaluation on diverse models and datasets.
* Explore the impact of different initialization strategies, such as contrastive divergence.
* Discuss the trade-offs between the fast-mixing constraint and other parameter space restrictions in more detail.
