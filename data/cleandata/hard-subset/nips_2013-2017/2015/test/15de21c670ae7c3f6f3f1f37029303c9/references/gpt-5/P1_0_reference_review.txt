Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families when exact inference is intractable, and proposes a different notion of tractability: constrain parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ such that an associated MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast for all θ ∈ Θ, and project onto Θ at each step. The main result is that, under bounded sufficient statistics and the ability to project onto Θ, a projected gradient method that estimates gradients with MCMC yields, with high probability, an FPRAS for the constrained MLE: total work KMv (K iterations, M samples per iteration, v transitions per sample) is polynomial in 1/ε and other problem parameters. Separate analyses are given for the convex (possibly unregularized) and strongly-convex (ridge-regularized) cases, using inexact gradient descent guarantees (Schmidt et al.), concentration bounds, and mixing-time assumptions. A Lipschitz bound on ∇f is provided. The paper connects to mixing-time literature (e.g., Gibbs/Swendsen-Wang on Ising/Potts), prior work on fast-mixing parameter projections (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), and learning fast-mixing models via restarts (Steinhardt & Liang). Compared to alternatives like pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, score matching, or tree/higher-girth restrictions, this approach directly optimizes the likelihood (within Θ) and guarantees tractable inference via fast mixing.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: reframes tractability for learning by constraining to fast-mixing parameter sets, instead of restricting graph structure or using surrogate criteria.
- Generality: results stated for general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics; covers Ising/Potts as special cases.
- Theoretical contribution: clear convergence bounds for convex and strongly convex cases; schedules for K, M, v; and matching lower bounds up to constants/logs. The “FPRAS for constrained MLE” framing is appealing.
- Links disparate areas: learning with biased stochastic gradients, mixing times, and projections to contractive sets.
- Clear articulation of limitations (projection cost, initialization effects, persistent sample pools) and relation to practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality of assumptions: the guarantees require a known convex Θ with known mixing constants (C, α) and an efficient projection ΠΘ. For general exponential families beyond pairwise MRFs, both the existence of useful Θ with explicit mixing bounds and efficient projections are unclear. Even in Ising/Potts, spectral-norm based Θ can be conservative and the projection may be nontrivial computationally.
- Strength of constants: The bounds (e.g., L = 4R2^2 + λ) and mixing-derived schedules can be very conservative, leading to large KMv. The single toy example hints at this conservatism.
- FPRAS claim: While total work is polynomial in 1/ε and other parameters, the paper largely abstracts away input-size dependence (dimension N, number of sufficient statistics, data size D) and the cost of projection and sampling steps. For a formal FPRAS claim, the runtime must be polynomial in input size and 1/ε; clarifying this dependence would strengthen the claim.
- Limited empirical validation: Only a small 4×4 Ising example is shown; no comparisons to pseudolikelihood, CD, or restart-based methods, and no demonstration that projection to Θ improves practical mixing/learning trade-offs.
- Technical details deferred: Several key inequalities (e.g., ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2R2∥θ − φ∥2 used for Lipschitzness) are asserted with “some effort,” but this step is nontrivial and should be checked carefully; alternative routes via Pinsker + KL bounds may yield different constants.

Quality
- The analysis is careful and builds on standard tools: inexact projected gradient descent, concentration inequalities (Efron–Stein, Bernstein, Hoeffding), and geometric mixing. The decomposition of bias/variance errors and schedules is well executed, including lower bounds.
- Some technical steps deserve fuller proofs in the main text or at least clearer sketches (the TV-parameter distance bound; how R2 bounds propagate to KL/TV).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; notation is heavy but mostly well defined. The exposition of mixing-time assumptions and how they translate into α, C for different chains is useful.
- Suggestions: clarify what is counted in “total work” (random-bit complexity? transitions only?), give explicit dependence on N, D, and the number of sufficient statistics; restate Algorithm 1 clearly; and make the FPRAS definition precise in the context of learning.

Originality
- The synthesis—projecting to fast-mixing Θ plus an FPRAS-style learning guarantee for arbitrary treewidth exponential families—appears novel relative to Domke & Liu (projection for fast mixing, but not MLE FPRAS) and Steinhardt & Liang (learn fast-mixing models with restarts, not constrained MLE with tight schedules/lower bounds). The lower bounds matching schedules are a nice addition.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is solid: it offers a principled, analyzable path to maximum-likelihood learning when exact inference is hard, with guarantees that tie computational effort to mixing properties. It may spur further work on defining/learning useful Θ for broader model classes and on adaptive schemes that estimate mixing on the fly.
- Practical impact is limited at present due to conservative constants and the challenge of specifying Θ with tractable projection in large, rich models.

Questions for the authors
- How can one estimate or adaptively tune C and α in practice? Can the algorithm be made adaptive without knowing them a priori?
- For exponential families beyond pairwise discrete MRFs, what examples admit both (i) explicit fast-mixing Θ with known projections and (ii) known mixing bounds?
- Can you clarify and/or provide an alternative proof for the TV bound used in Theorem 1 (perhaps via Pinsker + smoothness of A)?
- How does the runtime scale explicitly with N, number of sufficient statistics, and D? Under what conditions does the overall algorithm meet the formal FPRAS definition?
- Can the analysis be extended to persistent chains or sample pools across iterations?

Pros
- Clear, novel theoretical contribution connecting fast mixing to ML learning with near-tight complexity.
- Applicable to general exponential families with bounded stats.
- Careful schedules and lower bounds; honest discussion of limitations.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on strong, sometimes impractical assumptions (known mixing constants; efficient projection).
- Weak empirical support; unclear practical competitiveness.
- Some technical steps lack detail in the main text; FPRAS claim would benefit from explicit input-size scaling.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and framing are interesting and relevant to NeurIPS’ core topics, even if empirical impact is modest. Strengthening the clarity around assumptions, formalizing the FPRAS claim with input-size dependence, and adding more empirical evidence would further improve the paper.
