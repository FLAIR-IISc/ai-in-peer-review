

# Peer Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical model learning: the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models. The authors propose a novel approach to tractability by focusing on the mixing time of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) samplers rather than the treewidth of the graph. The core insight is that if the parameter set Θ is constrained to be "fast-mixing" (meaning MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution), then maximum likelihood learning can be performed efficiently with theoretical guarantees.

The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing that under the fast-mixing assumption, there exists a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning inside Θ. The authors provide detailed bounds on the number of gradient descent iterations K, the number of samples M, and the number of Markov chain transitions v required to achieve a given optimization accuracy ϵ. The results hold for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases, with computational complexities of (1/ϵ_f)^3 log(1/ϵ_f) and (1/ϵ_θ)^2 log(1/ϵ_θ) respectively.

This work builds on and extends the literature on mixing times in Markov chains (Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993; Domke and Liu, 2013; Liu and Domke, 2014) by applying it to the maximum likelihood learning problem. It differs from previous approaches that focus on low-treewidth models (Chow-Liu algorithm) or well-specified models with alternative estimators (pseudolikelihood, score matching).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the proposed method. The proofs are well-structured, with clear proof sketches that provide intuition for the main results. The authors carefully establish the Lipschitz continuity of the gradient (Theorem 1) and provide tight bounds on the convergence rates (Theorems 2-9).

2. **Clear Framework**: The paper establishes a clear theoretical framework for understanding when MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning can be efficient. The fast-mixing parameter set assumption is well-motivated and connects to existing results on mixing times in the literature.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The paper addresses a real practical challenge in graphical model learning: the intractability of exact inference. While the authors acknowledge this is primarily a theoretical contribution, the bounds they derive provide concrete guidance for practitioners on how to set hyperparameters for MCMC-based learning.

4. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The paper thoroughly analyzes both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases, providing separate bounds for each. The discussion of the relationship between log-likelihood optimality (ϵ_f) and parameter distance (ϵ_θ) is particularly insightful.

5. **Good Connection to Related Work**: The paper effectively situates its contribution within the broader literature on graphical models, mixing times, and MCMC-based learning, citing relevant work from NIPS and other venues.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: While the paper includes a simple Ising model example (Section 6), it lacks substantial empirical evaluation. The example uses a very small 4×4 grid with only 5 training points, which is insufficient to demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation on larger datasets would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. **Limited Discussion of Practical Constraints**: The paper doesn't adequately address the practical challenges of implementing the proposed method. For instance:
   - It doesn't discuss how to choose the fast-mixing parameter set Θ in practice for different model classes.
   - It doesn't address how to handle cases where the fast-mixing assumption doesn't hold (which would be common in practice).
   - The projection step (required to maintain parameters in Θ) is mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed for computational complexity.

3. **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods**: The paper doesn't compare the proposed method to existing MCMC-based learning approaches (e.g., contrastive divergence, pseudo-likelihood) in terms of practical performance. While the paper claims no significant practical contribution, a brief comparison would help contextualize the theoretical results.

4. **Simplified Example**: The example in Section 6 is too simplistic to be convincing. The authors use L = 10 instead of the theoretically derived L = 97, which suggests the theoretical bounds might be conservative. A more realistic example with larger datasets would better demonstrate the practical relevance.

5. **Overemphasis on Complexity Bounds**: While the complexity bounds are theoretically interesting, the paper doesn't sufficiently address whether these bounds are achievable in practice. The example shows the algorithm converges faster than the theoretical bound, but this isn't explored in depth.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add Empirical Results**: Include experiments on larger datasets (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10) with more realistic model structures to demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.

2. **Discuss Practical Implementation**: Elaborate on how to choose Θ for different model classes and how to handle cases where the fast-mixing assumption doesn't hold. Discuss the computational cost of the projection step.

3. **Compare to Existing Methods**: Add a brief comparison of the proposed method to existing MCMC-based learning approaches, discussing their theoretical guarantees and practical performance.

4. **Refine the Example**: Use a more realistic example with larger grid size and more training data. Discuss the implications of the conservative theoretical bounds observed in the current example.

5. **Address Limitations**: More thoroughly discuss the limitations of the fast-mixing assumption and how the approach might be extended to handle cases where this assumption is violated.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The analysis is rigorous and the proofs are well-structured. However, the lack of substantial empirical validation is a notable weakness.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written with good organization. The notation is consistent and well-defined. The main results are stated precisely. The proof sketches provide good intuition for the main results.

**Originality**: The paper introduces a novel approach to tractability in graphical model learning by focusing on fast-mixing parameter sets rather than low-treewidth structures. This is a significant conceptual contribution that distinguishes it from previous work on maximum likelihood learning.

**Significance**: The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing conditions under which MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning can be efficient. While the practical implications are not fully demonstrated, the theoretical framework provides a foundation for future work on MCMC-based learning methods.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of graphical model learning by providing rigorous guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning under the fast-mixing assumption. The theoretical results are well-motivated, carefully derived, and well-structured.

The main weakness is the lack of substantial empirical validation, which I would strongly recommend addressing in a revised version. Adding even a few more realistic examples would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and relevance to the NIPS community.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a clear theoretical contribution to a fundamental problem in machine learning (maximum likelihood learning in graphical models) with connections to important areas like MCMC and exponential families.

## Pro/Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel theoretical framework for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning
- Rigorous bounds on computational complexity with practical implications
- Clear connection to existing work on mixing times and graphical models
- Well-structured and clearly written
- Addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical model learning

**Con:**
- Limited empirical validation of the theoretical results
- Example is too simplistic to be convincing
- Doesn't adequately discuss practical implementation challenges
- Doesn't compare to existing MCMC-based learning methods

Overall, the paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of when MCMC-based learning can be efficient. With the addition of more substantial empirical results, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
