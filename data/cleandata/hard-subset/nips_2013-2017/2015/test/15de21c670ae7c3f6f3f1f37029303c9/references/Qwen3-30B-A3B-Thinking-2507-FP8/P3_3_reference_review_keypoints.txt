- Summary: Proposes constraining parameters to a fast-mixing set Θ so MCMC-based gradient descent gives a FPRAS for maximum likelihood in undirected graphical models, shifting tractability from treewidth to mixing guarantees.

- Strength: Novel framework — fast-mixing parameter sets offer a meaningful alternative to treewidth-based tractability and align with practical MCMC learnability.

- Strength: Rigorous analysis — provides careful bounds on bias and variance for gradient descent with approximate (MCMC) gradients and clear proof sketches.

- Strength: Practical relevance — gives explicit schedules for number of gradient steps, samples, and MCMC transitions (K, M, v) and a concrete Ising-model example.

- Strength: Comprehensive coverage — treats both convex and strongly convex cases, with lower bounds showing near-optimality of upper bounds.

- Strength: Clear exposition — well-organized notation and logical flow, with good distinction between convex and strongly convex analyses.

- Weakness: Projection complexity — assumes projection onto Θ is trivial but doesn’t address potentially expensive projections for complex constraints.

- Weakness: Model expressiveness — lacks analysis of how restricting to fast-mixing Θ limits the model’s ability to capture data dependencies.

- Weakness: Empirical validation — provides only a minimal 4x4 Ising example and 5 samples; stronger empirical results are missing.

- Weakness: Contrastive divergence omission — analysis does not cover the common practice of maintaining a sample pool between iterations (contrastive divergence).

- Weakness: Mixing time assumptions — assumes uniform mixing-time bounds over Θ, but doesn’t handle parameter-dependent or highly variable mixing times within Θ.

- Critical: Theoretical contribution is significant and advances understanding of MCMC-based learning efficiency.

- Critical: Practical relevance is limited without deeper discussion of implementation challenges, expressiveness trade-offs, and empirical evidence.

- Suggested improvement: Include more empirical validation, discuss projection costs and practical implementation details, and compare expressiveness versus treewidth-based approaches.

- Recommendation: Accept — strong theoretical contribution merits acceptance despite limited practical validation.
