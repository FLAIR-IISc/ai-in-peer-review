Summary
This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families under a “fast-mixing” constraint on parameters Θ: for all θ ∈ Θ, a chosen MCMC kernel Mθ mixes geometrically with known constants C and α. The authors analyze projected gradient descent where each gradient is approximated via MCMC samples obtained by running v steps starting from an arbitrary initialization, and then projecting back to Θ. They prove: (i) the regularized negative log-likelihood has an L-Lipschitz gradient given bounded sufficient statistics; (ii) high-probability convergence rates for the convex (λ=0) and strongly convex (λ>0) cases, with explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, number of samples M per iteration, and chain length v; (iii) overall work (total number of Markov transitions KMv) scaling as O(εf^-3 polylog(1/εf)) for the convex case and O(εθ^-2 polylog(1/εθ)) for the strongly convex case. The authors interpret this as an FPRAS for ML within Θ, assuming efficient projection onto Θ and known mixing constants. A small Ising toy example illustrates the procedure.

Relation to prior work
The work connects several strands: MCMC-based maximum likelihood estimation (Geyer; Gu & Zhu), learning under fast-mixing parameter restrictions (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke; Steinhardt & Liang), and convergence of inexact/proximal gradient methods (Schmidt et al.). The novelty is a unified finite-sample high-probability analysis that couples MCMC mixing error and sampling error with projection onto a provably fast-mixing Θ, and explicit schedules that yield polynomial-time guarantees in 1/ε.

Strengths
- Clear, self-contained theoretical framework linking mixing-time conditions to optimization error in ML estimation with biased MCMC gradients.
- High-probability convergence bounds with explicit dependences on K, M, v, α, C, and the Lipschitz/strong convexity constants; schedules are given and compared to lower bounds up to logs/constant factors.
- Generality: results stated for general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not just Ising/Potts.
- The discussion explicitly acknowledges limitations (projection cost, initialization, sample pool strategies).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical relevance hinges on the existence of nontrivial Θ with (a) meaningful modeling capacity, (b) uniform, usable mixing bounds (C, α), and (c) an efficiently computable projection. Outside a few special cases (e.g., Ising with spectral/induced-norm constraints), this is unclear. The paper does not substantiate feasibility for broader families.
- The FPRAS framing seems overreaching. Classical FPRAS is for approximating a value within relative error in time poly(size,1/ε). Here, the guarantee is on achieving ε-suboptimality (additive) for an optimization problem under assumptions that include access to projections and uniform mixing constants. The claim should be carefully qualified.
- The constants and scaling may be very loose. α is often close to 1 in high dimensions unless constraints are very restrictive (e.g., far from criticality), making v and thus total work large. The empirical example (4×4 Ising, 5 data points) is too small to be informative.
- Empirical validation is minimal. There is no comparison to established baselines (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, contrastive divergence), no study of mis-specified regimes (where the authors argue ML enjoys M-projection guarantees), and no demonstration that projection-into-Θ + ML offers a better computation-statistics tradeoff in practice.
- Clarity issues:
  - “Algorithm 1” is referenced but not shown; the algorithmic details (step sizes, projection operator specifics) should be explicit.
  - Notation for the bound on sufficient statistics alternates between Ra and R2; TV Lipschitz bound constants could be stated precisely.
  - In the example, using L = 10 when the theoretical L is ~97 undermines the guarantees unless a line-search or adaptive step is used.

Quality
The theoretical arguments appear largely sound at a high level and appropriately leverage mixing-time bounds and inexact gradient convergence. However, key assumptions (uniformly known C, α across Θ; efficient projection) are strong and limit applicability. Proofs are deferred; some steps (e.g., TV distance Lipschitz in parameters, uniformity of mixing constants over Θ) would benefit from explicit statements with conditions.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, but the absence of the algorithm box and some notation inconsistencies reduce readability. The example is helpful but too small. Tightening the presentation and explicitly stating assumptions and constants would improve clarity.

Originality
Moderate. The paper integrates known ingredients (fast-mixing parameter sets, inexact gradient descent, MCMC error decomposition) into a clean finite-sample analysis with explicit schedules. While the synthesis is valuable, the conceptual novelty over prior work on fast-mixing projections and MCMC-based ML is incremental.

Significance
Limited at present due to the gap between theory and practice. Without convincing empirical evidence on realistic models, or broader classes where projection and mixing constants are practical, impact on applied learning appears small. As a theoretical contribution, it clarifies conditions under which MCMC-based ML with projection can be made polynomial-time, which may interest a niche audience.

Questions for the authors
1. For which families beyond pairwise Ising/Potts do you have efficient projection operators and uniform mixing constants with usable magnitudes? Any results for higher-order cliques or continuous exponential families?
2. How are C and α obtained in practice when setting v? Can you propose adaptive schemes (e.g., coupling-from-the-past, mixing diagnostics) that preserve guarantees?
3. Can you provide experiments on larger grids/graphs, with comparisons to pseudolikelihood/score matching/composite likelihood/contrastive divergence, including mis-specified settings?
4. Please clarify the FPRAS claim—what exactly is approximated (objective value vs. parameters), and under what oracle assumptions (projection, mixing constants)?
5. How sensitive is the method to the tightness of L? Would a backtracking line-search preserve guarantees?
6. Your analysis assumes independent samples per iteration. Do you require independent chains initialized afresh for each sample, or can you reuse chains without invalidating bounds?

Pros
- Clean, general theoretical analysis with explicit high-probability rates and schedules.
- Connects fast-mixing constraints and ML optimization rigorously.
- Acknowledges limitations; points to interesting future directions.

Cons
- Strong assumptions (uniform mixing constants, efficient projection) with limited evidence of practicality.
- Minimal empirical validation; no baseline comparisons.
- FPRAS terminology potentially misleading.
- Clarity issues (missing algorithm, notation).

Recommendation: Weak reject
The theoretical synthesis is interesting, but the paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence and clarity, and relies on strong assumptions whose practicality is not demonstrated. Strengthening experiments, clarifying assumptions, and tempering the FPRAS claim would substantially improve the submission.

Confidence: High
