Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a tractability notion based on fast-mixing Markov chains. The key idea is to restrict parameters to a convex set Θ for which a chosen MCMC kernel Mθ has uniform geometric mixing with known constants (C, α), and to assume we can project onto Θ. The authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated via MCMC sampling run for v steps from arbitrary initializations, yielding both sampling noise and bias due to finite mixing. For general convex f (unregularized or with λ ≥ 0), they derive high-probability bounds on suboptimality of the averaged iterate, showing that with suitable schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain steps v, one attains an additive εf-accurate solution with total Markov transitions scaling as O(εf−3 log(1/εf)). For the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case, they obtain parameter error bounds ∥θK − θ∗∥2 ≤ εθ with total work O(εθ−2 polylog(1/εθ)), relying on inexact gradient descent results (Schmidt et al.). They provide background on mixing bounds and examples of Θ in Ising/pairwise MRFs (e.g., per-edge bounds, or spectral/induced norm constraints on interaction matrices), and include a small illustrative experiment on a 4×4 Ising grid. The authors argue this yields an FPRAS for maximum-likelihood learning over Θ.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on several strands: (i) learning with tractable structures (trees, high-girth) and fast-mixing constraints (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang, “Learning fast-mixing models”), (ii) MCMC-based maximum likelihood (Geyer; Gu & Zhu; contrastive divergence), and (iii) projection onto fast-mixing parameter sets (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke). The novelty here is an end-to-end complexity analysis showing that MCMC-based projected gradient descent over Θ yields ε-accurate solutions in polynomial time in 1/ε, with explicit schedules and near-matching lower bounds within the analysis framework. Compared to Steinhardt & Liang, who enforce mixing via restarts and study structured prediction, this paper targets MLE over arbitrary exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics and gives convergence/work guarantees for standard MCMC kernels, under uniform mixing assumptions and a projection oracle.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation: learning constrained to fast-mixing parameter sets, with a clean projected gradient descent scheme and explicit scheduling for K, M, v.
- Solid theoretical analysis separating sampling variance and mixing bias, and leveraging modern inexact gradient descent bounds. The convex and strongly convex cases are treated carefully.
- Concrete examples of Θ for Ising/pairwise MRFs and discussion of projection algorithms, connecting to known mixing-time results.
- Near-optimality results: lower bounds on total work and schedules that are close up to constants/log factors.
- The framework is general for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not tied to a specific graph structure.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality of assumptions: The analysis requires explicit, uniform mixing constants (C, α) for the chosen kernel over Θ and an efficient projection ΠΘ. For many models/kernels, tight computable C, α are unavailable or very conservative, making prescribed v overly large. The paper acknowledges conservatism in the example.
- Scope of Θ: For discrete MRFs, fast-mixing sets defined by spectral/induced norm or per-edge bounds can be quite restrictive, potentially excluding regimes of practical interest (e.g., moderately strong couplings). The paper would benefit from discussing the statistical price of enforcing Θ (bias vs. M-projection) in mis-specified settings beyond a brief mention.
- Experimental support: Only a single small synthetic example is shown, without comparisons to alternatives (pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, CD, or the restart-based approach). While this is a theory paper, minimal empirical validation on larger graphs would strengthen significance.
- Terminology: Referring to the result as an FPRAS may be imprecise. FPRAS typically pertains to approximate counting; for optimization, RP-TAS/PTAS terminology is more common. Here the method yields an additive ε-approximation in polynomial time with high probability; clarifying the approximation notion would avoid confusion.
- Some technical details could be tightened/clarified: e.g., the Lipschitz gradient constant for A(θ) could be derived via bounding the operator norm of Cov[t(X)], which with ∥t(x)∥2 ≤ R implies a simpler bound; the current Theorem 1 uses a TV-based argument and constants (4R2^2) that may be loose. The TV Lipschitz bound ∥pθ−pφ∥TV ≤ 2R∥θ−φ∥2 is stated without proof; conditions and constants should be clarified.

Quality
Overall technically sound within the stated assumptions. Proof sketches are plausible; however, several key inequalities (Lipschitz constants; TV-parameter Lipschitzness) are relegated to the appendix; ensuring full proofs and conditions (e.g., bounded statistics, compact Θ) are precise is important. The dependence on δ and logs is carefully tracked. The discussion section fairly notes limitations (projection cost, sample pool reuse not covered).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Notation around R, R2, and R22 is confusing and should be standardized (e.g., define R := supx ∥t(x)∥2; avoid R2 and R22). Some typos remain (e.g., “Inferning” likely “Inferring”; occasional spacing/parentheses). It would help to explicitly state what information is assumed known to the algorithm (bounds on C, α; R; L) and how to set them in practice.

Originality
Moderately high. While components (fast-mixing constraints, projection operators, MCMC-based learning, inexact gradient analysis) exist, the paper’s combination and the end-to-end work/accuracy guarantees with schedules and near-matching lower bounds appear novel.

Significance
Conceptually valuable: it provides a theoretically grounded pathway to MLE with arbitrary treewidth under an alternative tractability notion. The practical impact depends on how broadly suitable Θ and mixing bounds can be obtained. With more empirical validation or broader classes of Θ, impact would increase.

Questions for authors
1. Can you comment on how one would estimate or adapt to unknown C, α in practice? Could you propose an adaptive schedule for v based on diagnostics (e.g., autocorrelation), and can the analysis tolerate such adaptivity?
2. For the Lipschitz gradient of A, can you provide a tighter bound via Cov[t(X)] operator norm? This may improve constants throughout.
3. How does your approach compare empirically and in complexity to Steinhardt & Liang’s restart-based fast-mixing learning, on similar models?
4. Does your analysis extend to reusing samples (persistent chains/pools) with appropriate mixing assumptions (Markovian noise SA)?
5. Beyond Ising/pairwise MRFs, can you give examples of Θ and projection algorithms for other exponential families?

Minor comments/typos
- Standardize R notation; replace R22 by R^2 or similar.
- Clarify FPRAS terminology for optimization.
- Provide precise assumptions for the TV-parameter Lipschitz bound and cite a reference if available.
- Fix minor typos and citation formatting.

Pros
- Clear theoretical contribution with explicit complexity guarantees.
- General exponential-family framework; covers both convex and strongly convex regimes.
- Connects to and extends prior fast-mixing/projection literature.
- Transparent discussion of limitations.

Cons
- Strong assumptions on known mixing constants and efficient projection; limited practical validation.
- Potentially restrictive Θ for interesting regimes.
- Some technical constants/inequalities could be sharpened and better justified.

Recommendation: Weak accept (borderline). The theoretical contribution is meaningful and well-executed within its scope; strengthening clarity and adding modest empirical comparisons would solidify it.

Confidence: High (theoretical assessment).
