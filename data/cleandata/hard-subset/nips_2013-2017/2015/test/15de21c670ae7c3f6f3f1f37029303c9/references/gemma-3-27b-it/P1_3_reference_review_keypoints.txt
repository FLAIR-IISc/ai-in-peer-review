- Summary: Proposes constraining parameters to fast-mixing sets Θ and shows that, if projection onto Θ is feasible, an FPRAS exists for maximum likelihood within Θ using MCMC-based gradient estimates.
- Summary: Provides convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases and handles model mis-specification.
- Relation to prior work: Positions approach relative to pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, and tractable-structure methods, highlighting different trade-offs.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on MCMC-based gradient estimation and existing mixing-time results (e.g., Ising model work by Jerrum & Sinclair, Hayes) and projection techniques.
- Strength: Novel idea to use fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractability criterion, shifting focus from structure to sampling efficiency.
- Strength: General framework applicable to exponential families beyond graphical models.
- Strength: Theoretical rigor with an FPRAS proof and detailed analysis for convex and strongly convex scenarios.
- Strength: Practical guidance via schedules for iterations, samples, and Markov transitions; illustrative Ising model example.
- Strength: Provides guarantees under model mis-specification, unlike many alternatives.
- Strength: Clear and well-organized presentation with consistent notation and explicit main results.
- Weakness: Identifying and constructing fast-mixing parameter sets Θ in practice is nontrivial and not systematically addressed.
- Weakness: Theoretical bounds appear conservative and may demand impractical numbers of samples/iterations.
- Weakness: Dependence on accurately knowing the Lipschitz constant L is problematic; example uses an ad hoc L value.
- Weakness: Limited discussion of the effect of initialization (θ0) on convergence behavior.
- Weakness: Connection to contrastive divergence as an initialization or practical strategy is underdeveloped.
- Weakness: Projection onto Θ may be computationally expensive; complexity of projection algorithms is not fully explored.
- For acceptance: Offers a fundamentally new perspective, rigorous theory, broad generality, and potential to stimulate further research.
- Against acceptance: Practical applicability depends on finding fast-mixing sets, bounds may be overly conservative, and lacks compelling empirical superiority on realistic tasks.
- Overall assessment: Strong, theoretically significant contribution with practical challenges that do not outweigh its novelty and rigor.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to (1) discuss limitations of finding/projection onto fast-mixing sets, (2) investigate initialization strategies (e.g., CD), (3) clarify Lipschitz constant estimation, and (4) expand on projection computational costs.
