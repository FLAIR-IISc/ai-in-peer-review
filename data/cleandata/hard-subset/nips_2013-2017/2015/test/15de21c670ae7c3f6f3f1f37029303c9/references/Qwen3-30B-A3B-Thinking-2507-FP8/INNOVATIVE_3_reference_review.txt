

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing sets" - where MCMC sampling converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The core insight is that if the parameter set Θ is constrained such that MCMC mixes rapidly (with geometric convergence rate), then maximum likelihood learning can be performed efficiently with theoretical guarantees. This approach fundamentally differs from prior work that restricts model structure (e.g., tree-structured models via Chow-Liu) or uses approximate inference methods without guarantees.

The paper makes two key theoretical contributions: 1) It proves that the regularized log-likelihood has a Lipschitz gradient, and 2) It establishes convergence guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC sampling when parameters are constrained to fast-mixing sets. For the convex case, it shows that the total computational effort scales as (1/ϵ)^3 log(1/ϵ), while for strongly convex cases it scales as (1/ϵ)^2 log(1/ϵ). The authors provide explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps, samples, and Markov transitions to achieve desired accuracy.

This work is conceptually distinct from previous approaches to tractable maximum likelihood learning. While Jerrum and Sinclair (1993) established the intractability of partition function evaluation, and previous methods like pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22], and composite likelihoods [20, 30] have focused on model structure restrictions or alternative estimators, this paper introduces a new tractability condition based on mixing properties rather than structural constraints. The connection between fast mixing and tractable learning represents a significant conceptual advance.

**Strengths:**
1. **High novelty:** The "fast-mixing parameter sets" concept is genuinely novel and opens a new theoretical direction for maximum likelihood learning.
2. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides rigorous proofs of Lipschitz continuity and convergence guarantees, with careful analysis of error terms.
3. **General applicability:** The results apply to any exponential family, not just graphical models, making them broadly relevant.
4. **Clear exposition:** The paper is well-structured with good notation and logical flow, making complex theoretical results accessible.
5. **Honest discussion of limitations:** The authors appropriately acknowledge the lack of empirical validation and potential computational costs of projection.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited empirical validation:** The single example in Section 6 (4x4 Ising model with 5 training samples) is insufficient to demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical results. While the authors acknowledge this is not a practical contribution, some empirical evidence would strengthen the paper.
2. **Assumptions about mixing rates:** The paper assumes the mixing time bound (Equation 2) holds for all θ ∈ Θ, which may be restrictive for some models. While justified by existing mixing time results, the practical feasibility of identifying such Θ for general models requires more discussion.
3. **Projection complexity:** The paper acknowledges that projection might be costly but doesn't provide analysis of when this becomes a bottleneck relative to sampling.

This paper is a significant theoretical contribution that introduces a fundamentally new perspective on tractable maximum likelihood learning. While the lack of substantial empirical validation is a minor weakness, the novelty and theoretical soundness outweigh this limitation. The paper's core insight - that fast mixing properties of MCMC can serve as a tractability condition for maximum likelihood learning - is bold, novel, and potentially transformative for the field.

Compared to recent NIPS papers on graphical models (e.g., Wainwright's work on "estimating the wrong graphical model," or Steinhardt and Liang's "learning fast-mixing models"), this paper offers a substantially different theoretical framework. Unlike previous approaches that focus on model structure or approximate inference, this work rethinks the fundamental tractability condition for learning.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of graphical models and maximum likelihood learning. The conceptual novelty is substantial, and the theoretical results are rigorous and well-supported. While the empirical component is limited, the paper's primary contribution is theoretical, and the authors appropriately acknowledge this.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper's core idea represents a significant conceptual advance that could influence future research on maximum likelihood learning in graphical models and exponential families. The theoretical framework is sound, the novelty is clear, and the results are important for the field. The minor weaknesses in empirical validation are outweighed by the paper's strong theoretical contribution and conceptual novelty.
