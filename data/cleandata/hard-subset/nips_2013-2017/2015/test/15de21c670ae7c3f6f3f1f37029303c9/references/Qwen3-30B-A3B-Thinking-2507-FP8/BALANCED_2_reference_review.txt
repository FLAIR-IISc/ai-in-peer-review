

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in maximum likelihood learning for undirected graphical models by proposing a novel tractability condition based on fast-mixing Markov chains rather than low-treewidth structures. The authors establish theoretical guarantees for using MCMC-based gradient descent when parameters are constrained to a set Θ where MCMC mixes rapidly. The work extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's core insight—using fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractability condition—represents a novel departure from traditional approaches based on treewidth or other structural constraints. This is well-motivated by the limitations of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching) that either have inferior sample complexity or fail to provide guarantees when models are mis-specified. The paper's focus on the exponential family framework makes it broadly applicable.

2. **Technical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing precise convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases. The authors carefully distinguish between these two settings, which is important for practical applications. The proof sketches provide sufficient intuition for the main results, and the technical development is sound.

3. **Practical Relevance:** The paper connects theoretical results to practical considerations by providing concrete schedules for setting algorithm parameters (K, M, v) to achieve desired accuracy. The example with the Ising model demonstrates how the theoretical bounds translate to practical implementation.

4. **Comprehensive Treatment of Mixing Times:** The authors provide a valuable overview of mixing time bounds for various models (Ising, Potts) and sampling algorithms (Gibbs, Swendsen-Wang), which is helpful for understanding the scope of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the absence of any empirical results to validate the theoretical claims. The example section is purely theoretical (no actual experiments), and the paper doesn't compare the proposed approach with existing methods like contrastive divergence or variational inference. This is particularly problematic given that the paper claims "no significant practical contribution," which undermines the significance of the work.

2. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation:** The paper doesn't address how to select the fast-mixing parameter set Θ in practice. While the projection step is assumed to be efficient, the paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of projection for large models, which could be a bottleneck in real applications.

3. **Comparison with Existing Methods:** The paper doesn't adequately compare its approach with established methods for maximum likelihood learning. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the proposed method relates to Steinhardt and Liang's (2013) work on fast-mixing chains for learning, or how it compares to contrastive divergence methods that are commonly used in practice.

4. **Restrictive Mixing Assumption:** The mixing time assumption (Equation 2) might be too strong for many practical applications. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but doesn't explore how to relax these assumptions or whether they hold in realistic settings.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing conditions under which MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning can be justified. The results are technically sound and provide important insights for the field. However, the lack of empirical validation and limited discussion of practical implementation significantly weaken the paper's impact.

The work would be substantially strengthened by:
1. Adding empirical results comparing the proposed method with existing approaches (e.g., contrastive divergence, variational inference) on standard benchmark datasets.
2. Providing a more detailed discussion of how to select the fast-mixing parameter set Θ in practice, including computational considerations for projection.
3. Including a thorough comparison with related work, particularly methods that use MCMC for learning (e.g., Steinhardt and Liang, 2013; Tieleman, 2008).

The theoretical results are strong and novel, but the absence of empirical validation and practical discussion prevents this from being a significant contribution to the field of machine learning. The paper would be suitable for publication at NIPS if the authors address these weaknesses through additional experiments and discussion.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject (with strong encouragement to address the empirical validation and implementation concerns before resubmission). The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the lack of empirical support and practical discussion limits its significance for the NIPS community.
