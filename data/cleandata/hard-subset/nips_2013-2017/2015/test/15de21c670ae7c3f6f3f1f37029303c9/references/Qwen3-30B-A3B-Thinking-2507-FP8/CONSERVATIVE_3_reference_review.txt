

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing parameter set" where MCMC sampling converges rapidly. The authors show that under appropriate mixing time assumptions, gradient descent using MCMC-based gradient estimates converges to the maximum likelihood solution within the constrained parameter set with high probability. The main contribution is a theoretical analysis of convergence rates for both convex and strongly convex cases, establishing computational complexity bounds in terms of optimization accuracy.

The paper makes several technically sound theoretical contributions. Theorems 1-5 establish Lipschitz continuity of the gradient and convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithm under various conditions. The analysis builds on existing work in optimization with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011) and mixing time theory for MCMC (Levin et al., 2009). The paper is clearly written, with well-structured proofs and good notation. The theoretical results are presented in a logically organized manner.

However, the paper has significant limitations that undermine its contribution to the NeurIPS community. The most critical weakness is the complete lack of empirical validation. While the paper provides a small synthetic example in Section 6, it doesn't compare the proposed method to established baselines (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.) on any real datasets. The example merely demonstrates convergence without addressing whether the approach is practically useful or competitive with existing methods.

The theoretical results depend on strong assumptions that are rarely satisfied in practice. The key assumption is that the parameter set Θ is such that MCMC mixes rapidly for all θ ∈ Θ, with a geometric mixing rate (Equation 2). While the paper provides examples where this might hold (e.g., Ising models with bounded interaction strengths), it doesn't address how to construct such sets for general graphical models or how restrictive these constraints are. The paper acknowledges this limitation but fails to provide any practical guidance on how to implement the method in real-world applications.

The paper's claim that "this paper claims no significant practical contribution" is telling. While theoretical contributions are valuable, NeurIPS particularly values work that advances practical understanding or provides meaningful insights for practitioners. This paper's theoretical analysis doesn't address the core question: why would anyone use this method over established approaches like pseudolikelihood or score matching, which have known theoretical guarantees and practical implementations?

The paper also makes several problematic claims. It states that "the maximum-likelihood solution will converge to the M-projection (minimum of the KL-divergence), but these estimators do not have similar guarantees" for well-specified models. This is incorrect - pseudolikelihood and score matching do converge to the M-projection when the model is mis-specified, as shown in Wainwright (2006) and Hyvärinen (2005). The paper's critique of existing methods is therefore based on a misunderstanding of the literature.

The practical relevance of the proposed "fast-mixing parameter set" is unclear. The paper doesn't explain how to select meaningful constraints beyond simple clipping (as in the Ising model example), nor does it address how this approach compares to existing methods that also restrict the parameter space (e.g., Heinemann and Globerson, 2014, on high-girth models).

While the theoretical analysis is technically sound, the paper fails to demonstrate why these results matter for real machine learning problems. The absence of empirical results, the strong assumptions, and the lack of comparison to established methods make it difficult to assess the practical significance of the contribution. For a NeurIPS paper, this represents a significant gap.

In summary, the paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution but fails to demonstrate its practical relevance or advantages over existing methods. The lack of empirical validation and the narrow scope of the theoretical results limit its impact. The paper would benefit from substantial empirical validation, comparison to established methods, and a clearer discussion of practical implications.

**Recommendation: Reject**

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical analysis of gradient descent with MCMC-based gradients
- Well-organized presentation of technical results
- Good understanding of relevant literature in mixing times and optimization

**Weaknesses:**
- No empirical validation beyond a single synthetic example
- Strong assumptions about mixing times not verified in practice
- Claims about existing methods are inaccurate
- Fails to demonstrate practical significance or advantages over established methods
- Theoretical results don't address whether the fast-mixing constraint set is meaningful for real problems
