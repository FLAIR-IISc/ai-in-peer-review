Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families under a novel tractability notion: restricting parameters to a convex set Θ that guarantees rapid mixing of a chosen MCMC kernel. Assuming (i) bounded sufficient statistics, (ii) access to a projection oracle ΠΘ, and (iii) geometric mixing with known constants C, α uniformly over Θ, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where the likelihood gradient is approximated by MCMC samples. They derive finite-sample, high-probability convergence guarantees in both the convex (λ = 0) and strongly convex (λ > 0) settings, translating into a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained MLE. They give explicit schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and chain steps, as well as matching (up to small factors) lower bounds that elucidate near-optimal trade-offs. The analysis is framed for general exponential families and instantiated on Ising/MRFs via known fast-mixing parameter sets (e.g., bounded couplings or spectral-norm constraints).

Relation to prior work
The work connects classical MCMC-MLE ideas (Geyer, 1991; Gu & Zhu, 2001) with modern mixing-time bounds (e.g., Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Hayes; Jerrum-Sinclair) and projection to fast-mixing sets (Domke & Liu, NIPS’13; Liu & Domke, NIPS’14). Compared to Steinhardt & Liang (ICML’14), who ensure fast mixing via restart chains, this paper treats arbitrary exponential families with a general Θ guaranteeing geometric mixing and provides finite-time complexity schedules and lower bounds for projected gradient descent with biased (MCMC) gradients. The use of inexact gradient descent theory (Schmidt et al., NIPS’11) to obtain explicit complexity bounds for MLE under mixing assumptions appears novel, and the framing as an FPRAS for constrained MLE is a clean contribution.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The convex/strongly-convex analyses are careful and leverage standard tools (Bernstein, Efron-Stein, Hoeffding-type bounds; projected GD with errors). The decomposition of gradient error into sampling noise and bias due to incomplete mixing is standard but well executed. The lower/upper bound trade-off for K, M, v is insightful.
- Assumptions: The central assumption is the existence of Θ with uniform geometric mixing (C, α) and a tractable projection. This is realistic in some MRF families (bounded couplings, spectral-norm constraints), but can be restrictive and may limit modeling power. The analysis presumes knowledge of mixing constants to set schedules.
- Completeness: Full proofs are deferred to the appendix; the main text provides sketches sufficient for program committee purposes.
- Correctness: One potential place to tighten is Theorem 1’s bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2R2∥θ − φ∥2, which is nontrivial in general exponential families. A brief derivation or pointer to a standard inequality (e.g., via Lipschitz control of log-density and Pinsker) would improve clarity.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with a helpful notation block and intuitive proof sketches. Minor notation issues: using “R2” to denote a bound on ∥t(x)∥2 and then writing constants like 4R2^2 may confuse; consider renaming the bound to R (for the 2-norm) or R_t to avoid squaring ambiguity. The FPRAS claim would benefit from a precise statement (additive approximation of the convex objective with runtime poly in problem size, 1/ε, and log(1/δ)).

Originality
The combination of (i) fast-mixing parameter sets with (ii) projected GD + explicit bias/variance control and (iii) finite-time complexity and lower bounds for MLE is, to my knowledge, novel. Prior works either propose particular fast-mixing mechanisms (restarts) or consider projection for inference, not for end-to-end MLE optimization with explicit FPRAS guarantees.

Significance
This paper advances our theoretical understanding of when MLE is computationally feasible beyond low-treewidth. It provides a principled alternative to pseudolikelihood/score matching that retains M-projection guarantees under misspecification within Θ. While the practical scope is limited by the need for Θ with uniform mixing and projection oracles, the framework is broadly applicable to exponential families and could influence future algorithm design (e.g., learning under spectral-norm constraints).

Strengths
- Clear, general framework that decouples modeling from mixing guarantees.
- Concrete complexity schedules and matching lower bounds.
- Applicability to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Bridges MCMC-based learning practice with provable guarantees.
- Discussion is candid about limitations and practical considerations.

Weaknesses
- Strong reliance on uniform mixing constants C, α across Θ; in many realistic models these are hard to verify or may degrade with N.
- Projection oracles are assumed; computational cost can be nontrivial for dense models (e.g., repeated SVDs).
- Experiments are minimal (toy 4×4 Ising) and do not validate scaling or compare with standard estimators.
- The FPRAS claim is additive on the objective; clarify relation to statistical error and how to choose ε relative to D, N.
- The independence assumption across iterations (no persistent chains) limits relevance to common practice; theory for persistent MCMC would be impactful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely formalize the FPRAS guarantee (objective approximation, dependence on log 1/δ) and discuss the input size parameters explicitly.
- Provide a short proof or citation for the TV bound used in Theorem 1; alternatively, state conditions under which it holds (e.g., bounded domain and Lipschitz log-density).
- Expand on concrete Θ constructions beyond Ising (e.g., general discrete pairwise models with Rij(θ) bounds), including projection algorithms and their complexity.
- Add experiments on larger graphs, showing empirical scaling of K, M, v, and the effect of projection cost; compare to pseudolikelihood and contrastive divergence under misspecification.
- Discuss adaptive schedules and warm starts; outline a path to analyze persistent chains (e.g., via coupling or regeneration) to align theory with practice.
- Clean up notation (R2) and minor typos; define all constants where first used.

Pros/Cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Theoretically solid, general, and insightful analysis with near-optimal schedules.
- Clarifies when MCMC-based MLE is computationally feasible via fast-mixing sets.
- Builds a bridge between mixing-time theory and learning.

Cons:
- Practical impact limited without stronger guidance on constructing Θ and projection at scale.
- Experimental validation is too limited for NeurIPS standards.
- Assumptions on uniform mixing may be hard to meet/verify in practice.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Confidence: 3/5
