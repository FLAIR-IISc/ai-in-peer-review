### Summary

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in general undirected graphical models. Instead of resorting to common approaches that either use approximate objectives (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching) or restrict the graph structure to be tractable (e.g., trees via Chow-Liu), the authors propose a fundamentally different notion of tractability. They constrain the model parameters to a convex set `Θ` for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing," meaning the chain converges geometrically to its stationary distribution.

The paper's main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a widely used but previously unanalyzed algorithm: projected gradient descent where the gradient is estimated using MCMC samples. The authors prove that this procedure constitutes a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood parameters within the constrained set `Θ`. The analysis provides explicit schedules for setting the number of gradient iterations (`K`), MCMC samples per iteration (`M`), and MCMC chain length (`v`) to achieve a desired accuracy `ϵ` with high probability. The analysis is performed for both the general convex case (unregularized MLE) and the strongly convex case (ridge-regularized MLE), yielding different convergence rates and complexity bounds. This work successfully bridges the literature on MCMC mixing times with the literature on optimization with inexact gradients to provide the first formal guarantees for this important class of algorithms.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, carefully building upon established results in concentration inequalities (Bernstein, Hoeffding) and optimization with stochastic gradients (Schmidt et al. [26]). The assumptions—namely, the existence of a convex set of parameters with a geometric mixing rate and an efficient projection operator—are clearly stated and justified for several important model classes, such as Ising models with bounded interaction strengths. The derivation of explicit hyperparameter schedules and complexity bounds is a key strength, moving the results beyond a mere existence proof to a quantitative characterization.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction provides excellent motivation by clearly outlining the limitations of existing methods and positioning the proposed approach as a novel alternative. The problem setup, notation, and algorithm are defined precisely. The main theorems are stated clearly, and the accompanying proof sketches provide valuable intuition for the reader before deferring the full details to the appendix. The overall structure is logical and guides the reader effectively through the paper's arguments.

**Originality & Significance:** This work is highly original and significant. While using MCMC to approximate gradients for MLE is a long-standing heuristic, it has largely lacked formal theoretical justification. This paper provides the first end-to-end convergence guarantee, proving that the method can provably find a near-optimal solution. The conceptual shift from structural tractability (low treewidth) to parametric tractability (fast mixing) is a powerful and novel contribution that could inspire new ways of designing and learning expressive probabilistic models. By providing a theoretical foundation for a common practice, this work makes a substantial contribution to our understanding of learning in energy-based models and is likely to be highly influential and spur future research at the intersection of MCMC theory and machine learning.

### Weaknesses & Constructive Suggestions

While this is an excellent paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or strengthened.

1.  **Complexity of Projection:** The analysis of the total computational effort focuses on the number of MCMC transitions (`KMv`), effectively assuming the projection operator `Π_Θ` is computationally cheap. While this holds for simple constraint sets (e.g., element-wise clipping for bounded `θ_ij`), it may not be true for more complex fast-mixing sets, such as those defined by spectral norm constraints on the interaction matrix, which can require an iterative optimization procedure themselves. It would strengthen the paper to more explicitly discuss the cost of projection and how it factors into the overall complexity. A brief discussion in Section 5 or 7 on which known fast-mixing sets admit efficient vs. expensive projections would give the reader a more complete picture.

2.  **Connection to Contrastive Divergence (CD):** The paper briefly mentions that initializing the Markov chains from the empirical data distribution connects to contrastive divergence. This is a very interesting link. However, the analysis assumes a fixed number of MCMC steps (`v`) that is potentially very large, whereas CD typically uses a very small, constant number of steps (e.g., CD-1). It would be beneficial to expand the discussion on this point. What specific parts of the analysis would break down for small, constant `v`? Could this framework be adapted to provide insight into why CD works well in practice, or to characterize its bias? Even if a full analysis is out of scope, a more detailed discussion of the challenges would be valuable for future work.

3.  **Conservatism of Bounds:** As is common with theoretical analyses of this type, the derived bounds appear quite conservative, which is acknowledged in the experiment where a much more aggressive step size (`L=10` vs. theoretical `L=97`) was used. The hyperparameter schedule derived for the small 4x4 grid example also seems orders of magnitude larger than what one might use in practice. While this doesn't diminish the theoretical contribution, it would be helpful to add a sentence or two in the discussion reflecting on the sources of this conservatism (e.g., worst-case assumptions, use of union bounds, loose concentration inequalities) and its implications for direct practical application of the proposed schedules.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper provides the first rigorous, end-to-end theoretical analysis for the widely used family of MCMC-based MLE algorithms for intractable models.
*   **Pro:** The core contribution is highly original and significant, introducing the idea of "parametric tractability" and bridging the fields of MCMC theory and optimization.
*   **Pro:** The paper is technically sound and exceptionally clear, making a complex topic accessible.
*   **Pro:** The results are likely to have a high impact, inspiring follow-up research and improving our foundational understanding of learning energy-based models.

*   **Con:** The analysis overlooks the computational cost of the projection step, which can be non-trivial.
*   **Con:** The practical applicability of the derived hyperparameter schedules is limited due to their conservative nature.

The arguments in favor of acceptance are overwhelmingly stronger. This is a landmark theoretical paper that provides long-sought-after guarantees for a fundamental machine learning problem.

### Final Recommendation: Accept
