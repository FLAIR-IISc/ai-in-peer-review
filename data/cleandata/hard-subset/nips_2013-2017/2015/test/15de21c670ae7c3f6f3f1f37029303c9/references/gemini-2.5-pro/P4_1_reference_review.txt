### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood (ML) learning in undirected graphical models with high treewidth. While alternative tractable estimators like pseudolikelihood exist, they can be inconsistent under model misspecification and may have inferior sample complexity. The authors propose a different notion of tractability, shifting the constraint from the graph's structure (e.g., low treewidth) to the model's parameters. Specifically, they consider learning within a convex parameter set `Θ` for which any parameter `θ` in `Θ` corresponds to a distribution `p_θ` where a given MCMC sampler is provably fast-mixing.

The core contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a common algorithm for ML learning: projected gradient descent where the gradient is approximated using MCMC samples. The authors prove that this method constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the ML estimate within the constrained set `Θ`, provided an efficient projection oracle for `Θ` exists. The analysis is presented for two cases: the general convex case (e.g., unregularized ML) and the strongly convex case (e.g., with L2 regularization). For both scenarios, the paper derives explicit schedules for setting the number of gradient steps (`K`), MCMC samples per step (`M`), and MCMC chain lengths (`v`) required to achieve a desired accuracy `ϵ` with high probability. The resulting total computational complexity is shown to be polynomial in the problem parameters and the inverse of the desired accuracy.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs that build upon established results in optimization and probability theory. The analysis carefully accounts for the biased and stochastic nature of the gradient estimates that arise from finite-length MCMC sampling. The distinction between the convex and strongly convex cases is handled rigorously, leading to different convergence guarantees (in function value vs. parameter distance) and complexity bounds, which is appropriate. The authors are also transparent about the limitations of their analysis, such as the conservatism of the bounds and the assumption of an efficient projection operator. The work represents a complete theoretical investigation into the convergence properties of a widely used heuristic.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed framework is introduced logically. The inclusion of a notation table (Section 2) is helpful for the reader. The main theorems are stated precisely, and the accompanying proof sketches in the main body effectively convey the key ideas and technical steps, improving readability. The background section provides the necessary context on MCMC mixing times and identifies concrete examples of fast-mixing parameter sets from prior work (e.g., for Ising models), grounding the abstract framework. The experimental section, while simple, serves its purpose of illustrating the concepts and the conservative nature of the theoretical bounds.

**Originality:** The algorithm under analysis—projected gradient descent with MCMC-based gradients—is not new and is a common practical heuristic. However, the originality of this work lies in its novel conceptual framing and its rigorous analysis. The idea of defining tractability through "fast-mixing parameter sets" is a departure from the more common structure-based approaches (e.g., Chow-Liu) and provides a valuable alternative perspective. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a full end-to-end complexity analysis and formal convergence guarantees for this class of algorithms, turning a popular heuristic into a provably correct approximation scheme. The work adequately references related literature, including the foundational work on intractability, alternative learning methods, and prior results on MCMC mixing.

**Significance:** The paper's primary significance is theoretical. It provides a formal foundation for a class of algorithms that have been used for decades without strong guarantees. This result deepens the field's understanding of the interplay between MCMC mixing, optimization, and statistical learning. Researchers can now build upon this framework to potentially derive tighter bounds or analyze more sophisticated and practical variants of the algorithm.

However, the direct practical significance is limited, a point the authors acknowledge. The derived hyperparameter schedules depend on problem-specific constants (e.g., mixing rate, bounds on sufficient statistics) that may be unknown, and the resulting complexity, while polynomial, may be too high for practical application due to large constants. Furthermore, the analysis does not cover more computationally efficient heuristics like contrastive divergence or persistent chains, where samples are not generated independently at each iteration. Despite these practical limitations, the theoretical insight is a substantial contribution and provides a crucial first step toward provable learning in these complex models.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees and complexity analysis for the widely used MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning procedure, a significant theoretical result for a long-standing problem.
2.  **Novel Framework:** It introduces and formalizes the concept of learning within "fast-mixing parameter sets," offering a valuable alternative to structure-based tractability assumptions.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous, complete, and technically sound, leveraging appropriate tools from optimization and concentration inequalities.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex theoretical argument accessible and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Practical Impact:** The derived theoretical bounds are likely too conservative for direct practical use, and the paper itself does not claim a practical contribution. The analysis does not cover more efficient and commonly used variants of the algorithm.
2.  **Strong Assumptions:** The analysis relies on the knowledge of a convex, fast-mixing set `Θ` with an efficient projection operator, which may only be available for a limited class of models.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to our understanding of learning in intractable graphical models. While its direct practical utility is limited, its theoretical significance and clarity make it a very suitable candidate for publication at NIPS.
