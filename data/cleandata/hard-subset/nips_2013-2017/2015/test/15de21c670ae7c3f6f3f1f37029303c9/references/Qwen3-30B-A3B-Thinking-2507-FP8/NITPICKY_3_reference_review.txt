

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in exponential families where parameters are constrained to a "fast-mixing" set Θ, such that MCMC sampling converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The authors establish that gradient descent using MCMC samples for gradient approximation can yield a FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The main contributions include theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, with bounds on the total computational effort required.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses an important practical challenge in maximum likelihood learning for complex models. The connection between fast-mixing parameter sets and efficient learning is theoretically interesting, and the analysis extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families. The paper's technical depth is commendable, with careful treatment of the interplay between mixing times, gradient errors, and convergence rates. The example in Section 6 provides a useful concrete illustration of the method's application.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical inconsistencies:** The paper contains multiple mathematical inconsistencies. Theorem 3 states "KMv ≥ a^4b^2 ϵ^3 log acϵ (− logα)" which is clearly malformed (log should apply to a quantity, not a product). Theorem 7 has an unclear expression "log aϵ log c ϵ" that is mathematically nonsensical. The proof sketch for Theorem 1 claims ||dA/dθ - dA/dφ||_2 ≤ 2R2||p_θ - p_φ||_TV without justification, which is not a standard result.

2. **Notational and formatting issues:** The paper references Figures 1 and 2 but provides no figures in the text. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., "f" for negative log-likelihood versus "f(θ)" for the function), and there are multiple typographical errors like "√×" in Section 6. The mixing time assumption (Equation 2) claims "for all distributions q" which is too strong—standard mixing time bounds depend on the starting distribution.

3. **Mischaracterization of related work:** The paper claims their approach is "fundamentally different" from previous work on fast-mixing models, but this is not accurate. The work by Steinhardt and Liang [28] already proposes learning using stationary distributions from fast-mixing chains. The paper fails to properly contextualize how its contribution differs from this and similar prior work.

4. **Incomplete theoretical treatment:** The paper claims their results apply "generally to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics" but doesn't address the case where sufficient statistics are unbounded. The mixing time assumption (Equation 2) is stated as holding for "all distributions q," which is not standard for Markov chains and would require the constant C to depend on q, making the bound useless for practical applications.

5. **Missing critical analysis:** The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of the projection step Π_Θ[φ]. While the authors note this might be negligible compared to sampling time, they don't provide evidence for this claim, especially for large models where projection could be expensive.

**Specific concerns:**
- The example in Section 6 contains a mathematical error: "∥θ0 − θ∗∥2 ≤ D = √× (2× .2)2" (the "√×" is clearly a typo).
- The bound in Theorem 5 includes "log 4DR2C/β1β3ϵf" which should be "log(4DR2C/(β1β3ϵf))" but is written ambiguously.
- The paper doesn't properly define what a "FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution" means in the context of parameter vectors, which is a critical conceptual issue.

**Recommendation:** While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and contains theoretically interesting results, the numerous technical inconsistencies, notational errors, and incomplete theoretical treatment undermine its credibility. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to correct these issues before being considered for publication. The theoretical results are interesting but currently presented with insufficient rigor to meet NIPS standards.

**Pro:** Theoretical connection between fast-mixing parameter sets and efficient learning; careful analysis of gradient errors in MCMC-based optimization.

**Con:** Multiple mathematical errors and inconsistencies; inadequate treatment of projection complexity; mischaracterization of related work; unclear definition of FPRAS in context of parameter optimization.

**Overall:** The paper makes a promising theoretical contribution but currently suffers from significant technical flaws that need to be addressed before it can be considered for publication. I recommend rejection.
