Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under the constraint that parameters lie in a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ, i.e., for any θ ∈ Θ the chosen MCMC kernel Mθ mixes in total variation at a geometric rate ∥Mθv q − pθ∥TV ≤ C αv. The authors analyze projected gradient descent where the model expectation in the gradient is approximated by short MCMC runs started from arbitrary initial distributions, with fresh independent samples per iteration. They provide non-asymptotic guarantees in two regimes: convex (no regularization) where convergence is in objective suboptimality, and strongly convex (with ℓ2 regularization) where convergence is in parameter distance. They derive sample/iteration/mixing schedules that yield polynomial total work KMv, with near-matching lower bounds, and argue this yields an FPRAS for MLE within Θ. They connect to prior work on MCMC-MLE (Geyer; Gu & Zhu), contrastive divergence, pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood, and fast-mixing parameter sets for Ising/MRFs (e.g., spectral-norm or degree-based constraints; Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke). They also relate to Steinhardt & Liang’s “restart” chains guaranteeing fast mixing by design.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution: Explicit, non-asymptotic bounds for projected gradient with biased MCMC gradients under a mixing-time assumption, in both convex and strongly convex settings.
- Generality: Results are stated for arbitrary exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not just specific graphical models.
- Concrete complexity guarantees: Total work O(εf^−3 log(1/εf)) in the convex case and O(εθ^−2 polylog) in the strongly convex case, with constructive schedules for K, M, v and lower bounds that nearly match (up to constants/logs). This is valuable because many MCMC-based learning procedures lack finite-sample guarantees.
- Placement within literature: Good discussion of fast-mixing sufficient conditions (degree/interaction-strength bounds, spectral norm bounds) and projection algorithms for Ising/MRFs; relates to subgraphs-world/Swendsen–Wang where relevant.
- Honest discussion of limitations (projection cost; independence of samples across iterations; conservatism of bounds), plus a small illustrative example.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Practicality/assumptions: The main guarantees rely on the existence of a convex Θ with known projection operator and known/usable mixing constants C, α for the specific MCMC kernel. For many models/graphs these constants are unknown or extremely conservative, leading to very large v. The method requires fresh independent chains per iteration, which is atypical in practice (persistent chains/pools are common), and the analysis does not cover that regime.
- FPRAS terminology: The paper claims an FPRAS for MLE in Θ. Typically, FPRAS refers to relative-error randomized approximation schemes for counting, while for optimization one usually uses FPTAS (relative error) or additive PTAS. The provided guarantees are additive in objective (convex case) or in parameter norm (strongly convex case). The authors should define precisely the approximation notion and justify the “FPRAS” usage or adjust terminology.
- Tightness of constants and Lipschitz argument: The L-Lipschitz gradient bound hinges on a TV-Lipschitz relation ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ−φ∥2 derived from bounded sufficient statistics. This is nontrivial; more detail would help, and the constants may be loose. Similarly, using R2 to denote both a bound and what appears syntactically like a squared quantity makes notation hard to parse (e.g., 4 R2^2 + λ).
- Experimental validation: Only a toy 4×4 Ising example is shown. There is no empirical study on larger graphs or comparisons to alternatives (pseudo-/composite likelihood, contrastive divergence, restart-chain learning) to illustrate trade-offs, practicality of projections, or the gap between prescribed and effective schedules.
- Scope of Θ: Beyond Ising-like pairwise MRFs, it remains unclear how broadly one can construct useful Θ with efficient projection and meaningful mixing certificates (e.g., for higher-order factors, heterogeneous potentials). The discussion hints at adaptations but concrete recipes/examples would increase impact.
- Algorithmic model: The analysis assumes independent sampling within each iteration and resets chains from arbitrary distributions. This is analytically convenient but may be inefficient compared to reusing/persistent chains; the paper acknowledges this but offers no partial analysis.

Quality:
- The theoretical development appears sound and builds appropriately on concentration inequalities and inexact gradient descent results (Schmidt et al.). The convex vs strongly convex treatments and the lower/upper bound pairing are well-motivated. However, some key steps (TV-Lipschitzness, constants in Theorem 1) are only sketched; full proofs in the appendix are essential to assess correctness.

Clarity:
- Generally well-organized with helpful background. Notation could be cleaned up: R2 is used as a bound but reads as “R squared”; at times R2^2 appears, which is confusing. Define precisely the notion of approximation (relative vs additive) and avoid overloaded FPRAS terminology. Be explicit about which chain (Gibbs vs Swendsen–Wang vs subgraphs-world) is assumed in each example and how expectations are estimated if sampling happens in an auxiliary state space.

Originality:
- While constraining to fast-mixing parameter sets and projecting has appeared before for inference/learning, this work’s main novelty is a unified, non-asymptotic analysis of MLE via projected gradient with biased MCMC gradients, with explicit polynomial-time schedules and near-matching lower bounds, applicable to general exponential families. The connection to an “FPRAS”-style statement for MLE within Θ is a fresh angle.

Significance:
- Theoretical significance is moderate to good: it provides the first clean convergence and complexity guarantees for a common practical heuristic (MCMC-based gradient descent) under transparent mixing assumptions, and frames MLE with arbitrary treewidth as tractable when restricting to fast-mixing Θ. Practical significance is limited by the strength of assumptions (knowledge of Θ, C, α; projection efficiency) and the conservative nature of mixing bounds. Still, it could influence future algorithms that regularize toward fast mixing or design Θ with certifiable mixing.

Suggestions/questions for authors:
- Clarify the approximation notion and revise the “FPRAS” terminology if needed.
- Provide more detail/proof for the TV-Lipschitz bound used in Theorem 1 and tighten constants/notation.
- Discuss how to estimate or adapt to unknown C, α in practice; can adaptive schedules be derived that only require empirical mixing diagnostics?
- Include additional experiments on larger graphs and comparisons to existing learning methods to assess practical trade-offs and projection costs.
- Explore analysis for persistent chains or pool-based sampling, even if partially (e.g., bounds under bounded drift between successive θk).

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a clear, general, and carefully reasoned theoretical contribution that should be of interest to the NeurIPS community, despite practical limitations and some terminological/clarity issues that can be addressed in revision.
