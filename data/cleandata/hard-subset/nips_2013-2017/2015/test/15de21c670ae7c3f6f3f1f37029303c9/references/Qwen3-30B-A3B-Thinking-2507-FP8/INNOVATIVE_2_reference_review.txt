

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper introduces a fundamentally novel approach to tractable maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by shifting the focus from low-treewidth graphical models to parameter sets where MCMC mixes rapidly. The authors propose that instead of restricting to models with low treewidth (where inference is tractable), we can restrict parameters to a set Θ where MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution, enabling efficient maximum likelihood learning. This is a bold conceptual shift from the standard approach in graphical models literature (e.g., Chow-Liu, pseudolikelihood, and score matching methods), which typically assumes well-specified models and low-treewidth structures.

The paper makes two key contributions: (1) a theoretical framework showing that for any fast-mixing parameter set Θ, maximum likelihood learning can be performed efficiently using gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates, and (2) rigorous convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases. The authors prove that with appropriate scheduling of gradient descent steps, MCMC samples, and Markov chain transitions, the algorithm achieves near-optimal likelihood with high probability. The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear assumptions and rigorous proofs for the main results, though detailed proofs are relegated to an appendix.

**Strengths:**

1. **Exceptional Novelty**: This is the paper's strongest aspect. The authors introduce a completely new notion of tractability that is not just an incremental improvement but a paradigm shift. While prior work has focused on low-treewidth models or approximate inference techniques, this paper proposes a different route to tractable maximum likelihood learning that could potentially apply to models with high treewidth but fast-mixing parameters. This is the kind of bold conceptual contribution that NIPS values highly.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides clean theoretical guarantees for the convergence behavior of the proposed method. The analysis carefully characterizes the trade-offs between optimization accuracy, number of gradient steps, samples, and Markov chain transitions. The results are general enough to apply to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models.

3. **Clear Problem Framing**: The authors effectively articulate the limitations of existing approaches (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.) and position their work as addressing these limitations, particularly the issue of model misspecification.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper provides no substantial empirical results to validate the theoretical claims in practice. The example in Section 6 is minimal (only 4x4 grid Ising model with 5 training examples) and lacks comparison to existing methods. This is a significant drawback, as the practical viability of the approach is unclear without proper empirical evaluation.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't address how to select the fast-mixing parameter set Θ for real-world problems. While the paper assumes such a set is known, it doesn't explain how to construct it for a given problem domain. The theoretical bounds also suggest that the computational cost (KMv) is quite high (O(1/ϵ^3 log(1/ϵ))), which might make the approach impractical for many applications.

3. **Limited Discussion of Practical Challenges**: The paper doesn't address important practical considerations like how to initialize the Markov chains (beyond noting the proof works for any initialization), how to handle the projection step efficiently for large models, or how the approach would interact with techniques like contrastive divergence.

**Originality and Significance:**

The core idea of using fast-mixing parameter sets as a notion of tractability is exceptionally original. It's not a minor variation on existing methods but a fundamentally different perspective on tractable learning. This could open up new research directions in maximum likelihood learning for graphical models and exponential families. While the paper doesn't make a strong practical contribution, the theoretical novelty is substantial enough to be of interest to the community.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is technically sound and clearly written. The theoretical analysis is well-structured and the main results are presented clearly. The paper effectively relates its contributions to prior work and articulates the limitations of existing methods.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. While the paper has weaknesses (particularly the lack of empirical validation), the novelty and theoretical contribution are sufficiently strong to outweigh these limitations. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper introduces a significant new direction in maximum likelihood learning that deserves to be presented at NIPS. The authors have identified a fundamental conceptual shift that could influence future work in graphical models and exponential families, even if the practical implementation details require further exploration.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of empirical results, but this is a common issue in theoretical papers, and the theoretical contribution here is substantial enough to justify the omission. The authors could potentially address this limitation in future work, but the current contribution is sufficiently novel to warrant publication.

In summary, this paper is a bold theoretical contribution that introduces a completely new notion of tractability for maximum likelihood learning. While practical implementation details and empirical validation are limited, the core idea is highly novel and significant for the field. I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to NIPS and should be accepted.
