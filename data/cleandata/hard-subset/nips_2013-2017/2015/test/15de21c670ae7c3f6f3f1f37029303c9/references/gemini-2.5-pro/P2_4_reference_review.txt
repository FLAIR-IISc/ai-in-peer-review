### Summary
This paper addresses the problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in exponential families, particularly motivated by undirected graphical models where exact MLE is intractable due to the difficulty of computing the partition function. Existing approaches either restrict the model structure (e.g., to trees) or use surrogate objective functions (e.g., pseudolikelihood), which may not converge to the true M-projection under model misspecification.

The authors propose a fundamentally different notion of tractability: restricting the model parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are guaranteed to mix rapidly. The core contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the widely used heuristic of performing projected gradient descent where the gradient is approximated using MCMC samples. The paper proves that this procedure constitutes a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for the MLE problem within the constrained set `Θ`. Separate convergence guarantees and explicit schedules for setting hyperparameters (number of gradient steps `K`, MCMC samples `M`, chain length `v`) are provided for both the general convex case (unregularized MLE) and the strongly convex case (ridge-regularized MLE).

### Quality
The technical quality of the paper is high. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis, with clear proof sketches provided in the main text. The authors build upon established results in optimization (specifically, convergence of gradient descent with inexact gradients from Schmidt et al. [26]) and MCMC theory.

**Strengths:**
1.  The analysis is thorough, carefully accounting for both the sampling error (variance from finite `M`) and the mixing error (bias from finite `v`).
2.  The paper correctly distinguishes between the convex and strongly convex settings, deriving appropriate bounds for each. In the convex case, convergence is shown for function value sub-optimality, while in the strongly convex case, a stronger result on parameter distance is provided.
3.  The results are explicit, providing concrete schedules for setting `K`, `M`, and `v` to achieve a desired accuracy `ϵ` with high probability `1-δ`. This moves beyond mere asymptotic convergence claims and provides a non-asymptotic characterization of the algorithm's complexity.
4.  The authors are honest and careful about the limitations of their work, such as ignoring the complexity of the projection step and not analyzing more complex sampling schemes like contrastive divergence or persistent chains.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The derived bounds appear to be quite conservative. The authors' own experiment demonstrates this, where they use a much smaller Lipschitz constant than the one suggested by their theory and find that the algorithm converges to a solution well within the desired tolerance `ϵθ`. While this is common for theoretical bounds, it does raise questions about the direct practical applicability of the proposed schedules.
2.  The analysis relies on a crucial assumption: the existence of a convex, fast-mixing set `Θ` for which an efficient projection oracle exists. While the paper provides compelling examples for Ising and pairwise MRFs with norm-bounded interactions, the feasibility of defining and projecting onto such sets for more complex, arbitrary models is not fully addressed. This projection step itself could be a computationally hard problem.

### Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
1.  The introduction clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the existing literature, and states the main contribution.
2.  A dedicated "Setup" section with a table of notation is very helpful for the reader.
3.  The theoretical background on MCMC mixing times is concise and sufficient to understand the core assumption (Eq. 2).
4.  The main results are presented as clearly stated theorems, followed by intuitive proof sketches that illuminate the key steps of the argument without getting lost in technical minutiae.
5.  The discussion and conclusion sections provide thoughtful reflection on the results and their limitations, which is commendable.

The paper is written with enough detail that an expert could follow the derivations and understand the core arguments of the proofs.

### Originality
The algorithm itself—projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients—is not new; it is a widely used heuristic in practice. The originality of this work lies entirely in its rigorous theoretical analysis. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide formal, non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for this method by explicitly linking the learning rate, number of samples, and MCMC chain length to the mixing time of the underlying Markov chain.

The core conceptual contribution is framing tractability in terms of fast-mixing parameter sets rather than structural properties like treewidth. This provides a new and valuable theoretical lens through which to view learning in intractable models. The work clearly differentiates itself from prior art like Heinemann and Globerson [2014], which relies on high-girth assumptions, and Steinhardt and Liang [2028], which modifies the stationary distribution itself to ensure fast mixing.

### Significance
The significance of this paper is primarily theoretical, but it is substantial. It provides a formal justification for a common and intuitively appealing class of algorithms, turning a heuristic into a provably correct approximation scheme. This is a valuable contribution to the machine learning community's understanding of the interplay between sampling, optimization, and learning.

**Potential Impact:**
1.  **For Researchers:** This work provides a foundation for analyzing MCMC-based learning algorithms. It could inspire further research into tighter bounds, analysis of more sophisticated sampling schemes (e.g., persistent contrastive divergence), and the design of new models with provably fast-mixing parameter sets.
2.  **For Practitioners:** The direct practical impact is limited by the conservative nature of the bounds. A practitioner is unlikely to use the derived schedules directly. However, the theoretical insights are still valuable: they formally show that for this method to work, the number of MCMC steps (`v`) must scale with `log(K)` and the number of samples (`M`) must scale with `K^2` (in the convex case). This provides qualitative guidance on hyperparameter scaling.

The paper successfully addresses a difficult and foundational problem (learning in intractable models) and advances our theoretical understanding in a demonstrable way.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**For Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Theoretical Framework:** Provides the first rigorous convergence guarantees for MCMC-based MLE by defining and leveraging the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets."
*   **High Technical Quality:** The analysis is sound, detailed, and provides explicit, non-asymptotic bounds.
*   **Significant Contribution:** Turns a widely-used heuristic into a provable approximation algorithm (an FPRAS), which is a fundamental contribution to the theory of learning in graphical models.
*   **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a complex topic accessible.

**Against Acceptance:**
*   **Limited Practical Utility:** The derived bounds are likely too loose for direct practical use, as acknowledged by the authors' own experiment. The paper itself states it makes "no significant practical contribution."
*   **Strong Assumptions:** The approach hinges on the ability to define and efficiently project onto a convex fast-mixing set `Θ`, which may be a strong requirement for many models of interest.

**Recommendation:**
Despite the limited direct practical application of the derived schedules, the paper's theoretical contribution is significant, original, and of high quality. It provides a solid foundation for a principled approach to learning in intractable models. The clarity of the exposition is a major plus. This is a strong theoretical paper that would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings. I strongly recommend acceptance.
