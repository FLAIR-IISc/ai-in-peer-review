Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a convex set Θ that guarantees rapid mixing of a chosen MCMC kernel for all θ ∈ Θ. Under this tractability notion—fast mixing rather than low treewidth—the authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are estimated by finite-length MCMC runs started from arbitrary initializations. They provide high-probability convergence guarantees in both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, including explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and Markov steps v. The total sampling work scales as roughly 1/ε^3 (convex) and 1/ε^2 (strongly convex), up to logarithmic factors. Under standard mixing-time assumptions (geometric decay in TV norm with constants C, α) and an efficient projection oracle onto Θ, they conclude there is an FPRAS for the MLE restricted to Θ. The framework is developed generally for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics and is illustrated via Ising models; projection onto fast-mixing sets is tied to known conditions (e.g., spectral-norm or degree/interaction-strength bounds).

Relation to prior work
- MCMC-ML and stochastic approximation have a long history (e.g., Geyer ’91; Gu & Zhu ’01), but prior work typically lacked polynomial-time guarantees for optimization accuracy.
- Pseudolikelihood, score/ration matching, composite likelihood, and high-girth approaches trade statistical optimality or model correctness for tractability; they do not recover the M-projection under misspecification nor guarantee tractable inference post-learning.
- Steinhardt & Liang (’15) ensure fast mixing by design via restarts but optimize a different stationary distribution; here the target remains the original exponential-family likelihood restricted to a fast-mixing Θ.
- Projection-based fast-mixing constraints for Ising/MRFs have been explored (Domke & Liu ’13; Liu & Domke ’14), but without end-to-end ML optimization FPRAS guarantees.
This paper unifies these strands by pairing projection to fast-mixing sets with an explicit inexact-gradient analysis that yields runtime/sample-complexity guarantees.

Strengths
- Conceptually bold: reframes tractability for learning around fast-mixing parameter sets and gives an FPRAS-type result for MLE at arbitrary treewidth.
- General exponential-family treatment with clear modular assumptions: bounded stats, geometric mixing, projection oracle.
- Clean, transparent analysis using concentration (Bernstein/Hoeffding, Efron–Stein), TV mixing bounds, and convergence of inexact projected gradient [Schmidt et al.].
- Explicit schedules for (K, M, v) and lower bounds highlight near-optimality up to constants/log factors; the strongly convex case gives the expected 1/ε^2 scaling.
- Bridges a gap between widely used but heuristic MCMC-based ML and provable optimization guarantees, with interpretable dependence on mixing parameters.

Weaknesses/Concerns
- FPRAS claim hinges on the scaling of C and α with problem size. While plausible for specific Θ (e.g., bounded interactions/degree, spectral-norm constraints), the paper does not make explicit, dimension-dependent statements ensuring that 1/(1−α) and C are polynomial in input size for broad classes. As written, “FPRAS” is correct only under such families; please clarify the regimes where polynomial dependence provably holds.
- The projection oracle is assumed efficient. This is available for some pairwise models (e.g., spectral-norm Ising) but is unclear for higher-order cliques, large alphabets, or other norms; a more systematic discussion of projection complexity vs. model class would strengthen the contribution.
- Practicality: the prescribed v from worst-case TV bounds can be very large; the method restarts chains at every iteration and for every sample, discarding the pooling/persistent strategies used in practice. The tiny illustrative experiment is insufficient to assess empirical viability.
- Notational clarity: there are confusing uses of R2/R22 (e.g., L = 4R22 + λ). A careful pass to unify notation and constants in all theorems would help reproducibility.
- The analysis assumes independent samples across gradient estimates (via fresh chains). Extensions to Markovian dependence (e.g., sample pools) are discussed but not treated; even a partial bound would improve practical relevance.

Questions for authors
- Formalize the FPRAS claim: what is the input size (N, |E|, alphabet sizes, D, bit complexity), and under which concrete Θ (e.g., ∥R(θ)∥ ≤ c with spectral/∞/1 norms) do C and 1/(1−α) remain polynomial? Can you add a theorem instantiating polynomial dependence for at least one widely used family?
- Beyond pairwise Ising/MRFs, what projection algorithms (with complexity) are known? Any approximations with guarantees if exact projection is expensive?
- Can v be set adaptively using online mixing diagnostics or coupling/spectral-gap estimates, reducing pessimism from worst-case TV bounds?
- Is there an analysis accommodating persistent chains or sample pools (dependent samples), which could reduce KMv in practice?
- How would you handle hidden-variable models within this framework?
- Could you include a moderate-scale empirical comparison (even synthetic) versus pseudolikelihood/CD to illustrate convergence to the M-projection within Θ?

Minor comments
- Tighten statements with consistent constants and norms; fix typos in Theorems 1–2 (R2 vs R22).
- Clarify that the convex result bounds f(average iterate) while the strongly convex case bounds parameter error; a short note mapping between εθ and εf (you partly discuss this in Section 5) would aid readers.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline-to-strong). The paper advances a novel and compelling direction—learning by restricting to provably fast-mixing parameter sets—and provides the first end-to-end FPRAS-style guarantees for MLE under this paradigm. Despite practical limitations and reliance on mixing/projection oracles, the conceptual contribution is significant and likely to stimulate further work on designing Θ, adaptive mixing control, and practical algorithms.

Confidence
High.
