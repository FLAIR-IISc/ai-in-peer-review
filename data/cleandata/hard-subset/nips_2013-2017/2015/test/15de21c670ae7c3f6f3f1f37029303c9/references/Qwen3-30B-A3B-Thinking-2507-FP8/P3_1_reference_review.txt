

# Peer Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical models: the intractability of maximum likelihood learning due to the #P-completeness of partition function computation. The authors propose a novel approach where the parameter space Θ is constrained to "fast-mixing" parameter sets, ensuring that MCMC sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. They then analyze gradient descent using MCMC samples to approximate the gradient, providing theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases. The main contribution is a rigorous analysis showing that with appropriate scheduling of gradient descent iterations, MCMC samples, and Markov chain transitions, one can achieve an approximate maximum likelihood solution within the constrained parameter set with polynomial computational complexity.

The paper builds on previous work on fast-mixing Markov chains (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Domke & Liu, 2013) and MCMC-based learning methods (Geyer & Thompson, 1992; Steinhardt & Liang, 2015), but makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting the mixing time of MCMC to the convergence guarantees of gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical framework connecting fast-mixing properties of MCMC to convergence guarantees for maximum likelihood learning. This is a significant theoretical advance over previous heuristic approaches.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.) and justifies the need for a different approach.

3. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The authors provide thorough analysis for both convex and strongly convex cases, with detailed bounds on computational complexity. The distinction between these cases is well-motivated and properly handled.

4. **Appropriate Theoretical Tools**: The use of mixing time bounds, Lipschitz continuity, and concentration inequalities is well-chosen and applied correctly.

5. **Relevant Example**: The Ising model example in Section 6 provides a concrete illustration of the theory, demonstrating that the theoretical bounds are not overly conservative.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Application**: The paper focuses on exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, which excludes many important models (e.g., models with unbounded sufficient statistics like Poisson distributions). The authors should discuss whether their approach can be extended to such models.

2. **Projection Complexity**: While the paper mentions that projection onto the fast-mixing set Θ is trivial for Ising models (by clipping parameters), it does not analyze the computational complexity of projection for more general models. For many models, this projection could be expensive, potentially negating the benefits of the approach. The paper should discuss how the projection cost scales with model size.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The paper provides a single small-scale Ising model example, but lacks comprehensive empirical evaluation on larger or more complex models. It would be valuable to see how the method performs compared to other approaches (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching) on standard benchmark datasets.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't address important practical considerations such as:
   - How to choose the fast-mixing parameter set Θ in practice
   - How to handle cases where the true parameter vector is not in Θ
   - How to adapt the algorithm for models with hidden variables

5. **Comparison to Related Work**: While the paper references relevant work on fast-mixing chains, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from recent work on "learning with fast-mixing models" (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang, 2015). The relationship to contrastive divergence should be clarified.

## Technical Critique

The theoretical results are generally sound, but I have some concerns about the practical applicability of the assumptions:

1. **Mixing Time Assumption**: The paper assumes a geometric bound on the mixing time (equation 3) for all θ ∈ Θ. While this is reasonable for some models (like the Ising model with bounded interactions), it's unclear whether this holds for more general exponential families. The authors should provide more examples where this assumption is satisfied.

2. **Lipschitz Constant**: The bound on the Lipschitz constant L = 4R²₂ + λ (Theorem 1) seems quite loose. In the Ising model example, the authors note that a much smaller value (L = 10 instead of L = 97) was sufficient in practice. It would be valuable to investigate whether this bound can be tightened.

3. **Computational Complexity**: The complexity bounds (e.g., KMv = O(1/ϵ³ log(1/ϵ))) are for the convex case. For the strongly convex case, the bound is better (O(1/ϵ² log(1/ϵ))), but this requires regularization. The paper should clarify whether the regularization is necessary for practical applications.

4. **Convergence in Parameter Space vs. Objective Value**: The paper shows convergence in terms of the objective value (f(θ)) for the convex case and in terms of parameter distance (||θ - θ*||) for the strongly convex case. This makes it difficult to directly compare the two cases. The paper should clarify the relationship between these two metrics.

## Relation to Related Work

The paper makes a good effort to situate itself in the literature. It correctly references key works on fast-mixing Markov chains (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Dyer et al., 2002), MCMC-based learning (Geyer & Thompson, 1992), and related theoretical guarantees (Schmidt et al., 2011). However, it could better distinguish its contribution from recent work on fast-mixing models (Steinhardt & Liang, 2015) and contrastive divergence (Carreira-Perpiñán & Hinton, 2005).

The paper also misses an important connection to work on "learning with fast-mixing models" (Wainwright, 2006), which discusses the relationship between mixing times and the accuracy of approximate inference. This connection would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation.

## Significance

The theoretical contribution of this paper is significant for the field of graphical models. It provides a rigorous foundation for using MCMC-based gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning, which has been widely used in practice but lacked theoretical guarantees. This is particularly valuable for researchers interested in understanding the theoretical properties of MCMC-based learning methods.

However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the restrictive assumption that the parameter set must be constrained to a fast-mixing set. While this constraint is natural for many models (like Ising models with bounded interaction strengths), it may not be applicable to all graphical models of interest.

## Recommendations

1. **Expand the empirical evaluation**: Add results on standard graphical model benchmarks (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10, or standard benchmark datasets like the ones used in Wainwright's work).

2. **Analyze projection complexity**: Provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set for different models.

3. **Discuss limitations more thoroughly**: Explicitly address the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding models with unbounded sufficient statistics.

4. **Clarify the relationship to contrastive divergence**: Explain how the proposed algorithm relates to and differs from contrastive divergence, which is a common practical approach for MCMC-based learning.

5. **Tighten the Lipschitz bound**: Investigate whether the bound L = 4R²₂ + λ can be tightened for specific models.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models and maximum likelihood learning. The connection between fast-mixing parameter sets and convergence guarantees for MCMC-based gradient descent is novel and significant. While the paper has some limitations regarding practical applicability and empirical validation, the theoretical results are sound and well-motivated.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by providing more comprehensive empirical results and a deeper analysis of the projection step.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Provides the first rigorous theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning
- Makes a clear connection between mixing times and learning convergence
- Well-written and technically sound
- Has potential to influence future work on theoretical foundations of MCMC-based learning

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Limited empirical validation beyond a single small example
- Projection complexity not adequately analyzed
- Some assumptions (e.g., geometric mixing time bound) may be restrictive for general applications
- Doesn't fully distinguish from related work on fast-mixing models

## Final Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that would be valuable to the NIPS community, and the weaknesses identified can be addressed with reasonable revisions.
