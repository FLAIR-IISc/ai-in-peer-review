This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, specifically addressing the intractability of learning parameters when the treewidth is high. The core idea is to restrict learning to a tractable set of parameters Θ, ensuring fast mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. The authors establish a Finite-Time Approximation (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within this constrained set, analyzing the convergence of gradient descent augmented with MCMC for gradient estimation. They provide convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex scenarios, accompanied by a schedule for the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC transitions required to achieve a desired accuracy.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.) and the need for a principled approach to tractable learning. The connection to fast-mixing parameter sets is a clever one, and framing the problem in the context of exponential families allows for a more general applicability beyond graphical models. The authors thoroughly analyze the convergence behavior of gradient descent with MCMC, addressing both the bias and variance introduced by approximate gradient estimates. The inclusion of both convex and strongly convex cases provides a more complete understanding of the algorithm’s behavior. The inclusion of an example and discussion of potential practical impacts and limitations are also valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets to guarantee FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning is a novel and interesting contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm, with clear bounds on the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC transitions required for a given accuracy.
*   **Generality:** The work extends beyond graphical models, formulating the problem within the broader framework of exponential families.
*   **Completeness:** The analysis covers both convex and strongly convex cases, providing a comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's behavior.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, although some sections (especially the background) are dense.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The inclusion of an example, even a simple one, and discussion of practical considerations strengthens the paper's impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The reliance on knowing constants (C, α) characterizing the mixing rate of the Markov chain is a significant limitation. In practice, obtaining accurate estimates of these constants can be challenging, and the theoretical guarantees rely heavily on their correctness. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn't fully explore strategies for handling uncertainty in these parameters.
*   **Projection Complexity:** The paper glosses over the computational cost of projecting onto the constraint set Θ. While mentioned, the impact of projection complexity, particularly for large-scale models, isn’t thoroughly investigated. The authors mention it might not always dominate the sampling cost, but this needs more justification.
*   **Tightness of Bounds:** The bounds on the required number of samples and iterations, while theoretically sound, may be conservative. The authors acknowledge this by providing lower bounds and discussing that their schedule isn't far off, but further analysis to tighten these bounds would be beneficial.
*   **Background Density:** Section 3 (Background) is quite dense and contains a large number of references. It would benefit from a more streamlined presentation, focusing only on the most essential results needed for the main contribution.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the example is helpful, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation with real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the paper. Evaluating performance on various graphical models and comparing against existing methods would be valuable.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper references relevant work, a deeper discussion of how the proposed approach relates to existing techniques for fast mixing (e.g., those based on coupling or spectral gap estimates) would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The rigorous analysis and convergence guarantees provide a strong foundation for future research.
*   The generality of the framework beyond graphical models is a significant advantage.
*   The potential for enabling maximum likelihood learning in settings where it was previously intractable is promising.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on knowing the mixing rate constants (C, α) is a significant practical limitation.
*   The paper lacks a comprehensive empirical evaluation, making it difficult to assess the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
*   The computational complexity of the projection step could be a bottleneck for large-scale models.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of maximum likelihood learning. The approach of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets is innovative and offers a principled way to address the intractability of learning in high-treewidth graphical models. However, the practical limitations related to estimating mixing rates and the lack of extensive empirical evaluation raise concerns.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically:

1.  **Address the issue of estimating mixing rates:** Explore strategies for handling uncertainty in C and α, or investigate approaches that are less sensitive to these parameters.
2.  **Provide a more thorough analysis of the projection complexity:** Investigate the computational cost of projection for different types of constraint sets and explore potential optimizations.
3.  **Conduct a more comprehensive empirical evaluation:** Evaluate the algorithm's performance on a wider range of datasets and compare it against existing methods.
4.  **Streamline the background section:** Focus on the most essential results needed for the main contribution.
5. **Elaborate on the connection to existing fast mixing methods.**

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
