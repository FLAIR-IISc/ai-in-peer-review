- Paper proposes using fast-mixing parameter sets to obtain an FPRAS for maximum likelihood within that set and analyzes gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients for convex and strongly convex cases.  
- Result applies broadly to exponential families, not just graphical models.  
- Situates well in prior work, contrasting with pseudolikelihood/score/composite likelihood and restricting-to-tractable-structures approaches; builds on MCMC mixing theory and SGD analyses.  
- Theoretical contributions are rigorous with detailed (sound) proofs and convergence guarantees.  
- Generality of the approach increases potential impact beyond specific graphical-model classes.  
- Provides a useful, well-written exposition with clear notation and a comprehensive background on mixing times.  
- Practical relevance is potential but limited immediately; analysis may guide parameter choices and MCMC settings.  
- Theoretical guarantees are likely conservative and may imply prohibitively large sample complexities due to dependence on constants like R and L.  
- Projection onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ may be computationally expensive and is not analyzed; this could negate benefits in some cases.  
- Empirical validation is limited to a small example; broader experiments are needed to demonstrate practical effectiveness.  
- Initialization sensitivity is not fully addressed; analysis assumes arbitrary start and lacks empirical study of initialization effects.  
- Novel combination of FPRAS existence, fast-mixing parameter sets, and noisy-gradient analysis is original and significant in theory.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, generality, novel synthesis, and addressing a fundamental learning challenge.  
- Arguments against acceptance: conservative/sample-inefficient bounds, limited experiments, and ignoring projection costs.  
- Overall recommendation: solid contribution, recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: acknowledge limitations of bounds and practical efficiency, provide wider empirical evaluation, and discuss computational cost/impact of projection.
