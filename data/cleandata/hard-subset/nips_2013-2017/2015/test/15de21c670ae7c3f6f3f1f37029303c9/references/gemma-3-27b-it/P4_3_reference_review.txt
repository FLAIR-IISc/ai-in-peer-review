This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in exponential families, focusing on scenarios where direct optimization is intractable. The core idea is to restrict learning to a tractable set Θ where MCMC sampling mixes quickly, guaranteeing a fast-mixing parameter set. The authors demonstrate that, under this condition, a Fast Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for maximum likelihood estimation within Θ. Their analysis covers both convex and strongly convex cases, providing convergence guarantees and computational complexity bounds for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial limitation of existing methods that either rely on model well-specification or suffer from high sample complexity.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical rigor and generality. The authors move beyond graphical models, framing the problem within the broader context of exponential families. The FPRAS result is a significant contribution, providing a theoretical foundation for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning in restricted parameter spaces. The careful analysis of convergence rates under different convexity assumptions (convex vs. strongly convex) is commendable. The inclusion of lower bounds on computational complexity adds to the theoretical completeness. The discussion of practical considerations, like initialization strategies and the interplay with contrastive divergence, demonstrates awareness of real-world applications. The example with the Ising model provides a concrete illustration of the theoretical results.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While mathematically sound, the practical relevance of the fast-mixing parameter set assumption could be limited. Identifying such sets for complex models might be challenging. The analysis assumes a known Lipschitz constant, which may not be readily available in practice and estimating it could add computational overhead. The paper primarily focuses on theoretical guarantees and does not provide extensive empirical validation beyond the Ising model example. The discussion of related work, while comprehensive, could benefit from more direct comparison of the proposed approach to existing alternatives in terms of practical performance. The proof sketches, while helpful, are somewhat terse, and readers may need to consult the appendix for full details. The discussion of the interplay with contrastive divergence is brief and could be expanded.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The FPRAS result for maximum likelihood learning with fast-mixing parameter sets is a significant theoretical advance.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous and provides strong convergence guarantees.
*   **Generality:** The framework extends beyond graphical models to the broader class of exponential families.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental challenge in statistical learning and provides a theoretical basis for practical algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practicality:** Identifying fast-mixing parameter sets may be difficult for complex models.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks extensive empirical evaluation beyond a single example.
*   **Assumption of Known Lipschitz Constant:** Practical estimation of the Lipschitz constant could be challenging.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with significant contributions to the field of statistical learning. The results are novel, technically sound, and generally applicable. While practical limitations exist, the theoretical foundation laid by this work is valuable and could inspire future research in both algorithm design and analysis. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting the authors to elaborate on the practical challenges of identifying fast-mixing parameter sets and to provide a more thorough comparison of their approach to existing methods in terms of empirical performance.
