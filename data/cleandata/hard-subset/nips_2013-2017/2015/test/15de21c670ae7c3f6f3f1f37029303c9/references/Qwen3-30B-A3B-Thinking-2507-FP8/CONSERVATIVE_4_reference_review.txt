

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes an approach to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families where the parameter set Θ is constrained to be "fast-mixing" - meaning MCMC sampling from the model converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The authors claim that if Θ satisfies certain mixing conditions and can be projected onto, then gradient descent using MCMC-based gradient estimates will converge to the maximum likelihood solution within Θ with high probability. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, deriving bounds on the number of gradient steps, samples, and MCMC transitions needed to achieve a given accuracy.

The paper builds on existing work on MCMC mixing times (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair [1993], Domke and Liu [2013], Liu and Domke [2014]) and recent work on fast-mixing MCMC for learning (Steinhardt and Liang [28]). While the theoretical analysis is mathematically sound, the paper's main contribution appears to be a formalization of the connection between fast-mixing parameter sets and maximum likelihood learning, rather than a fundamentally new method.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with clear proofs and proof sketches.
2. The paper correctly identifies a limitation of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.) that assume well-specified models.
3. The distinction between the standard treewidth-based tractability and the proposed fast-mixing approach is conceptually interesting.
4. The paper provides a clear analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of practical significance:** The paper explicitly states "no significant practical contribution," which is a major red flag for a NIPS paper. NIPS values both theoretical and practical contributions, and the paper fails to demonstrate how this approach would be useful in real-world applications. The only example provided (a 4x4 Ising model with 5 training examples) is trivial and doesn't compare with existing methods.

2. **Unjustified assumptions about fast-mixing sets:** The paper assumes that a fast-mixing parameter set Θ exists and can be projected onto, but provides no practical guidance on how to construct such sets for arbitrary models. For most real-world applications, it's unclear whether the fast-mixing condition would be satisfied or how to determine the appropriate Θ.

3. **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper provides no experiments comparing the proposed method with existing approaches (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching, contrastive divergence). The only example uses an extremely small dataset (5 examples) and doesn't demonstrate any advantage over simpler methods.

4. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims this approach "applies generally to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics," but the theoretical guarantees depend on constants (C, α, R2) that are hard to estimate in practice. The example section shows they had to use a lower value for the Lipschitz constant than theoretically derived to achieve convergence, indicating potential conservatism in the bounds.

5. **Limited novelty:** The paper cites Steinhardt and Liang [28] who already proposed learning using a stationary distribution from a fast-mixing chain. The paper doesn't clearly differentiate its approach from this prior work or demonstrate significant improvements.

**Comparison to existing work:** The paper's approach is related to but distinct from contrastive divergence and other MCMC-based learning methods. However, it doesn't address key practical considerations that make contrastive divergence useful in practice (e.g., how to initialize chains, how to handle non-fast-mixing regions of parameter space). The paper also doesn't connect to the large body of work on fast-mixing conditions for specific models (e.g., Ising models with bounded interactions).

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a theoretically interesting but practically unconvincing approach. While the mathematical analysis is sound, the paper fails to demonstrate how this method would be useful in real applications. The lack of empirical validation, the trivial example, and the explicit admission of "no significant practical contribution" make it difficult to justify acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking, and it doesn't address the practical challenges of applying fast-mixing constraints to real-world learning problems.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Empirical comparison with existing methods on standard benchmarks
2. A more detailed discussion of how to construct fast-mixing parameter sets for practical models
3. Analysis of the computational cost of the projection step, which could be significant
4. A clearer explanation of how this approach differs from Steinhardt and Liang [28] and why it's superior

Given the skepticism about new/unconventional approaches without extensive evidence, and the lack of practical significance, I recommend rejection. The paper's theoretical contribution is marginal given existing work on fast-mixing MCMC and the lack of convincing evidence that this approach would be useful in practice.
