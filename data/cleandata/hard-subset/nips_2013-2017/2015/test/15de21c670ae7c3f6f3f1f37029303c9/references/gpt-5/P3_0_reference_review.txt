Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood estimation in exponential families under a tractability constraint: parameters are restricted to a convex set Θ over which a chosen MCMC sampler is provably fast mixing (uniformly in θ ∈ Θ). The authors analyze projected gradient descent where each gradient is approximated by MCMC samples that are only run for a finite number of steps from an arbitrary initialization at each iteration. They provide high-probability convergence guarantees and explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and mixing steps v that yield an FPRAS for: (i) optimizing the (regularized or unregularized) likelihood over Θ (convex case), and (ii) obtaining parameter error guarantees when λ > 0 (strongly convex case). The analysis quantifies the bias-variance tradeoff induced by finite-time MCMC and the error accumulation under inexact projected GD using results by Schmidt et al. The results apply beyond graphical models to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, provided one can project onto Θ and has geometric-mixing bounds with known constants.

Relation to prior work
The idea of restricting parameters to ensure fast mixing has been explored for Ising/MRFs (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral norm conditions and projections: Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke). Learning with fast-mixing chains by construction (restart chains) was considered by Steinhardt & Liang. MCMC-ML and stochastic approximation for MLE date back to Geyer, Gu & Zhu, and others, but typically lack finite-sample, high-probability complexity guarantees. This paper’s novelty is to tie these threads together: it shows that if Θ admits uniform geometric mixing and efficient projection, then standard projected GD with finite-time MCMC gradients yields an FPRAS, with explicit K, M, v schedules.

Strengths
- Clear, modular theoretical framework: assumptions on bounded sufficient statistics, projection onto Θ, and uniform mixing are stated and used consistently.
- Concrete complexity bounds with explicit constants and schedules for K, M, v in both convex and strongly-convex cases, including lower bounds showing near-tightness up to constants/log factors.
- Generality: applies to any exponential family once you can certify fast mixing and project.
- Sound use of inexact gradient methods (Schmidt et al.) to control error accumulation and bias from finite-time MCMC.
- Useful perspective: “MLE under a tractable set Θ” yields an M-projection when the model is misspecified, contrasting with pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood which lack that guarantee.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strength of the mixing assumption: The main guarantees hinge on existence of a convex Θ with uniform geometric mixing constants C and α across all θ ∈ Θ and all initializations, and on our ability to compute or at least bound these constants. In many models, known bounds either scale with N (e.g., τ(ε) = O(N log(N/ε)/(1 − κ))) or become weak near phase transitions; to maintain a constant margin 1 − κ the set Θ may shrink with N, potentially harming statistical efficiency. The FPRAS claim is conditional on these constants behaving polynomially; this should be made explicit.
- Practicality of projection: For Ising with spectral norm constraints, convex projections exist but can be nontrivial (SVD per iteration). For general exponential families, the feasibility and cost of projection onto a “fast-mixing” Θ is unclear; the paper’s generality rests on an assumption that is hard to verify broadly.
- Gradient Lipschitz bound and notation: Theorem 1 states L = 4 R2^2 + λ where earlier ∥t(x)∥2 ≤ R2 and Ra was introduced as the bound. There is notational inconsistency between Ra, R2, and R2^2. Moreover, the proof sketch bounding ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ − φ∥ is nonstandard; typically, smoothness follows by bounding the Hessian (covariance) operator norm using bounded range arguments. Please tighten this argument and unify notation.
- Definition of FPRAS: FPRAS is usually defined for approximating function values within (1 ± ε) multiplicative error in randomized polynomial time. Here the claim is “FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution inside Θ” in terms of additive objective (convex case) or parameter error (strongly convex case). The framing is reasonable, but the definition should be clarified to avoid confusion.
- Experimental validation is minimal: a single toy Ising example, with hand-tuned L and large v, offers little empirical insight into the tightness of constants, benefits of projection, or comparison to alternatives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, CD, restarts). While the paper is primarily theoretical, some empirical comparison would strengthen the case.

Quality
The analysis is technically sound under the stated assumptions. The reliance on uniform mixing bounds and projection oracles is explicit. Some proof sketches are terse, and key smoothness/mixing-to-TV steps would benefit from more rigorous derivations in the appendix. The lower/upper bounds for total work are well structured.

Clarity
Overall clear, with helpful intuition and schedules. However, there are notational inconsistencies (Ra vs R2; R2 vs R2^2), occasional typos, and heavy use of abstract constants (a, b, c, α, βi) that could be summarized in a small table for readability. Clarify precisely which MCMC kernel is assumed (e.g., single-site Gibbs) when invoking mixing bounds in examples.

Originality
The main contribution is a clean, end-to-end complexity analysis for MLE over fast-mixing parameter sets with biased MCMC gradients. While built on known building blocks (mixing-time bounds; projections; inexact GD), the synthesis and explicit FPRAS-style schedules appear novel.

Significance
Conceptually important: it formalizes a tractable learning regime that guarantees both inference (via fast mixing) and approximate MLE optimality. Practically, applicability depends on having useful Θ with polynomially-behaved mixing constants and efficient projection, which is currently limited to certain discrete models (e.g., Ising/Potts) under conservative interaction bounds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Unify notation for the bound on ∥t(x)∥ and re-derive the Lipschitz constant via the Hessian/operator norm argument; provide a clean, dimension-free bound if possible.
- Clarify the dependence of C and α on N and graph structure; state explicitly the conditions under which the total runtime is polynomial in N and 1/ε.
- Discuss more concretely how to construct Θ and perform projection for general exponential families, beyond Ising.
- Add experiments comparing to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood and to “restart-based” learning (Steinhardt & Liang) on modest-size graphs, reporting accuracy vs runtime and illustrating the benefit/cost of projection.
- Consider analyzing the “persistent pool” variant, even if only to state why similar bounds may hold under mixing assumptions for coupled chains.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide a rigorous proof of the TV Lipschitz bound used in Theorem 1, or alternatively a direct bound on the Hessian operator norm given ∥t(x)∥ ≤ R?
2) How do C and α scale in the spectral-norm Θ for general graphs as N grows? Under what regimes does your algorithm run in time poly(N, 1/ε)?
3) Beyond pairwise discrete models, what families admit efficient projection onto a certified fast-mixing Θ?
4) Could your analysis be adapted to the persistent-sample or “pool” strategy, perhaps via mixing of coupled chains and Marton couplings?

Pros
- Solid, general theoretical contribution with explicit complexity schedules.
- Clear articulation of a principled tractable-learning regime.
- Near-tight upper/lower bounds and high-probability guarantees.

Cons
- Strong assumptions on uniform mixing and projection; limited demonstrated practicality.
- Notational inconsistencies and some loose proof sketches.
- Minimal empirical validation.

Overall: A theoretically interesting paper; with improved clarity around assumptions/notation and some empirical support, it would make a valuable contribution.
