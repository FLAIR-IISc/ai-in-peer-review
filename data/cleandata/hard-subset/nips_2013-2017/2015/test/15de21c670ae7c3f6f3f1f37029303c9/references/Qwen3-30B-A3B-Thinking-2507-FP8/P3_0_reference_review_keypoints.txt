- Paper proposes restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set Θ so MCMC-based gradient descent yields tractable maximum likelihood learning.
- Formalizes fast-mixing parameter sets with a geometric mixing bound (Equation 2).
- Shows maximum likelihood learning under Θ admits an FPRAS.
- Provides convergence guarantees for convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases.
- Derives computational complexity bounds: O(1/ϵ³) for convex and O(1/ϵ²) for strongly convex cases.
- Novel conceptual shift from structure-based tractability (low treewidth) to parameter-space restrictions ensuring fast mixing.
- Main technical contribution (Theorems 2–9) rigorously bounds MCMC bias and sampling variance and uses gradient descent-with-error results.
- Analysis is thorough, well-structured, and covers both convex and strongly convex scenarios.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and provides reproducible theoretical detail.
- Authors candidly discuss limitations, including projection cost, chain initialization, and conservative bounds.
- Strongest limitation: reliance on geometric mixing assumption, which can be restrictive for common models (e.g., high-interaction Ising).
- Practical demonstration is limited (single 4×4 Ising example) and insufficient to show scalability to complex models.
- Assumes projection onto Θ is cheap but lacks analysis of scaling cost for projections like spectral-norm constraints (can be O(N³)).
- Fails to adequately connect to contrastive divergence (CD) and common "pool" strategies used in practice.
- Theoretical bounds are conservative and do not reflect the better empirical performance observed in the example.
- Correctly situates work relative to pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, and treewidth-based approaches.
- Could better relate to CD and to Steinhardt & Liang's work on learning with fast-mixing chains.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, original, significant theoretical advance; recommended for acceptance.
- Suggested improvements: expand discussion of fast-mixing prevalence, add larger practical experiments, clarify CD connections, tighten bounds, and analyze projection complexity.
