Summary
This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a tractability notion defined by fast-mixing MCMC parameter sets. The key idea is to restrict parameters to a convex set Θ for which a Markov chain (e.g., Gibbs) mixes geometrically fast with known constants (C, α) and to assume projection onto Θ is available. The authors analyze projected gradient descent where expectations are approximated via finite-length MCMC, yielding convergence guarantees despite biased gradients. They give two regimes: (i) convex (unregularized or regularized), providing bounds on suboptimality of the averaged iterate, and (ii) strongly convex (ridge-regularized), bounding the parameter error. Scheduling of iterations K, number of samples M, and chain length v is derived to achieve an ε-accurate solution with high probability; the total number of Markov transitions scales as O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)) in the convex case and O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) in the strongly-convex case. The authors claim this constitutes an FPRAS for MLE within Θ. A small Ising example illustrates the recipe.

Relation to prior work
The idea of constraining to fast-mixing parameter sets appears in Domke & Liu (NIPS 2013) and Liu & Domke (NIPS 2014), who also discuss projection schemes under matrix-norm mixing conditions. Steinhardt & Liang (ICML 2014) design a fast-mixing chain via restarts and learn in that stationary distribution. This paper’s contribution is to provide end-to-end convergence guarantees for constrained MLE via biased MCMC gradients and to position the result as an FPRAS under bounded sufficient statistics and known mixing constants.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal: provable learning with arbitrarily high treewidth by trading structural tractability for dynamical (mixing) tractability.
- Uses standard machinery (projected gradient with inexact/bias errors; Schmidt et al. bounds; concentration inequalities) to derive finite-sample, high-probability rates with explicit dependence on K, M, v, C, α.
- General exponential-family setting (bounded sufficient statistics), not limited to Ising.
- Discussion section is self-aware, noting limitations (pool sampling, initialization, projection cost).

Major concerns
1) Overstated FPRAS claim and missing complexity model details:
- An FPRAS is typically a randomized algorithm returning a (1±ε)-multiplicative approximation in time poly(input size, 1/ε). Here the guarantee is additive suboptimality in f (or parameter error), not a multiplicative objective guarantee. The input encoding and bit complexity are not specified; dependence on model dimension, alphabet size, and data must be made explicit. The mixing constants (C, α) and projection cost are treated as given; without bounding these in terms of input size, the “FPRAS” claim is not justified. Please either (a) restate as a randomized PTAS/additive-approximation under explicit oracles, or (b) prove bit-complexity and multiplicative guarantees appropriate for MLE objective scales.

2) Dependence on unknown mixing constants and oracle assumptions:
- The schedules require known (C, α). For general models, these are unavailable or extremely loose. The paper should clarify how (C, α) are computed from Θ (e.g., via ∥R(θ)∥ bounds), and for which Θ projection is tractable. Without constructive bounds per iteration, the results are largely existential.

3) Missing algorithmic details and figures:
- “Algorithm 1” is referenced but not present; Figure 1 and Figure 2 are referenced but not included. This is crucial for clarity and reproducibility (step size, projection, sampling protocol, handling of independence across samples).

4) Notation inconsistencies and technical imprecision:
- R2 seems to denote the bound on ∥t(x)∥2, yet R22 appears repeatedly (apparently (R2)^2); Ra is listed in the symbol table but unused; L = 4R22 + λ is ambiguous. Please cleanly distinguish R (norm bound), R2 (2-norm), and their squares.
- The TV bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2R2∥θ−φ∥2 used in Theorem 1 is nonstandard; a precise lemma and proof (or citation) are needed. Pinsker-type bounds involve KL; relating TV directly to parameter distance through bounded statistics requires care.
- Equation formatting issues: e.g., in the Ising mixing bound, denominator “1−Δ tanh(β)” must be positive; define the required β-range explicitly. The sample schedules for v use log terms with constants that appear dimensionally inconsistent (e.g., “C = log(16)” in the example).
- In Theorem 2: the use of Efron–Stein and Bernstein for vector norms needs precise statements (dimension-free concentration via bounded norm is plausible but should be stated precisely).
- The strongly convex bound (Theorem 6) seems to omit dimension dependencies that can arise in vector Hoeffding unless the bound is in terms of ∥t(x)∥2 almost surely; this should be explicit and consistent.

5) References are badly broken:
- Citation numbers do not match the text (e.g., “[19, Theorem 4.9]” is attributed to mixing-time convergence; in the bibliography [19] is “Composite likelihood methods” by Lindsay). Many entries are incomplete (years, venues, pages), and some titles have typos (“Inferning”). This is unacceptable for NeurIPS.

6) Empirical evaluation is negligible:
- A single 4×4 Ising toy with hand-tuned L contradicts the theory’s prescribed constants. No comparison to established baselines (pseudo/composite likelihood, score matching, contrastive divergence) or to existing fast-mixing constrained learners (Domke & Liu; Steinhardt & Liang). No exploration of projection costs, nor of how tight schedules are in practice.

Minor issues and suggestions
- Title: “Arbitrary treewidth” is potentially misleading; the approach applies when Θ ensures fast mixing, which typically implies weak couplings rather than arbitrary interactions on dense graphs.
- Clarify independence assumptions: to get M independent samples at each iteration you must run M chains from fresh starts; the cost KMv hinges on this. If using a persistent pool, the current analysis does not apply.
- State precisely the required properties of Θ: convexity, projection oracle complexity, and a constructive mapping from θ ∈ Θ to mixing bounds for the specific chain used.
- Provide a clean, self-contained Algorithm 1, matching the analysis (step size = 1/L? projection, sampling, averaging, stopping).
- Fix all notation and index conventions (pk, qk, ek, θk−1 vs θk).
- Strengthen the experimental section or explicitly position the paper as purely theoretical (and remove the hand-tuned constants in the example).

Pros
- Conceptually neat: learning by constraining to fast-mixing regions and analyzing biased MCMC gradients.
- General exponential-family treatment with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Finite-time schedules and work bounds derived; connects mixing-time literature to learning guarantees.

Cons
- FPRAS claim overstated without a fully specified computational model and multiplicative guarantees.
- Strong dependence on unknown mixing constants and oracle projections; limited constructiveness.
- Serious clarity/notation/reference problems.
- Minimal empirical support; figures/algorithm missing.
- Several technical steps and constants are imprecise or potentially incorrect.

Recommendation: Reject
While the high-level idea is interesting and the direction is valuable, the paper in its current form has substantial issues in rigor, clarity, and presentation (especially the references and missing algorithm/figures). The FPRAS claim needs to be toned down or supported with a proper complexity model and multiplicative approximation guarantees. I encourage the authors to substantially revise the exposition, correct references, include the full algorithm and proofs, and provide more compelling empirical validation or clearly position the contribution as a theoretical existence result under explicit oracle assumptions.
