## Peer Review: Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in general graphical models, particularly concerning the computation of the partition function. Instead of attempting to approximate the partition function directly, the authors propose a novel approach: restricting the parameter space to a set Θ where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling converges quickly (i.e., is fast-mixing). They demonstrate that, under this condition, a Fast Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The core contribution lies in establishing a theoretical framework linking fast mixing of MCMC with the existence of a FPRAS for ML estimation, allowing for efficient optimization using gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation. The work builds on existing strategies like gradient descent with MCMC and provides guarantees on convergence rates, differing from methods like pseudolikelihood which lack similar assurances when the model is misspecified.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature on approximate inference and learning in graphical models. It acknowledges limitations of methods like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods, highlighting their susceptibility to model misspecification and often intractable inference. It draws inspiration from tractable families like tree-structured distributions (Chow-Liu algorithm) but proposes a more general approach based on fast mixing rather than strict tree structure.  The paper builds on theoretical results regarding MCMC convergence (Jerrum & Sinclair, Convergence Theorem) and proximal gradient descent (Schmidt et al.). It’s commendable that the authors reference relevant works extensively, providing a solid context for their contribution.  Recent work on fast-mixing models (Steinhardt & Liang) is also appropriately mentioned.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Theoretical Framework:** The central idea of leveraging fast mixing for FPRAS-based ML estimation is a significant contribution. It shifts the focus from approximating the partition function to ensuring efficient sampling within a restricted parameter space.
*   **Generality:** The results are presented in the more general framework of exponential families, broadening the applicability beyond graphical models.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of gradient descent with MCMC, establishing convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases. The convergence rates (KMv) are clearly stated and analyzed.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of an example with the Ising model and discussion of initialization strategies (contrastive divergence) ground the theoretical work in practical considerations.
*   **Clear Presentation (mostly):**  The paper is generally well-structured, with important symbols defined upfront.  The outline of the gradient descent strategy (Figure 1) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality of Fast-Mixing Set Identification:** The biggest practical hurdle is identifying parameter sets Θ that guarantee fast mixing. The paper mentions existing bounds for Ising models, but extending these bounds to more complex models is non-trivial and could be extremely difficult. This is acknowledged in the Discussion section, but it represents a major limitation.
*   **Conservative Bounds:** The theoretical bounds, while rigorous, appear quite conservative, particularly the dependence on parameters like *R* (sufficient statistic norm bound) and *L* (Lipschitz constant).  The example demonstrates this conservatism.
*   **Complexity of Projection:** While the paper acknowledges the complexity of projection onto Θ, it doesn't delve deeply into the computational cost of this operation, which can be significant for complex models.  It’s stated that the projection may be less costly than sampling, but this isn't universally true.
*   **Clarity in some sections:** Certain sections, particularly those with more complex mathematical derivations (e.g., Theorem 6 proofs), could benefit from more detailed explanations and intuitive justifications. The jump between theorems and proof sketches is sometimes abrupt.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:**  While the Ising model example is helpful, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation on a wider range of models would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to maximum likelihood learning in intractable models.
*   The results are general and applicable to a broad class of exponential families.
*   The rigorous analysis provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of MCMC-based optimization.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in statistical inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical applicability is limited by the difficulty of finding fast-mixing parameter sets Θ.
*   The theoretical bounds may be too conservative to be immediately useful in practice.
*   The paper lacks extensive empirical validation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical contribution with the potential to impact the field of graphical model learning. However, the practical limitations regarding the identification of fast-mixing sets and the conservative nature of the bounds raise concerns.  The paper meets the criteria of *Quality*, *Originality*, and *Significance*, but *Clarity* could be improved in certain sections.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance contingent on the following revisions:

1.  **Expanded Discussion of Practical Challenges:** Elaborate on the challenges of identifying fast-mixing parameter sets for complex models and discuss potential strategies for overcoming these challenges.
2.  **Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis to explore the impact of different parameter settings (R, L, α) on the convergence rates.
3.  **Improved Clarity:** Revise sections with dense mathematical derivations to improve clarity and provide more intuitive explanations.
4.  **Additional Empirical Evaluation:** Consider including additional empirical results on more diverse models.
