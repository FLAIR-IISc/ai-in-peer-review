

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing" sets where MCMC sampling converges rapidly. The main contribution is showing that under appropriate conditions, gradient descent using MCMC samples for gradient estimation yields a FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution inside the constraint set Θ. The paper provides convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by connecting MCMC mixing properties to maximum likelihood learning guarantees. The results generalize beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. The convergence analysis is technically sound, and the paper properly acknowledges related work on pseudolikelihood, score matching, and MCMC-based learning.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
1. The paper repeatedly uses "FPRAS" without defining the acronym in the abstract or main text (it's only defined in the reference section). This is a basic presentation flaw in a technical paper.

2. Mathematical notation is inconsistent throughout. For example, "θ" appears in both standard and bold forms, and "∥·∥" is sometimes used without specification of the norm (e.g., 2-norm vs. other norms). The paper claims "∥t(x)∥_2 ≤ R_2" but later uses "R_2" without defining it consistently.

3. The example section contains a critical formatting error: "τ(ϵ) ≤ ⌈ N log(N/ϵ)1−4 tanh(.2)⌉" should be "τ(ϵ) ≤ ⌈ N log(N/ϵ)/(1−4 tanh(.2))⌉" or similar. This error could lead to misinterpretation of the mixing time bound.

### Technical Gaps
1. The paper assumes "a convex set Θ and constants C and α are known such that for all θ ∈ Θ and all distributions q, ||M^v_θ q - p_θ||_TV ≤ Cα^v" (Equation 2), but provides no method to determine C and α for practical implementation. This is a significant gap, as these constants are crucial for the algorithm's parameters.

2. The proof sketch for Theorem 1 claims "||dA/dθ - dA/dφ||_2 ≤ 2R_2||p_θ - p_φ||_TV" as "easy" to derive, but the derivation is missing. The paper states "by the triangle inequality" but doesn't show the steps, making it difficult to verify this key bound.

3. The paper claims "the gradient of the likelihood is determined by the marginal distributions, whose difficulty is typically determined by the treewidth of the graph" but then shifts focus to "fast-mixing parameter sets" without explaining the relationship between treewidth and fast mixing. This disconnect weakens the motivation.

4. The paper states "the total number of Markov transitions applied through the entire algorithm, KMv grows as (1/ϵ_f)^3 log(1/ϵ_f) for the convex case" but fails to provide the derivation in the main text (it's "postponed to the appendix" without the appendix being included in the submission).

### Minor but Significant Issues
1. The paper claims "this result works entirely in the exponential family framework" but provides no concrete example of a non-graphical-model exponential family where this would be useful. This makes the claimed generality seem superficial.

2. The paper states "the paper claims no significant practical contribution" but then provides an example with a 4x4 grid Ising model. This contradiction undermines the paper's positioning.

3. The paper uses "q_k" to denote both a distribution and a parameter (in "q_k = M^v_θ_{k-1} r"), creating significant notational confusion.

4. The Conclusions section mentions "the complexity of projection itself" but provides no analysis of how the projection step scales with model size, which is critical for understanding practical feasibility.

## Overall Assessment
While the theoretical results are interesting and the paper makes a novel contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning, the technical gaps and presentation issues are substantial. The missing derivations, inconsistent notation, and formatting errors make it difficult to verify the claims, and the practical relevance is unclear due to the lack of concrete examples beyond the trivial Ising model example.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is potentially valuable, but the presentation and technical execution fall short of NIPS standards. The authors have not provided sufficient detail to allow verification of the claims, and the paper's formatting issues suggest a lack of attention to detail that is expected in top-tier machine learning conferences.

The minor methodological flaws and presentation inconsistencies are too numerous and significant to overlook, despite the paper's theoretical interest. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for acceptance at NIPS.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains too many technical and presentation flaws that undermine its contribution. The theoretical results are interesting but the paper fails to properly support its claims with sufficient detail and clarity. The minor methodological inconsistencies and formatting errors are too numerous to be overlooked in a perfectionist review.
