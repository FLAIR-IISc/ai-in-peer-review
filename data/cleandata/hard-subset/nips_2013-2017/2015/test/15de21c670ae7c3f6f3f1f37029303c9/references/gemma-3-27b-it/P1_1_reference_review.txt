## Peer Review for "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a theoretical framework for performing maximum likelihood learning in exponential families when restricted to a set of parameters that guarantee fast mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. The core contribution is demonstrating the existence of a Fully Polynomial Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within such a constraint set. The work builds upon existing gradient descent approaches utilizing MCMC for gradient approximation, providing convergence guarantees based on the mixing rate of the Markov chain and offering schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC transitions needed to achieve a desired accuracy. The authors highlight the applicability of this approach beyond traditional graphical models, framing it more generally within the exponential family.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of challenges in maximum likelihood learning for graphical models. It acknowledges the intractability of general maximum likelihood estimation (e.g., Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993) and discusses limitations of alternative approaches like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods, particularly their lack of guarantees when the model is misspecified. The work also contrasts itself with methods that restrict the model structure to a tractable form (e.g., Chow-Liu algorithm) by proposing a different notion of tractability based on MCMC mixing.  The connections to recent work on fast-mixing models like Steinhardt & Liang (2018) and earlier work on mixing times in the Ising model (e.g., Hayes, 2010) are clearly stated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Theoretical Result:**  The existence of an FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning under the specified conditions is a significant theoretical contribution. It provides a principled way to approach ML estimation in situations where traditional methods are intractable.
* **Generality:** Framing the results within the broader exponential family context expands the applicability beyond graphical models, making it relevant to a wider audience.
* **Clear Analysis:** The paper provides a relatively detailed analysis of the convergence of gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients, considering both convex and strongly convex cases. The theorems and associated proof sketches provide a reasonable level of insight.
* **Practical Guidance:** The inclusion of schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC transitions offers practical guidance, although acknowledging the conservatism inherent in the bounds is important.
* **Illustrative Example:** The included example with the Ising model helps to ground the theoretical results and demonstrates a practical application.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Practical Contribution:** The authors themselves admit that the paper lacks a significant practical contribution. The constant factors in the bounds on computational complexity are likely to be large, potentially making the method less competitive than existing heuristics in practice.
* **Mixing Time Assumption:** The dependence on knowing constants (C, α) characterizing the mixing rate of the MCMC chain is a significant limitation. In many real-world scenarios, obtaining tight bounds on these constants can be challenging or even impossible. The paper acknowledges this, but it’s a substantial hurdle for practical application.
* **Projection Complexity:**  While the paper briefly touches upon projection complexity, it doesn't delve deeply into the computational cost of projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ, particularly for complex models. This cost could be a significant factor in the overall computational budget.
* **Conservative Bounds:**  The example provided suggests that the theoretical bounds might be quite conservative.  A more thorough empirical evaluation, perhaps comparing to existing methods on a range of datasets, would be valuable.
* **Lack of Discussion on Initialization:** The impact of the initial parameter estimate (θ0) on convergence is somewhat understated. While acknowledged, exploring different initialization strategies and their effect on the required number of iterations would be beneficial.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result addressing a fundamental challenge in maximum likelihood learning.
* The generality of the results to exponential families is a positive aspect.
* The analysis provides valuable insights into the interplay between MCMC mixing and gradient-based optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical applicability of the method is questionable due to potentially large constant factors and the difficulty in obtaining tight mixing time bounds.
* The reliance on known mixing time parameters represents a significant limitation.
*  The lack of a comprehensive empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the real-world performance of the proposed approach.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a solid theoretical contribution, but its practical impact is uncertain. The result is interesting and the analysis is rigorous, but the limitations related to mixing time estimation and computational complexity raise concerns.  I lean towards **acceptance with minor revisions.**  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Address the Projection Complexity:**  Discuss in more detail the computational cost of projection for different types of models and parameter sets.
2.  **Expand on Initialization:**  Provide a more thorough discussion of initialization strategies and their impact on convergence.
3.  **Acknowledge Conservatism:** Explicitly state the potential for significant conservatism in the theoretical bounds and discuss how this might impact practical performance.
4.  **Consider a brief discussion of alternative MCMC algorithms:** Mentioning the potential for using different MCMC algorithms, and how those might affect the mixing rate (and hence the results), would be valuable.




**Rating:** 6/10 (Accept with minor revisions)
