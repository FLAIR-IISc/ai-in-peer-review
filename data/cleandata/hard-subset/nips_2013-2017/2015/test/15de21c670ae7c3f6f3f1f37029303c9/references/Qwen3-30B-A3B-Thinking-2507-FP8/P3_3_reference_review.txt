

# Peer Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the long-standing challenge of tractable maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by shifting the notion of tractability from treewidth to fast-mixing Markov chains. Instead of restricting to tree-structured models (Chow-Liu) or high-girth models (Heinemann & Globerson), the authors propose constraining the parameter space to Θ where MCMC mixing is guaranteed to be fast. They show that with this constraint, gradient descent using MCMC to approximate gradients yields a FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning within Θ. This work extends the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning methods (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang, 2015) by providing rigorous convergence guarantees under the fast-mixing assumption.

## Strengths

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions:

1. **Novel Framework**: The fast-mixing parameter set concept represents a meaningful alternative to treewidth-based tractability. It directly addresses the practical reality that many models are learnable via MCMC-based methods even when exact inference is intractable.

2. **Rigorous Analysis**: The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of gradient descent with approximate gradients, carefully bounding both bias (from MCMC sampling) and variance. The proof sketches effectively convey the intuition behind complex results.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The paper connects theory to practice by providing explicit schedules for K, M, and v (gradient steps, samples, and MCMC transitions) that achieve desired accuracy. The Ising model example demonstrates how these bounds can be computed for a concrete case.

4. **Comprehensive Coverage**: The paper handles both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases, providing complementary guarantees. The lower-bound analysis (Theorems 3, 7) shows their upper bounds are nearly optimal.

5. **Clear Exposition**: Despite technical depth, the paper is well-organized with clear notation and logical flow. The distinction between convex and strongly convex cases is particularly well-handled.

## Weaknesses

While theoretically strong, the paper has several limitations:

1. **Projection Complexity**: The paper assumes projection onto Θ is "trivial" (e.g., clipping θij), but this is not generally true. For complex models (e.g., with non-trivial constraints), projection could be computationally expensive. The paper doesn't address whether the projection cost is negligible compared to sampling costs (as claimed in the discussion).

2. **Model Expressiveness**: The paper doesn't analyze how the fast-mixing constraint affects model expressiveness. If Θ is too restrictive, the model might not capture important data dependencies, potentially limiting practical utility.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The paper claims "no significant practical contribution" and provides only a minimal example (4x4 Ising model with 5 samples). More substantial empirical results would strengthen the case for the approach's practical relevance.

4. **Contrastive Divergence**: The authors acknowledge their analysis doesn't cover the common practice of maintaining a "pool" of samples between iterations (contrastive divergence), which is widely used in practice. This is a notable limitation since contrastive divergence is a standard technique for MCMC-based learning.

5. **Mixing Time Assumptions**: The mixing time bound (Equation 2) is assumed to hold for all θ ∈ Θ, but in practice, mixing time often depends on the specific parameters. The paper doesn't address how to handle cases where mixing time varies significantly within Θ.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a theoretically important contribution to understanding when MCMC-based learning can be made efficient. The fast-mixing parameter set concept is novel and addresses a gap in the literature between theoretical guarantees and practical MCMC-based learning methods.

However, the claim of "no significant practical contribution" is problematic for a NeurIPS paper. While the primary contribution is theoretical, the paper could have strengthened its position by:
1. Providing more substantial empirical validation (even if limited)
2. Discussing practical implementation challenges more thoroughly
3. Exploring how the fast-mixing constraint compares to other tractability constraints (e.g., treewidth) in terms of model expressiveness

The theoretical analysis itself is strong and well-executed, but the practical relevance needs to be better established. The bounds provided (e.g., KMv ~ 1/ε³ log(1/ε)) are technically impressive, but their practical value depends on whether the constants in these bounds are reasonable for real-world models.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of when MCMC-based learning can be made efficient. The framework is novel, the analysis is rigorous, and the results are important for the field. While the practical validation is limited, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS.

The paper would be even stronger with:
1. More discussion of how the fast-mixing constraint affects model expressiveness
2. A more detailed comparison of the fast-mixing approach versus treewidth-based approaches
3. Some empirical results demonstrating the practical viability of the approach

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses an important practical challenge in machine learning. The paper is well-written, carefully argued, and makes a clear contribution to the field of graphical models and maximum likelihood learning. The theoretical guarantees fill an important gap between existing practical methods and theoretical understanding.
