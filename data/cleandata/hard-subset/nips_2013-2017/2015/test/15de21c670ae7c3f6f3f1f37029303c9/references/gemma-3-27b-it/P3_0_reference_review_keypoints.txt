- Proposes learning maximum likelihood within “fast-mixing” parameter sets, enabling an FPRAS for models where MCMC mixes rapidly.  
- Generalizes beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.  
- Provides convergence guarantees for gradient descent using MCMC-estimated gradients in both convex and strongly convex settings.  
- Builds on and contrasts with approximate likelihood methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching) and tractable parameterizations, focusing on sampling rather than structure simplification.  
- Connects to prior mixing-time analyses (e.g., Ising models) and leverages those results for theoretical guarantees.  
- Uses projected gradient descent with error bounds adapted to the MCMC context.  
- Novel shift from structural tractability to algorithmic tractability by exploiting fast-mixing parameter sets.  
- Broad applicability due to extension to the exponential family framework.  
- Strong mathematical analysis with clear rates and separation of convex/strongly convex cases.  
- Completeness of theoretical treatment, including lower bounds and an empirical example.  
- Well-written and organized presentation with helpful notation and flow.  
- Practicality concerns: projecting onto the fast-mixing set may be computationally expensive, especially for dense graphs.  
- Theoretical bounds appear conservative in examples, potentially overestimating computational cost.  
- Limited discussion of sample initialization strategies (e.g., contrastive divergence) and their integration into the framework.  
- Heavy reliance on accurate mixing-time bounds; sensitivity to constants C and α is not fully analyzed.  
- For acceptance: strong theory, promising direction, and high-quality presentation argue in favor.  
- Against acceptance: practical projection cost and dependence on external mixing-time bounds raise concerns.  
- Overall: strong, novel, and theoretically solid paper with practical limitations; recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: analyze projection computational cost, explore efficient initialization techniques, perform sensitivity analysis for C and α, and clarify limitations when mixing-time bounds are unavailable.
