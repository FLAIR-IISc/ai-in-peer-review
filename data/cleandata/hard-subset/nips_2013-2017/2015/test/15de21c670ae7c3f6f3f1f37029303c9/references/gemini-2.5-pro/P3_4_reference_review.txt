This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood learning in general undirected graphical models (and more broadly, exponential families). The central idea is to constrain the model parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is guaranteed to be fast-mixing. By doing so, the gradient of the log-likelihood, which depends on intractable expectations, can be tractably approximated using MCMC samples. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of a projected gradient descent algorithm that uses this approximation strategy. The key contribution is proving that this common, yet largely heuristic, approach constitutes a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood parameters within the constrained set `Θ`. The analysis derives explicit convergence rates and complexity bounds for both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (L2-regularized) settings, providing schedules for setting the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and MCMC transitions to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.

This work is well-positioned within the literature. It acknowledges the intractability of exact ML learning and contrasts its approach with two dominant paradigms for achieving tractability: (1) methods like pseudolikelihood or score matching, which alter the objective function and may not converge to the correct M-projection in misspecified settings, and (2) methods like the Chow-Liu algorithm, which restrict the graph structure to be tractable (e.g., a tree). The paper's novelty lies in defining tractability via the dynamics of inference (fast mixing) rather than the model's static structure. This provides a formal theoretical foundation for a family of widely used MCMC-based learning algorithms (e.g., MCMC-ML, and related ideas in contrastive divergence), for which rigorous convergence guarantees have been elusive due to the biased nature of the MCMC gradient estimate. The analysis correctly builds upon established results in MCMC mixing time theory and optimization with inexact gradients.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem in machine learning. Providing the first formal convergence guarantee for maximum likelihood learning within a "fast-mixing" parameter set is a significant theoretical contribution. It formalizes and legitimizes a popular class of practical algorithms, bridging the gap between heuristic practice and theoretical understanding.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The proofs combine concepts from optimization theory (convergence of projected gradient with errors), probability (concentration inequalities like Bernstein's and Hoeffding's), and MCMC theory in a non-trivial way. The derivation of explicit schedules for the hyperparameters (`K`, `M`, `v`) and the resulting complexity bounds on the total work (`KMv`) are a major strength, making the results concrete and interpretable.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem setup is clear, the assumptions are explicitly stated, and the main theorems are presented with helpful proof sketches that provide intuition before deferring the full details to the appendix. This structure makes a dense theoretical paper highly readable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality of Assumptions:** The entire framework rests on the ability to define a convex set `Θ` of fast-mixing parameters and, crucially, an efficient algorithm to project an arbitrary parameter vector onto this set. While the paper provides compelling examples for Ising models (e.g., bounding interaction strengths or the spectral norm of the interaction matrix), the class of models for which such sets and efficient projection operators are known remains limited. This may restrict the immediate practical applicability of the proposed method.
*   **Conservatism of the Bounds:** As honestly demonstrated in the toy example, the derived theoretical bounds on the required number of samples and iterations can be very loose, leading to schedules that are likely overly conservative for practical use. While this is common in theoretical analyses, it does mean the primary contribution is one of understanding and guarantees, rather than a recipe for a state-of-the-art practical algorithm. The `O(1/ϵ^3)` complexity for the convex case, in particular, is quite steep.
*   **Lack of Empirical Comparison:** The paper explicitly notes it "claims no significant practical contribution," which is a fair stance for a theoretical work. However, the paper would have been strengthened by a more substantial empirical evaluation. For instance, on a synthetic problem where the true distribution is known but has high treewidth (and is outside `Θ`), it would be valuable to compare the quality of the M-projection found by this method against the solutions found by pseudolikelihood or score matching. This would help ground the theoretical guarantee (convergence to the true M-projection) in an empirical result.
*   **Divergence from Practical Heuristics:** The analysis relies on restarting the MCMC chain from an arbitrary distribution for each gradient step to ensure independence for the concentration bounds. This contrasts with common and effective heuristics like contrastive divergence or maintaining a persistent "particle pool" across iterations, which use the previous state to warm-start the chain. While the authors correctly note their proof technique does not apply to such dependent sampling schemes, this is a key limitation in relating the theory to how these methods are most successfully used in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality, technically deep, and significant theoretical paper. It provides the first formal justification for a widely used class of algorithms for learning intractable graphical models. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the gap between theory and practice—a common and acceptable feature of foundational theoretical work—rather than any technical flaws. The paper is clearly written, thoroughly executed, and makes a valuable and lasting contribution to our understanding of the interplay between inference, learning, and optimization in probabilistic models. It is an excellent fit for NIPS and I strongly recommend acceptance.
