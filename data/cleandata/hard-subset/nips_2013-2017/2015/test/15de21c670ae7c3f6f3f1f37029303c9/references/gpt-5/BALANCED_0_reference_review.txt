Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood estimation (MLE) for exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting graph structure (e.g., trees) or optimizing surrogate objectives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood), the authors restrict parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ for which an MCMC transition operator mixes geometrically with known constants (C, α). Given access to a projection onto Θ, they analyze a widely used learning strategy—projected gradient descent with gradients estimated by running MCMC for v steps and averaging M samples—providing explicit finite-time accuracy guarantees. They derive rates for both the convex (λ = 0) and strongly convex (λ > 0, via ridge) cases, choose K, M, v schedules, and show near-optimality via lower bounds. The resulting total work scales as ~O(ϵf^−3 log(1/ϵf)) for the convex objective gap and ~O(ϵθ^−2 log(1/ϵθ)) for the strongly convex parameter error, establishing an FPRAS for the constrained MLE provided mixing and projection oracles exist. The framework applies to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, and the paper discusses instantiations, especially Ising/Potts models under known mixing conditions (e.g., Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum/Hayes bounds for single-site Gibbs; Jerrum–Sinclair and subgraphs-world chain for ferromagnetic Ising). The analysis combines Lipschitzness of the gradient (via TV bounds), concentration (Efron–Stein, Bernstein, Hoeffding), and convergence of projected (proximal) gradient descent with errors (Schmidt et al.).

Relative to prior work: Chow–Liu provides tree-structured MLE; pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, and their variants trade statistical for computational tractability; projection onto fast-mixing sets for MRFs was studied by Domke & Liu (NIPS’13) and Liu & Domke (NIPS’14), and Steinhardt & Liang (ICML’15) proposed learning with fast-mixing restart chains. MCMC-MLE and stochastic approximation (Geyer; Gu & Zhu; Salakhutdinov) are classical but lack nonasymptotic complexity guarantees. This paper’s contribution is a general, explicit, finite-time complexity analysis yielding an FPRAS under fast-mixing parameter constraints, with near-optimal schedules and lower bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: isolates a tractable subset (fast-mixing Θ) and shows that within Θ, MLE is efficiently approximable with explicit K/M/v schedules.
- Generality: phrased for exponential families with bounded statistics; not limited to a single chain or model class.
- Theoretical completeness: convex and strongly convex regimes; Lipschitz gradient bound; high-probability guarantees; lower bounds that show near-optimality up to constants/logs.
- Connection to practice: analyzes a widely used heuristic (MCMC-based gradient descent) and clarifies conditions under which it provably works; relates to contrastive divergence and restart chains.

Weaknesses
Quality (soundness and completeness)
- The FPRAS claim hinges on knowing or being able to enforce a convex Θ with explicit C, α and an efficient projection ΠΘ. For important models, computing tight C, α or guaranteeing geometric mixing can be intricate; constants can be extremely loose or scale poorly near phase transitions. The paper treats these as given, pushing substantial difficulty into the oracle assumptions.
- The independence assumptions across iterations (fresh M independent samples from chains run v steps from an arbitrary r) and the lack of sample recycling limit realism; many practical algorithms use persistent chains or sample pools. The analysis does not cover these widely used variants.
- Projection costs are ignored in the complexity, yet for spectral-norm constraints the SVD-based projection scales superlinearly and could dominate for large models.
- Some proof elements are sketched; while plausible and standard, fuller details (especially for Theorems 3/7 lower bounds and TV-to-Lipschitz conversion) would aid verification.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, but notation around R2 is confusing: R2 denotes a norm bound while expressions like 4R2^2 appear; consider renaming the bound (e.g., R) to avoid squared-looking symbols.
- “FPRAS” is used informally; a formal statement in the bit-complexity model specifying dependence on problem size (N, parameter dimension) and on C, α, and projection accuracy would strengthen the claim.
- Minor typos and citation inconsistencies (e.g., “Inferning,” “Heinemann”) should be fixed.

Originality
- The novelty lies in unifying fast-mixing parameter projections with an explicit MLE FPRAS and near-optimal schedules/lower bounds. However, the techniques (inexact gradient descent with biased MCMC gradients + standard concentration) are largely adaptations of known tools. Relative to Domke & Liu and Steinhardt & Liang, the main advance is a more complete optimization-time analysis for MLE inside Θ.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is moderate: clarifies when mainstream MCMC-trained exponential families can be optimized with provable complexity. Practical impact is limited at present: experiments are minimal (a 4×4 Ising), constants are conservative, and oracle requirements are strong.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical contribution that fills a gap (finite-time bounds/FPRAS) for a widely used learning approach.
- Broadly applicable framework for exponential families; insightful connection to mixing-time constrained parameter sets.
- Near-optimal schedules supported by lower bounds.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on strong oracle assumptions (known Θ with efficient projection and explicit C, α); practical feasibility and tightness of constants are unclear.
- Minimal empirical validation; no comparisons demonstrating utility relative to pseudolikelihood, score matching, or restarted-chain learning.
- Does not analyze practical variants (persistent chains, sample pools) that practitioners use.
- Some clarity/notation issues and proof details deferred.

Suggestions for improvement
- Formalize the FPRAS statement in the standard complexity model, detailing dependence on N, dimension, bit-precision, and on C, α, and projection error; discuss when these constants are polynomial in N.
- Expand discussion and algorithms for projection onto Θ for different chains (Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, subgraphs-world) beyond Ising, including computational costs and approximation/projection error.
- Provide experiments on medium-scale grids and dense graphs comparing to pseudolikelihood/score matching and to restarted-chain training, reporting optimization error vs. wall-clock and sensitivity to K/M/v.
- Analyze or at least bound the effect of sample reuse/persistent chains; even partial results (e.g., under mixing between iterations) would enhance practicality.
- Tighten constants in the Lipschitz bound and explore adaptive schedules/step sizes; report empirical conservatism.
- Improve notation and fix typos.

Recommendation: Reject (leaning borderline). The paper presents a clean and potentially useful theoretical framework, but the reliance on strong oracle assumptions, the lack of substantive empirical evidence, and the limited treatment of practical training variants make it premature for NeurIPS. Strengthening the formal FPRAS claim, broadening projection/mixing instantiations, and adding meaningful experiments would substantially improve the case.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
