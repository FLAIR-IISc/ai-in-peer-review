This paper addresses the fundamental and long-standing problem of maximum likelihood learning in intractable undirected graphical models. The key idea is to depart from traditional notions of tractability based on graph structure (e.g., low treewidth) and instead define a tractable set of parameters `Θ` as one for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is provably fast-mixing. The authors then analyze a projected gradient descent algorithm that uses MCMC samples to approximate the intractable gradient of the log-likelihood. The main contribution is a rigorous proof that this common heuristic can be turned into a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained maximum likelihood problem. The paper provides explicit schedules for setting the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and MCMC chain lengths to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability, for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (L2-regularized) objectives.

This work provides a significant theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms. While methods like contrastive divergence and its variants have been practically successful, they have largely lacked convergence guarantees to the maximum likelihood estimate. This paper bridges the gap between MCMC theory (mixing time bounds) and optimization theory (convergence of stochastic gradient methods with biased gradients) to provide the first such guarantees, under the assumption of a fast-mixing parameter set.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The core concept of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets is both elegant and powerful. It provides a new lens through which to view the problem of learning intractable models. By providing a rigorous theoretical analysis for MCMC-based MLE, the paper makes a foundational contribution that could influence future research in this area. It formalizes the intuition that if MCMC works well for a given set of parameters, learning within that set should also be possible.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is detailed and appears correct. The authors carefully decompose the error in the gradient approximation into two components: the bias due to a finite number of MCMC transitions (`v`) and the variance from a finite number of samples (`M`). They then leverage existing results on the convergence of projected gradient descent with inexact gradients (specifically, Schmidt et al. [26]) and combine them with concentration inequalities to derive their final complexity bounds. The separate treatments for the convex and strongly convex cases are thorough and highlight the different convergence behaviors.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem setup, motivation, and assumptions are laid out clearly in the introduction and Section 2. The main results are stated precisely as theorems, and the accompanying proof sketches provide valuable intuition without getting bogged down in excessive detail (which is deferred to the appendix). The example in Section 6, while simple, effectively illustrates the application of the theory and demonstrates that the derived schedules are sufficient (if conservative) in practice.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Practicality of the "Fast-Mixing Set" Assumption:** The entire framework hinges on the existence of a known, non-trivial, convex set `Θ` where mixing is guaranteed, and onto which one can efficiently project. While the paper provides compelling examples for Ising models (e.g., bounding interaction strengths or the spectral norm of the interaction matrix), the practical utility for more complex, general MRFs is less clear. The key questions are:
    *   How expressive are these fast-mixing sets? Does constraining parameters to `Θ` severely limit the model's ability to capture the target distribution?
    *   How difficult is it to find/characterize `Θ` for new models or MCMC samplers? Many mixing time results in the literature are asymptotic or apply to specific cases (e.g., uniform interactions), making them difficult to adapt for learning.
    *   The efficiency of the projection operator `Π_Θ` is critical. While trivial for box constraints, it can be computationally expensive for other norms (e.g., the spectral norm projection requires SVDs). This cost is not included in the final complexity analysis (`KMv`).

2.  **Approximation Quality vs. True M-projection:** The paper correctly points out that methods like pseudolikelihood do not converge to the M-projection of the data distribution onto the model family. However, the proposed algorithm converges to the M-projection onto the *restricted set* `Θ`, i.e., `argmin_{θ∈Θ} KL(p_data || p_θ)`. The true M-projection may lie outside `Θ`. A more nuanced discussion of this trade-off is warranted. It is conceivable that for some problems, the solution from a method like pseudolikelihood could be closer in KL divergence to the data distribution than the constrained MLE solution, if the true M-projection is far from `Θ`. The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging this approximation gap.

3.  **Conservatism of the Bounds:** As is common with such theoretical analyses, the derived bounds on `K`, `M`, and `v` are likely to be very conservative in practice, a fact acknowledged by the authors in the example section. The bounds depend on worst-case constants like the norm of the sufficient statistics (`R2`) and the Lipschitz constant (`L`). While providing a crucial existence proof, these bounds might be too loose to be used prescriptively. It would be interesting to see a discussion on whether problem-dependent or adaptive schedules could lead to tighter, more practical results.

4.  **Relationship to Contrastive Divergence (CD):** The discussion briefly touches on CD, but could be expanded. The proposed method uses a cold start for the MCMC chains at each iteration, which is crucial for the theoretical analysis based on standard mixing time definitions. This is in stark contrast to CD, which uses a warm start from the data itself and typically very few MCMC steps (`v=1`). While the authors correctly state their proof technique does not apply to that setting, a deeper discussion of the implications would be valuable. Does this analysis suggest that CD's success relies on implicit regularization or some other mechanism not captured by standard MLE? This work provides the perfect context for such a discussion.

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of learning in graphical models. It tackles a difficult, important problem with a novel approach and provides a rigorous, well-executed analysis. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations on the immediate practical applicability and avenues for future work, rather than fundamental flaws in the presented results. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and its ideas are likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
