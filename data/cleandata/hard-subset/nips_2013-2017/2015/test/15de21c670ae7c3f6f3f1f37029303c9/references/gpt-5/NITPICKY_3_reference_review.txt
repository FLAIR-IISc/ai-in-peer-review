Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families under a tractability notion based on fast-mixing Markov chains. It assumes a convex parameter set Θ such that, for any θ ∈ Θ, a chosen Markov chain Mθ mixes geometrically in total variation with known constants C, α, and that one can project onto Θ. The main results analyze projected gradient descent in which gradients are estimated via MCMC: (i) a Lipschitz gradient bound L = 4R^2_2 + λ (R_2 bounds the ℓ2 norm of sufficient statistics) and (ii) high-probability convergence rates for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, including schedules for iterations K, number of samples M, and Markov steps v to achieve target accuracy in function value (convex) or parameters (strongly convex). The total MCMC work scales roughly as O(ϵ_f^-3 log(1/ϵ_f)) in the convex case and O(ϵ_θ^-2 polylog) in the strongly convex case. An Ising 4×4 toy example illustrates the schedules.

Relation to prior work
This extends longstanding MCML ideas (e.g., Geyer 1991) by giving finite-sample complexity bounds under fast-mixing parameter sets, using inexact projected gradient descent analyses (Schmidt et al. 2011). It connects to literature on fast mixing via Dobrushin and spectral-norm conditions (e.g., Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes), and to projections onto fast-mixing sets for Ising/MRFs (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke). It also positions itself relative to surrogate objectives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood) and to Steinhardt & Liang’s restart-augmented fast-mixing approach.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean: decouples learning tractability from treewidth by constraining to fast-mixing Θ.
- Provides explicit high-probability bounds tying K, M, v to accuracy, with dependence on mixing parameters (C, α), R_2, and L.
- Bridges fast-mixing theory and learning via projection onto Θ, and gives schedules close to lower bounds (up to constants/logs).
- General exponential-family framing (bounded sufficient statistics), not limited to Ising.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- FPRAS claim is not precisely formulated. For optimization, an FPRAS typically requires polynomial time in problem size and 1/ϵ with a multiplicative accuracy notion; here guarantees are additive in f or ℓ2 in θ. Moreover, runtime depends on C and α, which may scale poorly with N; without explicit polynomial dependence in model size, the FPRAS claim is overstated.
- Mixing assumption and projection: Assuming known C, α for all θ ∈ Θ (and an efficient projector) is very strong and poorly instantiated beyond a few special cases (Ising with Dobrushin/spectral-norm constraints). Many exponential families lack tractable projection operators or usable bounds for C, α.
- Sampling independence and algorithm specification are unclear. The analysis appears to require M i.i.d. samples from q_k each iteration (total cost KMv). The text neither states that M independent chains are restarted from r per sample nor provides Algorithm 1. If samples are taken sequentially from a single chain, dependence invalidates the concentration bounds used. Please clarify independence, burn-in, and step-size; they are critical for Schmidt et al.’s results.
- Lipschitz bound (Theorem 1) and constants: Notation is inconsistent (R_2 vs R2, R_a; 4R22 appears repeatedly). The claimed inequality ∥p_θ − p_φ∥_TV ≤ 2R_2 ∥θ − φ∥_2 is nonstandard and needs a careful proof (typically one goes via bounded likelihood-ratio or Pinsker from KL; dimension factors may appear). The constant 4R^2_2 seems suspicious dimensionally. Full proof is deferred and not available.
- Concentration in the strongly convex case: A “multidimensional Hoeffding” is invoked to control ∥M^-1 ∑ t(x) − E[t]∥_2 without dimension dependence. Most vector concentration inequalities introduce either dimension or a sub-Gaussian parameter; using only a bound on ∥t(x)∥_2 ≤ R_2 to obtain R_2(1+√(2 log(1/δ)))/√M seems too optimistic. Please cite a precise result and reconcile any dimension dependence.
- Lower bounds (Theorem 3/7) are derived by forcing each term under a square-root bound below √ϵ and multiplying. This is a crude decoupling; calling them “lower bounds” on total work should be qualified as heuristic/loose.
- Title and positioning: “Arbitrary treewidth” is potentially misleading. The approach restricts to fast-mixing regimes (e.g., Dobrushin), which exclude many high-treewidth, low-temperature models where learning/inference is difficult. This should be emphasized earlier.
- Relation to literature on stochastic approximation with Markovian noise is missing (e.g., Moulines & Bach 2011; related SA/MCMC analyses), as is work on unbiased gradient estimators via coupling/Poisson tricks. This weakens novelty claims.
- Example is too small and deviates from theory: it uses L=10 instead of the theoretical 97, uses C=log(16) (units mismatch since C is multiplicative in TV bounds), and ε_θ=2 is very loose. Figure 2 is missing. This does not validate the schedules or constants.
- Formatting and clarity:
  - Algorithm 1 and Figure 1 are referenced but not present; the paper is not self-contained.
  - Many typos and notation glitches: R2 vs R_2, R_a; KMV vs KMv; missing parentheses in logs; “Inferning”; inconsistent use of bold/indices; equations with mismatched symbols (e.g., a/√K term derivations).
  - References incomplete (years, venues missing), and some key works are absent.

Quality
Technical direction is interesting, but multiple core claims hinge on strong assumptions, and several bounds appear to rely on unproven or misstated inequalities. Missing algorithm details and proof sketches reduce reproducibility.

Clarity
Currently below NeurIPS standards due to missing algorithm/figures, notation inconsistencies, and numerous typos.

Originality
Moderate. The angle of projecting onto fast-mixing sets and analyzing MCML via inexact projected gradient is nice, but adjacent to existing lines. The FPRAS framing would be more original with a precise formulation and verified polynomial dependence on model size.

Significance
Potentially useful if assumptions can be instantiated for broad classes and constants clarified. As written, practicality is limited and empirical support is minimal.

Questions for authors
1) Precisely define the FPRAS notion used here (objective, additive vs multiplicative accuracy) and state runtime polynomially in problem size parameters. How do C and α scale with N for your Θ?
2) Do you require M independent chains per iteration? If not, how do you handle dependence? Please include Algorithm 1 with step sizes.
3) Provide a complete proof or citation for Theorem 1 and the “multidimensional Hoeffding” bound, clearly stating any dimension dependence.
4) Can your analysis handle inexact projection onto Θ? What is the impact on rates?
5) Can you provide experiments beyond 4×4 Ising, and with constants faithful to the theory?

Pros
- Clear conceptual framework linking fast mixing and learnability.
- Finite-sample convergence schedules with explicit dependencies.
- Bridges mixing-time literature and learning via projection.

Cons
- FPRAS claim unclear/overstated; dependence on mixing constants not tied to problem size.
- Strong, hard-to-satisfy assumptions (known C, α; efficient projection).
- Missing algorithm, proofs, and rigorous handling of sample dependence.
- Notation/formatting issues; minimal, unconvincing experiments.
