

# NIPS Peer Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is intractable for general models due to the #P-completeness of computing the partition function (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993). The authors propose a novel approach by restricting the parameter space Θ to "fast-mixing" sets, where MCMC sampling converges quickly to the stationary distribution. Their key theoretical contribution is showing that for such parameter sets, there exists a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning using gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation.

The paper builds on the observation that while methods like pseudolikelihood and Chow-Liu (for tree-structured models) have limitations (inferior sample complexity, well-specified model assumption, intractable inference), the authors' approach guarantees convergence to the M-projection even when the model is mis-specified. They provide rigorous theoretical guarantees for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases, deriving bounds on the computational complexity (KMv) needed to achieve a desired optimization accuracy.

This work relates to several threads in the literature: Jerrum & Sinclair (1993) on intractability of partition functions, Steinhardt & Liang (2013) on learning with fast-mixing chains, and Domke & Liu (2013) on projection for fast-mixing Ising models. The paper is distinct from previous work by providing a formal theoretical guarantee for the widely-used MCMC-based gradient descent approach under the fast-mixing assumption.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis with well-motivated assumptions and carefully proven theorems. The bounds on computational complexity (KMv) are derived with appropriate attention to the interplay between the number of gradient steps, samples, and Markov chain transitions.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, tree-structured models) and positions their approach as a solution to these limitations.

3. **General Framework**: The results apply broadly to any exponential family, not just graphical models, which is a significant strength. The connection to existing mixing time results for models like Ising (e.g., Domke & Liu, 2013; Liu & Domke, 2014) is well-established.

4. **Appropriate Theoretical Framework**: The use of mixing time bounds (Equation 2) as a key assumption is well-justified by the literature on MCMC convergence (e.g., [19, Theorem 4.9]).

5. **Practical Insight**: The example with the 4×4 Ising model, while small, demonstrates the feasibility of the approach and shows that the theoretical bounds are conservative in practice.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Practical Contribution**: The paper explicitly states "no significant practical contribution" and provides only a single small-scale example. For a NIPS paper, this is a significant weakness, as empirical validation is expected. The theoretical bounds are quite conservative (as demonstrated in the example where the algorithm found a solution tighter than the specified ϵθ).

2. **Implementation Details Missing**: The paper doesn't address how to choose the fast-mixing set Θ in practice. While projection onto Θ is mentioned, it doesn't discuss how to determine what set Θ should be for a given problem (e.g., what values of β to use for the Ising model constraints).

3. **Strong Assumptions**: The mixing time assumption (Equation 2) is quite strong and may not hold for many practical models. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't discuss how to verify it in practice or what alternatives exist if the assumption fails.

4. **Comparison with Existing MCMC-Based Methods**: The paper doesn't compare with existing methods that use MCMC for learning (e.g., contrastive divergence, MCMC maximum likelihood) in terms of practical performance. The analysis would be strengthened by showing how the proposed method compares to these approaches.

5. **Computational Complexity**: The bounds (O(1/ϵ^3) for convex case) are quite high, making the approach potentially impractical for many applications. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't discuss whether these bounds can be improved.

6. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: Beyond a single small example, there are no experiments comparing the proposed method with existing approaches in terms of convergence speed, accuracy, or computational efficiency.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The proofs (as sketched) appear to be correct, and the theorems are well-motivated. The paper carefully distinguishes between convex and strongly convex cases and provides appropriate bounds for each. However, the lack of empirical validation is a significant weakness for a machine learning conference like NIPS. The paper would be stronger with more experimental evidence showing how the method performs on real-world problems.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The notation is consistent throughout, and the main results are clearly stated. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical implications is made explicit. The example in Section 6 provides a good concrete illustration of the theoretical results. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how to choose the fast-mixing set Θ in practice, which would improve clarity for potential implementers.

## Originality

The core idea of using fast-mixing parameter sets for maximum likelihood learning is novel. While MCMC-based gradient descent is not new (e.g., contrastive divergence), the paper provides the first theoretical guarantee for this approach under the fast-mixing assumption. The paper is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang, 2013; Domke & Liu, 2013) by providing a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the computational complexity rather than focusing on a specific model or algorithm.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning (intractable maximum likelihood learning), but the proposed solution has limited practical significance due to the high computational complexity and lack of empirical validation. The theoretical contribution is significant in the sense of providing a formal guarantee for a widely-used approach, but the bounds are not tight enough to be practically useful. For the field, the work provides a theoretical foundation for MCMC-based learning methods, but it doesn't offer a practical improvement over existing methods.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding when MCMC-based gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning can be guaranteed to converge efficiently. However, the lack of empirical validation and the conservative theoretical bounds significantly limit its practical significance. For a NIPS paper, the theoretical contribution is strong, but the absence of empirical results is a notable weakness.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject**

The paper would be significantly stronger with empirical validation showing the practical performance of the method on real-world datasets. Without this, the contribution is primarily theoretical with limited practical impact, which is less compelling for NIPS compared to papers that combine strong theory with empirical validation.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and addresses a fundamental problem in graphical models
- The paper provides the first formal guarantees for MCMC-based gradient descent under fast-mixing assumptions
- The results apply broadly to any exponential family, not just graphical models
- The work builds on and extends existing literature on mixing times (Jerrum & Sinclair, Domke & Liu)

**Con Acceptance:**
- No significant empirical validation beyond a single small example
- Theoretical bounds are quite conservative (as shown in the example)
- Lack of discussion on how to choose the fast-mixing set Θ in practice
- Computational complexity bounds (O(1/ϵ^3)) are high for practical applications
- Doesn't compare with existing MCMC-based learning methods in terms of practical performance

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add empirical results on larger datasets to demonstrate the practical feasibility of the approach.

2. Discuss how to choose the fast-mixing set Θ in practice, including heuristics for determining appropriate constraints.

3. Compare the proposed method with existing MCMC-based learning approaches (e.g., contrastive divergence) in terms of convergence speed and accuracy.

4. Discuss potential ways to improve the theoretical bounds (e.g., by making weaker assumptions about mixing times).

5. Add a more detailed discussion of the practical limitations of the approach and when it might be preferable to use other methods.

This paper has strong theoretical foundations but would benefit significantly from empirical validation and a more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations to be a compelling NIPS submission.
