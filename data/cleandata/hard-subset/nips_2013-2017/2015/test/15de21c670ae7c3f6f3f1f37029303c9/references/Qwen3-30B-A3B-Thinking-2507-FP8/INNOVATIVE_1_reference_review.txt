

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper presents a fundamentally novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by shifting the focus from restricting graphical structure (e.g., to trees) to restricting parameter space to "fast-mixing sets" where MCMC sampling converges rapidly. The authors demonstrate that when parameters are constrained to such sets, gradient descent using MCMC samples to approximate gradients converges to the maximum likelihood solution with theoretical guarantees. This represents a significant conceptual shift from traditional tractable learning approaches (Chow-Liu, pseudolikelihood, score matching) that typically restrict model structure rather than parameter space.

The paper's core contribution is its theoretical analysis showing that for fast-mixing parameter sets, a FPRAS exists for maximum likelihood learning within the constrained set. The authors provide detailed complexity bounds for both convex and strongly convex cases, showing that total computational effort scales as (1/ϵ)^3 log(1/ϵ) for convex optimization and (1/ϵ)^2 log(1/ϵ) for strongly convex cases. These results establish a theoretical foundation for using MCMC-based learning strategies with rigorous guarantees, addressing a key gap in the literature where such methods typically lack theoretical support.

What makes this paper particularly compelling is its bold reimagining of tractability. Traditional approaches assume low-treewidth models for tractable inference, but the authors instead focus on ensuring that MCMC sampling is tractable (fast-mixing), which enables learning even in models where inference would otherwise be intractable. This perspective opens up a new direction for tractable learning that could potentially be more expressive than tree-structured models.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with careful definitions of key concepts and thorough theoretical analysis. The authors effectively situate their work within the broader context of graphical models and maximum likelihood learning, clearly differentiating their approach from prior work. The example in Section 6, while simple, provides empirical validation that aligns with the theoretical predictions.

Strengths:
- Highly novel conceptual framework that rethinks tractable learning
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with clear complexity bounds
- Applicable to general exponential families, not just graphical models
- Clear differentiation from prior work (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.)
- Well-structured and clearly written

Weaknesses:
- The paper makes a strong assumption about the existence of a fast-mixing parameter set Θ, which may not always be easy to characterize
- Projection onto Θ is mentioned but not deeply analyzed for computational complexity
- The example provided is quite simple and doesn't fully demonstrate the approach's potential
- The claim of "no significant practical contribution" seems somewhat self-defeating for a NIPS paper

However, the paper's core idea is so fundamentally novel that these weaknesses are outweighed by its theoretical contribution. The authors have successfully identified a new direction for tractable learning that could potentially lead to more expressive models than traditional tree-structured approaches. This represents a significant theoretical advance that should stimulate future research on the relationship between mixing times and learning guarantees.

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in graphical models with a fresh perspective, providing rigorous theoretical guarantees where previous MCMC-based learning approaches lacked. While the practical implementation details could be explored further in future work, the theoretical foundation established here is substantial and represents a clear contribution to the field.

I believe this paper would be highly valuable to the NIPS community for its bold approach and theoretical insights. It exemplifies the kind of innovative thinking that advances the field beyond incremental improvements. The novelty and significance of the core idea far outweigh the minor weaknesses in the paper, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

Pro: Novel conceptual framework that rethinks tractable learning, comprehensive theoretical analysis with clear complexity bounds, applicable to general exponential families, well-written and clearly organized.

Con: Assumption about fast-mixing sets may be restrictive in practice, projection complexity not deeply analyzed, example too simple, self-critical claim about practical contribution.
