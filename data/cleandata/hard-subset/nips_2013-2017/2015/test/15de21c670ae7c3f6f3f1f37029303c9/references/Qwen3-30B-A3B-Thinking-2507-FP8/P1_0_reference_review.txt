

# Peer Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is intractable due to the #P-completeness of partition function evaluation. While existing methods like pseudolikelihood or score matching have been proposed, they suffer from inferior sample complexity, require well-specified models, and often yield distributions with intractable inference. The authors propose a novel approach that restricts the parameter space to "fast-mixing parameter sets" where MCMC sampling mixes rapidly. They prove that with gradient descent using MCMC-based gradient estimates, one can achieve a nearly optimal solution within the constraint set with high probability. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the total computational effort (number of Markov transitions) grows as (1/ϵ)^3 log(1/ϵ) for the convex case and (1/ϵ)^2 log(1/ϵ) for the strongly convex case.

The paper is framed in the general exponential family context, making the results applicable beyond graphical models. The authors provide rigorous theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, deriving precise complexity bounds on the number of gradient steps, samples, and Markov transitions needed to achieve a given optimization accuracy. They illustrate their approach with an example on the Ising model, showing the theoretical bounds are conservative but practical.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Rigor**: The paper provides comprehensive theoretical analysis with well-defined assumptions and rigorous proofs. The authors carefully handle the bias introduced by MCMC sampling and derive precise bounds on the number of samples and Markov transitions required. The analysis of both convex and strongly convex cases is thorough and well-structured.

2. **Novel Conceptual Contribution**: The "fast-mixing parameter set" concept is a significant theoretical contribution that reframes tractability in learning. Unlike previous approaches that restrict to low-treewidth graphs, this approach allows for more general models while guaranteeing efficient MCMC mixing. This is a fresh perspective on tractability that could influence future work on learning in graphical models.

3. **Clear Organization and Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with a clear progression from problem statement to background, results, and example. The notation is consistently defined, and the proof sketches provide valuable intuition without overwhelming detail. The example with the Ising model effectively bridges theory and practice.

4. **Practical Relevance**: While primarily theoretical, the paper provides concrete guidance for practitioners on setting algorithm parameters. The complexity bounds (Theorems 4-5 and 8-9) are explicit and could guide implementation of MCMC-based learning methods.

5. **Honest Assessment of Limitations**: The authors acknowledge the conservative nature of their theoretical bounds (as demonstrated in the example where the algorithm converged faster than predicted) and discuss important practical considerations like projection complexity and sample initialization.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical results to validate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. While the example with the Ising model provides a numerical illustration, it doesn't compare the method to existing approaches on real-world datasets, which would strengthen the significance claim.

2. **Practical Projection Complexity**: While the authors mention that projection onto the fast-mixing set is often negligible compared to sampling time, they don't provide concrete evidence of this for large-scale models. For complex models where projection might scale super-linearly, the theoretical guarantees could be less meaningful in practice.

3. **Limited Discussion of Parameter Set Selection**: The paper doesn't adequately address how to choose the fast-mixing parameter set Θ in practice beyond the Ising model example. For more complex models, determining an appropriate Θ that balances tractability with model expressiveness remains a significant practical challenge.

4. **Connection to Existing MCMC-Based Learning Methods**: The paper mentions contrastive divergence in the discussion but doesn't deeply explore how their theoretical framework relates to or improves upon existing practical MCMC-based learning methods. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the significance.

5. **Assumptions on Mixing Time Bounds**: The paper relies on geometric mixing bounds (equation 2), which may not hold for all practical MCMC samplers or model classes. The authors acknowledge this but don't explore how robust their results are to violations of this assumption.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis that well-supported by the proofs. The authors carefully address both the theoretical and practical challenges of MCMC-based learning. The paper is a complete theoretical contribution rather than a position paper. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach and the conservative nature of their bounds.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The organization is logical, the notation is consistent, and the proofs are well-explained with helpful proof sketches. The example with the Ising model provides concrete context for the theoretical results. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the theoretical results.

**Originality**: This paper presents a novel approach to tractable maximum likelihood learning by focusing on fast-mixing parameter sets rather than low-treewidth structures. While MCMC has been used for learning in graphical models before (e.g., Geyer and Thompson, 1992; Carreira-Perpiñán and Hinton, 2005), the theoretical guarantees provided here for gradient-based learning with MCMC sampling are new. The connection to exponential families rather than just graphical models broadens the applicability of the results.

**Significance**: The significance of this work is primarily theoretical, providing the first rigorous guarantees for MCMC-based gradient descent in maximum likelihood learning. While the paper doesn't claim practical novelty, the theoretical foundation could guide future practical implementations. The results are particularly significant for the learning community as they provide a theoretical basis for a widely used but poorly understood practical approach. The complexity bounds offer concrete guidance for practitioners on setting algorithm parameters.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of statistical learning with a well-motivated approach to tractable maximum likelihood learning. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides valuable theoretical guarantees for a common practical approach. While the lack of empirical results is a limitation, the primary contribution is theoretical, and the paper is well-suited for NIPS as a theoretical contribution.

The paper is significantly different from recent NIPS papers on related topics. While there are papers on fast-mixing Markov chains (e.g., Hayes, 2006; Dyer et al., 2009), this paper is the first to apply fast-mixing guarantees to maximum likelihood learning with theoretical convergence rates. The connection to exponential families rather than just graphical models also broadens the scope beyond recent NIPS work.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the fast-mixing parameter set Θ could be adapted for specific model classes beyond the Ising model example.

2. While the paper acknowledges the conservative nature of the bounds, including a short discussion of how the constants (C, α) might be estimated in practice would be helpful for practitioners.

3. The connection to contrastive divergence could be expanded to discuss how the theoretical results might inform practical implementations of this common learning method.

4. The example section would be strengthened by including a comparison of the actual number of iterations/samples required versus the theoretical bounds.

## Final Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically rigorous approach to maximum likelihood learning with practical implications. The main contribution is the formalization of "fast-mixing parameter sets" and the derivation of convergence guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates. The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning methods, which is a topic of considerable practical importance. The results are well-supported by careful analysis and provide valuable guidance for practitioners implementing these methods. I believe this paper would be of interest to a wide audience at NIPS and would advance the theoretical understanding of learning in graphical models and exponential families.
