Summary
This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families/undirected graphical models under an alternative notion of tractability: constraining parameters to a set Θ for which a chosen MCMC sampler is provably fast mixing. Within this setting, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated via finite-time MCMC. They provide high-probability convergence guarantees despite biased gradient estimates, for both (i) the convex (possibly unregularized) case and (ii) the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case. They derive explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps K, samples per step M, and Markov transitions per sample v that yield target objective/parameter accuracy with polynomial complexity, thereby constituting an FPRAS for MLE restricted to Θ (assuming one can project onto Θ and obtain uniform mixing bounds over Θ). They connect Θ to known fast-mixing sufficient conditions (e.g., bounded l∞ or spectral norms of pairwise couplings in Ising/MRFs) and show projection can be done efficiently in some cases. A small Ising example illustrates the procedure.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on: (i) classical MCMC-MLE and stochastic approximation (e.g., Geyer, Gu & Zhu), which typically lack rigorous runtime guarantees; (ii) fast-mixing sufficient conditions and mixing-time bounds for Gibbs/Swendsen-Wang (Hayes; Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Jerrum-Sinclair); (iii) projection to fast-mixing sets (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke); (iv) fast-mixing learning via restart chains (Steinhardt & Liang); and (v) inexact/proximal gradient convergence (Schmidt et al.). The novelty is in combining these to obtain end-to-end complexity and accuracy guarantees for MLE under mixing constraints, with explicit tradeoffs and near-matching lower bounds.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: if you can ensure and project to a fast-mixing parameter region, then MLE over arbitrary treewidth becomes tractable with provable accuracy and polynomial complexity.
- Technically careful treatment of biased, noisy gradients from MCMC via error decomposition (sampling error + bias due to finite mixing), using Efron–Stein/Bernstein in the convex case and multidimensional Hoeffding + union bounds in the strongly convex case.
- Explicit schedules for K, M, v with complexity that is near lower bounds proved in the paper; nice transparency about constants and their dependence on mixing parameters.
- General exponential-family framing (bounded sufficient statistics), not limited to a particular model.
- Good contextualization with fast-mixing parameter sets and projection methods, with concrete examples for Ising and pairwise MRFs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strength of assumptions: the existence of uniform mixing constants (C, α) across all θ ∈ Θ and a sampler is strong, and known worst-case bounds are very conservative. Practical utility depends heavily on how tight these bounds are; the example suggests conservatism.
- FPRAS claim relies on efficient projection onto Θ; for general models, projection (e.g., spectral-norm constraints with per-iteration SVDs) may be nontrivial and could dominate computation, yet this cost is not in the main complexity accounting.
- Practical sampling regimen: the analysis assumes independent samples from distributions after v transitions starting from an arbitrary r at each iteration; this is atypical (practitioners maintain chains/pools). The paper notes its analysis does not cover sample-pool reuse; this limits practical guidance.
- Limited empirical validation: only a toy example with hand-chosen parameters. No comparisons to pseudolikelihood, score matching, or CD in terms of fit vs. computational budget; no study of projection-induced bias versus unconstrained estimators.
- Some technical steps are summarized as “some effort can bound,” e.g., the Lipschitz result via a TV bound between exponential-family members as a function of parameter distance. While plausible with bounded sufficient statistics, readers would benefit from a more explicit, transparent argument (constants and norms).
- Scope of samplers: while Gibbs and some specialized chains are discussed, it’s less clear how broadly the projection-and-mixing framework applies to non-binary/multinomial models with nonuniform couplings, or to block Gibbs/tempered transitions, and how to choose Θ and mixing proofs there.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Good to very good. The theoretical statements are sensible, well connected to prior tools, and the analysis is careful about bias and concentration. Some derivations (e.g., Lipschitz via TV) are only sketched; full proofs in the appendix are crucial to verify constants. Empirical support is minimal, but the paper explicitly targets theory.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. The notation summary is helpful. Minor typos exist; improving the exposition of the Lipschitz proof and providing a short table mapping Θ choices to known projection algorithms and mixing rates would help.
- Originality: High. The combination of fast-mixing projections with an FPRAS-style learning guarantee and near-matching lower/upper complexity bounds appears novel relative to MCMC-MLE and prior fast-mixing learning work.
- Significance: Moderate. The result clarifies when MLE is tractable under mixing constraints and offers a sound alternative to treewidth-based tractability. Practical impact may be limited by restrictive Θ and conservative mixing bounds, but the framework should stimulate further work on learnable fast-mixing classes and tighter, data-dependent mixing analyses.

Questions and suggestions
- Please provide the full derivation and constants for the TV Lipschitz bound between pθ and pφ under bounded sufficient statistics, and discuss tightness.
- Can your analysis handle block Gibbs or other samplers (e.g., Swendsen–Wang) more directly, and how does that change Θ and projection?
- Give a more explicit complexity accounting for projection (per step) under the spectral-norm constraint; when does sampling cost dominate?
- Could you extend the analysis to cover sample-pool reuse or persistent chains (common in practice)?
- Add experiments comparing constrained MLE vs. pseudolikelihood/score matching/CD on moderate-size models to illustrate the computation–accuracy tradeoff.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and novel theoretical contribution with clean, general guarantees.
- Bridges MCMC mixing theory and optimization with explicit complexity tradeoffs.
- Near-tight schedules and lower bounds are informative.

Cons against acceptance
- Strong assumptions and conservative bounds limit immediate practical impact.
- Minimal empirical validation; practicality of projection and independent sampling not addressed experimentally.
- Some technical arguments only sketched in the main text.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept for a theory-focused venue like NeurIPS: the paper is technically interesting, well-situated in prior work, and advances our understanding of computation-limited MLE via mixing constraints. Strengthen clarity on key bounds and add at least modest empirical comparisons if possible.
