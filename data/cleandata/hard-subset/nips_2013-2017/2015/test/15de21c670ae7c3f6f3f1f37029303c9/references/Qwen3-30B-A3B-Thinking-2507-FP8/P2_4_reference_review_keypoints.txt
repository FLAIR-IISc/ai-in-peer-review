- Proposes restricting parameters to "fast-mixing" sets so MCMC sampling converges rapidly for maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models.
- Provides theoretical guarantees that gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients converges near-optimally with high probability given appropriate iterations, samples, and transitions.
- Extends the approach to general exponential families and supplies explicit computational complexity bounds for convex and strongly convex cases.
- Strong technical quality: proves Lipschitz gradient and gives rigorous convergence theorems (Theorems 1–9) with well-structured proof sketches.
- Derives bounds correctly from mixing-time assumptions and noisy-gradient analysis, and situates work relative to prior literature.
- Acknowledges limitations: assumes efficient projection onto parameter sets and notes that theoretical bounds are conservative.
- Clear organization and notation; main results are precisely stated and readable.
- Includes a concrete Ising-model example that illustrates theoretical bounds and makes results more tangible.
- Proof sketches provide useful intuition without excessive technical detail.
- Would benefit from more discussion of how to project onto fast-mixing parameter sets for general exponential families beyond the Ising example.
- Originality: introduces "fast-mixing parameter sets" as a new tractability notion and links fast mixing to efficient learning.
- Theoretical framework for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximations is a conceptual advance, including explicit computational complexity measures.
- Significance: gives the first theoretical guarantees for common MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning and shows polynomial dependence on 1/ε under conditions.
- Demonstrates improved complexity for strongly convex cases (linear in 1/ε_f versus cubic for convex).
- Weaknesses: limited empirical validation (single small 4×4 Ising example), projection complexity not thoroughly analyzed, and no practical performance comparisons.
- Recommendation: accept — strong, novel, and technically rigorous theoretical contribution despite limited empirical work.
