Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum likelihood (ML) learning for exponential families/undirected graphical models when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting graph structure (e.g., trees via Chow–Liu) or using surrogate objectives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood), the authors propose constraining parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ where a chosen MCMC kernel has a uniform geometric mixing rate: for all θ ∈ Θ and initial distributions q, ||Mθ^v q − pθ||TV ≤ C α^v. Assuming a projection oracle onto Θ, the paper analyzes projected (proximal) gradient descent where gradients are estimated by MCMC at each iterate from scratch, yielding biased, noisy gradients.

The main technical results are:
- A Lipschitz gradient bound for the (optionally ridge-regularized) negative log-likelihood with L = 4R^2 + λ, assuming bounded sufficient statistics ||t(x)||2 ≤ R, so smooth convex optimization tools apply.
- High-probability convergence guarantees for projected gradient descent with inexact (biased) gradient estimates, both in the convex (λ = 0) and strongly convex (λ > 0) regimes. The analysis combines concentration for Monte Carlo averages, mixing time bounds to control bias, and known results for inexact gradient descent (Schmidt et al., NIPS’11).
- Schedules for number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and MCMC steps v that achieve ε-accuracy with high probability, together with lower bounds. Total MCMC work scales as Θ(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)) in the convex case and Θ(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) in the strongly convex case, polynomial in other problem parameters and 1/(1−α).
- The authors interpret this as an FPRAS for ML restricted to Θ when projection is efficient and C, α are known. They provide examples of Θ using known sufficient conditions for fast mixing (e.g., bounded couplings or spectral-norm conditions for Ising/MRFs; Domke & Liu NIPS’13; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes).

Compared to prior learning methods for graphical models (Besag; Hyvärinen; Lindsay/Varin; Abbeel–Koller–Ng; Mizrahi et al.; Heinemann–Globerson) that avoid the ML objective or require special structure, this work targets the true ML problem but within a tractable, mixing-guaranteed subset. Compared to MCMC-ML (Geyer; Gu & Zhu; Salakhutdinov), it provides finite-sample, high-probability optimization guarantees under explicit mixing assumptions. It is related to Steinhardt & Liang (ICML’14), who enforce fast mixing via modified chains; here the chain is standard (e.g., Gibbs/Swendsen–Wang), and the constraint is on parameters. The analysis builds on inexact gradient/proximal methods (Schmidt et al.) and concentration inequalities (Boucheron et al.).

Strengths
- Clear, principled framing: restrict to parameter sets with provable rapid mixing and derive concrete, high-probability optimization guarantees for MCMC-based ML.
- Generality: results stated for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics; the argument is not tied to specific graph structures.
- Technically careful: separates convex vs. strongly convex cases; provides both upper and lower bounds and explicit parameter schedules; articulates dependence on mixing constants.
- Bridges two literatures (mixing time bounds and inexact gradient descent) to yield an FPRAS-style statement for ML in fast-mixing sets.
- Honest discussion of limitations (projection cost, initialization, persistent chains), and a small illustrative example.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practicality of Θ and projection: While projection is assumed available, efficient projection is only known for certain families/constraints (e.g., Ising with spectral-norm bound). The paper would benefit from a more systematic discussion of projection complexity vs. model size and a summary of which Θ are tractable beyond pairwise binary models (e.g., Potts, higher-order factors).
- Knowledge of C and α: Schedules require explicit mixing constants, which are often unknown and worst-case bounds can be very loose. A discussion of adaptive schemes (e.g., doubling v until a heuristic convergence diagnostic stabilizes) and how this impacts guarantees would strengthen the practical relevance.
- Dependence on dimension and data: The complexity is expressed in terms of R, L, and mixing parameters, but not explicitly in terms of the number of statistics d or dataset size D. Since R often scales as O(√d), making the d- and D-dependence explicit would clarify overall polynomiality in input size, an important aspect of an FPRAS claim.
- Assumption of independent fresh chains per iteration: The analysis excludes commonly used persistent chains/pools (e.g., persistent CD), which are often more efficient. Extending the theory to Markovian noise across iterations (or citing relevant results on Markov-dependent stochastic gradients) would broaden impact.
- Conservativeness of constants: The Lipschitz bound L = 4R^2 + λ seems loose (the Hessian equals Cov[t(X)], whose operator norm is ≤ E||t||^2 ≤ R^2); tightening constants would improve schedules. Also, some notation inconsistencies (R2 vs. R, Ra) could be cleaned up.
- Scope of “FPRAS” claim: Strictly, FPRAS typically refers to approximating a counting function (e.g., partition function). Here it denotes an algorithm that returns an ε-optimal ML solution in time polynomial in problem parameters. Clarifying terminology and the precise notion of approximation would avoid confusion.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The theoretical development is sound under stated assumptions and cites appropriate tools (mixing time theory, inexact gradients). Proofs are deferred; key lemmas are plausible. The main limitation is reliance on known mixing constants and projection oracles.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized with a helpful symbol table and proof sketches. Some notation typos and minor inconsistencies should be addressed. The example is illustrative but minimal.
- Originality: The combination of fast-mixing parameter sets, projection, and inexact gradient analysis to obtain an ML FPRAS appears novel, beyond prior works on tree-structured ML and fast-mixing learning with modified chains.
- Significance: Conceptually valuable: it identifies a tractable slice of high-treewidth models where ML is provably optimizable with approximate inference. The immediate practical impact is limited by projection and mixing-constant issues but could motivate further algorithmic work on constructing/learning within fast-mixing sets.

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides the first (to my knowledge) general high-probability optimization guarantees for ML over fast-mixing parameter sets with explicit complexity.
- Unifies mixing-time conditions and convex optimization to yield a clean framework applicable to exponential families.
- Clear articulation of schedules and lower bounds; contributes to theory of MCMC-ML.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical utility is limited without robust projection methods and accessible mixing constants; experiments are minimal.
- The FPRAS claim depends on parameters that may scale poorly with dimension and are not empirically validated.
- Excludes persistent-chain practice; constants may be conservative.

Recommendation
This is a solid theoretical contribution with clear novelty and careful analysis, squarely in scope for NeurIPS (graphical models, optimization, MCMC). If the program committee prioritizes theory papers with conceptual impact, I lean toward acceptance, provided the authors clarify the FPRAS terminology, tighten/clean notation, and expand discussion on projection complexity and dimension dependence.
