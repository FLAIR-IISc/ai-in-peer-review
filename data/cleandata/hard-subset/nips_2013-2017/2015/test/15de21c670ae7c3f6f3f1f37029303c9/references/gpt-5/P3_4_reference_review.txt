Summary and contributions
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a convex set Θ that guarantees fast mixing of a chosen MCMC kernel. Within this set, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where each gradient is approximated via MCMC sampling run for a finite number of steps v from an arbitrary initialization, with M samples per iteration and K iterations. They provide non-asymptotic high-probability convergence guarantees:

- Convex (λ = 0): suboptimality in objective at the averaged iterate scales as O( (K−1 (const + K/√M + Kα^v)^2) ), yielding a total Markov-transition complexity KMv of order 1/ε^3 up to logs.
- Strongly convex (λ > 0): error in parameter norm scales as geometric decay plus additive terms from sampling error and bias O(1/√M + α^v), leading to total complexity of order 1/ε^2 up to logs.

They prove the regularized objective has L-Lipschitz gradient under bounded sufficient statistics, provide schedules for (K,M,v) near-matching lower bounds (up to logs), and argue this yields an FPRAS for the MLE constrained to Θ. They relate Θ to known sufficient conditions for rapid mixing (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral-norm bounds for Ising/MRFs, or ferromagnetic subgraphs-world chains) and discuss how to project onto such Θ in certain cases (e.g., spectral norm projection for Ising).

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, CD, and other local or surrogate objectives, emphasizing that those methods can have worse sample complexity and may not converge to the M-projection under misspecification. It is close in spirit to MCMC-MLE (Geyer; Gu & Zhu) but provides explicit polynomial-time guarantees conditioned on fast mixing and projection. It connects to “projecting for fast mixing” (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke) by using those Θ as feasible sets, and to Steinhardt & Liang’s restart-chain fast-mixing learning by instead focusing on the original pθ restricted to a fast-mixing Θ. The use of inexact/proximal gradient convergence (Schmidt et al.) with biased MCMC gradients is standard, but here the bias is controlled via explicit mixing bounds, combined with concentration (Hoeffding/Efron–Stein/Bernstein) to get high-probability control.

Strengths
- Clear, general exponential-family formulation with bounded statistics; results are not limited to pairwise MRFs.
- Explicit, nonasymptotic schedules and high-probability guarantees; lower bounds that match the upper bounds up to logs and constants are valuable.
- The FPRAS framing for constrained MLE is conceptually clean and connects learning to mixing-time certified sets.
- Thoughtful discussion of practical variants (e.g., pool/persistent chains, CD connections), and how projection onto Θ leverages known sufficient conditions.
- The separation of convex vs strongly-convex analyses and the observation that strong convexity yields work scaling roughly linear in 1/εf (up to constants/logs) are insightful.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Assumptions and constants:
  - The guarantees depend on known constants (C, α) that quantify mixing uniformly over Θ and from arbitrary initializations. In practice, these are rarely known sharply; worst-case bounds can be extremely conservative, limiting practical usefulness of the prescribed schedules.
  - Verifying fast mixing or computing a tight Θ beyond special cases (Ising with Dobrushin/spectral norm, or ferromagnets) remains challenging. The generality of the exponential-family claims is thus practically constrained by availability of Θ and projections.
- Gradient Lipschitz bound:
  - Theorem 1 asserts ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ−φ∥2 and hence L = 4 R2^2 + λ. This is plausible with bounded sufficient statistics via Lipschitz continuity of the log-density and a TV bound, but the argument is only sketched. A precise, self-contained proof or reference is needed; alternatively, Pinsker’s inequality (TV ≤ √(KL/2)) and standard smoothness of A(θ) under bounded t could yield tighter/more standard constants.
  - Notation around R2 is confusing (R2 versus R^2). Since R2 may scale like √dim, L could be very large; a discussion of dimension dependence would help.
- Practicality/experiments:
  - Empirical validation is minimal (a single 4×4 Ising toy), with ad hoc choices (e.g., using L=10 instead of the theoretical 97) and some apparent typos in constants. There is no comparison to pseudolikelihood, CD, or restart-chain learning, nor a study of how schedules based on theoretical C, α compare to adaptive choices.
  - The independence assumption across samples per iteration (freshly initialized chains) is unrealistic in large models; the commonly used “persistent” or “sample pool” strategy is excluded by the proof technique. This gap is acknowledged but is a significant limitation to practical relevance.
- Scope of Θ:
  - For subgraphs-world/Swendsen–Wang-type guarantees (ferromagnetic Ising with unidirectional fields), the projection and learning within such Θ are discussed only briefly; convexity of these feasible sets and tractability of projection deserve more detail.
- Presentation issues:
  - Several references are incomplete (years, venues). Some equations and constants in the example are garbled. Providing an explicit algorithm box (Algorithm 1) and tightening notation would improve clarity.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a rigorous proof (or citation) of the TV bound used in Theorem 1 and discuss the dependence on dimension? Could you instead use standard exponential-family smoothness bounds on A (e.g., Lipschitz Hessian under bounded t)?
- How do you recommend obtaining usable C and α in practice? Could one design an adaptive scheme that increases v until a coupling/mixing diagnostic passes, while preserving guarantees?
- Can your analysis be extended to cover “persistent” chains by quantifying initialization bias via contractivity (e.g., Dobrushin) across iterations?
- Beyond Ising with spectral-norm or Dobrushin constraints, what classes admit efficient projection operators defining Θ?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and careful theoretical contribution with clear positioning relative to prior work on fast-mixing learning and MCMC-MLE.
- Near-optimal complexity bounds (up to logs) under explicit assumptions; generality across exponential families.
- Provides a useful conceptual framework (FPRAS for constrained MLE) that may influence future algorithm design and regularization toward fast mixing.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical impact is limited by the need for tight mixing constants and projection oracles; experiments are too weak to assess usability.
- Some key technical steps are only sketched; notation and presentation need polishing.

Overall
A strong theoretical paper that clarifies when and how ML learning with MCMC can be made provably polynomial via fast-mixing parameter sets, with near-tight schedules. I find the contribution valuable for the theory community at NeurIPS, but I encourage the authors to strengthen rigor in Theorem 1, improve clarity, and add more empirics or case studies translating mixing bounds into concrete schedules in realistic models.
