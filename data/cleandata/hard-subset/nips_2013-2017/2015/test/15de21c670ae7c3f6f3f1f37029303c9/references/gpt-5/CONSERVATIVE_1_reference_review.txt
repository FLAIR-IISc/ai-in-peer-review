Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a tractability notion based on fast mixing of an MCMC kernel. The key idea is to restrict parameters to a convex set Θ for which the Markov chain is guaranteed to mix geometrically in total variation at a known rate (C, α), and to project iterates onto Θ. The authors analyze projected gradient descent where each gradient is approximated via MCMC samples from the current parameter, deriving high-probability convergence rates for both the convex (λ = 0) and strongly convex (λ > 0) cases. They establish a Lipschitz bound on the gradient, provide schedules for the number of iterations K, samples M, and chain steps v, and present total work bounds: roughly O(εf^-3 log(1/εf)) for optimizing the (regularized) negative log-likelihood and O(εθ^-2 log(1/εθ)) to achieve parameter accuracy, assuming access to Θ and its projection. They frame this as an FPRAS for MLE within Θ. A small Ising grid example illustrates parameter choices but is not intended as a practical demonstration.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on classical intractability of exact MLE in graphical models and alternatives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, local learning). It connects mixing time literature (Gibbs, Dobrushin-type conditions, spectral norm bounds, Swendsen–Wang, subgraphs world) with optimization results for inexact gradients (Schmidt et al.). The projection-onto-fast-mixing-sets idea follows Domke & Liu/Liu & Domke for Ising/MRFs. It contrasts with Steinhardt & Liang’s approach (designing fast-mixing chains via restarts) and classic MCMC-MLE work (Geyer; Gu & Zhu). The positioning is largely accurate, though several assumptions are stronger than in prior learning analyses.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: optimization over fast-mixing parameter sets to make both learning and inference tractable.
- Sound use of convex analysis with inexact and biased gradient oracles, carefully separating convex vs. strongly convex cases and specifying explicit (K, M, v) schedules.
- Theoretical bounds are explicit and interpretable, tying optimization error to sampling steps and mixing rates.
- Projections onto Θ are concrete for certain Ising/MRF settings (elementwise bounds, spectral norm), with references to known algorithms.
- The discussion section candidly acknowledges limitations (projection cost, sample initialization, inability to analyze persistent chains).

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on strong, often impractical assumptions:
  - The method assumes known global mixing constants (C, α) for all θ ∈ Θ to set v and claim guarantees. In most nontrivial graphs, tight computable α is unavailable; existing bounds are loose and uniform assumptions rarely hold in heterogeneous parameters typical of learning from data.
  - Independence of MCMC samples across the M draws is assumed. This typically requires restarting many chains, which is highly inefficient; the common and more practical persistent-chain approach is excluded by the analysis.
  - Efficient projection onto Θ is assumed. While tractable in some Ising cases, in general exponential families (and even pairwise MRFs with arbitrary potentials) it can be nontrivial; the complexity of projection is not integrated into overall runtime guarantees.

- The “FPRAS” claim is not well-aligned with standard usage:
  - FPRAS typically refers to multiplicative approximation to a value (e.g., partition function) in time poly(size, 1/ε). Here, guarantees are additive in function gap or parameter distance, and the algorithm’s runtime depends on unobservable mixing parameters. Moreover, the optimum value is not approximated multiplicatively. The paper should either define a nonstandard FPRAS notion explicitly or avoid this terminology.

- Limited significance without empirical validation:
  - Only a toy 4×4 Ising example with D=5 is provided, using a hand-tuned smaller L than the theory suggests. There is no comparison to established baselines (pseudolikelihood, score matching, CD, variational EM), no assessment of the statistical price of the Θ restriction, and no demonstration on larger graphs.
  - The approach trades model capacity for mixing. Whether this yields better M-projections or practical performance is unsubstantiated.

- Missing statistical analysis in D:
  - The results treat the empirical t̄ as fixed and provide optimization error rates, but do not translate to estimation error as a function of sample size D. Without generalization/statistical rates, it is unclear how the fast-mixing restriction interacts with estimation accuracy under model misspecification.

- Technical clarity issues:
  - The Lipschitz gradient bound uses R2 and R2^2 (written as R22), which is confusing; please standardize notation and clarify constants (dimensional dependence).
  - The claim ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ − φ∥2 is strong; provide a precise statement and conditions (bounded sufficient statistics are assumed, but the dependence on dimension and tight constants matter).
  - The algorithm pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and the exact sampling setup for obtaining independent qk-samples are not fully specified in the main text.

- Scope and generality:
  - Beyond Ising-like pairwise models, constructive Θ and projections are unclear. The discussion of subgraphs-world mixing for ferromagnets is detached from the proposed learning algorithm (which assumes gradient estimation via expectation of t under pθ, not necessarily available from that chain in general exponential families).

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, rigorous optimization analysis that ties MCMC mixing assumptions to concrete convergence/work bounds.
- Bridges two literatures (mixing times and inexact gradient methods) in a way that could inform future algorithm design for tractable learning.
- Provides explicit schedules and lower bounds that may be pedagogically useful.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong, hard-to-satisfy assumptions (known mixing constants, independent samples, efficient projection).
- Questionable use of “FPRAS” terminology and limited novelty beyond assembling known tools for this setting.
- Minimal empirical validation; no comparisons to established baselines; unclear practical impact.
- No statistical (in D) guarantees; the benefit of restricting to Θ is not quantified empirically or theoretically in terms of estimation error.
- Clarity issues and some potentially loose inequalities that need careful proof.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper’s theoretical development is careful and interesting, but the strength of the assumptions, the lack of meaningful experiments or statistical analysis, and the overreach of the FPRAS claim limit its significance for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to (i) precisely define or drop the FPRAS claim, (ii) develop adaptive procedures that do not require known (C, α), (iii) extend the analysis to persistent chains or provide independence-preserving sampling schemes with tractable cost, (iv) integrate projection cost into complexity, (v) add substantial empirical comparisons, and (vi) clarify technical constants and notation.
