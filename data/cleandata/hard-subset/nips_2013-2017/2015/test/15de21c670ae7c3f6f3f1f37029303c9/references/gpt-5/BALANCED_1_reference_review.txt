Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Rather than restricting structure (e.g., Chow-Liu trees), it considers parameter sets Θ that guarantee fast mixing of an MCMC kernel Mθ. Within this set, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where the gradient is approximated by MCMC samples after v steps from an arbitrary initialization, with projection back onto Θ each iteration. They prove:

- A Lipschitz bound on the gradient of the regularized negative log-likelihood under bounded sufficient statistics.
- High-probability convergence rates for the convex (λ=0) and strongly convex (λ>0) cases, explicitly quantifying the roles of the number of iterations (K), number of samples per iteration (M), and mixing steps (v), as well as mixing-rate constants (C, α).
- Schedules for K, M, v achieving target accuracy with total Markov transitions KMv scaling as O(εf^−3 polylog(1/εf)) in the convex case and O(εθ^−2 polylog(1/εθ)) in the strongly convex case, along with lower bounds showing near-optimality up to constants/log factors.
- The claim that this yields an FPRAS for constrained MLE within Θ, assuming one can project onto Θ and has bounds on mixing.

The work relates to: MCMC-ML (Geyer; Gu & Zhu), fast-mixing parameter constraints and projections (Dobrushin/matrix-norm conditions; Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), learning fast-mixing models (Steinhardt & Liang), and alternatives like pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, and Chow-Liu. It distinguishes itself by tying fast-mixing constraints to guaranteed convergence of MLE optimization with explicit complexity schedules and near-matching lower bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: shifts from graph-structural tractability to mixing-time-based tractability; works in general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Technically solid high-level roadmap: uses standard concentration tools (Efron–Stein, Bernstein, Hoeffding-type bounds), mixing-time to TV bounds, and stability of projected gradient with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al.).
- Explicit complexity–accuracy tradeoffs: clean schedules for K, M, v; lower bounds that largely match the upper bounds.
- Proper connection to prior literature on mixing, projections, and learning under fast mixing; cites appropriate NeurIPS/AISTATS/ICML references.
- Potentially broad applicability where one can define Θ via known sufficient mixing conditions (e.g., matrix-norm constraints) and project efficiently.

Weaknesses
- FPRAS claim needs precision: classical FPRAS is a multiplicative approximation to an optimum with polynomial complexity in input size and 1/ε. The paper delivers additive objective (or parameter) accuracy; it should clarify the approximation notion, scaling with problem encoding length, and whether the guarantee is multiplicative in the likelihood gap.
- Dependence on mixing constants and projection: guarantees require known C, α and an efficient projection ΠΘ. For many models/Θ (e.g., subgraphs-world chains, beyond pairwise Ising/Potts with norm constraints), computing/upper-bounding C, α and performing exact projection may be nontrivial. Practical estimators of C, α are not discussed.
- Constants and dimensional dependence: bounds scale with R2 (norm bound on t), which in graphical models grows with the number of sufficient statistics (potentially large). The writeup should make explicit the dependence on number of variables, cliques, and alphabet sizes. The example uses a smaller-than-theory L to converge, hinting at conservativeness.
- MCMC usage is conservative: analysis assumes fresh chains from an arbitrary r and independent samples per iteration; does not cover persistent chains or sample pools that are commonly used for efficiency. This limits practical relevance; at least a discussion of how the analysis might extend would help.
- Experimental evidence is minimal (a toy Ising example) and largely illustrates conservativeness rather than stress-tests scaling or alternative Θ choices. For a paper arguing a computational tractability pathway, a few more empirical explorations (e.g., projection cost, sensitivity to Θ tightness, performance vs pseudolikelihood) would strengthen significance.
- Some proof details are deferred; key lemmas (e.g., ∥pθ−pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ−φ∥2 used for Lipschitzness) deserve either a precise citation or a complete derivation given their centrality.

Quality
- The techniques are standard but carefully assembled; the resulting bounds appear correct at a high level. However, the FPRAS terminology and the TV-to-parameter-difference bound require sharpening. Practical feasibility is constrained by the availability of Θ with efficient projection and usable mixing rates.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized; notation is heavy but mostly well-defined. The paper would benefit from explicitly stating the precise FPRAS notion, detailing dimension dependence in the final bounds, and adding a brief guide on how to set or upper-bound C, α in practice for popular Θ choices.

Originality
- Moderate to strong. While components exist in prior work (projecting to fast-mixing sets; inexact gradient descent; MCMC-ML), the combination to produce end-to-end optimization guarantees with schedules and lower bounds for MLE within fast-mixing parameter sets is, to my knowledge, new.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is solid for the community interested in learning under mixing-time constraints. Practical significance is limited by strong assumptions and conservative constants, but the framework could inform future algorithm design.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely define the approximation notion behind “FPRAS” (additive vs multiplicative in objective) and relate it to standard definitions; adjust terminology if needed.
- Make dimension dependence explicit in all main theorems (in terms of number of sufficient statistics and variable/cardinality).
- Provide guidance on constructing Θ and computing projections beyond Ising/spectral-norm, including complexity of projection and when it might dominate KMv.
- Discuss estimating or bounding C, α in practice, and sensitivity analyses if only loose bounds are available.
- Extend analysis to persistent chains or sample pools, or explain obstacles and potential avenues (e.g., coupling/mixing-from-stationarity arguments).
- Strengthen experiments: compare to pseudolikelihood/score matching on moderate-size models under different Θ tightness; report actual projection cost and empirical mixing.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, general, and rigorous framework tying fast mixing to provably efficient MLE optimization.
- Near-matching lower/upper bounds with explicit K/M/v schedules are valuable.
- Connects multiple strands of literature in a coherent way; likely to stimulate follow-up.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical applicability is limited; key assumptions (known C, α, efficient projection) are strong.
- FPRAS claim ambiguous; needs refinement.
- Empirical validation is minimal; lacks demonstration that the approach is useful beyond a toy setting.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and careful complexity schedules with lower bounds merit publication, provided the authors clarify the FPRAS claim, make dimensional dependencies explicit, and better discuss the feasibility of obtaining Θ, C, α and projections in practice.
