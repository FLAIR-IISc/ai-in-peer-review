

# Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is generally intractable due to the #P-completeness of partition function computation. The authors propose a novel approach where they restrict the parameter space to a "fast-mixing" set Θ, where MCMC sampling converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The key insight is that if Θ is such a set, then gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation converges to the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The paper provides theoretical guarantees showing that the total computational effort scales as (1/ϵ)^3 log(1/ϵ) for convex cases and (1/ϵ)^2 log(1/ϵ) for strongly convex cases, establishing a FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning within Θ.

The work builds on the theoretical foundation of MCMC mixing times and gradient descent with error, extending previous work on fast-mixing models (e.g., Liu & Domke, 2014) and MCMC-based learning (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang, 2015) by providing rigorous convergence guarantees for the maximum likelihood problem.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors establish the Lipschitz continuity of the gradient (Theorem 1) and provide rigorous convergence guarantees for both convex (Theorem 2-5) and strongly convex (Theorem 6-9) cases. The proof techniques combine standard optimization theory with MCMC mixing time analysis, and the arguments are logically sound. The theoretical bounds are carefully derived, with appropriate attention to the constants involved.

The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the conservatism in theoretical bounds (as evidenced in the example where the algorithm achieved tighter results than predicted) and the assumption that a fast-mixing set Θ is known. The example in Section 6 provides a concrete illustration of the theory, though the authors rightly note this is not a practical contribution.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is consistently defined, and the mathematical arguments flow logically. The proof sketches in the main text provide sufficient intuition for the key results, while the full proofs are relegated to the appendix as appropriate. The example in Section 6 is well-chosen and effectively demonstrates the theoretical framework in action. The presentation of the main results (Theorems 2-9) is clear and well-motivated, with careful attention to the trade-offs between different convergence parameters.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by introducing the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" as a tractable constraint for maximum likelihood learning. While MCMC-based learning has been used in practice, this is the first work to establish rigorous theoretical guarantees for this approach under the specific condition of fast mixing. This differs fundamentally from traditional tractability approaches that restrict to tree-structured models (Chow-Liu algorithm) or high-girth models (Heinemann & Globerson, 2014).

The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from related work, including pseudolikelihood (Besag, 1974), score matching (Hyvärinen, 2005), and composite likelihood (Lindsay, 1988). The theoretical framework provided is novel and general, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.

## Significance

This work has important theoretical significance for the field of graphical models and machine learning. It provides a new perspective on tractability for maximum likelihood learning, showing that fast mixing can be a sufficient condition for efficient learning. The results establish that maximum likelihood learning can be made polynomial-time under the fast-mixing assumption, which is a substantial theoretical advance.

While the paper is purely theoretical, it provides a framework that could guide future practical work. The results are particularly relevant for problems where the model is expected to mix quickly (e.g., Ising models with weak interactions), and the theoretical bounds could help in understanding when MCMC-based learning is feasible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Practical relevance**: The paper acknowledges it's not making a practical contribution, but this limitation isn't sufficiently emphasized. The theoretical bounds appear quite conservative (as seen in the example), which might make the results less compelling for practitioners. A discussion of when the fast-mixing assumption is likely to hold in real-world models would strengthen the paper.

2. **Projection complexity**: The paper states that projecting onto Θ is "trivial" for some cases (e.g., bounding Ising model parameters), but doesn't address the computational complexity of projection for more complex sets. For large models, projection might not be as simple as claimed.

3. **Lack of empirical comparison**: The paper makes no empirical comparisons to other methods (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching). While this is understandable for a theoretical paper, a brief comparison of the theoretical sample complexities would help contextualize the contribution.

4. **Mixing time assumptions**: The paper relies on the geometric mixing time assumption (Equation 2), which is strong but common in the literature. It would be helpful to discuss how this assumption compares to the mixing time bounds in existing graphical models literature.

## Conclusion

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing rigorous convergence guarantees for maximum likelihood learning with MCMC-based gradient estimation under the fast-mixing assumption. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important theoretical gap in the literature. While the practical relevance is limited (as acknowledged), the theoretical advances are substantial and could influence future research on tractable learning methods.

The paper is clearly original, making a novel contribution to the theoretical understanding of tractable maximum likelihood learning. The significance of this contribution for the field of graphical models is substantial, as it provides a new perspective on tractability that is fundamentally different from traditional approaches.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The paper is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, and it meets all four NIPS criteria with high marks.
