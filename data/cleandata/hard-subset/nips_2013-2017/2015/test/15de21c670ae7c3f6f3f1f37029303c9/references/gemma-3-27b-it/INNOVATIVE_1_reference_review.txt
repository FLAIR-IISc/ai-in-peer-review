This paper presents a fascinating and, to me, genuinely novel approach to maximum likelihood estimation in graphical models. Instead of striving for tractability via model structure (like Chow-Liu) or approximation (pseudolikelihood, score matching), the authors cleverly exploit the idea that a *fast-mixing* parameter set guarantees a fully polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning. This reframing is a significant conceptual leap and offers a new perspective on what constitutes a “tractable” model. The fact that this result extends beyond graphical models to the broader exponential family framework further strengthens its appeal.

The technical work appears sound, building on established results in MCMC convergence and optimization. The separation of analysis for ridge-regularized and unregularized cases is sensible, and the provided theorems, though dense, seem well-motivated and appropriately framed. The detailed discussion of mixing times and the linking to existing bounds (Ising model, etc.) provides crucial context. The inclusion of the detailed complexity analysis (Theorems 3-5) demonstrates a serious attempt to quantify the practical implications of the theoretical results.  While I appreciate the example provided, it feels slightly disconnected from the core theory and relies on a somewhat arbitrary choice of L.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the paper's strongest asset. The idea of leveraging fast-mixing Markov chains for FPRAS-style maximum likelihood learning is genuinely new and has the potential to open up new avenues of research.
*   **Generality:** The extension beyond graphical models to the broader exponential family is commendable and increases the impact of the work.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is heavily grounded in theoretical analysis, with clear statements of theorems and proof sketches.
*   **Detailed Complexity Analysis:** The inclusion of theorems quantifying the computational cost provides valuable insight into the practical feasibility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Implications are Unclear:** While the theory is solid, the practical benefits are less obvious. The constants involved in the complexity bounds are likely large, and the overhead of ensuring fast-mixing may outweigh the advantages in many realistic scenarios.
*   **Projection Complexity:** The analysis downplays the potential cost of projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ. While examples are provided, the complexity of this projection step can vary significantly depending on the specific model and may become a bottleneck.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The single example feels insufficient to demonstrate the practical performance of the method. A more thorough empirical evaluation on a wider range of models would be highly valuable.
*   **Density of Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and can be challenging to follow, especially for readers not deeply familiar with MCMC theory and optimization. More intuitive explanations and illustrative examples would improve clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and conceptually important idea.
*   The theoretical results are solid and well-motivated.
*   The work has the potential to inspire new research in tractable machine learning.
*   The generality of the approach is a significant strength.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical implications of the work are unclear.
*   The empirical validation is limited.
*   The paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and conceptual significance of the idea are compelling, and the theoretical work is rigorous. While the practical implications remain to be fully explored, the paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to stimulate significant future research.  I believe the theoretical insight outweighs the current lack of extensive empirical validation, and I am optimistic that further work will demonstrate the practical utility of this approach. Minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the empirical evaluation would be beneficial, but are not essential for acceptance.
