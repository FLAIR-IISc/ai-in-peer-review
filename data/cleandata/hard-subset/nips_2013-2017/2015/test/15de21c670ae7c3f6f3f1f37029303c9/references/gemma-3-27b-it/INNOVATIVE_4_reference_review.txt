This paper tackles a fundamental problem in graphical model learning: maximum likelihood estimation when exact computation is intractable. Instead of pursuing approximate methods that often sacrifice statistical efficiency or generalizability (like pseudolikelihood or score matching), the authors propose a fascinating and bold approach – restricting parameters to a set where MCMC converges quickly, thereby enabling a theoretically grounded, FPRAS-style algorithm. This is a genuinely novel direction. The core idea of trading off model flexibility for computational tractability via fast mixing is compelling and represents a significant conceptual shift.

The paper’s strength lies squarely in its theoretical contribution. Establishing a FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning within a constrained parameter space, leveraging fast mixing guarantees, is a valuable result. The proofs, while dense, appear rigorous and the connection to existing MCMC convergence theory is well-established. The analysis covering both convex and strongly convex cases provides a useful granularity. The exploration of the computational complexity—linking the desired accuracy to the number of MCMC transitions and gradient descent steps—is also a significant contribution.  The inclusion of example bounds for the Ising model adds concrete grounding.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. The most prominent is the limited empirical evaluation. While a small example is provided, it feels somewhat tokenistic. A more extensive set of experiments, perhaps on synthetic or real-world datasets, would substantially bolster the claims. Demonstrating the practical performance—specifically, the scaling behavior of the algorithm with model size and data dimensionality—is crucial.  The sensitivity of the performance to the choice of the 'fast-mixing' parameter set (e.g., the constraint on |θij| in the Ising model example) is also not thoroughly investigated.  How robust is the algorithm to violations of the fast mixing assumption? Is there a practical way to *verify* fast mixing for arbitrary parameter sets?

The discussion also glosses over potential challenges in applying this framework to more complex models. While the theoretical results are general, the actual *identification* of tractable parameter sets (those guaranteeing fast mixing) seems inherently model-specific and potentially difficult. The projection step, while mentioned, is only briefly discussed, and its computational cost could be a bottleneck in practice. The paper doesn’t adequately address the trade-off between the restrictiveness of the parameter set (and therefore potential model misspecification) and the computational gains.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets for tractable ML learning is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The FPRAS guarantee and the accompanying analysis are mathematically sound and build on established MCMC theory.
*   **Potential Significance:** If the practical challenges can be overcome, this work could open up new avenues for learning in complex graphical models where traditional methods are intractable.
*   **Generality:** The results are stated in a general exponential family framework, increasing their applicability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The lack of comprehensive experiments raises concerns about the practical viability of the proposed approach.
*   **Practical Challenges:** Identifying tractable parameter sets and the cost of projection are not adequately addressed.
*   **Sensitivity to Assumptions:** The dependence on the fast-mixing assumption and the robustness of the algorithm to its violation are not sufficiently explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of extensive empirical validation, the novelty and theoretical significance of this work are compelling. The paper introduces a fundamentally new perspective on tractable learning in graphical models. The weaknesses, while important, are not fatal. I believe the potential impact of this work justifies acceptance, *contingent on the authors addressing the concerns about empirical validation and practical considerations in a future version*. This paper represents a genuinely new idea, and I am willing to prioritize that over methodological perfection at this stage.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with encouragement to address the reviewer's concerns in a future version).
