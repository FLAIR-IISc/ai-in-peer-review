Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families when parameters are constrained to a “fast-mixing” set Θ, i.e., for all θ ∈ Θ a chosen MCMC kernel Mθ mixes geometrically fast with known constants C and α. The authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated via finite-length MCMC samples initialized from an arbitrary distribution. They prove convergence rates in two regimes: (i) convex (possibly unregularized) objective, providing bounds on suboptimality in objective value using sum-of-errors control via Efron–Stein/Bernstein, and (ii) strongly convex (ridge-regularized) objective, giving bounds on parameter error via per-iteration concentration (Hoeffding-type) and a union bound. They derive explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and Markov steps v to reach a target accuracy with high probability, and claim this yields an FPRAS for maximum-likelihood within Θ when projection onto Θ is efficient. They illustrate with a small Ising-grid example.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on (i) fast-mixing parameter sets for Ising/MRFs (e.g., Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum, Domke–Liu, Liu–Domke) and (ii) inexact gradient/proximal methods (Schmidt et al.), as well as classical MCMC-MLE (Geyer; Gu & Zhu) and alternatives to MLE (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood). Steinhardt–Liang’s fast-mixing-by-design chains are discussed as related motivation. The central novelty is to combine mixing-time guarantees for Θ with a finite-time convergence analysis of projected gradient descent using biased (MCMC) gradients and to turn this into a polynomial-time (in accuracy) learning guarantee.

Strengths
- Clear and useful high-level idea: restrict to Θ where mixing is guaranteed and project, so that both inference (sampling) and learning become tractable.
- Concrete convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, with explicit scheduling of K, M, v and total sampling complexity scaling polynomially in 1/ε and other parameters.
- The analysis framework is general to bounded-statistics exponential families, not just specific graphical models.
- Helpful survey-style background on mixing times and examples of Θ with known projections (e.g., spectral-norm constraints for Ising/MRFs).
- Connection to practical algorithms (CD-like schemes) is discussed, including limitations.

Weaknesses and concerns
- FPRAS claim is imprecise/overstated. In complexity theory, an FPRAS provides a multiplicative (relative) approximation with probability ≥ 3/4 in time polynomial in input size and 1/ε. Here the guarantees are additive (either in objective gap εf or parameter error εθ) and depend on model-dependent constants (C, α, R, L) that can scale with dimension. The paper should precisely define the approximation notion and avoid overloading FPRAS terminology unless a multiplicative approximation guarantee is proved and runtime is polynomial in the input encoding (including bounds on C, α).
- Algorithmic details missing. “Algorithm 1” is referenced but not provided; step-size policy is not explicitly stated (the analysis implicitly assumes 1/L). Assumptions on sample independence are unsatisfactory: the text first allows any initialization r, but the concentration proofs require independent samples from qk; practically, generating M iid samples per iteration from qk via v steps each either requires independent restarts or sufficient spacing—this should be specified and costed.
- Notation inconsistencies and mathematical gaps:
  - R2 is used for a norm bound with ∥t(x)∥2 ≤ R2, but later expressions use R22 (apparently (R2)^2), which is confusing. Choose a symbol (e.g., R) with clear exponents.
  - The bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ − φ∥2 is asserted without proof; this is nontrivial. A standard route would go via bounded-difference of log-densities and Dobrushin or Pinsker; please provide a rigorous derivation with constants.
  - The Lipschitz constant L = 4 R22 + λ depends on the previous unclear notation.
  - Several theorems have expressions with ambiguous parentheses and missing multiplications (e.g., “8R22 KL(... )^2”).
- Scope/generalization claims: While the framework “applies generally to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics,” the existence of a convex fast-mixing Θ with computable projection and known C, α is only demonstrated for certain Ising/MRF families. The paper should temper the generality claim or provide additional families and projection procedures.
- Complexity accounting omits projection cost. Although acknowledged in Discussion, the main claims (e.g., FPRAS) should carefully incorporate projection complexity; for spectral-norm projections the per-iteration SVD can dominate for large models.
- Dependence on mixing constants: Many bounds hide potentially large constants (C, 1/(1−α)), and α may approach 1 as N grows (e.g., single-site Gibbs on sparse Ising), inflating v and total work. A clearer translation from typical τ(ε) bounds (often O(N log N/(1−κ))) to the α,C parameterization and its impact on overall complexity would improve usefulness.
- Practical relevance is limited: only a toy 4×4 Ising example is shown, and even there L in practice is tuned far below the theoretical bound. No ablations on K/M/v schedules or comparisons to standard MCMC-MLE or CD variants.
- Prior art coverage: There is substantial literature on stochastic approximation for MCMC-based MLE with Markovian noise and bias control (e.g., Younes 1989; Atchadé et al.; Andrieu & Moulines), and on convergence of inexact gradient methods with Markovian sampling. These should be cited and contrasted, especially regarding bias due to finite v and reuse of chains (“persistent” strategies).
- Missing figures/algorithms: Figure 1 and 2 and Algorithm 1 are referenced but not included in the submission excerpt; this hampers reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. Please define precisely what you mean by FPRAS in this optimization context and whether your guarantees are additive or multiplicative. If additive, consider using “randomized FPTAS (additive)” or similar, and clarify runtime dependence on input size and on C, α.
2. What stepsize is used in the algorithm? Is it 1/L, and if so, how is L chosen in practice without being overly conservative?
3. How are M iid samples from qk obtained efficiently? Do you restart each chain from r for each sample or thin a long chain? Please account for correlation if not iid, or adjust the concentration analysis.
4. Can you provide the missing proof of ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ const · ∥θ − φ∥ and the exact constant?
5. Can your analysis be extended to “persistent” sampling (reusing a pool of chains), which is standard in practice?
6. How sensitive are K/M/v schedules to unknown or loose C, α? Can you adaptively tune v?

Minor comments and formatting
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies in references (missing years/venues/pages; misspellings like “Inferning”; incorrect author names). Please standardize to NeurIPS format.
- Notation: avoid R2/R22; use a single symbol R with clear exponents. Use consistent vector norms (ℓ2) and clarify whether ∥·∥ denotes Euclidean or operator norms in each context.
- Theorem statements contain typographical errors (missing multiplications, stray parentheses).
- In Section 6, the bound “D = √ × (2× .2)2” is garbled.
- Clearly present Algorithm 1 with all inputs (Θ, projection ΠΘ, stepsize, K, M, v), and include the figures referenced.

Pros
- Conceptually clean framework tying mixing-time constraints to learnability with polynomial guarantees.
- Provides finite-sample, finite-time bounds with explicit schedules for K/M/v.
- Bridges convex and strongly convex analyses.

Cons
- Overstated/unclear FPRAS claim; reliance on strong and sometimes unverifiable assumptions (known C, α; efficient projection).
- Missing algorithm/figures; many notation/formatting issues.
- Limited empirical validation; practical guidance incomplete.
- Some mathematical steps are asserted but not justified.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The central idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the paper in its current form has too many technical, clarity, and presentation issues, and the FPRAS claim needs to be carefully rephrased/justified. I encourage the authors to revise, tighten the theory (with full proofs and precise constants), include the missing algorithm/figures, temper generality claims, and expand experiments. Confidence: 0.8.
