Summary
The paper studies maximum likelihood learning in exponential families under a tractability notion based on fast mixing of an associated MCMC sampler. The core idea is to restrict parameters to a convex set Θ for which a uniform geometric mixing bound holds, and for which one can efficiently project. Within this set, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated using MCMC samples, and they provide high-probability convergence guarantees in both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) regimes. They derive Lipschitzness of the gradient, explicit schedules for number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain steps v, and total work bounds (KMv) that are polynomial in the desired accuracy and other problem parameters. They also give near-matching lower bounds for work, arguing near-optimality. The results imply a randomized polynomial-time approximation scheme for the constrained MLE (subject to remarks below on terminology). The work is related to and extends prior ideas on fast-mixing parameter sets and projections (e.g., Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), sampling-based MLE (Geyer; Gu & Zhu), and fast-mixing-by-design chains (Steinhardt & Liang), but focuses on end-to-end optimization guarantees for MLE under MCMC gradient bias.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level arguments are consistent with standard tools: concentration for Monte Carlo error, bias control via total variation mixing bounds, Lipschitz-smoothness of A(θ) from bounded sufficient statistics, and convergence results for projected gradient descent with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al.). The separation between convex and strongly convex cases and the explicit schedules are carefully reasoned. The assumption of a convex Θ with uniform (C, α) and efficient projection is explicit and crucial.
- Potential gaps/assumptions: (i) The bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R^2 ∥θ−φ∥2 is strong; while plausible under bounded t(x), it would help to reference a precise lemma or tighten constants (Pinsker-based arguments typically give dimension/curvature-dependent constants). (ii) The FPRAS claim is contingent on known uniform mixing constants and projection; obtaining or certifying (C, α) in practice is nontrivial and sometimes impossible; the algorithm’s complexity depends logarithmically on 1/(1−α), but correctness hinges on the validity of the bound. (iii) The analysis assumes independent sampling at each iteration (no persistence), which diverges from common practice; although the limitation is acknowledged, it affects practical relevance. (iv) Statistical error (generalization) is not treated; this is purely an optimization analysis. (v) The example uses an L smaller than the theoretical bound; as an illustration it is fine, but the guarantees require the conservative constants.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, with a clear setup, notation summary, and proof sketches. The linkage from mixing bounds to gradient bias and then to optimization guarantees is easy to follow. Some notational issues should be cleaned up: R, R2, and R22 appear; standardize to a single symbol for supx ∥t(x)∥2 and its square. Clarify the algorithmic template (Algorithm 1) inline since the figure is referenced but not provided here. The discussion section candidly addresses limitations.

Originality
The combination of (i) projecting into fast-mixing parameter sets, (ii) using MCMC to estimate gradients, and (iii) providing end-to-end, high-probability convergence and work complexity bounds, including near-matching lower bounds, appears novel. Prior work has studied projections for fast mixing and learning fast-mixing models, but did not, to my knowledge, provide a general optimization FPTAS-style guarantee for constrained MLE across exponential families with bounded statistics. The lower bounds and explicit schedules are a nice addition.

Significance
Conceptually, this work advances our understanding of when MCMC-based MLE can be made provably efficient by restricting the parameter space. It offers a formal justification for a popular heuristic (MCMC gradients) under clear assumptions and connects mixing time theory to optimization complexity. Practically, the utility is tempered by the strength of the assumptions: identifying a convex Θ with uniform mixing constants and efficiently projecting is only known in certain families (e.g., Ising with spectral/induced norm bounds or bounded edge weights). Still, the framework is likely to influence subsequent theoretical and algorithmic work, and provides a benchmark for analyzing variants (e.g., persistent chains, restart-based chains).

Specific comments and suggestions
- Terminology: For optimization problems, “FPTAS” is standard; “FPRAS” is customary for counting. Please clarify the approximation notion (additive versus multiplicative, objective versus parameters) and adjust terminology.
- Dependence on problem size: Make explicit the dependence of KMv on dimension N, dataset size D, and graph structure through R and L. The work bounds currently hide these via R and L; a short discussion for common models (Ising, Potts) would help.
- Obtaining (C, α): Discuss how to compute or bound mixing constants in practice, and implications when only loose upper bounds are known (impact on v and work).
- Projection cost: Provide complexity estimates (or references) for the projection in the cited Ising cases (spectral/induced norms), and clarify when projection is the computational bottleneck.
- Broader models: Comment on extending to continuous or unbounded t(x) settings (e.g., Gaussians), where bounded-statistics assumptions fail.
- Experimental illustration: Even a small comparison to pseudolikelihood or CD on the toy Ising model would situate the algorithm’s practical behavior and constants.

Pros
- Clear, principled framework linking mixing guarantees to optimization guarantees.
- High-probability convergence results with explicit near-optimal work bounds in both convex and strongly convex cases.
- General exponential family treatment with bounded statistics.
- Addresses a widely used practice (MCMC gradients) and clarifies when it is sound.

Cons
- Strong assumptions: known uniform mixing rates and efficient projection onto a convex fast-mixing set.
- Practical gap: independence across iterations and large constants limit immediate applicability.
- Some technical details (TV bound constants, FPRAS terminology) need tightening.
- Limited empirical validation.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a solid, novel theoretical contribution with clear assumptions, likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on graphical models, MCMC, and optimization.

Reviewer confidence
Moderate to high.
