## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, which is generally intractable. Instead of directly addressing intractability, the authors propose a tractable framework based on restricting parameters to a set where MCMC sampling mixes quickly. They demonstrate that if such a set exists and projection onto it is feasible, a Fast Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for the maximum likelihood solution within that set.  The analysis is broad, applying to the exponential family, and provides convergence guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation, both in the convex and strongly convex cases. The paper carefully analyzes the computational complexity required to achieve a desired accuracy.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The paper offers a fundamentally different perspective on tractable learning in graphical models.  Instead of focusing on specific tractable graph structures (like trees) or approximation techniques, it leverages the power of fast-mixing MCMC to define tractability. This is a significant conceptual shift.
* **Generality:** The results are stated in the broader context of exponential families, rather than being limited to graphical models, increasing their applicability.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous convergence guarantees for gradient descent with noisy gradients from MCMC.  The analysis is thorough, covering both convex and strongly convex scenarios, and carefully considers the impact of mixing rates.  The bounds on the computational cost (number of samples, MCMC transitions, gradient descent steps) are particularly valuable.
* **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of a discussion on projection onto the tractable set and an example application to the Ising model with concrete parameter choices demonstrates awareness of practical implementation challenges.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The notation is clearly defined, and the proofs (while not included in the main body) are sketched to provide intuition.
* **Addresses Weaknesses of Existing Approaches:** The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods like pseudolikelihood and score matching, justifying the need for a new approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Practical Impact (Acknowledged by Authors):** The paper readily admits that its primary contribution is theoretical. While the example is illustrative, the computational cost, even with the derived bounds, might be prohibitive for very large models. The advantage over existing approximation techniques is not fully quantified in practical terms.
* **Reliance on Fast-Mixing Guarantee:** The entire framework hinges on the ability to define a parameter set Θ where MCMC mixes rapidly and to efficiently project onto this set. While the paper discusses existing results for Ising models, the applicability to more complex graphical models might be limited. Identifying such sets for arbitrary models remains a challenge.
* **Projection Complexity:** The paper acknowledges that the complexity of projection isn't always trivial, but further discussion on this aspect, especially for more complex models, would be beneficial. The complexity of projection can potentially dominate the overall computational cost.
* **Conservative Bounds:** The example application indicates the theoretical bounds may be conservative. While conservatism is preferable to overestimation, understanding the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical performance would be valuable.



**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous (based on the sketches provided), and the authors are careful in their assumptions and derivations. It is a complete piece of work, providing a well-defined theoretical framework.  The acknowledgement of limitations is also a sign of carefulness.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and the proposed approach. However, the mathematical details could benefit from more in-line explanations.
* **Originality:** The approach of framing tractability in terms of MCMC mixing rates is novel and represents a significant departure from traditional methods. While the individual techniques used (gradient descent, MCMC, FPRAS) are not new, their combination and the theoretical framework are original.
* **Significance:** The results are important in a theoretical sense, offering a new way to think about tractable learning in graphical models. However, the practical impact remains to be seen. The development of efficient projection algorithms and the identification of fast-mixing parameter sets for a wider range of models are crucial for realizing the full potential of this work.



**Arguments Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides a novel and theoretically well-grounded framework for tractable learning.
* Offers general results applicable to exponential families.
* Carefully analyzes the computational complexity of the proposed approach.
* Addresses a fundamental problem in graphical model learning.

**Con:**

* Limited immediate practical impact due to potential computational cost.
* Heavily relies on the existence of fast-mixing parameter sets, which may be difficult to identify for complex models.
* Projection complexity can be significant.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper, despite the limitations, because of its significant theoretical contribution and originality. The paper introduces a novel and potentially powerful framework for tractable learning.  I suggest the authors address the following in a revision:

* **Expand the discussion on projection complexity:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of projection for different types of graphical models.
* **Discuss potential strategies for finding fast-mixing parameter sets:**  Outline techniques for identifying suitable sets Θ in practice.
* **Further investigate the gap between theoretical bounds and practical performance:**  Provide more empirical results or a more detailed analysis of the conservatism in the bounds.
