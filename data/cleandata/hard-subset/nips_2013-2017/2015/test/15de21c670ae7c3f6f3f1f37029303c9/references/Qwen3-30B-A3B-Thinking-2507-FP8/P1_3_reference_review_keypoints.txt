- Problem: Maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models is generally intractable due to the #P-completeness of partition function computation.  
- Prior alternatives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, Chow-Liu) are tractable but have inferior sample complexity and may yield intractable inference.  
- Proposal: restrict parameters to a "fast-mixing parameter set" Θ where MCMC mixes rapidly for all θ ∈ Θ.  
- Method: perform maximum likelihood within Θ using gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates.  
- Practical algorithmic outputs: explicit schedules for gradient steps (K), samples per gradient (M), and chain transitions (v) to reach desired accuracy.  
- Theoretical guarantees: prove Lipschitz continuity of the gradient and convergence bounds for both convex and strongly convex cases.  
- Analysis accounts for both sampling statistical error and MCMC mixing bias, improving over heuristic approaches.  
- Generality: framed in the exponential family, so results extend beyond just graphical models.  
- Novelty: introduces the fast-mixing parameter set concept and connects Markov chain mixing properties to ML learning guarantees.  
- Significance: provides principled computational-complexity bounds linking resources (K, M, v) to optimization accuracy in MCMC-based learning.  
- Quality: rigorous, well-structured proofs with clear intuition and sound mathematical treatment.  
- Clarity: paper is well-organized with clear notation and helpful examples, though some theorem connections could be made more explicit.  
- Scope limitation: mainly targeted at graphical models; unclear how broadly Θ can be efficiently defined and projected onto.  
- Practical limitation: assumes knowledge of mixing-rate parameters (C, α) and lacks concrete solutions for initialization and projection in practice.  
- Empirical gap: no empirical validation to demonstrate practicality or applicability to real models.  
- Related-work gap: could better contrast with more recent MCMC-based learning work (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang 2014; Tieleman 2008).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framework, rigorous complexity bounds, significant insight into computation–accuracy trade-offs, and strong technical presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of empirical validation, questionable practical applicability of fast-mixing Θ for many models, and unrealistic assumption of known mixing rates.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — strong theoretical contribution with minor practical/empirical shortcomings.  
- Specific comments: clarify Theorem 6’s relation to standard strongly convex results; expand discussion of projection complexity scaling; include empirical/theoretical comparison in Section 6 to standard MCMC learning; explicitly compare to Steinhardt & Liang (2014).
