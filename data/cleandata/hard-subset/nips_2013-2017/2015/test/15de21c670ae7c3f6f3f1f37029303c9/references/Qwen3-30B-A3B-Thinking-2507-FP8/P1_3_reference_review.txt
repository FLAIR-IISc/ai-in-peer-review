

# Peer Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is generally intractable due to the #P-completeness of partition function computation. While previous approaches (pseudolikelihood, score matching, Chow-Liu) provide tractable alternatives, they suffer from inferior sample complexity, lack of guarantees under model misspecification, and often yield distributions where inference remains intractable.

The authors propose a novel approach that restricts the parameter space to a "fast-mixing parameter set" Θ, where MCMC sampling mixes rapidly for all θ ∈ Θ. Their key insight is that if Θ has this property, then maximum likelihood learning within Θ can be efficiently performed using gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates. The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, deriving explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps (K), samples (M), and Markov chain transitions (v) to achieve desired optimization accuracy with high probability. The analysis is framed in the exponential family context, making it more general than just graphical models.

This work builds on prior research on fast-mixing Markov chains (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Liu & Domke, 2014) and gradient descent with approximate gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011), but makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting these concepts to maximum likelihood learning with formal guarantees.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with well-structured proofs. The authors carefully establish the Lipschitz continuity of the gradient (Theorem 1), and derive convergence guarantees for both convex (Theorem 2) and strongly convex (Theorem 6) cases. The proof sketches provide sufficient intuition while maintaining mathematical rigor. The analysis properly accounts for both the statistical error from approximate sampling and the bias from MCMC mixing time, which is a significant improvement over previous heuristic approaches.

**Originality (Strong):** The concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" is novel and represents a fundamental shift from traditional approaches that restrict to low-treewidth models. The paper establishes a new theoretical framework connecting Markov chain mixing properties to maximum likelihood learning guarantees, which is distinct from previous work on graphical models. The analysis of the trade-offs between computational resources (K, M, v) and optimization accuracy is particularly original.

**Significance (High):** The paper addresses a core challenge in statistical learning with wide applicability. The theoretical guarantees provide a principled way to understand the computational complexity of maximum likelihood learning with MCMC, which has been widely used in practice but lacked formal guarantees. The results have the potential to guide future algorithm design for learning in complex exponential families.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized with clear notation and logical flow. The main results are presented with intuitive explanations and proof sketches. The example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the practical implications of the theory. Some sections could benefit from more explicit connections between theorems (e.g., how Theorem 4 relates to the lower bounds in Theorem 3).

## Weaknesses

**Scope (Moderate):** While the paper is framed in the exponential family context, the primary application is to graphical models. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could more explicitly discuss the range of models where fast-mixing parameter sets Θ can be efficiently defined and projected onto.

**Practical Considerations (Moderate):** The paper focuses on theoretical guarantees but doesn't address practical implementation challenges. For instance, the analysis assumes that the mixing rate parameters (C, α) are known, which is often not the case in practice. The discussion of initialization (Section 7) identifies this as a limitation but doesn't propose solutions.

**Comparison to Related Work (Moderate):** The paper cites relevant work on mixing times (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Liu & Domke, 2014) but could better contrast its approach with more recent work on learning with MCMC (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang, 2014; Tieleman, 2008), which also uses MCMC for gradient estimation.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Novel theoretical framework connecting fast-mixing Markov chains to maximum likelihood learning guarantees
- Rigorous analysis with explicit computational complexity bounds
- Significant contribution to understanding the trade-offs between computational resources and optimization accuracy
- Well-structured and clearly written paper with strong theoretical foundations
- Addresses a fundamental problem in graphical models with broad relevance

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- The paper makes strong theoretical claims but doesn't provide empirical validation of the proposed algorithm
- The "fast-mixing parameter set" concept may not be practically applicable to many interesting models
- The analysis assumes knowledge of mixing rate parameters (C, α) which is often not available in practice

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of maximum likelihood learning with MCMC sampling. The core idea of "fast-mixing parameter sets" is novel and well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous and comprehensive. While the paper doesn't claim practical contributions, the theoretical framework provides valuable insights for future work on learning in exponential families.

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical learning with important theoretical implications. The quality of the analysis is excellent, and the results represent a meaningful advancement over previous heuristic approaches to MCMC-based learning. The minor weaknesses regarding practical applicability and empirical validation don't detract from the strong theoretical contribution.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of maximum likelihood learning with MCMC, which aligns with NIPS's focus on fundamental advances in machine learning theory.

## Specific Comments

1. In Section 4.3, the authors could better clarify the relationship between Theorem 6 and the standard strongly convex convergence results (e.g., the dependence on λ).

2. The discussion of projection complexity (Section 7) is valuable but could be expanded to discuss how the projection complexity scales with model size for different choices of Θ.

3. The example in Section 6 is helpful but could include a comparison to standard MCMC-based learning methods to better illustrate the theoretical bounds.

4. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to the recent work by Steinhardt & Liang (2014) on learning with fast-mixing chains.

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the computational complexity of maximum likelihood learning. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field.
