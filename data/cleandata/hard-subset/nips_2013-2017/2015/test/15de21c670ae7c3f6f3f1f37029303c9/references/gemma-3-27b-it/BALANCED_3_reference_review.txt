This paper investigates the theoretical guarantees for maximum likelihood learning in constrained parameter spaces designed for fast mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. The core idea is that by restricting the parameter space to ensure rapid convergence of MCMC, one can establish a First-Order Primal Approximation (FPRAS) scheme for maximum likelihood estimation within that space. The authors focus on exponential families and provide convergence rates for gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients, distinguishing between strongly convex and merely convex cases.

The paper builds upon established literature concerning the intractability of maximum likelihood estimation in graphical models (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993) and alternative estimation techniques like pseudolikelihood and score matching. It positions itself as a method to overcome the limitations of these alternatives, particularly their potential for poor sample complexity and lack of guarantees when the model is misspecified.  The connection to tractable families like tree-structured distributions (Chow-Liu, 1968) and high-girth models (Heinemann & Globerson, 2014) is well-articulated.  The novelty lies in framing tractability not through specific structures (trees, high girth) but through the guarantee of fast MCMC mixing.  The authors properly situate their work within the broader context of using MCMC for gradient estimation (e.g., Tieleman, 2012; Steinhardt & Liang, 2016) and acknowledge the lack of existing theoretical guarantees for such approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the convergence properties of gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients, establishing FPRAS guarantees under the assumption of fast mixing.
* **Generality:** The results are presented in a general exponential family framework, extending beyond graphical models, a noteworthy contribution.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The motivation for focusing on fast-mixing parameter sets is well-explained and clearly connects to practical challenges in maximum likelihood estimation.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The authors provide separate analyses for strongly convex and convex cases, offering nuanced results.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:**  The paper demonstrates a good understanding of the relevant literature, including connections to graphical models, MCMC sampling, and optimization theory. The breadth of citations is commendable.
* **Illustrative Example:**  The included example provides a concrete instantiation of the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Relevance:** While theoretically sound, the practical implications are less clear. The fast-mixing assumption can be difficult to verify in complex models, and the projection step onto the constrained parameter space may itself be computationally expensive.  The paper acknowledges this, but further discussion on practical considerations would be beneficial.
* **Conservatism in Bounds:** The example indicates that the theoretical bounds might be conservative, potentially overestimating the required sample complexity. Further investigation into tightening these bounds could improve the practical utility of the results.
* **Limited Discussion of Projection Complexity:** The computational complexity of projecting onto the constraint set Θ is only briefly mentioned.  The paper references Domke and Liu (2013), but doesn't fully explore the implications of projection complexity for different types of models and constraint sets.  The choice of projection algorithm could heavily influence overall performance.
* **Assumptions on Lipschitz Constant:** The derivation of the Lipschitz constant (Theorem 1) relies on an assumption of a bound (R2) on the sufficient statistics. While reasonable, the impact of inaccurate estimation of this bound is not discussed.
* **Notationally Dense:** The paper is quite dense with notation, which can make it challenging to follow the proofs. Although a list of symbols is provided, the constant use of subscripts and symbols requires careful attention.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to maximum likelihood learning in constrained parameter spaces.
* The FPRAS guarantees are a significant contribution to the field.
* The generality of the results, extending beyond graphical models, is a strength.
* The paper is well-written, rigorously analyzed, and thoroughly referenced.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The practical relevance of the results is uncertain, and the assumptions required for fast mixing may be difficult to satisfy in real-world applications.
* The potential conservatism of the bounds and the lack of detailed discussion on projection complexity limit the immediate practical impact of the work.
* The notationally dense presentation may hinder accessibility for readers unfamiliar with the specific technical details.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of maximum likelihood learning in constrained parameter spaces.  However, its practical relevance is somewhat limited by the challenges of verifying the fast-mixing assumption and the potential computational cost of projection.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit.  The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by:

1. **Expanding the discussion of practical considerations:** Discuss strategies for verifying the fast-mixing assumption in different models. Analyze the computational complexity of projection for various constraint sets and explore alternative projection algorithms.
2. **Investigating tighter bounds:**  Explore techniques for tightening the theoretical bounds on sample complexity.
3. **Providing more detailed analysis of projection:**  Dedicate a section to a more thorough discussion of projection methods and their computational cost.
4. **Considering alternative initializations:** Investigate the use of contrastive divergence or other initialization strategies and their impact on convergence.
5. **Improving readability:** Consider streamlining the notation and providing more intuitive explanations of the key concepts.
