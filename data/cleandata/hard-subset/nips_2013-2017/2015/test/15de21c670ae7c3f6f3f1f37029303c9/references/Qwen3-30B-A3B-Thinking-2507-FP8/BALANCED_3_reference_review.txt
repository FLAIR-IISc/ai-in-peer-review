

# Review for "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary
This paper proposes a theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families constrained to "fast-mixing" parameter sets, where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. The authors establish that for such constrained sets, there exists a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, showing how to set the number of gradient descent iterations K, samples M, and Markov chain transitions v to achieve desired approximation accuracy with high probability.

The work builds on existing literature about mixing times (e.g., Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Liu & Domke, 2014) and gradient descent with noisy gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011), but makes a novel contribution by connecting these concepts to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. The paper distinguishes itself from previous approaches that either assume well-specified models (pseudolikelihood, score matching) or constrain model structure (tree-structured models).

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed. The paper successfully establishes the Lipschitz property of the regularized log-likelihood gradient and provides clear convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases.

2. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear notation, well-structured arguments, and helpful proof sketches. The relationship between mixing times, parameter constraints, and learning guarantees is presented logically.

3. **Novelty**: The core idea of constraining parameters to ensure fast-mixing MCMC for maximum likelihood learning represents a meaningful conceptual advance beyond previous work on tractable graphical models. The exponential family framework makes the results broadly applicable.

4. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The paper provides both lower bounds and explicit schedules for the computational parameters, showing that the proposed approach is nearly optimal.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper explicitly states "This paper claims no significant practical contribution" and provides only a single illustrative example without actual experiments. NeurIPS typically values papers that demonstrate both theoretical and practical impact. Without empirical results comparing the proposed method to existing approaches, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the theoretical results.

2. **Limited Practical Discussion**: The paper doesn't address how the projection step (onto the fast-mixing set) scales with model size, which could be a bottleneck for large-scale problems. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't explore it.

3. **Comparison to Existing Methods**: The paper doesn't compare the proposed approach to existing MCMC-based learning methods (e.g., contrastive divergence, tempered transitions) in terms of computational efficiency or quality of the learned model. This makes it hard to evaluate the relative advantages of the proposed method.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't discuss how to practically implement the algorithm, such as how to handle the initialization of Markov chains or how to adapt the method to different model types beyond the Ising model example.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The theoretical contribution is strong and well-supported, but the lack of empirical validation is a significant weakness for a NeurIPS submission. The paper is technically sound but doesn't fully demonstrate the completeness of the work.

**Clarity**: Excellent - the paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

**Originality**: The work presents a novel theoretical perspective on maximum likelihood learning with a clear distinction from previous approaches. It's sufficiently different from recent NIPS papers on MCMC-based learning.

**Significance**: The theoretical contribution is significant for understanding the computational complexity of learning with MCMC, but the lack of empirical results and practical demonstration significantly limits its perceived significance for the field.

## Recommendation

The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of maximum likelihood learning with MCMC. However, the absence of empirical validation and practical demonstration is a major drawback for a NeurIPS submission, which typically expects both theoretical and practical contributions. The paper would be significantly stronger with empirical results comparing the proposed method to existing approaches.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add empirical results**: Include experiments on small-scale problems demonstrating the practical feasibility of the approach, comparing with existing methods like contrastive divergence.

2. **Discuss computational scaling**: Analyze how the projection step scales with model size, and provide concrete examples of how the method could be applied to larger models.

3. **Compare with existing methods**: Include a comparison of the computational efficiency and quality of learned models against existing approaches for maximum likelihood learning.

4. **Address practical implementation**: Discuss how to handle Markov chain initialization, how to adapt the method to different model types, and how to tune the parameters in practice.

5. **Reconsider the "no practical contribution" statement**: The paper's theoretical contribution is valuable, but it would be strengthened by demonstrating how it could inform practical algorithm design.

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the lack of empirical validation and practical discussion makes this paper less suitable for NeurIPS as it currently stands. With additional empirical results and practical discussion, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
