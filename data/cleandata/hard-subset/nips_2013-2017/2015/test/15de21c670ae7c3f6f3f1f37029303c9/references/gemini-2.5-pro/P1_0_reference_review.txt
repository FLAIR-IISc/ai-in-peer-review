### Paper Review

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood (ML) learning in general undirected graphical models. While many alternative estimators exist (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching), they often lack the desirable M-projection property of ML and can result in a learned model where inference is still intractable. The authors propose a novel and principled approach: constraining the model parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is provably fast-mixing. The core contribution is a theoretical analysis showing that projected gradient descent, using MCMC to estimate the intractable gradient, provides a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the ML solution within this constrained set `Θ`.

The key technical challenge addressed is that MCMC with a finite number of steps yields a biased, not just noisy, estimate of the gradient. The authors provide a rigorous analysis that bounds this error. They derive explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples per step (M), and MCMC transitions per sample (v) required to achieve a desired accuracy `ε` with high probability. The analysis is presented for two cases: the general convex (unregularized) case, which converges in function value at a rate of roughly `O(1/ε_f^3)`, and the strongly convex (L2-regularized) case, which converges in parameter distance at a rate of roughly `O(1/ε_θ^2)`. The work connects the vast literature on MCMC mixing times with the practical problem of learning graphical models, providing the first formal non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for this widely-used class of MCMC-based learning algorithms.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs that build upon established results in optimization (specifically, convergence of gradient descent with inexact gradients) and probability (concentration inequalities). The analysis carefully accounts for the two sources of error in the gradient estimate: the Monte Carlo sampling error and the bias from incomplete MCMC mixing. The authors are also honest about the limitations of their work, discussing the ignored complexity of the projection step and the inability of the current analysis to cover common heuristics like contrastive divergence or persistent chains. This is a complete and sound piece of theoretical work.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem setup, notation, and assumptions are clearly defined in Section 2. The main results in Section 4 are presented logically, with theorems preceded by intuitive explanations and followed by insightful proof sketches that convey the core ideas. The inclusion of lower bounds on computational work (Theorems 3 and 7) alongside achievable upper bounds (Theorems 4 and 8) provides a complete picture of the complexity. The small example in Section 6, while simple, effectively illustrates the concepts and acknowledges the conservative nature of the theoretical bounds in practice.

**Originality:** The central idea of defining a tractable learning problem by constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing set" is highly original. While MCMC-based ML estimation is a classic technique, it has largely been treated as a heuristic lacking formal guarantees. This paper provides the first rigorous, non-asymptotic convergence analysis for this setting. It cleverly reframes the notion of a "tractable model class" away from purely structural properties like treewidth towards a dynamic property related to inference. The synthesis of MCMC mixing time theory with the analysis of inexact proximal-gradient methods is novel and insightful. The paper correctly positions itself with respect to related work on approximate learning methods and MCMC theory.

**Significance:** The paper's primary significance is theoretical. It provides a foundational "proof-of-concept" that MCMC-based learning can be made provably efficient for certain model classes. This is a substantial contribution that elegantly unifies the goals of tractable learning and tractable inference: by learning within a fast-mixing set, the resulting model is guaranteed to be amenable to efficient MCMC-based inference. This could inspire future research in two key directions: (1) identifying and characterizing larger and more expressive fast-mixing parameter sets for various model families, and (2) developing tighter analyses or new algorithms that close the gap between the conservative theoretical bounds and practical performance.

However, the immediate practical impact is limited. The main weakness is the reliance on a strong assumption: one must know a convex, fast-mixing set `Θ` and have an efficient algorithm to project onto it. While the paper provides examples for Ising models, these known sets are often quite restrictive (e.g., requiring very weak potentials), and finding such sets for more general models is a major research challenge in itself. Furthermore, as the authors' own example suggests, the derived schedules for `K`, `M`, and `v` are likely too conservative for practical application, leading to computationally prohibitive runtimes.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** It introduces a new and important way to think about tractable learning, linking it directly to the tractability of inference via MCMC mixing times.
2.  **First Rigorous Guarantees:** It provides the first non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for the widely used but poorly understood method of ML learning with MCMC-based gradients, correctly handling the inherent bias.
3.  **High-Quality Theory:** The analysis is technically sound, thorough (covering both convex and strongly convex cases), and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Strong Assumptions:** The applicability of the method hinges on knowing a fast-mixing set `Θ` and an efficient projection operator, which may not be available for many models of practical interest.
2.  **Limited Practicality of Bounds:** The derived computational complexity bounds are likely too loose to guide practical algorithm design, as suggested by the example.
3.  **Minimal Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are limited to a single toy example, which serves only to illustrate the theory rather than demonstrate its utility on a realistic problem.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the limited immediate practical applicability, this is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the foundations of learning in graphical models. It formalizes a popular heuristic and provides a novel conceptual framework that connects learning and inference tractability. The quality of the analysis and the clarity of the presentation are very high. This work is likely to be influential and to spur further research in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
