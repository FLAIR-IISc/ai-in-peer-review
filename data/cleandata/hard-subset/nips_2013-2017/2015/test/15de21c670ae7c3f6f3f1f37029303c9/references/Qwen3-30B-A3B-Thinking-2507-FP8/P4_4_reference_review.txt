

# Review: Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical model learning: maximum likelihood estimation is generally intractable due to the #P-completeness of partition function computation. The authors propose a novel approach by restricting parameter space to "fast-mixing" sets where MCMC sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. This differs from previous tractability approaches (e.g., treewidth constraints) and provides theoretical guarantees for maximum likelihood learning using gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it establishes that the regularized log-likelihood has a Lipschitz gradient. Second, for the convex case (unregularized), it provides a convergence guarantee showing that the algorithm achieves ϵf-optimality with total computational effort O((1/ϵf)³ log(1/ϵf)). Third, for the strongly convex case (regularized), it shows that ϵθ-optimality can be achieved with effort O((1/ϵθ)²). The authors derive explicit schedules for setting gradient descent steps (K), samples (M), and Markov chain transitions (v) to achieve these guarantees. The theoretical results are supported by a practical example on an Ising model.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for a widely used practical approach (MCMC-based gradient descent). The proofs are well-structured, with clear proof sketches for key results and appropriate citations to relevant literature (e.g., Schmidt et al. [26] for gradient descent with errors). The authors properly address the limitations of their approach, including the computational cost of projection and initialization of MCMC chains.

2. **Novelty:** The paper introduces a fundamentally different tractability criterion (fast-mixing parameter sets) compared to the traditional treewidth-based approach. This is a significant conceptual advance that extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families. The connection between mixing times and computational complexity of maximum likelihood learning is novel and well-motivated.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Key notation is consistently defined, and the main results are presented with precise theorems. The proof sketches provide sufficient intuition for the main technical contributions. The example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical feasibility of the approach.

4. **Significance:** The results provide valuable theoretical guidance for practitioners using MCMC-based gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning. The paper establishes a theoretical foundation for a common practice in the field and advances our understanding of the computational complexity of maximum likelihood learning in exponential families.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Practical Impact:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper acknowledges it makes "no significant practical contribution" (Section 6). The example provided is illustrative but does not demonstrate substantial improvements over existing methods. The theoretical bounds appear conservative in practice, as noted in the example.

2. **Scope Limitation:** The paper focuses on the theoretical analysis of the algorithm but does not address important practical considerations like how to efficiently compute the projection onto the fast-mixing set for complex models. While the authors acknowledge this limitation (Section 7), it represents a gap between theory and practice.

3. **Comparison with Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work on well-specified models (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.), it could more explicitly contrast its approach with recent methods that also address intractable likelihoods, such as Steinhardt and Liang's fast-mixing learning approach (which the authors cite but don't deeply compare).

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper presents a novel theoretical framework that connects mixing times to computational complexity of maximum likelihood learning.
- The results are technically sound and rigorously proven, addressing a fundamental gap in the literature.
- The approach is generalizable beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.
- The paper provides explicit schedules for algorithm parameters, making the theory actionable for practitioners.

*Con:*
- The paper lacks a demonstration of significant practical improvements over existing methods.
- The theoretical bounds appear conservative in practice, and the paper doesn't explore ways to tighten them.
- The connection to practical implementation details (e.g., efficient projection algorithms) is underdeveloped.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of maximum likelihood learning with MCMC sampling. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a novel conceptual contribution by introducing fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractability criterion. While the practical impact is limited, the theoretical results are significant and provide valuable insights for the field. The paper meets all criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission, with particular strength in novelty and technical soundness.

The paper's main limitation is its limited practical demonstration, but this is common in theoretical works and does not diminish the contribution. The results are sufficiently novel compared to recent NIPS publications, and the theoretical framework provides a foundation for future work on practical implementations.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution that advances the field and provides valuable insights for both theoretical understanding and practical implementation of maximum likelihood learning in complex models.
