

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in graphical models by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing parameter set" where MCMC sampling converges quickly. The authors claim that for such parameter sets, gradient descent using MCMC samples for gradient approximation will converge to a near-optimal solution within the constraint set with high probability. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, showing computational complexity of O(1/ϵ^3 log(1/ϵ)) for convex problems and O(1/ϵ^2 log(1/ϵ)) for strongly convex problems.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically interesting connection between fast-mixing Markov chains and maximum likelihood learning. The authors clearly define their assumptions (geometric mixing bound with known constants, bounded sufficient statistics) and provide a rigorous analysis of gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation. The theoretical results are sound, and the proofs (though sketched) follow standard approaches for analyzing gradient descent with noisy gradients.

The paper properly situates itself within the literature, acknowledging related work on fast-mixing chains (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Domke & Liu, 2013) and existing approaches to intractable likelihoods (pseudolikelihood, score matching). The example in Section 6, while small, demonstrates the algorithm's operation on a concrete problem.

## Weaknesses

The primary weakness of this paper is the disconnect between the theoretical contribution and practical utility. The authors explicitly state "this paper claims no significant practical contribution," which is a major red flag for a NIPS submission. The theoretical guarantees are interesting but do not translate to practical advantages over existing methods.

The assumptions required for the results are quite strong and may not hold in practice. The paper assumes knowledge of constants C and α for the mixing time bound (equation 2), which would be difficult to obtain without prior knowledge of the mixing time. The projection step onto the fast-mixing parameter set is acknowledged as potentially computationally intensive, yet the paper does not analyze how this scales with model size.

The computational complexity results are actually worse than existing methods. The paper's convex case requires O(1/ϵ^3) work to achieve ϵ-optimality, while standard approaches like pseudolikelihood or score matching typically achieve better complexity (often O(1/ϵ^2) or better) with much simpler implementation. The paper fails to demonstrate how these theoretical guarantees translate to practical advantages.

The empirical validation is extremely limited - just a small 4x4 grid example with 5 training samples. This does not provide sufficient evidence to support the theoretical claims or demonstrate practical utility. The paper does not compare with existing methods on standard benchmarks, making it impossible to assess whether the approach is actually better in practice.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper's approach bears similarities to Steinhardt & Liang (2015) who also proposed learning with fast-mixing chains. However, this paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from their work or demonstrate how it improves upon their approach. The paper also fails to adequately address how its method compares to established approaches like pseudolikelihood or score matching, which are known to work well in practice for graphical model learning.

## Significance

The theoretical contribution is interesting but lacks clear significance for the machine learning community. The paper does not demonstrate how this approach would be useful in real-world applications or how it would outperform existing methods. The computational complexity results are actually worse than standard approaches, and the practical implementation challenges are not addressed.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to demonstrate practical utility. The authors explicitly state "no significant practical contribution," which is a serious deficiency for a NIPS submission. The theoretical results do not translate to clear practical advantages over existing methods, and the assumptions required for the results are quite strong and potentially unrealistic for real-world applications.

The paper would benefit from substantial empirical validation on standard graphical model datasets, a comparison with existing methods, and a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation challenges. Without these elements, the paper remains a theoretical curiosity without clear significance for the field.

I would encourage the authors to reconsider the scope of their contribution and either provide stronger empirical validation to support their theoretical claims or clarify the theoretical contribution in a more compelling way. As it stands, the paper does not meet NIPS' standards for significance and practical relevance.
