Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families under a tractability notion defined by fast-mixing parameter sets. The core idea is to constrain parameters to a convex set Θ such that a chosen MCMC kernel Mθ mixes geometrically fast uniformly over Θ. The algorithm is projected gradient descent where Monte Carlo estimates (from v steps of Mθ) are used to approximate gradients; projections keep θ in Θ. The paper analyzes convergence with biased stochastic gradients in two regimes: convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized). It gives nonasymptotic complexity schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and Markov steps v to reach ε-accuracy, and asserts that this yields an FPRAS for ML within Θ. It connects to mixing-time results for Ising/MRFs and gives a small illustrative example.

Relation to prior work: The work is conceptually related to (i) mixing-time guarantees for Gibbs dynamics (Dobrushin-type conditions, spectral norm bounds), (ii) learning with fast-mixing constraints (Domke & Liu 2013; Liu & Domke 2014; Steinhardt & Liang 2014), and (iii) optimization with inexact/bias gradients (Schmidt et al. 2011). The contribution is to combine (i)–(iii) to produce end-to-end guarantees for ML optimization within Θ with explicit complexity in ε and mixing constants.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual message: if you can project onto a uniformly fast-mixing set, ML learning with MCMC-based gradients can be made polynomial-time with explicit accuracy–cost tradeoffs.
- Covers both convex and strongly convex cases with appropriate complexity scalings ((1/εf)^3 and (1/εθ)^2 up to logs) and explicit K/M/v schedules; uses established inexact-gradient convergence results carefully.
- Relates to known fast-mixing sufficient conditions (degree/interaction, spectral-norm R(θ)) and discusses projection routines for Ising/MRFs.
- The framework highlights when contrastive divergence–like updates could be given provable guarantees.

Weaknesses
Methodological/technical
- The “FPRAS” terminology is inappropriate. The paper provides additive ε guarantees on the objective (or parameter) using a randomized algorithm with polynomial cost; FPRAS is classically for approximate counting with multiplicative guarantees. If the intent is a randomized PTAS/FPTAS-like statement, it should be rephrased carefully (and multiplicative vs additive clarified).
- Uniform mixing assumption over all θ ∈ Θ with common C, α is strong and chain-specific. The paper alternates between Gibbs and subgraphs-world chains, but the algorithm and bounds should fix the kernel Mθ. The projection set Θ must be defined relative to that kernel’s mixing bound; otherwise the guarantees are ambiguous.
- Dependence on dimension and graph size is largely hidden in R2, C, α. The claim of “polynomial in all parameters” is true only if C and 1/(1−α) scale polynomially with problem size; this should be made explicit (e.g., for Dobrushin-type conditions α ≈ 1−Ω(1/N), inflating costs).
- Off-by-one index inconsistency: The text defines ek as the error relative to f′(θk−1) but later bounds terms like ∥Eqk[t] − Epθk[t]∥, which should be Epθk−1[t]. Please fix throughout.
- The Lipschitz gradient bound (Theorem 1) relies on a TV bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ − φ∥2. This is nonstandard and requires proof via e.g. Pinsker + smoothness of A(θ) and bounded t(x); the sketch is too hand-wavy. Precise constants and assumptions (bounded stats in ℓ2 vs ℓ∞) must be stated and proved.
- Convex-case analysis averages iterates; the algorithm should explicitly state step sizes (likely 1/L) and projection operator used every iteration to ensure the Schmidt et al. conditions are met.
- The analysis assumes independent M samples per iteration. Practically, this requires M independent chains (costly) or long chains with burn-in and thinning. The independence requirement is crucial for concentration (Efron–Stein/Bernstein/Hoeffding) yet impractical; a discussion of Markovian dependence (e.g., using spectral gap/mixing to get effective sample size) is missing.
- Schedules in Theorems 3–8 are algebraic manipulations of the generic inexact-gradient bounds; novelty here appears limited. The alleged near-optimality relative to lower bounds is only up to logs but hides constants (C, α, L, R2) that can be large.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous notation inconsistencies and typos hinder readability: R2 vs R22 (is R2 a scalar bound or a norm?), L = 4R22 + λ uses R22 ambiguously; “Eqk[t] − Epθk [t]” vs θk−1; “D” denotes dataset size but later reused as a bound (“If D ≥ max(...), then...”), and in the example D is undefined (“D = √ × (2× .2)2”). Please unify symbols.
- Algorithm 1, Figure 1, Fig. 2, and the appendix are referenced but not provided here; the main algorithmic details (step size, projection frequency, how samples are drawn) must be clearly specified in the main text.
- Several constants in the example appear ad hoc or incorrect: setting C = log(16) for the TV contraction prefactor is dimensionally odd; α derived from τ(ε) bounds does not match the earlier formula; choosing L = 10 “which converged faster in practice” contradicts the theory and undermines reproducibility.
- References are incomplete (many missing years and venues), contain typos (“Inferning”), inconsistent capitalization, and in places appear inaccurate.

Originality and significance
- The high-level idea—optimize within a fast-mixing set with MCMC-based gradients—is not entirely new (e.g., Domke & Liu’s projection for fast mixing, Steinhardt & Liang’s fast-mixing via restart); this paper’s main addition is a consolidated nonasymptotic complexity accounting with explicit K/M/v schedules. While useful, the step beyond prior work seems incremental, and several assumptions (uniform mixing, independence, known projection) limit practical impact.

Questions and suggestions
- Precisely define Θ relative to a fixed MCMC kernel. Provide explicit examples where Θ is nontrivial, projection is polynomial, and C, α scale favorably with N.
- Replace the FPRAS claim with a precise randomized additive ε-approximation statement for optimization, or provide a multiplicative guarantee if that is intended.
- Provide rigorous proofs for Theorem 1 (TV vs parameter-distance), and fix the θk vs θk−1 indexing.
- Relax the independence assumption using mixing/concentration for Markov chains, or at least bound the bias/variance under geometric ergodicity with dependent samples.
- Clean up notation, constants, and references; include the missing algorithm and figures; ensure the example’s constants are consistent with the theory.

Pros
- Clear, motivated framework; explicit nonasymptotic schedules.
- Bridges mixing-time theory and inexact-gradient optimization.
- Covers both convex and strongly convex cases.

Cons
- Strong/ambiguous assumptions (uniform mixing, independence, known projections).
- Misuse of “FPRAS” and hidden dimension dependence.
- Numerous notation/formatting errors; missing details.
- Incremental novelty relative to prior fast-mixing learning work.

Recommendation: Reject
