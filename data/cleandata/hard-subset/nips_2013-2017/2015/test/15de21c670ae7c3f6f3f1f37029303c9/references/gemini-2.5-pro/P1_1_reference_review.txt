### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of intractable maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in undirected graphical models. Instead of resorting to common approximations like pseudolikelihood or restricting the model structure to be tractable (e.g., a tree), the authors propose a novel approach: restricting the model parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing." The key idea is that if MCMC can efficiently generate samples from the model `p_θ`, then the gradient of the log-likelihood, which depends on expectations under `p_θ`, can be accurately estimated.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of a projected gradient descent algorithm that uses MCMC to estimate gradients. The main contribution is to prove that this common heuristic is, under the fast-mixing assumption, a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained MLE problem. The analysis is performed for two cases:
1.  **The Convex Case (Unregularized MLE):** The authors show that by appropriately setting the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples per step (M), and MCMC transitions per sample (v), the algorithm can achieve an `ϵ_f` error in the objective function with high probability. The total computational effort (KMv) scales roughly as `O(1/ϵ_f^3)`.
2.  **The Strongly Convex Case (Ridge-Regularized MLE):** For the L2-regularized objective, a stronger result is shown, providing a convergence guarantee on the parameter error `||θ_K - θ*||_2`. The total work in this case scales roughly as `O(1/ϵ_θ^2)`.

The paper connects existing results on MCMC mixing times for models like the Ising model to define concrete examples of such fast-mixing parameter sets `Θ` and discusses the required projection operator for these sets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The central idea of defining tractability for learning not through graph structure (like treewidth) but through the dynamics of sampling (mixing time) is elegant and powerful. It provides a formal bridge between the theory of rapid mixing Markov chains and the practice of learning graphical models. This is a significant departure from prior work that focused on structural restrictions (e.g., Chow-Liu) or alternative objectives (e.g., pseudolikelihood).

2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a thorough and complete theoretical analysis. It carefully bounds the biased and noisy gradient estimates that arise from finite-length MCMC runs and propagates these error bounds through the optimization process. Deriving explicit schedules for K, M, and v to achieve a target accuracy is a major strength, moving beyond simple asymptotic convergence claims.

3.  **Addresses a Long-Standing Gap:** MCMC-based MLE (like variants of Contrastive Divergence) has been a popular heuristic for decades, but often lacks strong theoretical guarantees about convergence to the true MLE. This work provides the first (to my knowledge) formal guarantees of this kind, showing that under the right conditions, the algorithm can indeed approximate the MLE solution arbitrarily well.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem is clearly motivated, the setup and notation are well-defined (the symbol table is helpful), and the main results are stated precisely before their proof sketches. The authors are also commendably honest about the limitations of their work in the conclusion.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practicality of the Bounds:** The derived bounds on the number of samples (M) and chain length (v) appear to be quite loose, a fact acknowledged by the authors in the experimental section where they use a much smaller Lipschitz constant than theory suggests. While the primary contribution is theoretical, the resulting `O(1/ϵ^3)` complexity for the convex case may be too high for many practical applications. This might limit the direct application of the proposed schedules, though the qualitative insights remain valuable.

2.  **The Projection Oracle Assumption:** The entire framework relies on the ability to efficiently project an arbitrary parameter vector onto the fast-mixing set `Θ`. While the paper gives examples where this is possible (e.g., clipping for element-wise bounds, SVD-based methods for spectral norm bounds), this projection can be a computationally hard problem in itself for other, more complex definitions of fast-mixing sets. The paper's analysis effectively offloads some of the difficulty of the learning problem onto this projection step, and the cost of this projection is not included in the complexity analysis.

3.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiment on a single 4x4 Ising model serves as a proof of concept but is not sufficient to demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. A comparison with standard baselines like pseudolikelihood or heuristically-tuned Contrastive Divergence on a slightly larger or real-world problem would have strengthened the paper by contextualizing the performance and cost of the proposed guaranteed method.

### Detailed Comments

- The distinction and separate analysis of the convex and strongly convex cases are very well done. The discussion in Section 5, relating the `ϵ_f` and `ϵ_θ` bounds and highlighting that the strongly convex analysis implies a `O(1/ϵ_f)` complexity, is an important insight.
- The paper correctly identifies that its analysis does not cover common heuristics like using a persistent "pool" of samples or adaptive step sizes, which are key components of methods like Persistent Contrastive Divergence (PCD). However, the work provides a crucial theoretical baseline against which such heuristics can be understood and potentially analyzed in the future.
- In the proof sketch for Theorem 6 (strongly convex case), the paper uses a union bound over K iterations. This is a standard technique but can be loose. It might be interesting to comment on whether a more direct analysis of the expected error could yield tighter bounds, similar to what was done for the convex case.

### Recommendation: Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and highly significant reconceptualization of "tractable learning" in graphical models.
*   Presents a rigorous and complete theoretical analysis of a widely used but poorly understood class of algorithms, establishing an FPRAS for constrained MLE.
*   The work is clear, well-written, and connects two important fields (MCMC theory and ML optimization).
*   Likely to inspire follow-up work on designing new learning algorithms, analyzing existing heuristics, and defining new tractable parameter sets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical relevance is somewhat limited by the conservative theoretical bounds and the strong assumption of an efficient projection oracle.
*   The experimental validation is minimal and does not compare against relevant baselines.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to the understanding of learning in graphical models. The weaknesses related to practical applicability and limited experiments are outweighed by the novelty, significance, and rigor of the theoretical results. It addresses a fundamental problem with a fresh perspective and provides long-sought-after guarantees for a popular class of methods. The paper is an excellent fit for NIPS and is likely to be influential.

### Evaluation Criteria

*   **Quality:** Excellent. The paper is technically sound, claims are well-supported by rigorous analysis, and it represents a complete piece of theoretical work. The authors are honest about its limitations.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow for a reader with the appropriate background.
*   **Originality:** Excellent. The core idea of linking tractability to mixing times for a learning algorithm is highly novel. It clearly delineates how it differs from previous work on both exact and approximate learning.
*   **Significance:** High. The paper provides a theoretical foundation for a family of practical algorithms and opens up a new avenue for research in tractable learning. Researchers are likely to build on these ideas, both by trying to tighten the bounds and by designing new algorithms for different fast-mixing parameter sets.
