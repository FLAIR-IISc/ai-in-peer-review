- Paper proposes learning within a constrained parameter set Θ where MCMC is fast-mixing and proves a Fast Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning.  
- Framework applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models.  
- Provides detailed convergence analysis and schedules for iterations, samples, and Markov chain transitions for convex and strongly convex cases.  
- Strong theoretical rigor with provable guarantees distinguishing convex vs. strongly convex scenarios.  
- Offers practical guidance by prescribing hyperparameter schedules (K, M, v) and includes an illustrative example.  
- Builds on prior hardness results and learning alternatives, distinguishing itself by leveraging fast-mixing rather than approximating likelihoods.  
- Extends and connects to existing MCMC and convex optimization theory, citing relevant theorems.  
- Core assumption of identifying fast-mixing parameter sets (and knowing constants C and α) is strong and may be hard to satisfy in practice.  
- The derived theoretical bounds are likely conservative and may impose higher-than-necessary computational costs.  
- Projection onto Θ may be nontrivial in high dimensions; the paper understates projection complexity and techniques.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited to a small example and lacks comprehensive experiments across models and datasets.  
- Treatment of initialization is brief; the impact of starting distribution r and data-based initializations (e.g., contrastive divergence) on mixing is under-discussed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound FPRAS, broad applicability, and useful practical guidance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive fast-mixing assumption, potentially conservative bounds, and insufficient empirical validation.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions despite practical limitations.  
- Recommended improvements: expand discussion on projection methods and complexity, broaden empirical evaluation, analyze initialization strategies, seek ways to tighten bounds or adaptively set hyperparameters, and compare practical costs with alternatives like pseudolikelihood.
