Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families when exact inference is intractable due to arbitrary treewidth. It proposes constraining parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ, for which a chosen Markov chain has a uniform geometric mixing rate across Θ. Under this assumption and the ability to project onto Θ, the paper analyzes projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated by MCMC sampling run for a finite number of steps from an arbitrary initialization. Using concentration bounds and existing results on inexact (projected) gradient descent (Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011), it derives high-probability convergence guarantees:
- Convex case (λ = 0): a bound on suboptimality f(average iterate) − f(θ∗), with total sampling work scaling as O(εf−3 log(1/εf)).
- Strongly convex case (λ > 0): a bound on parameter error ∥θK − θ∗∥2 with total sampling work O(εθ−2 up to logarithmic factors).
A key auxiliary result shows the regularized log-likelihood has an L-Lipschitz gradient with L = 4R2 2 + λ, assuming bounded sufficient statistics norm ∥t(x)∥2 ≤ R2. The work interprets these convergence results as providing a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for MLE restricted to Θ, provided projection is efficient and uniform mixing bounds (C, α) are known. The paper situates Θ using known mixing-time conditions, e.g., Dobrushin-type bounds for Gibbs on Ising/MRFs (Hayes; Dyer et al.; Liu & Domke, NIPS 2014), and discusses projection operators (e.g., spectral-norm-based projections for Ising/MRFs; Domke & Liu, NIPS 2013; Liu & Domke, NIPS 2014). It contrasts with surrogate estimators such as pseudolikelihood (Besag), score matching (Hyvärinen), composite likelihood (Lindsay; Varin et al.), and methods based on local moments or clusters (Abbeel et al.; Mizrahi et al.), and with fast-mixing-by-design chains with restarts (Steinhardt & Liang, ICML 2015). A small Ising example illustrates the prescribed schedule.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: isolates a tractable parameter regime (fast mixing) rather than a tractable graph structure, with clean exponential-family generality.
- Technical contribution: rigorous high-probability convergence bounds for MCMC-based projected gradient descent with biased gradients, for both convex and strongly convex cases, including explicit schedules for iterations, sample sizes, and chain lengths and corresponding lower bounds.
- Connection to mixing-time literature: leverages uniform mixing conditions (e.g., Dobrushin-type/spectral-norm constraints) and known projection procedures, offering a principled pathway to learning with provable guarantees even at high treewidth.
- Objective bound structure: decomposes gradient error into sampling variance and mixing bias; uses Bernstein/Efron-Stein and vector Hoeffding appropriately; employs known inexact-gradient convergence results to translate per-iteration errors into optimization guarantees.
- Theoretical significance: formalizes an FPRAS-like guarantee for MLE restricted to Θ (given projection and mixing bounds), filling a gap in prior MCMC-ML works (e.g., Geyer; Gu & Zhu; Salakhutdinov) that lacked runtime/accuracy schedules.

Weaknesses and limitations
- FPRAS claim scope: The runtime accounting focuses on total Markov transitions KMv and largely ignores projection cost. An FPRAS requires overall runtime polynomial in input size and 1/ε; this holds only if (i) per-transition cost and (ii) projection onto Θ are polynomial in problem size. The paper acknowledges this but the main statement could be more precisely qualified.
- Uniform mixing constants: The analysis assumes known, uniform (C, α) over Θ. For common constructions (e.g., Gibbs under Dobrushin-type conditions), α often depends on problem size (e.g., α = 1 − Θ(1/N)), which is acceptable but should be explicitly accounted for in the complexity statements to substantiate the “fully polynomial” claim with respect to N.
- Lipschitz gradient bound: Theorem 1 relies on bounding ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2R2∥θ − φ∥2 under ∥t(x)∥2 ≤ R2. While plausible (via bounded log-density differences and Pinsker-type arguments), this step is nontrivial; providing the full derivation (or citing a standard result) would strengthen soundness and clarify constants.
- Independence assumptions: The analysis assumes independent samples within each iteration (fresh chains initialized from a fixed r). This implies M parallel chains per iteration; the practical “single long chain” or “persistent pool” strategies are not covered by the proofs (as noted by the authors).
- Practical significance: Θ constraints ensuring fast mixing may be restrictive for some applications (e.g., strong couplings). The single small-scale experiment is purely illustrative; there is no empirical comparison to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood/CD to assess statistical efficiency or practical trade-offs.
- Notational clarity: Minor inconsistencies (e.g., R2 vs R22; Ra defined in the setup but R2 used later) can hinder readability. Some references contain typos.

Quality
The technical development is coherent and uses appropriate tools. The main potential gap is the terse justification of the TV–parameter Lipschitz bound and the precise conditions under which uniform mixing bounds hold across Θ. The reduction from gradient error bounds to convergence rates leverages well-established inexact optimization results. The schedules and lower bounds are consistent and informative.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized, with helpful proof sketches. Improving notation consistency, explicitly stating all assumptions (bounded sufficient statistics, availability and cost of projection, precise chain used, uniformity of mixing constants), and collecting the algorithmic steps (Algorithm 1) in the main text would enhance clarity.

Originality
Moderately novel. Restricting to fast-mixing parameter sets and projection has appeared (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke). The novel aspect here is a comprehensive convergence and complexity analysis for MCMC-based projected gradient descent that yields an FPRAS-style result for MLE within Θ, with explicit trade-offs and schedules, and coverage of both convex and strongly convex regimes.

Significance
Theoretical significance is solid: it provides a rigorous template to convert fast-mixing assumptions into learning guarantees at arbitrary treewidth. Practical impact may be limited by the restrictiveness of Θ and the requirement for uniform mixing constants/projection, but the work can influence future designs of learning algorithms with provable guarantees and inform variants of contrastive divergence.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Precisely state the FPRAS claim, clarifying dependence on problem size (N, D), per-step computational costs, and projection complexity.
- Provide or cite a full proof of the TV–parameter Lipschitz bound used in Theorem 1; alternatively, bound ∥∇A(θ) − ∇A(φ)∥ via the Hessian Lipschitz/variance properties under bounded t(x).
- Explicitly specify the Markov chain used (e.g., single-site Gibbs) in the main results and the uniformity of (C, α) over Θ.
- Discuss how to construct Θ and project for general exponential families beyond pairwise MRFs; provide concrete examples or oracle assumptions.
- Include an ablation or small empirical comparison to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood or CD to illustrate trade-offs in practice.
- Clean up notation (R2/R22/Ra) and minor typos in references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides a clear, rigorous convergence analysis for a widely used but under-theorized learning strategy (MCMC-based ML) with explicit schedules and lower bounds.
- Bridges mixing-time theory and optimization to offer an FPRAS-style guarantee for MLE under fast-mixing constraints.
- General framework for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical utility is limited without stronger evidence that useful Θ sets exist and can be efficiently projected onto for realistic models; experiments are minimal.
- Some technical steps (notably the global TV–parameter Lipschitz bound and uniform mixing assumptions) require fuller justification.
- FPRAS claim needs qualification regarding projection costs and dependence on problem parameters.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a complete proof or citation for ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2R2∥θ − φ∥2?
- For which families beyond pairwise MRFs can Θ be constructed with efficient projection and known uniform (C, α)?
- How does the runtime depend on N explicitly when using Dobrushin-type mixing bounds (e.g., α = 1 − c/N)?
- Can the analysis be extended to persistent chains or sample pools across iterations?
- Any empirical comparison to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood on moderate-size problems?

Overall, the paper offers a solid theoretical contribution; strengthening the technical justifications and clarifying the FPRAS scope would improve it.
