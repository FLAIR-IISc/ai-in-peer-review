## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, which is generally intractable. Instead of seeking polynomial-time exact solutions, the authors propose a framework leveraging the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets." They demonstrate that if a parameter space Θ allows for fast mixing in MCMC sampling, a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for finding the maximum likelihood solution *within* that space.  The core contribution is a theoretical analysis of gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients, establishing convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases.  The work generalizes beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper situates itself well within the existing literature on approximate maximum likelihood estimation. It correctly identifies the drawbacks of methods like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods (inferior sample complexity, lack of M-projection guarantees under model misspecification).  It contrasts its approach with restricting parameter spaces to tractable forms like trees (Chow-Liu algorithm), and acknowledges recent work on high-girth models.  The paper builds upon established results in MCMC theory (Jerrum & Sinclair, Convergence Theorem) and stochastic gradient descent analysis (Schmidt et al.).  The authors appropriately cite relevant works throughout the manuscript.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for its claims, rigorously analyzing the convergence of gradient descent with noisy gradients.  The proofs, while detailed (and relegated to the appendix), appear sound.
*   **Generality:**  The result isn't limited to graphical models; it applies broadly to exponential families, increasing its potential impact.
*   **Practical Relevance (Potential):** While the authors acknowledge limited immediate practical impact, the analysis provides a theoretical framework for understanding the convergence of MCMC-based learning algorithms, and potentially guiding parameter settings. The example in Section 6 demonstrates a plausible application scenario.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The notation is clearly defined (with a helpful reference section), and the logical flow is easy to follow.
*   **Comprehensive Background:** The background section on mixing times and related concepts is thorough and provides necessary context for understanding the main results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Conservatism & Practicality:**  The theoretical guarantees are likely conservative. The example in Section 6 shows that in practice, one can achieve better performance than the bounds suggest.  The dependence on parameters like *R* (sufficient statistics norm) and *L* (Lipschitz constant) might lead to prohibitively large sample complexities in certain applications.
*   **Computational Cost of Projection:** The paper acknowledges that the cost of projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ is not considered. In some cases, this projection step itself could be computationally expensive, negating the benefits of fast mixing. The examples given (Ising model) have relatively simple projection algorithms, but this is not always the case.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the example provides a small illustration, more extensive empirical evaluation would strengthen the paper.  Demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach on a wider range of models and datasets would be valuable.
*   **Missing Discussion of Initialization:** The analysis assumes an arbitrary starting point. How sensitive is the algorithm’s performance to the initialization scheme?  The authors briefly touch on this in the discussion section, but further investigation would be beneficial.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The rigorous treatment of MCMC errors and convergence guarantees is commendable.
*   **Clarity:**  The paper is generally clearly written, although the mathematical details can be challenging. The organization is logical, and the notation is well-defined.
*   **Originality:** The combination of FPRAS existence proofs, fast-mixing parameter sets, and analysis of gradient descent with MCMC errors is novel. While the individual components are known, their synthesis represents a significant contribution.
*   **Significance:**  The results are potentially significant, as they provide a theoretical framework for analyzing MCMC-based learning algorithms.  However, the practical impact remains to be seen, as the algorithm's performance will be sensitive to parameter choices and the cost of projection.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical results with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Generality to exponential families.
*   Novel combination of existing techniques.
*   Addresses a fundamental challenge in statistical learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical guarantees may be conservative, leading to high sample complexities.
*   Limited empirical validation.
*   Ignores the computational cost of projection onto the parameter set.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field of statistical learning. While the theoretical results may be conservative and the practical implications remain to be fully explored, the paper provides a valuable framework for analyzing MCMC-based learning algorithms. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest that the authors:

*   Discuss the limitations of the theoretical bounds and acknowledge the potential for more efficient implementations in practice.
*   Include a more extensive empirical evaluation, demonstrating the algorithm's performance on a wider range of models and datasets.
*   Add a discussion of the computational cost of projection and its impact on overall performance.
