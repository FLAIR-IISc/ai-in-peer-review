### Review

**Summary:**
This paper analyzes the widely used strategy of performing maximum likelihood learning in exponential families via gradient descent, where the intractable gradient is approximated using MCMC sampling. The authors' central contribution is to provide a formal theoretical guarantee for this procedure. They show that by restricting the parameter vector `θ` to a convex set `Θ` where the corresponding Markov chain is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" (i.e., converges to its stationary distribution at a geometric rate), the algorithm constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). The analysis provides explicit schedules for setting the number of gradient iterations `K`, MCMC samples per iteration `M`, and Markov chain transitions `v` to achieve a desired accuracy `ϵ`. The paper presents separate results for the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (L2-regularized) settings, building upon prior work on inexact proximal-gradient methods and known mixing-time bounds for specific models like the Ising model.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Theoretical Significance:** The paper tackles a significant open problem: providing rigorous convergence guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood estimation, a method that is popular in practice but has largely remained a heuristic. Establishing an FPRAS under the fast-mixing assumption is a novel and important theoretical contribution that formally bridges the fields of MCMC theory and stochastic optimization for this learning problem.
2.  **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical framework, carefully laying out assumptions and deriving explicit complexity bounds. The separate treatment of the convex and strongly convex cases is appropriate, and the analysis correctly highlights the improved convergence rates obtainable with regularization (Theorems 6-9 vs. Theorems 2-5).
3.  **Clarity of Goal:** The paper's objective is well-defined, and the core idea of trading off model expressiveness (by constraining `θ` to `Θ`) for computational tractability (guaranteed fast mixing and convergence) is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising theoretical direction, the paper is beset by a substantial number of flaws ranging from major omissions to minor but pervasive signs of carelessness. These issues severely undermine the work's quality and call its readiness for publication into question.

1.  **Incompleteness and Verifiability:** The paper is critically incomplete.
    *   The authors state that "detailed proofs are postponed to the appendix," but no appendix is provided. This is a fatal flaw, as it renders the central theoretical claims of the paper entirely unverifiable. The supplied "proof sketches" are far too brief to substitute for rigorous proofs.
    *   The text refers to a "cartoon in Figure 1" and experimental results in "Fig. 2," neither of which are included in the manuscript. Without these figures, the algorithmic description is less clear, and the experimental results cannot be assessed.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from poor presentation and numerous formatting errors.
    *   **Citations:** The paper uses a numerical citation style (e.g., `[1993]`) which is inconsistent with the standard author-year format for NIPS. Furthermore, the bibliography is poorly formatted, with several incomplete entries (e.g., "Learning factor graphs in polynomial time and sample complexity, P. Abbeel, D. Koller, A. Ng, Journal of Machine Learning Research,, , ,"). This suggests a lack of attention to detail.
    *   **Notation:** The notation is occasionally non-standard and cumbersome. For instance, `f'` is used for the gradient instead of the more common `∇f`. The definition `qk = Mvθk−1r` is dense and could be clarified.
    *   **Redundancy and Typos:** There are several typos ("at long as", "for more reference") and instances of redundant phrasing (e.g., the near-identical definitions in Eqs. 2 and 3).

3.  **Practical Implications and Experimental Support:** The paper's connection to practical application is tenuous.
    *   The derived hyperparameter schedules (Theorems 4, 5, 8, 9) are highly complex and depend on unknown quantities like the Lipschitz constant `L` and the initial distance to the optimum `∥θ0 − θ∗∥2`. While the authors acknowledge this, the proposed upper bounds are likely to be extremely conservative.
    *   The single example provided in Section 6 undermines the paper's claims more than it supports them. The authors admit to using an ad-hoc value for `L` (`L=10`) because the theoretically derived one (`L=97`) "converged slower in practice." This demonstrates that the derived bounds are not practically useful for setting hyperparameters, which is one of the explicit goals of the analysis. The experiment itself is on a trivial scale (4x4 grid, 5 data points) and its results cannot be seen due to the missing figure.

4.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is limited to a simplified algorithm that may not reflect common practice. The authors correctly note in their conclusion that the analysis does not apply to popular, sophisticated heuristics like persistent contrastive divergence, where samples are "recycled" between iterations. This limits the direct relevance of the guarantees to state-of-the-art implementations. Furthermore, the complexity of the projection step `ΠΘ` is ignored in the overall work calculation, which could be a significant oversight if this projection is itself computationally expensive.

**Decision:**
I recommend **rejection**.

While the core theoretical idea is novel and significant, the paper in its current state is unacceptable for publication. The omission of the appendix with all the proofs is a critical failure that makes a proper technical review impossible. This, combined with missing figures, pervasive formatting and citation errors, and an unconvincing experimental section that contradicts its own theoretical findings, indicates that the manuscript is a premature submission. The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address these fundamental issues of completeness, clarity, and substantiation before it can be reconsidered.
