

# Peer Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is intractable due to the #P-completeness of partition function evaluation (Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993). The authors propose a novel approach to tractable maximum likelihood learning by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing parameter set" Θ, where MCMC sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. Their key insight is that if Θ is such a set (with geometric mixing time bounds), then gradient descent using MCMC to approximate gradients will converge to a near-optimal solution with polynomial computational complexity.

The paper makes several significant contributions:
1. Formalizing the concept of fast-mixing parameter sets with a geometric mixing bound (Equation 2)
2. Showing that for such sets, maximum likelihood learning has a FPRAS
3. Providing rigorous convergence guarantees for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases
4. Deriving precise computational complexity bounds (KMv = O(1/ϵ³) for convex case, O(1/ϵ²) for strongly convex case)

This approach differs fundamentally from traditional tractability notions based on low-treewidth models (e.g., Chow-Liu algorithm) or correlation decay (Heinemann and Globerson, 2014). Instead of restricting the graph structure, the authors restrict the parameter space to ensure fast MCMC mixing.

## Strengths

### Theoretical Soundness and Novelty
The paper presents a technically sound and novel theoretical contribution. The authors correctly identify that while maximum likelihood learning is intractable in general, restricting to fast-mixing parameter sets provides a viable path to tractable learning. Their approach is conceptually clean and bridges a significant gap between theory and practice—MCMC-based gradient estimation is widely used in practice, but this is the first work to provide rigorous theoretical guarantees under a fast-mixing condition.

The main technical contribution (Theorems 2-9) is impressive in its completeness. The authors carefully bound both the bias (from MCMC mixing) and variance (from sampling) of the gradient estimates, and then leverage results from Schmidt et al. (2011) on gradient descent with errors. The analysis of the convex and strongly convex cases is thorough and well-structured.

### Clarity and Organization
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and motivation, the setup section carefully defines all necessary notation, and the main results are presented with clear proof sketches and intuitive explanations. The example in Section 6 effectively demonstrates the practical feasibility of the approach without overwhelming the reader with implementation details.

### Honest Discussion of Limitations
The authors are remarkably honest about the limitations of their approach. In Section 7, they explicitly discuss:
- The computational cost of the projection step (though they argue it's typically negligible)
- The initialization of MCMC chains (noting their analysis doesn't cover the "pool" strategy used in practice)
- The conservative nature of their theoretical bounds (their example shows practical performance exceeding theoretical guarantees)

This level of honesty is rare and highly commendable.

## Weaknesses

### Strong Assumption on Mixing Time
The most significant limitation is the assumption of geometric mixing (Equation 2). While this is a standard assumption in mixing time analysis, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how restrictive this is for practical models. For example, the Ising model with arbitrary interactions is known to mix slowly when |θ| is large (Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993), and the fast-mixing set Θ they propose (|θ_ij| ≤ β) is quite restrictive. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how common this fast-mixing condition is in practice for natural models.

### Limited Practical Demonstration
While the paper claims "no significant practical contribution," the single example (4×4 Ising model) is too simple to demonstrate the approach's value for complex models. A more substantial example (e.g., on a larger grid or with more complex sufficient statistics) would better illustrate the practical relevance of their theoretical results.

### Projection Complexity
The paper assumes the projection onto Θ is cheap, but doesn't analyze how this scales with model size. For some models (e.g., Ising with spectral norm constraints), the projection requires singular value decomposition, which is O(N³) for an N×N matrix. For large models, this could become the dominant computational cost, contradicting the authors' assertion that it's "often very small in comparison to sampling time."

### Missing Connection to Contrastive Divergence
The paper mentions contrastive divergence in Section 7 but doesn't explicitly connect their work to this widely used practical technique. Contrastive divergence (CD) [5, 29] is a specific implementation of MCMC-based gradient estimation that uses a "pool" of samples between iterations. The authors' analysis doesn't cover this common strategy, which is a significant gap given CD's widespread use in practice.

### Conservative Theoretical Bounds
The theoretical bounds are quite conservative (as the authors acknowledge in Section 6), which makes the practical utility less clear. For example, their bound for the 4×4 Ising model requires K=46, M=1533, v=561, but they note the algorithm finds a solution tighter than the specified ϵ. A more refined analysis that better matches practical performance would strengthen the paper.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper correctly situates itself against key prior work:

- It properly contrasts with pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22], and composite likelihood [20, 30], noting their limitations (inferior sample complexity, well-specified model assumption, intractable inference).
- It acknowledges the treewidth-based approaches (Chow-Liu, Heinemann and Globerson) and shows how their fast-mixing approach is fundamentally different.
- It cites relevant work on fast-mixing Markov chains (Jerrum and Sinclair [1993], Domke and Liu [2013], Liu and Domke [2014]).

However, it could better connect to the work on contrastive divergence (Carreira-Perpiñán and Hinton [5], Tieleman [29]) and Steinhardt and Liang [28], which also use fast-mixing chains for learning. Steinhardt and Liang [28] propose learning in terms of the stationary distribution from a fast-mixing chain, but their work is on structured prediction, not maximum likelihood learning.

## Overall Assessment

### Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and complete, and the authors are honest about limitations. The main weakness is the strong geometric mixing assumption, but they acknowledge this limitation.

### Clarity
Exceptionally clear and well-organized. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the theoretical results.

### Originality
The core idea—using fast-mixing parameter sets for tractable maximum likelihood learning—is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance. It's not merely a combination of existing techniques but a fundamentally new perspective on tractability.

### Significance
This work is highly significant as it provides the first theoretical justification for a widely used practical technique (MCMC-based gradient estimation for maximum likelihood learning). It advances the state of the art by showing that under a natural condition (fast mixing), maximum likelihood learning can be done efficiently. While the fast-mixing condition is restrictive, the paper provides a clear path to understanding when this approach will work.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** for NIPS. This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in graphical models with novel insights. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning algorithms.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand discussion of fast-mixing condition**: Provide more examples of models where this condition holds naturally (beyond the Ising model) and discuss how restrictive it is in practice.

2. **Include a more substantial practical example**: Extend the example to a larger model (e.g., 10×10 grid) or a different model family (e.g., Potts model) to better demonstrate practical relevance.

3. **Clarify connection to contrastive divergence**: Explicitly show how their framework relates to and improves upon CD, which is a dominant practical technique for this problem.

4. **Refine theoretical bounds**: Consider whether the bounds can be tightened to better match practical performance, as the current bounds are quite conservative.

5. **Discuss projection complexity more thoroughly**: Provide a more detailed analysis of how the projection step scales with model size for different constraint sets (e.g., spectral norm vs. element-wise bounds).

This paper represents an important step toward understanding the theoretical underpinnings of MCMC-based learning algorithms, and I believe it will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
