## NIPS Review: Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, circumventing the intractability typically associated with general graph structures. Instead of seeking efficient solutions for arbitrary graphs, the authors propose a novel approach centered around identifying parameter sets (Θ) for which MCMC sampling converges rapidly.  They demonstrate that, given such a set and a projection operator onto it, a Fast Polynomial Random Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for finding the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The work is grounded in the exponential family framework and offers theoretical guarantees for gradient descent optimization using MCMC for gradient estimation, focusing on both convex and strongly convex cases.  The paper bridges theoretical results on Markov chain mixing times with practical optimization techniques, providing a principled way to define tractable parameter spaces beyond tree-structured models.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper acknowledges and builds upon a rich history of approximate inference and learning in graphical models.  It correctly positions itself against methods like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods, highlighting their limitations in sample complexity and performance under model mis-specification.  The authors also relate their work to the Chow-Liu algorithm and high-girth models as existing approaches to tractable parameter spaces. However, the key novelty lies in shifting the focus from specific graph structures (trees, high-girth) to the *mixing properties* of MCMC within a defined parameter space. They appropriately cite seminal work on mixing times (Jerrum & Sinclair, Hayes, etc.) and leverage recent advances (Steinhardt & Liang) in utilizing fast-mixing chains for learning.  The work is clearly aware of and draws inspiration from related literature on optimization with noisy gradients, citing Schmidt et al.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets is a significant departure from traditional approaches. This provides a more flexible and potentially powerful framework for tackling complex models.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for its claims. The FPRAS guarantee is a strong result, and the analysis of gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients is thorough and well-motivated. The separate analysis for convex and strongly convex cases demonstrates a nuanced understanding of optimization.
* **Generality:** Framing the problem within the exponential family allows for broader applicability beyond graphical models.  This increases the impact and potential use-cases of the proposed framework.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The paper offers detailed bounds on the number of MCMC steps and iterations required to achieve a desired level of accuracy. Theorems 7 and 8, specifically, provide practical guidance for parameter tuning.
* **Illustrative Example:** The provided Ising model example, while simple, demonstrates the feasibility and potential of the approach in practice.
* **Comprehensive Background:** The detailed background section on mixing times, including specific bounds for Ising models and related work, is highly valuable for readers unfamiliar with the area.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations:** While theoretically sound, the paper lacks a comprehensive discussion of the practical challenges associated with finding and projecting onto fast-mixing parameter sets Θ. The example highlights that selecting L can be non-trivial, and the computational cost of projection could be significant for complex models.  The authors acknowledge this, but a more in-depth discussion would be beneficial.
* **Tightness of Bounds:** The theoretical bounds, while correct, appear quite loose, particularly the multiplicative constants. The authors acknowledge this and discuss potential conservatism. It would be valuable to investigate the tightness of these bounds further, perhaps through additional experiments or refined analysis.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The single Ising model example, while helpful, is not sufficient to demonstrate the practical efficacy of the approach on more complex datasets or models. A more extensive empirical evaluation would strengthen the paper considerably.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of knowing constants like *C* and *α* that define the mixing rate is strong.  In practice, these parameters often need to be estimated, adding computational overhead and potentially affecting the performance of the algorithm.
* **Projection Complexity:** While the paper acknowledges the potential cost of projection, a more detailed discussion of algorithms for projection onto different types of fast-mixing sets (beyond the Ising model example) would be helpful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to maximum likelihood learning in complex models.
* The FPRAS guarantee is a significant contribution.
* The generality of the exponential family framework increases the potential impact of the work.
* The paper provides a comprehensive and well-motivated analysis of the algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of extensive empirical evaluation limits the practical demonstration of the approach.
* The theoretical bounds appear somewhat loose.
* The practical challenges of finding and projecting onto fast-mixing parameter sets are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that presents a novel and promising approach to maximum likelihood learning. While the practical challenges and loose bounds are concerns, the theoretical contributions are significant and the paper is well-written and meticulously analyzed.  The work has the potential to stimulate further research in this area.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  **Expand the discussion of practical challenges:**  Elaborate on the complexities of finding and projecting onto fast-mixing parameter sets for different model types.
2.  **Provide a more extensive empirical evaluation:**  Include results on more complex datasets and models to demonstrate the practical efficacy of the approach.
3.  **Investigate the tightness of the bounds:** Explore ways to refine the analysis and potentially tighten the theoretical bounds.
4.  **Discuss strategies for estimating mixing parameters:** Address how the required constants *C* and *α* could be estimated in practice.
