## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, which is often intractable. Instead of aiming for a universally tractable class of models, the authors propose a novel approach: constraining the parameter space to a set Θ where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling mixes quickly. They demonstrate that if such a set Θ exists and projection onto it is feasible, then a Fast Polynomial Random Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for finding the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The core contribution is a theoretical analysis linking fast mixing times to the existence of an FPRAS for maximum likelihood estimation, applicable to the broader exponential family framework, and crucially, providing convergence guarantees even in the mis-specified model case. The work builds on existing gradient descent strategies utilizing MCMC, and provides a schedule for choosing the number of iterations, samples, and Markov transitions to achieve a desired level of accuracy.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the landscape of alternatives to full maximum likelihood estimation, such as pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods, highlighting their limitations in sample complexity and guarantees for mis-specified models. It also acknowledges previous work leveraging tractable parameter sets (like tree-structured models via Chow-Liu), noting their limitations in representational power and inference tractability. The work’s connection to MCMC-based gradient estimation is explicitly recognized, placing the paper within a growing body of research attempting to provide theoretical foundations for these widely used practical methods. It correctly references and builds on work regarding mixing times for Ising models (Jerrum & Sinclair, Hayes) and parameter projection techniques (Domke & Liu). Finally, the connection to stochastic gradient descent and the use of results from Schmidt et al. regarding projected gradient descent are important and appropriately acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of leveraging fast mixing times as a criterion for tractability in maximum likelihood estimation is a novel and insightful contribution. It shifts the focus from model structure to sampling efficiency.
* **Generality:**  The framework extends beyond graphical models to the broader exponential family, increasing its potential impact.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for its claims, establishing an FPRAS under well-defined conditions.  The analysis of both convex and strongly convex cases is comprehensive.
* **Practical Implications:**  While not a primary focus, the provided schedules for K, M, and v offer guidance for practical implementation. The example Ising model result is helpful in illustrating the theory.
* **Handles Mis-Specification:** Unlike many existing methods, this framework provides guarantees even when the assumed model is incorrect.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is reasonably consistent and the main results are clearly stated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality of Fast-Mixing Sets:** A significant challenge lies in identifying parameter sets Θ that guarantee fast mixing. While the paper references examples like the Ising model with bounded interaction strengths, it doesn't offer a systematic way to construct such sets for arbitrary models.  The complexity of projection onto these sets also needs further investigation in practice.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** As acknowledged in the example, the theoretical bounds may be conservative, potentially requiring significantly more samples/iterations than necessary in practice. 
* **Dependency on Lipschitz Constant:** The analysis relies on accurately determining the Lipschitz constant L, which can be challenging in complex models. The choice of L=10 in the example seems somewhat arbitrary given the theoretical L=97.
* **Limited Discussion of Initialization:** The influence of the initial parameter estimate (θ0) on convergence is not extensively explored. While the theory handles an arbitrary starting point, understanding its impact could be valuable.
* **Connection to Contrastive Divergence:** The brief mention of contrastive divergence (CD) as a potential initialization strategy feels underdeveloped. A deeper analysis of how CD fits into this framework would be beneficial.
* **Projection Complexity:** The paper acknowledges that projection might be expensive, but further investigation into the computational cost of different projection algorithms for common model classes would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a fundamentally new perspective on tractable maximum likelihood learning.
* The theoretical results are rigorous and provide strong guarantees.
* The generality of the framework extends beyond traditional graphical models.
* The work addresses a significant challenge in the field and has the potential to inspire further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical applicability of the approach hinges on finding fast-mixing parameter sets, which may be difficult in many scenarios.
* The theoretical bounds may be overly conservative, leading to high computational costs.
* While theoretically sound, the paper lacks a compelling demonstration of practical superiority over existing methods in realistic settings.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and theoretically significant paper. While practical challenges remain, the authors have made a valuable contribution to our understanding of maximum likelihood learning. The novelty of the approach, the rigor of the analysis, and the generality of the framework outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following:

1. **Discuss limitations in finding fast-mixing parameter sets:**  Expand the discussion on the challenges of constructing and projecting onto fast-mixing sets in practice.
2. **Investigate initialization strategies:**  Explore the impact of different initialization methods (e.g., contrastive divergence) on convergence.
3. **Clarify Lipschitz constant estimation:** Provide more guidance on estimating the Lipschitz constant L in practice.
4. **Expand on Projection Complexity:** Add a brief discussion about the computational costs of projection algorithms for different model families.
