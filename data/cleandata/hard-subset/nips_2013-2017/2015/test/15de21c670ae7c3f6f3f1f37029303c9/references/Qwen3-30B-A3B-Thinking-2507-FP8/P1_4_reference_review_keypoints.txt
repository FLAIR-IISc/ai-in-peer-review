- Main contribution: Introduces "fast-mixing parameter sets" and proves gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates converges to the maximum likelihood solution within such sets.  
- Novel perspective: Focuses on parameter-set tractability (fast mixing) instead of structural tractability (low treewidth).  
- Theoretical foundation: Provides rigorous guarantees for widely used MCMC-based learning methods, bridging theory and practice.  
- Detailed analysis: Gives explicit computational bounds for convex and strongly convex cases, with matching lower bounds (Theorems 3 and 7).  
- Presentation quality: Well-organized paper with clear notation, structured proofs, and an illustrative example.  
- Generality: Results are stated for general exponential families, not just graphical models.  
- Authors’ candor: Paper honestly acknowledges limitations like projection cost and initialization issues.  
- Weakness — experiments: Lacks empirical validation to test theoretical bounds in practice.  
- Weakness — choosing Θ: Provides little practical guidance on selecting fast-mixing constraint sets for complex models.  
- Weakness — projection complexity: Does not analyze how projection computation scales with model size, a potential practical bottleneck.  
- Weakness — mixing assumptions: Relies on mixing-time bounds that often assume uniform interactions and offers limited adaptation guidance for more complex cases.  
- Arguments for acceptance: First rigorous guarantees for MCMC-based ML learning, technically sound, general, and addresses a fundamental problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: No experiments and unclear practical applicability (choosing Θ) may limit impact.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant and well-executed theoretical contribution despite experimental absence.  
- Suggestions: Add guidance for selecting Θ across model classes, briefly state practical implications of the bounds, and expand discussion on when mixing-time bounds apply or need adaptation.
