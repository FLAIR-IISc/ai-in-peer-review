- Proposes restricting parameters to "fast-mixing" sets so MCMC-based gradient descent yields an FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning.  
- Main theoretical contribution: formal convergence and computational-complexity guarantees (KMv bounds) for convex and strongly-convex cases under a mixing-time assumption.  
- Framework is general: applies to any exponential family, not just specific graphical models.  
- Connects to prior work on intractability and mixing-time results (Jerrum & Sinclair; Steinhardt & Liang; Domke & Liu).  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous with well-motivated assumptions and carefully proven theorems.  
- Clear problem formulation that identifies limitations of pseudolikelihood, score matching, and tree-structured methods.  
- Uses standard mixing-time bounds as a central, literature-supported assumption.  
- Small 4×4 Ising example shows feasibility and that theoretical bounds are conservative in practice.  
- Limited practical contribution: only a single small-scale experiment; lacks broader empirical validation.  
- Missing implementation guidance on how to choose or construct the fast-mixing set Θ in practice.  
- Relies on a strong mixing-time assumption that may not hold for many practical models and lacks methods to verify it.  
- Does not compare empirical performance to existing MCMC-based learning methods (e.g., contrastive divergence).  
- Computational complexity bounds (O(1/ε^3) for convex case) are high and may be impractical.  
- Overall technically sound, but lack of experiments weakens suitability for a primarily empirical ML venue.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and clear, though it should better explain practical selection of Θ.  
- Idea is original: first formal guarantee for MCMC-based gradient descent under fast-mixing assumptions.  
- Significance is theoretical: advances understanding of when MCMC-based learning can be efficient, but limited practical impact due to conservative bounds and sparse experiments.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — strong theory but insufficient empirical validation and practical guidance.  
- Pro acceptance points: rigorous theory, general applicability, novel formal guarantees, builds on mixing-time literature.  
- Con acceptance points: lack of empirical validation, conservative bounds, no practical method to choose Θ, high complexity, no empirical comparisons.  
- Suggested improvements: add larger real-world experiments; provide heuristics/algorithms for selecting Θ; compare to existing MCMC learning methods; explore relaxing mixing assumptions or tightening bounds; discuss practical limitations and when to prefer other methods.
