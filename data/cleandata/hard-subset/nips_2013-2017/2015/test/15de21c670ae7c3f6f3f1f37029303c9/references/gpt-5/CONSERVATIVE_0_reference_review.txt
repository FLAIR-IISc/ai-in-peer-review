Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a restriction to a convex parameter set Θ that guarantees rapid mixing of an MCMC sampler. The main contribution is a non-asymptotic convergence analysis of projected gradient descent where gradients are estimated via finite-length MCMC runs. Under bounded sufficient statistics and fast-mixing assumptions (∥Mθ
v q − pθ∥TV ≤ C αv), the authors give:
- A Lipschitz gradient bound for the (optionally ridge-regularized) negative log-likelihood.
- High-probability convergence guarantees in the convex (λ=0) and strongly convex (λ>0) cases, with explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps K, samples per step M, and chain steps v.
- Complexity bounds for total sampling effort KMv, scaling as ~1/εf^3 (convex) and ~1/εθ^2 (strongly convex), up to logs.
They connect to known fast-mixing sufficient conditions for Ising/MRFs (e.g., spectral-norm constraints) and discuss projection onto such Θ. They claim that, given projection and mixing-rate constants, this yields an FPRAS for MLE within Θ. A small 4×4 Ising example illustrates the procedure.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation in the exponential-family setting; results apply beyond specific graphical models.
- Methodologically careful: separates convex vs strongly convex cases; uses standard tools (Hoeffding/Bernstein/Efron–Stein, Schmidt et al. bounds) to handle biased, noisy gradients.
- Provides explicit schedules for K, M, v and lower bounds, making the trade-offs transparent.
- Connects optimization guarantees to mixing-time literature and fast-mixing parameter projections (e.g., spectral norm).
- The paper is honest about limitations (projection cost, sample persistence, initialization).

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions that are hard to verify or use in practice:
  - Existence of a convex Θ with known mixing-rate constants (C, α) and an efficient Euclidean projection ΠΘ. For many models, tight, instance-specific C and α are unknown; available bounds are notoriously loose, making prescribed v impractically large.
  - The projection itself can be costly or nontrivial beyond pairwise models and specific norms; this cost is largely ignored in the complexity accounting.
- FPRAS claim needs clarification/substantiation. “Fully polynomial” requires time polynomial in input size and 1/ε. Here, runtime depends on 1/(1−α) (and C inside logs). If α→1 rapidly with N (as is common), the guarantee may cease to be fully polynomial in input size. The paper should provide explicit dependence for at least one family (e.g., Ising on bounded-degree graphs with |θij|≤β) showing polynomial dependence in N, 1/ε, and 1/(1−Δ tanh β), not only asymptotic in α.
- Minimal empirical validation. A single toy example with handpicked constants and no comparisons to standard estimators (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, CD) does not demonstrate practical usefulness, robustness to mis-specification, or scaling. No evidence that restricting to fast-mixing Θ yields a useful trade-off between tractability and statistical efficiency.
- Incomplete algorithmic details in the main text (Algorithm 1 not shown here). The analysis assumes fresh, independent chains initialized from an arbitrary r at each iteration, which is at odds with common practice (sample persistence). The paper explicitly does not cover persistence, which further limits practical relevance.
- Related work coverage is incomplete. There is a substantial literature on stochastic approximation/SGD with Markovian noise and non-asymptotic analyses (e.g., Borkar 2008; Moulines & Bach 2011, and successors) that should be contrasted with the present analysis. The relationship to Steinhardt & Liang’s restart chain is noted but could be deepened.
- Presentation issues: some notational inconsistencies (e.g., R22), minor typos, and reliance on appendices for key proofs. The Lipschitz constant bound appears conservative; implications for step-size choices and practical convergence could be discussed.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely define “FPRAS for MLE within Θ” in this context. What is the input size, and which parameters are encoded as inputs? Under which explicit structural conditions (e.g., bounded degree Δ and coupling β) do you guarantee polynomial dependence on N and 1/ε?
- For spectral-norm constraints on pairwise MRFs: what is the complexity of a practical projection routine (per iteration) as a function of graph size? Does this dominate sampling cost in realistic regimes?
- Can you provide empirical comparisons against pseudolikelihood/score matching and standard CD on moderate-size graphs, under equal compute budgets, to illustrate the utility of learning within Θ?
- How sensitive are your bounds and actual performance to the looseness of C and α?

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten and make explicit the complexity dependence on N, D, Δ, β (not only α, C), at least for one canonical model class, to substantiate the FPRAS claim.
- Include stronger experiments with baselines and ablations across constraint strengths, illustrating the trade-off between mixing guarantees and fit quality.
- Discuss/compare with stochastic approximation under Markovian noise; clarify whether your results improve constants or assumptions over that line of work.
- Provide main proof ideas in the paper (beyond sketches), fix notation/typos, and detail Algorithm 1.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean theoretical framing linking mixing-time constraints and ML optimization.
- Non-asymptotic convergence analysis with explicit schedules; potentially of interest to theory-minded NeurIPS audience.
- Unifies several strands (projection to fast-mixing sets, MCMC-based gradient methods).

Cons for acceptance
- Practical impact is unclear; empirical evidence is insufficient.
- Strong assumptions (known mixing constants, efficient projection) limit applicability.
- FPRAS claim is not fully convincing without explicit polynomial dependence on model size; could be vacuous under loose mixing bounds.
- Overlaps with existing analyses of inexact/proximal gradient methods and Markovian noise; novelty seems incremental.

Recommendation
Given the heavy reliance on idealized assumptions, limited empirical support, and an FPRAS claim that needs clearer, model-size–explicit justification, I lean toward rejection in the current form. Strengthening the theoretical guarantees with explicit dependence for concrete model classes and providing substantive empirical validation would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
