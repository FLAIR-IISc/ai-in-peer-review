

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary
This paper addresses the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by introducing the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" - constraints on model parameters that guarantee rapid mixing of MCMC samplers. The authors prove that for such sets, maximum likelihood learning becomes tractable via gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates, establishing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). This work differs from previous approaches (e.g., tree-structured models, pseudolikelihood) by focusing on the mixing properties of the Markov chain rather than structural constraints like treewidth. The paper builds on foundational work by Jerrum & Sinclair (1993) on computational complexity of partition functions, and connects to recent work on fast-mixing models (Steinhardt & Liang, 2015; Domke & Liu, 2013).

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first formal guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning. The connection between fast mixing properties and tractable learning is novel and well-motivated.

2. **Rigorous Analysis**: The proofs are thorough and well-structured, with clear proof sketches for each major result. The paper distinguishes between convex and strongly convex cases with appropriate analysis.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The authors provide a concrete example with an Ising model that demonstrates how their theoretical bounds translate to practical parameter settings. The example shows their approach converges faster than theoretically predicted, suggesting the bounds may be conservative but valid.

4. **Comprehensive Literature Context**: The paper properly situates itself within the broader literature on graphical models and MCMC, citing relevant work from NIPS and elsewhere.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Experimental Validation**: The authors explicitly state "no significant practical contribution" and provide no empirical results. While the paper is theoretical, a small set of experiments demonstrating the practical feasibility of their approach would strengthen the contribution significantly.

2. **Projection Complexity Ignored**: While the paper acknowledges that projection complexity might be significant for large models, it does not analyze this aspect. For many practical applications, the cost of projection could dominate the sampling cost, undermining the practical utility of the approach.

3. **Simplified Mixing Time Assumptions**: The mixing time analysis assumes uniform interaction strengths and simple Ising model structures, which may not reflect real-world complex models where interactions vary significantly.

4. **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods**: The paper doesn't compare the computational efficiency of their approach to existing methods (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching) in terms of actual runtime or sample complexity.

## Constructive Feedback

**For Improvement**:
1. **Add Experimental Results**: Include at least one small-scale experiment demonstrating the practical performance of the proposed method on a non-trivial model. This would help bridge the gap between theory and practice.

2. **Analyze Projection Complexity**: Provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the projection step, especially for large models. This would help readers understand the practical applicability of the approach.

3. **Expand on Practical Implementation**: Discuss how the method could be adapted to handle varying interaction strengths (not just uniform) and how to handle initialization strategies like contrastive divergence.

4. **Compare with Existing Methods**: Include a brief comparison of the theoretical sample complexity with existing methods to contextualize the contribution.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The claims are well-supported by theorems and proof sketches. However, the lack of empirical validation is a notable gap in a paper claiming to address a practical learning problem.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The notation is consistently defined, and the main results are well-explained. The example section helps make the theory more accessible.

**Originality**: The concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" is novel and represents a significant departure from previous tractability approaches (treewidth, tree-structured models). The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work.

**Significance**: The theoretical results advance our understanding of when MCMC-based learning is tractable. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of empirical validation and the conservative theoretical bounds. The results provide valuable theoretical guidance for practitioners but don't yet demonstrate a practical advantage over existing methods.

## Recommendation
The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding the conditions under which MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning is tractable. The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation, which is particularly notable given the paper's focus on practical learning. With the addition of a small set of experiments demonstrating practical feasibility and a more detailed analysis of projection complexity, this would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by adding empirical validation and more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations. The theoretical contribution is strong, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by bridging the gap between theory and practice.
