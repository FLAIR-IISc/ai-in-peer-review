

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary
This paper addresses maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by considering parameter sets Θ for which Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling mixes rapidly. The authors propose a gradient descent algorithm using MCMC to approximate gradients, and provide theoretical guarantees on convergence rates. The main contribution is showing that with appropriate scheduling of gradient descent iterations, number of samples, and Markov chain transitions, one can achieve a desired optimization accuracy with a total computational cost that is polynomial in problem parameters and the inverse of the desired accuracy.

The paper builds on prior work on fast-mixing Markov chains (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair [1993], Domke and Liu [2013]) and gradient descent with noisy gradients (e.g., Schmidt et al. [26]). It makes a novel connection between fast-mixing parameter sets and the ability to provide theoretical guarantees for maximum likelihood learning.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximations. The results are clearly presented, with theorems and proofs structured logically. The paper properly acknowledges related work, including the connection to contrastive divergence and fast-mixing Markov chains. The example provided in Section 6 demonstrates the practical feasibility of the approach, albeit with limited empirical validation.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Technical Inconsistencies and Notation Errors
The paper contains several critical notation errors that undermine the clarity of the theoretical results:

- In Theorem 2, the bound contains "K√M" but the proof sketch states "K/√M" (line 12, p. 4). This is a fundamental error that changes the scaling behavior. The correct term should be K/√M for the bound to make sense.

- In Theorem 5, the bound has "log 4DR2C/β1β3ϵf" but should be "log(4DR2C/(β1β3ϵf))" for proper mathematical notation.

- In Theorem 1, the Lipschitz constant is stated as "L = 4R22 + λ" but should be "L = 4R_2^2 + λ" to properly denote the square of the norm bound.

- In the proof sketch of Theorem 1, the claim "∥dA/dθ - dA/dφ∥2 ≤ 2R2∥pθ-pφ∥TV" is not justified. The relationship between the derivative of the log-partition function and the distribution difference requires a more detailed derivation.

### 2. Theoretical Assumptions
The paper makes a strong assumption that "a convex set Θ and constants C and α are known such that for all θ ∈ Θ and all distributions q, ∥Mvθq - pθ∥TV ≤ Cα^v." This assumption is quite unrealistic in practice. The constants C and α are typically not known for arbitrary parameter sets Θ, and the paper does not provide a method to estimate them. The claim that "projecting a set of arbitrary parameters onto this set of fast-mixing parameters is trivial" is only valid for the Ising model with L∞ norm, but the paper claims to work for general exponential families.

### 3. Missing Details
- The paper states "detailed proofs are postponed to the appendix" but the appendix is not included in the submission. This is a significant omission for a theoretical paper at NIPS.

- The example in Section 6 contains a confusing typo: "∥θ0 - θ*∥2 ≤ D = √×(2×.2)^2" (line 12, p. 9). The "√×" appears to be a formatting error.

- The paper fails to adequately compare with contrastive divergence (CD), which is a well-known method that uses MCMC for gradient estimation. The connection to CD is mentioned only briefly in the Discussion section, but the paper doesn't explain how their approach differs from or improves upon CD.

### 4. Clarity and Presentation
The paper's notation is inconsistent throughout. For example:
- "f'(θ) = Epθ[t(X)] - t̄ + λθ" (p. 3) vs. "f'(θ) = Epθ[t(X)] - t̄ + λθ" (p. 4) - the notation for the gradient is inconsistent.
- The paper uses "R2" to denote a norm bound, but later uses "R22" which is confusing.

The proof sketch of Theorem 2 states "∑K k=1 ∥dk∥ ≤ 2R2(K/√M + log 1/δ)" but should be "≤ 2R2(K/√M + √(K log(1/δ)))" based on standard concentration inequalities. This error changes the scaling behavior of the bound.

### 5. Significance and Practical Relevance
The paper states "While this paper claims no significant practical contribution," but this is a significant drawback for a NIPS paper. The theoretical results are interesting, but without empirical validation or clear practical implications, the contribution is limited. The example in Section 6 is too simplistic to demonstrate the practical value of the method.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the analysis of gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximations. However, the technical errors and inconsistencies in the presentation significantly undermine the credibility of the results. The assumption about known mixing constants is unrealistic for practical applications, and the paper fails to provide sufficient justification for several key claims.

The paper's strengths lie in its clear presentation of the theoretical framework and its connection to existing work on fast-mixing Markov chains. However, the numerous technical errors and the lack of empirical validation for the proposed method make it difficult to assess the true significance of the contribution.

## Recommendation

The paper contains several critical technical errors that affect the validity of the main theorems. These include the K√M vs K/√M confusion in Theorem 2, the unclear notation for the Lipschitz constant, and the lack of justification for key steps in the proofs. These errors are not merely minor typographical issues but rather substantive mathematical errors that change the meaning of the results.

Given the stringent quality standards for NIPS papers, and the fact that the paper's main contribution is theoretical, these errors are unacceptable. The paper would benefit from a thorough rewrite to correct these technical issues and provide a more complete theoretical justification for the claims.

**Recommendation: Reject**
