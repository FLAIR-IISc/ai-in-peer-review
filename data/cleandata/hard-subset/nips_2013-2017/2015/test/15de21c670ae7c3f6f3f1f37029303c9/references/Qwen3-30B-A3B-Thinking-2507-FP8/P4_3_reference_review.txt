

# Review

## Summary
This paper addresses the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by introducing the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" Θ, where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling mixes rapidly. The authors establish that when parameters are constrained to such a set, there exists a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning. This differs from prior approaches like tree-structured models (Chow-Liu), pseudolikelihood, or score matching, which either assume well-specified models or yield distributions where inference remains intractable. The paper analyzes gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation, providing theoretical guarantees for convergence when the Markov chain mixes rapidly. The results apply broadly to exponential families, not just graphical models, and are supported by rigorous bounds on computational complexity.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a complete theoretical analysis with well-motivated proof sketches and rigorous bounds on the computational complexity of achieving desired optimization accuracy. The authors carefully distinguish between convex and strongly convex cases, providing separate convergence guarantees. The mixing time assumptions are well-grounded in existing MCMC literature (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair [1993], Liu and Domke [2014]).

2. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The notation is consistently defined, and the main results are presented in a logical sequence from problem setup to theoretical guarantees. The empirical example (Section 6) effectively illustrates the theoretical results with concrete parameter choices.

3. **Originality**: The concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" represents a novel approach to tractable maximum likelihood learning. While previous work has focused on specific tractable model classes (trees, high-girth models), this paper establishes a general framework applicable to any exponential family where MCMC mixes rapidly. The distinction from prior methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.) is clearly articulated.

4. **Significance**: The results provide a theoretical foundation for a practical approach to maximum likelihood learning in models where exact inference is intractable but fast-mixing MCMC is available. The paper establishes a precise relationship between computational effort and optimization accuracy, which is valuable for understanding the trade-offs in practical applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Practical Demonstration**: The paper is purely theoretical, with the empirical example (Section 6) serving only as a proof of concept rather than a comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm's performance on real-world datasets. The paper acknowledges this limitation but provides no discussion of computational bottlenecks or practical implementation challenges.

2. **Abstract Nature of Fast-Mixing Sets**: While the paper defines fast-mixing parameter sets generally, it offers limited concrete examples beyond the Ising model. The practical applicability of the framework depends on identifying such sets for specific model classes, which the paper does not elaborate on.

3. **Projection Complexity**: The paper assumes that projection onto Θ can be efficiently performed, but it does not analyze the computational complexity of this projection step, which could be significant for large models. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation but do not provide guidance on when projection would be feasible.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the computational complexity of maximum likelihood learning in exponential families.
- The concept of fast-mixing parameter sets is novel and provides a general framework that extends beyond previous tractable model classes.
- The analysis is rigorous, with explicit bounds on computational complexity that are valuable for practitioners.
- The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and carefully situates itself within the existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The purely theoretical nature of the contribution limits its immediate practical impact without empirical validation on meaningful datasets.
- The paper does not provide sufficient guidance on how to identify fast-mixing parameter sets for practical model classes beyond the Ising model example.
- The assumption of efficient projection onto Θ is potentially restrictive for large-scale applications.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models and maximum likelihood learning. The concept of fast-mixing parameter sets is novel, and the rigorous analysis of gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation provides valuable theoretical insights. While the paper lacks empirical validation on real-world datasets, its theoretical soundness, clarity, and originality make it a significant contribution to the field. The limitations noted are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the strength of the theoretical results.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel theoretical approach that advances our understanding of computational trade-offs in maximum likelihood learning. The results are clearly distinguished from previous work, and the theoretical analysis is complete and rigorous.
