

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is generally intractable due to the computational difficulty of evaluating the partition function. The authors propose a novel approach that restricts the parameter space to a "fast-mixing" set Θ, where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The key contribution is showing that when parameters are constrained to such a set, there exists a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning inside Θ.

This work differs significantly from previous approaches like the Chow-Liu algorithm (which restricts to tree-structured models) or methods like pseudolikelihood and score matching (which assume well-specified models). The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximations converges to the maximum likelihood solution within Θ with theoretical guarantees. They derive explicit schedules for the number of gradient descent steps, samples, and Markov chain transitions to achieve a desired accuracy.

The paper builds on foundational work by Jerrum and Sinclair [1993] on the intractability of partition function evaluation, and connects to recent advances in fast-mixing MCMC (e.g., Domke and Liu [2013], Liu and Domke [2014]). It also relates to the growing body of work on MCMC-based learning approaches (e.g., Steinhardt and Liang [28]) but provides the first theoretical guarantees for this approach under fast-mixing conditions.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide clear definitions, well-motivated assumptions, and complete theoretical analysis for their main results. The proofs are rigorous and build on established results in optimization (Schmidt et al., 2011) and MCMC mixing times (Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993).

The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis. The authors provide concrete bounds on the computational complexity (KMv) for achieving a given accuracy ϵ, which is a significant contribution. They carefully distinguish between the convex and strongly convex cases, which is important for understanding the theoretical guarantees.

The paper is complete as a theoretical work rather than a position paper. The authors acknowledge limitations, including the computational cost of projection and the fact that their analysis doesn't cover the "pool of samples" strategy common in contrastive divergence. They also provide a small example to illustrate their theory.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The notation is introduced consistently, and the main results are presented with appropriate theorems. The proof sketches provide good intuition for the main results without overwhelming the reader with technical details.

The paper would benefit from a few minor improvements:
- The definition of "v" (number of Markov chain transitions) is sometimes used as a variable in the mixing time bound (Equation 2), which could cause confusion with its usage as the number of transitions.
- The relationship between theoretical bounds and practical performance could be clarified with more discussion of the constants involved (C, α, R2).
- A brief comparison between the theoretical convergence rates and empirical performance would strengthen the example in Section 6.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by connecting fast-mixing parameter sets to tractable maximum likelihood learning. While there is related work on fast-mixing MCMC (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair [1993]; Domke and Liu [2013]), this is the first to establish a direct connection to maximum likelihood learning with theoretical guarantees.

The approach differs substantially from previous work in several important ways:
1. Instead of restricting to low-treewidth models (as in Chow-Liu), they focus on fast-mixing parameter sets
2. They provide convergence guarantees to the M-projection even when the model is misspecified
3. They establish the existence of a FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning in this setting

The paper thoroughly references related work, including previous approaches to tractable learning (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods) and fast-mixing results for MCMC. The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous contributions, particularly in the introduction.

## Significance

This work is significant for several reasons:

1. It provides a theoretically grounded approach to maximum likelihood learning that is tractable and guarantees convergence to the M-projection even when the model is misspecified. This addresses key limitations of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.).

2. It establishes a connection between fast-mixing parameter sets and tractable learning that could inspire future work on practical implementations.

3. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a unified theoretical framework for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning.

While the computational cost (O(1/ϵ³) for convex case) is still high, the theoretical foundation is important for the field. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a rigorous theoretical analysis where previous MCMC-based approaches lacked guarantees.

## Strengths

- Novel theoretical contribution connecting fast-mixing parameter sets to tractable maximum likelihood learning
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees
- Addresses important limitations of existing methods (misspecification, intractable inference)
- Well-organized and clearly written
- Provides concrete bounds on computational complexity
- The example provides a concrete illustration of the theory

## Weaknesses

- The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical validation
- The computational cost (O(1/ϵ³) for convex case) is still quite high
- The projection step is not analyzed in depth, though the authors acknowledge this as a limitation
- The analysis doesn't cover the "pool of samples" strategy used in contrastive divergence, which is common in practice
- The relationship between theoretical bounds and practical performance could be better explained

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a fundamental problem in the field. While it's purely theoretical with no empirical results, this is a common pattern in theoretical machine learning papers at NIPS, and the theoretical contribution is substantial.

The main reason I recommend acceptance is that this paper provides a novel theoretical framework that could inspire future work on practical implementations of MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning. The connection between fast-mixing parameter sets and tractable learning is particularly valuable for the field.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the constants in your theoretical bounds (C, α, R2) relate to practical implementations. This would help readers understand the practical implications of your theoretical results.

2. The paper mentions that "the existence of a FPRAS is established by analyzing a common existing strategy for maximum likelihood learning of exponential families, namely gradient descent where MCMC is used to generate samples and approximate the gradient." It would be helpful to clarify how this differs from previous work that has used MCMC-based gradient descent without theoretical guarantees (e.g., Geyer [1991], Carreira-Perpiñán and Hinton [2005]).

3. The example in Section 6 is helpful, but could be strengthened with a discussion of the practical trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost. Specifically, it would be valuable to discuss how the theoretical bounds compare to the actual performance in the example.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how your approach might extend to models with hidden variables, which is a common application of MCMC-based learning.

5. The paper states that "the total number of Markov transitions applied through the entire algorithm, KMv grows as (1/ϵf)³ log(1/ϵf) for the convex case." It would be helpful to clarify whether this is the minimal possible complexity or whether there could be improvements.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models. The authors establish a novel connection between fast-mixing parameter sets and tractable learning, providing rigorous convergence guarantees. While the paper is purely theoretical, the theoretical foundation is significant and could inspire future work on practical implementations. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in the field. I recommend acceptance.
