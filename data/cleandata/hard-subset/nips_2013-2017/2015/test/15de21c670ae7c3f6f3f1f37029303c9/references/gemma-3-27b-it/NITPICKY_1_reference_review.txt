This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in constrained parameter spaces designed for efficient MCMC sampling. The core idea is that if a parameter set Θ allows for fast mixing MCMC, a FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution within Θ exists. This is framed within the broader context of limitations of common alternatives like pseudolikelihood and score matching, particularly their performance when the model is misspecified. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for both regularized and unregularized cases, linking convergence rates to mixing times and gradient Lipschitz constants.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical rigor and its attempt to bridge the gap between MCMC convergence guarantees and optimization performance. The authors correctly identify the practical and theoretical limitations of existing methods. The theorems are well-stated, and the sketched proofs (detailed proofs are in the appendix) provide reasonable intuition. The inclusion of explicit schedules for parameters (K, M, v) and a lower bound on the total computational effort are valuable. The example application to the Ising model, while simple, helps contextualize the results. 

However, despite the theoretical contribution, a significant number of issues detract from the overall quality of the paper. A primary concern is the level of detail and, at times, sloppiness in presentation. 

1. **Notation and Definitions:** The notation is inconsistent and unnecessarily dense. While a list of symbols is provided, several are redefined or used interchangeably without clear indication. For example, 'r' is introduced as an arbitrary starting distribution, but later seems to refer to a specific initialization strategy. Similarly, the meaning of 'v' shifts between the number of Markov chain transitions *per sample* and the total number of transitions. These ambiguities complicate understanding.

2. **Assumptions:** The core assumption of "fast mixing" (Equation 2) is stated without sufficient justification for its broad applicability. While the paper cites relevant literature on mixing times, it glosses over the challenges of verifying this condition in practical scenarios. The constant C and α are presented as “known,” but the paper does not address how these constants would be estimated or determined in real-world applications. The examples provided—Ising model—are relatively well-studied, but generalizing to more complex models is not adequately discussed.

3. **Lipschitz Constant:** Theorem 1 states the gradient is L-Lipschitz with L = 4R²² + λ. However, R is defined as the norm of the sufficient statistics, meaning R² is related to the dimensionality of the feature space, not necessarily a constraint on the function itself. This definition of L seems unusually high and potentially weakens the convergence guarantees unnecessarily. The subsequent numerical example utilizes L=10 instead, suggesting the authors recognize this issue but don't address it theoretically.

4. **Proof Sketches:** The “proof sketches” provided lack sufficient detail to fully assess the validity of the results. They often rely on hand-waving arguments or cite results without providing enough context or explanation. For instance, the transition from bounding the errors in each step to the overall convergence guarantee in Theorem 2 feels rushed.

5. **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The paper contains significant redundancy, particularly in the background sections. The discussion of mixing times and related works is overly lengthy and could be condensed. The repetition of similar arguments across theorems and proofs adds to the feeling of unnecessary verbosity.

6. **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the text detract from the paper’s credibility. Examples include typos, incorrect references (e.g., [19] refers to a 1993 paper and then to a mixing time theorem), and inconsistencies in mathematical notation. The presentation of Corollary 9 feels somewhat tacked on and lacks clear justification for the chosen parameters.

7. **Practical Impact:** Despite the theoretical guarantees, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate the practical relevance of the proposed approach. The numerical example, while illustrating the correctness of the theoretical bounds, is limited in scope and doesn’t provide insight into the computational costs or performance compared to existing methods on more challenging datasets. The cost of projecting onto Θ could outweigh the benefits of faster mixing.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for analyzing maximum likelihood learning in constrained parameter spaces.
*   The existence of a FPRAS guarantee is a significant result.
*   The inclusion of explicit parameter schedules is valuable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from numerous presentation flaws (inconsistent notation, verbose writing, minor errors).
*   The assumption of fast mixing is not adequately justified for general applications.
*   The theoretical results may be overly conservative due to the definition of the Lipschitz constant.
*   The practical impact of the proposed approach is not convincingly demonstrated.
*   The level of detail in the proofs is insufficient for thorough assessment.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the core idea of the paper is interesting and the theoretical results are potentially valuable, the numerous flaws in presentation, the lack of practical demonstration, and the questionable applicability of the fast-mixing assumption outweigh the positive contributions. A significant revision addressing these issues would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper, in its current state, does not meet the standards of quality, clarity, and significance expected of a NIPS publication.
