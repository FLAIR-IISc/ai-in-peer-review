- Paper studies ML estimation in discrete exponential families with θ constrained to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ and analyzes projected gradient descent using MCMC-approximated gradients.  
- Provides high-probability convergence rates in two regimes: convex (λ=0) for objective suboptimality and strongly convex (λ>0) for parameter error.  
- Derives schedules for iterations K, samples per iteration M, and Markov steps v that give polynomial total work KMv in 1/ε and other problem parameters, framed as an additive randomized polynomial-time approximation.  
- Connects abstract results to concrete fast-mixing conditions for Ising/MRFs (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral-norm) where projection is convex; includes a small Ising example showing conservatism of schedules.  
- Positions work relative to pseudolikelihood/score matching/composite likelihood and prior learning-under-fast-mixing literature, and builds on inexact/proximal gradient and mixing-time analyses.  
- Strength: clear, general framework (bounded sufficient statistics, convex fast-mixing Θ, projection oracle) with broadly applicable guarantees.  
- Strength: technically sound decomposition of gradient error into sampling error and finite-mixing bias, controlled by concentration and TV bounds.  
- Strength: provides concrete instantiations of fast-mixing conditions and connects them to efficient projection for Ising/MRFs.  
- Strength: practical takeaways with explicit schedules and total-work bounds and discussion of limitations like pooling samples and projection cost.  
- Strength: conceptual significance—enforcing fast mixing makes constrained ML computationally tractable with polynomial guarantees even for arbitrary treewidth.  
- Weakness: requires known uniform geometric-mixing constants (C, α) over Θ, which are rarely tight or known in high-dimensional models, limiting practical guidance.  
- Weakness: treats M samples per iteration as independent (implying M independent chains), excluding common single-chain or persistent-pool practices and reducing practical applicability.  
- Weakness: Lipschitz constant L in theorem is loose; a tighter proof via covariance gives L ≤ R^2 + λ, and the TV-based Lipschitz step is nonstandard and needs justification.  
- Weakness: “FPRAS” terminology is misleading—results are additive and depend on problem-specific constants and a projection oracle; should be rephrased as randomized polynomial-time additive ε-approximation.  
- Weakness: generality claim limited by practicality of defining Θ and projecting for other models/samplers (e.g., Swendsen–Wang, ferromagnetic constraints).  
- Weakness: limited empirical validation—only a toy 4×4 Ising example; lacks comparisons to pseudolikelihood/CD or demonstration that fast-mixing constraints yield useful models.  
- Question: provide a self-contained proof of Theorem 1 via the covariance bound or justify the TV-parameter Lipschitz inequality.  
- Question: are (C, α) assumed known to the algorithm; if not, can a doubling scheme for v and M with stopping criteria achieve comparable guarantees up to log factors?  
- Question: can concentration analysis be adapted to dependent samples (single long chain per iteration) using Markov-chain concentration inequalities to allow pooling?  
- Question: beyond Ising/Dobrushin, which other families (e.g., Potts, higher-order factors) admit practical projection onto a mixing-certified Θ and guidance for Swendsen–Wang-type guarantees?  
- Question: comment on statistical vs optimization error—sample complexity and how constrained MLE compares to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood under misspecification.  
- Minor: clarify notation R2 and use R := supx ||t(x)||2 with L ≤ R^2 + λ to avoid confusion.  
- Minor: note that practical L used (e.g., 10) is much smaller than bound (97) and that backtracking could remove preset L.  
- Minor: “arbitrary treewidth” in title can mislead—tractability stems from fast mixing, not direct handling of large treewidth.  
- Pros: clean theoretical framework, explicit complexity, and solid convex/strongly convex analyses with interpretable schedules.  
- Cons: strong assumptions on known mixing constants and sample independence, some technical constants can be tightened, and practical impact unclear due to limited experiments.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept, contingent on clarifying Theorem 1, refining the “FPRAS” wording, and adding an adaptive strategy for unknown mixing constants or more empirical validation.
