### Summary

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood (ML) learning in general undirected graphical models, or more broadly, in exponential families. Instead of relying on structural constraints like low treewidth to ensure tractability, the authors propose a different notion: constraining the model parameters `θ` to lie within a convex set `Θ` where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to be fast-mixing. The central contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a common learning algorithm: projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The paper proves that this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the ML estimate within the set `Θ`.

The analysis is presented for two settings: the general convex case (e.g., unregularized ML) and the strongly convex case (e.g., L2-regularized ML). For both, the authors derive explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps `K`, MCMC samples per step `M`, and MCMC transitions per sample `v`, required to achieve a desired accuracy `ϵ` with high probability. The total computational complexity is shown to be polynomial in the problem parameters and `1/ϵ`. This work connects to a long line of research on using MCMC for learning intractable models [10, 29] but distinguishes itself by providing formal convergence guarantees, which are often absent in prior work that relies on heuristics like contrastive divergence [5]. It also provides a valuable alternative to other approximate learning methods like pseudolikelihood [3] or score matching [16], which do not generally converge to the M-projection (the best KL-divergence approximation) when the model is misspecified.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis that builds upon established results in optimization theory (specifically, convergence of inexact gradient methods [26]) and concentration inequalities. The proof sketches provided in the main text are intuitive and follow a clear logical path, bounding the two sources of error in the gradient estimate—sampling noise and MCMC bias—and then incorporating these bounds into a standard convergence proof for gradient descent. The authors are careful to state all assumptions clearly, such as the convexity of the constraint set `Θ`, the geometric mixing rate of the MCMC chain, and the existence of an efficient projection operator. The paper presents a complete theoretical result, from problem formulation to complexity analysis. The authors also honestly discuss the limitations of their work in Section 7, such as the potential cost of the projection step and the fact that the analysis does not cover common practical heuristics like using persistent chains.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and context, clearly positioning the work relative to prior art. The notation is defined systematically in Section 2, which aids readability. The main results are stated precisely as theorems, making the contributions easy to identify and understand. The structure flows logically from background and setup to the core theoretical results and a concluding discussion. The inclusion of an illustrative example in Section 6, while not a practical contribution, is helpful for grounding the abstract theoretical bounds. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to grasp the methodology and its implications.

**Originality:** While the algorithm under analysis (MCMC-based gradient descent) is not new, the theoretical contribution is highly original. The key novelty lies in several aspects:
1.  It formalizes the notion of tractability based on fast MCMC mixing, providing a compelling alternative to structural constraints like treewidth.
2.  It provides, to my knowledge, the first end-to-end FPRAS guarantee for ML learning within this framework, rigorously quantifying the trade-offs between optimization parameters (`K`, `M`, `v`) and the final approximation accuracy.
3.  The specific combination of mixing time theory, concentration inequalities, and inexact optimization analysis to provide concrete complexity bounds for this learning problem is a novel and significant contribution.
The paper does an excellent job of citing related work and clearly articulating how its contributions differ from and advance upon previous research.

**Significance:** The results presented are of significant theoretical importance. They provide a solid mathematical foundation for a class of algorithms that are widely used in practice but often lack formal guarantees. This work helps bridge the gap between machine learning theory and practice by showing that under well-defined conditions (fast-mixing), these practical methods can provably converge to the correct solution. The perspective of defining tractable model families via dynamic properties (mixing times) rather than static ones (graph structure) is a powerful idea that could inspire further research. While the derived hyperparameter schedules are likely too conservative for direct practical application (as suggested by the authors' own experiment), they provide crucial insight into the asymptotic scaling and dependencies of the algorithm, which can guide heuristic choices in practice. This paper advances the state-of-the-art in our understanding of learning in intractable probabilistic models.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first formal FPRAS guarantee for a widely used but poorly understood class of learning algorithms, a significant result for the community.
*   **Novelty and Perspective:** The framing of tractability in terms of fast-mixing parameter sets is an original and insightful contribution that opens new avenues for research.
*   **High Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound, exceptionally well-written, and easy to follow.
*   **Broad Significance:** The results are relevant to anyone working on learning for intractable probabilistic models, a core area of research in machine learning.

### Arguments Against Acceptance
*   **Limited Direct Practical Impact:** The derived schedules for `K`, `M`, and `v` are likely too conservative for practical use, making the contribution primarily theoretical.
*   **Strength of Assumptions:** The analysis depends on having a known convex set `Θ` with uniform mixing guarantees and an efficient projection oracle, which may not be available for all models of interest.

In summary, the strengths of this paper, particularly its theoretical novelty and significance, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It provides a rigorous foundation for a fundamental machine learning problem.
