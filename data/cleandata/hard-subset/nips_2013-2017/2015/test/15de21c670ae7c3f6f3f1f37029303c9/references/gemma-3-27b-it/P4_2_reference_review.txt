This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in exponential families, focusing on scenarios where direct optimization is intractable. The core idea is to restrict the parameter space to a set Θ that allows for fast mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling, thereby enabling a fast-mixing parameter set. This guarantees a Finite-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The analysis centers on gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, providing convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in statistical inference – efficient maximum likelihood estimation in complex models. The connection to graphical models, while present, is appropriately generalized to the broader exponential family framework. The work builds upon existing methods like pseudolikelihood and score matching, highlighting their limitations and proposing a more theoretically grounded approach. The focus on fast-mixing parameter sets is a novel and promising direction. The theoretical results—FPRAS guarantees and convergence rates for gradient descent—are substantial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets to guarantee FPRAS for maximum likelihood estimation is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence rates for gradient descent under MCMC approximation, addressing both convex and strongly convex scenarios.
*   **Generality:** The framework extends beyond graphical models, applicable to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The mathematical notation is generally clear and well-defined, though dense. The organization is logical, progressing from setup and background to main results.
*   **Detailed Background:** A comprehensive review of relevant literature on mixing times and related algorithms is provided, demonstrating awareness of existing work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Relevance:** While theoretically strong, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of the practical challenges of finding and projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ. The example provided is limited and doesn't fully address this concern. The choice of parameters (e.g., choosing the right Lipschitz constant) could be problematic in practice.
*   **Computational Complexity of Projection:** The analysis doesn’t deeply consider the computational cost of projecting onto Θ. The complexity of this projection step can be significant and may negate the benefits of fast mixing, especially for high-dimensional models. References [12, 9, 20] highlight the computational burden of projection.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of a known mixing rate (Equation 2) might be unrealistic in many scenarios. Estimating or bounding this rate can be difficult.
*   **Tightness of Bounds:** The bounds on the computational effort (KMv) are expressed in terms of parameters that are not always easily estimable in practice. While Theorems 7 and 8 strive for tighter bounds, the constant factors and logarithmic terms might still hide practical limitations.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** The discussion on initialization (contrastive divergence) is brief and doesn’t fully explore its impact on convergence.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical framework with strong guarantees for maximum likelihood learning in a challenging setting.
*   The results are significant as they provide a rigorous justification for using MCMC-based gradient descent in restricted parameter spaces.
*   The generality of the framework expands the applicability of the proposed approach beyond graphical models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical implications of the work are not fully explored, and the computational challenges of projection and mixing rate estimation are not adequately addressed.
*   The theoretical results rely on assumptions that might be difficult to satisfy in real-world applications.
*   The complexity analysis, while present, does not provide concrete guidance for choosing parameters in practice.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference. The novelty of the approach and the rigor of the analysis are commendable. However, the lack of detailed discussion on practical considerations and potential computational bottlenecks raises concerns about its immediate applicability.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should expand the discussion on the practical challenges of finding and projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of projection, along with examples demonstrating its feasibility in different scenarios, would significantly strengthen the paper. A more thorough exploration of initialization strategies and their impact on convergence would also be valuable.
