This paper addresses the problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, circumventing intractability by restricting parameters to a set guaranteeing fast mixing of MCMC sampling. The core idea is that if a set of parameters allows for rapid MCMC convergence, a FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution within that set exists. While the authors correctly identify limitations of existing approximate learning methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.) and the challenges of mis-specification, the presented work feels largely theoretical and lacks compelling evidence of practical benefit. The connection to graphical models feels tenuous; the results are framed as generally applicable to exponential families, diminishing the specific relevance to the NIPS audience interested in graphical models.

The paper is technically sound, with careful definitions and proofs. Theorem 1 establishes Lipschitz continuity of the gradient, and Theorems 2 & 6 provide convergence guarantees for convex and strongly convex cases respectively, building on existing gradient descent analysis. The careful analysis of computational complexity (Theorems 3, 4 & 5) is appreciated, though the constants involved (a, b, c, βi) are rather abstract and lack clear interpretation in a practical setting. However, the reliance on pre-existing results regarding mixing times (e.g., Ising models) and the somewhat hand-wavy justification for their applicability feels weak. The assumption of known constants C and α governing mixing rates is a substantial limitation; obtaining these in practice can be as hard as the original learning problem. 

The clarity of the writing is moderate. The notation is dense, and while a list of symbols is provided, constant referencing back to it is necessary. The proof sketches are helpful but require significant familiarity with optimization theory to fully grasp. The example in Section 6, while intended to illustrate the theoretical results, feels contrived and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the practical viability of the approach. The claim that the algorithm finds a solution “tighter than the specified ϵθ” is vague; a quantitative comparison to other methods would be far more informative.

The originality of the work is questionable. The idea of restricting parameters to tractable sets is not new (Chow-Liu algorithm, high-girth models). The core contribution—establishing a FPRAS under fast mixing conditions—feels incremental. The paper essentially states that *if* you can efficiently sample from the constrained parameter space, *then* you can efficiently learn the maximum likelihood parameters within that space. This connection, while formally correct, doesn't represent a breakthrough. The framing shifts the difficulty from learning to ensuring fast mixing, which is itself often a challenging problem.

The significance of the work is limited. While the theoretical results are correct, the practical impact is unclear. The paper doesn’t address crucial aspects of real-world applications, such as how to choose an appropriate constraint set Θ, how to verify the fast-mixing assumption, or how to efficiently project parameters onto Θ for complex models. The paper’s focus on asymptotic guarantees and abstract complexity bounds, without concrete empirical validation, further diminishes its impact. The authors acknowledge this weakness themselves in the conclusion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound analysis with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Identifies a connection between fast mixing and FPRAS for ML learning.
*   Careful analysis of computational complexity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks compelling practical demonstration or empirical evaluation.
*   Relies on strong assumptions (known mixing rates) that are difficult to verify.
*   The core idea is incremental and doesn’t represent a significant advance.
*   The connection to graphical models is weak; the results are more general, but less impactful for the NIPS audience.
*   The example feels contrived and doesn’t offer convincing evidence of utility.



**Overall assessment:** Reject. The paper presents a technically correct but ultimately unconvincing argument. The theoretical results are interesting, but the lack of practical validation and the strong assumptions significantly limit the work's impact. It feels more like a theoretical exploration than a contribution ready for a venue like NIPS. Further work is needed to address the practical challenges and demonstrate the benefits of this approach on realistic datasets and models.
