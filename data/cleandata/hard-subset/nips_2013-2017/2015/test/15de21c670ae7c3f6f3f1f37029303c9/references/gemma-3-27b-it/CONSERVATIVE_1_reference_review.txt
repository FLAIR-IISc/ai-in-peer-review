This paper addresses the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, proposing a framework based on fast-mixing parameter sets to establish a finite-sample complexity guarantee. The core idea – leveraging the efficiency of MCMC within a constrained parameter space – is conceptually interesting. However, despite the theoretical result establishing a FPRAS, the practical relevance and significance of this work are questionable, and the presentation suffers from a lack of concrete examples demonstrating the practical benefits over existing approaches.

The paper correctly identifies the intractability of maximum likelihood estimation in general graphical models and the limitations of common alternatives like pseudolikelihood and score matching, particularly when the model is misspecified. The focus on restricting the parameter space to achieve tractability is a reasonable direction, but the proposed approach feels somewhat orthogonal to more established methods like Chow-Liu and high-girth models, which are not thoroughly compared. The theoretical contribution – demonstrating a FPRAS under the fast-mixing assumption – is mathematically sound, but relies heavily on the assumption of a pre-defined, fast-mixing parameter set. The authors acknowledge this, providing the Ising model as an example, but fail to convincingly demonstrate how to *construct* such sets for more complex or arbitrary graphical models. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical rigor. The proofs appear careful and well-structured, with a clear outline of the steps involved. The analysis of gradient descent with approximate gradients is a valuable contribution, and the theorems establishing convergence rates are well-defined. The detailed breakdown of the computational complexity and the lower bounds on the number of samples and iterations are also appreciated. 

However, several weaknesses undermine the overall impact. The most significant is the lack of compelling evidence for the practical utility of this approach. The example provided (Ising model) is already well-studied, and the benefit of this framework over existing techniques is not clearly demonstrated. The example feels more like a validation of the theory on a known case than a demonstration of broader applicability. The discussion of projection onto the fast-mixing set is also superficial; while algorithms exist for certain models (Ising), extending this to arbitrary models is a significant challenge not adequately addressed. 

Further, the presentation is dense and somewhat inaccessible. The notation is heavy, and the motivation for certain choices (e.g., the regularization term) is not always clear. While detailed proofs are relegated to the appendix, the main text could benefit from more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples. The extensive references to mixing time results in the literature, while comprehensive, feel somewhat disconnected from the core argument; the reader is left wondering how these results are *actually used* to construct practical fast-mixing sets. The claim that the analysis has no significant practical contribution, made in the conclusions, is a rather damning self-assessment.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a mathematically sound theoretical result establishing a FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning in a constrained parameter space.
*   The analysis of gradient descent with approximate gradients is a valuable contribution.
*   The convergence rate analysis provides useful insights into the trade-offs between accuracy, sample complexity, and computational cost.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The practical relevance of the approach is questionable. The paper lacks compelling evidence demonstrating its advantages over existing methods.
*   The construction of fast-mixing parameter sets for arbitrary graphical models is a significant challenge not adequately addressed.
*   The presentation is dense and inaccessible, hindering understanding and broader impact.
*   The self-assessment of limited practical contribution weakens the justification for publication.



**Overall assessment:** Reject. While the paper demonstrates a rigorous theoretical analysis, it falls short of making a significant contribution to the field. The lack of practical validation and the challenges associated with constructing fast-mixing parameter sets outweigh the theoretical benefits. The paper feels more like a mathematical exercise than a solution to a real-world problem. The authors need to provide more concrete evidence of the practical utility of their approach or significantly expand the discussion of how to construct fast-mixing sets for a wider range of models before this work would be suitable for NIPS.
