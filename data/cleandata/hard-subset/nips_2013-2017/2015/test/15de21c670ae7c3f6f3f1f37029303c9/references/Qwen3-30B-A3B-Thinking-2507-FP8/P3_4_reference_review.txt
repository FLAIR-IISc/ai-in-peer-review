

# Peer Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is intractable due to the #P-completeness of partition function computation. The authors propose a novel approach by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing parameter sets" where MCMC sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. Their key insight is that for such parameter sets, gradient descent using MCMC-based gradient estimates will converge to the maximum likelihood solution within the constrained set with theoretical guarantees. This differs from prior tractable approaches like tree-structured models (Chow-Liu) or pseudolikelihood methods, which either require specific model structures or lack guarantees for mis-specified models.

The paper establishes rigorous convergence guarantees for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases, showing that the total computational effort scales as O(1/ϵ³ log(1/ϵ)) for convex and O(1/ϵ² log(1/ϵ)) for strongly convex problems. The authors connect their work to the exponential family framework, demonstrating the generality of their approach beyond specific graphical models.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper makes a technically sound contribution with well-justified assumptions and rigorous proofs. The Lipschitz gradient bound (Theorem 1) is particularly elegant and well-motivated. The convergence analysis carefully handles the dual sources of error: MCMC approximation and gradient estimation.

2. **Novel Perspective**: The focus on "fast-mixing parameter sets" rather than structural constraints (like treewidth) is conceptually fresh. It shifts the focus from model structure to computational tractability of inference, which is a more direct path to practical implementation.

3. **Clear Theoretical Bounds**: The paper provides precise computational complexity bounds that are both theoretically interesting and practically informative. The explicit parameter schedules (Theorems 4, 5, 8, 9) help practitioners understand how to implement the approach.

4. **Comprehensive Related Work**: The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contribution against prior work on tractable learning methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, tree-structured models) and fast-mixing MCMC results, demonstrating a deep understanding of the field.

## Weaknesses and Limitations

1. **Practical Implementation Concerns**: While the theoretical bounds are impressive, the paper doesn't address key practical implementation challenges. The required number of MCMC steps (v) in their example (v=561 for a 4x4 Ising model) is significantly higher than typical practice (e.g., contrastive divergence uses v=1). This suggests the theoretical bounds may be conservative for real-world applications.

2. **Projection Complexity**: The paper acknowledges that the complexity of the projection operation (onto Θ) is ignored in the complexity analysis. For large models, projection could become computationally expensive, potentially negating the benefits of the fast-mixing approach. This is a significant limitation for large-scale applications.

3. **Initialization Strategy**: The analysis assumes arbitrary initialization of MCMC chains, but in practice, initializing from the empirical distribution (as in contrastive divergence) could substantially reduce the required mixing time. The paper doesn't explore how this would affect the theoretical bounds.

4. **Limited Empirical Validation**: The paper provides a single synthetic example (Section 6), but no comprehensive experiments comparing to existing methods. Without empirical evidence of the practical benefits (beyond the theoretical bounds), it's difficult to assess the real-world value of the approach.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding when MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning can be guaranteed to converge. The results are novel, technically sound, and address an important gap in the literature. However, the paper's practical relevance is somewhat limited by the conservative theoretical bounds and lack of comprehensive empirical validation.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a strong connection between theory and practice. The main limitations concern the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical implementation, which is common in theoretical machine learning papers but particularly relevant here given the focus on computational tractability.

## Recommendation

**Accept with minor revisions**

The paper's theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed. It addresses an important problem in graphical models with a novel perspective that advances our understanding of when MCMC-based learning can be guaranteed to work. The paper should be accepted as it makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field.

**Minor revisions needed**:
1. Expand the discussion of projection complexity and its potential impact on practical implementation
2. Add a brief discussion of how initialization strategies (like using the empirical distribution) might improve the practical applicability of the method
3. Consider adding a short comparison of the theoretical bounds to typical practical implementations (e.g., contrastive divergence) to help readers understand the gap between theory and practice

While the paper doesn't claim practical superiority over existing methods, its theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant publication at NeurIPS. The results provide a foundation for future work on MCMC-based learning that could inform both theoretical understanding and practical implementation.
