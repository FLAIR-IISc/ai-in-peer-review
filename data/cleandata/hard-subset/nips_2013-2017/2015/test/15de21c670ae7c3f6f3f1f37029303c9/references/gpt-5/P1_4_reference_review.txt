Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under an alternative tractability notion: restricting parameters to a convex set Θ for which a chosen MCMC kernel is provably fast-mixing (with known constants C, α). Assuming an oracle to project onto Θ and bounded sufficient statistics, the authors analyze projected gradient descent that uses MCMC to estimate expectations in the gradient. They provide high-probability convergence guarantees with explicit schedules for number of iterations K, number of samples per iteration M, and number of Markov steps v per sample. In the convex case (λ = 0) they obtain suboptimality guarantees of the objective with total sampling work scaling as O(ϵf^-3 polylog(1/ϵf)). In the strongly convex (regularized) case (λ > 0) they obtain parameter error guarantees with O(ϵθ^-2 polylog(1/ϵθ)) sampling work, and via standard inequalities relate these to objective suboptimality. A Lipschitz bound for the gradient is proved based on total variation perturbation and bounded statistics. The paper connects to prior work on low-treewidth and correlation-decay learning (e.g., Chow-Liu, high-girth models), MCMC-MLE, fast-mixing parameter projection for Ising/MRFs (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), and fast-mixing-by-design learning (Steinhardt & Liang). A small Ising example illustrates the prescriptions.

Strengths
- Clear and general theoretical framing: works at the level of exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not tied to specific graphical structures.
- Conceptual contribution: shows that restricting to a fast-mixing parameter set plus a projection oracle yields a randomized approximation scheme for ML within Θ, with explicit complexity in terms of mixing (C, α), Lipschitz/strong convexity, and sample sizes.
- Technically solid high-level approach: clean decomposition of error into stochastic sampling variance and MCMC bias; appropriate use of Efron–Stein/Bernstein (convex case) and Hoeffding plus union bounds (strongly convex case); application of inexact projected gradient bounds (Schmidt et al.).
- Bridges two literatures: learning under computational constraints (e.g., Wainwright’s computation-limited perspective) and rapid mixing bounds for spin systems (Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes; Jerrum–Sinclair).
- Practical handles: recognizes available Θ for Ising/MRFs via spectral/induced norm constraints and ferromagnetic regimes; discusses projection feasibility.

Weaknesses and questions
- Strength of assumptions and practical reach:
  - The approach hinges on having a convex fast-mixing set Θ with a projection oracle and known conservative mixing constants C, α. For many models these constants are either unavailable or extremely loose, making the schedules impractical. Please clarify how C, α would be set in practice beyond the specific Ising cases, and how sensitive the schedules are to overestimation.
  - The algorithm assumes independent samples per iteration via fresh restarts and v-step chains, incurring cost M·v each iteration. This is at odds with common practice (persistent chains/pools) and likely far more expensive; the paper notes this but provides no analysis. Can the analysis be extended to warm starts or sample pools using mixing from a nonstationary initialization (e.g., via Dobrushin or contractivity arguments)?
- Claims and terminology:
  - The paper states there exists an FPRAS for maximum likelihood inside Θ. FPRAS is typically used for approximate counting; for optimization, randomized FPTAS is standard terminology. Please formalize the notion (output θ with f(θ) ≤ f(θ∗)+ϵf w.h.p.) and state the theorem precisely with all dependencies (N, D, 1/ϵ, log 1/δ, 1/(1−α), bounds on ∥t(x)∥).
- Clarity and rigor:
  - Notation is inconsistent and sometimes ambiguous: R2, R22, R2^2 appear; please standardize the bound R on ∥t(x)∥2 and carefully track powers. Similarly, Theorem statements sometimes elide constants and conditions; a consolidated theorem stating the main complexity as a function of problem parameters would help.
  - The TV perturbation bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2R2 ∥θ−φ∥2 used in the Lipschitz proof sketch is nonstandard; please provide a precise lemma with proof or citation (Pinsker/transport inequalities do not apply directly in this form).
  - Algorithm 1 is referenced but not shown in the excerpt; ensure the algorithm (step sizes, projection, sampling protocol) is fully specified for reproducibility.
- Scope of experiments:
  - The empirical section is a toy 4×4 Ising with 5 data points; no ablations or stress tests of K/M/v schedules or projection overhead vs sampling cost. This limits evidence of practical significance. Even if the contribution is theoretical, basic experiments varying graph size/density and comparing to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood or to persistent-contrastive schemes under the same Θ would be valuable.
- Missing or underemphasized connections:
  - Contrastive divergence and PCD are discussed, but there is room to connect to concentration for Markov chains (e.g., spectral gap-based bounds) that might allow using correlated samples to reduce M·v.
  - Hidden variables and non-Gibbs samplers (e.g., Swendsen–Wang, subgraphs world) are mentioned; a brief treatment of how the analysis would change for these kernels would strengthen the generality claim.

Quality
Overall technically sound at the level of proof sketches, relying on well-known inequalities and inexact gradient descent theory. Some steps would benefit from fuller, self-contained proofs (especially the TV–Lipschitz connection). Assumptions are clearly stated; limitations are candidly discussed.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable, but notation inconsistencies and missing algorithm details hinder reproducibility. Tighten definitions, standardize symbols, and gather main results into a single theorem/corollary with explicit dependencies.

Originality
Moderately novel. The idea of learning within fast-mixing parameter sets has appeared (e.g., Domke & Liu; Steinhardt & Liang), but providing end-to-end convergence rates for MLE via projected inexact gradients with explicit K/M/v schedules and complexity bounds is a useful, nontrivial advance.

Significance
Theoretical significance is solid: it formalizes when MCMC-based ML is provably efficient under mixing constraints. Practical impact is limited by strong assumptions and loose constants, but the framework could inspire better-justified variants of contrastive divergence and projection-based regularization.

Pros
- Clear theoretical framework linking ML, mixing, and optimization.
- General exponential-family treatment.
- Explicit complexity and parameter schedules; lower/upper bounds nearly match up to logs.
- Connects to concrete fast-mixing Θ sets known for Ising/MRFs.

Cons
- Strong, hard-to-verify assumptions (known C, α; projection oracle).
- Inefficient sampling protocol vs practice; no analysis of persistent variants.
- Very limited empirical validation.
- Some technical/notation clarity issues and terminology around FPRAS.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. As a primarily theoretical contribution with careful analysis and relevant connections to NIPS topics (graphical models, MCMC, optimization), it merits consideration. Strengthening clarity and adding modest empirical evidence would move it to a clear accept.

Questions for authors
- How do you propose to set or estimate C and α in practice beyond Ising with degree bounds or spectral-norm constraints?
- Can you extend the analysis to warm starts/persistent chains to reduce M·v?
- Please clarify the TV perturbation bound and fix notation around R.
- Could you include experiments varying graph size/density and comparing against pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood under the same Θ?
