This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, particularly focusing on scenarios where direct optimization is intractable. The core idea is to restrict the parameter space to a “fast-mixing” set – a set where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. The authors demonstrate that within such a set, a Fast Polynomial Random Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for the maximum likelihood solution, even when the underlying model is misspecified. This is achieved by analyzing gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation. The work provides theoretical guarantees on the convergence rate, contingent on the mixing rate of the Markov chain and careful parameter scaling.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in graphical model learning. The problem of intractability in ML estimation is well-established (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993), and the limitations of alternative methods like pseudolikelihood are accurately pointed out. The shift from directly tackling treewidth to focusing on MCMC mixing times is a novel and insightful direction. Framing the problem within the broader exponential family context is a strength, increasing the generality of the results. The rigorous analysis of gradient descent with MCMC gradient estimates is commendable, providing concrete convergence guarantees. The connection to existing results like those of Chow-Liu and Heinemann & Globerson is clearly established, contextualizing the contribution. The inclusion of practical examples, even if simple, helps illustrate the applicability of the theoretical findings. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the theoretical results are solid, their practical implications are not fully explored. The constants hidden within the asymptotic notation are likely large, potentially rendering the algorithm inefficient in practice. The dependence on the Lipschitz constant *L* is particularly concerning. The authors acknowledge this with the example and choosing a smaller L that converges faster, but this feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more careful analysis of the constant factors would be beneficial. The discussion of initialization is brief and glosses over potential issues. While the theory assumes an arbitrary starting distribution, the choice of initialization can significantly impact MCMC convergence in practice.  The paper could benefit from a more in-depth comparison with existing methods, beyond simply stating their limitations. For example, how does the sample complexity compare to composite likelihood methods when the model is misspecified?  The notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, even with the provided reference list. While thorough, the paper could benefit from clearer explanations of key concepts and a more streamlined presentation. The many theorems and corollaries, while mathematically precise, contribute to a somewhat overwhelming reading experience.  The section on background, while comprehensive, feels disconnected from the main results. A tighter integration of relevant background material into the presentation would improve readability.  Finally, the reliance on existing mixing time bounds, which often assume specific graph structures or interaction strengths, could limit the applicability of the results to more complex models.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets for tractable ML learning is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees on the convergence of the proposed algorithm.
*   **Generality:** The results are framed within the broader exponential family context, increasing their applicability.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The problem is well-motivated, and the limitations of existing methods are clearly articulated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practicality:** The theoretical guarantees may not translate to practical efficiency due to large constant factors and dependence on potentially hard-to-estimate parameters.
*   **Notation & Presentation:** The dense notation and numerous theorems can make the paper difficult to follow.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is limited and does not provide strong evidence of practical performance.
*   **Missing Comparisons:** Lack of detailed comparison with other methods (e.g., composite likelihood) in the misspecified case.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel contribution to the field of graphical model learning. However, the practical implications of the results are uncertain, and the presentation could be improved. While the theoretical rigor is commendable, the lack of a more thorough discussion of constant factors and practical considerations weakens the overall impact.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and address the aforementioned weaknesses. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Investigate the constant factors in the complexity bounds and explore techniques to minimize them.
2.  Provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly in the misspecified model setting.
3.  Improve the clarity and readability of the presentation, streamlining the notation and focusing on the key takeaways.
4.  Expand the empirical evaluation to demonstrate the practical performance of the algorithm on more challenging datasets.
5.  Discuss the limitations of relying on existing mixing time bounds and explore potential strategies for addressing them.
