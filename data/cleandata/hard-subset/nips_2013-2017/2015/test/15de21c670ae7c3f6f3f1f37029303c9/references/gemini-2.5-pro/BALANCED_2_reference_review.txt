**Summary**

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood (ML) learning in undirected graphical models and, more generally, in exponential families. The core idea is to reframe tractability not in terms of graphical structure (e.g., low treewidth) but in terms of the mixing time of a Markov chain. The authors propose to constrain the parameter vector `θ` to a convex set `Θ` where an associated MCMC sampler is guaranteed to mix rapidly.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of a common learning heuristic: projected stochastic gradient descent, where the gradient (the expectation of sufficient statistics) is estimated using MCMC sampling. The main contribution is to prove that this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the ML solution within the tractable set `Θ`. The authors provide explicit convergence guarantees and schedules for setting the number of gradient steps (`K`), MCMC samples per step (`M`), and MCMC chain length (`v`) to achieve a desired accuracy `ϵ` with high probability `1-δ`. Separate, detailed analyses are provided for the general convex case (unregularized ML) and the strongly convex case (ridge-regularized ML), leading to different complexity bounds and convergence in terms of function value and parameter distance, respectively.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous analysis of a widely used but theoretically underserved learning algorithm. While using MCMC to approximate gradients is a staple technique, it has largely been treated as a heuristic. This paper provides the first (to my knowledge) formal proof that this approach, under the assumption of fast mixing, yields an FPRAS for the constrained ML problem. This is a significant step forward in understanding the theoretical foundations of learning intractable models.

2.  **Novel and Important Notion of Tractability:** The paper's framing of tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets is both elegant and powerful. It provides a compelling alternative to structure-based tractability constraints (like low treewidth), which may be overly restrictive for some modeling problems. This perspective connects the vast literature on MCMC mixing times directly to the problem of provable ML learning.

3.  **Constructive and General Results:** The analysis is not merely an existence proof; it provides explicit, actionable schedules for setting the algorithm's hyperparameters (`K`, `M`, `v`) to achieve a desired accuracy. This is highly valuable. Furthermore, the results are presented in the general context of exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, making them broadly applicable beyond the motivating example of Ising models.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The problem setup, assumptions, and main theorems are stated precisely. The inclusion of proof sketches gives the reader good intuition for the technical arguments, and the background on mixing times is helpful for context.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Strong Assumptions and Practicality:** The analysis relies on several strong assumptions. It requires prior knowledge of the mixing rate constants (`C`, `α`) for the entire parameter set `Θ`. While such bounds exist for certain canonical models like the Ising model under specific constraints, they are often loose and may not be available for more complex or novel models. The analysis also assumes the existence of an efficient projection operator `Π_Θ`, and as the authors note, the complexity of this projection is not factored into the final work bounds. For some complex constraints (e.g., based on spectral norms), this projection can be computationally non-trivial.

2.  **Conservatism of Theoretical Bounds:** As is common with theoretical analyses, the derived bounds appear to be quite conservative. The authors' own experiment in Section 6 demonstrates this: they use a much smaller Lipschitz constant (`L=10`) than the theoretical one (`L=97`) for better practical performance, and the algorithm achieves a much better solution than the target `ϵ_θ`. This gap between theory and practice may limit the direct utility of the derived schedules for practitioners, who would likely continue to rely on hyperparameter tuning.

3.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's contribution is almost entirely theoretical. The single experiment on a small 4x4 grid serves as a useful proof of concept but is insufficient to demonstrate the practical relevance or scalability of the approach. There is no comparison to other standard approximate learning techniques such as persistent contrastive divergence (PCD), variational methods, or pseudolikelihood, making it difficult to gauge its empirical performance relative to the state of the art.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

*   A more detailed discussion on the practicality of the core assumptions would strengthen the paper. For which families of models can we realistically obtain the mixing constants `C` and `α`? For which "fast-mixing" sets `Θ` are the projection operators known to be efficient? A table summarizing known results would be very helpful.
*   While the focus is theoretical, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation would significantly increase the paper's impact. This could include: (1) testing on larger or more complex graphical models, (2) comparing the performance (in terms of both solution quality and wall-clock time) against established baselines like PCD and variational inference, and (3) investigating the sensitivity of the algorithm to misspecified mixing time constants.
*   The connection to contrastive divergence (CD) and its persistent variant (PCD) could be explored more deeply. The algorithm analyzed requires a full "burn-in" of `v` steps from an arbitrary starting distribution for each gradient estimate, which is computationally expensive. The analysis requires independent samples, precluding the sample reuse of PCD. It would be valuable to discuss if the analytical framework could be extended to analyze PCD, which often uses `v=1`, potentially leading to tighter or more practical guarantees under different assumptions (e.g., assuming the parameter `θ_k` changes slowly).

**Decision**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and novel theoretical contribution to the field. It provides a much-needed formal foundation for a common class of learning algorithms for intractable models. The weaknesses identified primarily concern the practical application and empirical validation, which, while important, do not detract from the core theoretical achievement. The paper is well-written, the claims are well-supported by the analysis, and the results are significant for researchers studying probabilistic modeling and inference. I recommend acceptance.
