### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of maximum likelihood learning (MLL) in undirected graphical models, which is generally intractable due to the difficulty of computing the partition function. Instead of common approaches that either approximate the likelihood (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching) or restrict the model structure to be tractable (e.g., low treewidth), this work proposes a different notion of tractability. The authors constrain the model parameters to lie within a convex set `Θ` for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing."

The paper analyzes a widely used but theoretically un-analyzed algorithm: projected gradient descent where the intractable gradient of the log-likelihood is approximated using MCMC samples. The key challenge is that running the MCMC chain for a finite number of steps introduces a bias in the gradient estimate, in addition to variance. The main contribution is a rigorous, non-asymptotic convergence analysis of this procedure. The authors prove that, under the fast-mixing assumption, this algorithm constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained MLL problem.

The analysis is presented for general exponential families and considers two cases. For convex objectives (unregularized MLL), the paper provides a convergence rate on the function value, showing the total computational effort (number of gradient steps × samples × MCMC transitions) scales as `O(1/ε_f^3 log(1/ε_f))` to achieve `ε_f`-optimality. For strongly convex objectives (L2-regularized MLL), a rate on the parameter error is given, with a complexity of `O(1/ε_θ^2 log(1/ε_θ^2))` for `ε_θ`-optimality. The paper derives explicit schedules for setting the algorithm's hyperparameters to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. This work connects the extensive literature on MCMC mixing times from theoretical computer science and statistical physics to the practical problem of learning graphical models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs, which are clearly sketched in the main body. The analysis methodically bounds the error in the gradient estimate by decomposing it into two sources: a statistical error from finite sampling and a bias from the finite-length Markov chain. These error terms are then controlled using concentration inequalities (Bernstein's, Hoeffding's) and the paper's core assumption on the geometric decay of the total variation distance. These bounds are integrated into existing convergence results for projected gradient descent with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011) to yield the final guarantees. The work appears complete, addressing both convex and strongly convex settings, and provides explicit (though complex) schedules for hyperparameters. The authors are also forthright about the limitations of their analysis, such as the potential conservatism of the bounds and the omission of the projection step's complexity from the final analysis.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, distinguishing the proposed approach from prior work. Section 2 provides a concise summary of notation, which is invaluable for the reader. The background on mixing times is presented effectively, connecting abstract definitions to concrete examples from the literature, such as for the Ising model. The main results in Section 4 are stated precisely as theorems, followed by intuitive proof sketches that illuminate the core arguments without getting lost in technical details. The overall structure is logical, making the paper easy to follow despite the technical density of the topic.

**Originality:**

The work is highly original. While using MCMC to estimate gradients for MLL is a long-standing heuristic (e.g., in training Restricted Boltzmann Machines via contrastive divergence), this paper appears to be the first to provide a rigorous, non-asymptotic convergence guarantee for this general strategy. The central idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets, rather than graph structure, is a novel and powerful perspective. It cleverly leverages a rich body of work on MCMC theory to provide a formal foundation for a learning algorithm. The paper does an excellent job of referencing related work and clearly articulating how its contribution—a formal FPRAS for MCMC-based MLL—is distinct from prior art.

**Significance:**

The theoretical significance of this paper is substantial. It provides the first formal justification for a class of widely used algorithms, explaining *when* and *why* they can be expected to converge to the true maximum likelihood solution. This is a fundamental contribution that bridges a gap between theory and practice. The framework could inspire further theoretical investigation into related heuristics, such as persistent contrastive divergence or other MCMC-based learning schemes. By connecting the machine learning literature with the theoretical computer science literature on mixing times, the paper may foster new cross-disciplinary research.

The direct practical significance may be more limited, a point the authors acknowledge. The derived schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC steps depend on multiple problem-dependent constants and are likely too conservative for direct use in practice, where these parameters would be tuned empirically. Furthermore, the requirement of an efficient projection operator onto a known fast-mixing set `Θ` may be a bottleneck for complex models, although tractable examples exist. Nevertheless, the conceptual contribution and the derived scaling laws provide valuable insights for practitioners.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper provides the first formal, non-asymptotic convergence proof for the widely used heuristic of MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning.
2.  **Novel Perspective:** It introduces and formalizes the idea of tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets, offering a compelling alternative to structure-based tractability assumptions.
3.  **High Technical Quality:** The analysis is rigorous, complete, and builds upon established results in both optimization and MCMC theory.
4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a complex theoretical argument accessible and easy to follow.
5.  **High Significance:** The results are of high theoretical importance and have the potential to influence future research by providing a solid foundation for analyzing MCMC-based learning algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Direct Practicality:** The derived hyperparameter schedules are likely too loose for practical application, and the overall algorithm relies on the existence of an efficient projection operator, which may not be available for all models of interest.
2.  **Complexity of Projection:** The analysis focuses on the sampling complexity (`KMv`) and does not incorporate the computational cost of the projection step, which could be significant in some cases.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This is a landmark theoretical paper that provides much-needed rigor to a common area of practice. Its novelty, clarity, and significance make it an excellent candidate for NIPS.
