### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the long-standing problem of intractable maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models with high treewidth. Instead of resorting to common surrogate objectives (like pseudolikelihood or score matching) which lack guarantees under model misspecification, or restricting the graph structure (e.g., to trees), the authors propose a novel approach. They constrain the model parameters `θ` to lie within a convex set `Θ` where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is provably fast-mixing. The core contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of projected gradient descent where the gradient is estimated via MCMC. The paper demonstrates that this common heuristic, under the assumption of fast mixing, constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood parameters within `Θ`. The analysis provides explicit schedules for setting the number of gradient iterations, MCMC samples, and MCMC steps to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. Separate convergence guarantees are derived for the general convex case (unregularized) and the strongly convex case (ridge-regularized), leading to different complexity bounds and convergence in terms of function value and parameter error, respectively.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its conceptual contribution of shifting the notion of tractability from graph structure (low treewidth) to parameter-induced dynamics (fast mixing). This is a powerful and elegant idea that connects the fields of optimization, statistical learning, and MCMC theory. Providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning—a widely used but theoretically underserved heuristic—is a significant achievement. This work provides a solid theoretical foundation for a class of algorithms that includes variants of contrastive divergence.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and thorough. The authors carefully account for the two sources of error in the gradient estimate: the bias from a finite number of MCMC steps (`v`) and the variance from a finite number of samples (`M`). The proofs leverage established results on projected gradient descent with inexact gradients and concentration inequalities to derive explicit, non-asymptotic bounds. The derivation of concrete schedules for the hyperparameters (`K, M, v`) is a particularly strong feature, making the theoretical results concrete.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem setup, motivation, and main results are presented with clarity. The authors do an excellent job of placing their work in the context of prior literature, clearly articulating the limitations of existing methods and how their approach differs. The inclusion of a simple illustrative example in Section 6, while not a major practical contribution, effectively grounds the theory and demonstrates the relationship between the theoretical bounds and empirical performance.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Practicality of the "Fast-Mixing Set" Assumption:** The entire framework hinges on the ability to define a convex set `Θ` of parameters that guarantees fast mixing and onto which one can efficiently project. While the paper provides important examples from the literature (e.g., bounded interaction strengths for Ising models), the class of models for which such sets are known is somewhat limited. For many complex, real-world models, deriving the mixing-time constants (`C`, `α`) and designing an efficient projection operator for `Θ` remains a formidable, and often open, research problem. The analysis is thus conditional on having solved this potentially harder problem first.

2.  **Conservatism of Theoretical Bounds:** As the authors rightly acknowledge in their example, the derived theoretical bounds can be quite conservative. The constants in the complexity results (e.g., the Lipschitz constant `L`, the norm bound `R₂`, mixing parameters `C, α`) can be large, leading to hyperparameter schedules (`K, M, v`) that may be impractical. This gap between theory and practice is common but prominent here, and it slightly tempers the direct applicability of the proposed schedules.

3.  **Limited Discussion of Model Expressivity Trade-off:** The method finds the optimal model within the restricted set `Θ`. A key question left underexplored is the trade-off between the tightness of this constraint and model quality. A very restrictive `Θ` might guarantee rapid mixing but may not contain a model that fits the data well, potentially leading to a worse KL divergence to the true data distribution than a model found by an inconsistent estimator like pseudolikelihood. A more detailed discussion of this expressivity vs. optimization-guarantee trade-off would strengthen the paper.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Elaborating on the Scope of `Θ`:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive discussion on the known classes of fast-mixing sets. A table summarizing models, their corresponding fast-mixing conditions (the definition of `Θ`), and the complexity of the projection operator would be invaluable to readers. It would also be helpful to discuss the primary challenges in extending these results to other model classes (e.g., models with higher-order potentials or categorical variables).

2.  **Addressing the Theory-Practice Gap:** While tightening the bounds is likely beyond the scope of a revision, the authors could add a brief discussion on potential reasons for the conservatism. For instance, are the bounds loose due to worst-case assumptions on the geometry of the function, or the use of general concentration inequalities? Could problem-specific structure or adaptive estimation of constants like `L` lead to more practical algorithms while retaining some guarantees?

3.  **Deeper Connection to Contrastive Divergence (CD):** The relationship to CD is mentioned only briefly. Given CD's popularity, a more detailed discussion in Section 5 or 7 would be beneficial. The authors could clarify how their analysis applies to "CD-v" with independent chains and a fixed step size, and explicitly state which assumptions are violated by common CD variants like persistent CD or those with decaying learning rates. This would better position the paper's contribution for the large community of researchers using these methods.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on learning graphical models. It formalizes and provides guarantees for a popular class of algorithms under a novel and insightful notion of tractability. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a fundamental problem. While the practical applicability of the specific schedules is limited by conservative bounds and the need to pre-specify a fast-mixing set, the conceptual framework and theoretical foundation are of high value to the NeurIPS community. The weaknesses identified are primarily avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws. I strongly recommend acceptance.
