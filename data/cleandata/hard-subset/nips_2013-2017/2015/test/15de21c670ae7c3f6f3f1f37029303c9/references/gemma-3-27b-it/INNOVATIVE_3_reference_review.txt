This paper tackles a fundamental challenge in graphical model learning – maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) when exact computation is intractable. The core idea, leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets to guarantee a FPRAS for MLE, is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from standard approaches like pseudolikelihood or score matching. While these alternatives offer practical solutions, they sacrifice statistical efficiency and guarantees, particularly in model misspecification scenarios, issues this work directly addresses. The connection to MCMC convergence analysis and the framing of tractability in terms of mixing rates is clever and broadly applicable beyond graphical models, extending to the general exponential family framework.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical contribution. Establishing a FPRAS under the fast-mixing assumption is a strong result with potential implications for a wider range of models where MLE is otherwise inaccessible. The thoroughness of the analysis – providing convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly-convex cases, and deriving explicit schedules for parameters – is commendable. The inclusion of a concrete example, even acknowledging a conservative application of the bounds, adds practical grounding. The theoretical framework is clearly presented, building from fundamental concepts and providing necessary background on mixing times.

However, the work isn’t without weaknesses. The primary concern is the practicality of the fast-mixing assumption. While the paper cites examples (Ising model), verifying this condition for complex graphical models in real-world applications could be challenging. The dependence on parameters like C and α, defining the mixing rate, requires careful assessment, and the theoretical bounds might be loose in practice. The analysis assumes bounded sufficient statistics, which might not always hold, and the impact of violating this assumption isn’t explored. While the authors acknowledge the computational cost of projection, a more detailed discussion of its scalability would be beneficial.  The empirical validation, though illustrative, is limited and could be strengthened with comparisons to existing methods on more challenging datasets.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of guaranteeing a FPRAS via fast-mixing parameter sets is a genuinely new perspective on MLE in intractable models.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous and provides concrete convergence guarantees.
* **Generality:** The framework extends beyond graphical models to the broader exponential family.
* **Potential Impact:**  If the fast-mixing assumption can be realistically verified, this work opens avenues for statistically efficient learning in previously intractable scenarios.
* **Bold Approach:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem with a non-standard, yet well-motivated, approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Practicality of Fast-Mixing Assumption:** Verifying the fast-mixing condition in complex models is a significant hurdle.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The example provides proof-of-concept but lacks extensive experimental evaluation.
* **Assumption of Bounded Sufficient Statistics:** The impact of violating this assumption is unexplored.
* **Scalability of Projection:** A more detailed discussion of projection’s computational cost is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations, I believe the novelty and theoretical significance of this work outweigh its weaknesses. The paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to MLE in intractable models, and the FPRAS result is a significant contribution. While the practicality of the fast-mixing assumption remains a concern, the theoretical framework provides a valuable foundation for future research. I recommend **acceptance**, with a request for the authors to address the scalability of the projection step and provide further discussion on the implications of unbounded sufficient statistics in the camera-ready version.  I am willing to overlook the limited empirical validation given the strong theoretical contribution. The paper presents a bold idea with a solid theoretical underpinning, which aligns well with the spirit of NIPS.
