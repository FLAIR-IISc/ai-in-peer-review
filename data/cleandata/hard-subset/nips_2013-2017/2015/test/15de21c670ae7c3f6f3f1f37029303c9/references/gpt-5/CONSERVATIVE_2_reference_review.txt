Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to a set Θ for which a chosen MCMC kernel is uniformly fast-mixing. The authors analyze projected (proximal) gradient descent that uses biased, MCMC-based estimates of the gradient, and give high-probability convergence and work bounds for both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases. They derive explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and Markov steps v, plus lower bounds suggesting near-optimal dependence on the target accuracy. They argue this yields an FPRAS for maximum-likelihood within Θ, under bounded sufficient statistics and the availability of projection onto Θ. The results are illustrated on a small Ising model.

Relation to prior work
The work connects several existing lines: MCMC-MLE (Geyer; Gu & Zhu), learning with approximate gradients (Tieleman; Hinton; Salakhutdinov), convergence of inexact proximal gradients (Schmidt et al.), fast-mixing parameter projections (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), and “fast-mixing by design” models (Steinhardt & Liang). It contrasts with tractable-structure approaches (Chow–Liu), high-girth/decay (Heinemann & Globerson), and surrogate-likelihood methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood).

Strengths
- Clear theoretical framework: explicit assumptions (bounded t(x), uniform geometric mixing over Θ), and clean use of projected gradient with biased/noisy gradients.
- Separation of convex vs strongly convex cases with different targets (objective suboptimality vs parameter error), and explicit dependence on K, M, v.
- Inclusion of lower bounds that almost match the proposed schedules improves credibility of the complexity claims.
- Generality beyond pairwise MRFs to exponential families (given bounded sufficient statistics and a fast-mixing kernel).
- Good synthesis of mixing-time literature with optimization under error.

Weaknesses and concerns
- FPRAS claim feels overstated/conditional. Standard FPRAS definitions pertain to relative-error approximations with runtime polynomial in input size and 1/ε. Here, guarantees are mostly additive (objective gap or parameter error), with runtime depending on unknown/vacuous mixing constants (C, α), Lipschitz constants, and the cost of projection. Please clarify the exact notion of approximation and the polynomial dependence on model size N, dataset size D, and encoding length of Θ; as written, the “FPRAS” label is not fully justified.
- Practical viability hinges on having (i) a projection oracle onto Θ and (ii) usable, uniform mixing bounds (C, α) over Θ. Beyond special cases (e.g., Ising with spectral/1/∞ norm bounds), such projections and mixing bounds are rare and/or very conservative. The paper largely assumes these oracles exist without demonstrating them for realistic, larger models.
- Empirical validation is minimal (a single 4×4 Ising with five data points), with no comparison to pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, or CD/PCD. There is no evidence that the proposed schedules or constraints deliver superior estimation or practical runtime in realistic settings, nor that restricting to Θ meaningfully improves downstream inference relative to baselines.
- Assumption that samples per iteration are independent conflicts with common practice (persistent CD / sample pools). The paper notes this but does not offer analysis for that setting, limiting applicability.
- Some technical details are loose: the Lipschitz constant bound appears very conservative; notational inconsistencies (R2 vs R^2 vs Ra), and reliance on TV→Euclidean norm bounds may hide large constants. Proofs are deferred; the main paper offers sketches only.
- Projection cost is ignored in the complexity measure though it can be nontrivial (e.g., spectral norm projections repeatedly on high-dimensional parameter tensors). This is acknowledged but not quantified.
- The example uses L tuned smaller than the theoretical bound to “converge faster,” which weakens the connection between theory and practice.

Quality
Technically competent overall, with careful derivations and good use of known concentration and optimization results. However, key assumptions (uniform mixing over Θ with known constants; existence and efficiency of projection) are strong and insufficiently substantiated empirically. The “FPRAS” claim needs a more precise, standard-compliant statement.

Clarity
Generally readable and well-structured, but notation could be tightened, some typos remain, and important algorithmic details (projection complexity, kernel choice beyond Ising, estimating C, α) are underspecified. The gap between theoretical constants and practical settings is unclear.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of constraining to fast-mixing sets and projecting has appeared (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), as has learning fast-mixing models by design (Steinhardt & Liang). The main novelty is a unified, explicit convergence/work analysis for projected MLE with MCMC-biased gradients, plus lower bounds. This is a useful synthesis but incremental.

Significance
Primarily theoretical interest. Practical significance is limited absent stronger evidence that the required Θ and oracles exist and help on nontrivial problems. The current empirical section does not support impact claims.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely define the approximation notion behind the “FPRAS” statement and spell out polynomial dependence on input size; consider downgrading the claim if it does not meet standard FPRAS criteria.
- Provide concrete projection procedures and mixing constants for at least one nontrivial model family beyond small Ising, and quantify projection cost in the total runtime.
- Add substantive experiments on larger graphs and real/synthetic datasets, including mis-specified regimes, with baselines (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, CD/PCD). Report estimation error, likelihood, and inference tractability.
- Empirically estimate effective mixing and assess the tightness of the theory’s schedules; explore adaptive choices of v and M.
- Clarify notation, fix typos, and include more proof details for key steps (or a longer appendix).

Questions for the authors
- How are C and α obtained in practice for a given Θ and kernel, and how sensitive are the schedules to misestimation?
- Beyond pairwise Ising, what families admit efficient projection oracles with meaningful mixing guarantees?
- Can your analysis extend to persistent sample pools, which are common in practice?
- Can you provide a relative-error guarantee (in likelihood) to better align with FPRAS terminology?

Recommendation
Lean reject. The theoretical synthesis is neat and may be of interest, but the empirical support is too weak, and the central claims rely on strong assumptions rarely met in practice. Strengthening the experimental section, clarifying the FPRAS claim, and demonstrating practical oracles for Θ would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
