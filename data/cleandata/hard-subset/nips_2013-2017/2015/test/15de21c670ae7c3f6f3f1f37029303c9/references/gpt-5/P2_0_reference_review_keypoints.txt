- Paper studies ML in exponential families restricted to a convex Θ that guarantees fast MCMC mixing; projected gradient with MCMC gradients yields an FPRAS-like polynomial-time approximation to ML within Θ.
- Analysis covers convex and strongly convex (ridge) cases, giving schedules for iterations K, samples per iteration M, chain length v, and total work KMv polynomial in 1/ε and problem parameters for bounded sufficient statistics.
- Builds on prior work: mixing-time bounds for spin systems, projection-to-fast-mixing sets (Domke & Liu), and convergence of inexact/biased-gradient methods; contrasts with Steinhardt & Liang and pseudolikelihood-type alternatives.
- Theoretical analysis is careful and modular, converting geometric TV mixing bounds into gradient-bias bounds and yielding O((1/ε)^3) objective and O((1/ε)^2) parameter rates (up to logs).
- Central assumption: existence of a convex, efficiently projectable Θ with known uniform mixing constants C and α; practicality hinges on how tight and attainable these Θ are.
- The use of “FPRAS” is imprecise for optimization; the paper should formalize additive vs multiplicative approximation and consider FPTAS terminology.
- Many proofs are deferred to the supplement; empirical validation is minimal (only a 4×4 Ising toy), so the contribution is primarily theoretical.
- Clarity is generally good, but notation for bounds on sufficient statistics is inconsistent and needs unification (R, R2, R2 2).
- Must precisely define approximation notion (function value vs parameter norm), success probability, and explicit polynomial dependence on problem size and mixing parameters.
- Suggest deriving Lipschitzness of ∇f via Hessian = Cov[t(X)] using bounded sufficient statistics, rather than the current TV-based sketch.
- When invoking mixing bounds, explicitly state the chain (e.g., single-site Gibbs) and how C, α scale with N to justify “polynomial in problem size” claims.
- Originality: novel end-to-end complexity analysis combining projection to fast-mixing sets, MCMC gradient approximation, and inexact-gradient convergence for general bounded exponential families.
- Significance: reframes tractability as “fast mixing” rather than treewidth and gives a principled ML alternative to pseudolikelihood, though practical impact may be limited by restrictive Θ and large mixing-constant factors.
- Strengths: clear theoretical contribution, general exponential-family scope, transparent assumptions, and thoughtful limitations discussion.
- Weaknesses: imprecise terminology, minimal experiments and comparisons (pseudolikelihood, CD, restart chains), reliance on uniform mixing constants, assumption of fresh independent samples per iteration (no persistent sampling), and possibly loose constants.
- Suggestions: formalize approximation/FPRAS vs FPTAS, catalog Θ and projection algorithms beyond Ising, discuss adaptive schemes when C, α are unknown, include larger empirical comparisons, and fix notation and Lipschitz derivation.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — solid, useful theoretical work that would benefit from tighter definitions and stronger empirical context.
