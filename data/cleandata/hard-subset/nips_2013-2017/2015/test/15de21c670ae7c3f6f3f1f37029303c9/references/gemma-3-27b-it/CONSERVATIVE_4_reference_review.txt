This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, proposing a framework based on restricting parameters to sets guaranteeing fast mixing of MCMC sampling. The core idea – establishing a fast-mixing parameter set to enable a FPRAS for maximum likelihood estimation – is theoretically interesting, and presented with a rigorous mathematical treatment. It attempts to bridge a gap between intractable ML estimation and tractable approximations. The work builds upon well-established concepts from statistical physics (Ising models) and optimization (gradient descent), framing them within a more general exponential family context.

However, despite the theoretical soundness, I find the practical significance of this work limited, and am skeptical of its immediate impact. My primary concern revolves around the disconnect between the theoretical guarantees and the practical realities of applying this approach. The paper repeatedly emphasizes the need for a “fast-mixing” parameter set, but the practical identification of such sets, beyond the Ising model examples provided, is not addressed. While the authors acknowledge this, and provide some guidance (citing Domke & Liu, 2013), it feels insufficient. The success of this method hinges heavily on being able to efficiently project parameters onto a fast-mixing set Θ, and the computational cost of such projections is not fully analyzed, particularly for more complex models than the Ising model. The claim of trivial projection in the example doesn’t extend well to more general graphical models, potentially negating the benefits of the FPRAS.

The analysis is also overly conservative, as demonstrated by the example in Section 6. The authors acknowledge that a lower value of L (gradient Lipschitz constant) leads to faster convergence in practice, suggesting the theoretical bounds might be loose. While a rigorous analysis is appreciated, a bound that significantly underestimates the achievable performance becomes less useful. Furthermore, the assumption of knowing bounds like R (sufficient statistic norm), C and α (mixing rate parameters) in practice is often unrealistic, and requires further investigation into how these parameters can be estimated or adapted during learning.

The paper's comparison to existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods) feels somewhat dismissive. While these methods may have drawbacks (inferior sample complexity, lack of guarantees for mis-specified models), they are often computationally cheaper and more readily applicable in practice. The paper doesn't adequately demonstrate how the proposed approach overcomes these limitations sufficiently to justify the added complexity. Moreover, the authors acknowledge that inference remains intractable even with the learned distribution in Θ, limiting the direct usability of the learned model. This point feels critical, as the ultimate goal of learning a graphical model is often to perform efficient inference.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical framework for analyzing the convergence of gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates.
*   **Generality:** The results are presented in the general exponential family framework, extending beyond graphical models.
*   **Novelty:** The notion of utilizing fast-mixing parameter sets to enable a FPRAS for maximum likelihood estimation is a novel contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Applicability:** The identification of fast-mixing parameter sets and the cost of projection remain significant challenges.
*   **Conservative Bounds:** The theoretical guarantees appear conservative, as evidenced by the example.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to existing methods doesn’t fully address the trade-offs in terms of computational cost and practical applicability.
*   **Inference Intractability:** Inference remains intractable, limiting the usefulness of the learned model.
*   **Reliance on Parameter Knowledge:** Requires accurate estimation of parameters R, C, and α, which may be difficult in practice.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to maximum likelihood learning.
*   It contributes to the understanding of the interplay between MCMC sampling and optimization in statistical models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical applicability of the proposed approach is limited.
*   The theoretical guarantees may be too conservative to be useful in practice.
*   The paper doesn’t adequately address the trade-offs with existing methods.
*   The reliance on knowing the mixing rate parameters and projection costs hinders real-world usage.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the theoretical contributions are noteworthy, the practical limitations and the lack of convincing evidence of real-world impact outweigh the strengths. The paper feels incomplete in its consideration of the practical challenges associated with applying this approach, and a more thorough analysis of these challenges is needed before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical result is interesting, but without a clearer path to practical implementation, its impact will be limited.
