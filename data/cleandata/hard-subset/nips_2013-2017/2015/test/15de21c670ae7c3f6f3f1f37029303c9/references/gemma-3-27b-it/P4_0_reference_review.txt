This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in exponential families with constraints guaranteeing fast mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. The core contribution is establishing a Fast Polynomial Random Access Scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within such a constrained parameter set. The authors demonstrate this by analyzing gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, providing convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, and deriving schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC transitions required to achieve a desired accuracy.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, setup, main results, and discussion. It appropriately connects the work to existing literature on maximum likelihood learning, graphical models, and MCMC methods, citing relevant papers like Jerrum & Sinclair (1993), Chow & Liu (1968), and Steinhardt & Liang (2018). The notation is consistently used, and the authors provide a helpful summary of key symbols. The inclusion of a detailed example with an Ising model further aids understanding.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of leveraging fast mixing properties for tractable maximum likelihood learning is a novel perspective. While restricted parameter spaces have been explored (e.g., Chow-Liu algorithm), framing tractability in terms of MCMC convergence rates is a distinct contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the convergence of gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The analysis distinguishes between convex and strongly convex cases, providing tailored convergence rates. The use of existing results (e.g., Schmidt et al., 2013) is appropriate and well-justified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The proofs are sketched with sufficient detail to convey the main ideas, and the discussion section addresses potential limitations and future work.
*   **Completeness:** The paper seems to present a complete work. The theoretical results are supported by a practical example illustrating their application. The paper also discusses the limitations and potential directions for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Significance:** The practical impact of the work is limited. The assumption of knowing a parameter set guaranteeing fast mixing can be challenging in many real-world scenarios. The theoretical guarantees, while rigorous, might be conservative, as indicated by the example where a lower Lipschitz constant yielded faster convergence.
*   **Complexity of Projection:** The paper acknowledges that the complexity of projecting onto the constraint set Θ is not fully addressed. In situations where projection is computationally expensive, it could negate the benefits of using a fast-mixing parameter set.
*   **Initialization:** The analysis assumes arbitrary initialization of the Markov chains, while practical implementations often utilize informed initialization strategies (e.g., contrastive divergence) that could influence the mixing time and convergence rate. The paper briefly mentions contrastive divergence but does not formally incorporate it into the analysis.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the example demonstrates the applicability of the framework, a more thorough empirical evaluation with different models and datasets would strengthen the paper’s claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning with tractable constraints.
*   The analysis is rigorous and provides convergence guarantees for a widely used optimization method (gradient descent).
*   The work contributes to the understanding of the interplay between MCMC mixing rates and optimization performance.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and complete.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical applicability of the work is limited by the assumption of knowing a fast-mixing parameter set.
*   The paper lacks a comprehensive empirical evaluation.
*   The analysis does not fully address the computational cost of projection onto the constraint set.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of maximum likelihood learning in constrained parameter spaces. While the practical significance may be limited, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and clarity of the presentation make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly expanding the discussion on the cost of projection and exploring the impact of informed initialization strategies.
