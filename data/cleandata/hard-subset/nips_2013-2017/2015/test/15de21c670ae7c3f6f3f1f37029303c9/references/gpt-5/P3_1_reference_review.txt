Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a tractability constraint defined not by graph structure (e.g., low treewidth) but by fast mixing of an associated MCMC kernel. The authors assume access to a convex parameter set Θ for which a Markov chain Mθ mixes geometrically with uniform constants C, α over Θ, and that one can project onto Θ. They analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated by MCMC averages, providing high-probability convergence guarantees in two regimes: convex (unregularized or regularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized). They also give work–accuracy tradeoffs (schedules for number of iterations K, samples M, and chain steps v), and “lower bounds” that are necessary to satisfy their sufficient error conditions. They argue this yields an FPRAS for MLE restricted to Θ. The theory applies to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, with Ising/MRFs used as canonical examples. Related work includes MCMC-MLE (Geyer), learning with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al.), mixing-time bounds for Ising/MRFs (Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum, Hayes), and learning methods that avoid intractable likelihoods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood). Compared to Chow-Liu trees and correlation-decay/high-girth learning, the contribution is a different tractability axis (fast mixing) and global MLE within that set. Compared to Steinhardt–Liang’s restart-augmented chain learning, this paper uses intrinsic fast-mixing parameter sets rather than modifying the chain.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual shift: tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets, not structure. This neatly connects mixing-time theory with optimization of the (regularized) likelihood.
- General framework for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics; not limited to specific graph topologies.
- Technically careful use of inexact projected gradient analysis (via Schmidt et al.) with MCMC bias and variance control; distinguishes convex vs strongly convex regimes and provides explicit schedules with polynomial work in 1/ε and other parameters.
- The Lipschitz gradient argument for the regularized likelihood is clean, with a tight vector-valued TV bound that avoids an unnecessary √d factor.
- Discussion of projection onto Θ grounded in known sufficient conditions for fast mixing (Dobrushin/spectral-norm-type constraints) and prior projection methods for Ising/MRFs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumption realism: The existence of a convex Θ with uniform mixing constants C, α known a priori and a practical projection oracle is strong. In many models, C and α depend nontrivially on θ and N (e.g., via spectral norms or Dobrushin coefficients). The guarantees hinge on these being available and sufficiently favorable; clearer guidance on how to compute/tune them in practice would help.
- “Lower bounds” (Theorems 3 and 7) are not information-theoretic; they follow from separately forcing each error term below √ε or ε. This is useful but should be framed as necessary conditions under the chosen proof technique, not fundamental complexity lower bounds.
- Missing algorithmic specifics: Algorithm 1 is referenced but not shown. Explicit step size (e.g., 1/L), projection step, sample independence assumptions, and the precise update rule should be stated in the main text for reproducibility.
- Notation/typos impede clarity: R2 is used as a bound on ∥t(x)∥2, but expressions like R22 appear, creating ambiguity (is that (R2)2?). The intro lists Ra as the “sufficient statistics norm bound,” then switches to R2. Some quantities in the example are garbled (e.g., D = √ × (2×.2)2). These should be cleaned up.
- Scope of the FPRAS claim: The runtime depends polynomially on 1/(1−α) and log(1/ε). If α can approach 1 as N grows (common for high-dimensional systems near criticality), constants can be large. The paper should state the parameter and dimension dependence more explicitly to substantiate the FPRAS claim in standard complexity terms.
- Empirical validation is minimal (one toy Ising instance) and mainly illustrative. While the paper is primarily theoretical, even small-scale experiments demonstrating projection practicality and sensitivity to C, α, and projection choices would strengthen it.
- Practical sampling considerations: The analysis assumes independent samples per iteration (fresh restarts and v steps), which is often much more expensive than maintaining persistent chains. The authors acknowledge that their proof doesn’t handle sample pools/persistent CD; any partial analysis or discussion of how to extend the theory to dependent samples would be valuable.

Quality
The technical development is largely sound and aligned with established tools (mixing-time bounds, TV inequalities, concentration and inexact gradient descent). The Lipschitz gradient bound is plausible and, with a careful duality argument, avoids dimension blow-up. The convergence theorems are reasonable, with explicit dependencies. The main caveats are the strong uniform-mixing assumption and the characterization of the “lower bounds.”

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with helpful proof sketches, but clarity is hampered by notation inconsistencies and missing Algorithm 1. Tightening notation (use a single symbol R for supx ∥t(x)∥2, avoid R22), explicitly specifying norms, and providing the algorithm box would improve readability.

Originality
The specific combination—projected MLE onto fast-mixing parameter sets with an FPRAS-style convergence analysis—appears novel. Prior work on fast-mixing learning (e.g., Steinhardt–Liang) modifies the chain; here the model class is constrained. The projection approach for Ising/MRFs builds on earlier NIPS work (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), but the learning guarantees and work–accuracy schedules are new.

Significance
Conceptually significant: it reframes tractability in learning via mixing constraints and gives concrete guarantees for MLE within that class. Practically, adoption depends on the availability and tightness of Θ, the cost of projection, and mixing constants. Even if not immediately plug-and-play, the paper can influence how practitioners regularize toward fast-mixing regimes.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include Algorithm 1 with explicit step sizes, projection, and sampling protocol; specify independence assumptions.
- Unify notation and fix typos; clearly define R = supx ∥t(x)∥2 and carry it consistently.
- Be precise about the FPRAS claim: state the full dependence on C, α, N, and projection complexity; clarify when α can be made uniform over Θ.
- Expand discussion of how to obtain C, α and projection operators for common families (Ising, Potts, general MRFs), including computational cost.
- Reframe Theorems 3 and 7 as necessary conditions for the chosen decomposition rather than general lower bounds.
- If space permits, add a small empirical study comparing persistent vs independent chains and the effect of projection strength on optimization speed and final likelihood.

Pros
- Novel, principled tractability notion for MLE via fast-mixing Θ.
- Solid theoretical convergence with explicit work–accuracy tradeoffs.
- Broad applicability to exponential families with bounded statistics.

Cons
- Strong assumptions on uniform mixing and projection availability.
- Notation/clarity issues; algorithm missing from main text.
- Minimal empirical support; “lower bounds” are methodological, not fundamental.
