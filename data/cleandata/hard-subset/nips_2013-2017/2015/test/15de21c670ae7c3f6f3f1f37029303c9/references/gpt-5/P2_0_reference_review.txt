Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families with intractable exact inference by restricting parameters to a convex set Θ that guarantees fast mixing of an associated MCMC sampler. The core claim is that, if one can project onto such a Θ and has geometric mixing bounds uniformly over Θ, then projected gradient descent with MCMC-based approximate gradient estimates yields a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) to the ML solution within Θ. The analysis treats both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, providing schedules for number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain length v, and total work bounds KMv that are polynomial in 1/ε and other problem parameters. The results are presented in the general exponential-family setting with bounded sufficient statistics.

The work builds on several strands: (i) mixing time bounds for Gibbs and related chains on spin systems (e.g., Jerrum–Sinclair; Hayes; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum), (ii) prior work on projecting parameters into fast-mixing sets (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), and (iii) convergence of gradient methods with inexact, biased gradients (Schmidt et al.). It is related to Steinhardt & Liang’s “fast-mixing by design” learning via restart chains, but differs conceptually by learning within a set Θ where standard MCMC mixes rapidly and by targeting the maximum-likelihood objective within Θ. Compared to alternatives like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihood, their approach recovers the ML/M-projection within a computationally tractable class and provides end-to-end complexity bounds for an MCMC-based ML procedure.

Quality
- Theory: The convergence analysis is careful, modular, and uses standard tools (concentration inequalities, mixing-to-TV error bounds, and proximal gradient error analyses). The convex case yields objective suboptimality guarantees for the averaged iterate, scaling as O((1/ε)^3 up to logs), and the strongly convex case yields parameter error O((1/ε)^2 up to logs), consistent with known rates for projected gradient with biased/noisy gradients. The key technical bridge—turning a geometric TV mixing bound into a bound on the gradient bias at each iteration—is standard but well-executed.
- Assumptions: The central assumption is the existence of a convex, efficiently projectable Θ with known uniform mixing constants C and α for the chosen chain. For Ising/Potts models, the paper points to concrete constructions based on bounded couplings or spectral-norm constraints, with known projections. This is reasonable, though application breadth hinges on the practicality and tightness of these sets.
- Claims: The “FPRAS” claim would benefit from precise definition in the optimization context (usually FPRAS is for counting; for optimization, FPTAS is more conventional, and multiplicative vs additive criteria matter). As stated, the results provide an additive ε-approximation to the optimal objective/value in polynomial time in 1/ε (and other parameters), which is in the spirit of an FPRAS; please formalize this to avoid confusion.
- Completeness: Many proofs are deferred; assuming a full appendix/supplement, this is acceptable for NeurIPS. The 4×4 Ising toy example is illustrative but too small to validate constants or practicality; the contribution is predominantly theoretical.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The setup cleanly introduces notation and the algorithmic template. A few issues:
- Be consistent with notation for the bound on sufficient statistics (R, R2, R2 2 appear; define once and use consistently).
- Precisely define the notion of approximation (additive in function value or parameter norm), success probability, and what “polynomial time” depends on (N, D, dimension of θ, 1/ε, 1/(1−α), etc.).
- When asserting Lipschitzness of ∇f, it may be clearer to derive it via the Hessian = Cov[t(X)] and bound the operator norm using bounded sufficient statistics; the current TV-based sketch is less standard.
- Briefly articulate the chain used (e.g., single-site Gibbs) when invoking mixing bounds and how C, α scale with N (e.g., 1/(1−α) = O(N/(1−Δ tanh β))) to make the “polynomial in problem size” statement explicit.

Originality
The paper’s main novelty is an end-to-end complexity analysis showing that ML learning within a fast-mixing parameter set admits a randomized polynomial-time approximation scheme with explicit sampling budgets, extending beyond specific models to general bounded exponential families. While the technical ingredients are known, the combination—projection onto fast-mixing sets + MCMC gradient approximation + inexact gradient convergence—with explicit work bounds appears new and useful. The connection to learning within computation-constrained model classes is well-motivated and complements prior works (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang’s restart chains; Domke & Liu’s fast-mixing projections).

Significance
Conceptually, this reframes “tractability via treewidth” to “tractability via fast mixing” and shows ML within that class is algorithmically sound with polynomial complexity. This provides a theoretically grounded alternative to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood when one desires the M-projection within a class where sampling-based inference is tractable. Practically, the impact may be tempered by the restrictiveness of Θ (which may harm fit), the cost/engineering of projections for complex models, and large constants hidden in mixing bounds. Still, the results should stimulate further research on algorithmically meaningful parameter classes and principled learning procedures under mixing constraints.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution linking fast-mixing sets and ML learning with explicit complexity.
- General exponential-family formulation; not limited to Ising grids.
- Uses standard, robust tools; assumptions are transparent.
- Thoughtful discussion of limitations (projection cost, initialization, persistent chains).

Weaknesses
- The “FPRAS” terminology is imprecise; formalize the approximation notion and dependence on input size.
- Empirical validation is minimal; no comparisons to pseudolikelihood, CD, or restart-chain methods.
- Uniform mixing constants over Θ may be hard to obtain in practice; discuss how to estimate or adapt to unknown C, α.
- Analysis assumes independent fresh samples per iteration; persistent-sample schemes used in practice are not covered.
- Some constants (e.g., L = 4R^2 + λ) may be loose; clarifying tightness could improve practical guidance.

Questions and suggestions
- Please formalize the approximation guarantee (additive vs multiplicative) and the exact definition of FPRAS/FPTAS you intend, and specify polynomial dependence on N, D, |θ| explicitly.
- Provide a brief catalog of Θ and projection algorithms beyond Ising (e.g., general pairwise discrete MRFs with Liu–Domke R(θ) norms), with computational costs.
- Discuss how to set or adapt K, M, v when C, α are unknown; can one use diagnostics to increase v or M on the fly?
- Include a larger synthetic experiment comparing to pseudolikelihood and CD under model misspecification, showing the benefit of optimizing ML within fast-mixing Θ.
- Clarify the Lipschitz gradient bound derivation via covariance bounds; fix notation inconsistencies.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a neat, well-argued theoretical contribution with clear relevance to learning in graphical models/exponential families. Tightening definitions and adding a bit more empirical context would strengthen it further.
