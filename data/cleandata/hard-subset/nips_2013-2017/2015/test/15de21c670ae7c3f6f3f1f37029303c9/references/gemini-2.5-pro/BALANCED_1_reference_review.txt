### Paper Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) for undirected graphical models (and more generally, exponential families). Instead of restricting the model structure (e.g., to low treewidth) to ensure tractability, the authors propose a fundamentally different approach: restricting the model parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing". The core contribution is a rigorous proof that projected gradient descent, with gradients approximated via MCMC, constitutes a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within this constrained set `Θ`. The analysis provides explicit schedules for setting the algorithm's hyperparameters—number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples per step (M), and MCMC chain length (v)—to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. The paper provides separate convergence analyses and complexity bounds for the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (L2-regularized) cases.

**Relation to Prior Work**

The work is well-situated within the literature on learning probabilistic models. It correctly identifies the computational hardness of exact MLE as a central challenge (Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993). It contrasts its approach with two dominant paradigms for tractable learning:
1.  **Approximate estimators:** Methods like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods are efficient but do not generally converge to the M-projection (the KL-closest distribution in the model family) under model misspecification, a key guarantee that constrained MLE retains.
2.  **Structural restrictions:** Methods like the Chow-Liu algorithm for trees or learning high-girth models rely on graph structures that permit efficient exact or approximate inference. This paper's approach of defining tractability via parameter constraints that guarantee fast MCMC mixing, rather than graph structure, is a significant departure and can in principle handle models with arbitrary treewidth.

The paper also connects to the vast literature on using MCMC to approximate gradients for MLE, which includes popular heuristics like contrastive divergence (Hinton, 2002) and more recent work like Steinhardt and Liang (2018). The key distinction of the present work is its focus on providing rigorous, non-asymptotic convergence guarantees to the true constrained MLE objective, whereas much of the prior work relies on heuristics or lacks formal proofs of convergence.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining a tractable learning regime based on fast-mixing parameter sets is novel and conceptually elegant. It opens a new perspective on the trade-off between model expressivity and computational tractability, moving beyond purely structural constraints. If such fast-mixing sets can be characterized for a broad range of models, this framework could have a significant impact on learning complex, densely connected graphical models.

2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's primary strength is its rigorous theoretical analysis. Providing an FPRAS for this notoriously hard problem is a substantial achievement. The derivations of convergence rates and computational complexity for both convex and strongly convex settings are thorough. The analysis carefully accounts for the biased and noisy nature of MCMC-based gradient estimates, which is a difficult theoretical challenge.

3.  **Constructive and General Results:** The results are not merely existence proofs; the authors provide explicit schedules for setting the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC steps to achieve a target accuracy. Furthermore, the framework is presented in the general context of exponential families, making the results broadly applicable beyond specific graphical models like the Ising model.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, the setup and assumptions are stated precisely, and the main theorems are easy to understand. The inclusion of proof sketches provides valuable intuition for the main arguments.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The framework hinges on two crucial assumptions: (1) the existence of a known, convex, fast-mixing parameter set `Θ`, and (2) the ability to efficiently project onto this set. While the paper provides compelling examples for Ising models (e.g., constraining interaction strengths or matrix norms), the generality of this assumption is a major open question. For more complex, non-pairwise models, identifying such sets `Θ` and designing efficient projection operators may be extremely difficult.

2.  **Conservatism of Theoretical Bounds:** As the authors commendably acknowledge in their example, the derived theoretical bounds on K, M, and v are likely to be very conservative in practice. The required number of MCMC steps (`v=561` for a 4x4 grid) and samples appears prohibitively large for real-world applications. This significant gap between theory and practical heuristics (like single-step contrastive divergence) limits the immediate applicability of the proposed schedules.

3.  **Minimal Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's contribution is primarily theoretical, but the experimental section is too limited. A single toy example on a 4x4 grid serves as a proof of concept but does little to validate the approach's utility. A more thorough empirical study on benchmark datasets, comparing the performance against standard baselines (e.g., pseudolikelihood, persistent contrastive divergence), would be invaluable. Such a study could help answer key questions: How much performance is lost by constraining parameters to `Θ`? How does the method scale? How loose are the bounds in practice?

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Expanded Discussion on `Θ`:** The paper would be strengthened by a more extensive discussion on the scope of the main assumption. Are there other classes of models beyond pairwise MRFs where fast-mixing sets and efficient projections are known? Could the authors speculate on how one might construct such sets for other models of interest (e.g., models with higher-order potentials)?
*   **Stronger Empirical Section:** I strongly encourage the authors to add experiments on a standard, medium-scale dataset (e.g., from computer vision or computational biology). This would allow for a comparison of the learned model's quality (e.g., held-out log-likelihood) against practical baselines. It would also provide a more realistic setting to study the empirical convergence versus the theoretical bounds.
*   **Relating to Heuristics:** The discussion mentions that the analysis does not cover common strategies like persistent contrastive divergence (PCD), where samples are reused across iterations. It would be helpful to elaborate on the specific theoretical obstacles (e.g., the breakdown of independence assumptions) and perhaps suggest what new analytical tools might be needed to tackle these more practical, but harder to analyze, algorithms.

**Decision**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original theoretical contribution. The novel formulation of tractable learning via fast-mixing parameter sets is an important conceptual advance. Despite the practical limitations stemming from strong assumptions and conservative bounds, the rigorous establishment of an FPRAS for this problem is a valuable result for the machine learning theory community. The strengths in originality and theoretical depth outweigh the weaknesses in empirical validation. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
