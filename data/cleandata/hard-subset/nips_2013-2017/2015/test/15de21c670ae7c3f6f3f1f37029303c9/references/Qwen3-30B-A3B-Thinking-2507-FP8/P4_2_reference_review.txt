

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary
This paper addresses the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by introducing a novel approach based on restricting parameters to "fast-mixing" sets where MCMC sampling mixes rapidly. The authors establish theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates when the parameter space is constrained to such sets. The work extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families, with the key insight that if the parameter set Θ guarantees rapid mixing of MCMC (with known mixing rate), then maximum likelihood learning within Θ can be efficiently performed. This differs from previous tractable approaches that focused on low-treewidth models (e.g., Chow-Liu algorithm for trees) or well-specified model assumptions. The paper builds on existing mixing time literature (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993; Domke and Liu, 2013) but provides the first convergence guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning under mixing time constraints.

## Quality
The paper presents technically sound analysis with rigorous theoretical guarantees. The authors provide clear proofs for key theorems, including Lipschitz continuity of the gradient, convex convergence bounds, and strongly convex convergence results. The analysis correctly accounts for both the sampling error from MCMC and the bias from mixing time. The example in Section 6 demonstrates practical applicability with concrete parameter calculations for an Ising model. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, such as the conservative nature of theoretical bounds compared to empirical performance. The theoretical framework is complete, with careful attention to both convex and strongly convex cases, and the authors properly distinguish between the two settings.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Key notation is consistently defined, and the logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results is effective. The proof sketches provide useful intuition for readers who may not follow all technical details. The example in Section 6 helps ground the theoretical analysis in a concrete setting, making the contributions more accessible. The paper adequately explains the connection between mixing time guarantees and the tractability of learning, which is the central contribution. The exposition is suitable for a NIPS audience with background in graphical models and optimization.

## Novelty
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by introducing the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" and demonstrating that maximum likelihood learning within such sets can be efficiently performed with convergence guarantees. This is distinct from previous approaches that either assumed low treewidth (Chow-Liu) or well-specified models (pseudolikelihood, score matching). The authors correctly identify that while MCMC-based learning has been widely used, this work provides the first theoretical analysis showing convergence under mixing time constraints. The extension to general exponential families (beyond graphical models) further broadens the scope of the contribution. The paper properly situates itself against related work in NIPS proceedings (e.g., Steinhardt and Liang, 2015; Domke and Liu, 2013) and demonstrates clear differentiation.

## Significance
The results provide a theoretically grounded approach to maximum likelihood learning in models where exact inference is intractable, addressing a fundamental challenge in graphical models. The convergence guarantees and computational complexity bounds are valuable for practitioners implementing MCMC-based learning algorithms. While the paper acknowledges limited practical contribution (Section 6), the theoretical foundation enables better understanding of how to set parameters (number of iterations, samples, transitions) for MCMC-based learning. The work advances the state of the art by showing that fast-mixing parameter sets can be used as a tractability criterion alternative to treewidth constraints, potentially enabling learning in more complex models. The results are likely to be influential for both theoretical and applied researchers working with exponential families.

## Strengths
- Provides rigorous convergence guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning under mixing time constraints
- Correctly distinguishes between convex and strongly convex cases with appropriate analysis
- Establishes clear computational complexity bounds with explicit parameter schedules
- Extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families
- Properly situates the work within existing literature and identifies clear differentiation
- Example in Section 6 demonstrates practical applicability of theoretical results

## Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation beyond the single example in Section 6
- Minimal discussion of practical implementation details for the projection step onto fast-mixing sets
- Theoretical bounds appear conservative compared to empirical performance (as noted in the example)
- Does not address potential limitations of the approach in high-dimensional settings
- The paper does not discuss how to determine the mixing time parameters (C, α) for new models

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Novel theoretical contribution with clear differentiation from existing work
- Rigorous analysis with well-justified assumptions
- Provides valuable computational complexity bounds for practitioners
- Correctly identifies a gap in the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning
- Well-written with clear organization and notation

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Limited empirical validation beyond a single example
- Theoretical results may not be immediately practical due to conservative bounds
- Minimal discussion of implementation challenges for the projection step
- Does not address potential limitations in high-dimensional settings

## Recommendation
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning. The novelty of the fast-mixing parameter sets approach is significant, and the theoretical guarantees are sound and well-justified. While the paper has limited empirical validation, the primary contribution is theoretical, and the example provides sufficient demonstration of the practical relevance of the theory. The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical models with clear differentiation from previous work. Given the quality of the theoretical analysis and the significance of the contribution to the field, I recommend acceptance.
