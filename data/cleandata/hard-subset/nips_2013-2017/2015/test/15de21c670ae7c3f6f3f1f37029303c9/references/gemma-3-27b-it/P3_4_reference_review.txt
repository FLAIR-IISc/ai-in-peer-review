## NIPS Review: Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, which is generally intractable. Instead of focusing on finding tractable model classes directly (like tree-structured models via Chow-Liu), the authors propose a fundamentally different approach: leveraging parameter sets where MCMC sampling is *fast-mixing*. They demonstrate that if a constraint set Θ guarantees fast mixing, a First-Order Primal-Dual (FOPD) method, specifically gradient descent with MCMC for gradient estimation, achieves a FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution *within* that constrained set. The work provides theoretical guarantees on the convergence rate, considering both convex and strongly convex cases, and analyzes the computational complexity in terms of the desired accuracy.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing landscape of intractable ML learning. It acknowledges and contrasts its approach with alternatives like pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods, and methods based on local learning (Abbeel et al., Mizrahi et al.).  The connection to Chow-Liu and high-girth models (Heinemann & Globerson) as examples of tractable families is also well-established.  Critically, the authors highlight that prior work often lacks theoretical guarantees, especially when the model is misspecified, and/or yields intractable inference. The references to mixing time theory (Jerrum & Sinclair, Hayes, Dyer et al.) are relevant and demonstrate a solid understanding of the underlying mathematical framework.  The connection to Schmidt et al.’s work on projected gradient descent with errors is also appropriate.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of exploiting fast-mixing parameter sets for tractable ML learning is a genuinely new perspective. It moves beyond trying to directly define tractable model structures and instead focuses on ensuring efficient *computation* within arbitrary constraint sets.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases. The derivation of the FPRAS and the complexity analysis are well-motivated and seem mathematically sound.
* **Generality:** The framework isn't limited to graphical models. The authors correctly emphasize its applicability to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and the proposed solution.  The inclusion of a “symbols” section is helpful.
* **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of an example Ising model calculation provides a glimpse into how the theoretical results might translate into practice. The discussion on projection complexity is also important.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality Concerns:** While theoretically appealing, the applicability of this framework is heavily dependent on finding parameter sets that *both* satisfy the fast-mixing condition and are expressive enough to capture the underlying data distribution. The example is illustrative but doesn't fully address the challenge of finding such sets in more complex scenarios.  The projection step, while acknowledged, could be a bottleneck depending on the chosen parameter set.
* **Tightness of Bounds:** The theoretical bounds, while correct, are likely to be conservative. The example shows a discrepancy between the theoretical Lipschitz constant and a faster-converging value used in practice. Further analysis to tighten these bounds would strengthen the results.  The dependency on parameters like `R` (sufficient statistics norm) is potentially problematic if the data distribution has very large norms.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The example is fairly limited.  A more extensive empirical evaluation, comparing the proposed approach to existing methods on a variety of datasets and model structures, would be highly beneficial.
* **Connection to Variational Inference:** The authors mention variational inference as a surrogate likelihood approach, but don't explicitly draw a comparison to the fast-mixing framework.  It would be valuable to discuss the trade-offs between the two approaches – when would one be preferred over the other?
* **The Role of λ:** The transition between the convex and strongly convex analysis relies heavily on the regularization parameter λ.  A more nuanced discussion about how to choose λ optimally, balancing bias and variance, would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a long-standing problem in machine learning.
* The generality of the framework is a significant strength.
* The paper is well-written and provides a clear and rigorous analysis.
* The work has the potential to inspire new research directions in tractable ML learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical applicability of the framework remains unclear.
* The theoretical bounds may be overly conservative.
* The empirical evaluation is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of maximum likelihood learning. The idea of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets is novel and potentially impactful. While practical concerns and the need for more extensive empirical evaluation remain, the strength of the theoretical analysis and the generality of the approach justify acceptance.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**

I recommend acceptance with the following minor revisions:

1. **Expand the discussion of practical considerations.**  Address the challenges of finding fast-mixing parameter sets in real-world scenarios and discuss strategies for dealing with potentially expensive projection steps.
2. **Investigate the tightness of the theoretical bounds.** Explore opportunities to refine the analysis and obtain more accurate estimates.
3. **Add a brief discussion of the relationship to variational inference.** Compare and contrast the two approaches, highlighting their respective strengths and weaknesses.
4. **Consider adding a paragraph discussing limitations and future directions.**

I am confident that addressing these points will further enhance the quality and impact of this paper.
