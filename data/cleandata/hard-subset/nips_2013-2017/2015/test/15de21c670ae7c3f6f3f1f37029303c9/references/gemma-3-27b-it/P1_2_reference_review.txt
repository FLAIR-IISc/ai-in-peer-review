## Peer Review: Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in general graphical models. It proposes a framework for efficient learning within a constrained parameter set Θ, where MCMC sampling converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The core result is a Fast Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within such a set Θ, leveraging the fast-mixing property to obtain theoretical guarantees for gradient descent-based optimization. The work generalizes beyond graphical models, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. The authors provide detailed convergence analysis for both convex and strongly convex cases, along with schedules for setting the number of iterations, samples, and Markov chain transitions.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established limitations of maximum likelihood learning in general graphical models (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993) and acknowledges existing approaches like pseudolikelihood (3), score matching (16, 22), and composite likelihoods (20, 30). It distinguishes itself by focusing on *tractability via fast mixing* rather than approximating the likelihood directly, as these previous methods do.  The paper connects to work on learning tree-structured models (Chow-Liu, 1968) and high-girth models (Heinemann & Globerson, 2014), framing its approach as a more general strategy for achieving tractability. It also acknowledges recent work leveraging MCMC for learning (Steinhardt & Liang, 2018) but notably provides theoretical guarantees lacking in those approaches. The work builds on and extends existing theory in Markov Chain Monte Carlo and convex optimization, citing relevant theorems from various sources.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method, establishing a FPRAS with provable guarantees. The analysis distinguishes between convex and strongly convex cases, offering nuanced results.
*   **Generality:** The framework extends beyond graphical models, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. This broad applicability is a significant strength.
*   **Practical Guidance:**  The authors provide schedules for setting key hyperparameters (K, M, v) based on desired accuracy levels, bridging the gap between theory and practice. The illustrative example further demonstrates this.
*   **Clear Exposition:** The paper is well-structured and generally clearly written, despite the complexity of the topic.  The introduction effectively motivates the problem and presents the core contribution.
* **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** Tackling the intractability of ML learning in graphical models, even within constraints, is an important contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Limitations:** The core assumption of “fast-mixing parameter sets” is crucial. While the paper provides examples (Ising model), identifying such sets for arbitrary models may be challenging and require specialized knowledge. The reliance on knowing constants *C* and *α* characterizing the mixing rate is a potential bottleneck.
*   **Conservatism of Bounds:** As acknowledged by the authors, the derived bounds might be conservative, potentially leading to higher computational costs than necessary in practice. The example section confirms this.
*   **Projection Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions the complexity of projection onto Θ. While stating it’s often negligible, this can become a significant factor for high-dimensional parameter spaces, and a deeper discussion of projection techniques and their computational cost would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  While an example is provided, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the practical performance of the algorithm across different model types and datasets would significantly strengthen the paper. The example is a little too small to be truly convincing.
* **Discussion of Initialization:** The paper largely treats the initial distribution *r* as arbitrary. Further discussion of the impact of initialization, particularly leveraging the training data (e.g., contrastive divergence), and its effect on mixing time, would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a fundamental problem in statistical inference.
*   The FPRAS result is a significant contribution to the field, providing guarantees for learning in constrained parameter spaces.
*   The generality of the framework makes it applicable to a wide range of models.
*   The practical guidance provided regarding hyperparameter settings is valuable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumption of fast-mixing parameter sets may limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The theoretical bounds might be conservative, leading to potentially high computational costs.
*   The lack of a comprehensive empirical evaluation raises concerns about the practical performance of the algorithm.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. While the practical limitations related to identifying fast-mixing parameter sets and potential conservatism of the bounds are concerns, the novelty and rigor of the work outweigh these weaknesses. The FPRAS result is a valuable addition to the theoretical toolkit for learning in graphical models and beyond. 

**Rating:**  **Accept (with minor revisions)**

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*   **Expand Discussion on Projection:** Provide a more detailed discussion of projection techniques and their computational complexity, particularly for high-dimensional parameter spaces.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** Include a more comprehensive empirical evaluation of the algorithm on a wider range of models and datasets.
*   **Initialization Strategies:**  Discuss the impact of different initialization strategies, such as contrastive divergence, on mixing time and overall performance.
*   **Address Conservatism:** Explore methods for tightening the theoretical bounds or adaptively adjusting hyperparameters during optimization to mitigate the potential for conservatism.
*   **Clarify Practical Guidance:** More directly compare the proposed method with existing alternatives like pseudolikelihood or composite likelihoods in terms of practical computational cost and performance.
