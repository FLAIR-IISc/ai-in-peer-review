This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in general undirected graphical models. Instead of restricting the graph structure to be tractable (e.g., a tree or low-treewidth graph), the authors propose a fundamentally different approach: restricting the model parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing." The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a projected gradient descent algorithm that uses MCMC to approximate the gradient of the log-likelihood. The paper proves that this common heuristic, under the fast-mixing assumption, constitutes a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained MLE problem. The analysis is presented for the general exponential family and provides explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples per step (M), and MCMC chain length (v) to achieve a desired accuracy `ϵ` with high probability. Separate, detailed convergence results are provided for the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (L2-regularized) cases.

The work is well-situated within the literature. It directly tackles the limitations of alternative estimators like pseudolikelihood or score matching, which lack guarantees of converging to the M-projection (the KL-divergence minimizer) in the misspecified model setting. It also provides a formal theoretical foundation for a large family of MCMC-based learning algorithms (e.g., contrastive divergence and its variants), which are widely used in practice but often lack convergence guarantees due to the biased nature of the gradient estimates. The paper cleverly leverages classic results on MCMC mixing times (e.g., for Ising models) to define the tractable parameter sets `Θ` and combines this with modern analyses of gradient descent with inexact gradients (e.g., Schmidt et al. [26]) to derive its main results.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)
1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Core Idea:** The central concept of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets, rather than graph structure, is both elegant and significant. This perspective shift connects the fields of MCMC theory and optimization for machine learning in a novel way, providing a new lens through which to view the problem of learning intractable models.
2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's primary strength is its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors carefully account for the bias in the MCMC-based gradient estimator, which is the key technical challenge. Deriving explicit complexity bounds (`KMv`) in terms of problem parameters (`L`, `R_2`, `C`, `α`) and desired accuracy (`ϵ`, `δ`) is a substantial contribution. This elevates a common practical heuristic to a provably convergent algorithm.
3.  **Generality:** The results are framed in the general context of exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics. While motivated by graphical models like the Ising model, this generality makes the theoretical framework applicable to a wider range of problems.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem setup, assumptions, and main theorems are stated with precision. The inclusion of proof sketches in the main body provides valuable intuition for the reader, and the notation is clearly defined.

### Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement)
1.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The analysis hinges on two key assumptions: (i) knowledge of a convex, fast-mixing set `Θ`, and (ii) the ability to efficiently project onto `Θ`. While the paper provides examples where this is feasible (e.g., element-wise bounds or spectral norm constraints on Ising parameters), the projection step itself can be a complex optimization problem. The overall complexity analysis, focused on `KMv`, neglects the cost of these `K` projections, which might not be negligible, especially for large models.
2.  **Conservatism of the Bounds:** As acknowledged by the authors in the example, the derived theoretical bounds are likely to be very conservative. The schedules for `K`, `M`, and `v` may be orders of magnitude larger than what is needed in practice. This limits the direct practical utility of the results for setting hyperparameters, making the contribution more of a theoretical existence proof than a practical guide. The fact that a hand-tuned Lipschitz constant `L` worked better than the theoretical one in the experiment highlights this gap.
3.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the paper's contribution is primarily theoretical, it would be strengthened by a more thorough empirical section. The single small-scale example demonstrates the algorithm's feasibility but does not provide insight into how the learned M-projection within `Θ` compares to solutions from other approximate methods (like pseudolikelihood) in a misspecified setting. Such a comparison would help substantiate the claim that optimizing the "correct" (though constrained) objective function leads to better models.
4.  **Gap with Common Practice:** The analysis assumes independent MCMC runs from an arbitrary starting distribution for each gradient step. This diverges from common practical strategies like contrastive divergence (which initializes from data) or persistent contrastive divergence (which uses a "pool" of particles from the previous iteration). The authors correctly note this limitation, but it means the formal guarantees do not apply to some of the most popular variants of the algorithm being analyzed.

### Summary of Evaluation

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, and the theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs. The authors are upfront about the limitations of their analysis.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is very well-written, organized, and easy to follow for an expert in the field.
*   **Originality:** High. The conceptual framing of tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets is novel and insightful. It provides the first formal convergence guarantees of this type for MCMC-based MLE.
*   **Significance:** High. This work provides a crucial theoretical foundation for a widely-used class of algorithms. It has the potential to influence future research on learning intractable models by encouraging a focus on parameter constraints that ensure computational tractability for both learning and inference.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

The weaknesses, particularly regarding the practicality of the assumptions and the conservatism of the bounds, are valid but are characteristic of initial theoretical work in this domain. They do not undermine the core contribution. The paper's conceptual novelty and theoretical rigor are significant and provide a valuable new perspective on a fundamental problem in machine learning. It lays a solid foundation upon which future work can build to bridge the gap between theory and practice.
