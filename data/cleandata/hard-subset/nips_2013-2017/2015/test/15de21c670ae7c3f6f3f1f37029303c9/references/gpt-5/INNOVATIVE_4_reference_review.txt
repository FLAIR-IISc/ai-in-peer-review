Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families by restricting parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ where an MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast for all θ ∈ Θ. Under this assumption and the ability to project onto Θ, the authors analyze projected gradient descent that uses MCMC to estimate gradients, and provide high-probability convergence guarantees with explicit schedules for (i) the number of iterations K, (ii) number of MCMC samples M per iteration, and (iii) chain length v. They treat both the convex case (λ=0) and the strongly convex case (λ>0) and derive overall work bounds in terms of total Markov transitions KMv. For convex f they show additive objective accuracy O(ε) with total work roughly O(ε−3 log(1/ε)); for the strongly convex case they show parameter error O(ε) with total work O(ε−2 up to logs). They also prove a Lipschitz gradient bound for the regularized log-likelihood. The work is framed as an FPRAS for the MLE within Θ, and includes discussion of projecting to known fast-mixing sets for Ising/MRFs (e.g., bounded interaction strength or spectral norm).

Relation to prior work
- MCMC-MLE: Builds on long-standing practice (Geyer; Gu & Zhu) of using MCMC-estimated gradients for MLE, but those lacked rigorous end-to-end optimization complexity under mixing assumptions.
- Fast-mixing parameterization: Extends and leverages mixing-time results and “project to fast-mixing” ideas (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), but pushes them into an optimization-complexity framework for MLE.
- Alternatives to likelihood (pseudo-likelihood, score matching, composite likelihood): Contrasts with these in that it targets the M-projection even under model misspecification and provides a way to learn models where inference is tractable via MCMC.
- Contrastive divergence and fast-mixing training (Hinton; Tieleman; Steinhardt & Liang): The paper clarifies when CD-like schemes could approximate MLE under mixing guarantees and how to tune K, M, v.

Strengths
- Novel angle: Recasting tractability as membership in a fast-mixing set and showing that this yields an approximation scheme for MLE feels like a substantial conceptual contribution. It connects mixing time theory, convex optimization with inexact gradients, and statistical learning in a clean way.
- Generality: The results are stated for general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not only pairwise MRFs.
- Technical care: Clear decomposition of bias/variance from MCMC, use of concentration inequalities, and adoption of inexact projected gradient descent bounds (Schmidt et al.). Separate treatments for convex and strongly convex cases are appreciated.
- Explicit schedules and lower bounds: The matching upper/lower bounds (up to logs and constants) on total work are valuable for understanding the fundamental cost of MCMC-based MLE under fast-mixing.
- Honest scope: The paper is transparent about practical limitations and does not oversell empirical performance.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions/practicality:
  - Uniform mixing constants: The guarantees require known C and α that hold uniformly over Θ. In practice these constants are unknown and often extremely conservative; guidance on robust tuning without them would improve applicability.
  - Projection oracle: The results hinge on efficient projection onto Θ. While specific constructions exist for certain Ising/MRF norms, it is unclear how broadly this is computationally feasible for general exponential families or large-scale settings where SVD-based projections may dominate runtime.
  - Choice of sampler: Results assume a fixed kernel Mθ with TV-geometry mixing. Are the guarantees sensitive to the sampler (e.g., Gibbs vs. Swendsen–Wang), and can nonreversible samplers or SGLD variants be handled?
- FPRAS terminology: The paper claims an FPRAS for MLE in Θ, but the theorems provide additive accuracy for f (or parameter distance) with polynomial effort. For optimization, “(F)PTAS” or “additive PTAS” is more standard. Please clarify the approximation notion and whether guarantees are additive or multiplicative in f.
- Independence of samples across iterations: The analysis requires fresh independent chains per iteration; this excludes the common “persistent chains/pools” strategy that works well empirically. Discussion of whether the analysis can be extended to dependent initializations would broaden impact.
- Limited empirical validation: The 4×4 Ising toy is illustrative but very small and uses extremely weak couplings. Even a small suite (e.g., grids of moderate size) to show the schedules’ qualitative behavior and projection costs would help.
- Missing algorithm details: The text references Algorithm 1 but it is not included in the provided excerpt. Precise pseudocode, including step sizes and projection steps, is needed.

Quality
The main claims are primarily theoretical and appear technically sound given the sketches, standard tools, and cited lemmas. Some constants and norms (R2 vs. R2 2, etc.) could be double-checked for consistency. The dependence of L on R2 is reasonable. The work is complete as a theory paper, but adding full proofs (or a clearly accessible appendix) is important.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Suggestions:
- Define precisely the approximation guarantee when stating “FPRAS.”
- State the algorithm explicitly with step-size policy and projection operator.
- Collect assumptions (bounded sufficient statistics, projection oracle, uniform mixing) in a single assumption block.

Originality
High. The unification of fast-mixing parameter sets with provably efficient MLE optimization, including explicit work bounds and projection-based enforcement of mixing constraints, feels new and impactful.

Significance
Potentially high. This work charts a principled path for learning high-treewidth models by focusing on parameter regimes where MCMC is provably tractable. It may influence both theory (optimization with biased MCMC gradients) and practice (designing learnable, amenable model classes).

Pros
- Conceptually bold reframing of tractability.
- Solid theoretical development with concrete complexity bounds.
- Broad applicability to exponential families.
- Clear connections to existing MCMC learning practices.

Cons
- Strong assumptions (uniform mixing constants; projection oracle).
- Limited empirical support; unclear practical tightness of constants.
- Terminology around FPRAS may be nonstandard for optimization.
- Analysis excludes persistent-chain strategies common in practice.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). The paper introduces a compelling and novel direction with solid theory that could stimulate follow-up work, even if practicality is currently limited by assumptions. I would support acceptance conditional on clarifying the approximation terminology and including the full algorithm and proof details.

Confidence
Moderate.
