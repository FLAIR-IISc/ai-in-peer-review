- Proposes restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set Θ so MCMC-based gradient descent for undirected graphical models has rigorous ML convergence guarantees.  
- Provides theoretical bounds on the number of gradient steps K, samples M, and MCMC transitions v needed for target accuracy.  
- Distinguishes and analyzes both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases with separate convergence rates.  
- Builds on existing mixing-time literature and connects it to practical MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning.  
- Gives rigorous proofs (Theorems 1–9) with clear proof sketches and well-justified mixing-time assumptions.  
- Offers concrete parameter schedules and an illustrative Ising-model example to demonstrate application of the bounds.  
- Clearly acknowledges limitations, including focus on theory over empirical validation and assumptions about projection efficiency.  
- Paper is well-organized with consistent notation and accessible to readers familiar with graphical models and optimization.  
- Lacks systematic empirical validation to test whether theoretical parameter choices (K, M, v) are necessary in practice.  
- Assumes efficient projection onto Θ without discussing the computational complexity or scalability of that projection.  
- Does not address common practical strategies like maintaining a sample pool between iterations (e.g., contrastive divergence).  
- Limited discussion on extending results beyond exponential families or relating to other tractable-learning approaches (e.g., variational methods).  
- Technically sound and complete theoretical contribution rather than a position paper.  
- Novel contribution by providing rigorous ML guarantees for MCMC-based learning under fast-mixing parameter constraints.  
- Significant for bridging theoretical understanding and common practical methods; recommended for acceptance (Strong Accept).  
- Suggested improvements: discuss projection complexity scaling, add empirical comparison for theoretical parameters, and relate results to other tractable-learning approaches.
