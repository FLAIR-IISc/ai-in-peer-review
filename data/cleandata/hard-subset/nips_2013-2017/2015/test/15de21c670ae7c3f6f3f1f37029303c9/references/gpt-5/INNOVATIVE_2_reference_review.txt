Summary
This paper proposes a principled way to make maximum-likelihood learning tractable for arbitrary-treewidth exponential families by restricting parameters to a set Θ in which a chosen MCMC operator mixes rapidly. Under two key capabilities—(i) projection onto Θ and (ii) known geometric mixing bounds (C, α) uniform over Θ—the authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated by MCMC samples and prove an FPRAS for ML within Θ. They provide convergence guarantees in both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, derive Lipschitz constants, quantify the dependency of optimization error on the number of iterations (K), samples (M), and Markov steps (v), and give near-optimal schedules for K, M, v. The framework is general to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics and is instantiated for Ising/Potts models using known mixing bounds and projection operators. A small Ising example illustrates the prescriptions.

Relation to prior work
The work contrasts with tractable learning via treewidth (e.g., Chow-Liu) and with surrogate/approximate objectives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood), and with correlation-decay/high-girth approaches. It builds on mixing-time literature (e.g., Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum, Hayes), “subgraphs world” chains for ferromagnetic Ising (Jerrum–Sinclair; Huber), and prior work on projecting parameters to fast-mixing sets (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke). It differs from Steinhardt & Liang’s fast-mixing restart chains by targeting ML within a predefined fast-mixing Θ with explicit optimization complexity bounds. To my knowledge, the explicit FPRAS for ML in general exponential families under fast-mixing parameter sets is novel.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: reframes tractability around mixing rather than graph structure, yielding an ML estimator whose inference is tractable by design.
- Generality: applies to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics; not tied to a specific MCMC or model, as long as mixing bounds and projection are available.
- Theory with actionable prescriptions: clear, quantitative dependence on K, M, v, and mixing constants; near-matching lower and upper bounds; both convex and strongly convex regimes.
- Addresses biased stochastic gradients: careful handling of bias due to finite-time MCMC, with concentration arguments and use of inexact gradient descent theory.
- Bridges communities: connects learning guarantees to mixing-time results and projection oracles studied in earlier NIPS work.

Weaknesses and questions
- Strong assumptions on Θ: The guarantees hinge on having a convex Θ with uniform geometric mixing constants (C, α) and an efficient projection operator. For many models, tight, instance-specific mixing bounds are unknown or very loose; the practicality of specifying Θ with usable C, α is unclear.
- Access to constants: The schedules require C, α, R, L. In practice these are hard to estimate; discuss adaptive variants that avoid prior knowledge (e.g., doubling strategies, diagnostics-based stopping) while retaining PAC-style guarantees.
- Sampling independence: The analysis restarts M independent chains per iteration to get iid draws from qk. This can be substantially more costly than using a single long chain or a persistent pool. The paper notes this limitation; a theoretical treatment of persistent chains would substantially strengthen impact.
- Projection complexity ignored in total work: While the paper argues projection cost is often negligible, for spectral-norm constraints or general pairwise potentials it may dominate for large models. A complexity account including typical projection oracles would improve the story.
- Empirical validation is minimal: Only a toy Ising example is shown; there is no evaluation of projection overheads, tightness of bounds, or comparison to standard MCMC-ML, pseudolikelihood, or CD on moderate-sized graphs. Even small-scale empirical studies showing end-to-end feasibility would help.
- Scope of “FPRAS” claim: The result is an FPRAS conditional on a projection oracle and known mixing-rate constants. Please clarify the formal oracle model and whether the algorithm remains FPRAS if C, α are estimated on the fly.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The notation block helps. Some notational glitches (R2 vs R2 squared, D used ambiguously in the example) could be cleaned up. Proof sketches are informative; assuming a full appendix exists, this is fine for conference length.

Significance
I view this as a bold and promising direction. It offers a new lens: learn under a fast-mixing constraint to obtain an ML estimator that admits efficient sampling/inference and has polynomial-time learning guarantees. Even if immediate practicality is limited by the availability of Θ and tight mixing bounds, the conceptual and technical contributions could influence future work on mixing-aware learning, design of Θ for richer models, and adaptive mixing-time control in learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the precise algorithmic oracle assumptions under which the FPRAS holds (projection oracle, access to C, α), and discuss robustness if these are misspecified.
- Provide guidance or diagnostics for choosing Θ beyond simple norm constraints; e.g., nonuniform bounds respecting graph degree, or extensions to non-Gibbs samplers.
- Explore adaptive schedules that do not require a priori L, C, α.
- Add experiments on modest-sized graphs comparing to pseudolikelihood/score matching and CD, reporting optimization error and total wall-clock including projection.
- Discuss extension to continuous exponential families and other MCMC kernels (e.g., Langevin), and to hidden-variable models.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel conceptual framework and clear theoretical advances (FPRAS, convergence schedules, lower bounds).
- General and potentially impactful direction that may spur new research.
- Solid, careful analysis of biased MCMC gradients.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions (known Θ with uniform mixing bounds and efficient projection).
- Limited empirical evidence and unclear practical competitiveness today.
- Some important practical regimes (persistent chains, pool sampling) not covered theoretically.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline-strong). The paper is bold and novel with solid theory; despite limited experiments and strong assumptions, it opens an interesting direction and fits NeurIPS.

Confidence
Moderate.
