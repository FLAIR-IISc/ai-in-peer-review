## NIPS Review: Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

**Summary:**

This paper presents a fascinating theoretical result: under the assumption of a "fast-mixing parameter set" (defined by a geometrically decaying total variation distance between the MCMC sampling distribution and the stationary distribution), a Fast Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for maximum likelihood learning within that set.  The work frames this in the context of graphical models, motivated by the intractability of maximum likelihood estimation for general graphs, and suggests constraining parameters to a tractable set.  However, it generalizes the result to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. The core contribution is establishing conditions under which gradient descent, combined with MCMC for gradient estimation, provably converges to a near-optimal solution within the constrained parameter space, with explicit bounds on the computational cost (number of MCMC samples and iterations).

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the landscape of approaches to intractable maximum likelihood estimation. It acknowledges limitations of pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods (sample complexity, M-projection convergence for mis-specified models).  It contrasts its approach with methods that exploit specific graph structures (Chow-Liu, high-girth models), highlighting that it offers a more general framework based on mixing properties rather than explicit graph structure.  The connection to prior work on mixing times (Jerrum & Sinclair, Hayes, Dyer et al.) is clearly established, and the paper builds on this by connecting mixing time guarantees to learning guarantees. The references are comprehensive and demonstrate a good understanding of the field.  The comparison to Steinhardt & Liang’s work on learning with restart probabilities is relevant and provides useful context.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper's main result is a significant theoretical advance.  Establishing the existence of an FPRAS under the fast-mixing assumption is a valuable contribution.  The proofs appear rigorous, and the decomposition of error terms is well-motivated.
* **Generality:**  Moving beyond graphical models to the broader exponential family setting enhances the applicability and theoretical importance of the result.
* **Explicit Computational Guarantees:** Providing bounds on the number of samples, MCMC iterations, and gradient descent steps needed to achieve a specified accuracy is crucial for understanding the practical implications of the theory.
* **Clear Presentation of Key Assumptions:** The assumption of a fast-mixing parameter set is clearly defined and justified, allowing readers to assess its applicability to various models.
* **Practical Illustration:** The Ising model example, although simple, demonstrates the applicability of the theory and provides some numerical insights.
* **Thorough Discussion:** The authors engage with potential limitations and future directions, acknowledging issues like the cost of projection and the initialization of MCMC chains.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality Concerns:** While theoretically sound, the constant factors hidden in the bounds on the computational cost (KMv) could be very large.  The required number of MCMC samples, particularly in the convex case, might be prohibitive for many real-world applications.  The example highlights this, requiring a substantial number of samples even for a relatively small Ising model.  The paper doesn't offer much guidance on how to tighten these bounds in practice.
* **Fast-Mixing Assumption:**  The core assumption of a fast-mixing parameter set is strong. While the paper discusses models where this holds, verifying this assumption for complex models might be challenging, and could require significant effort.  The practical cost of *finding* such a set, or projecting onto it, isn’t fully addressed.
* **Projection Complexity:** The paper acknowledges that projection onto the constraint set Θ can be computationally expensive. Although examples like the Ising model exist, there isn’t a general, efficient projection algorithm applicable to all exponential families.  This adds to the practical overhead.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The example is primarily illustrative. A more thorough empirical evaluation on more realistic datasets and models would strengthen the paper and provide more insights into the practicality of the approach.
* **Repetitive Explanations:** Some concepts and derivations are repeated several times throughout the paper, increasing its length without adding significant value.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically significant result with potential implications for learning in intractable graphical models and more general exponential families.
* The mathematical analysis is rigorous and well-presented.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized, providing a thorough discussion of related work and limitations.
* The generality of the result is a key strength.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical feasibility of the approach is questionable due to potentially large constant factors in the computational cost and the difficulty of verifying the fast-mixing assumption.
* The lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation limits the ability to assess the real-world performance of the algorithm.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of statistical learning. However, the practical limitations and lack of extensive empirical validation raise concerns about its immediate impact. While the theoretical results are compelling, the substantial computational costs associated with the proposed method need further investigation.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, but with the following requests for revision:

1. **Discussion of Constant Factors:**  Address the potential for large constant factors in the computational bounds. Can the authors provide any insights into how these factors might be reduced in practice, or how they depend on specific model parameters?
2. **Projection Complexity:** Expand the discussion of projection complexity. Briefly survey existing projection algorithms for different exponential families, and highlight the challenges involved.
3. **Empirical Exploration:** Consider adding a small-scale empirical evaluation on a slightly more complex dataset or model (beyond the Ising model). This would provide some preliminary evidence of the algorithm's performance.
4. **Streamline Presentation:**  Reduce some of the repetitive explanations to improve conciseness.




I believe that addressing these points will strengthen the paper and enhance its impact on the NIPS community.
