Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a tractability notion based on fast-mixing Markov chains. The key idea is to restrict parameters to a convex set Θ that guarantees geometric mixing (with known constants C, α), and to project gradient updates onto Θ. The authors analyze a standard projected gradient method that uses MCMC to approximate gradients, and prove high-probability convergence rates in both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) settings. They give explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain length v, with total work scaling polynomially in 1/ε, and argue this yields an FPRAS for ML within Θ. The paper connects to fast-mixing conditions for Ising and general pairwise MRFs (e.g., via spectral/induced norm constraints on couplings) and to projection schemes that enforce such constraints. Experiments are minimal (a 4×4 Ising toy), and the authors focus on theory.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned against classical surrogates (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood) and structure-constrained families (e.g., Chow–Liu trees). It builds on (i) MCMC-based ML estimation (e.g., Geyer; Gu & Zhu), (ii) mixing time bounds for spin systems (Jerrum–Sinclair, Hayes, Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum), (iii) projection to fast-mixing parameter sets (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), (iv) inexact gradient descent (Schmidt et al.), and (v) fast-mixing-with-restarts learning (Steinhardt & Liang). The combination—projecting onto a uniformly fast-mixing Θ and providing an end-to-end convergence complexity for MLE within Θ—appears to be the main incremental step.

Strengths
- Provides clear, end-to-end high-probability convergence guarantees for MLE within a mixing-safe parameter set using a widely used MCMC-in-the-loop gradient method.
- Clean decomposition of error into sampling variance and mixing bias; schedules for K, M, v are explicit, and lower bounds are discussed.
- The framework is general for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics; not tied to a specific model or sampler.
- Relates practical projection schemes for Ising/MRFs (e.g., spectral-norm bounds) to learning guarantees.
- Writing is generally clear; limitations are candidly discussed.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heavy reliance on strong, hard-to-verify assumptions: a convex Θ with uniform geometric mixing constants C, α known a priori, and an efficient projection oracle ΠΘ. For many realistic models, certifying (let alone estimating) C, α is nontrivial; the schedules critically require them.
- Practicality gap: The analysis requires independent chains per iteration started from an arbitrary r and sufficiently long v, which is not how practitioners typically run MCMC-ML (persistent chains, pools, CD/PCD). The paper explicitly does not handle these widely used strategies.
- Minimal empirical validation. Only a toy Ising example with chosen constants (including an adjusted Lipschitz L) is shown; no comparison to pseudolikelihood/score matching/composite likelihood or to standard MCMC-ML heuristics. No demonstration that projection to fast-mixing Θ yields competitive fit or inference quality on realistic data.
- The “FPRAS” claim is imprecise. For optimization, the standard terminology is (randomized) PTAS/FPTAS; FPRAS is typically for counting. The results are additive guarantees on f or parameter error, not multiplicative approximation to an optimum. Please clarify the problem formalization and accuracy notion consistent with standard definitions.
- Constants and dependence: The bounds hide potentially large dependence on dimension via R and L and on (1−α)−1 (which can be huge near phase transitions). The results are “polynomial” but may be vacuous in regimes where fast mixing is fragile.
- Some steps need tighter justification/notation cleanup. The Lipschitz gradient claim uses a TV bound ∥pθ−pφ∥TV ≤ 2R2∥θ−φ∥2 under ∥t(x)∥2 ≤ R2; this is stronger than typical inequalities and depends on precise norms. Notation around “R2” (a bound vs its square) is confusing.
- Projection cost is relegated to a footnote in the discussion. For spectral-norm constraints, repeated SVDs can dominate runtime for large models; the end-to-end complexity should incorporate projection cost or at least bound it in standard regimes.
- Mixed regularization/objective guarantees: convex case bounds suboptimality in f (average iterate); strongly convex bounds parameter error (last iterate). Mapping between the two is discussed qualitatively, but a unified result or sharper high-probability f-suboptimality under λ>0 would be stronger.

Questions for the authors
1. Precise FPRAS statement: What is the exact optimization problem instance (input size), output, and accuracy notion? Is the guarantee additive in f or multiplicative relative to f(θ∗)? Please align with standard terminology.
2. How are C and α obtained in practice for a given Θ beyond textbook Ising cases? Can the algorithm be made adaptive to unknown mixing rates (e.g., via coupling/diagnostics) while retaining guarantees?
3. What is the formal dependence of R and L on model size (e.g., number of factors, alphabet size)? Can you provide explicit dimension dependence in the main theorems?
4. Can your analysis be extended to persistent chains or pool-based initializations (as commonly used), perhaps via mixing-from-warm-start bounds?
5. For general pairwise MRFs, which Θ sets admit efficient projection beyond spectral/induced norms? What are the complexities per projection?

Suggestions for improvement
- Substantially expand experiments: include moderate-size grids and random graphs, compare to pseudolikelihood/score matching/composite likelihood and to MCMC-ML heuristics; measure likelihood gap vs unconstrained estimators, mixing speed, and runtime.
- Clarify and standardize the “FPRAS” claim, or adopt more conventional optimization terminology (randomized PTAS/FPTAS) with precise additive/multiplicative accuracy.
- Provide a self-contained proof or clear reference for the Lipschitz gradient bound and the TV–parameter distance inequality; fix notation ambiguities around R, R2.
- Discuss/derive schedules that do not require knowing C, α, potentially using doubling/halving schemes with checks.
- Account for projection cost in the stated complexity, or bound it under standard Θ choices.

Overall assessment
Technically careful and potentially useful theoretical contribution, but under strong assumptions and with limited empirical grounding. The main guarantees are not surprising given existing tools (mixing bounds + inexact projected gradient), and the practical impact is unclear without validating that projection to fast-mixing Θ yields competitive models and that required constants can be handled in practice.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). I would reconsider if the claims are reframed (terminology, constants), empirical validation is strengthened substantially, and some adaptation to unknown mixing rates or more realistic sampling regimes is provided.

Confidence: High.
