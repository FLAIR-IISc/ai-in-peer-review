

# Review

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by focusing on "fast-mixing parameter sets" rather than traditional tractable structures like low-treewidth models. The core insight is that if we constrain parameters to a set where MCMC mixing is guaranteed to be rapid (i.e., the Markov chain converges quickly to the stationary distribution), then we can achieve a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within that set. This represents a fundamentally different perspective on tractability compared to previous work, which typically focuses on structural constraints like treewidth (e.g., Chow-Liu trees) or variational approximations.

The authors establish convergence guarantees for gradient descent using MCMC-based gradient approximations. They prove that with appropriate scheduling of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and Markov transitions, the algorithm achieves a desired optimization accuracy with a total computational effort that scales as (1/ϵ)^3 log(1/ϵ) for the convex case and (1/ϵ)^2 log(1/ϵ) for the strongly convex case. This is a significant theoretical contribution as it provides the first rigorous analysis showing that MCMC-based gradient descent can converge to the maximum likelihood solution within a fast-mixing parameter set.

The paper's novelty is particularly striking because it shifts the focus from the structure of the graphical model (treewidth) to the mixing properties of the MCMC sampler. Previous work on tractable learning (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching, or structured models with low treewidth) all assume specific structural constraints on the model, whereas this approach provides a more general framework that applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. The connection to fast-mixing parameter sets is particularly elegant, as it leverages known results on mixing times in statistical physics to provide theoretical guarantees for learning algorithms.

The theoretical analysis is sound, with careful attention to the interplay between the mixing rate of the Markov chain, the number of samples, and the number of gradient steps. The authors provide both convex and strongly convex convergence results with explicit bounds on the computational complexity. While the paper acknowledges that projection onto the fast-mixing parameter set might be computationally expensive for large models, they correctly note that this is typically negligible compared to the sampling cost.

The main weakness is the lack of practical contribution—the paper is purely theoretical and the example in Section 6 is very basic and doesn't demonstrate significant practical value. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper's conceptual contribution outweighs this limitation. The authors have identified a new direction for tractable learning that could inspire future practical implementations.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The background on mixing times and fast-mixing parameter sets is well-explained, and the proofs (while not detailed in the main text) appear to be technically sound based on the sketch provided. The comparison to previous work on graphical models (e.g., Chow-Liu trees, pseudolikelihood) is appropriate and highlights the novelty of the approach.

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling by introducing a new perspective on tractable learning. While it doesn't provide a practical algorithm that outperforms existing methods, it establishes a new theoretical framework that could guide future research. The results have broad applicability beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.

In terms of significance, this work represents a conceptual leap in how we think about tractable maximum likelihood learning. It addresses a fundamental limitation of existing methods (their lack of guarantees under model misspecification) by providing a novel framework that works for arbitrary models as long as we can identify a fast-mixing parameter set. This could lead to new approaches for learning in complex models where traditional methods fail.

The paper is original in its core idea, clearly differentiating itself from previous work on tractable learning. While there are related works on fast-mixing MCMC (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang, 2013), this paper is the first to connect fast-mixing properties directly to maximum likelihood learning guarantees.

Given the high novelty of the core idea and the sound theoretical contribution, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant conceptual advance in the field, even if it doesn't provide a practical algorithm. The theoretical framework it establishes is valuable and could inspire future work on both theoretical and practical aspects of tractable learning.
