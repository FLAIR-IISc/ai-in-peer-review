

# Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is generally intractable due to the #P-completeness of partition function evaluation (Jerrum and Sinclair [1993]). The authors propose a novel approach by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing parameter set" Θ, where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. The key contribution is theoretical: they show that when parameters are constrained to such a set, gradient descent using MCMC-based gradient estimates can achieve a maximum likelihood solution inside Θ with rigorous convergence guarantees. The paper provides bounds on the computational complexity (in terms of the number of gradient descent steps K, samples per step M, and Markov chain transitions v) required to achieve a desired accuracy for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases.

The work builds on existing literature about mixing times (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair [1993], Domke and Liu [2013]) and connects it to the practical problem of maximum likelihood learning. The authors distinguish between the convex and strongly convex cases, providing separate convergence guarantees for each, and illustrate their theoretical results with an example on an Ising model.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Rigor**: The paper provides rigorous proofs for all main results (Theorems 1-9), with clear proof sketches that convey the intuition behind the technical arguments. The assumptions about mixing times (Equation 2) are well-justified by existing literature on Markov chain mixing.

2. **Clear Distinction Between Convex and Strongly Convex Cases**: The paper carefully distinguishes between the unregularized (convex) and regularized (strongly convex) cases, which is important for understanding the practical implications of the results. The different convergence rates and computational complexities for these two cases are clearly articulated.

3. **Practical Relevance**: While theoretical, the paper addresses a widely used practical approach (MCMC-based gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning) and provides concrete schedules for the algorithm's parameters (K, M, v) to achieve desired accuracy. The example in Section 6 demonstrates how the theoretical bounds can be applied in practice.

4. **Honest Assessment of Limitations**: The authors clearly acknowledge the limitations of their approach, such as the focus on theoretical guarantees rather than empirical validation, the assumption of efficient projection onto Θ, and the exclusion of common strategies like maintaining a "pool" of samples between iterations. This demonstrates scholarly integrity.

5. **Clear Organization and Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to example. The notation is consistent and well-defined, making it accessible to readers familiar with graphical models and optimization.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: While the paper provides a theoretical analysis and a single example, it doesn't include systematic empirical results to validate whether the theoretical bounds match practical performance. The example in Section 6 shows convergence but doesn't test whether the theoretically derived parameters (K=46, M=1533, v=561) are indeed necessary or if the algorithm could converge faster with fewer resources.

2. **Projection Complexity**: The paper assumes that projection onto the fast-mixing set Θ is efficient, but doesn't discuss the computational complexity of this projection step. For large models, this could be a significant bottleneck, especially if the projection algorithm scales super-linearly with model size.

3. **Exclusion of Common Practical Strategies**: The paper acknowledges that it doesn't address the common strategy of maintaining a "pool" of samples between iterations (e.g., contrastive divergence), which is widely used in practice. This limits the immediate applicability of the theoretical results to existing implementations.

4. **Limited Discussion of Broader Implications**: While the paper is focused on exponential families, it doesn't discuss whether the results extend to more general models or how they might interact with other approaches to tractable learning (e.g., variational methods).

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs that carefully address the dependencies between algorithm parameters and convergence guarantees. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis, and the authors are careful to acknowledge both the strengths and limitations of their approach. The paper is a complete theoretical contribution rather than a position paper.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The notation is consistent and well-defined, and the proof sketches provide good intuition for the main results. The example in Section 6 helps illustrate the theoretical results in a concrete setting. The only minor issue is that the mathematical notation could be dense for readers unfamiliar with mixing time theory, but this is to be expected for a theoretical paper at this level.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a novel contribution by providing rigorous theoretical guarantees for a widely used practical approach (MCMC-based gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning) under the assumption of fast-mixing parameter sets. While there is related work on mixing times (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair [1993], Domke and Liu [2013]), the specific application to maximum likelihood learning with convergence guarantees is new. The distinction between convex and strongly convex cases is particularly novel and valuable.

## Significance Assessment

The paper provides a theoretical foundation for a method that is commonly used in practice but lacks theoretical backing. This is significant because it helps explain why certain approaches work in practice and provides guidance for their implementation. The results also help bridge the gap between theoretical analysis and practical implementation in graphical models. While the paper doesn't claim practical improvements, it advances the theoretical understanding of a fundamental problem in graphical models.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a strong theoretical contribution to a fundamental problem in graphical models, with rigorous analysis that is well-justified and clearly presented. The paper is well-written, carefully considers its limitations, and provides valuable insights that could guide future work in both theory and practice.

The main limitations (lack of empirical validation, projection complexity, exclusion of common strategies) are acknowledged by the authors and are common in theoretical papers. The theoretical contribution is significant enough to warrant publication at NeurIPS, and the results could inform future work on practical implementation of MCMC-based learning algorithms.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the projection complexity might scale with model size, and whether it could become a bottleneck for large-scale applications.

2. If the authors have the opportunity, they could add a small empirical comparison showing whether the theoretically derived parameters (K, M, v) match practical performance, at least for the example they provide.

3. The paper could briefly discuss how their results might relate to other approaches for tractable learning (e.g., variational methods) to provide a broader context for their contribution.

## Final Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important problem in graphical models. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear distinction between convex and strongly convex cases. While it has some limitations in terms of empirical validation and practical considerations, these are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the theoretical significance of the work. I believe this paper would be of significant interest to the NeurIPS community and would advance the theoretical understanding of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models.

**Overall Rating: Strong Accept**
