Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families under an alternative notion of tractability: restricting parameters to a convex subset Θ for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes rapidly (geometric decay in total variation with constants C, α) and where Euclidean projection onto Θ is available. Within this regime, the authors analyze projected gradient descent that uses MCMC to approximate expectations in the likelihood gradient. They provide finite-sample, high-probability convergence guarantees in two regimes: convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (with ridge regularization). The analysis bounds bias (from finite mixing) and variance (from finite sampling), leverages Lipschitz smoothness of the regularized log-likelihood (new bound L = 4R2^2 + λ under bounded sufficient statistics), and applies results for inexact projected gradient methods (Schmidt et al., NIPS’11). They produce explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and mixing steps v to achieve additive ε accuracy in objective (convex case) or parameters (strongly convex), show lower bounds, and argue near-optimality up to constants/log factors. They position the results relative to treewidth-based tractability (e.g., Chow–Liu) and methods like pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood, and connect to prior work on fast-mixing parameter projections for Ising/MRFs.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on mixing-time results for Gibbs and related dynamics (Hayes; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Jerrum–Sinclair; Huber) and projection onto fast-mixing parameter sets (Domke & Liu NIPS’13; Liu & Domke NIPS’14).
- Uses inexact gradient descent theory (Schmidt et al., NIPS’11).
- Relates to MCML (Geyer’91; Gu & Zhu’01) and learning with restart-mixing chains (Steinhardt & Liang, ICML’15).
- Distinct from pseudolikelihood/score-matching/composite likelihood (Besag; Hyvarinen; Lindsay/Varin et al.) in targeting the M-projection, not a surrogate.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: reframing “tractability” via mixing-time-constrained parameter sets and showing that MLE within Θ is approximable efficiently using a standard algorithm with a principled schedule.
- Generality: applies to arbitrary exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics; does not rely on treewidth.
- Solid, careful finite-sample analysis that separates bias/variance contributions, with explicit, interpretable bounds and schedules for K, M, v; lower bounds help justify near-optimality.
- Connects to practical constructions of Θ (e.g., box or spectral norm constraints for Ising/MRFs) where projection is convex and implementable.
- Honest discussion of limitations (e.g., ignoring projection cost, not covering persistent chains), and a small illustrative example.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strength of assumptions and scope of Θ: The existence of a practically useful fast-mixing convex Θ and an efficient projection oracle is nontrivial beyond pairwise discrete models. For many exponential families (higher-order, latent-variable, continuous), constructing Θ and projecting may be difficult or open.
- FPRAS claim clarity: The paper states “there exists an FPRAS” but the guarantees are additive (on f or parameter error), while the canonical FPRAS definition is relative error with runtime polynomial in input size and 1/ε. Moreover, the constants (C, α, R2, L) and the projection oracle are treated as given; verifying/estimating them may not be polynomial in general, and mixing-time constants are rarely tight. The authors should precisely define their notion of approximation and the computational model (oracle assumptions) under which the FPRAS statement holds.
- Practicality and experiments: Only a tiny toy example is provided, and the constant choices (e.g., using smaller L than the bound) highlight the looseness. No demonstration on larger graphs or comparison with alternatives (pseudolikelihood, contrastive divergence under Θ, persistent chains) limits empirical relevance.
- Literature positioning: While MCML is cited, there is limited discussion of alternative analyses of MCMC-biased gradients (e.g., stochastic approximation with Markovian noise) and how the present finite-time bounds compare. The relation to contrastive divergence under explicit mixing constraints could be elaborated.
- Technical clarity: Some notation is confusing (R2 vs R2^2; L = 4R2^2 + λ), and several references in the bibliography are incomplete. The TV-to-expectation Lipschitz steps are plausible but would benefit from more explicit constants and conditions (e.g., boundedness of t is critical).

Quality
- The theoretical development appears sound, with the right tools for bias/variance control and inexact projected gradient. Proof sketches are reasonable; full proofs (appendix) are assumed correct. The lower/upper bound pairing strengthens the contribution. The FPRAS claim needs tightening/qualification.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized, with helpful summaries and intuition. However, improve notation consistency (define R clearly once and use consistently), state the exact definition of fast-mixing set Θ and oracle assumptions upfront, and polish the references.

Originality
- The idea of optimizing MLE within fast-mixing parameter sets and delivering explicit finite-time schedules seems novel as a unified, general framework. It extends prior projection-to-fast-mixing work from inference to learning with finite-time guarantees.

Significance
- For researchers interested in principled learning under mixing constraints, this gives a useful blueprint and bounds. Practical impact depends on availability of Θ and projection routines; currently most compelling for pairwise discrete models (Ising/Potts) with known mixing surrogates.

Specific suggestions/questions
- Precisely define the approximation notion underlying the “FPRAS” claim; if additive error, consider using FPTAS terminology or explicitly qualify the statement with oracle assumptions.
- Discuss how to estimate or upper-bound C and α in practice, and how mis-specification of these constants affects guarantees.
- Include runtime that accounts for projection cost, at least for the spectral-norm and box-constraint cases, to provide end-to-end complexity.
- Can the analysis be extended to persistent chains (sample pools) by handling dependence across iterations (e.g., via mixing coefficients)?
- Clarify applicability beyond discrete models with bounded t(x). For continuous families, is truncation or coercivity needed?
- Minor: fix notation (R2, R^2), typos, and complete references (years, venues).

Pros
- Solid, general theoretical contribution; explicit schedules and near-optimal bounds.
- Connects mixing-time theory to learning guarantees in a clean way.
- Applicable to widely studied discrete models with known Θ.

Cons
- Strong assumptions (existence/projection onto Θ, known mixing constants).
- Overstated FPRAS phrasing without precise model/relative-error guarantee.
- Minimal empirical validation.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6). The paper advances a clear theoretical line with appropriate rigor and breadth, though practical impact and some claims should be moderated. Confidence: medium.
