

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary
This paper addresses the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by introducing a novel approach that restricts parameters to "fast-mixing sets" where MCMC sampling converges quickly. The authors establish theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates when the parameter set Θ satisfies a geometric mixing condition (equation 2). They derive complexity bounds showing that the total computational effort grows as (1/ε)^3 log(1/ε) for convex cases and (1/ε)^2 log(1/ε) for strongly convex cases. This work differs fundamentally from previous tractable approaches like tree-structured models (Chow-Liu algorithm) or high-girth models (Heinemann and Globerson, 2014), which rely on specific graphical structure constraints rather than mixing properties. The paper builds on the exponential family framework and connects to related NIPS work on fast-mixing Markov chains (Steinhardt and Liang, 2014; Domke and Liu, 2013).

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis for both convex and strongly convex cases. The authors carefully account for both bias (from MCMC sampling not being exact) and variance (from sampling noise) in gradient estimates. The key theorems establish clear relationships between the desired optimization accuracy (ε), the mixing rate (α), and the total computational effort (KMv). The proof sketches are well-structured and provide good intuition for the main results, with detailed proofs deferred to the appendix as appropriate.

**Novelty:** The concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" represents a significant theoretical contribution that is distinct from prior tractable approaches to graphical model learning. While previous work has studied fast mixing for inference (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993; Hayes, 2006), this paper is the first to leverage fast mixing as a constraint for maximum likelihood learning with theoretical guarantees. The paper carefully differentiates its approach from related work on tree-structured models (Chow-Liu, 1968), high-girth models (Heinemann and Globerson, 2014), and pseudolikelihood (Y. Mizrahi et al., 2014), demonstrating clear novelty.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The notation is consistent and well-defined from the beginning. The main results are presented in logical sequence, with theorems building on each other. The example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the theoretical results with concrete parameter choices. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations (e.g., ignoring projection complexity, not covering the "pool of samples" approach), demonstrating intellectual honesty.

**Significance:** The paper provides a theoretically grounded foundation for a widely used practical approach (MCMC-based gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning). The complexity bounds offer concrete guidance for practitioners on setting algorithm parameters. While the paper notes it makes no significant practical contribution, the theoretical results could inform future algorithm design for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Practical Application:** The paper explicitly states it makes no significant practical contribution. While the theoretical results are sound, the paper does not demonstrate how the fast-mixing constraint could be applied to real-world models beyond the Ising example. The projection methods discussed (e.g., setting θij = β for |θij| > β) are trivial for some models but may be computationally challenging for others, which the authors acknowledge but do not elaborate on.

**Assumption of Known Mixing Parameters:** The approach requires knowing constants C and α that bound the mixing rate (equation 2). While the paper provides examples where these can be derived (e.g., for Ising models), it doesn't address how these constants would be estimated in practice for arbitrary models. This could limit the applicability of the approach without additional assumptions.

**Comparison to Existing MCMC-Based Methods:** The paper could more thoroughly compare its theoretical guarantees to existing MCMC-based learning methods. While it cites several papers (e.g., Geyer, 1991; Carreira-Perpiñán and Hinton, 2005), it doesn't explicitly contrast the complexity bounds with those of other MCMC-based learning algorithms.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- Provides the first theoretical guarantees for maximum likelihood learning using MCMC-based gradient descent with fast-mixing parameter sets
- Demonstrates clear novelty compared to previous tractable approaches (tree-structured, high-girth models)
- Rigorous analysis of both bias and variance in gradient estimates
- Well-structured, clearly written, and carefully references related work

**Con:**
- Limited practical contribution beyond theoretical analysis
- Assumes knowledge of mixing parameters C and α, which may not be readily available
- Does not address how to handle projection for complex fast-mixing sets
- The trivial projection for Ising models (setting θij = β for |θij| > β) may not generalize to other models

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first formal guarantees for maximum likelihood learning using MCMC-based gradient descent when the parameter set satisfies a fast-mixing condition. The results are technically sound, clearly presented, and represent a meaningful advance over previous work on tractable graphical model learning. While the practical contribution is limited to theoretical foundations, the results provide valuable insights for practitioners and future research in this area. The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS as it advances the theoretical understanding of a widely used learning approach.

The paper meets all four evaluation criteria:
- **Quality:** High - Rigorous theoretical analysis with careful attention to bias and variance
- **Clarity:** High - Exceptionally well-organized and clearly written
- **Originality:** High - Novel concept of fast-mixing parameter sets with clear differentiation from prior work
- **Significance:** Medium-high - Theoretical foundation for a common approach, with implications for future algorithm design

I recommend acceptance.
