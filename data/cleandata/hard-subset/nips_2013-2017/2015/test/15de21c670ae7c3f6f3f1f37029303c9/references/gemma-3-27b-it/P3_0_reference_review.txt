## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, a task typically intractable due to the partition function’s computational complexity. Instead of aiming for general tractability, the authors propose a novel approach focusing on parameter sets that guarantee fast mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. They demonstrate a surprising and valuable result: within such a “fast-mixing” parameter set Θ, a Fast Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for finding the maximum likelihood solution. The work generalizes beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics and provides convergence guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients, both in the convex and strongly convex settings. 

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of approximate maximum likelihood methods like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods, highlighting their shortcomings in sample complexity and guarantees under model misspecification. It also acknowledges the existing work on tractable parameterizations, like Chow-Liu trees and high-girth models, pointing out that those approaches still often lead to intractable inference. This work distinguishes itself by directly tackling the *sampling* bottleneck that plagues MCMC-based approaches, rather than attempting to simplify the model structure.  The connections to mixing time analysis for Ising models and other graphical models (Jerrum & Sinclair, Hayes, Liu & Domke) are well-articulated and form the foundational basis for the theoretical results. The use of projected gradient descent with error bounds builds on existing work (Schmidt et al.) but applies it to this specific MCMC context.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets to enable an FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning is a significant contribution. This shifts the focus from structural tractability to algorithmic tractability, opening up new possibilities for learning in complex models.
* **Generality:**  Extending the results beyond graphical models to the broader exponential family framework significantly enhances the applicability of the findings.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a detailed and mathematically sound analysis, establishing clear convergence guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC approximation. The separation of convex and strongly convex cases and the associated rate analysis are commendable.
* **Completeness:** The paper offers a complete theoretical analysis, including lower bounds on the computational cost.  The inclusion of an example with practical results strengthens the analysis.
* **Clear Presentation:**  Despite the technical complexity, the paper is reasonably well-written and organized, with helpful notation and a clear flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality Concerns:** While theoretically appealing, the practical implications remain somewhat unclear. The paper admits that the projection onto the “fast-mixing” set may itself be computationally expensive, especially for dense graphs. While examples are given for Ising models, the generalizability to other exponential families isn't fully explored in terms of projection cost.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** The example section reveals that the theoretical bounds are somewhat conservative, leading to potentially over-estimated computational costs. While a conservative bound is acceptable, further analysis to tighten these bounds could improve the practical utility of the method.
* **Limited Discussion of Sample Initialization:** The paper acknowledges the potential impact of sample initialization but does not delve deeply into strategies like contrastive divergence or other techniques for efficiently initializing the MCMC chains within the fast-mixing set.
* **Dependence on Mixing Time Bounds:** The performance crucially relies on accurate mixing time bounds. Obtaining tight bounds for complex models can be challenging, and the sensitivity of the FPRAS to the constants *C* and *α* (defining the mixing rate) isn't fully explored.  



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous and novel theoretical result with significant implications for maximum likelihood learning.
* **Potential for impact:** The idea of leveraging fast-mixing properties is a promising direction for tackling intractable learning problems.
* **High-quality presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it accessible to a broad audience. 

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Practical challenges:** The computational cost of projection and the conservatism of the bounds raise concerns about the immediate practicality of the approach.
* **Dependence on external bounds:** The effectiveness hinges on obtaining accurate mixing time bounds for specific models, which can be a non-trivial task.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and theoretically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical model learning.  The core idea of exploiting fast-mixing parameter sets to enable an FPRAS is novel and impactful. While practical concerns remain regarding the cost of projection and the tightness of the bounds, the theoretical framework is solid, and the generality of the results is commendable. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The authors should address the following minor revisions:

1. **Discuss the computational cost of projection** more thoroughly. Provide examples or insights into how the projection complexity scales with model size and density.
2. **Explore techniques for efficient sample initialization**, such as contrastive divergence, and discuss their potential benefits within the proposed framework.
3. **Consider sensitivity analysis** of the FPRAS performance with respect to the mixing time constants *C* and *α*. 
4. **Clarify the limitations** of the approach in scenarios where tight mixing time bounds are unavailable.
