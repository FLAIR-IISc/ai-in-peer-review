Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a different notion of tractability than bounded treewidth: constrain parameters to a convex set Θ for which an associated MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast, uniformly over Θ. Given the ability to project onto Θ and to run the mixing Markov chain, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where expectations in the gradient are approximated by MCMC samples. They provide convergence guarantees in two regimes: convex (λ=0) and strongly convex (λ>0) with explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain steps v, yielding polynomial total sampling effort. They claim this implies a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the MLE restricted to Θ. The analysis is framed for general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, with examples and projections for fast-mixing Ising/MRF parameter sets via Dobrushin/spectral-norm-type conditions. A toy 4×4 Ising example illustrates the schedules.

Strengths
- Conceptually interesting: makes the tractability set Θ be “fast-mixing,” tying learning and inference tractability through MCMC.
- Unified analysis for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not just specific MRFs.
- Uses standard inexact/proximal gradient tools (Schmidt et al.) to convert mixing bounds into optimization-time guarantees; schedules and (simple) lower bounds are explicit.
- Discussion is candid about limitations and common heuristics (e.g., contrastive divergence, sample pools).

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Quality/technical correctness
- FPRAS claim is not substantiated as per standard complexity-theory definitions. FPRAS typically refers to a multiplicative (1±ε) approximation with running time polynomial in input size and 1/ε. Here the guarantees are additive in the objective (and parameter distance for λ>0), with runtime depending on constants C and α from mixing bounds and on R (bound on sufficient statistics), which may be large or unknown. Moreover, the paper does not show how to compute C, α for a given Θ nor that they are polynomially bounded/available from the input. As stated, the “existence of an FPRAS” claim is misleading.
- Assumption of a uniform geometric TV bound ∥Mθ^v q − pθ∥TV ≤ C α^v for all θ∈Θ is very strong. The cited bounds (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral-norm) provide sufficient conditions but the constants can scale with N and can be very conservative; the paper treats C, α as given. The complexity expressions depend on 1/(1−α) and C, but there is no demonstration these are polynomially bounded in problem size for nontrivial Θ beyond simple bounded-interaction Ising.
- The Lipschitz gradient bound (Theorem 1) uses a nonstandard inequality ∥pθ−pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ−φ∥2 derived from bounded sufficient statistics. The proof sketch is too terse to assess validity; constants and norms (R2 vs R^2) are inconsistent, and the step “some effort can bound...” is the crux. Please provide a rigorous proof or appropriate references (e.g., Lipschitzness via bounded log-density differences, Pinsker + self-concordance bounds).
- The analysis assumes M i.i.d. samples per iteration from qk = Mθk−1^v r. This requires running M independent chains initialized from r and run for v transitions. In practice, one reuses a single chain or a sample pool; dependence breaks the independence-based concentration used here. The paper acknowledges this but then its guarantees are for an algorithm that is rarely used due to cost.
- Ambiguity in what a “Markov transition” means. For Gibbs, is v the number of single-site updates or full sweeps? The mixing bounds cited use single-site updates with random scans and scale with N; the total work KMv should reflect per-transition cost. This lack of precision affects whether the claimed polynomial runtime is meaningful.
- Lower bounds (Thm 3, 7) are arithmetic constraints on K, M, v, not complexity-theoretic lower bounds; useful but should be labeled as such.
- The example instantiates constants loosely (e.g., takes L=10 instead of the derived L=97) and yields weak evidence; no comparison to baselines (pseudolikelihood, score matching) or empirical verification of mixing assumptions.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous notational inconsistencies and typos impede readability:
  - R2 vs R^2 vs R2^2 vs Ra; N used for dimension and elsewhere; D is dataset size but also used as a bound in Theorem 5; V vs v in KMV; “K/K ∑ θk” inside f(·); “1δ ” missing parentheses; “√ × (2×.2)^2” in the example.
  - Some constants a,b,c appear abruptly in theorems without clear mapping back to model parameters.
  - Algorithm 1 is referenced but not shown; step sizes, projection step, and the exact update are not specified in the main text.
- Many references are incomplete/malformed (missing years/venues, inconsistent capitalization: e.g., “Inferning”; “Asuncion et al.” title; “Geyer” interface venue; etc.).

Originality
- The idea of projecting to fast-mixing parameter sets has appeared (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke). The present contribution is the convergence/runtime analysis of MCMC-based projected gradient under uniform mixing assumptions and bounded stats. This is incremental but potentially useful; however, the FPRAS positioning overstates novelty given the caveats above.

Significance
- Practical impact is limited by the strength and unverifiability of assumptions (uniform mixing over Θ; ability to compute/provide C, α; projection oracles). The algorithm variant analyzed (i.i.d. M samples from qk) is rarely used due to cost. The single toy experiment does not demonstrate utility or competitiveness.

Questions for authors
1. Please precisely define your use of “FPRAS” for optimization. Are your guarantees additive or multiplicative? On what quantity? How are C and α obtained from the input in polynomial time?
2. Clarify what a “Markov transition” counts and align v with the mixing bounds’ unit (single-site vs sweep).
3. Provide a complete proof (or citation) for Theorem 1’s TV bound and fix R-related notation.
4. Specify Algorithm 1 in the main text: step size, projection, sampling protocol (independence), and averaging.
5. Can your analysis be adapted to Markovian sampling noise (dependent samples per iteration) as in standard MCMC-SA frameworks?
6. In Theorem 5, D is overloaded; please fix. Similarly for KMV vs KMv.

Pros
- Clean, general analysis connecting mixing assumptions to optimization accuracy.
- Explicit schedules and complexity expressions.
- Conceptually appealing alternative to treewidth-based tractability.

Cons
- Dubious/overstated FPRAS claim; key constants are opaque and may be exponential.
- Strong, uniform mixing assumptions; independence of per-iteration samples is impractical.
- Many notation/formatting issues; missing algorithm details hinder reproducibility.
- Minimal empirical support; weak evidence of significance beyond prior work.

Recommendation: Reject (score: 4/10). The paper has an interesting angle and some useful analyses, but the FPRAS claim is not adequately justified, the assumptions are strong and difficult to verify, the presentation has many issues, and the experimental support is insufficient. With a clarified, rigorous statement of guarantees (eschewing FPRAS unless warranted), tightened proofs, corrected notation, explicit algorithms, and more substantive experiments, this could be a solid theoretical contribution.

Confidence: High.
