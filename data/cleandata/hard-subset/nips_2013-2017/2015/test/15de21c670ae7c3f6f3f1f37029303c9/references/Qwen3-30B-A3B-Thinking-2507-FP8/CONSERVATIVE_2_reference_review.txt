

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a set Θ where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling mixes rapidly. The authors claim that with such a constraint, gradient descent using MCMC samples can converge to the maximum likelihood solution within Θ with high probability, providing theoretical bounds on the required number of gradient steps, samples, and Markov chain transitions.

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for a gradient-based approach that uses MCMC to approximate gradients. The authors derive bounds for both convex and strongly convex cases, showing that the total computational work scales as O(1/ϵ³) for convex and O(1/ϵ²) for strongly convex problems. The analysis builds on existing work on gradient descent with noisy gradients and mixing times for MCMC.

The main strength of the paper is its theoretical rigor in establishing convergence guarantees for a common practical approach (using MCMC to approximate gradients). The paper carefully addresses the bias and variance in gradient estimation due to MCMC sampling, which is a genuine challenge in this domain.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and practical relevance, I have significant concerns about this paper:

1. **Lack of Practical Contribution**: The authors explicitly state "This paper claims no significant practical contribution," which is problematic for a NIPS submission. NIPS values both theoretical and practical advances, and the theoretical results don't translate to meaningful improvements in practice. The example in Section 6 uses an extremely small Ising model (4×4 grid with 5 training samples) that doesn't reflect real-world applications.

2. **Unrealistic Assumptions**: The approach relies on the strong assumption that the parameter set Θ can be constrained such that MCMC mixes rapidly (Equation 2). The paper doesn't provide evidence that such a constraint is meaningful for practical models. The example uses a very tight constraint (|θ_ij| ≤ 0.2) that severely limits model expressiveness.

3. **No Comparison to Established Methods**: The paper mentions pseudolikelihood, score matching, and other established approaches but fails to compare them to the proposed method on standard benchmarks. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the theoretical guarantees translate to practical advantages.

4. **Loose Theoretical Bounds**: The theoretical bounds are quite loose, as evidenced by the example where the theory suggests L=97 but the authors use L=10 for faster convergence. This suggests the bounds may not be tight enough to guide practical implementation.

5. **Neglected Practical Challenges**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of projecting onto Θ, which could be significant for large models. It also ignores how to choose the constraint set Θ in practice, which is a major practical hurdle.

6. **Lack of Experimental Validation**: The paper provides no experiments on real datasets or meaningful comparisons to established methods, which is a significant omission for a machine learning conference like NIPS.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a theoretical extension of existing work on gradient descent with noisy gradients, but it doesn't advance the state of the art in a meaningful way. The approach doesn't offer clear advantages over established methods like pseudolikelihood or score matching, which have been shown to work well in practice with strong theoretical guarantees.

While the theoretical analysis is technically sound, the paper fails to demonstrate why this approach is preferable to existing methods that are simpler to implement and have been validated on standard problems. The theoretical results don't translate into practical improvements, which is a critical shortcoming for a NIPS submission.

In conclusion, this paper makes a modest theoretical contribution to the analysis of gradient-based learning with MCMC, but it lacks the practical significance and thorough validation expected for NIPS. The authors fail to demonstrate why their approach should be preferred over established methods, and the theoretical bounds appear too loose to guide practical implementation.

I recommend rejection. While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that this approach offers meaningful advantages over existing methods. For NIPS, we expect papers to either make significant theoretical advances with clear practical implications or demonstrate substantial practical improvements over existing methods. This paper falls short on both counts.
