

Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical model learning: the intractability of maximum likelihood estimation due to the partition function being #P-complete. The authors propose a theoretical framework where the parameter space is constrained to a set Θ where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) mixing is guaranteed, establishing convergence guarantees for maximum likelihood learning using gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates. The work extends beyond specific graphical models to the exponential family framework, making it more general than previous approaches like tree-structured models (Chow-Liu) or well-specified model assumptions (pseudolikelihood, score matching).

Strengths:
1. Technical Soundness: The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases. The authors carefully analyze the error terms from MCMC sampling and provide precise computational complexity bounds. The proofs are well-structured with clear proof sketches, and the analysis of mixing times (Section 3.1) properly contextualizes their assumptions within existing literature on MCMC convergence.

2. Novelty: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first convergence guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning within fast-mixing parameter sets. While MCMC-based learning has been used in practice (e.g., contrastive divergence), the paper provides the first theoretical justification for this approach under specific constraints on the parameter space, distinguishing it from previous approximate likelihood methods.

3. Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized with clear notation, well-defined assumptions, and a logical progression from background to results. The example in Section 6 effectively illustrates the theoretical bounds with concrete parameter settings, making the abstract results more tangible.

4. Significance: The work bridges a critical gap between theory and practice in graphical model learning. By providing convergence guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning, it offers a theoretically sound foundation for using this widely employed practical approach. The computational complexity bounds (e.g., KMv = O(1/ϵ^3 log(1/ϵ)) for convex case) provide valuable guidance for practical implementation.

Weaknesses:
1. Practical Impact: The paper acknowledges it makes no significant practical contribution, and the example provided is somewhat simplistic. The theoretical results would benefit from a more substantial empirical demonstration showing how these bounds translate to practical performance improvements over existing methods.

2. Assumption Constraints: The analysis requires the parameter set Θ to be fast-mixing, which imposes specific constraints on the model parameters (e.g., bounded interaction strengths for Ising models). While the paper provides examples of constructing such sets, it doesn't thoroughly explore how restrictive these constraints might be for real-world applications.

3. Projection Complexity: The authors note that the computational complexity of the projection operation onto Θ is ignored in their analysis. While they argue this is typically small compared to sampling time, this could be a significant limitation for larger models or more complex constraint sets.

Pro and Con Acceptance:
Pros: Provides the first theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning within fast-mixing parameter sets; rigorously analyzes computational complexity with precise bounds; well-organized and clearly written; extends results to the exponential family framework for broader applicability.

Cons: Limited practical demonstration of results; assumes constraints on parameter space that may be restrictive; ignores projection complexity which could be significant for large models.

The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to graphical model learning, advancing our understanding of when and how MCMC-based approaches can be guaranteed to converge. The results are technically sound, novel, and significant for the field. While the practical impact is limited by the paper's theoretical focus, the work provides a crucial foundation for future research on MCMC-based learning algorithms.

The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on rigorous scientific contributions to machine learning. The theoretical analysis addresses a fundamental challenge in the field and provides a clear roadmap for future work. The authors carefully situate their work within the literature, acknowledging related approaches (pseudolikelihood, score matching) while clearly distinguishing their novel contribution.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in maximum likelihood learning for graphical models. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant addition to the literature on tractable learning in exponential families. The results are important for both theoretical understanding and potential practical algorithm design.

Recommendation: Accept
