

# Review for "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper addresses the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by proposing a novel approach that restricts parameter space to "fast-mixing parameter sets" where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling converges rapidly. The authors prove that under this restriction, a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) exists for maximum likelihood learning, providing theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based gradient descent methods. The work extends beyond graphical models to apply generally to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.

## Strengths

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing rigorous convergence guarantees for a widely used practical approach (MCMC-based gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning) under the fast-mixing assumption. The main results are well-motivated and clearly distinguished from previous work on tractable graphical models (e.g., treewidth-based methods like Chow-Liu, pseudolikelihood, or score matching). The analysis covers both convex and strongly convex cases with appropriate technical depth, providing explicit schedules for algorithm parameters (K, M, v) that achieve desired accuracy. The theoretical bounds are carefully derived, with lower bounds that are nearly matched by the proposed schedules. The example section provides a concrete illustration of how the theory applies to an Ising model, demonstrating the practical relevance of the approach.

## Weaknesses

The primary weakness is the lack of significant practical contribution. While the theoretical results are sound, the paper acknowledges no meaningful improvement over existing methods in practice, and the example is quite simplistic (4x4 Ising model with 5 training samples). The theoretical bounds are somewhat conservative, as noted in the example where the algorithm converged faster than predicted. The paper does not adequately address how to identify or construct fast-mixing parameter sets for general models beyond specific examples (Ising model), which limits the applicability of the approach. The discussion of initialization strategies (e.g., contrastive divergence) is superficial, and the paper does not connect the theory to practical implementation details that would help practitioners.

## Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs are carefully constructed, and the authors are honest about limitations (e.g., conservative bounds, projection complexity). The work represents a complete theoretical contribution, not a position paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Key notations are defined early, and the main results are presented with sufficient detail. The example provides a concrete illustration of the theoretical results. However, some technical details (e.g., the projection complexity for general models) could be clarified.

**Originality:** The main contribution—restricting to fast-mixing parameter sets rather than treewidth-based tractability—is novel. The connection between fast mixing and maximum likelihood learning represents a fresh perspective that distinguishes this work from previous NIPS papers on tractable graphical models.

**Significance:** The theoretical guarantees are valuable for understanding why MCMC-based approaches work in practice. However, the lack of practical contribution and the conservative nature of the bounds limit the paper's immediate impact. The results could guide practitioners in setting algorithm parameters, but the paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to identify fast-mixing parameter sets for real-world models.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning. The results are novel, technically sound, and well-motivated. While the practical significance is limited, the theoretical contribution is valuable to the field.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand the discussion of practical implementation:** Address how to identify fast-mixing parameter sets for more complex models beyond the Ising example. Provide guidance on how practitioners can construct or identify such sets for their specific applications.

2. **Improve the practical relevance:** Include a more substantial empirical comparison showing how the theoretical guarantees translate to practical performance improvements over existing methods. Even a simple comparison with standard MCMC-based learning approaches would strengthen the significance claim.

3. **Refine the bounds:** Acknowledge the conservatism in the bounds more thoroughly and suggest potential ways to tighten them. The example already shows the algorithm converges faster than predicted—exploring why this happens could lead to tighter theoretical results.

4. **Clarify the projection complexity:** While the paper acknowledges that projection complexity is often negligible, provide a more detailed analysis of when this is true and when it might become a bottleneck (e.g., for large-scale models with complex projection requirements).

5. **Connect to existing practice:** Discuss how the theoretical results relate to common practices like contrastive divergence or tempered transitions, and how the theory could inform better parameter choices for these methods.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a meaningful advance in understanding MCMC-based learning for exponential families. With modest improvements addressing practical relevance and implementation details, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
