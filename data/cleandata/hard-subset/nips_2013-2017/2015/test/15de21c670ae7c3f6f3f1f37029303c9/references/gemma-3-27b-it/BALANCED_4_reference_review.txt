This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, specifically addressing the intractability of standard maximum likelihood estimation. The core idea is to restrict parameters to a “tractable” set, defined not by structural properties like treewidth, but by guaranteeing fast mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling within that set. The authors demonstrate that if a projection onto this set exists, a Fast Polynomial Random Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within the set is also guaranteed. The analysis extends to exponential families generally, and provides convergence guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, differentiating between strongly convex and merely convex cases.

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a fundamental challenge in statistical modeling. The connection between fast mixing and tractable learning is a novel and interesting contribution. The theoretical results—establishing an FPRAS under these conditions—are significant, and the analysis using projected gradient descent is sound. The inclusion of detailed proofs (deferred to the appendix) and proof sketches is appreciated. The authors also appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their work and suggest avenues for future research, such as considering alternative initialization strategies and the projection cost. The example provided, though simplified, reinforces the feasibility of the approach. Related work is comprehensively cited, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.

However, several weaknesses detract from the paper’s overall impact.

**Clarity:** While mathematically rigorous, the paper suffers from some readability issues. The notation is dense, and while a list of symbols is provided, many are introduced without sufficient contextual explanation. The flow between sections could be improved – the connection between the initial motivation and the core theoretical result isn’t always immediately clear.  The informal proof sketches, while helpful, are still quite technical and may not be accessible to a broad NIPS audience. The discussion section is a bit disjointed, listing weaknesses without fully integrating them into a cohesive narrative.

**Originality:** The idea of leveraging fast mixing for tractable learning, while novel in its formalization and guarantees, builds heavily upon existing techniques. The gradient descent with MCMC approximation is a standard practice, and the connection to FPRAS, while important, doesn’t represent a groundbreaking algorithmic advance. The main contribution lies in the *theoretical justification* of this commonly used approach under the fast-mixing constraint.  More emphasis should be placed on positioning this work within the broader context of approximate inference and learning in graphical models.

**Significance:** The practical impact of this work is somewhat limited, as the authors themselves concede. The computational cost of projection, and the need to verify the fast-mixing property for a given set Θ, may outweigh the benefits in many real-world scenarios. The theory is presented for general exponential families, which might dilute the focus on the motivating graphical model application. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, their tightness and applicability to complex models warrant further investigation.  The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the conditions under which this approach would be demonstrably superior to existing methods.

**Quality:** The mathematical analysis appears sound, and the proofs (while not reviewed in detail) seem logically structured. However, the reliance on existing results (e.g., Schmidt et al. [26]) without a more in-depth explanation of their relevance to this specific context makes it harder to assess the novelty of the technical contributions. The theoretical bounds, while tight, are expressed in terms of numerous parameters, which can make them difficult to interpret and apply in practice.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical justification for a common practical approach (gradient descent with MCMC).
*   Strong mathematical guarantees (FPRAS) under reasonable assumptions (fast mixing, projection existence).
*   Comprehensive related work and clear presentation of the theoretical framework.
*   Well-motivated problem and clear exposition of the core idea.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Limited practical impact due to potential computational cost of projection and fast-mixing verification.
*   Readability issues and dense notation may hinder accessibility.
*   The core contribution is primarily theoretical, with limited algorithmic novelty.
*   The paper lacks a compelling demonstration of superior performance compared to existing methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution, its limited practical impact, readability issues, and incremental algorithmic novelty make it a less compelling fit for NIPS. However, the work has merit and could be significantly strengthened with revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Improve clarity by providing more intuitive explanations and simplifying the notation.
2.  Explicitly compare the computational cost and performance of this approach to existing methods in more detail.
3.  Explore potential applications where the fast-mixing constraint can be readily satisfied and the projection cost is manageable.
4.  Address the limitations discussed in the "Discussion" section with more concrete proposals for future work.
5.  Consider including a more detailed analysis of the conditions required for fast mixing in common graphical models.
