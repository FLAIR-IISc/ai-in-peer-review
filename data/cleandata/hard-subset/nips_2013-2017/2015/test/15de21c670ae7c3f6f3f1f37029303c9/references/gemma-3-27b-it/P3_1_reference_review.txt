## NIPS Review: Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, particularly when the treewidth is high, rendering exact inference intractable. Instead of directly tackling inference, the authors propose a fundamentally different approach: restricting learning to a *tractable set* of parameters that guarantee fast mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. They demonstrate that, under this condition (fast mixing and projectability onto the parameter set), a Fast Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) exists for the maximum likelihood solution within that set. The work provides theoretical guarantees for a gradient descent approach coupled with MCMC for approximate maximum likelihood estimation, analyzing both convex and strongly convex cases and deriving bounds on the computational effort needed to achieve a desired level of accuracy.  The results are presented generally for exponential families, moving beyond the specific context of graphical models, and complemented with an illustrative Ising model example.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of approximate maximum likelihood learning. It acknowledges the limitations of methods like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods (inferior sample complexity, failure under model misspecification). It contrasts its approach with those that attempt to efficiently *learn* tractable structures (like Chow-Liu trees or high-girth models) and instead focuses on finding a tractable *parameter space* allowing efficient sampling. The connection to existing work on MCMC-based learning is also well-established, highlighting the lack of theoretical guarantees for such approaches. The references to mixing time results for Ising models and other graphical models are appropriate and demonstrate an understanding of the relevant literature. The paper builds upon the established theoretical framework for FPRAS and gradient descent with noisy gradients.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of defining tractability through MCMC mixing time, rather than explicit model structure, is a significant contribution.  This opens up a potentially new avenue for tackling complex models where direct inference is impossible.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the proposed approach, establishing an FPRAS under well-defined conditions. The analysis is careful, distinguishing between convex and strongly convex cases and providing clear bounds on computational complexity.
* **Generality:**  Framing the results in terms of exponential families extends the applicability beyond graphical models, making the work more broadly relevant.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the core ideas are presented in a logical manner.  The included example provides some concrete grounding.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Relevance:** While theoretically sound, the practical impact of this work is uncertain. The requirement of knowing (or bounding) the mixing rate *a priori* for a given parameter set Θ is a significant hurdle. Determining such bounds in complex, real-world models could be as difficult as performing inference directly. The example, while illustrating the theory, doesn’t fully address this concern – L was empirically reduced, but the process of finding a *practical* fast-mixing parameter set remains unclear.
* **Projection Complexity:** The paper acknowledges the potential complexity of projection onto the parameter set Θ but doesn't delve deeply into this issue.  If the projection step is computationally expensive, it could negate the benefits of fast mixing. The paper mentions existing algorithms for specific models (Ising model), but a more general discussion of projection algorithms and their complexity would be valuable.
* **Assumptions on Mixing Rate:** The assumption of a geometrically decaying mixing rate (Eq. 2) is somewhat strong.  Real-world MCMC chains may exhibit more complex mixing behavior.  Exploring the robustness of the results to slower mixing rates or alternative mixing time bounds could strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The single example is insufficient to demonstrate the practical viability of the approach. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation on a variety of datasets and models would be highly desirable.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** The authors acknowledge conservatism in the theoretical bounds. While understandable, this reduces the immediately apparent practical impact.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Novel and theoretically sound approach to a fundamental problem.**
* **Strong theoretical guarantees and well-defined computational bounds.**
* **Generality of the results to exponential families.**
* **Well-written and clearly presented paper.**

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Potential practical limitations due to the need to know mixing rates and the complexity of projection.**
* **Limited empirical validation.**
* **Strong assumptions about mixing rate decay.**




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that introduces a novel and potentially impactful approach to maximum likelihood learning. While the practical challenges are significant, the theoretical contribution is substantial. The paper is well-written and rigorously analyzed.  However, the lack of extensive empirical validation and the potential difficulties in applying the method to real-world problems raise some concerns.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following points:

1. **Discussion of Projection Complexity:** Expand the discussion of projection algorithms and their computational complexity, especially for more general parameter sets.
2. **Robustness to Mixing Rates:** Briefly discuss the sensitivity of the results to deviations from the assumed geometric mixing rate.  Could the approach be adapted to handle slower mixing rates?
3. **Additional Empirical Evaluation:** While a full-scale empirical study may be beyond the scope of this paper, consider including a second, more challenging example to demonstrate the approach's feasibility in a more complex setting.
4. **Clarify Practical Implications**: Elaborate on how one might *discover* a fast-mixing parameter set Θ for a given problem.  Acknowledging this challenge and offering potential strategies (e.g., exploiting domain knowledge or using adaptive MCMC techniques) would be beneficial.
