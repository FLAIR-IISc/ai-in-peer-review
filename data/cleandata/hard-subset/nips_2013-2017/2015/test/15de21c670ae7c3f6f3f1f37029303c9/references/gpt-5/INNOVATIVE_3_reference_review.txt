Summary
This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting structure (e.g., trees), the authors constrain parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically with uniform constants. They analyze projected gradient descent that estimates gradients via MCMC, and show that, provided one can project onto Θ and the sufficient statistics are bounded, there is a randomized polynomial-time approximation scheme for the MLE within Θ. They give convergence bounds in two regimes: convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized), derive Lipschitz constants, concentration-based error control for biased, noisy gradients, and prescribe explicit schedules for number of iterations K, samples M, and chain steps v to reach prescribed error with high probability. They also discuss concrete fast-mixing sets and projections for Ising/MRFs based on matrix norm bounds. A small Ising example illustrates the procedure.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against surrogates like pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, and learning for trees/high-girth models; against MCMC-MLE/stochastic approximation (Geyer; Gu & Zhu); and against “learning for mixing” approaches (e.g., Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke; Steinhardt & Liang). The novelty is to formalize MLE constrained to fast-mixing parameter sets and to give explicit, high-probability, polynomial complexity guarantees for the MCMC-based projected gradient algorithm, with general exponential-family statements rather than model-specific analyses.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: reframing tractability via fast mixing rather than low treewidth, and tying this to provable MLE optimization guarantees. This is a bold and timely shift that connects mixing time theory to learning objectives.
- Generality: results apply to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not just specific MRFs.
- Clear, quantitative convergence theorems for both convex and strongly convex cases, including explicit schedules and near-matching lower bounds (up to constants/log factors). The dependence on mixing parameters is made explicit.
- Concrete bridges to known fast-mixing sufficient conditions and projection procedures (e.g., spectral-norm constraints), making the framework actionable for Ising/Potts-like models.
- Honest discussion of limitations (projection cost, initialization, persistent chains).

Weaknesses
- Practical validation is minimal (a 4×4 Ising toy). While acceptable for a theory paper, even a moderate-sized synthetic study would help calibrate constants and the tightness of schedules.
- The “FPRAS” terminology for an optimization procedure with additive accuracy in objective or parameter norm is somewhat nonstandard; typically, FPRAS refers to multiplicative approximation for counting problems. Clarifying the approximation notion (and whether it’s an RPAS/RPTAS for optimization) would avoid confusion.
- The uniform mixing assumption over Θ (constants C, α known and valid for all θ ∈ Θ) is central. In practice, computable, non-vacuous bounds can be loose, and α may degrade with N; while still polynomial, this could be large. It would help to spell out dependence on N, degree, and norm bounds for a canonical case to make the FPRAS claim more concrete.
- Independence across iterations is assumed (fresh chains from arbitrary r at each step). This excludes popular persistent-sampling heuristics; extending the analysis to dependent samples or to warm-starts would broaden impact.
- Bounded sufficient statistics assumption excludes many continuous exponential families.

Quality
The technical development appears sound: the Lipschitz bound, bias/noise decomposition, use of concentration (Efron–Stein/Bernstein or vector Hoeffding), and coupling with inexact projected gradient results from Schmidt et al. are appropriate and carefully assembled. The schedules and lower bounds are informative and nearly tight. The paper is upfront about assumptions and limitations. I did not find major mathematical gaps based on the provided sketches; full proofs in the appendix would be important to verify.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The role of each assumption and constant is explained, and the link between mixing bounds and projection is made concrete with examples. Minor suggestions:
- Define precisely the approximation guarantee (additive in f vs. parameter norm) and adjust “FPRAS” terminology accordingly.
- Summarize the dependence of (1−α) (or log(1/α)) on N and degree for the Ising/Gibbs case in the main text.
- Briefly discuss the computational complexity of the projection step for the spectral-norm constraint (e.g., per-iteration SVD cost).

Originality
High. While pieces exist (fast-mixing parameter projections; MCMC-MLE; inexact gradient methods), the synthesis into an optimization FPRAS inside fast-mixing sets, with explicit complexity and schedules across convex/strongly convex regimes and general exponential families, is a fresh contribution and opens a new direction.

Significance
Potentially high. This work reframes what “tractable learning” can mean, and offers a pathway to principled MLE when inference is hard but mixing can be certified by parameter constraints. It provides theory to support practice in areas where contrastive divergence–style training and mixing-aware regularization are common. I expect follow-up on tighter mixing/projection sets, better constants, and extensions to persistent chains and hidden-variable models.

Questions for authors
- Can you formalize the approximation notion to avoid ambiguity with FPRAS terminology?
- Are C and α assumed known to the algorithm, or only used in analysis? If known, how are they computed for a given Θ?
- Could your concentration argument be adapted to warm starts/persistent chains (e.g., via martingale bounds)?
- Any insight on extending beyond bounded t(x) (e.g., sub-Gaussian tails)?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and bold conceptual reframing with solid theory.
- General results likely to spur new research on mixing-aware learning.
- Clear presentation and near-tight complexity analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Very limited empirical validation.
- Dependence on sometimes-loose mixing bounds and projection availability.
- Terminology around “FPRAS” could mislead.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper advances a compelling new angle on maximum likelihood learning with rigorous guarantees, and its novelty and potential impact outweigh the limited experiments and some practical caveats.

Confidence
High (theory/ML expertise; not all appendix proofs reviewed).
