This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in exponential families, focusing on scenarios where direct optimization is intractable due to computational complexity. The core idea is to restrict learning to a tractable set of parameters, Θ, guaranteeing fast mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. The authors establish a Finite-Time Random Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within such a set, analyzing gradient descent coupled with MCMC for gradient approximation. They provide convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, and discuss practical implications.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing challenges in maximum likelihood learning for graphical models and acknowledging limitations of alternative methods like pseudolikelihood and score matching. The connection to treewidth and fast-mixing parameter sets is clearly articulated. The theoretical results, particularly the FPRAS guarantee and convergence rates, are the main contributions. The analysis is rigorous and considers both regularized and unregularized cases.  The inclusion of a concrete example, while simplified, aids in understanding the practical relevance of the work. The related work section is extensive and demonstrates awareness of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of leveraging fast mixing properties of a restricted parameter set to establish an FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning is a novel and potentially valuable contribution. This differs significantly from standard approaches that rely on approximate likelihoods or modified optimization procedures.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis of the gradient descent algorithm with MCMC-based gradient estimation, establishing convergence guarantees under specific conditions. The separation of analysis for convex and strongly convex cases is appropriate.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clearly defined (with a helpful list at the beginning), and the concepts are explained reasonably well. The step-by-step breakdown of the proofs (although detailed proofs are deferred to the appendix) aids understanding.
* **Significance:** The results contribute to a better understanding of the fundamental limits of maximum likelihood learning in complex models. The FPRAS guarantee, if achievable in practice, could offer a significant advantage over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality:** While theoretically sound, the paper doesn’t deeply address the practical challenges of finding and projecting onto suitable fast-mixing parameter sets Θ. The example provides some intuition, but scaling this to high-dimensional, real-world problems is not fully explored. The projection step's complexity isn't extensively discussed, and could be a bottleneck.
* **Assumptions:** The reliance on known mixing rates (Equation 2) and the ability to project onto Θ may be restrictive in some applications. Obtaining such guarantees in practice can be difficult. While the authors acknowledge this, further discussion on how to relax these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Results:** The numerous theorems and corollaries, while rigorously derived, can be difficult to digest without careful study. A more streamlined presentation of the key results and their implications might enhance accessibility. The interplay between parameters (K, M, v) and their impact on computational cost could be better highlighted.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The example is illustrative but limited. More extensive experimental evaluation on various datasets and model structures is needed to demonstrate the practical effectiveness and scalability of the proposed approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning with strong convergence guarantees.
* The results advance our understanding of the fundamental limits of learning in complex models.
* The paper is technically sound and well-written.
* The potential impact on fields relying on maximum likelihood estimation in graphical models is significant.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The practical applicability of the proposed approach is not fully demonstrated.
* The reliance on strong assumptions (known mixing rates, efficient projection) may limit its widespread adoption.
* The complexity of the theoretical results could hinder accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with a novel contribution to the field of maximum likelihood learning. While practical considerations and the strength of the assumptions represent potential limitations, the rigor and significance of the theoretical results warrant acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically focusing on a more detailed discussion of practical challenges and potential strategies for addressing them, and perhaps streamlining the presentation of the theoretical results. It would also benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the computational cost of the projection step.
