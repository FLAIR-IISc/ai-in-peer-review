### **Summary**

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in general undirected graphical models. The central idea is to reframe the notion of tractability. Instead of restricting the model's graph structure (e.g., to low treewidth), the authors propose restricting the model's parameters to a convex set `Θ` for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is provably "fast-mixing." The main contribution is a theoretical analysis showing that for any such set `Θ`, there exists a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for the MLE problem. The paper analyzes a projected gradient descent algorithm where the intractable gradient is approximated using MCMC samples. It provides explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples (M), and chain lengths (v) required to achieve a desired accuracy `ϵ` with high probability `δ`. The analysis is presented for both the general convex case (unregularized MLE) and the strongly convex case (ridge-regularized MLE), yielding convergence guarantees on the objective function value and parameter error, respectively.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its novel and elegant conceptual shift. The idea of defining a tractable class of models based on fast-mixing parameter sets, rather than graph topology, is a significant and insightful contribution. This provides a new theoretical lens through which to view the trade-offs in learning complex probabilistic models. It connects the fields of MCMC theory and convex optimization in a direct and meaningful way to solve a fundamental machine learning problem.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper presents a rigorous and sound theoretical analysis. It carefully bounds the error introduced by using a finite-length MCMC chain to approximate the gradient, treating it as a biased and noisy estimator. The analysis correctly leverages established results on gradient descent with inexact gradients [26] and concentration inequalities (Efron-Stein, Hoeffding) to derive convergence rates. The derived schedules for K, M, and v, while complex, provide a concrete and provable guarantee, which is often missing for heuristic MCMC-based learning algorithms. The distinction and separate analysis for the convex and strongly convex cases are well-handled and lead to qualitatively different and expected results (`O(1/ϵ³)` vs `O(1/ϵ²)`, ignoring log factors).

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated in the introduction, contrasting the proposed approach with existing methods like pseudolikelihood and Chow-Liu. The setup section, with its list of notation, is very helpful. The theoretical development is logical, and the inclusion of proof sketches in the main body provides valuable intuition for the reader before deferring the full details to the appendix. The overall structure makes a complex theoretical argument accessible.

4.  **Generality:** While motivated by undirected graphical models like the Ising model, the entire theoretical framework is developed for general exponential families. This makes the results broadly applicable to any model in this class where a fast-mixing parameter set `Θ` can be identified and projected onto.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Practical Implications and Assumptions:** The analysis relies on two strong assumptions that may limit its direct practical application:
    a. **Knowledge of the Fast-Mixing Set `Θ`:** The algorithm requires prior knowledge of a convex set `Θ` and its associated mixing parameters (`C`, `α`). While the paper provides examples for the Ising model based on bounds on interaction strengths, identifying such sets and their precise mixing properties for more complex, arbitrary models is a highly non-trivial problem in itself and an active area of research.
    b. **Efficient Projection:** The algorithm requires an efficient method for projecting an arbitrary parameter vector onto the set `Θ`. While this is trivial for simple constraints (e.g., element-wise bounds), for more complex sets (e.g., based on spectral norms as in [8, 21]), this projection can be a computationally expensive sub-problem. The paper's complexity analysis focuses solely on the number of MCMC transitions (`KMv`) and does not account for the cost of the `K` projections, which could dominate in some scenarios.

2.  **Conservatism of Bounds:** The derived bounds on K, M, and v are likely to be very conservative in practice. This is a common feature of high-probability theoretical bounds in machine learning. The paper's own example acknowledges this by using a manually tuned (smaller) Lipschitz constant for faster convergence. While the asymptotic scaling is the key theoretical result, the constants involved may render the prescribed schedule impractical for all but the smallest problems, thus limiting the work's direct impact on practitioners.

3.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The single experiment on a 4x4 Ising model serves as a proof-of-concept but is insufficient to demonstrate the practical utility or characteristics of the proposed method. There is no comparison to standard, albeit heuristic, methods like Persistent Contrastive Divergence (PCD) or even simpler approximations like pseudolikelihood. Such a comparison would help contextualize the trade-off between the theoretical guarantees of this method and the empirical performance of others.

### **Questions and Suggestions for the Authors**

*   Could you elaborate on the complexity of the projection step for the spectral norm constraint mentioned in Section 3.1? How does its cost compare to the sampling cost (`Mv`) per iteration, and how would this affect the overall complexity analysis?
*   The analysis assumes initializing the MCMC chain from an arbitrary distribution `r`. A common heuristic (e.g., in contrastive divergence) is to use the data distribution or samples from the previous iteration. Could your theoretical framework be extended to analyze such "warm-start" strategies, potentially leading to tighter bounds or a reduction in the required chain length `v`?
*   While the paper claims "no significant practical contribution," a comparison with pseudolikelihood on the example problem would be illuminating. It would show how the M-projection found by this guaranteed method differs from the solution of the surrogate objective, especially in a (potentially) mis-specified setting.

### **Final Assessment**

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a novel and significant conceptual contribution. The idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter regimes is insightful and opens up a new avenue for research in learning complex probabilistic models. The analysis is rigorous and the paper is written with exceptional clarity.

The primary weaknesses lie in its immediate practical applicability, stemming from strong assumptions and conservative theoretical bounds. However, for a conference like NIPS, which values fundamental theoretical advances, these weaknesses are acceptable. The paper's contribution is not a new state-of-the-art practical algorithm, but a new, provably correct framework for thinking about and performing maximum likelihood learning. This work is very likely to be built upon by future researchers in both MCMC theory and probabilistic machine learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly original and significant core idea.
*   Rigorous, sound, and comprehensive theoretical analysis.
*   Clearly written and well-structured.
*   Results are general to the exponential family.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Strong assumptions about knowing the fast-mixing set `Θ` and having an efficient projection.
*   Limited and preliminary experimental validation.
*   The derived bounds are likely too loose for direct practical use.

**Recommendation: Accept.**

The paper's theoretical novelty and significance outweigh its practical limitations. It introduces a valuable new perspective and provides the first formal convergence guarantees for this type of MCMC-based MLE procedure. It is a strong contribution to the foundations of learning in graphical models.
