

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing parameter sets," where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling converges quickly. The main claim is that if the parameter set Θ satisfies certain mixing conditions (equation 3), then gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates will converge to the optimal solution with high probability. The paper provides convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, showing that the total computational effort scales as O(1/ϵ³) for convex optimization and O(1/ϵ²) for strongly convex optimization.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a solid theoretical foundation. The authors establish a connection between fast-mixing Markov chains and gradient descent convergence, which is theoretically interesting. The proofs appear technically sound, and the paper correctly cites relevant literature on mixing times (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Domke & Liu, 2013; Steinhardt & Liang, 2014). The distinction between convex and strongly convex cases is well-motivated, and the analysis of error bounds for MCMC sampling is rigorous.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weakness is its lack of practical significance. The authors explicitly acknowledge in Section 6 that "this paper claims no significant practical contribution," which is a serious concern for a NIPS submission. The theoretical result is a natural extension of existing work on fast-mixing parameter sets (Domke & Liu, 2013; Liu & Domke, 2014), but it fails to demonstrate how this approach improves upon existing methods in practice.

The paper makes several strong assumptions that are difficult to verify in practice:
1. The existence of a tractable projection onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ (which the authors admit is trivial for Ising models with bounded interactions but may not scale well for larger models)
2. The availability of constants C and α that characterize the mixing rate (which would need to be estimated for each specific model)
3. The boundedness of sufficient statistics (R₂), which is not always guaranteed

The theoretical guarantees do not translate to clear practical advantages. The example in Section 6 is minimal (a 4x4 Ising model with only 5 training points) and doesn't compare the proposed method against established baselines like pseudolikelihood, score matching, or variational methods. The paper doesn't address how the computational complexity (O(1/ϵ³) for convex case) compares to existing methods that can achieve similar accuracy with lower computational cost.

The paper also fails to address key practical considerations:
- How to initialize MCMC chains (the paper assumes arbitrary starting distribution, but in practice, initialization significantly impacts mixing time)
- How to handle cases where the target distribution isn't in the assumed family (the paper notes this as a limitation of existing methods but doesn't show how their approach improves upon them)
- The computational cost of the projection operation, which the authors acknowledge is "often very small" but doesn't quantify this claim

**Originality and Significance:**
While the paper makes a technically correct connection between fast-mixing properties and gradient descent convergence, this is not a substantial advance over existing work. The paper builds on Steinhardt & Liang (2014) who proposed learning using fast-mixing chains, but extends their work by providing convergence guarantees. However, these guarantees don't translate to practical improvements, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how this approach outperforms established methods like pseudolikelihood or variational inference.

The paper's claim of a "fundamentally different notion of tractability" is misleading. The authors themselves note that their approach works "entirely in the exponential family framework" and applies more generally than graphical models. This is not a new concept but rather an application of existing mixing time theory to an already-studied problem.

**Conclusion:**
This paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution but fails to demonstrate significant practical value or novel insights that would advance the field. The theoretical guarantees are interesting but lack empirical validation and practical context. The authors acknowledge the lack of practical contribution, which is a major red flag for a NIPS submission that should ideally provide both theoretical and practical advances.

I recommend rejection. The paper's main contribution is a straightforward extension of existing theory that doesn't substantially improve upon established methods. For NIPS, we need papers that either provide significant theoretical advances with clear practical implications or novel practical approaches with strong theoretical grounding. This paper falls short on both counts. While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, it doesn't address the core challenges of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models in a way that would be useful to practitioners or advance the state of the art.
