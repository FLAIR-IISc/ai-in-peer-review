### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in general undirected graphical models (and more broadly, exponential families). Instead of common approaches that either restrict the model's graph structure to be tractable (e.g., low treewidth) or use computationally efficient but potentially inconsistent proxy objectives (e.g., pseudolikelihood), this work proposes a different paradigm. The authors suggest constraining the model parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` for which any corresponding model is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing," meaning a standard Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampler converges to the stationary distribution at a geometric rate.

The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a projected gradient descent algorithm where the gradient is estimated using MCMC. The authors prove that this common heuristic, under the fast-mixing assumption, constitutes a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained MLE problem. The paper provides two key results:
1.  For the general convex case (e.g., unregularized MLE), it provides a schedule for the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples per step (M), and MCMC chain length (v) to achieve a desired accuracy `ϵ_f` in the objective function. The total computational effort (KMv) scales as `O(1/ϵ_f^3)`.
2.  For the strongly convex case (i.e., with L2 regularization), it provides a similar schedule to achieve an accuracy `ϵ_θ` in the parameter space, with a total effort scaling as `O(1/ϵ_θ^2)`.

These results provide the first formal convergence guarantees for MCMC-based MLE to the true (constrained) optimum, carefully accounting for both the bias (from finite chain length) and variance (from finite samples) in the gradient estimates.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** This work provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for the widely-used family of MCMC-based learning algorithms (such as those related to contrastive divergence). While practitioners have used these methods successfully for years, a rigorous understanding of their convergence to the maximum likelihood estimate has been lacking. By shifting the notion of tractability from graph structure to parameter-induced fast mixing, the paper offers a novel and powerful perspective. The establishment of an FPRAS for this problem is a significant theoretical achievement.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The analysis is rigorous, complete, and technically sound. The authors skillfully combine existing results on optimization with inexact gradients [26] with concentration inequalities (Bernstein, Hoeffding) and MCMC mixing time theory. The separate, detailed treatment of the convex and strongly convex cases is thorough and insightful. The proofs appear correct and carefully handle the interplay between the three key algorithmic meta-parameters (K, M, v).

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem setup, assumptions, and algorithm are clearly articulated. The main results are stated upfront, with intuitive proof sketches provided in the main text that convey the core arguments effectively, while deferring full details to the appendix. This makes the paper accessible without sacrificing rigor.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Practicality of the Bounds:** As is common with theoretical analyses of this type, the derived bounds on the required computational effort (K, M, v) appear quite conservative. The authors' own experiment in Section 6 demonstrates this, where the algorithm converges successfully with a much smaller Lipschitz constant and achieves a tighter solution than the theory guarantees. While providing the first-ever bounds is a major contribution, the paper would benefit from a discussion on the sources of this looseness (e.g., worst-case assumptions on mixing, loose concentration bounds, etc.) and the prospects for tightening them in future work.

2.  **Limited Scope of the Analyzed Algorithm:** The analysis assumes that at each gradient step, a new MCMC chain is run from an arbitrary starting distribution. This does not cover more sophisticated and practically common sampling schemes like persistent contrastive divergence (PCD), where samples from the previous iteration are used to initialize the current MCMC chains. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but the gap between the analyzed algorithm and state-of-the-art practice is a notable weakness.

3.  **The Challenge of Defining `Θ`:** The entire framework hinges on the ability to define a suitable convex set `Θ` of fast-mixing parameters that is also easy to project onto. While the paper provides good examples from existing literature (e.g., Dobrushin-type conditions for Ising models), for many complex models of interest, identifying such a set that is not overly restrictive remains a major open challenge. The practical applicability of the method is therefore constrained by progress in MCMC theory.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   It would be valuable to add a paragraph in the discussion explicitly conjecturing how the analysis might change for a persistent sampling scheme (PCD). What are the key technical hurdles? For example, the current proof relies on the independence of samples drawn at each iteration `k`, which PCD violates. Discussing this would provide a clear roadmap for future research aiming to bridge this theoretical gap.
*   To address the conservative bounds, a brief discussion on which components of the proof contribute most to the slack could be helpful. For instance, is it the `4R_2^2` term in the Lipschitz constant, or the worst-case `Cα^v` mixing assumption? This could guide efforts to develop more practical, data-dependent bounds.
*   The connection to contrastive divergence (CD) is mentioned briefly. It might be worth explicitly stating that the analysis with `v=1` (or a small constant) corresponds to a variant of CD-1, and thus the paper provides the first (albeit for a constrained parameter space) end-to-end guarantee for a CD-like algorithm converging to the MLE.

**Arguments for Acceptance**

*   **Pro:** The paper provides a landmark theoretical result for a fundamental problem in machine learning. It is the first to establish a rigorous convergence guarantee and computational complexity for MCMC-based MLE. The approach is novel, the analysis is sound, and the paper is written with outstanding clarity. Its significance lies in providing a solid theoretical footing for a class of algorithms that have hitherto been largely heuristic.
*   **Con:** The immediate practical impact may be limited due to conservative bounds and the assumption of a simplified sampling scheme. The reliance on pre-existing knowledge of fast-mixing parameter sets also constrains its applicability.

**Conclusion**

Despite some limitations regarding immediate practical application, this is a beautiful and important theoretical paper that makes a fundamental contribution to our understanding of learning in intractable probabilistic models. The strengths in originality, significance, and technical quality are overwhelming. It lays a solid foundation upon which future theoretical and practical work can be built. I strongly recommend acceptance.
