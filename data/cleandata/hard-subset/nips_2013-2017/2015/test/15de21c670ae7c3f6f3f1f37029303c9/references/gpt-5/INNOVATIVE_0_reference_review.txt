Summary
This paper reframes tractability for maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ for which a chosen Markov chain Mθ mixes geometrically quickly (uniformly over Θ). Given a projection oracle onto Θ and bounded sufficient statistics, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are estimated via MCMC. They prove high-probability convergence guarantees for both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, including explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain steps v that yield ε-accurate solutions with total work KMv scaling as O(ε−3 log(1/ε)) in the convex case and O(ε−2 log(1/ε)) in the strongly convex case (up to problem-dependent constants). They also establish matching lower bounds (up to logarithms and constant factors), Lipschitzness of the gradient, and show that the framework yields an FPRAS for MLE restricted to Θ. The results are stated for general exponential families and specialized to Ising/pairwise MRFs with concrete Θ and projection procedures (e.g., clipping or spectral-norm constraints) supported by known mixing-time results. A small Ising example illustrates the setup.

Relation to prior work
- Learning with approximate likelihoods: pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods, and local/ratio matching estimators are efficient but do not target the M-projection under misspecification and often yield models where inference remains intractable. This work instead targets true MLE but within a tractable Θ defined by mixing properties.
- MCMC-ML (Geyer; Gu & Zhu) is widely used but suffers from biased gradient estimates and lacks global convergence guarantees; this paper provides high-probability rates under fast-mixing assumptions.
- Fast-mixing parameterization: Domke & Liu and Liu & Domke propose projecting parameters to ensure rapid mixing for inference; Steinhardt & Liang analyze learning fast-mixing models via restart chains. The present work combines fast-mixing projection with MLE optimization and provides full end-to-end complexity guarantees and (near-)optimal schedules, which appears novel.
- Optimization with inexact gradients: The analysis leverages Schmidt et al.’s results to handle biased/noisy gradients, but extends them with mixing-induced bias bounds and matching lower bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Uses “fast-mixing parameter sets” as the tractability notion for learning, decoupled from treewidth. This is a clean, general idea with potential to influence how we design parametric constraints for learnability.
- Generality: Works for arbitrary exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics and any Markov chain with known geometric mixing bounds over Θ.
- Provable guarantees: Provides explicit high-probability convergence rates, schedules for K, M, v, and lower bounds that show near-optimality of the proposed schedules.
- FPRAS perspective: Under the stated assumptions, the algorithm is a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme for constrained MLE—an appealing and strong theoretical claim.
- Misspecification: Optimizes the true likelihood within Θ and thus converges to the M-projection inside Θ, unlike many surrogate objectives.
- Clear connections to mixing-time literature and practical parameterizations (e.g., Ising with degree/tanh bounds, spectral norm constraints) and projection oracles.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strength of assumptions: The existence of a convex Θ with uniform geometric mixing constants (C, α) and an efficient projection oracle is critical. For many realistic, high-degree, or strongly coupled models, such Θ may be overly restrictive or difficult to characterize, and α may be close to 1, making constants large. The paper acknowledges this but the practicality hinges on nontrivial modeling work.
- Chain specificity and generality: Results are stated for “a known sampling algorithm,” but mixing bounds and projections are tightly tied to specific chains (e.g., single-site Gibbs) and models (Ising/Potts). For broader exponential families, identifying both Θ and Mθ with rigorous bounds may be challenging.
- FPRAS claim scope: The complexity depends on 1/(1−α) and C; if these deteriorate with problem size, polynomial-time claims may not be meaningful. Clarifying the computational model (cost of projection, cost per MCMC step) and conditions under which the scheme is polynomial in N and 1/ε would strengthen the FPRAS statement.
- Ignoring projection cost in total work: For spectral-norm constraints, projecting can be SVD-based and expensive; in some regimes this could dominate KMv. The discussion notes this but the main theorems measure only sampling work.
- Empirical validation: Only a toy 4×4 Ising example is shown, with conservative constants. While acceptable for a theory paper, even a few more experiments demonstrating projection and mixing-aware learning at moderate scales would increase confidence.
- Clarity/notation: There are notational inconsistencies (e.g., “R22,” “R2” as both index and squared norm bound). A careful pass to standardize notation and restate assumptions would help readability.

Questions and suggestions
- Please clarify the definition and role of R2 (bounded sufficient-statistics norm): is it a single constant R with R = supx ||t(x)||2, or “R2” denotes a specific bound? This affects Theorem 1 constants.
- State explicitly the computational model for “FPRAS”: what inputs are required (bounds on C, α, L, R, projection oracle), and how do costs scale with N?
- Discuss how to obtain usable upper bounds on L, C, α in practice, and whether adaptive schedules can remove the need for knowing them a priori.
- Can the analysis be extended to sample-pool or persistent chains (common in practice)? Even a high-level remark on technical obstacles/possible remedies would be valuable.
- For non-binary discrete MRFs, can you provide a concrete projection algorithm analogous to the spectral-norm case?

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, clean idea connecting MLE tractability to fast mixing with rigorous, near-optimal rates.
- General exponential-family formulation; unifies several strands (MCMC-ML, fast-mixing constraints, inexact gradient methods).
- Potential to spur new work on designing Θ and projection oracles, and on mixing-aware learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical impact uncertain due to strong, model-specific assumptions on Θ and mixing constants.
- Limited experiments; projection cost not incorporated in main complexity.
- Some clarity issues in notation and the exact FPRAS scope.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite practical limitations and strong assumptions, the paper introduces a significant conceptual shift and provides solid theoretical contributions (upper/lower bounds, schedules, and the FPRAS framing) that are likely to influence future work on learning under mixing constraints. The novelty and direction-setting value outweigh the empirical and scope limitations.
