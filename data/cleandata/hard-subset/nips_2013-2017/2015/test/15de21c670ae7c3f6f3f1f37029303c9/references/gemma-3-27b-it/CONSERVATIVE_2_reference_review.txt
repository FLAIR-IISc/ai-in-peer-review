This paper proposes a framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a set Θ where MCMC mixing is guaranteed. The core idea is that if one can efficiently project onto such a set, a fast-mixing property leads to a FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation, analyzing both convex and strongly convex cases.

The motivation is clear: standard ML learning is intractable, existing approximations have drawbacks, and restricting the parameter space can offer tractability. However, the presented work feels highly theoretical and lacks convincing demonstration of practical impact. While the mathematical development appears rigorous, the connection to real-world problems feels tenuous. The central result – the existence of a FPRAS given fast mixing – is interesting, but largely a statement about the feasibility of an approach, rather than a practical algorithm.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence of gradient descent with MCMC approximations, addressing both convex and strongly convex cases. The Lipschitz constant analysis and derived convergence rates are well-defined.
* **General Framework:** The framework extends beyond graphical models, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, increasing its potential scope.
* **Clear Presentation of Theoretical Foundations:** The background on mixing times and related results (e.g., Jerrum & Sinclair’s work on the Ising model) is clearly explained.
* **Lower Bounds:** The inclusion of lower bounds on the computational cost (KMv) is a valuable addition, providing a more complete picture of the algorithm’s complexity.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Practicality:** The primary concern is the limited practical relevance. The paper focuses heavily on theoretical guarantees, neglecting crucial aspects of implementation and scalability. The examples, while illustrative, are simplistic and don't demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* **Assumptions on Fast Mixing:** The core assumption of fast mixing (Equation 2) is a strong one. While the paper acknowledges this and provides examples where it holds (Ising model), establishing this property for complex models is non-trivial and often computationally expensive. The practical applicability heavily relies on knowing, *a priori*, that the chosen parameter set Θ satisfies this condition. The paper doesn't address how one might *discover* such sets for new problems.
* **Computational Cost:** While lower bounds are provided, the dependence on parameters like 1/ϵ³ or 1/ϵ² suggests potentially high computational costs, especially for achieving high accuracy. The scaling with problem dimensionality isn't explicitly discussed.
* **Projection Complexity:** The paper acknowledges that projection onto Θ can be computationally demanding but glosses over this aspect. For many interesting models, finding an efficient projection algorithm is a significant challenge.
* **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper briefly mentions alternatives like pseudolikelihood or score matching, but doesn’t provide a concrete comparison in terms of computational cost, sample complexity, or accuracy, even in the simplified example. It's unclear if the theoretical guarantees translate to a practical advantage.
* **Overly Detailed Background:** While thorough, sections like the extensive discussion of mixing time bounds (Section 3.1) feel disproportionately long given their direct relevance to the main contribution.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Rigorous theoretical foundation.
* General applicability to exponential families.
* Provides a FPRAS guarantee under specific conditions.

**Con:**

* Limited practical relevance and scalability.
* Strong assumption of fast mixing is difficult to verify.
* High computational cost for achieving high accuracy.
* Lack of comparison to existing methods.
* Projection can be computationally expensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound but practically limited contribution. While the mathematical analysis is impressive, the lack of concrete examples, comparison to existing methods, and discussion of implementation challenges raise serious concerns. The strong assumption of fast mixing and potentially high computational costs further detract from its practical value. I believe this work falls short of the NIPS bar for acceptance, given its limited impact on real-world problems. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should focus on demonstrating the practical applicability of their framework, perhaps by exploring specific applications where the fast-mixing assumption can be readily verified and by providing a thorough comparison to existing methods. A discussion of the computational cost of projection for different model classes would also strengthen the paper.
