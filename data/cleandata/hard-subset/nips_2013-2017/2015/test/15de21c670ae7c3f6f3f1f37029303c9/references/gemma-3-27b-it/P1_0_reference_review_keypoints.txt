- Proposes restricting parameters to a fast-mixing set Θ so MCMC mixes quickly, enabling an FPRAS for maximum likelihood within Θ.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates, including bounds on iterations, samples, and MCMC transitions.  
- Framework extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.  
- Contrasts with pseudolikelihood/score matching and structural tractability approaches by focusing on tractability via mixing rather than model structure.  
- Builds on MCMC convergence theory (Jerrum & Sinclair) and stochastic gradient results (Schmidt et al.) to link mixing times to ML feasibility.  
- Novel idea of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets is a significant contribution.  
- Strong theoretical rigor: establishes an FPRAS and convergence bounds for the proposed method.  
- General applicability beyond specific graphical models increases potential impact.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written; illustrative figures (e.g., gradient descent cartoon) aid understanding.  
- Ising model example shows feasibility and suggests bounds are not overly conservative in practice.  
- Projection onto Θ can be computationally expensive and may be a practical bottleneck depending on the parameter set.  
- Theoretical bounds appear loose, particularly dependence on the Lipschitz constant L, leaving room for tighter analysis.  
- Impact of MCMC initialization is underexplored; better initialization strategies (e.g., contrastive divergence) are mentioned but not analyzed.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited to a simple example and lacks diverse models/datasets to demonstrate practical benefits.  
- Paper lacks a deep comparison of trade-offs between the fast-mixing constraint and other parameter restrictions like low-treewidth models.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, general framework, and clearly presented — strong contribution.  
- Against acceptance: potentially loose bounds, projection costs, limited experiments, and insufficient analysis of initialization and alternative constraints.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper worthy of acceptance despite practical considerations.  
- Recommended improvements: analyze projection complexity across Θ choices, tighten convergence bounds and Lipschitz estimates, expand empirical evaluation, study initialization strategies, and compare trade-offs with other parameter constraints.
