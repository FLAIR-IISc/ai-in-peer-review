### Summary

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models. Instead of restricting the model's graphical structure to be tractable (e.g., a tree), the authors propose a fundamentally different approach: restricting the model's parameters to a convex set Θ for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" (i.e., converges geometrically to the stationary distribution).

The core contribution is a theoretical analysis of a common practical heuristic: performing projected gradient descent on the negative log-likelihood, where the gradient's expectation term is estimated using MCMC samples. The authors prove that this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within the constrained set Θ, provided one can efficiently project onto this set. The paper provides explicit schedules for setting the number of gradient iterations (K), MCMC samples per iteration (M), and MCMC chain length (v) to achieve a desired accuracy (ϵ) with high probability (1-δ). The analysis is presented for both the general convex case (e.g., unregularized) and the strongly convex case (e.g., L2-regularized), deriving total computational complexities (in terms of MCMC transitions) of O(1/ϵf³) and O(1/ϵθ²) respectively. The work connects the large body of literature on MCMC mixing times with the theory of stochastic optimization, providing the first formal convergence guarantees for this class of learning algorithms.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Approach:** The core idea of defining a tractable learning problem by constraining parameters to a fast-mixing set is novel and insightful. It reframes tractability from a structural property (low treewidth) to a dynamical one (fast convergence of sampling), which is a significant conceptual contribution.
2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a complete and rigorous theoretical analysis, formalizing a popular but previously heuristic-driven method. The derivations of explicit schedules for K, M, and v, and the resulting complexity bounds, are a strong technical achievement. This work places MCMC-based maximum likelihood estimation on a much firmer theoretical footing.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem setup, motivation, and main results are laid out logically. The inclusion of proof sketches in the main body provides valuable intuition without overwhelming the reader.
4.  **Generality:** Although motivated by graphical models like the Ising model, the theoretical framework is developed for general exponential families. This makes the results broadly applicable to other models where MCMC is used for learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practical Applicability of the Bounds:** As acknowledged by the authors in the example, the derived theoretical bounds are likely very conservative. The schedules for K, M, and v would likely be impractically large for real-world problems. The paper's contribution is therefore primarily theoretical, offering understanding rather than a ready-to-use algorithm.
2.  **Assumption of a Projection Oracle:** The entire framework hinges on the ability to efficiently project an arbitrary parameter vector onto the fast-mixing set Θ. While the paper provides examples where this is possible (e.g., clipping for bounded interactions, SVD-based for spectral norm bounds), the complexity of this projection step is not included in the main `KMv` analysis. For more complex constraints, this projection could be a computational bottleneck.
3.  **Limited Expressiveness of Known Fast-Mixing Sets:** The classes of models for which fast-mixing is proven are often quite restrictive (e.g., Ising models with weak interactions). This might limit the expressive power of the models that can be learned under these guarantees.
4.  **Lack of Empirical Comparison:** The paper provides a single, small-scale illustrative example. There is no comparison to other approximate learning methods like pseudolikelihood, score matching, or even more heuristic MCMC-based methods like contrastive divergence. An empirical evaluation showing how the solution quality or computational cost compares to these alternatives, even on small problems, would have strengthened the paper.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the proof sketches, which build upon established results in convex optimization (Schmidt et al., 2011) and concentration inequalities. The authors are honest about the limitations of their work, discussing the ignored cost of projection and the relationship to other heuristics like contrastive divergence. The work is a complete theoretical contribution.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is a model of clarity. The introduction perfectly situates the work within the existing literature, clearly articulating the drawbacks of prior methods and motivating the paper's novel approach. The notation is well-defined in Section 2. The separation of the analysis into convex and strongly convex cases is logical and helps the reader appreciate the different guarantees.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While using MCMC to approximate gradients is a classic technique (e.g., Geyer, 1991; Tieleman, 2008), this paper is the first to provide a full, non-asymptotic convergence analysis by explicitly leveraging the mixing properties of the underlying Markov chain to bound the optimization error. This connection between mixing times and learning guarantees is a novel and important contribution that is distinct from prior work on tractable learning which focuses on graphical structure.

**Significance:** The paper holds significant theoretical importance. It provides a foundational understanding for a widely used class of algorithms, explaining *why* and *under what conditions* they should work. This could inspire future research in several directions: (1) developing tighter, more practical bounds; (2) designing new regularization techniques that directly encourage fast mixing; (3) analyzing more sophisticated MCMC-based learning schemes (e.g., those with sample pools or adaptive step sizes) through a similar lens. While direct practitioners may not implement the derived schedules, the insights are valuable for anyone developing or using these methods. The paper demonstrably advances the state of the art in the theoretical understanding of learning in intractable models.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Highly original and conceptually elegant approach to tractable learning.
*   Provides the first rigorous, non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for the widely used MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning procedure.
*   The theoretical analysis is sound, complete, and clearly presented.
*   The work has high potential for theoretical impact, opening up new avenues for analyzing and designing learning algorithms for intractable models.

**Con:**
*   The practical utility is limited due to the conservative nature of the theoretical bounds.
*   The analysis side-steps the potentially significant computational cost of projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ.
*   The empirical validation is minimal, consisting of only a single toy example with no comparison to baseline methods.

**Recommendation:** I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper's weaknesses, primarily related to its immediate practical utility, are far outweighed by its theoretical novelty and significance. It provides a foundational result that bridges the gap between MCMC theory and optimization for machine learning, and it will likely become a key reference in this area.
