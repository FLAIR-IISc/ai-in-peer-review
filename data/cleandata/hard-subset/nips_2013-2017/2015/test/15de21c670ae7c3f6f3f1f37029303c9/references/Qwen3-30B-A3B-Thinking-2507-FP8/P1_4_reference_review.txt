

# Peer Review: "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is intractable for general models due to the #P-completeness of partition function computation. While previous work has focused on tractable constraints like tree-structured models (Chow-Liu) or well-specified models (pseudolikelihood, score matching), this paper proposes a fundamentally different approach. The authors introduce the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" – constraint sets Θ for which MCMC sampling mixes rapidly – and show that gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates converges to the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. 

The paper builds on theoretical work on mixing times (Jerrum & Sinclair, 2013; Domke & Liu, 2013) but applies it to the learning setting in a novel way. Unlike previous approaches that restrict to low-treewidth models (where inference is tractable), this work focuses on parameter sets where MCMC converges quickly, which is a different notion of tractability. The authors also relate their work to Steinhardt & Liang's (2014) fast-mixing Markov chains for learning.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Novel Framework**: The paper establishes a rigorous theoretical foundation for MCMC-based learning methods, which have been widely used in practice but lacked theoretical guarantees. The concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" is a significant conceptual contribution that bridges the gap between theory and practice.

2. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The authors provide thorough analysis for both convex and strongly convex cases, with explicit bounds on the computational effort required to achieve a desired accuracy (ϵf for convex, ϵθ for strongly convex). The bounds are tight, as shown by the lower bounds in Theorems 3 and 7.

3. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with clear notation, well-structured proofs (with proof sketches provided), and a helpful example in Section 6 that illustrates the theoretical results. The relationship between the theoretical bounds and practical implementation is clearly explained.

4. **Applicability Beyond Graphical Models**: While motivated by graphical models, the results are stated in the general exponential family framework, making them applicable to a broader range of models.

5. **Honest Assessment of Limitations**: The authors clearly acknowledge the limitations of their approach, including the computational cost of projection and the potential for more efficient initialization strategies.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Experimental Validation**: While the authors correctly state they don't claim practical contribution, the paper lacks any experimental results to validate the theoretical bounds. The example in Section 6 is purely illustrative and doesn't test the theoretical bounds against empirical performance.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't address how to choose the constraint set Θ in practice beyond simple examples (like bounding Ising model parameters). For many real-world models, determining an appropriate fast-mixing set Θ could be non-trivial.

3. **Projection Complexity**: While the authors acknowledge that projection complexity might be significant for large models, they don't analyze how the complexity of projection scales with model size, which could be a bottleneck in practice.

4. **Mixing Time Assumptions**: The paper relies on mixing time bounds that often require uniform interaction strengths (as noted in Section 3.1), which may not hold in many practical scenarios. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide guidance on adapting their framework to more complex mixing time bounds.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first rigorous guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning.
- The results are general, applicable to any exponential family, and provide explicit computational complexity bounds.
- The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims.
- The work addresses a fundamental problem in statistical learning with clear implications for both theory and practice.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The lack of experimental results makes it difficult to assess how the theoretical bounds translate to practical performance.
- The paper's primary contribution is theoretical, and while this is acceptable for NIPS, the absence of any empirical validation might limit its impact.
- The practical applicability of the proposed method is unclear, as the paper doesn't address how to choose Θ for specific models beyond simple examples.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed. While the lack of experimental validation is a limitation, the paper is clearly positioned as a theoretical contribution, and the authors appropriately acknowledge this. The results are novel, well-supported, and provide a crucial theoretical foundation for a widely used class of learning methods (MCMC-based maximum likelihood).

The paper advances the state of the art by establishing the first FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning within fast-mixing parameter sets. This work will be valuable to researchers developing theoretical foundations for statistical learning methods, particularly those working with exponential families and graphical models. The detailed analysis of computational complexity provides a roadmap for practitioners to understand the trade-offs between accuracy and computational effort.

The main weakness (lack of experiments) is understandable given the paper's theoretical focus, and the authors correctly state they're not claiming practical contribution. For a theoretical paper at NIPS, the strength of the theoretical results outweighs this limitation. The paper is of high quality, original, and significant for the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how to choose Θ for specific model classes (beyond the Ising example), perhaps referencing recent work on fast-mixing constraints.

2. While the paper is theoretical, a sentence or two about potential practical implications of the bounds (e.g., "These bounds suggest that for models with fast-mixing parameters, MCMC-based learning can achieve ϵ-accuracy with computational effort proportional to ϵ^{-3} log(1/ϵ)") would help bridge theory and practice.

3. In Section 3.1, the discussion of mixing time bounds could be slightly expanded to clarify when these bounds apply and how they might need adaptation for different models.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of MCMC-based learning methods in exponential families.
