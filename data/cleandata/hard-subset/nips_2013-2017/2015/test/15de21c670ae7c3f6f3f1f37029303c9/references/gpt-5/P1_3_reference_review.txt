Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a novel tractability constraint: parameters are restricted to a convex set Θ for which a chosen MCMC kernel is guaranteed to be fast-mixing with uniform geometric contraction in total variation. Within this “fast-mixing parameter set,” the authors analyze projected gradient descent where the intractable model expectation is approximated by finite-length MCMC sampling, yielding biased and noisy gradient estimates. They show that, with appropriate settings of (i) the number of gradient steps K, (ii) samples per step M, and (iii) Markov transitions per sample v, the algorithm reaches an ε-accurate solution with high probability. They provide two regimes: a convex analysis (possibly λ=0) that controls suboptimality of the objective f at the average iterate, and a strongly convex analysis (λ>0) that controls parameter error at the last iterate. They derive explicit complexity bounds, schedules for K, M, v close to lower bounds, and interpret these as an FPRAS-type result for ML within Θ. They illustrate the approach on a small Ising example, and connect to known fast-mixing conditions (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral-norm-type conditions and ferromagnetic Ising via subgraphs world).

Relation to prior work
The work relates to: (i) classical MCMC maximum likelihood learning [10, 11]; (ii) surrogate estimators (pseudo-likelihood, score matching, composite likelihood, contrastive divergence) [3, 16, 20, 29], which can be efficient but may not target the M-projection under misspecification; (iii) learning with fast-mixing chains (e.g., restarts) [28]; and (iv) projecting to fast-mixing parameter sets for inference/learning [8, 21]. The novelty is a unified finite-sample convergence analysis of MLE within a fast-mixing set for general exponential families, explicitly handling the bias induced by finite-length MCMC and giving near-optimal work bounds in terms of ε.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: treats tractability via fast mixing rather than low treewidth, and analyzes learning end-to-end under this assumption.
- Generality: framed for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not only discrete MRFs.
- Theory: clear decomposition of gradient error into sampling noise and mixing bias; convex vs strongly convex regimes; Lipschitz gradient bound; explicit schedules close to provable lower bounds.
- Bridges literature: connects mixing-time bounds (Glauber, spectral norms, subgraphs world) with inexact projected gradient descent [26].
- Clarifies what it would take to turn “MCMC-in-the-loop learning” into a provably polynomial-time approximation scheme when Θ admits uniform mixing.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope of Θ and constants: The results require a convex Θ with uniform TV-contraction constants C, α known a priori and the ability to project onto Θ. For many models/families, constructing such Θ and computing projections (beyond the Ising/spectral-norm cases) may be nontrivial; C and α are often unknown and very loose in practice.
- FPRAS terminology: In counting, FPRAS denotes multiplicative guarantees for partition functions. Here the guarantee is additive in objective (or parameter norm). Please clarify the sense in which the algorithm is an FPRAS for maximum likelihood and define the approximation notion precisely.
- Practicality of bounds: Total work scaling as εf^−3 (convex) or εθ^−2 (strongly convex), with logarithmic factors and large constants (via R, C, α, L), may be overly conservative; schedules depend on unknown quantities. The small 4×4 Ising example does not stress the method nor test the projection runtime.
- Limited empirical validation: No comparison to standard alternatives (pseudo-likelihood, score matching, CD, learning with restarts [28]), no larger models, and no study of trade-offs in K/M/v under a fixed compute budget.
- Notational clarity: The bound uses R2 variably; e.g., “R2” appears to be a bound on ||t(x)||2, then “4 R22” looks like 4 R^2 but is easy to misread. Earlier “Ra” is introduced but unused later. Algorithm 1 is referenced but not shown in the excerpt; full pseudocode would help.

Quality
The analysis appears sound and carefully built on standard tools (Efron–Stein, Bernstein, multidimensional Hoeffding, projected gradient with errors [26]). Proof sketches are plausible; full details should be checked in the appendix. The mixing assumptions and projection step are clearly stated, and limitations are acknowledged in the discussion. Constants may be pessimistic, but that is typical for this line of work.

Clarity
Generally well-written and well-organized, with intuitive explanations after theorems and “back-of-the-envelope” guidance for parameter schedules. Suggestions:
- Unify notation for the sufficient-statistics norm bound (use R with ||t(x)||2 ≤ R), and rewrite L = 4R^2 + λ to avoid R22.
- Precisely define the approximation guarantee (additive vs multiplicative) and the use of “FPRAS.”
- Include Algorithm 1 pseudocode with inputs/outputs and projection details.
- When citing mixing bounds (e.g., (4)), briefly state assumptions (binary vs multi-valued; update scheme; graph structure) and whether constants are uniform over Θ.

Originality
The core idea—provable, end-to-end ML within a fast-mixing set with explicit complexity vs ε under biased MCMC gradients—appears novel, going beyond prior work on CD or learning with restarts and beyond previous “projection for fast mixing” which focused on inference or lacked learning guarantees. The convex/strongly-convex separation and near-matching lower/upper bounds are also nice additions.

Significance
The results provide a rigorous pathway to polynomial-time learning in otherwise intractable models, under a defensible and increasingly studied notion of tractability. Even if practical impact today is limited by constants and availability of Θ, the framework is likely to influence subsequent theoretical and algorithmic work on learning with MCMC in discrete and continuous exponential families.

Suggestions for improvement
- Empirics: add experiments on larger grids or dense graphs; compare to pseudo-likelihood, score matching, CD/persistent CD, and the restart-based method [28]; report objective gap vs compute; study sensitivity to K/M/v; show projection runtime.
- Adaptive schedules: discuss how to set K/M/v when C, α, L, R are unknown (e.g., doubling schemes, diagnostics for mixing, adaptive burn-in).
- Beyond Gibbs: clarify applicability when using alternative kernels (Swendsen–Wang, subgraphs world) and how projection to Θ interacts with kernel-specific mixing conditions.
- Continuous families: discuss extension to log-concave continuous exponential families with Langevin/HMC mixing, where fast mixing can be certified (e.g., via log-Sobolev/Poincaré).
- Related work: briefly situate against recent analyses of biased/inexact stochastic gradients and MCMC-based optimization in ML.

Minor comments
- Check typos and missing years/page numbers in references; standardize citation formatting.
- Spell out assumptions in Theorem 1 and restate the boundedness of t(x).

Pros
- Clear, rigorous theoretical contribution connecting mixing, MCMC bias, and ML optimization.
- General exponential-family framing; explicit schedules and near-optimal complexity.
- Useful perspective on tractable learning beyond treewidth.

Cons
- Strong assumptions on Θ, constants, and projection; limited practical guidance to instantiate Θ broadly.
- Minimal experiments; unclear empirical competitiveness.
- FPRAS terminology potentially misleading; notation could be clearer.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a clean and potentially influential theoretical framework with solid analysis. Strengthen the empirical section and clarify assumptions/notation to maximize impact.
