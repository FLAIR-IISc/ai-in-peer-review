

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper proposes a theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families where the parameter set is constrained to a "fast-mixing" set Θ. The key insight is that if Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling mixes rapidly for all parameters in Θ, then gradient descent with approximate gradients (using MCMC samples) can efficiently find the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, showing that the total computational effort (KMv, where K is the number of gradient steps, M is the number of samples per step, and v is the number of Markov chain transitions per sample) scales polynomially with the desired accuracy.

## Technical Strengths
The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by connecting fast-mixing properties of Markov chains to the convergence guarantees of gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning. The framework is general and applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models. The distinction between convex and strongly convex cases is well-motivated, and the analysis carefully handles the bias introduced by MCMC sampling.

## Significant Technical Flaws
Despite its promising theoretical framework, the paper contains several critical technical errors that undermine its main claims:

1. **Incorrect Lipschitz Constant Bound**: Theorem 1 claims L = 4R₂² + λ, but the proof sketch incorrectly states ∥dA/dθ - dA/dφ∥₂ ≤ 2R₂∥pθ - pφ∥TV. This should be ∥dA/dθ - dA/dφ∥₂ ≤ R₂²∥pθ - pφ∥TV (see Wainwright & Jordan, 2008, for the correct bound), making the Lipschitz constant likely L = R₂² + λ rather than 4R₂² + λ.

2. **Incorrect Total Variation Bound**: In the proof sketch for Theorem 1, the claim ∥pθ - pφ∥TV ≤ 2R₂∥θ - φ∥₂ is mathematically incorrect. The correct bound should be ∥pθ - pφ∥TV ≤ R₂∥θ - φ∥₂ (see Theorem 2.1 in Wainwright & Jordan, 2008), which would change the scaling of the Lipschitz constant.

3. **Scaling Errors in Error Bounds**: In Theorem 2, the bound on the sum of errors is stated as ∑∥ek∥ ≤ R₂(K/√M + log 1/δ) + 2KR₂Cαᵛ. The term K/√M is inconsistent with standard concentration bounds, which would yield √K/√M. This error would invalidate the complexity analysis.

4. **Notation Inconsistency**: The paper uses R₂ to denote both the norm bound of sufficient statistics and the square of R (e.g., "R2 = √24" in Section 6). This should be consistently denoted as R₂ or R_2 throughout.

5. **Missing Justification for Key Steps**: The proof sketches omit critical steps, such as how ∥pθ - pφ∥TV ≤ 2R₂∥θ - φ∥₂ is derived (which is mathematically questionable), making the theoretical results difficult to verify.

## Clarity and Presentation Issues
- The paper uses inconsistent notation for the norm bound (R2 vs R₂ vs R_2).
- The example in Section 6 states "R2 = √24" but then uses R₂ in the theoretical analysis, creating confusion.
- The relationship between ϵf (optimality in log-likelihood) and ϵθ (parameter distance) is not clearly explained, making it difficult to interpret the complexity bounds.
- The paper claims to provide a FPRAS (Fully Polynomial-time Randomized Approximation Scheme) but does not define this in the context of maximum likelihood learning, which is confusing.

## Significance and Originality
The paper builds on existing work on fast-mixing Markov chains (e.g., Domke & Liu, 2013; Liu & Domke, 2014) and applies it to maximum likelihood learning. While the theoretical framework is valuable, the significant technical errors substantially weaken the contribution. The results are not as novel as claimed, as similar connections between mixing times and learning have been explored in works like Steinhardt & Liang (2013) and Domke & Liu (2013).

## Recommendations
The paper has a promising theoretical framework but contains critical technical errors that invalidate its main claims. The errors in the Lipschitz constant bound and total variation distance would require substantial reworking of the proofs. The inconsistent notation and missing justifications further undermine the paper's credibility.

Given the paper's fundamental technical flaws, I recommend rejection. The errors are not minor but affect the core theoretical claims. While the problem addressed is significant, the paper fails to provide a rigorous theoretical foundation due to these errors.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses:**

*Strengths:*
- Clear theoretical framework for connecting fast-mixing properties to learning guarantees
- General applicability to exponential families (not just graphical models)
- Distinction between convex and strongly convex cases is well-motivated

*Weaknesses:*
- Critical errors in the Lipschitz constant bound (Theorem 1)
- Incorrect total variation distance bound (used in proof sketches)
- Inconsistent notation for norm bounds (R2 vs R₂)
- Scaling errors in error bounds (K/√M instead of √K/√M)
- Lack of justification for key technical claims
- Confusing example in Section 6 with inconsistent notation

The paper's contribution is undermined by these technical errors, and it does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS. The theoretical results cannot be trusted due to these fundamental flaws, making the paper unsuitable for publication.
