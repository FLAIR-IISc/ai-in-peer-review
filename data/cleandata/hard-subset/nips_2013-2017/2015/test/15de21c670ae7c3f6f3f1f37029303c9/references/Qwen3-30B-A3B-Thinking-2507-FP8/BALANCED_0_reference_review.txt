

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical models: the intractability of maximum likelihood learning. The authors propose a novel approach to tractability by focusing on fast-mixing parameter sets Θ, where MCMC sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. Their main contribution is showing that for such sets, there exists a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning, with theoretical guarantees on the computational complexity required to achieve a desired accuracy.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for gradient descent using MCMC to approximate gradients. The authors provide rigorous bounds for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases, showing that the total computational effort KMv scales as (1/ϵ^3)log(1/ϵ) for the convex case and (1/ϵ^2)log(1/ϵ) for the strongly convex case. The theoretical framework is general, applying to any exponential family, not just graphical models.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a novel theoretical contribution by shifting the focus from low-treewidth models to fast-mixing parameter sets, which is a fundamentally different approach to tractability.
2. The results are technically sound, with careful proofs and clear theorems that build on existing mixing time theory.
3. The paper provides detailed theoretical bounds on computational complexity, which is valuable for understanding the practical feasibility of MCMC-based learning approaches.
4. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., ignoring projection complexity) and discuss potential extensions, demonstrating intellectual honesty.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper makes no practical contribution, explicitly stating "This paper claims no significant practical contribution." While theoretical work is valuable, the lack of empirical results or discussion of practical implementation challenges limits its impact for the NeurIPS community.
2. The example with a 4x4 Ising model is overly simplistic and does not demonstrate the approach's utility on meaningful problems. The paper acknowledges the theoretical bounds are conservative, but without empirical evidence, it's unclear how much conservatism exists in practice.
3. The paper does not compare the theoretical computational complexity with existing approaches (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching, or variational methods), making it difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed method.
4. The assumption of known mixing time parameters (C, α) is restrictive for real-world applications where these would need to be estimated.
5. The paper doesn't address how to select the fast-mixing set Θ for a given problem, which is crucial for practical implementation.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add empirical results on larger datasets to demonstrate the practical feasibility of the approach and validate the theoretical bounds. Even simple experiments on benchmark datasets would strengthen the paper significantly.
2. Discuss practical methods for estimating the mixing time parameters (C, α) and for selecting the fast-mixing set Θ. This would bridge the gap between theory and practice.
3. Compare the theoretical computational complexity with existing methods to provide context for the paper's contribution. A table summarizing complexity bounds for different approaches would be valuable.
4. Address the projection complexity more thoroughly, as this could be a bottleneck in some applications. Discuss how the projection complexity scales with model size.
5. Consider extending the discussion to include how the approach might interact with techniques like contrastive divergence, which the authors mention briefly in the discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning. The results are sound and extend the theoretical foundations of graphical models. However, the lack of practical contribution and empirical validation limits its significance for the NeurIPS community, which values both theoretical and practical advances. The theoretical results are novel and well-executed, but the paper would benefit substantially from empirical validation and a clearer connection to practical implementation.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and careful notation. The theoretical results are presented with appropriate rigor. While the work is significant from a theoretical perspective, the absence of practical validation and comparison with existing methods weakens its impact for a venue like NeurIPS.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution, but the lack of empirical results and practical discussion prevents it from being a strong candidate for acceptance. With additional empirical validation and a more thorough discussion of practical implementation, this could be a strong contribution.
