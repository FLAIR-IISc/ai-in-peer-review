Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families (with bounded sufficient statistics) under a tractability notion based on fast mixing of an MCMC sampler rather than graphical treewidth. The core idea is to constrain parameters to a convex “fast-mixing set” Θ for which a Markov chain Mθ has uniform geometric convergence in total variation, i.e., supq ∥Mvθ q − pθ∥TV ≤ C α^v for all θ ∈ Θ. Under this assumption and an oracle for Euclidean projection onto Θ, the paper analyzes projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated via MCMC samples, and provides schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and chain length v that guarantee—with high probability—either small suboptimality in the (regularized) negative log-likelihood (convex case) or small parameter error (strongly convex case). The resulting total sampling effort KMv yields a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained MLE inside Θ: O(ϵ_f^−3 log(1/ϵ_f)) work for the convex case and O(ϵ_θ^−2 polylog) for the strongly convex case. The analysis also gives lower bounds on KMv and explicit near-matching schedules.

The work relates to: (i) classical MCMC-MLE with stochastic approximation (Geyer; Gu & Zhu) and contrastive divergence (Hinton; Tieleman), which lack guarantees under biased MCMC gradients; (ii) structure-constrained learning (Chow–Liu; Heinemann & Globerson) and strategies with fast-mixing chains by design (Steinhardt & Liang); and (iii) fast-mixing parameterizations for Ising/MRFs (Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Liu & Domke), where projection onto spectral/entrywise norm constraints enforces rapid mixing of Gibbs or related dynamics. The novelty is to combine fast-mixing parameter sets with a principled inexact projected-gradient analysis to obtain global, high-probability approximation guarantees for maximum likelihood in arbitrary-treewidth models.

Quality
– Technical soundness: The analysis builds on standard tools: Lipschitz gradients for exponential families with bounded statistics; bias-variance decomposition of MCMC gradient errors; concentration (Efron–Stein, Bernstein; multidimensional Hoeffding); and convergence of projected gradient with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al.). The assumptions are explicit: convex Θ with uniform mixing constants (C, α) and a projection oracle. Theorems provide both upper bounds and lower bounds on the total sampling work, and schedules nearly match the lower bounds up to constants/log factors. The treatment of the regularized strongly convex case is careful in using uniform per-iteration error bounds and a union bound. The claims appear plausible and consistent with cited mixing results for Ising/MRFs. Some details would benefit from clarification (see below), but no obvious conceptual gaps are apparent in the provided sketches.

– Completeness and evaluation: The paper offers a toy Ising example to illustrate parameter choices. Empirical evaluation is not the focus; however, the example is minimal and does not stress-test the theory (e.g., scaling, mis-specification). Proof details are deferred to an appendix; the main text contains sketches.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized: setup, mixing-time background, main results for convex/strongly convex cases, and discussion. Notation is mostly coherent, though there are several minor issues:
- R2 is used to denote the bound on ∥t(x)∥2; sometimes written R22, which is easy to misread.
- Algorithm 1 is referenced but not shown in the excerpt. Ensure the algorithm is clearly specified (inputs, projection step, step sizes).
- Theorems 2 and 6 use abstract constants (a, b, c) then map them to problem parameters later; a consolidated theorem with explicit dependencies would improve readability.
- The FPRAS claim would benefit from a precise statement including input size and cost model (e.g., per MCMC transition and projection).

Originality
The key contribution—formalizing an FPRAS for constrained MLE via fast-mixing parameter sets and analyzing biased MCMC-gradient projected descent with explicit schedules and lower bounds—appears novel. Prior works either restrict structure (trees/high girth), adopt alternative estimators (pseudo-/composite likelihood, score matching), or use MCMC without guarantees; Steinhardt & Liang ensure mixing by design via restarts but do not target MLE in arbitrary exponential families. The integration of projection onto fast-mixing Θ with convergence guarantees for biased gradients is a new synthesis.

Significance
As a theoretical contribution, the paper clarifies when biased MCMC gradient methods can approximate MLE with high probability and quantifies the work needed. It provides a pathway for principled variants of contrastive divergence or MCMC-MLE in regimes where one can enforce or certify fast mixing (e.g., via spectral/entrywise bounds). Practical impact may be limited by the need to: (i) construct Θ with known C, α and a tractable projection; and (ii) accept potential statistical suboptimality if the true parameters lie outside Θ. Still, the results advance understanding of learning under mixing-time constraints and may influence algorithm design in energy-based models.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, general framework connecting mixing-time control to MLE with high-probability guarantees.
- Both convex and strongly convex regimes analyzed, with explicit complexity and lower bounds.
- Relates to and extends multiple lines of prior work (MCMC-MLE, CD, fast-mixing parameterizations).
- Applicable to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evidence is minimal; no demonstrations on larger or mis-specified settings to illustrate trade-offs.
- The FPRAS claim hinges on availability and cost of projection; complexity of projection is largely ignored in guarantees.
- Assumption of uniform mixing constants over Θ may be hard to verify or loose in practice; explicit dimension dependence (N, graph degree) could be made more transparent.
- Some notational inconsistencies and reliance on abstract constants reduce readability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise FPRAS theorem statement including input size, bit complexity assumptions, and how C, α scale with N for representative Θ (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral-norm sets).
- Include Algorithm 1 explicitly with step sizes and projection details.
- Add experiments beyond the 4×4 Ising toy: scaling with N; mis-specified targets; comparison to pseudo-/composite likelihood and CD under the same Θ constraints.
- Discuss approximate projections and their impact on guarantees; many projections may only be solved approximately.
- Clarify the Lipschitz proof step bounding ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ − φ∥2 and state any additional assumptions (finite sample space).
- Comment on sample reuse (“persistent” chains) and whether analogous guarantees might be obtained.

Scope
The topic is within NeurIPS scope (learning and inference in probabilistic models; MCMC; optimization).

Overall assessment
A theoretically solid and novel contribution that formalizes conditions under which MCMC-based approximate gradients yield provably near-optimal MLE in high-treewidth models, via fast-mixing parameter constraints. The work is primarily theoretical; strengthening clarity and empirical illustration would improve impact.
