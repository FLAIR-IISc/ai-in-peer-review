This paper addresses the problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, specifically focusing on scenarios where direct maximum likelihood estimation is intractable. It proposes a framework leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets to establish a fast-mixing parameter set to establish a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within a constrained parameter space. The core idea is that if one can efficiently project onto a set of parameters that guarantees fast mixing of MCMC, then approximate gradient descent can achieve a desired accuracy. The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the convergence rate of gradient descent in both convex and strongly convex settings, along with explicit schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC transitions required.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing approaches like pseudolikelihood and score matching. It correctly identifies the connection between treewidth and computational tractability, and proposes a different perspective based on MCMC mixing rates. The contribution lies in providing theoretical guarantees for a learning strategy commonly used in practice—gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimation—under a reasonable assumption of fast mixing. The analysis is thorough, covering both regularized and unregularized cases, and providing explicit rates. The presentation is generally clear, with the core ideas and key theorems well-defined. The inclusion of an example with a concrete Ising model instance and discussion of practical considerations (like initialization) adds value.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees for the convergence of gradient descent with MCMC estimation, a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** The focus on fast-mixing parameter sets as a means to achieve tractability is a novel and promising direction. It shifts the focus from graph structure (treewidth) to properties of the parameter space itself.
*   **Generality:** The results are stated in the general framework of exponential families, making them applicable to a wider range of models beyond graphical models.
*   **Explicit Schedules:** Providing explicit schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC transitions is valuable for practical implementation.
*   **Well-Organized and Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easier to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality Concerns:** The assumption of knowing and being able to efficiently project onto a set of fast-mixing parameters might be challenging in many real-world applications. The computational cost of projection is not fully considered in the complexity analysis.
*   **Conservatism of Bounds:** The theoretical bounds might be conservative, as indicated by the example where the algorithm converges faster than predicted.
*   **Limited Discussion of Initialization:** While the paper briefly mentions initialization, a more detailed discussion of its impact on convergence and potential strategies (e.g., contrastive divergence) would be beneficial.
*   **Dependency on Mixing Time Bounds:** The performance relies heavily on accurate bounds on the mixing time. Obtaining tight mixing time bounds for complex models can be challenging.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The single example, while helpful, is insufficient to demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach on a diverse set of problems.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important theoretical challenge in maximum likelihood learning.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous and provide valuable insights into the convergence properties of gradient descent with MCMC.
*   The approach offers a novel perspective on tractability based on fast-mixing parameter sets.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical applicability of the approach might be limited by the difficulty of finding and projecting onto fast-mixing parameter sets.
*   The theoretical bounds might be conservative, suggesting that further refinement is needed.
*   The limited empirical evaluation hinders a comprehensive assessment of the method's practical performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of maximum likelihood learning. The idea of leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets is novel and has the potential to advance our understanding of tractability in graphical models and related areas. While the practical challenges associated with finding and projecting onto such sets are real, the theoretical results are valuable and warrant acceptance. A stronger emphasis on practical considerations and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation would further enhance the paper's impact.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
