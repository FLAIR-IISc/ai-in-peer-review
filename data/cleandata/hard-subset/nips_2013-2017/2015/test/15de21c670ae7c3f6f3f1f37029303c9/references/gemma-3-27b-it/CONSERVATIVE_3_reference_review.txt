This paper explores maximum likelihood learning in exponential families with constraints, aiming to achieve tractability via fast-mixing Markov chains. The core idea is that if a parameter set guarantees rapid MCMC convergence, a FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution within that set exists. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients, considering both convex and strongly convex cases.

The paper is technically sound and builds on established concepts in graphical models, optimization, and MCMC. The formulation within the exponential family framework is a reasonable generalization. However, the significant reliance on the fast-mixing assumption feels like a substantial hurdle, and the practical implications are not convincingly demonstrated. While the theoretical results are presented with rigor, the work largely feels like a formalization of existing practices (gradient descent with MCMC) rather than a truly novel breakthrough. The authors correctly identify the lack of theoretical guarantees for these common strategies and address this gap, but the conditions required for the guarantees – specifically, guaranteeing a fast-mixing parameter set – are often difficult to verify in practice. 

The clarity is good, and the notation is well-defined, although the sheer number of symbols introduced upfront is somewhat daunting. The organization is logical, progressing from setup and background to main results. The inclusion of proof sketches is helpful, though a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the projection step (mentioned briefly) would be valuable.

The originality is moderate. The core idea of leveraging fast mixing for a FPRAS, while interesting, isn't entirely new; the connection between mixing rates and optimization has been explored in related areas. The main contribution lies in providing formal guarantees for a widely-used practice, but the conditions for those guarantees limit the broad applicability. The related work section is comprehensive, demonstrating awareness of the existing literature.

The significance is questionable. While theoretically interesting, the paper doesn't offer a compelling practical advantage over existing methods. The example provided is a relatively simple Ising model, and the gains from using this approach are not clearly quantified. The authors acknowledge that the projections can be computationally demanding, and the need for a fast-mixing parameter set presents a significant challenge. It’s unclear whether the theoretical benefits translate to real-world performance improvements, especially considering the often-complex process of finding/designing such parameter sets. The results primarily benefit those working in the theoretical foundations of statistical learning, rather than practitioners seeking more efficient learning algorithms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a theoretical gap in the understanding of MCMC-based maximum likelihood estimation.
*   Provides rigorous guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients.
*   Formalizes the connection between fast mixing and the existence of a FPRAS.
*   The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The fast-mixing assumption is a significant limitation, as it is often difficult to verify in practice.
*   The practical benefits are not convincingly demonstrated, and the computational cost of projection is a concern.
*   The work primarily offers a theoretical formalization of existing practices rather than a genuinely novel algorithmic contribution.
*   The example provided is relatively simple and doesn't showcase the full potential of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper is technically sound and addresses a relevant theoretical problem, the strong assumptions and limited practical implications outweigh the benefits. The paper feels more like a theoretical exploration than a contribution that will significantly impact the field. The lack of a compelling practical demonstration weakens the case for acceptance. Further investigation into the practical feasibility of finding fast-mixing parameter sets and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation are needed before this work could be considered for publication at NIPS. A revision addressing these concerns may be worth considering.
