Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood estimation in discrete exponential families under a constraint that the parameter vector θ lies in a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ, i.e., for all θ ∈ Θ a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically with known constants (C, α). Given a projection oracle onto Θ, the authors analyze projected gradient descent that uses MCMC to approximate the gradient, establishing high-probability convergence rates in two regimes: (i) convex (λ = 0), giving suboptimality in objective, and (ii) strongly convex (λ > 0, via ℓ2 regularization), giving parameter error. They derive schedules for the number of iterations K, samples per iteration M, and Markov steps v that yield polynomial total work KMv in 1/ε and other problem parameters, and frame this as an FPRAS for constrained ML. They also connect to concrete fast-mixing sets for Ising/MRFs (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral-norm conditions) for which projection is convex. A small Ising example illustrates the schedules’ conservatism.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned relative to pseudolikelihood/score matching/composite likelihood (Besag; Hyvärinen; Lindsay/Varin) and to learning under fast-mixing constraints (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke; Steinhardt & Liang). It also builds on inexact/proximal gradient descent analyses with errors (Schmidt et al.) and classical mixing-time results (Levin–Peres–Wilmer; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes; Jerrum–Sinclair). The contribution sits between MCMC-MLE/Stochastic Approximation MLE (Geyer; Gu & Zhu) and analyses of biased gradients in CD/PCD, providing explicit complexity in terms of mixing parameters and projection complexity.

Strengths
- Clear and general framework: exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, a convex fast-mixing set Θ, and a projection oracle lead to broadly applicable guarantees.
- Technically sound overall approach: decomposes gradient error into sampling error and bias from finite mixing, controls both via standard concentration and TV bounds, and plugs into established convergence results for projected (strongly) convex optimization with inexact gradients.
- Concrete instantiations: revisits well-known fast-mixing conditions (e.g., Dobrushin/spectral norm) and connects them to efficient projections for Ising/MRFs.
- Practical takeaways: explicit parameter schedules (K, M, v) and total-work bounds; discussion of limitations (pooling samples, projection cost).
- Conceptual significance: shows that if one enforces fast mixing, then ML within Θ is computationally tractable with polynomial-time guarantees, even for arbitrary treewidth graphs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions on mixing constants: The analysis requires that uniform geometric mixing holds for all θ ∈ Θ with known constants (C, α). In realistic high-dimensional models, tight and usable values of (C, α) are rarely known; available bounds are notoriously loose. The prescribed schedules depend explicitly on these constants, limiting practical guidance. A result with adaptive/doubling schedules that does not assume known (C, α) would strengthen the contribution.
- Independence of samples: The concentration arguments treat the M samples at each iteration as independent draws from qk. This implies M independent chains each run for v steps from independent initializations. While valid, it is unusual in practice (where one runs a single chain or maintains a persistent pool). The paper acknowledges this and excludes the “persistent pool” strategy from its guarantees; however, there exist concentration inequalities for Markov chains (e.g., Paulin 2015) that could allow single-chain sampling or pooling with dependent samples, potentially yielding more practical guarantees.
- Lipschitz constant of the gradient: Theorem 1 bounds L by 4 R2^2 + λ (with R2 the bound on the ℓ2-norm of t(x)). A simpler, tighter argument uses ∇^2A(θ) = Covθ[t(X)] whose operator norm is bounded by E||t||^2 ≤ R2^2, giving L ≤ R2^2 + λ. The proof sketch’s step asserting ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ − φ∥2 is nontrivial and, as stated, is not standard; the covariance bound avoids this. Tightening constants would sharpen all downstream rates.
- “FPRAS” terminology: Classical FPRAS refers to multiplicative approximation to a scalar quantity in time polynomial in input size and 1/ε. Here the guarantees are additive in objective or parameter distance, and the runtime depends on problem-dependent constants (C, α) and a projection oracle. The result is indeed a randomized polynomial-time approximation scheme for the constrained ML problem but does not strictly match the usual FPRAS definition. Rephrasing as a randomized polynomial-time additive ε-approximation would avoid confusion.
- Generality of projection: For Ising/MRFs under spectral-norm/Dobrushin constraints, projection is convex and polynomial-time. For other models/samplers (e.g., Swendsen–Wang or ferromagnetic constraints), defining Θ and providing a practical projector is less clear. The claim of generality to “any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics” is correct at the level of analysis, but the practicality hinges on model-specific Θ and projection algorithms.
- Limited empirical validation: The toy 4×4 Ising example illustrates conservatism but does not evaluate the practical efficacy of the approach versus pseudolikelihood/CD or demonstrate that the fast-mixing constraint yields useful models. Even a small comparative study would help assess significance.

Questions and requests for clarification
1. Can you provide a self-contained proof of Theorem 1 via the covariance bound to sharpen L and remove the TV-parameter Lipschitz step? If you retain the current proof, please justify the inequality ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2 R2 ∥θ − φ∥2.
2. Are (C, α) assumed known to the algorithm? If not, could you propose a doubling scheme for v and M with stopping criteria that provably achieves the same guarantees up to log factors?
3. Can your concentration analysis be adapted to dependent samples (single long chain per iteration) using Markov chain concentration inequalities, thereby dispensing with M independent chains?
4. Beyond Ising/Dobrushin, what other families admit practical projection onto a mixing-certified Θ (e.g., Potts, higher-order factors)? Any guidance on projecting under Swendsen–Wang-type guarantees?
5. Statistical vs optimization error: Can you comment on sample complexity (in D) for generalization, i.e., how the constrained MLE compares statistically to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood when the model is misspecified?

Minor comments
- Clarify the overloaded notation R2; define R := supx ||t(x)||2 and use L ≤ R^2 + λ to avoid confusion with squares of R2.
- The example uses L = 10 in practice versus the bound 97; note that using backtracking may remove the need to preset L.
- The term “arbitrary treewidth” in the title is potentially misleading; the tractability comes from fast mixing, not from handling large treewidth per se.

Pros
- Clean, general theoretical framework with explicit complexity.
- Connects fast-mixing constraints, projection, and MCMC-based gradient descent.
- Solid convex/strongly convex analyses with interpretable schedules.

Cons
- Strong assumptions on known mixing constants and independence of samples.
- Some technical details (Lipschitz constant) could be tightened.
- Practical impact unclear due to limited experiments; FPRAS terminology slightly overstated.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a clear and useful theoretical contribution tying together fast mixing, projection, and inexact gradient descent to obtain polynomial-time ML within a constrained class. With improved clarity on Theorem 1, a more precise statement about the “FPRAS,” and either an adaptive strategy for unknown mixing constants or a brief experiment beyond the toy example, the paper would be a solid addition.
