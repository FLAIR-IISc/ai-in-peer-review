Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting to low-treewidth structures (e.g., Chow–Liu), the authors restrict parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ, i.e., a set for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically for all θ ∈ Θ. Given the ability to project onto Θ and bounded sufficient statistics, they analyze projected gradient descent where the gradient is approximated by MCMC sampling. They provide finite-time high-probability convergence bounds: in the convex (possibly unregularized) case, an additive ϵf-optimality in objective with total transitions scaling as O(ϵ−3 log(1/ϵ)) (ignoring problem-dependent constants); in the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case, a parameter error ϵθ with O(ϵ−2 log(1/ϵ)) total transitions. They also give step-size and schedule choices, and derive lower bounds showing their schedules are near-optimal up to constants and logs. A toy Ising example illustrates the schedules.

This work connects to: (i) MLE with intractable partition functions (Jerrum–Sinclair; MCMC-MLE and SA: Geyer, Gu & Zhu), (ii) surrogate estimators (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood) which trade statistical efficiency for tractability, and (iii) fast-mixing parameter sets and projection for Ising/MRFs (Hayes; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke). It is also related to Steinhardt & Liang’s “learning fast-mixing models” with restart-augmented chains; here the analysis covers general exponential families and standard Gibbs-like chains without enforced restarts.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The analysis builds on standard smoothness/convexity arguments and known results on inexact (projected) gradient descent (Schmidt et al.). The bias/variance of stochastic gradients is controlled via concentration (Hoeffding/Efron–Stein/Bernstein) and mixing-time bounds translate chain length v to bias αv. The decomposition of error into Monte Carlo variance and mixing bias is appropriate, and the use of union bounds in the strongly convex case is standard.
- Assumptions: The key assumptions are (a) bounded sufficient statistics, (b) a convex Θ with geometric mixing rate parameters (C, α) for the chosen chain, and (c) an efficiently computable Euclidean projection onto Θ. These are reasonable for certain models (e.g., Ising with spectral/∞-norm interaction constraints) but can be nontrivial or conservative in general.
- Proof completeness: The main text provides sketches, referring to an appendix for details. One important step in Theorem 1 is the claimed Lipschitzness of the gradient via a bound of TV distance by parameter distance using bounded statistics; this deserves a complete proof (or pointer to a standard lemma), as it underpins the smoothness constant. Similarly, the exact step-size/algorithm specification (Algorithm 1) should be made explicit.
- Empirics: The work is primarily theoretical; the toy experiment is minimal, which is acceptable but more empirical illustration (e.g., Potts models, different Θ and chains) would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
The narrative is generally clear, with a concise setup and well-motivated assumptions. The organization separating convex vs. strongly convex cases and providing interpretable schedules is helpful. Some notation is potentially confusing: R2 is used to denote a bound on ∥t(x)∥2 (and appears squared in several places); consider standardizing to a single symbol (e.g., R) to avoid ambiguity. A number of references lack years or complete bibliographic details. Please include Algorithm 1 explicitly with step sizes, projection, and sampling details. The definition of “FPRAS” for this optimization setting should be clarified (additive vs multiplicative accuracy).

Originality
The paper offers a novel synthesis: turning fast-mixing parameter constraints into an end-to-end, finite-time MLE learning guarantee via biased stochastic gradients with explicit schedules and near-matching lower bounds. Prior work either addressed sampling/counting (Jerrum–Sinclair), surrogate estimators, or learning variants with different chains (e.g., restarts). To my knowledge, there is no prior finite-time “(F)PTAS-style” result for MLE under general exponential families using MCMC-approximated gradients within fast-mixing sets. The lower-bound/upper-bound pairing is a nice touch.

Significance
The contribution is conceptually significant: it formalizes a common practical approach (MCMC-based gradient updates) and shows that, under verifiable fast-mixing constraints and projections, MLE with arbitrary graph structure is computationally tractable with polynomial complexity. This provides a theoretical alternative to low-treewidth restrictions and to surrogate likelihoods, with the additional benefit that the learned model remains amenable to fast MCMC inference. Practically, the usefulness hinges on having non-vacuous Θ and efficient projections; for Ising-like models these exist, but constants can be conservative. Nevertheless, this should stimulate further work on designing richer fast-mixing sets and better projections.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled finite-time analysis of MCMC-based MLE within a broad exponential-family framework.
- Bridges theory and practice by giving concrete schedules and near-matching lower bounds.
- Complements and extends prior work on fast mixing and learning with approximate gradients.
- Potential impact on how we restrict models for both learnability and inferability.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical impact limited by conservativeness/availability of Θ, unknown/large mixing constants, and cost of projection in general models.
- Some technical steps deferred; key Lipschitz-TV bound deserves fuller treatment in the main text or appendix.
- Minimal empirical validation; only a small Ising example.
- Use of “FPRAS” terminology for optimization could be clarified and aligned with standard definitions.

Questions and suggestions
- Precisely define the approximation notion (“FPRAS”) for minimizing f: is the guarantee additive in f or multiplicative? If additive, consider using “(randomized) PTAS” terminology.
- Provide complete proof (or citation) of the bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ c∥θ − φ∥2 under bounded statistics, and derive the best constant c.
- State Algorithm 1 in full, including step size (1/L?), projection operator, sampling protocol, and independence assumptions across iterations/samples.
- Discuss the complexity and availability of projections beyond pairwise Ising (e.g., higher-order cliques, non-binary variables), and whether approximate projections would preserve guarantees.
- Comment on estimating or adapting to unknown C and α; can one use doubling/truncation schedules that do not require them?
- Explore whether the analysis can be extended to persistent chains or sample “pools,” which are common in practice.
- Add an additional experiment (e.g., Potts on grids, fully connected with spectral constraints) to illustrate constants and sensitivity to Θ.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a clean, well-motivated theoretical contribution with potential to influence subsequent work, despite limitations in practicality and empirical support.
