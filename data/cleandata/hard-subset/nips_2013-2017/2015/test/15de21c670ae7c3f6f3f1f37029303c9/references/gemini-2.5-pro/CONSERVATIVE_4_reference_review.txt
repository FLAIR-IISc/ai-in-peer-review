**Summary:**
This paper addresses the well-known problem of intractable maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in undirected graphical models. The standard approach to tractability is to enforce structural constraints, such as low treewidth, which allows for exact inference. This paper proposes an alternative notion of tractability based on fast-mixing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. The core idea is to constrain the model parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` where any parameter in the set is guaranteed to correspond to a distribution for which a given MCMC sampler mixes rapidly. The authors then analyze the convergence of a common heuristic for MLE: projected gradient descent where the gradient is approximated using samples from an MCMC chain. The main contribution is a formal proof that this procedure constitutes a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood parameters within the constrained set `Θ`. The analysis provides explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and MCMC chain length required to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability, covering both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (L2-regularized) objectives.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Methodological Rigor:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. It takes a widely used but largely heuristic procedure (MCMC-based gradient descent for MLE) and places it on a solid theoretical footing. The analysis carefully decomposes the error in the gradient estimate into a bias term (from finite-length MCMC chains) and a variance term (from finite samples) and bounds their combined effect on the optimization process. This is a valuable theoretical contribution.
2.  **Novel Perspective on Tractability:** The paper introduces a compelling alternative to the standard structure-based view of tractability in graphical models. Framing tractability in terms of fast mixing is an interesting and potentially useful concept, connecting the fields of optimization, statistical learning, and the analysis of Markov chains.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem setup, assumptions, and main results are presented precisely. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem and situating their contribution within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation and Comparison to Baselines:** This is a significant weakness of the paper. While the theoretical contribution is clear, its practical implications are entirely unsubstantiated. The authors provide a single toy example on a 4x4 Ising grid, which they correctly characterize as a "visualization" rather than a meaningful experiment. For a method that proposes an alternative to decades of work on approximate MLE, the absence of any comparison to established baselines is a critical omission. It is impossible to assess the quality of the solutions found by this constrained optimization method relative to standard, efficient alternatives like pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, or even the Chow-Liu algorithm on a spanning tree approximation of the graph. Such a comparison would be essential to understand the trade-offs between the proposed method's theoretical guarantees and the practical performance of established estimators.
2.  **Restrictive Assumptions and Practicality:** The entire framework hinges on the ability to identify a convex set `Θ` of fast-mixing parameters and an efficient algorithm to project onto this set. While the paper provides examples for Ising models based on bounding interaction strengths (e.g., via spectral norms), it is not clear how general this assumption is. For more complex, real-world graphical models, defining such a set and its corresponding projector may be a highly non-trivial problem in itself. This potentially limits the applicability of the framework to a narrow class of models, ironically making it as restrictive as the structural assumptions it seeks to replace.
3.  **Conservative Nature of Theoretical Bounds:** As is common with such theoretical analyses, the derived schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and MCMC steps are likely to be extremely conservative. The authors' own example hints at this, where they use a practical Lipschitz constant an order of magnitude smaller than the theoretical one. While the existence of these polynomial-time bounds is the main result, their looseness makes them more of a theoretical curiosity than a practical guide for setting hyperparameters. This further underscores the need for empirical work to demonstrate that the method is viable with practical hyperparameter choices.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first formal proof of convergence for the widely used MCMC-MLE heuristic, under the specified constraints.
*   The work is technically sound and offers a novel theoretical perspective on tractability for learning graphical models.
*   It rigorously connects concepts from optimization theory (inexact gradient descent) with MCMC mixing time analysis.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper makes strong theoretical claims with virtually no empirical support. The lack of comparison to any established baseline methods is a major flaw.
*   The central assumption of a known, convex, fast-mixing set `Θ` with an efficient projector may not hold for many models of interest, raising questions about the method's significance and applicability.
*   The paper itself states it makes "no significant practical contribution," which raises the question of its suitability for a conference like NIPS, where impact and applicability are important criteria. The work feels more like a foundational theoretical result that is not yet developed enough for the machine learning community.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a technically sound and elegant theoretical analysis of a popular heuristic for learning intractable graphical models. The framing of tractability in terms of MCMC mixing speed is a novel and interesting contribution. However, the work remains purely theoretical. It is presented without any meaningful empirical validation or comparison against the very methods it critiques in the introduction. As a reviewer who values established methods and rigorous validation, I find the absence of such experiments to be a critical failing. While the theory is a contribution, its significance is difficult to gauge without understanding the practical trade-offs involved. The strong assumptions and lack of empirical grounding make me skeptical of the approach's utility beyond the specific, simple models discussed. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The paper would be much stronger if the authors provided a thorough empirical study comparing their constrained optimization approach against standard approximate learning baselines on a set of benchmark problems.
