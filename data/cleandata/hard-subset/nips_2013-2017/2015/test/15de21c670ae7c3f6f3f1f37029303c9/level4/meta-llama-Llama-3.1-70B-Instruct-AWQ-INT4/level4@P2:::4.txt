Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

This paper proposes an innovative approach to maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rates of their proposed algorithm and demonstrate its effectiveness in approximating the maximum likelihood solution.

Summary of Main Ideas:
The paper explores an alternative notion of a tractable set, namely a set of "fast-mixing parameters" where MCMC inference can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution. The authors prove that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, gradient descent with gradients approximated by sampling will approximate the maximum likelihood solution inside the set with high-probability. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the convergence rates of the proposed algorithm.

Relation to Previous Work:
The paper builds upon existing work on maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, but proposes a novel approach by restricting parameters to a fast-mixing set. The authors also draw connections to previous work on MCMC-based approximability of expectations and provide a more thorough analysis of the convergence rates of their proposed algorithm.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes an innovative approach to maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rates of their proposed algorithm.
* The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in approximating the maximum likelihood solution.

Weaknesses:

* The lower bound (Theorem 7) is very difficult to interpret and could benefit from an expanded discussion.
* The significance of the paper could be clarified with further discussion of the work's potential impact, including future extensions and opportunities to build on it.

Quality:
The results give rates of convergence of learning algorithms in terms of mixing times. The bounds appear meaningful and useful. However, the lower bound (Theorem 7) is very difficult to interpret and could benefit from an expanded discussion.

Clarity:
The presentation is clear, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rates of their proposed algorithm.

Originality:
Both the framing of the problem (in terms of the "fast mixing" model class) and the theoretical results appear original.

Significance:
The significance is unclear to the reviewer. However, the paper does seem to fill a gap in the literature on learning intractable models using MCMC. The submission introduces a novel class of tractable models based on good MCMC-based approximability of expectations. The theoretical results giving convergence rates for learning these models appear novel and interesting.

Recommendation:
The submission should be accepted, but the authors could benefit from addressing the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by providing an expanded discussion of the lower bound (Theorem 7) and clarifying the significance of the paper.
