This paper presents a novel approach to efficient maximum likelihood estimation in exponential families through computational methods. Generally, finding the maximum likelihood solution is intractable due to the need for integral calculations with respect to the proposed exponential family parameter. However, by assuming fast-mixing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for all allowed parameters, the authors demonstrate that the required integrals for proximal gradient descent can be calculated with sufficient precision. This, combined with the results of Schmidt et al. (2011), yields a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for calculating the Maximum Likelihood Estimator (MLE). The approach considers both convex and strongly convex cases, leading to distinct guarantees on likelihood error and parameter error, respectively. A simple experimental demonstration of the theory is also provided.

Overall, this is a high-quality piece of work that offers a well-motivated alternative to relying on small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analyses appear sound, although a detailed proof check is pending. The proofs primarily involve combining the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and the results of Schmidt et al. (2011), making the paper a non-trivial combination of existing techniques.

The authors' honest discussion of the approach's limitations is appreciated. The conclusion thoughtfully analyzes the weaknesses of the approach, the pessimism of theoretical assumptions, and potential directions for future work. Furthermore, the paper provides valuable insights into Contrastive Divergence (CD)-type algorithms, despite analyzing a distinct algorithm.

The introduction is well-written, with substantial references to previous work. However, there are some issues with the clarity of technical presentation. A notable omission is the explicit statement that the log-likelihood is convex, which is crucial to the paper's premise. Clarifying this point would help guide the reader through the paper's logic. A clear summary of the assumptions made in the paper, such as the convexity of the parameter set Theta, would also be beneficial.

In conclusion, this paper is an original and substantial contribution to the field. While the results may have limited practical value due to the challenges of proving fast mixing in MCMC, the ability to convert one hard problem into another is often useful. Thus, making the paper's results available to the community is valuable.
