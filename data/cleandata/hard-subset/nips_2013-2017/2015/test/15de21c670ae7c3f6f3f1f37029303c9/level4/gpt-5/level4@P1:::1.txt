Summary
The paper proposes constraining parameters of an exponential family to a “fast-mixing” set Θ, defined so that an associated MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast and uniformly over θ ∈ Θ. Given a projection onto Θ, the authors run projected gradient descent on the (possibly ridge-regularized) negative log-likelihood, approximating gradients via MCMC at each iteration starting from a fixed distribution r and running v transitions. They prove: (i) the gradient is Lipschitz with constant L = 4R2^2 + λ under bounded sufficient statistics; (ii) in the convex (unregularized) case, an excess objective bound that yields a total sampling effort KMv scaling as O(εf^−3 log(1/εf)) for εf-accurate suboptimality; and (iii) in the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case, a parameter error bound yielding total effort O(εθ^−2 log(1/εθ)). They interpret both as FPRAS-type guarantees. The paper discusses examples for Ising models, projection onto fast-mixing sets via norm constraints on interactions, and acknowledges practical limitations (projection cost, initialization, and “pool” sampling not covered by the analysis).

Relation to prior work
The proposed algorithm and analysis are closely related to long-standing work on stochastic approximation and Monte Carlo methods for maximum likelihood with intractable expectations, including Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics. There is a well-developed theory that treats gradient noise generated by Markov chains without requiring uniform geometric ergodicity, and without reinitializing chains from a fixed r at each iteration. Warm-starting from previous states (as the authors note in the Discussion) has been extensively analyzed and often performs better in practice. Foundational results such as those in Benveniste, Metivier, and Priouret treat much more general settings and can yield convergence under weaker assumptions than those adopted here.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing notion of tractability via projection onto fast-mixing parameter sets, with concrete examples (Ising) and projection strategies.
- Clean convergence statements with explicit dependencies on K, M, v and an interpretable complexity schedule in both convex and strongly convex regimes.
- A unified exponential-family framing that abstracts beyond specific graphical models.

Weaknesses
- Literature coverage is incomplete. The connection to Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with controlled Markov chains is not adequately discussed, and several of the paper’s guarantees follow from more general existing theory (e.g., results in Benveniste, Metivier, and Priouret).
- The analysis imposes stronger assumptions than necessary: uniform geometric mixing over Θ, reinitialization from a fixed r at each iteration, and boundedness conditions that could be relaxed in standard SA frameworks. Warm-started (“persistent”) chains are both natural and well-studied but excluded by the current proof technique.
- Presentation issues: key theorems (e.g., Theorems 1 and 2) do not state their assumptions self-containedly; readers should not have to infer them from surrounding text.
- The novelty appears marginal: the main contribution is packaging known SA ideas with fast-mixing parameter projections and giving explicit ε-based effort bounds; similar convergence (often under weaker conditions) is available in the SA/MCEM literature.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The results are plausible and consistent with known SA analyses, but they rely on strong uniform mixing assumptions and an artificial reinitialization that is not needed in general and may be suboptimal in practice.
- Proof completeness: The paper provides sketches with details deferred. Several assumptions and constants are not stated crisply within theorem statements, which hampers verification.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but the presentation of assumptions and algorithmic details needs tightening. Theorems should list all conditions explicitly (bounded statistics, projection feasibility, uniform mixing constants C, α, initialization/reset policy, step sizes).
- The discussion could more clearly separate what is standard (stochastic approximation with Markovian noise) from what is specific here (projection onto fast-mixing sets and resulting explicit complexity schedules).

Originality
- The idea of constraining to fast-mixing parameter sets and projecting is an interesting angle. However, the core learning algorithm—projected gradient with MCMC-approximated gradients—and its convergence analysis are closely aligned with established SA/MCEM frameworks. The theoretical contribution appears incremental relative to existing general results.

Significance
- Practical impact may be limited by the need for uniform mixing guarantees over Θ and by the cost/availability of projection operators for realistic models. The explicit ε-based effort bounds are useful, but similar guarantees can be derived under broader conditions in the SA literature.
- The paper would be stronger if it positioned itself more clearly as either (a) a unifying SA view tailored to fast-mixing sets with novel projection algorithms, or (b) new mixing/projection results that materially expand Θ beyond known regimes.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the work within Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with controlled Markov chains; discuss when general SA results already imply your guarantees and what is genuinely new here. At minimum, compare your assumptions and conclusions to standard references (e.g., Benveniste, Metivier, and Priouret).
- Remove the unnecessary reinitialization requirement and analyze warm-started chains; cite and leverage prior analyses of persistent MCMC within SA.
- State all assumptions explicitly in theorem statements (bounded sufficient statistics, Lipschitz constants, projection properties, mixing conditions, initialization).
- Discuss to what extent uniform geometric ergodicity can be relaxed (e.g., drift/minorization, state-dependent kernels) and how this affects your schedules and guarantees.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, explicit convergence rates tied to fast-mixing parameter projections in exponential families.
- Potentially useful perspective for practitioners who can enforce/provide projections onto fast-mixing Θ.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient engagement with extensive prior work on SA/MCEM with Markovian noise; several results are not novel and are proved under stronger assumptions than necessary.
- Presentation needs improvement; theorem assumptions are not self-contained.
- Practical value constrained by strong uniform mixing assumptions and reinitialization design choice.
