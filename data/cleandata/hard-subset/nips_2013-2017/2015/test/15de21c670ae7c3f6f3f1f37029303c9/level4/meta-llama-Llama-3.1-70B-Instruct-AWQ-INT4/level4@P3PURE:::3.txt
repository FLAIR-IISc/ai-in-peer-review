A key problem in Markov networks is weight learning, which typically involves finding a setting of weights attached to the features such that the likelihood is maximized. To achieve this, a gradient descent algorithm is often employed to learn the weights, and a crucial sub-task in this algorithm is computing the gradient. However, since computing the gradient is NP-hard in general, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms are frequently used as a substitute for exact algorithms to compute it.

The paper presents theoretical results demonstrating that if the weights (parameters) are such that MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution on them, then the weight learning algorithm that utilizes MCMC to compute the gradient is a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The authors consider two cases: the unregularized case (convex case) and the ridge-regularized case (non-convex case), deriving separate bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, with a few typos scattered throughout. Although I was unable to verify all the proofs, they appear plausible.

While the theoretical results are impressive, they have limited practical relevance, a point the authors acknowledge. I believe it is virtually impossible to ensure that the parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems. It would be beneficial for the authors to consider whether it is possible to enforce this condition artificially, as the condition can be seen as a form of regularization. Furthermore, using ridge regression in addition to this condition may result in over-regularized models. If this issue can be addressed, combined with the theoretical results, the paper would be significantly strengthened.

In summary, the paper provides a formalization of the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, these conditions have limited practical relevance. Addressing non-trivial research problems is necessary before the results derived in the paper can yield a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning.
