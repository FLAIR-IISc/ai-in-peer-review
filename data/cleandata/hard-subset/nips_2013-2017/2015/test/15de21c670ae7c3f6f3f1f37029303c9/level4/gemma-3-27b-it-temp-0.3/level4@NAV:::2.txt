## Review:

This paper introduces a novel and computationally efficient method for maximum likelihood estimation within exponential families. Obtaining the maximum likelihood solution is generally intractable; a key obstacle in convex optimization formulations is the need to compute an integral with respect to the exponential family parameter. The authors circumvent this challenge by leveraging the assumption of fast-mixing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for all permissible parameters. This allows for sufficiently precise integral approximation within a proximal gradient descent framework, ultimately yielding a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for the MLE, building upon the work of Schmidt et al. (2011). The analysis encompasses both convex and strongly convex scenarios, resulting in distinct performance guarantees – likelihood error bounds for the former and parameter error bounds for the latter. A basic experimental validation of the theory is also presented.

This is a high-quality contribution. The proposed approach is well-justified as an alternative to methods reliant on small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, although a detailed verification of the proofs was beyond the scope of this review. The proofs primarily involve combining the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and the results from Schmidt et al., representing a nontrivial combination of existing techniques. 

I particularly appreciated the candid discussion of the method’s limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully analyzes the approach’s weaknesses, potential for overly conservative assumptions, and promising avenues for future research. Furthermore, the paper provides valuable insights into algorithms related to Contrastive Divergence, even though the specific algorithm analyzed differs.

The introduction is well-structured and thoroughly references relevant prior work. However, the technical presentation could benefit from some minor improvements in clarity. Specifically, explicitly stating the convexity of the log-likelihood function would strengthen the paper’s logical flow. Given that the entire work rests on the convexity of f(theta), acknowledging that the second and third terms in equation 1 are convex, and that the convexity of A(theta) follows from the positive semi-definiteness of its Hessian (representing the covariance of the sufficient statistics), would be beneficial. A concise summary of the paper’s core assumptions would also be helpful; for instance, the convexity requirement for the parameter set Theta is not explicitly stated in the main text.

Overall, I consider this paper to be an original and substantial contribution to the field. While the practical impact of the results may be limited by the inherent difficulty of proving MCMC fast-mixing, transforming one challenging problem (MLE computation) into another (fast-mixing Markov chain design) is a valuable outcome. Making these results available to the research community is therefore worthwhile.
