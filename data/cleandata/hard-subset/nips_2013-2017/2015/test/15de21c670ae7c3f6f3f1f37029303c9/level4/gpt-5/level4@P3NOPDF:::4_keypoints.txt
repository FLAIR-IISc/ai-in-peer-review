- Paper establishes meaningful convergence rates for learning algorithms in terms of MCMC mixing times, with both upper and lower bounds.  
- Inclusion of both upper and lower bounds is valuable for guiding algorithm design (e.g., MCMC steps, warm starts).  
- Main concern: Theorem 7 (lower bound) is hard to interpret—unclear regimes where it binds and how it scales with key parameters.  
- Request: provide an informal restatement of Theorem 7 highlighting core message, assumptions, and scaling.  
- Request: include a simple illustrative example (e.g., small Ising model) showing the lower bound numerically.  
- Request: discuss tightness—where the lower bound matches upper bounds and where gaps remain.  
- Clarify which notion of mixing time is used (total variation, spectral gap, etc.) and how constants depend on it.  
- State whether the lower bound assumes worst-case initialization or allows warm starts, and how parameter updates interact with initialization.  
- Suggest summarizing main dependencies of the upper bounds (dimension, spectral gap/mixing time, step size, sample size, MCMC steps, burn-in) in a corollary/table.  
- Presentation is generally clear and proofs readable, but add a concise early definition of the “fast-mixing” model class with canonical examples/non-examples.  
- Recommend a running example revisited throughout the main results and short proof sketches/intuitions for major theorems, especially the lower bound.  
- Ask for an explicit list of assumptions (ergodicity, reversibility, warm starts, boundedness/Lipschitz) before main results.  
- Suggest a brief discussion on practical estimation/bounding of mixing times (Dobrushin, coupling, spectral gap) and verifiable sufficient conditions.  
- Framing of tractable model class via fast MCMC approximability is original and complements existing approaches like pseudolikelihood and contrastive divergence.  
- Work fills a gap by linking learning convergence to mixing properties and can impact undirected graphical models, energy-based models, and structured prediction.  
- To strengthen significance, offer guidance for practitioners on verifying fast-mixing, implications for algorithm design (warm vs cold starts, persistent chains), and diagnostics for mixing.  
- Recommend adding a small empirical/synthetic illustration to demonstrate how mixing time affects optimization error.  
- Position results relative to alternatives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, CD) and outline extensions (non-reversible samplers, tempering, Langevin/SG-MCMC, high-dimensional dependence).  
- Specific revision checklist: improve Theorem 7 (informal, intuition, example, comparison to known bounds), summarize upper-bound dependencies, clarify uniformity of mixing times across parameter iterates, and discuss sensitivity to misspecified mixing estimates.  
- Minor issue: streamline notation and collect recurrent constants to improve readability.  
- Overall assessment: well-executed, original theoretical contribution linking learning convergence to MCMC mixing; recommend acceptance pending clearer lower-bound interpretation and expanded practical discussion.
