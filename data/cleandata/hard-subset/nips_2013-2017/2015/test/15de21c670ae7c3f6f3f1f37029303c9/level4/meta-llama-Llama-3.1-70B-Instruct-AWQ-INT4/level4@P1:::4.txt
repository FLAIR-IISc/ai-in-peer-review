Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets

This paper explores an alternative notion of tractable sets for maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models, specifically a set of "fast-mixing parameters" where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution. The authors prove that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, gradient descent with gradients approximated by sampling will approximate the maximum likelihood solution inside the set with high probability.

The paper's main contribution is establishing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the convergence rates of their proposed algorithm, including a lower bound on the total number of Markov transitions required to achieve a certain level of accuracy.

The results are significant, as they fill a gap in the literature on learning intractable models using MCMC. The paper introduces a novel class of tractable models based on good MCMC-based approximability of expectations, and the theoretical results giving convergence rates for learning these models appear novel and interesting.

The presentation is clear, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of their results and their implications. However, the lower bound (Theorem 7) is somewhat difficult to interpret, and an expanded discussion of this theorem and the lower bounds applicable to the problem would be desirable.

To improve the submission, the authors could further discuss the work's potential impact, including future extensions and opportunities to build on it. Additionally, the authors could provide more context on how their results relate to existing work in the field and how they can be applied in practice.

Overall, the submission is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field. With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, it would be suitable for publication.
