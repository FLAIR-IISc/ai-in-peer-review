- Paper studies MLE for exponential families when exact inference is intractable by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ and analyzing projected gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients.  
- Shows FPRAS-style guarantees: unregularized case needs sampling effort ~1/ε^3, ridge-regularized case ~1/ε^2, with explicit schedules and near-matching lower bounds.  
- Novel reframing: tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets rather than graph structure or surrogate losses.  
- General results applying to broad exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.  
- Solid technical contributions: convergence guarantees for convex and strongly-convex cases, explicit schedules, and careful use of concentration/inexact-gradient tools.  
- Good literature contextualization, connecting to prior work on mixing, projection to fast-mixing sets, and contrastive divergence variants.  
- Practical relevance is limited: verifying membership in Θ and obtaining mixing constants (C, α) is difficult for realistic models.  
- Projection onto Θ may be computationally expensive and could cause over-regularization or bias.  
- Strong assumptions (bounded statistics, known projection oracle) exclude many useful models (e.g., hidden-variable models).  
- Analysis assumes independent fresh chains per iteration and does not capture persistent-chain or sample-pool methods used in practice.  
- Empirical evaluation is minimal: only a small illustrative example, no systematic experiments or comparisons to pseudolikelihood/score matching.  
- Limited guidance on choosing MCMC kernels, estimating mixing rates, and hidden large constants in complexity bounds.  
- Technical development appears careful and plausible; assumptions and limitations are clearly stated.  
- Paper is generally well written; suggest clearer wording on convexity, more concrete projection algorithms, and how C and α are estimated.  
- Originality is moderately high: novel FPRAS-style guarantees for MLE with MCMC gradients over general exponential families.  
- Significance is primarily theoretical; practical impact is limited until projection/estimation of Θ is better addressed.  
- Suggestions: provide practical constructions and projection methods for Θ beyond Ising, explore adaptive/data-driven constraints, estimate mixing rates online, analyze persistent-chain variants, and add empirical comparisons.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear theoretical framework, explicit convergence rates and schedules, broad applicability, and careful discussion.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical applicability, mismatch with common algorithms, minimal empirical validation, and unresolved over-regularization concerns.  
- Overall: technically solid and conceptually interesting theoretical work, but practical relevance is constrained by strong assumptions and challenges in enforcing/estimating fast-mixing constraints.
