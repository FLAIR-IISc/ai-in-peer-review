Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies maximum likelihood learning for exponential families (including undirected graphical models) by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ: a convex set for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically with known constants (C, α). Within this set, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where each gradient is approximated via MCMC sampling, and prove high-probability convergence to the constrained MLE. They establish Lipschitz continuity of the (regularized) likelihood gradient, give explicit schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and chain steps per iteration, and provide lower bounds showing near-optimality up to log factors. For unregularized problems, ε-accuracy in objective value can be achieved with total sampling effort on the order of (1/ε)3 (ignoring logs); with ridge regularization (strong convexity), ε-accuracy in parameter distance is achievable with effort O((1/ε)2) up to logs, yielding FPRAS-type guarantees. The framework applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics and to several fast-mixing constructions (e.g., Ising models with bounded interactions and spectral-norm constraints), with projection onto Θ accomplished by simple clipping or convex optimization (as in Domke & Liu).

This work complements long-standing alternatives that avoid intractable inference: pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihoods (Besag; Hyvärinen; Varin et al.), as well as structure-restricted MLE such as Chow–Liu trees and high-girth/correlation-decay models (Heinemann & Globerson). It also connects to practical MCMC-based learning and contrastive divergence (Geyer; Tieleman; Hinton) by providing theoretical guarantees when mixing can be controlled, and relates to recent fast-mixing modeling ideas (Steinhardt & Liang). The results build on known mixing-time bounds for Gibbs/Swendsen–Wang on Ising/Potts systems and on convex optimization with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al.).

Strengths:
- Clear, general framework: casting tractability in terms of fast-mixing parameter sets rather than graph structure.
- Solid theoretical guarantees: high-probability convergence with explicit complexity in K (iterations), M (samples), and v (chain steps); both convex and strongly convex cases treated.
- Near-optimal schedules: upper bounds accompanied by interpretable lower bounds showing the dependence on α, C, and ε is essentially tight up to log factors.
- Broad applicability: results stated for general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not only specific graphical models.
- Concrete connections to known fast-mixing regimes and practical projection procedures.

Weaknesses:
- Practical scope limited by assumptions: the analysis requires known mixing-rate constants (C, α) for Θ and the ability to project efficiently onto Θ, which may be nontrivial to verify or compute in larger models.
- Experimental validation is minimal: a single small Ising example with conservative constants; limited insight into the tightness of bounds or performance in more realistic settings.
- Theorem 7 (lower bound) is hard to interpret: the statement and implications would benefit from more intuition, special-case discussion, and a clearer comparison to the corresponding upper bounds.
- Independence of samples across iterations: the theory does not cover the widely used “persistent” sampling/pool approach, reducing alignment with common practice.
- Treatment of computational costs: projection cost is excluded from the complexity accounting; guidance on when it is negligible versus dominant would be helpful.

Quality:
The technical development appears careful and sound. The Lipschitz gradient bound, error decomposition for biased/noisy MCMC gradients, and the use of concentration plus inexact gradient descent theory are appropriate. The schedules and lower bounds are thoughtfully derived. The main caveat is the reliance on known (C, α) and on bounded sufficient statistics; clarifying how these constants are estimated in practice would strengthen the claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The high-level algorithmic picture and the convex vs. strongly convex analyses are easy to follow. However, Theorem 7 and its parameters are difficult to parse; offering intuition (e.g., via a simplified corollary or a worked numerical example) would improve readability. It would also help to centralize assumptions (bounded t(x), projection oracle, known mixing bounds) and to summarize known Θ constructions for common models in one place.

Originality:
The notion of constraining to fast-mixing parameter sets to obtain provable, MCMC-based MLE learning guarantees is novel, as is the combination of upper/lower bounds and explicit schedules in this context. The generality beyond graphical models to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics is also original.

Significance:
The work fills a theoretical gap by providing convergence guarantees for a widely used practice (MCMC-based gradient approximations) under verifiable mixing assumptions. Its impact is likely strongest on the theory side and for model classes where fast-mixing sets and projections are tractable (e.g., bounded-interaction Ising models). Practical impact may be tempered by the difficulty of certifying mixing rates and the limited coverage of persistent-chain training. Nonetheless, the results provide a principled baseline and a direction for designing learnable, inference-tractable model classes.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Expand the discussion of Theorem 7: add intuition, special cases (e.g., fixed δ, simplified dependencies), and a side-by-side comparison with the upper bounds.
- Provide guidance on estimating or bounding (C, α) and R2 in practice, and on how sensitive the schedules are to misspecification.
- Clarify the computational cost of projection for different Θ (e.g., clipping vs. spectral-norm projection), and when it may dominate sampling.
- Discuss extensions to persistent sampling schemes and whether analogous guarantees might be obtained.
- Include additional experiments illustrating scaling, robustness to constants, and comparisons with pseudolikelihood/score matching.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually novel tractability notion with clear theoretical guarantees and near-optimal complexity.
- General framework applicable to broad exponential-family settings.
- Fills a meaningful gap in the literature on MCMC-based learning.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical applicability constrained by the need to certify mixing and to project efficiently.
- Limited empirical evidence and somewhat opaque lower-bound presentation.

Overall recommendation:
Accept. The paper offers a well-executed and original theoretical contribution that clarifies when MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning is provably efficient under fast-mixing constraints, and it is likely to stimulate further work on tractable, learnable model classes and practical certification of mixing.
