A key problem in Markov networks is weight learning, which is often defined as finding a weight setting—for the features—that maximizes the likelihood. Typically, a gradient descent algorithm is used to learn these weights, and a key sub-task within this algorithm is computing the gradient. Since computing this gradient is NP-hard in general, MCMC algorithms are often employed as an alternative to exact methods.

This paper derives theoretical results demonstrating that if the weights (parameters) are such that MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution, then the weight learning algorithm—which utilizes MCMC for gradient computation—is a FPRAS. The paper considers both an unregularized (convex) case and a ridge-regularized (non-convex) case, deriving separate bounds for each. 

The paper is generally well-written, with a few minor typos. While I was unable to verify all the proofs in detail, they appear plausible.

Although the theoretical results are impressive, their practical relevance is limited (a point the authors acknowledge). It seems virtually impossible to guarantee that the parameters satisfy the conditions required by the theorems. Have the authors considered this limitation? Specifically, is it possible to *enforce* this condition artificially—perhaps as a form of regularization? Adding ridge regression *in addition* to this condition may lead to over-regularized models, however. Addressing this issue, and potentially offering a method to satisfy the necessary conditions, would significantly strengthen the paper and its potential impact. 

Overall, this is a reasonably well-written paper that formally characterizes the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, the practical applicability of this condition is currently low. Further, non-trivial research challenges must be addressed before the results presented can lead to a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning.
