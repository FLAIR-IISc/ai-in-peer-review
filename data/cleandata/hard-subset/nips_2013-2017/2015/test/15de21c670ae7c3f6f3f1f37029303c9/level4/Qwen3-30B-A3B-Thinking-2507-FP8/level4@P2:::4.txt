

This paper introduces a novel framework for maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by considering "fast-mixing parameter sets" where MCMC inference converges rapidly. The authors prove that when parameters are constrained to such sets, gradient descent using MCMC approximated gradients can approximate the maximum likelihood solution with high probability. They establish convergence rates for both unregularized (cubic in 1/ε) and ridge-regularized (quadratic in 1/ε) cases, providing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme. The results extend beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, making the approach broadly applicable.

Quality: The theoretical results are technically sound and well-supported. The paper provides rigorous convergence guarantees that address a significant gap in the literature regarding MCMC-based learning intractable models. The proofs are comprehensive, with detailed proof sketches for key results. The main concern is that Theorem 7 (the lower bound) is difficult to interpret and lacks sufficient discussion of its implications. While the authors establish theoretical lower bounds, they would benefit from expanding this discussion to clarify how these bounds relate to practical implementation and how they compare to other theoretical limits in the field. This would strengthen the paper's contribution by providing clearer context for the optimality of their proposed approach.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors effectively introduce key concepts and build the necessary theoretical foundation before presenting their main results. The notation is consistently used, and the presentation of theorems and proof sketches is accessible. The example in Section 6 demonstrates the practical applicability of their theoretical results, further enhancing clarity. The only minor suggestion would be to explicitly state the relationship between the convex and strongly convex convergence results earlier in the paper to help readers better understand the connection between these two important cases.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by reframing tractability through the lens of fast-mixing MCMC rather than treewidth or other structural constraints. This perspective is both novel and theoretically rich. The authors establish that the "fast-mixing parameter set" concept provides a meaningful alternative to traditional tractability notions, and they successfully prove convergence guarantees for learning within this set. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work on tree-structured models (Chow-Liu), pseudolikelihood, and score matching by demonstrating how their approach can handle mis-specified models while maintaining computational tractability. The authors also appropriately reference recent work on fast-mixing models (e.g., Steinhardt and Liang [28]) while clearly establishing their novel contributions.

Significance: The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by providing theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based learning in high-treewidth models, which is a common practice in machine learning but lacks theoretical foundations. The results have the potential to guide future work on designing learning algorithms that can leverage MCMC without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. While the paper itself is primarily theoretical, the framework it establishes could inform practical implementations in areas where MCMC is commonly used for inference. The significance would be further strengthened by a more detailed discussion of potential applications, including how the fast-mixing parameter set could be constructed for specific model classes beyond the Ising example provided. The authors should also elaborate on how their approach might be extended to handle hidden variables or other complexities common in real-world applications.

Strengths: 
- The paper establishes a meaningful theoretical framework for understanding when MCMC-based learning is tractable.
- The convergence guarantees are tight and meaningful for practical implementation.
- The results extend beyond graphical models to general exponential families.
- The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and well-supported.
- The paper clearly distinguishes its contributions from related work.

Weaknesses:
- The lower bound (Theorem 7) requires more detailed interpretation and discussion.
- The significance section would benefit from a more thorough discussion of potential applications and future extensions.
- The practical implementation details (beyond the Ising model example) could be expanded to better demonstrate the approach's applicability.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important problem in machine learning. The paper makes a clear, novel contribution to the understanding of when MCMC-based learning can be theoretically justified, and the results are technically sound. While the significance discussion could be strengthened, the paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and would be valuable to the NIPS community. The work provides a foundation for future research on learning intractable models with theoretical guarantees, and the framework introduced has the potential to influence both theoretical and practical approaches to model learning. Given the high quality of the theoretical results and the novelty of the approach, I recommend acceptance.
