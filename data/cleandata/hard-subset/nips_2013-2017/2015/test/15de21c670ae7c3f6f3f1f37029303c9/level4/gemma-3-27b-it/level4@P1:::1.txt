This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by exploring the use of “fast-mixing parameters,” where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference can converge quickly. The core idea is to demonstrate that, when parameters are constrained to such a set, gradient descent with gradients approximated by sampling can approximate the maximum likelihood solution with high probability, providing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). The authors establish both convex and strongly-convex convergence rates, with computational effort scaling as 1/ε³ and 1/ε², respectively, ignoring logarithmic factors.

The paper builds upon established difficulties in learning with intractable partition functions, referencing Jerrum and Sinclair [1993] and the #P-completeness of Ising model partition function evaluation. It contrasts its approach with existing alternatives like pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22], and composite likelihoods [20, 30], highlighting their inferior sample complexity or lack of guarantees when the model is misspecified. The work also positions itself relative to restricting parameters to tractable families like tree-structured distributions [1968, 2014], arguing for the benefits of the fast-mixing approach.  While inspired by graphical models, the results are presented within a broader exponential family framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** The paper proposes a distinct perspective on tractability, focusing on the convergence properties of MCMC rather than graph structure. This offers a potentially valuable alternative for learning in complex models.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The FPRAS results for both convex and strongly-convex cases are a significant contribution, providing theoretical backing for a commonly used practical approach (gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients).
*   **Generality:** The results extend beyond graphical models to apply to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.
*   **Clear Presentation of Results:** The core theorems (2, 5, 6 and 8) are clearly stated and the proof sketches provide intuition.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Awareness of Related Work:** The main concern is a lack of engagement with the extensive literature on stochastic approximation algorithms, particularly those employing Markovian dynamics for intractable likelihood optimization (e.g., Monte Carlo EM). The authors should thoroughly compare their work to existing results and acknowledge the contributions of related fields. A substantial body of theory already exists, covering more general scenarios than those considered here.
*   **Assumptions and Clarity:** The assumptions underlying theorems 1 and 2 could be stated more explicitly within the theorem statements themselves, rather than relying on references to surrounding text.
*   **Conservatism in Assumptions:** The strong assumptions required for Theorem 6 appear overly restrictive. The authors should acknowledge that more general results exist (e.g., Benveniste, Metivier and Priouret) and discuss how their work relates to those broader theoretical frameworks.
*   **Re-initialization Strategy:** While acknowledging that re-initializing the Markov chain simplifies the analysis, the authors dismiss the potential benefits of re-initializing with the previous sample, a strategy extensively studied in related work.
*   **Projection Complexity:** The analysis doesn’t fully account for the computational cost of projecting parameters onto the fast-mixing set, which could be substantial depending on the projection algorithm and model complexity.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to maximum likelihood learning in intractable models.
*   The FPRAS results offer valuable theoretical insights into the convergence properties of a widely used practical technique.
*   The generality of the results, extending beyond graphical models, is a positive feature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited awareness of related work significantly diminishes the contribution. The paper would benefit greatly from a more comprehensive literature review and comparison to existing stochastic approximation algorithms.
*   The restrictive assumptions and lack of discussion regarding more general theoretical frameworks weaken the impact of the results.
*   The dismissal of potentially beneficial strategies like re-initializing with the previous sample feels unjustified given existing literature.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea and valuable theoretical results. However, the lack of awareness regarding related work is a significant concern. Addressing this deficiency, by thoroughly comparing the authors' approach to existing stochastic approximation algorithms and acknowledging relevant theoretical frameworks, would substantially strengthen the paper. Without this, the perceived novelty is diminished and the contribution feels incremental. I recommend **rejection** in its current form, but encourage resubmission after a significant revision addressing the identified weaknesses.
