This paper explores a novel approach to learning in intractable graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, guaranteeing rapid convergence of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. This contrasts with traditional approaches that limit parameters to tree-structured distributions. The authors prove a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within this fast-mixing set, analyzing both regularized and unregularized cases and providing convergence rate guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients.

The core idea of framing tractability in terms of MCMC mixing times is both interesting and potentially impactful. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, etc.) and positions its contribution as a distinct alternative. The theoretical results, particularly the FPRAS guarantees, are well-defined and appear meaningful. The analysis is rigorous and the proofs, while detailed and relegated to the appendix, are sketched with sufficient clarity to understand the main arguments.

A significant strength lies in the generality of the framework, extending beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. The inclusion of both convex and strongly-convex analyses adds to the completeness of the work. The authors acknowledge and discuss the interplay between mixing times, sampling effort, and optimization accuracy. The example provided, though simplified, helps illustrate the practical implications of the theoretical results.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. The lower bound on computational effort (Theorem 7) is indeed difficult to interpret and its significance relative to the upper bounds is not clearly elucidated. Expanding the discussion of this theorem, and providing more intuition for its implications, would be valuable. While the authors mention potential applications, a more thorough discussion of the work’s impact, including potential extensions and directions for future research, would strengthen the paper. For example, the paper could discuss the implications of the fast-mixing condition for specific model classes and suggest strategies for identifying or constructing such sets. The authors also appropriately acknowledge that the convergence analysis might be conservative.

**Review Summary:**

**Quality:** Technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical results are rigorous and the paper demonstrates a complete approach to the problem. The authors are careful in evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of their work.
**Clarity:**  The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with sufficient information for an expert reader to understand the key concepts.
**Originality:** The framing of tractability using fast-mixing parameter sets is a novel contribution. The theoretical results appear original and represent a significant advance in the field.
**Significance:** While the immediate practical impact is not fully demonstrated, the paper fills an important gap in the literature on learning intractable models and offers a promising new direction for research. The theoretical results are interesting and potentially useful for practitioners.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to learning in intractable models.
*   Provides FPRAS guarantees for a new class of tractable models.
*   Extends beyond graphical models to a broader class of exponential families.
*   Rigorous analysis and clear presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lower bound on computational effort (Theorem 7) is difficult to interpret.
*   The discussion of potential impact and future work could be more extensive.
*   The paper lacks a thorough exploration of the practical challenges and opportunities associated with identifying fast-mixing parameter sets.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. Despite some minor weaknesses, the originality, technical soundness, and potential significance of the work outweigh its limitations. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the literature on learning in intractable models and opens up new avenues for research. I encourage the authors to address the concerns regarding the lower bound and further elaborate on the practical implications of their work in a future revision.
