

This paper proposes a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing sets" where MCMC inference converges rapidly. The authors demonstrate that gradient descent with MCMC approximations achieves a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for both convex and strongly convex cases. While the technical results are mathematically sound, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings in its literature engagement and presentation.

The core contribution appears to be the application of MCMC-based gradient descent to exponential families with fast-mixing constraints, but the authors are not sufficiently aware of the extensive literature on stochastic approximation methods for intractable likelihoods. Their approach is fundamentally related to the Monte Carlo EM algorithm and more generally to stochastic approximation algorithms with Markovian dynamics, which have been extensively studied for optimizing intractable likelihood functions. The authors should have explicitly compared their framework to these established approaches, particularly highlighting how their results differ from or improve upon existing theory.

A critical issue is the lack of proper contextualization within the literature. For instance, the authors' assumption that the Markov chain must be reinitialized at each iteration from a fixed distribution r is unnecessary given existing theory. As acknowledged in the paper, reinitializing from the previous sample (as in contrastive divergence) is often more efficient in practice. This technique has been thoroughly analyzed in the literature, including for MCMC-based learning algorithms, yet the authors provide no meaningful comparison to these established approaches. The claim that "reinitializing with the previous sample may be a better idea" is insufficient justification for omitting a comprehensive literature review.

The presentation of results also needs substantial improvement. For Theorems 1 and 2, the assumptions are not clearly stated or justified in the main text, forcing readers to search through the surrounding context to understand the conditions under which these results hold. A well-written technical paper should present assumptions clearly at the outset of each result. The authors' reliance on strong assumptions for Theorem 6 is particularly problematic, as more general results exist without these constraints. For instance, the work by Benveniste, Metivier, and Priouret provides a more comprehensive framework for stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics that does not require the specific mixing rate assumptions used here.

While the theoretical results themselves are technically valid, they do not represent a significant advance over existing theory. The authors' approach does not offer substantially new insights compared to the well-established literature on stochastic approximation methods for intractable likelihoods. The claim that their results "work entirely in the exponential family framework" is misleading, as this is standard for such analyses. The paper would benefit from a more precise articulation of how their specific contributions differ from the existing body of work.

The paper's technical contributions are marginal given the established literature. The authors have not demonstrated that their specific assumptions (such as the need to reinitialize Markov chains at each iteration) are necessary for their results, nor have they shown that their approach provides advantages over existing methods in practical settings. The example section is particularly unconvincing, as the authors acknowledge the paper "claims no significant practical contribution."

In summary, while the paper presents technically correct theoretical results, it fails to properly contextualize these results within the broader literature on stochastic approximation for intractable likelihoods. The authors' lack of engagement with the extensive existing work on Monte Carlo EM and related methods represents a significant weakness that undermines the paper's contribution. The results are not novel in the context of the field, and the presentation would benefit from clearer statement of assumptions and more precise comparison to established approaches.
