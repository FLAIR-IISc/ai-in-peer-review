### Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting the model structure (e.g., to a tree), the authors propose constraining the parameter vector θ to a "fast-mixing" convex set Θ, within which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to converge rapidly. The core contribution is a formal proof that projected gradient descent, using gradients approximated by MCMC samples, serves as a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The paper provides separate convergence analyses and complexity bounds for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, demonstrating that this widely used heuristic can achieve theoretical guarantees under the fast-mixing assumption.

### Review

This paper tackles the important problem of learning in graphical models where inference is computationally difficult. The core idea of defining a tractable parameter set based on MCMC mixing time rather than graph structure is an interesting and valuable perspective. The primary strength of the work is its rigorous theoretical analysis, providing explicit, non-asymptotic convergence rates for a popular MCMC-based learning algorithm. However, the paper's main weakness is its apparent lack of connection to the extensive and well-established literature on stochastic approximation algorithms, which significantly limits its novelty and impact.

**Quality**
The paper appears to be technically sound, and the proofs provided in the appendix follow a clear logical progression. The analysis of gradient descent with biased, noisy gradients is detailed, and the derivation of explicit schedules for the number of iterations (K), samples (M), and MCMC steps (v) is thorough.

However, the technical contributions are built upon a set of strong assumptions that are often unnecessary in the broader context of stochastic approximation. The requirement that the Markov chain be initialized from a fixed distribution `r` at each iteration is a significant simplification made for the analysis. While the authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, it is a critical point because this approach is computationally inefficient compared to the common practice of "warm-starting" the chain from its state in the previous iteration. More importantly, the analysis of warm-started chains has been a central topic in the stochastic approximation literature for decades. Similarly, the assumption of a uniform geometric mixing rate for all parameters in Θ is a very strong condition.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem setup and the proposed algorithm are explained clearly. The inclusion of a table of notation is helpful.

A point for improvement would be to make the formal statements of the main results more self-contained. For example, Theorems 1 and 2 do not explicitly state their assumptions. While these can be inferred from the preceding sections, stating them directly within the theorem environment would improve clarity and make the results easier to parse for the reader.

**Originality**
This is the most significant concern. The proposed algorithm is a specific instance of the general class of stochastic approximation algorithms with Markovian dynamics, which have been used to optimize intractable likelihoods since the 1980s. There is a rich theoretical literature on this topic that establishes convergence under far more general conditions than those presented here.

Specifically, the analysis of algorithms that use MCMC to approximate gradients is a classic topic. Foundational works, such as "Stochastic Approximation and Recursive Algorithms" by Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret (1990), provide powerful convergence results for such systems. This body of work typically does not require uniform ergodicity or the re-initialization of the chain at each step. While the present paper provides explicit finite-sample rates, which is a modern focus, it fails to position itself relative to this vast prior literature. The authors should compare their results to existing work and clarify what is gained by their stronger assumptions (e.g., non-asymptotic rates versus asymptotic convergence). Without this context, the results appear to be a re-derivation of a known result in a less general setting.

**Significance**
The idea of constraining parameters for fast mixing is a potentially significant contribution and has been explored by others (e.g., [28]). Providing a full theoretical analysis of gradient-based learning in this context is a worthwhile goal.

However, the significance is diminished by the originality concerns. By not connecting to the stochastic approximation literature, the paper misses an opportunity to build upon existing theory or to clearly delineate how its finite-sample analysis advances the field beyond the classic asymptotic results. The practical implications are also limited by the inefficient re-initialization scheme required by the proof, which deviates from how these methods are typically implemented.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
*   Addresses the important and challenging problem of learning in intractable models.
*   The concept of defining tractability via "fast-mixing" parameter sets is a compelling alternative to structural constraints.
*   Provides a rigorous, finite-sample theoretical analysis with explicit complexity bounds for an MCMC-based learning algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Major Weakness:** The paper shows limited awareness of the extensive literature on stochastic approximation and Monte Carlo EM algorithms. The algorithm and its analysis are not adequately contextualized within this established field.
*   The results appear to be a special case of more general, classic convergence theorems, making the novelty marginal.
*   The analysis relies on strong and impractical assumptions, notably the re-initialization of the MCMC chain at each iteration and uniform geometric mixing, which have been relaxed in prior work.

### Recommendation

While the paper is technically sound and addresses an interesting problem, the lack of engagement with relevant prior literature is a critical flaw. The contributions feel incremental and less general than what is already known in the stochastic approximation community. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly review this literature, re-frame their contributions in that context, and ideally, extend their analysis to more practical algorithm variants (e.g., with warm-starts). In its current form, I lean towards rejection.
