Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies maximum-likelihood estimation (MLE) in exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting to low-treewidth models (e.g., Chow–Liu trees), the authors propose constraining parameters to a set Θ of fast-mixing parameters, i.e., a region where a chosen MCMC kernel mixes rapidly to the model’s stationary distribution. Within this set, they analyze projected gradient descent where the model expectation is approximated via MCMC. They prove that, for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, one obtains high-probability approximation guarantees to the MLE restricted to Θ, with explicit complexity schedules in the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) settings. In the unregularized case they achieve ε-accuracy in objective value with total sampling work scaling as ~1/ε^3 (up to logs), and in the ridge-regularized case they obtain ε-accuracy in parameter distance with work ~1/ε^2 (up to logs), constituting a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). An illustrative Ising example is provided.

This work connects to: pseudolikelihood (Besag), score matching (Hyvärinen), and composite likelihood (Lindsay; Varin et al.) as alternative tractable estimators; learning under structural restrictions (Chow–Liu; high-girth models); projections to fast-mixing regions for MRFs (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke); mixing-time bounds and fast mixing for Ising/Potts models and various chains (Jerrum & Sinclair; Dyer et al.; Hayes; Huber); MCMC-ML (Geyer; Gu & Zhu); contrastive divergence and its variants (Tieleman; Carreira-Perpiñán & Hinton); and convergence of inexact/proximal gradient descent (Schmidt et al.). The paper’s novelty lies in leveraging fast-mixing parameter sets to provide end-to-end MLE approximation guarantees beyond trees, and doing so for general exponential families.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled alternative to low-treewidth assumptions: learning within certified fast-mixing regions.
- Generality: applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just specific graphical models.
- Rigorous convergence guarantees that account for biased, sampling-based gradients; clean complexity schedules (K, M, v) for both convex and strongly convex cases.
- Thoughtful discussion of limitations and connections to practice (e.g., relation to CD and initialization choices).
- Useful synthesis of tools from mixing-time theory, concentration inequalities, and inexact gradient methods.

Weaknesses:
- Practical impact is limited by the difficulty of certifying fast mixing and computing projections onto Θ for realistic models; obtaining constants (C, α) is nontrivial and often yields conservative bounds.
- The analysis requires independent fresh samples per iteration (no persistence), which differs from several effective practical heuristics (e.g., persistent CD, sample pools).
- Experimental validation is minimal; no empirical study of trade-offs between projection complexity, chain length, and sample size, nor comparison to baselines such as pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood.
- A few clarity omissions and minor notational issues (see suggestions below).

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The technical approach is sound and well tied to prior theory. The main proofs are combinations of standard tools (mixing assumptions, concentration inequalities, and Schmidt et al.’s bounds), but the combination is nontrivial and carefully executed. Assumptions (bounded sufficient statistics; existence of a convex Θ with known mixing-rate bounds and a computable projection; independence of samples across iterations) are clearly the main levers; stating all up front would help. I did not check every proof detail, but the arguments and dependencies appear consistent.
- Clarity: Generally well written and well referenced. However, it would help to explicitly state early that f(θ) is convex (and strongly convex when λ > 0), and that Θ is assumed convex. A concise list of assumptions (bounded statistics, availability of projection, chain and mixing-rate form, independence of samples) up front would improve readability. Some notation (e.g., R2 vs R2^2) could be standardized, and the roles of constants C, α, L should be summarized in one place with guidance on how they might be obtained in practice.
- Originality: Moderate-to-high. The individual components exist, but the framing via fast-mixing parameter sets and the resulting FPRAS for MLE constrained to Θ across general exponential families is new and insightful.
- Significance: Primarily theoretical. The results clarify when MCMC-based likelihood learning can be made principled with explicit guarantees. While practical deployment is limited by certification/projection challenges, the framework can stimulate further work on certifiable fast-mixing regions and informs understanding of CD-like methods.

Suggestions for improvement:
- State explicitly and early: (i) f(θ) is convex (Hessian of A equals covariance; λ > 0 gives strong convexity), (ii) Θ is convex and a projection ΠΘ exists, (iii) sufficient statistics are bounded, and (iv) the MCMC kernel family and mixing-rate constants (C, α) are known on Θ.
- Provide concrete examples of Θ and projections beyond simple Ising cases (e.g., Potts, general pairwise discrete models), and summarize projection complexity. Comment on robustness to approximate projection.
- Discuss how to obtain or bound C and α in practice, and the sensitivity of schedules to their conservatism.
- Clarify the independence assumption across iterations and contrast it with “persistent” sampling; briefly discuss whether analogous guarantees might hold with dependent samples/pools.
- Unify notation for R, R2, Ra; ensure the Lipschitz constant expression L = 4R^2_2 + λ is consistently defined.
- Expand the empirical section slightly: include a comparison to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood on the same setup, and report the realized versus predicted schedules to illustrate conservatism.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually novel tractable-set perspective via fast mixing, broadly applicable to exponential families.
- Strong, carefully argued theoretical guarantees with explicit complexity schedules (FPRAS).
- Clear connection to ongoing NIPS themes (learning in high-treewidth models, MCMC-ML, CD).

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited practical applicability without reliable mixing certification and efficient projection for real-world models.
- Minimal empirical validation and no comparisons to strong baselines.
- Some clarity gaps (assumptions and convexity not stated prominently; minor notational issues).

Overall recommendation:
A solid theoretical contribution with clear novelty in framing and broadly applicable guarantees. I lean toward acceptance, with the understanding that the immediate practical impact is constrained by the difficulty of certifying and projecting onto fast-mixing parameter sets.
