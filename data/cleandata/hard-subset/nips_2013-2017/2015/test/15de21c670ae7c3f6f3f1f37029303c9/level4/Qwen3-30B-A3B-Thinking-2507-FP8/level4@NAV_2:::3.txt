

A key challenge in Markov network learning is optimizing the model parameters to maximize the likelihood. This typically involves gradient descent, where a critical component is computing the gradient. Since exact gradient computation is NP-hard in general, Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are frequently employed as an approximation.

This paper establishes theoretical guarantees showing that when parameters are constrained to a "fast-mixing set"—where MCMC converges rapidly to the stationary distribution—the standard weight learning procedure (using MCMC to approximate the gradient) becomes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). The authors analyze both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, providing separate theoretical bounds for each.

The paper is well-written overall, with only minor typographical errors. While I was unable to fully verify all proofs, they appear sound and plausible.

Although the theoretical contributions are mathematically impressive, their practical applicability is limited. The authors appropriately acknowledge this limitation. The primary concern is whether it is feasible to ensure parameters satisfy the fast-mixing constraints in practice. I would appreciate the authors' thoughts on whether these constraints could be enforced artificially (potentially as a form of regularization), and whether combining this with ridge regularization might lead to over-regularized models. If these practical challenges could be addressed, the paper would have much stronger impact.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that rigorously formalizes the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, the key constraint required for the theoretical guarantees has minimal practical relevance. Significant research challenges remain before these results can be translated into a practical and accurate weight learning algorithm.
