Quality:
The paper establishes convergence-rate guarantees for maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, with gradients approximated via MCMC. The resulting bounds, stated in terms of mixing times, appear meaningful and useful.

My main concern is that the lower bound in Theorem 7 is very hard to interpret in practice. A more extensive discussion of this theorem—and of lower bounds for the problem more generally—would strengthen the submission. In particular, intuition about when the bound is tight, how it compares to the upper bounds in typical regimes, and guidance on the role of constants would be valuable.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. Assumptions, definitions (e.g., fast-mixing sets, projection), and the algorithmic setup are presented cleanly, and the proof sketches give good intuition for the main results.

Originality:
Both the problem formulation—learning within a “fast-mixing” parameter class—and the technical results (convergence guarantees with explicit schedules for iterations, samples, and chain lengths) appear original. The framing moves beyond treewidth-based tractability to a mixing-time-based notion and is well motivated.

Significance:
The significance is somewhat unclear to me, but the work does seem to fill a gap in the literature on learning intractable models with MCMC-based gradient approximations. In this sense, it is a meaningful contribution. The paper would be strengthened by additional discussion of potential impact: for example, which model families and mixing-time bounds are likely to make these guarantees practically useful; how to balance projection cost versus sampling cost in larger systems; and opportunities for future extensions (e.g., alternative chains, improved projections, or reusing samples across iterations).

Overall, the submission introduces a novel tractable model class grounded in guaranteed fast mixing and provides new convergence-rate guarantees for learning in this class. The results seem novel and interesting, and I recommend acceptance.
