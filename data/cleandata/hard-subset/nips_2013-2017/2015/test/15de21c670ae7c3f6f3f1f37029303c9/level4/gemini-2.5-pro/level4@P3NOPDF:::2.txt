### Summary

This paper proposes a novel, computationally efficient approach to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families where computing the MLE is generally intractable. The primary difficulty from a convex optimization perspective is the calculation of an integral (i.e., the log-partition function and its gradient) with respect to the current parameter estimate. The authors circumvent this by assuming access to a fast-mixing MCMC sampler for all parameters within the allowed set. Under this assumption, they demonstrate that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be approximated with sufficient precision. By integrating this sampling-based approximation with the optimization framework of Schmidt et al. (2011), the paper establishes a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for calculating the MLE. The analysis considers both the convex and strongly convex cases, leading to guarantees on the likelihood error and parameter error, respectively. A simple experiment is included to demonstrate the theory in practice.

### Detailed Review

This is a high-quality and well-executed paper that makes a solid theoretical contribution. The approach is well-motivated, offering an alternative to methods that rely on restrictive structural assumptions, such as small treewidth, for tractability.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Combination of Techniques:** While the paper builds upon existing components—namely, fast-mixing MCMC, concentration inequalities, and the proximal gradient analysis of Schmidt et al.—the combination is non-trivial and insightful. The core contribution lies in rigorously connecting the precision of MCMC estimation to the convergence guarantees of a first-order optimization method.

2.  **Solid Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical analyses appear to be sound, though I have not verified the proofs in the supplementary material in detail. The derivation provides a clear pathway from the mixing time of the sampler to the overall complexity of finding an approximate MLE.

3.  **Clarity and Honesty:** I particularly appreciated the authors' candid discussion of the approach's limitations. The conclusion provides a thoughtful analysis of the method's weaknesses, acknowledges where the theoretical assumptions might be overly pessimistic, and outlines promising directions for future work. This transparency adds to the credibility and impact of the paper.

4.  **Broader Insights:** The paper offers valuable insights into the functioning of related algorithms, such as Contrastive Divergence, even though the analyzed algorithm is distinct. By formalizing the role of sampler accuracy in the optimization process, it helps contextualize a class of widely used but less understood heuristics.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the introduction and motivation are well-written, the clarity of the technical presentation could be enhanced in a few areas.

1.  **Convexity of the Objective:** The entire optimization framework is premised on the convexity of the negative log-likelihood function, `f(θ)`. A minor but crucial point that would improve the paper's self-containedness is an explicit statement and brief justification for this convexity. This follows from the fact that the Hessian of the log-partition function, `A(θ)`, is the covariance matrix of the sufficient statistics and is therefore positive semidefinite. Mentioning this early on would help guide the reader through the logic of the paper.

2.  **Consolidation of Assumptions:** To further improve clarity, it would be beneficial to explicitly list all key assumptions in one place. For example, the assumption that the parameter set `Θ` must be convex is not explicitly mentioned in the main body of the paper but is crucial for the optimization framework. A clear, enumerated list of assumptions would strengthen the presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an original and substantial contribution to the literature on learning in exponential family models. Its primary limitation, as the authors acknowledge, is the practical applicability of the results. Proving that an MCMC sampler is fast-mixing for a given model class is often an extremely challenging problem in its own right. In essence, the paper recasts one hard problem (computing the MLE) into another (proving fast mixing).

However, this is a valuable contribution. Establishing such theoretical equivalences can open new avenues for research and provide a deeper understanding of the problem landscape. For a researcher who can establish fast-mixing properties for a new class of models, this paper provides a direct, off-the-shelf result for tractable learning. Therefore, the paper is a valuable asset to the theoretical machine learning community.
