The paper proposes a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. In general, finding the maximum likelihood solution is intractable. From a convex optimization perspective, the primary challenge lies in the need to calculate an integral with respect to the currently proposed exponential family (EF) parameter. By assuming that Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is fast-mixing for all allowed parameters, the authors are able to demonstrate that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be calculated with sufficient precision. When combined with the results of Schmidt et al. (2011), this leads to a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for calculating the Maximum Likelihood Estimator (MLE). The authors consider both the convex and strongly convex cases, which yield different types of guarantees: the former on the likelihood error, and the latter on the parameter error. A simple experiment is also included to demonstrate the theory.

Overall, this is a high-quality piece of work. The approach is well-motivated as an alternative to relying on small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analyses appear solid, although I have not checked the proofs in detail. The proofs essentially involve combining the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and the results of Schmidt et al. While the paper combines existing techniques, the combination is nontrivial.

I appreciated the authors' honesty in discussing the limitations of their approach. In particular, the conclusion provides a thoughtful analysis of the weaknesses of the approach, the ways in which the theoretical assumptions are overly pessimistic, and potential directions for future work. The paper also offers valuable insights into Contrastive Divergence (CD)-type algorithms, even though the analyzed algorithm is not exactly contrastive divergence.

The introduction is well-written, with substantial references to previous work. However, there are a few issues with the clarity of technical presentation. One important omission is the statement that the log-likelihood is indeed convex. The convexity of the log-likelihood function follows from the fact that the Hessian of A(theta) is the covariance of the sufficient statistics, which is Positive Semi-Definite (PSD). Although this may seem minor, the entire paper relies on the convexity of f(theta), so explicitly discussing it would help guide the reader through the paper's logic. A clear summary of the assumptions made in the paper would also be beneficial. For example, one assumption not mentioned in the main paper is that the parameter set Theta must be convex.

Overall, I found the paper to be an original and substantial contribution. However, the results appear to be of limited practical value due to the fact that proving fast mixing of MCMC is extremely challenging. In essence, the paper has replaced one hard problem (finding the MLE) with another (finding a fast-mixing Markov chain). Nevertheless, being able to convert one problem into another is often useful, and having the results of the paper available to the community is valuable.
