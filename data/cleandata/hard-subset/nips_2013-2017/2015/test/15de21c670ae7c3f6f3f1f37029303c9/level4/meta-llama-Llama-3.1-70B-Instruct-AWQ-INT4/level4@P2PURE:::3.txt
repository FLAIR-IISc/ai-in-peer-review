A key challenge in Markov networks is weight learning, which typically involves finding the optimal setting of weights attached to features such that the likelihood is maximized. To accomplish this, a gradient descent algorithm is often employed, with a crucial sub-task being the computation of the gradient. However, since this computation is NP-hard in general, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms are frequently used as an alternative to exact algorithms.

This paper presents theoretical results demonstrating that if the weights (parameters) are such that MCMC converges rapidly to the stationary distribution on them, then the weight learning algorithm utilizing MCMC to compute the gradient is a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The authors consider two distinct cases - the unregularized case (convex case) and the ridge-regularized case (non-convex case) - and derive separate bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, with a few minor typos. Although I was unable to verify all the proofs, they appear plausible.

While the theoretical results are impressive, they have limited practical relevance, an issue acknowledged by the authors. I believe it is virtually impossible to ensure that the parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems. It would be beneficial for the authors to consider whether it is possible to artificially enforce this condition, which could be viewed as a form of regularization. Using ridge regression in conjunction with this condition may potentially lead to over-regularized models. If this issue can be addressed, the theoretical results presented in the paper could form the basis of a strong contribution.

Overall, the paper provides a formalization of the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, the practical relevance of these conditions is limited. Addressing non-trivial research problems is necessary before the results derived in the paper can be used to develop a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning.
