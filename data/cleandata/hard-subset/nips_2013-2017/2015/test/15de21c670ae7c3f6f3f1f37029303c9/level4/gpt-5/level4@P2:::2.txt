Summary
This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ, i.e., a set of parameters for which a chosen MCMC sampler mixes rapidly. Within this set, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where gradients are approximated using MCMC samples. Under bounded sufficient statistics and the ability to project onto Θ, they prove high-probability convergence to the MLE within Θ. They provide two regimes: (i) a convex case (unregularized or merely convex objective) giving guarantees in log-likelihood suboptimality with total sampling effort scaling roughly as O(ε−3) up to logs, and (ii) a strongly convex case (ridge-regularized) giving guarantees in parameter error with effort roughly O(ε−2) up to logs. The results hold for general exponential families, not only graphical models. A small Ising model experiment illustrates the bounds and shows conservatism in the constants.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned as an alternative to tractability via small treewidth (e.g., Chow–Liu trees) or correlation decay/high girth, and to surrogate learning criteria such as pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihood, which have different statistical and computational trade-offs. It connects to Steinhardt and Liang’s construction of fast-mixing chains via restarts, to classical mixing-time bounds for Ising/Potts models, and to convergence guarantees for inexact (proximal) gradient methods (Schmidt et al.). The novelty lies in showing that, assuming fast mixing and a projection oracle, one obtains an FPRAS-style procedure for MLE within Θ for general exponential families.

Quality
The theoretical development appears sound: the analysis relies on (i) boundedness of sufficient statistics to derive Lipschitz gradients, (ii) concentration inequalities to control sampling error, (iii) geometric mixing to control bias from finite-length chains, and (iv) convergence results for projected gradient methods with inexact gradients. I did not verify all proofs line-by-line, but the steps are standard and well-motivated. The complexity schedules (in K, M, v) and matching lower bounds (up to log factors and constants) are carefully derived. The empirical example is modest but appropriately used to illustrate conservatism of the theory rather than to claim practical superiority.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well referenced, but several points would improve readability:
- Make the convexity argument explicit early. State that f(θ) = A(θ) − θ·t̄ + (λ/2)∥θ∥2 is convex because ∇2A(θ) = Covpθ[t(X)] is PSD; with λ > 0, f is strongly convex. This is central to the logic of the paper.
- Clearly and compactly list all assumptions in one place: bounded sufficient statistics; existence of a convex parameter set Θ with geometric mixing constants (C, α) for the chosen Markov chain; availability and computational cost of the projection ΠΘ; and the sampler/model pairing for which the mixing assumption is claimed to hold.
- Clarify notation around the bound R2 (norm bound on sufficient statistics), and the dependence of L on R2 and λ.
- Briefly discuss the computational cost of projection up front (not only in the conclusion), since the overall runtime bounds hide this cost.

Originality
The contribution is primarily a nontrivial synthesis: combining fast-mixing parameter constraints with inexact projected gradient analysis to obtain an FPRAS for MLE within Θ for arbitrary treewidth. While the components are known, the generality (arbitrary exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics) and the explicit sample/iteration/mixing schedules add value.

Significance
The theoretical significance is clear: it turns the intractability of likelihood-based learning into a constrained problem where MCMC-based gradients are provably sufficient. Practically, the utility may be limited because certifying fast mixing and computing projections onto Θ can be difficult for many models of interest. Nonetheless, the framework clarifies when CD-type procedures can approximate ML and suggests principled regularization toward fast-mixing regimes.

Strengths
- Clear motivation as an alternative to treewidth-based tractability or surrogate objectives.
- General results for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Careful analysis for both convex and strongly convex cases, with concrete schedules and near-matching lower bounds.
- Honest discussion of limitations and connections to practice (e.g., CD, sample pools, initialization).

Weaknesses / points for improvement
- Practical applicability hinges on identifying and projecting onto fast-mixing sets; certifying mixing and implementing efficient projections can be challenging.
- Assumptions and convexity could be summarized more explicitly to guide readers.
- Some notation and constants (e.g., L and R2) could be defined more crisply where first used.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution with clear, general statements and constructive schedules.
- Bridges MCMC-based learning practice and theory; relevant to a broad NIPS audience interested in probabilistic modeling and optimization.
- Provides insights into when approximate likelihood gradients suffice for ML.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical impact is limited by the difficulty of establishing fast mixing and performing projections for complex models.
- The method largely combines existing tools; novelty lies in synthesis more than in new techniques.

Overall evaluation
A well-executed and thoughtful theoretical paper that offers a clear lens on likelihood-based learning under fast-mixing constraints. While practicality may be limited, the contribution is original in synthesis and likely to interest the NIPS community.
