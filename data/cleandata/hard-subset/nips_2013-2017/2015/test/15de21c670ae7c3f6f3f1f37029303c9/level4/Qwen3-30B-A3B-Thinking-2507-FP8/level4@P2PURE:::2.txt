

Summary: The paper proposes a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. Finding the maximum likelihood solution is generally intractable, with the key challenge being the need to compute an integral with respect to the current exponential family parameter from a convex optimization perspective. By assuming that MCMC mixes rapidly for all permissible parameters, the authors demonstrate that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be computed with sufficient precision. Combining this with Schmidt et al. (2011), they obtain a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for the maximum likelihood estimate. Both convex and strongly convex cases are analyzed, yielding distinct guarantees: the former on likelihood error and the latter on parameter error. A simple experimental demonstration of the theory is included.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution. The approach is well-motivated as an alternative to relying on small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analyses appear sound, though I have not verified the proofs in detail; they essentially combine the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and Schmidt et al. (2011). While the paper integrates existing techniques, the combination is nontrivial.

I appreciate the authors' honesty in discussing limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully analyzes weaknesses, identifies overly pessimistic theoretical assumptions, and suggests future directions. The paper also provides insights into CD-type algorithms, even though the analyzed algorithm is not contrastive divergence.

The introduction is well-written with substantial references, but the technical presentation has minor clarity issues. A significant omission is the explicit statement that the log-likelihood is convex: the second and third terms in Equation 1 are clearly convex, and the convexity of $A(\theta)$ follows from the Hessian of $A$ being the covariance of sufficient statistics (hence PSD). Given that the entire paper relies on the convexity of $f(\theta)$, explicitly stating this would guide the reader through the logical flow. Additionally, a clear summary of all paper assumptions is needed; one such assumption (that the parameter set $\Theta$ must be convex) is never mentioned in the main text.

The paper represents an original and substantial theoretical contribution. However, the results appear to have limited practical value due to the extreme difficulty of proving fast mixing for MCMC. In essence, the paper replaces one hard problem (finding the MLE) with another (finding a fast-mixing Markov chain). Despite this limitation, the ability to convert between problems is often valuable, making the paper's results beneficial for the community.
