A key problem in Markov networks is weight learning, which typically involves finding the optimal setting of weights attached to features that maximize the likelihood. Gradient descent algorithms are commonly used for weight learning, and a crucial step in this process is computing the gradient. However, since computing the exact gradient is NP-hard in general, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms are often employed as an alternative.

The paper presents theoretical results demonstrating that when the weights (parameters) are such that MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution, the weight learning algorithm using MCMC to compute the gradient is a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The authors consider two cases: the unregularized case (convex case) and the ridge-regularized case (non-convex case), deriving separate bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, with a few minor typos. Although I was unable to verify all the proofs, they appear plausible. The theoretical results are impressive; however, they have limited practical relevance, which the authors acknowledge. I believe it is challenging to ensure that the parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems. The authors may want to consider whether it is possible to artificially enforce this condition, potentially by introducing an additional form of regularization, such as using ridge regression in conjunction with the existing condition. This could, however, lead to over-regularized models. Addressing this issue, in conjunction with the theoretical results, would significantly strengthen the paper.

In summary, the paper formalizes the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, these conditions have limited practical relevance. To develop a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning, several non-trivial research problems need to be addressed, building upon the results derived in the paper.
