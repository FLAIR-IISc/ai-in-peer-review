A significant problem in Markov networks is weight learning, which is often defined as finding a set of weights (associated with features) that maximize likelihood. Typically, the gradient descent algorithm is used for weight learning, and a crucial sub-task in this algorithm is calculating the gradient. Since the latter is generally NP-hard, the MCMC algorithm is often used instead of an exact algorithm to calculate it.

This paper achieves theoretical results that show that if the weights (parameters) are such that MCMC converges quickly to a stationary distribution over them, then the weight learning algorithm (which uses MCMC to calculate the gradient) is an FPRAS. The paper considers two cases: the random case (convex case) and the ridge-regulated case (non-convex case), and obtains different limits for each.

The paper is generally well-written, with some typing errors. I was unable to verify all the evidence, however, they seem credible.

Although the theoretical results appear impressive, they are practically very less relevant (I like that the authors have acknowledged this). I think it is almost impossible to ensure that the parameters meet the constraints required by the theorem. Have the authors considered this? In particular, is it possible to artificially enforce this condition (this condition can be seen as a form of regularization and indeed using ridge regression in addition to this can lead to over-regularized models). If this latter problem can be solved with these theoretical results, then you have a very strong paper.

Overall, a reasonably well-written paper that formalizes the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) leads to FPRAS. However, this condition is practically very less relevant. The results obtained in the paper need to address non-trivial research problems before a practical/exact algorithm for weight learning can be obtained.
