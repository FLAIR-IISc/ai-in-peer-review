### **Review**

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a novel and important class of models defined by their efficient, MCMC-based approximability. It provides original theoretical results establishing convergence rates for learning these models. While the significance could be more strongly articulated and the lower bound analysis could be clarified, the paper's core contributions are strong and fill a notable gap in the literature. I recommend acceptance.

**Quality:**

The paper establishes convergence rates for learning algorithms, directly linking them to the mixing times of the underlying MCMC samplers. The resulting bounds are meaningful and appear useful for analyzing the practical performance of these methods.

The primary area for improvement concerns the lower bound presented in Theorem 7. In its current form, the theorem is difficult to interpret, and its implications are not immediately clear. The paper would be substantially strengthened by an expanded discussion clarifying this result, as well as a more general treatment of the lower bounds applicable to this problem setting.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The authors' arguments are easy to follow, and the notation is consistent and well-defined throughout the manuscript.

**Originality:**

The work is highly original in two key respects. First, the conceptual framing of the learning problem around a "fast mixing" model class is a novel and valuable contribution. Second, the theoretical results derived within this framework are, to my knowledge, new and provide important insights into learning intractable models.

**Significance:**

This work makes a significant contribution by addressing a recognized gap in the literature on learning intractable probabilistic models via MCMC. By providing a formal framework for models that are only approximately tractable, the work has the potential to be quite influential.

However, the submission could better articulate its broader impact. A more thorough discussion of potential applications, future research directions, and opportunities for others to build upon this work would help solidify its significance and encourage further research in this promising area.
