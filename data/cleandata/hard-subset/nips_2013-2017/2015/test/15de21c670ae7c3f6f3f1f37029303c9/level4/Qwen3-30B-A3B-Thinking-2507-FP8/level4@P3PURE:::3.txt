

The paper addresses weight learning in Markov networks, a problem typically framed as maximizing likelihood by setting feature weights. Gradient descent is commonly employed, with the key subtask being gradient computation. Since exact gradient computation is NP-hard, MCMC algorithms are frequently used as approximations. The paper establishes theoretical results demonstrating that if the weights (parameters) ensure rapid MCMC convergence to the stationary distribution, then the weight learning algorithm using MCMC for gradient computation becomes a Fully Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The authors analyze two cases—unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (non-convex)—deriving separate bounds for each.  

The paper is generally well-written, though it contains a few minor typographical errors. While I was unable to fully verify all proofs, they appear plausible. Although the theoretical results are impressive, they lack practical relevance (a point the authors themselves acknowledge). It is virtually impossible to ensure that parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems. Have the authors considered whether this condition can be enforced artificially? The condition resembles a form of regularization, and combining it with ridge regression may lead to over-regularized models. If this issue is resolved, the paper would become significantly stronger.  

Overall, the paper reasonably formalizes the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, this condition is of little practical relevance. Non-trivial research challenges must be addressed before the results can yield a practical or accurate algorithm for weight learning.
