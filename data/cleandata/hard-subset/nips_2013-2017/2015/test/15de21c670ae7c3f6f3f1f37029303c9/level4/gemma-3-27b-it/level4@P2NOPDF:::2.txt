This paper introduces a novel and computationally efficient approach to maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) within exponential families. Obtaining the MLE is generally intractable; a key obstacle in convex optimization formulations is the need to evaluate integrals involving the model’s parameters. The authors circumvent this challenge by leveraging the assumption of fast-mixing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for all parameter values. This allows them to approximate the required integrals with sufficient precision for use in proximal gradient descent, ultimately yielding a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for MLE, building upon the work of Schmidt et al. (2011). The analysis covers both convex and strongly convex scenarios, resulting in distinct error guarantees – likelihood error for the former and parameter error for the latter – and is supported by a simple experimental validation.

Overall, this is a strong contribution. The presented method provides a valuable alternative to approaches reliant on restrictive small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, though a detailed verification of the proofs was beyond the scope of this review. The core of the work lies in a clever combination of MCMC mixing assumptions, concentration inequalities, and existing results; while building on established techniques, this combination is non-trivial and insightful. 

I particularly appreciated the authors’ candid discussion of the approach’s limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully analyzes the weaknesses of the method, acknowledges potentially pessimistic assumptions, and proposes avenues for future research. The paper also sheds light on the connections to contrastive divergence (CD)-type algorithms, even though the specific algorithm analyzed differs from standard CD.

The introduction is well-written and provides comprehensive context with relevant citations. However, the technical presentation could benefit from some minor improvements to enhance clarity. Notably, the paper would be strengthened by explicitly stating the convexity of the log-likelihood function. While the convexity of the second and third terms in equation (1) is clear, and the convexity of A(θ) follows from the positive semi-definiteness of its Hessian (being the covariance of the sufficient statistics), explicitly stating this fact – which underpins the entire analysis – would improve the reader’s understanding. A consolidated summary of the paper’s key assumptions would also be beneficial. For example, the requirement that the parameter space Θ be convex is not explicitly stated in the main text. 

In conclusion, I found this to be an original and substantial contribution to the field. Despite its theoretical merits, the practical impact may be limited, as demonstrating fast-mixing behavior for MCMC chains remains a significant challenge. In essence, the paper transforms one difficult problem (MLE computation) into another (finding a fast-mixing Markov chain). Nevertheless, this transformation is valuable in itself, and the results presented are a useful addition to the existing literature.
