Summary
The paper proposes a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. In general, computing the MLE is intractable; from a convex optimization perspective, the bottleneck is evaluating an integral with respect to the current exponential-family parameter. Assuming that MCMC is fast-mixing for all admissible parameters, the authors show that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be estimated with sufficient accuracy to yield, when combined with Schmidt et al. (2011), a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the MLE. Both convex and strongly convex cases are analyzed, yielding different guarantees: likelihood error bounds in the convex case and parameter error bounds in the strongly convex case. A simple experiment is provided to illustrate the theory.

Strengths
- Well-motivated alternative to relying on small-treewidth assumptions.
- Theoretical analysis appears solid (though I did not check every proof in detail). The arguments essentially combine the fast-mixing MCMC assumption with concentration inequalities and the results of Schmidt et al. (2011). While the ingredients are known, their combination here is nontrivial.
- The paper is admirably candid about limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully discusses weaknesses, notes where assumptions may be pessimistic, and outlines directions for future work.
- Offers useful insight into CD-type algorithms, even though the algorithm analyzed is not exactly contrastive divergence.
- The introduction is well-written and well-referenced.

Weaknesses and limitations
- The practical impact seems limited because establishing fast mixing of MCMC for all allowed parameters is extremely challenging. In effect, the paper trades one hard problem (computing the MLE) for another (proving fast mixing of a Markov chain). That said, the reduction itself is valuable and can inform future work.

Clarity and presentation
- There are a few clarity issues in the technical presentation. An important omission is an explicit statement that the log-likelihood is convex: the second and third terms in Eq. 1 are clearly convex, and the convexity of A(θ) follows because its Hessian is the covariance of the sufficient statistics and hence PSD. While this may seem minor, the paper relies on the convexity of f(θ), so making this explicit would help guide the reader.
- Please provide a clear, consolidated summary of all assumptions. For example, the main text does not state that the parameter set Θ is assumed to be convex.

Minor comments and typos
- l185: “,w here” → “, where”
- l192: “this paper that” → “this paper assumes that”
- l207: “simple example fast-mixing” → “simple example of a fast-mixing”
- l281: “of of” → “of”
- l311–312: The sentence is nonsensical; please revise.

Overall assessment
An novel approach to developing conditions under which finding the (approximate) MLE is tractable. Overall, a high-quality and original contribution that combines existing techniques in a nontrivial way. Well-written and insightful, though of seemingly limited practical value given the difficulty of verifying fast mixing in practice.
