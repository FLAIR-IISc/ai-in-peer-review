Summary: The paper introduces a new route to computationally efficient maximum likelihood estimation in exponential families. Since exact MLE is generally intractable due to the need to evaluate expectations under the current parameter, the authors instead constrain parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, within which MCMC provably reaches stationarity rapidly. Under this assumption, the expectations needed for projected (proximal) gradient descent can be approximated accurately enough that, together with the inexact gradient results of Schmidt et al. (2011), one obtains a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme to the MLE within the set. The analysis covers both the merely convex and the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) regimes, yielding different guarantees: suboptimality in log-likelihood for the convex case and parameter-distance error for the strongly convex case. A small illustrative experiment is provided.

Overall, this is a strong paper. The motivation—eschewing small-treewidth assumptions in favor of fast-mixing parameter sets—is compelling. The theoretical development appears sound (though I did not check every proof in detail). The arguments largely arise from combining the mixing-time assumption with concentration inequalities and the convergence theory of Schmidt et al.; while built from known components, the synthesis is nontrivial and well executed.

I also appreciated the candid discussion of limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully examines the weaknesses and conservative nature of some assumptions, outlines future directions, and clarifies the relationship to contrastive divergence–style methods, even though the algorithm analyzed is not exactly CD.

The introduction is clear and well referenced, but a few aspects of the technical presentation could be sharpened. A small yet important omission is an explicit statement that the (regularized) negative log-likelihood in Eq. (1) is convex: the second and third terms are clearly convex, and A(θ) is convex since its Hessian equals the covariance of the sufficient statistics and is therefore PSD. Because the entire analysis hinges on convexity of f(θ), stating this explicitly would help orient readers. It would also help to provide a concise summary of all assumptions up front; for instance, the convexity of the constraint set Θ (needed for projection and the mixing assumption) is not stated in the main text.

In sum, I view the paper as an original and substantive contribution. Its immediate practical impact may be limited, given that certifying fast mixing for a given family and sampler is itself difficult. In that sense, the work trades one hard problem (computing the MLE) for another (establishing fast mixing). Nonetheless, reframing the problem in this way is valuable, and the results provide a useful tool for the community.
