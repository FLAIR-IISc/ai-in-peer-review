Summary
This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families when exact inference is intractable. The authors propose constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ, i.e., a set for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast for any θ ∈ Θ. Under this constraint and assuming bounded sufficient statistics, they analyze projected gradient descent where the likelihood gradient is approximated by MCMC samples. They prove that:
- In the unregularized (convex) case, the averaged iterate achieves ε-suboptimality in objective with total MCMC work KMv scaling as Õ(1/ε^3).
- With ridge regularization (strongly convex case), the final iterate attains ε accuracy in parameter distance with total work Õ(1/ε^2).
Both results yield fully polynomial randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) for maximum-likelihood learning restricted to Θ. They instantiate fast-mixing sets for Ising and pairwise MRFs using known mixing bounds (e.g., via bounded interactions or spectral/induced-norm constraints) and discuss projection operators onto Θ. A small illustrative example is provided.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects several strands of work: (i) MCMC maximum-likelihood estimation with biased gradients (Geyer; Descombes et al.; Salakhutdinov), (ii) surrogate estimators such as pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, and score matching (Besag; Lindsay; Varin et al.; Hyvärinen; Marlin and de Freitas), (iii) learning under tractability assumptions (Chow–Liu trees; correlation decay/high girth; Heinemann and Globerson), and (iv) fast-mixing parameter projections for Ising/MRFs (Domke & Liu, NIPS 2013; Liu & Domke, NIPS 2014). It also relates to Steinhardt & Liang (ICML 2015) who enforce fast mixing via restarts. The novelty here is a unified FPRAS analysis for maximum likelihood over an arbitrary exponential family constrained to any convex fast-mixing set, with explicit schedules for iterations, sample sizes, and chain lengths, using inexact projected gradient descent bounds (Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011).

Strengths
- Quality: The technical development is careful. The authors bound the gradient bias due to finite-time MCMC via total-variation mixing rates, control sampling noise via concentration inequalities, and combine these with stability of projected gradient descent for convex/strongly convex cases. The Lipschitz gradient bound and explicit parameter schedules are useful, and the dependence on mixing constants (C, α) is transparent.
- Originality: Formalizing “fast-mixing parameter sets” as a tractable alternative to low treewidth and showing that MCMC-based maximum likelihood within such sets admits an FPRAS appears novel and broadly applicable across exponential families.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well organized, with assumptions clearly stated and the role of each constant explained. The example helps ground the theory.
- Significance: Conceptually, the work clarifies when widely used MCMC-in-the-loop learning has end-to-end guarantees and quantifies the trade-offs among iterations, samples, and chain length.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practical enforceability of Θ: The guarantees hinge on projecting onto a convex set for which the chosen chain is provably fast-mixing with known constants. For Ising models, spectral/induced-norm constraints provide one option (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), but beyond these cases, computing a projection may be nontrivial, and mixing bounds/parameters (C, α) are rarely known or are loose. It would strengthen the paper to:
  - Provide concrete projection algorithms and complexity for broader model classes (e.g., Potts, general discrete pairwise MRFs under different norms).
  - Discuss how to pick/estimate (C, α) in practice or provide schedules that do not require them explicitly (e.g., via adaptive chain-length doubling).
- Conservatism of bounds: The example uses a smaller L than the bound suggests and still converges, indicating looseness. A discussion of typical constant magnitudes and empirical tightness would help.
- Interplay with regularization: The method assumes projection onto Θ and optionally ridge regularization. This can act as double regularization and potentially hurt statistical efficiency. Consider discussing alternative regularizers that directly penalize mixing-violating directions (e.g., spectral-norm penalties) and comparing projection vs penalty formulations.
- Initialization and sample reuse: The analysis assumes independent samples per iteration from chains started from arbitrary distributions. Common practices (persistent chains, sample pools, contrastive divergence initializations) are not covered. Extending the theory to these settings or commenting on how to adapt the analysis would broaden applicability.
- Scope of MCMC kernels: The results refer to “a sampling algorithm” with geometric mixing. Clarify how the guarantees change with different kernels (Glauber vs Swendsen–Wang vs subgraph-world chains), and whether Θ depends on the kernel definition.
- Minor clarity issues:
  - Ensure consistent notation for the sufficient-statistics norm bound (R vs R2); in several places “R22” appears and may be a typesetting artifact for R_2^2.
  - In Section 4.1, restate assumptions (bounded statistics, exponential family) before the Lipschitz bound for self-containment.
  - In the overview, explicitly state that ridge regularization induces strong convexity (the draft refers to “non-convex,” which is incorrect).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound with plausible proofs and appropriate use of concentration and optimization error bounds. Assumptions are clearly stated; the main limitation is reliance on mixing-rate constants and projection availability.
- Clarity: Good overall; can be improved by cleaning notation, fixing typos, and consolidating assumptions where theorems are stated. A brief table summarizing parameter schedules and total work for the two regimes would help.
- Originality: High, in formalizing fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable regime for ML and delivering FPRAS guarantees for general exponential families.
- Significance: Moderate. The results are theoretically meaningful and could shape future algorithm design, but immediate practical impact is limited by the difficulty of enforcing/estimating fast-mixing constraints and constants for real models.

Questions for the authors
1. Beyond Ising/pairwise discrete MRFs, what concrete Θ and projection procedures do you recommend? Any guidance for higher-order factors or continuous exponential families?
2. Can the schedules be made adaptive without knowing (C, α), e.g., by monitoring coupling diagnostics or TV surrogates?
3. How sensitive is statistical accuracy to the strength of the mixing constraint relative to standard regularizers (L2, sparsity)? Any simple experiments comparing statistical risk vs pseudo-likelihood or composite likelihood under mis-specification?
4. Could your analysis accommodate persistent chains or sample pools with mild dependence across iterations?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, general theoretical contribution establishing FPRAS for ML over fast-mixing parameter sets.
- Bridges MCMC mixing theory with optimization under biased gradients.
- Relates and extends prior NIPS work on fast-mixing projections.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited practical relevance at present: enforcing fast-mixing constraints and obtaining usable (C, α) is challenging.
- Projection may be model-specific and computationally nontrivial; guidance is incomplete beyond Ising-like cases.
- Experimental evaluation is minimal and does not demonstrate practical utility or robustness of the approach.

Overall
A well-written and technically solid paper that advances our theoretical understanding of MCMC-based maximum-likelihood learning under fast-mixing constraints. Its main limitation is practical enforceability of those constraints. If the authors can expand guidance on constructing Θ and projections for common models and offer more empirical evidence, the work would have stronger impact.
