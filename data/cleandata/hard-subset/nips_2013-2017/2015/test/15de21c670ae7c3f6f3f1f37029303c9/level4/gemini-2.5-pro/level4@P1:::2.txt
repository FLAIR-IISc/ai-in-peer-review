This paper presents a novel approach for computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families, particularly in high-treewidth models where exact learning is intractable. The core difficulty in maximizing the likelihood via gradient-based methods is computing the expectation of the sufficient statistics, which is often intractable. The authors propose constraining the parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are guaranteed to converge quickly. They then prove that using projected gradient descent with gradients approximated by MCMC samples provides a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood estimate within this set. The analysis covers both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases, providing guarantees on the sub-optimality of the log-likelihood and the parameter error, respectively. A simple experiment on an Ising model is included to illustrate the theoretical results.

**Strengths**

This is a high-quality and well-motivated paper. The proposed framework offers a compelling alternative to common approaches for tractable learning that rely on low-treewidth assumptions (e.g., the Chow-Liu algorithm). The theoretical analysis appears solid, skillfully combining known results on MCMC mixing times, concentration inequalities, and the convergence of gradient descent with inexact gradients [Schmidt et al., 2011]. While the paper builds on existing techniques, their combination here is novel and non-trivial.

I particularly appreciated the authors' candid discussion of the work's limitations and directions for future research in the conclusion. This thoughtful self-analysis strengthens the paper. Furthermore, the work provides useful theoretical insight into contrastive divergence (CD), even though the analyzed algorithm differs from standard CD implementations.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is generally well-written, the clarity of the technical presentation could be improved in a few areas. A key premise of the entire approach is the convexity of the negative log-likelihood function, f(θ). This fact is never explicitly stated or justified. Although this is a standard result for exponential families (the log-partition function A(θ) is convex because its Hessian is the covariance matrix of the sufficient statistics), a brief explanation would help guide the reader and make the paper more self-contained.

Similarly, the paper would benefit from a more prominent summary of its core assumptions. For instance, the assumption that the parameter set Θ must be convex is crucial for the projection step but is not mentioned until Section 3.1. Highlighting all key assumptions upfront would strengthen the exposition.

**Overall Assessment**

Overall, this paper represents a substantial and original theoretical contribution. Its main limitation from a practical standpoint is that the problem of defining and projecting onto a parameter set with provably fast MCMC mixing is itself extremely challenging. In a sense, the paper successfully trades one hard problem (intractable likelihood maximization) for another (finding a fast-mixing parameter set). Nevertheless, formally connecting these two problems is a valuable achievement. The theoretical framework established here is a worthwhile addition to the literature and could inspire new research directions. I recommend acceptance.
