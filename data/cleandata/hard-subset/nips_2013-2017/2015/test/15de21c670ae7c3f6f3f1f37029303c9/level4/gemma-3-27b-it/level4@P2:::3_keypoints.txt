- Addresses maximum likelihood learning in graphical models and its intractability due to computational complexity.
- Core idea: restrict parameters to a “fast-mixing” set where MCMC converges rapidly, then use MCMC-approximated gradients for learning.
- Shows gradient descent with MCMC gradients yields an FPRAS within the fast-mixing parameter set.
- Provides convergence guarantees and computational complexity bounds for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases.
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, with minor typographical errors.
- Theoretical results appear plausible though full proof verification was beyond the review scope.
- Novel perspective: defines tractability by MCMC mixing speed rather than graph structure.
- Connects appropriately to prior work on pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods, and mixing-time literature.
- Rigorous analysis of convergence rates for gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients is a key strength.
- Authors responsibly acknowledge practical limitations of their approach.
- Main weakness: the fast-mixing constraint is likely very difficult to enforce in real-world settings, limiting practical relevance.
- Paper lacks in-depth exploration of mitigation strategies (e.g., alternative regularization) and trade-offs like potential over-regularization.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, novel tractability definition, FPRAS guarantee, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical relevance, insufficient discussion of enforcement strategies, and perceived incremental practical advance.
- Overall assessment: solid theoretical work with important caveats about practical impact; reviewer leans weak accept pending stronger rebuttal on feasibility or practical mitigation.
