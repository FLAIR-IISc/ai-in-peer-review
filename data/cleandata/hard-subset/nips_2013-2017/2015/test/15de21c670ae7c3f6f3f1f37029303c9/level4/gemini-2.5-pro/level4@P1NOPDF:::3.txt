A central problem in Markov networks is weight learning, typically formulated as a maximum likelihood estimation task over the feature weights. Gradient-based optimization methods are standard for this task, but they require computing the gradient of the log-likelihood, a subroutine that is NP-hard in the general case. Consequently, MCMC algorithms are widely employed to approximate this gradient.

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of this approach, deriving conditions under which the overall weight learning algorithm constitutes a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The key condition is that the model parameters must allow for rapid mixing of the MCMC sampler. The authors establish these guarantees for both the unregularized (convex) and the ridge-regularized (non-convex) settings, providing separate bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, with only a few minor typos. While I was not able to verify all the proofs in detail, they appear sound upon initial inspection.

The primary weakness of the work, which the authors commendably acknowledge, is the practical relevance of its core assumption. Although the theoretical results are impressive, the condition of rapid MCMC mixing seems virtually impossible to verify or enforce in practice. This raises a crucial question for the authors: have you considered methods for artificially enforcing this condition during learning? One could view such an enforcement as a novel form of regularization. If so, it would be important to understand how this new constraint interacts with standard techniques like ridge regression, as their combination could potentially lead to over-regularized models. If this challenge of enforcing the rapid mixing condition could be addressed, the practical implications of the paper's theoretical results would be substantial, resulting in a very strong contribution.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution by formalizing the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning is a provably efficient approximation algorithm. However, the practical utility of these results is currently limited by the stringent and unverifiable assumption of rapid MCMC mixing. Significant further research is needed to bridge the gap between this theoretical foundation and a practical, provably accurate algorithm for learning Markov network weights.
