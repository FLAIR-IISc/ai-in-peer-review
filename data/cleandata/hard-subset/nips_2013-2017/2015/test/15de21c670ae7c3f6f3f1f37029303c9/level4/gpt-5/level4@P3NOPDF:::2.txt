Summary: This paper proposes a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families. The key obstacle in MLE for such models is the intractable expectation/integral with respect to the model at the current parameter. Assuming a uniformly fast-mixing MCMC sampler over the admissible parameter set, the authors show that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be estimated to sufficient accuracy to yield, together with results of Schmidt et al. (2011), a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the MLE. The analysis covers both the general convex and strongly convex cases, yielding guarantees on likelihood error in the former and parameter error in the latter. A simple experiment illustrates the theory.

Strengths:
- Well-motivated contribution that sidesteps small-treewidth restrictions by leveraging mixing-time assumptions instead.
- Theoretical development appears sound and is a thoughtful combination of existing tools: fast-mixing MCMC, concentration inequalities, and proximal-gradient convergence (via Schmidt et al., 2011). While compositional, the integration of these ingredients is nontrivial and valuable.
- Clear separation of guarantees between convexity regimes and a transparent statement of what the FPRAS certifies.
- Honest and insightful discussion of limitations, including the pessimism of some assumptions and the gap to practice. The paper also sheds light on the behavior of CD-type algorithms, even though the analyzed method is not contrastive divergence per se.
- Writing is generally strong, with a thorough and appropriately cited introduction.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Practical impact may be limited by the difficulty of certifying or ensuring fast mixing across the entire parameter set. In effect, the work trades the intractability of MLE for the challenge of establishing fast mixing, which is often comparably hard. Nonetheless, this reframing is itself useful, as it clarifies what must be controlled to obtain provable learning guarantees.
- The empirical validation is minimal. While the goal is primarily theoretical, additional experiments—especially probing the dependence on mixing time, step sizes, and tolerance parameters—would help calibrate the sharpness of the bounds and their relevance.

Clarity and presentation:
- Please explicitly state and briefly justify the convexity of the log-likelihood f(θ). The second and third terms in Eq. (1) are convex, and the log-partition function A(θ) is convex because its Hessian equals the covariance of the sufficient statistics, which is PSD. Given that your entire algorithmic framework leverages convex optimization, this explicit statement will help guide readers.
- Provide a concise, centralized summary of all standing assumptions (e.g., fast mixing holds uniformly over the admissible parameters; the parameter set Θ is convex and known; boundedness/Lipschitz properties of sufficient statistics and gradients; access to unbiased or controlled-bias MCMC estimates with concentration). Some of these are currently implicit or scattered; making them explicit will substantially improve readability.
- Clarify the relationship to contrastive divergence: what aspects of CD are captured by the analyzed algorithm and which are not? A short, focused comparison would sharpen the contribution and avoid confusion.

Technical comments:
- The proof strategy—combining concentration for MCMC estimates with inexact proximal-gradient analysis—is sensible. While I have not line-checked all proofs, the logic appears consistent with prior work. If space permits, a brief high-level intuition for how bias/variance of MCMC estimates propagate through the proximal updates (and how this affects step sizes and iteration/sample complexity) would be helpful.
- It would be useful to state more explicitly the final complexity in terms of accuracy ε, condition numbers (strong convexity/smoothness), mixing time parameters, and dimension, for both convex and strongly convex settings. Even if asymptotic, a consolidated statement would help readers interpret the FPRAS claim.

- l192: “this paper that” → “this paper assumes that”
- l207: “simple example fast-mixing” → “simple example of a fast-mixing”

Overall assessment: An original and substantial theoretical contribution that ties together fast-mixing MCMC and convex optimization to deliver an FPRAS for MLE in exponential families under clear (if strong) assumptions. The work advances our understanding of when approximate MLE is tractable and provides useful insights related to CD-like methods. The main opportunity for improvement lies in clarifying core assumptions (including convexity of the objective and of Θ), polishing some technical exposition, and, if space allows, slightly expanding the empirical section. Despite potentially limited immediate practical value, the conceptual reframing and rigorous guarantees make this a valuable addition to the literature. I lean positive.
