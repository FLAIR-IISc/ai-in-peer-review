- Problem: learns feature weights in Markov networks via gradient-based optimization where exact gradients are intractable, so MCMC is used for approximate gradients.  
- Main contribution: provides a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that MCMC-based weight learning is an FPRAS under certain conditions.  
- Condition: the FPRAS guarantee requires the MCMC sampler to mix rapidly for parameters encountered during optimization.  
- Results: derives performance bounds for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases.  
- Strength: theoretical novelty in formally connecting MCMC mixing time to end-to-end approximation guarantees and proving FPRAS results.  
- Strength: paper is clearly written and communicates main ideas well; proofs appear plausible.  
- Strength: authors are transparent about practical limitations of their assumptions.  
- Weakness: central assumption of rapid mixing is very strong, hard to verify, and may not hold for real-world problems or near optimal parameters.  
- Question/suggestion: could the authors propose ways to enforce or encourage fast mixing during learning (e.g., a mixing-based regularizer)?  
- Concern: need to study interaction between any mixing-based regularizer and standard penalties like L2 to avoid over-regularization or bias.  
- Impact: theoretical results are valuable but currently limited in practical applicability without methods to satisfy the fast-mixing assumption.  
- Recommendation: paper would be stronger by offering insights or preliminary methods to bridge the gap between the theory and practical enforcement of the mixing condition.
