A key problem in Markov networks is weight learning, which typically involves finding a setting of weights attached to the features that maximizes the likelihood. Gradient descent algorithms are commonly used to learn these weights, and a crucial sub-task in this process is computing the gradient. However, since this computation is NP-hard in general, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms are often employed as an alternative to exact algorithms.

This paper derives theoretical results demonstrating that if the weights (parameters) are such that MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution on them, then the weight learning algorithm that utilizes MCMC to compute the gradient is a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The authors consider two cases: the unregularized case (convex case) and the ridge-regularized case (non-convex case), deriving separate bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, with a few minor typos scattered throughout. While I was unable to verify all the proofs, they appear to be plausible. Although the theoretical results are impressive, they have limited practical relevance, a fact acknowledged by the authors. In practice, it is virtually impossible to ensure that the parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems.

I would have liked to see the authors give more thought to this issue, specifically exploring whether it is possible to artificially enforce this condition. This condition can be viewed as a form of regularization, and using ridge regression in conjunction with it may result in over-regularized models. If this problem can be addressed, the theoretical results presented in the paper could be significantly strengthened.

In conclusion, this is a reasonably well-written paper that formalizes the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, the practical relevance of these conditions is limited. Before the results derived in the paper can be translated into a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning, several non-trivial research problems need to be addressed.
