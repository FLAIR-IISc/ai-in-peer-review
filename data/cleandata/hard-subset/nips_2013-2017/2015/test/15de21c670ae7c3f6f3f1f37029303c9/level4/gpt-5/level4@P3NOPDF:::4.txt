Quality:
The paper establishes convergence rates for learning algorithms in terms of the mixing times of the underlying MCMC samplers. The bounds appear meaningful and potentially useful for guiding algorithm design (e.g., number of MCMC steps, warm starts). The inclusion of both upper and lower bounds is particularly valuable.

The main concern is that the lower bound (Theorem 7) is difficult to interpret. As written, it is hard to see in which regimes it binds, how it scales with key problem parameters, and how it compares to standard complexity or information-theoretic lower bounds. The paper would benefit from:
- An informal restatement highlighting the core message, assumptions, and scaling.
- A simple illustrative example (e.g., a small Ising model) that shows how the lower bound plays out numerically.
- A discussion of tightness: where does the lower bound match the upper bounds (up to constants/log factors), and where are there gaps?
- Clarification of which notion of mixing time is used (e.g., total variation, spectral gap) and how constants depend on it.
- A clear statement of whether the bound assumes worst-case initialization or allows warm starts, and how this interacts with parameter updates during learning.

Beyond Theorem 7, it would help to collect the main dependencies of the upper bounds in a summary corollary (dimension, spectral gap/mixing time, step size, sample size, number of MCMC steps, burn-in), so readers can quickly see how to trade off computation for accuracy.

Clarity:
The presentation is generally clear and well organized. The writing is precise, and the proofs are readable. That said, the paper could be even clearer with:
- A concise, early definition of the “fast-mixing” model class, accompanied by canonical examples and non-examples.
- A running example that is revisited when stating the main results.
- A short proof sketch or intuition for each major theorem, especially for the lower bound.
- An explicit list of assumptions (ergodicity, reversibility/non-reversibility, warm starts, boundedness/Lipschitz conditions) before the main results.
- A brief discussion on how one might estimate or bound mixing times in practice (e.g., Dobrushin’s condition, coupling, spectral gap estimates), and what verifiable sufficient conditions fit into your framework.

Originality:
The framing of a tractable model class defined by fast MCMC approximability of expectations is original and appealing, and the theoretical results connecting learning convergence rates to mixing times appear novel. This perspective complements and extends existing approaches that avoid intractable likelihoods (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching, contrastive divergence) by providing explicit guidance on when MCMC-based learning is theoretically justified and how its performance scales.

Significance:
While the overall impact is not entirely clear from the current presentation, the work fills a tangible gap in the literature on learning intractable models with MCMC. Establishing principled convergence rates in terms of mixing properties can influence both theory and practice, particularly for undirected graphical models, energy-based models, and structured prediction.

To strengthen the case for significance, consider:
- Offering guidance on how practitioners can verify “fast-mixing” in real problems (e.g., parameter regimes where Dobrushin or path coupling applies).
- Discussing concrete implications for algorithm design: warm starts vs. cold starts, persistent chains, number of transitions per parameter update, and diagnostics for sufficient mixing.
- Adding a small empirical or synthetic illustration to show how the theory predicts the effect of mixing time on optimization error and to demonstrate the practical relevance of the bounds.
- Positioning the results relative to alternatives like pseudolikelihood, score matching, and approximate gradients via contrastive divergence, emphasizing when your approach offers provable advantages.
- Outlining extensions: non-reversible samplers, adaptive or tempering methods, Langevin/SG-MCMC variants, and high-dimensional dependence structures.

Specific suggestions for revision:
- Theorem 7: provide an informal statement, intuition, a simple worked example, and a comparison to known lower bounds; clarify dependence on warm starts and explicit constants where possible.
- Summarize the main dependencies of the upper bounds in a compact corollary or table (dimension, data size, spectral gap/mixing time, step size, burn-in, variance terms).
- Clarify whether mixing times are assumed uniform across parameter iterates or whether the analysis requires stability of the chain as parameters change; if warm starts are assumed, explain how they are maintained across updates.
- Briefly discuss the sensitivity of the results to misspecification of mixing time estimates and whether conservative bounds suffice.

Minor issues:
- I did not identify specific typos, but a careful pass to streamline notation and collect recurrent constants would aid readability.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-executed and original theoretical contribution that links learning convergence to MCMC mixing behavior within a clearly defined “fast-mixing” class. With a clearer and more interpretable treatment of the lower bound and an expanded discussion of practical implications and verification of assumptions, the paper would be both more accessible and more impactful. I recommend acceptance.
