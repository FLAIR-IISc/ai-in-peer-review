- Paper studies ML in exponential families constrained to a “fast-mixing” parameter set Θ and analyzes projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, proving high-probability convergence to the MLE within Θ.  
- Provides two regimes: convex case with log-likelihood suboptimality and sampling effort ≈ O(ε−3) (up to logs); strongly convex (ridge) case with parameter-error guarantees and effort ≈ O(ε−2).  
- Results hold for general exponential families (not just graphical models) and a small Ising experiment illustrates the bounds and conservative constants.  
- Positions the approach as an alternative to small-treewidth/correlation-decay tractability and to surrogate learning methods; connects to Steinhardt & Liang and inexact-gradient literature.  
- Main novelty is the synthesis that fast-mixing constraints plus a projection oracle yield an FPRAS-style MLE procedure for Θ.  
- Theory appears sound, using bounded sufficient statistics, concentration bounds, geometric mixing control of bias, and inexact projected-gradient convergence; complexity schedules and near-matching lower bounds are carefully derived.  
- Empirical evaluation is modest but appropriate to show theoretical conservatism rather than practical advantage.  
- Clarity needs: explicitly state convexity early, list all assumptions in one place, clarify R2 and the dependence of Lipschitz constant L on R2 and λ, and discuss projection cost up front.  
- Originality: nontrivial and useful synthesis extending to arbitrary exponential families, with explicit sampling/iteration/mixing schedules.  
- Significance: theoretical contribution clarifies when MCMC-based gradients can approximate ML; practical impact limited by difficulty of certifying fast mixing and computing projections.  
- Strengths: clear motivation, general applicability, careful analysis for both convex and strongly convex cases, and honest discussion of limitations.  
- Weaknesses: practical applicability depends on identifying/projecting onto fast-mixing sets; assumptions and some notation/constants need crisper presentation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid, general theoretical contribution bridging MCMC learning practice and optimization theory, relevant to the community.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical impact and the work is largely a synthesis of existing tools rather than introducing fundamentally new techniques.  
- Minor comments/typos: several line edits noted; suggest adding an assumptions box and an explicit convexity statement with a reference (e.g., Wainwright & Jordan).  
- Overall: well-executed, thoughtful theoretical paper with original synthesis likely of interest despite limited practical applicability.
