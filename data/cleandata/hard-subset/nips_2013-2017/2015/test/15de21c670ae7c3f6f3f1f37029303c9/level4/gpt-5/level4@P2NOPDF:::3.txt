This paper studies weight learning in Markov networks, where the goal is to choose feature weights that maximize likelihood. Because computing the gradient of the likelihood is generally NP-hard, practitioners use MCMC to approximate expectations needed in gradient-based optimization.

The main contribution is a set of theoretical guarantees showing that, when the parameters lie in a regime where the MCMC sampler mixes rapidly to its stationary distribution, an MCMC-based learning algorithm constitutes a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). The authors analyze two settings separately: (1) the unregularized (convex) case and (2) the ridge-regularized (non-convex) case, providing distinct bounds for each.

The paper is generally well written, with only minor typos. I was not able to check every proof in detail, but the arguments appear sound and technically careful.

My primary concern is practical relevance. The guarantees hinge on the parameters satisfying conditions that ensure rapid mixing, yet in realistic models it is extremely difficult to verify, maintain, or even approximate these conditions during training. The authors briefly acknowledge this, but I would like to see more discussion on feasibility. For example:
- Can one enforce the fast-mixing constraints during learning (e.g., via projections, penalties on strong couplings, or other constraints on weights)?
- How would such enforcement interact with ridge regularization, and could it lead to significant over-regularization?
- Are there tractable, a priori certifiable sufficient conditions or diagnostics that could be monitored during optimization to avoid leaving the fast-mixing regime?
- If exact certification is intractable, are there practical proxies or empirical tests that could guide the algorithm adaptively?

Addressing even part of this—by proposing enforceable constraints, diagnostics, or practical criteria that correlate with the theoretical conditions—would substantially elevate the impact and usability of the results. In its current form, the paper offers a clean and valuable clarification of when MCMC-based learning admits FPRAS guarantees, but the central assumption limits applicability. Nontrivial work remains to translate these insights into a reliable, accurate, and broadly usable learning method.
