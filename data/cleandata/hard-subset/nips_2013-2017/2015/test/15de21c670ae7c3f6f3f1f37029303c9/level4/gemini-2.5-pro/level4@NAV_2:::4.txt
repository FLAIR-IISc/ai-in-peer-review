This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models where exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting the model structure to be tractable (e.g., tree-structured, as in the Chow-Liu algorithm), the authors propose an alternative notion of tractability: constraining the model parameters to a "fast-mixing" set Θ. Within this set, Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to converge rapidly to the stationary distribution. The paper's core contribution is to provide theoretical guarantees for the widely used but seldom analyzed heuristic of performing gradient descent with gradients approximated via MCMC samples.

The authors prove that this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within the fast-mixing set Θ, for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. The analysis is presented for two distinct cases: the unregularized, convex optimization problem, and the ridge-regularized, strongly convex problem. For the convex case, achieving an ε-accurate solution in log-likelihood requires effort cubic in 1/ε (ignoring log factors). For the strongly convex case, an ε-accurate solution in parameter distance is achievable with effort quadratic in 1/ε.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The primary strength of this work is its novel conceptualization of a tractable model class. Defining tractability based on the efficiency of MCMC inference, rather than on structural properties like treewidth, is a creative and powerful idea. This provides a new and potentially fruitful perspective on learning complex probabilistic models.
*   **Significance:** The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for the common practice of learning intractable models by approximating likelihood gradients with MCMC. This is a significant result that rigorously connects a widely used heuristic to the principles of optimization, bridging an important gap between theory and practice. The guarantee that the estimator converges to the M-projection within the constrained set, even when the model is misspecified, is another important advantage over alternative estimators like pseudolikelihood.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and thorough. The authors derive explicit schedules for setting the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples (M), and chain transitions (v) to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. The separate treatment of the convex and strongly convex cases adds to the completeness of the work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated, the technical setup is defined precisely, and the main results are presented as clearly stated theorems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Quality:** The lower bound on computational effort presented in Theorem 7 is difficult to interpret. Its presentation is very dense, and its tightness relative to the derived upper bounds is not discussed. The paper would be improved by an expanded discussion of this theorem's implications, helping the reader understand the fundamental limits of this problem class.
*   **Significance:** While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper could do more to articulate its potential practical impact. The authors rightly note that their contribution is not primarily a practical one, but a more detailed discussion of potential applications or future extensions would strengthen the submission. For instance, what other models besides the provided Ising examples possess non-trivial, projectable fast-mixing parameter sets?
*   **Completeness of Analysis:** The complexity analysis focuses on the number of MCMC transitions (KMv) and largely sidesteps the computational cost of projecting onto the fast-mixing set Θ. While this projection is trivial in the simple Ising model example, it may be computationally expensive for other models (e.g., the SVD-based projection mentioned), potentially becoming a bottleneck. A brief discussion of this limitation would be valuable.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality submission that makes a novel and significant theoretical contribution to the literature on learning intractable graphical models. By introducing the "fast-mixing" model class and providing the first FPRAS guarantee for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning, the paper opens up a new direction for research. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to the exposition and discussion, which could be addressed in a revision. The paper is a clear accept.
