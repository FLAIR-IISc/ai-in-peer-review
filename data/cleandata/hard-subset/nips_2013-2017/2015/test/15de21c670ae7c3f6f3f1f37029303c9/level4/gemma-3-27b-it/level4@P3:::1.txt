This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, guaranteeing rapid convergence of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The core contribution is proving a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within this set, utilizing gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The analysis distinguishes between regularized and unregularized cases, achieving quadratic and cubic complexity in 1/ε, respectively.

The paper’s approach is a valuable addition to the landscape of intractable likelihood learning, offering a theoretically grounded framework for utilizing MCMC within gradient-based optimization. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing methods like pseudolikelihood and composite likelihoods, particularly their lack of guarantees when the model is misspecified, and their continued intractability for inference. The focus on fast-mixing parameter sets provides a novel perspective on tractability beyond traditional tree-structured parameters. The theoretical results—the FPRAS guarantees—are significant, particularly the established complexity bounds for both regularized and unregularized scenarios.

However, the paper suffers from a substantial oversight: a limited engagement with the existing literature on stochastic approximation and Monte Carlo EM algorithms. The proposed method bears a strong resemblance to these well-established techniques, and the authors would benefit from a thorough comparison to existing work. A significant body of theory already addresses similar problems, often under more general conditions (e.g., not requiring uniformly ergodic Markov chains, or re-initialization of the MCMC chain at each iteration, as the authors acknowledge). Specifically, the work of Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret provides foundational results in stochastic approximation that appear highly relevant. 

Furthermore, the presentation of results could be improved. While the authors eventually state assumptions, it’s often necessary to cross-reference with surrounding text (e.g., in Theorems 1 & 2), hindering clarity. The strong assumptions employed in the derivation of Theorem 6 appear unnecessarily restrictive, given the breadth of results available in the broader stochastic approximation literature. The discussion section briefly acknowledges potential improvements like using a pool of samples, but doesn't fully explore how existing work addresses these considerations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Perspective on Tractability:** Framing tractability in terms of MCMC mixing times offers a valuable alternative to traditional treewidth-based approaches.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The FPRAS results provide strong theoretical support for the proposed method.
*   **Clear Identification of Limitations:** The paper accurately highlights the drawbacks of existing likelihood approximation techniques.
*   **Well-Defined Problem:** The problem formulation and the setting of the constraints are clearly defined.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Literature Review:** The paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work, particularly in stochastic approximation, Monte Carlo EM, and related algorithms.
*   **Lack of Comparison:** The authors do not adequately compare their approach to existing methods, potentially leading to an overestimation of novelty.
*   **Presentation Clarity:** Some results require readers to consult multiple parts of the manuscript to fully understand the underlying assumptions.
*   **Potentially Strong Assumptions:** The assumptions used in Theorem 6 may be overly restrictive given the broader literature.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The FPRAS results are a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The fast-mixing parameter set offers a novel perspective on tractable models.
*   The approach has the potential to be applicable to a wider range of exponential families beyond graphical models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of engagement with related work significantly weakens the paper’s contribution.
*   The novelty of the approach is questionable given the extensive literature on stochastic approximation.
*   The presentation could be improved for clarity and accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the core idea is interesting and the theoretical results are noteworthy, the lack of awareness of, and comparison to, the extensive literature on stochastic approximation algorithms severely limits its impact. Addressing the literature review and clarifying the novelty of the contribution are crucial for future revisions. The presentation of the results could also benefit from greater conciseness and clarity.
