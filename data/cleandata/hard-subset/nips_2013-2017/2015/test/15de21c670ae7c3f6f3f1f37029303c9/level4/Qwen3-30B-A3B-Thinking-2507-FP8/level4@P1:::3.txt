

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning with high treewidth, where exact inference is intractable. The authors propose a novel approach by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing sets" where MCMC converges rapidly to the stationary distribution, rather than relying on tree-structured constraints. They prove that under this constraint, gradient descent using MCMC approximations for the likelihood gradient yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases. For the unregularized case, achieving ε-accuracy in log-likelihood requires effort cubic in 1/ε, while ridge regularization reduces this to quadratic in 1/ε.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a thorough theoretical development. The authors properly contextualize their work within exponential family theory and connect it to prior NIPS contributions on tractable inference (e.g., Heinemann and Globerson, 2014; Steinhardt and Liang, 2014). The formalization of fast-mixing parameter sets and their relationship to mixing time bounds (e.g., from Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993; Domke and Liu, 2013) is particularly strong. The proofs, while technical, appear sound and build logically from the mixing time assumptions.

The main strength lies in the rigorous theoretical guarantee that MCMC-based learning becomes a FPRAS under fast-mixing constraints—a significant contribution to the understanding of approximate learning algorithms. The paper also appropriately acknowledges the limited practical relevance of its results, noting that enforcing fast-mixing constraints is challenging in practice. This self-awareness is commendable.

However, the practical applicability of the results hinges on the feasibility of enforcing the fast-mixing constraint, which the paper does not adequately address. The constraint requires bounding interaction strengths (e.g., |θ_ij| ≤ β for Ising models), but the authors do not discuss how to enforce this during optimization without introducing additional regularization. Crucially, ridge regularization (which the paper uses for strong convexity) would compound this constraint, potentially leading to over-regularized models. The review should clarify whether the fast-mixing constraint can be enforced via projection (as suggested in Section 3.1) without conflicting with the regularization term. For instance, the authors note that projecting onto fast-mixing sets is trivial for Ising models (e.g., clipping θ_ij to [-β, β]), but they do not analyze how this interacts with ridge regularization in the strongly convex case.

The paper’s theoretical results are impressive, but the practical gap is substantial. While the authors correctly state that their work is "of little to no practical relevance," they do not explore whether the constraint could be enforced via adaptive methods (e.g., dynamically adjusting β during optimization) or whether alternative fast-mixing guarantees might be more feasible. Without addressing this, the theoretical contribution remains largely abstract. The example in Section 6, though illustrative, relies on artificial constraints (|θ_ij| ≤ 0.2) and does not demonstrate how such constraints could be derived from data.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution by formalizing the conditions under which MCMC-based learning becomes a FPRAS. The work advances the state of the art in understanding approximate inference for maximum likelihood learning. However, the critical question of how to practically enforce the fast-mixing constraint—without compromising model accuracy or requiring excessive regularization—remains unanswered. Addressing this would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact. As it stands, the theoretical results are compelling but disconnected from standard practice, making them of limited value for practitioners. The paper would benefit from a more concrete discussion of the feasibility of enforcing the fast-mixing constraint in real-world applications.
