

This paper addresses weight learning in Markov networks, a critical problem where parameters are optimized to maximize likelihood. As exact gradient computation is NP-hard, MCMC methods are typically employed. The authors establish theoretical conditions under which an MCMC-based weight learning algorithm becomes a Fully Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS), deriving separate bounds for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (non-convex) cases. The results are presented clearly, though minor typographical errors occur. While the proofs appear plausible, I was unable to fully verify all technical details.

The theoretical contributions are mathematically rigorous and significant. However, the practical relevance of the core condition—that MCMC converges rapidly to stationarity under the learned parameters—is severely limited. The authors correctly acknowledge this gap, but the condition appears virtually unenforceable in practice. Specifically, ensuring rapid MCMC convergence for arbitrary parameter settings is implausible without prior knowledge of the network structure and parameter values. The paper does not explore whether this constraint can be artificially imposed (e.g., via additional regularization), though the authors hint at this possibility. Crucially, combining such enforcement with ridge regularization risks over-regularization, potentially degrading model performance. If the authors could demonstrate a feasible method to satisfy the convergence condition without excessive regularization, the work would become substantially stronger.

The paper’s central claim—that MCMC-based weight learning yields a FPRAS under specific conditions—remains valid in theory. Yet these conditions are so restrictive that they do not translate to practical algorithms. For instance, the required convergence rate must hold *for the exact weights being learned*, creating a circular dependency: the condition depends on the solution, which the algorithm is trying to find. In real-world applications, networks rarely satisfy such stringent mixing properties without prior tuning, which the paper does not address. Without a path to enforce the condition during learning (e.g., through adaptive MCMC or parameter constraints), the results remain abstract.

The authors’ focus on formalizing the theoretical boundary for MCMC efficacy is valuable for understanding algorithmic limits. However, the absence of a practical pathway to satisfy the assumptions undermines the work’s utility. Non-trivial research challenges—such as developing regularization techniques that *guarantee* rapid MCMC convergence without compromising model accuracy—must be resolved before these results can inform real-world weight learning. The paper stops at characterizing a theoretical regime rather than providing tools to enter it.

In summary, this is a well-written paper with a sound theoretical contribution, but its practical impact is negligible without addressing the feasibility of the core condition. The authors’ acknowledgment of this limitation is commendable, yet the work falls short of offering a viable path forward. To elevate the paper, future work should investigate: (1) whether the convergence condition can be enforced *during* learning, and (2) how to avoid over-regularization when combining this enforcement with existing techniques like ridge regression. Without these steps, the results remain an interesting but isolated theoretical observation rather than a foundation for practical algorithms. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of these challenges and potential strategies to overcome them.
