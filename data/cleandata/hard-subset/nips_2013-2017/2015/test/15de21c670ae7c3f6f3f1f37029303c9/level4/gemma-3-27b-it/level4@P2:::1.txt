This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, enabling efficient approximate inference via Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). The core idea is to demonstrate a fully polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximizing the likelihood within this constrained parameter space, leveraging gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The work establishes theoretical guarantees for both unregularized and ridge-regularized scenarios, achieving polynomial-time convergence rates.

The paper’s central contribution lies in providing rigorous convergence guarantees for a widely-used, yet often theoretically unsubstantiated, approach to maximum likelihood estimation: gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation. The authors successfully demonstrate that, under the assumption of a fast-mixing parameter set, this iterative procedure can indeed approximate the maximum likelihood solution with quantifiable accuracy. This result extends beyond graphical models, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. The established convergence rates – cubic in 1/ε for unregularized and quadratic for ridge-regularized cases – are significant.

However, the presentation suffers from a noticeable disconnect with related work. The approach bears strong similarities to the Monte Carlo EM algorithm and the broader class of stochastic approximation algorithms designed for intractable likelihood functions. A substantial body of literature exists in this area, and the paper would benefit significantly from a more thorough engagement with and comparison to these existing methods. Specifically, the authors should acknowledge related theoretical frameworks which already encompass many of their results in more general settings.  The current treatment overlooks existing results concerning non-uniform ergodicity and algorithms that avoid re-initialization of the Markov chain at each iteration (a simplification acknowledged by the authors), strategies which have been extensively analyzed.

Several aspects of the presentation could be improved for clarity. Key assumptions underlying theorems, such as Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, are not explicitly stated within the theorem statements themselves, requiring the reader to cross-reference preceding and following text. This disrupts the flow and makes it harder to quickly grasp the conditions under which the results hold. Additionally, the reliance on strong assumptions in Theorem 6 appears unnecessarily restrictive; existing literature (e.g., Benveniste, Metivier and Priouret) offers more general results that could potentially be applied. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty of Guarantee:** While the algorithm itself isn't entirely novel, the *guarantee* of a FPRAS under the fast-mixing constraint is a valuable theoretical contribution.
*   **Generality:** The extension of the result beyond graphical models to general exponential families strengthens its impact.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a mathematically sound analysis of the convergence properties of a common practical approach.
*   **Clear Presentation of Results:** Theorems and proof sketches are clearly structured, facilitating understanding.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Awareness of Related Work:** The paper demonstrates a clear lack of engagement with the substantial existing literature on stochastic approximation and Monte Carlo EM.
*   **Marginal Novelty:** The core algorithmic idea is not entirely new, and the results, while theoretically sound, build upon existing theoretical foundations without substantial deviation.
*   **Presentation Issues:** The omission of key assumptions within theorem statements detracts from clarity.
*   **Potentially Overly Restrictive Assumptions:**  The strong assumptions utilized in some theorems might be relaxed based on existing work.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid theoretical analysis of a commonly used optimization technique. However, the lack of awareness regarding related work and some presentation shortcomings significantly diminish its impact. While not entirely without merit, the paper, in its current form, falls short of the high bar for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend **rejection** but encourage resubmission after a substantial revision addressing the literature review and clarity issues. The authors should explicitly acknowledge and compare their work to existing approaches in stochastic approximation, carefully consider the generality of their assumptions, and improve the presentation for greater accessibility.
