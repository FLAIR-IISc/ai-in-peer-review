This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in graphical models with high treewidth by considering a novel notion of tractability: restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference converges quickly. It proves that, for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients will approximate the maximum likelihood solution within this set with a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme, achieving either ε-accuracy in log-likelihood (cubic in 1/ε) or ε-accuracy in parameter distance (quadratic in 1/ε) when ridge-regularized.

The core idea is compelling – leveraging fast mixing to provide theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based learning, a common practice lacking rigorous foundations. The results appear meaningful and offer valuable convergence rates. The presentation is generally clear and well-organized. The paper demonstrates originality in framing the problem and presenting the theoretical results.

However, the significance of this work, while potentially substantial, is not fully articulated. The paper fills a gap in the literature regarding learning intractable models via MCMC, and this contribution is valuable. Further discussion of the potential impact—including avenues for future research and applications—would strengthen the submission. Specifically, elaborating on practical scenarios where this framework offers advantages over existing methods (e.g., when models are misspecified, or when inference is crucial after learning) would be beneficial. 

A significant point for improvement concerns Theorem 7, which establishes a lower bound on computational effort. This theorem is difficult to interpret and lacks sufficient discussion. Expanding on its implications and connecting it more clearly to the practical trade-offs in parameter selection would enhance understanding. More broadly, a more detailed discussion of the limitations and applicability of the derived bounds would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-defined problem framing based on fast-mixing parameter sets.
*   Strong theoretical results guaranteeing convergence rates for MCMC-based learning.
*   Addresses a practical gap in the literature regarding theoretical guarantees for a widely-used learning technique.
*   Provides a foundation for future work exploring tractable model classes beyond tree-structured distributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The significance of the work could be more clearly articulated with a discussion of its practical impact.
*   Theorem 7 lacks sufficient explanation and its implications are unclear.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the derived bounds and their applicability to different scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite these minor weaknesses, the paper presents a significant theoretical contribution with the potential to influence future research in learning with intractable models. The novelty of the approach, combined with the rigorous theoretical analysis, justifies acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2015.
