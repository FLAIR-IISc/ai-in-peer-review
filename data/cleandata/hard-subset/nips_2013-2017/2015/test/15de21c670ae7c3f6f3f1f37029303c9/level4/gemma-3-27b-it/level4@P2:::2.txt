This paper proposes a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. In general, finding the maximum likelihood solution is intractable. The authors address this by demonstrating that, if parameters are constrained to a set guaranteeing fast-mixing Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) convergence, a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for calculating the maximum likelihood estimate can be obtained. This is achieved by leveraging existing results on stochastic gradient descent (Schmidt et al., 2011) and carefully analyzing the error introduced by approximating the gradient with MCMC samples. Both the convex and strongly convex cases are considered, leading to different guarantees on likelihood error and parameter distance, respectively. A practical example illustrates the theory.

Overall, this is a high-quality work. The approach is well-motivated as an alternative to methods relying on restrictive treewidth assumptions, such as Chow-Liu. The theoretical analyses appear solid, though a detailed verification of all proofs wasn't possible within the scope of this review. The core contribution lies in skillfully combining the fast-mixing MCMC assumption with concentration inequalities and established results from optimization (Schmidt et al.). While the combination isn’t trivial, the paper acknowledges it builds upon existing techniques.

I particularly appreciated the candid discussion of the approach’s limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully acknowledges potential shortcomings, over-pessimistic assumptions, and outlines promising avenues for future research. The paper also offers valuable insights related to contrastive divergence (CD)-type algorithms, even though the primary analysis focuses on a different sampling strategy. 

The introduction is well-written and provides substantial context with references to related work. However, the technical presentation could benefit from increased clarity. A crucial, though seemingly minor, omission is an explicit statement of the log-likelihood's convexity. While the terms in equation (1) individually suggest convexity, explicitly stating this property will strengthen the logical flow for readers. The paper would also benefit from a clearer summary of the core assumptions, such as the requirement for a convex parameter set Θ, which isn’t prominently mentioned in the main text.

Overall, I view this paper as an original and substantial contribution. While the practical impact may be limited – proving fast mixing for MCMC is a notoriously difficult task – the paper successfully reframes the problem of maximum likelihood estimation. It effectively transforms one challenging problem into another, potentially offering new perspectives and tools for the community. Even if proving fast-mixing remains difficult, identifying this connection is a valuable contribution.
