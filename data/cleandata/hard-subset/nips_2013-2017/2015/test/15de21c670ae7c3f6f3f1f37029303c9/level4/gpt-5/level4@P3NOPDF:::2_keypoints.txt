- Proposes a novel FPRAS for MLE in exponential families by estimating intractable model expectations via uniformly fast-mixing MCMC within a proximal-gradient framework.  
- Analysis covers both convex and strongly convex regimes, giving likelihood-error guarantees in the convex case and parameter-error guarantees in the strongly convex case.  
- Theoretical approach is a thoughtful combination of fast-mixing MCMC, concentration inequalities, and inexact proximal-gradient convergence (Schmidt et al., 2011).  
- Contribution is well-motivated for bypassing small-treewidth restrictions, reframing MLE intractability as a question of mixing-time control.  
- Clear separation of guarantees between convexity regimes and transparent statement of what the FPRAS certifies.  
- Honest discussion of limitations and the gap between assumptions and practice, including relevance to CD-type algorithms (though the method is not CD).  
- Major practical limitation: difficulty of certifying uniform fast mixing across the entire admissible parameter set, which can be as hard as the original inference problem.  
- Empirical validation is minimal; more experiments probing dependence on mixing time, step sizes, and tolerances would help calibrate bounds.  
- Request to explicitly state and justify convexity of the log-likelihood (A(θ) convex via covariance/Hessian).  
- Ask for a concise, centralized summary of standing assumptions (uniform fast mixing, convex/known Θ, boundedness/Lipschitz properties, MCMC estimate properties).  
- Request to clarify relationship to contrastive divergence—what aspects are captured and which are not.  
- Suggest adding high-level intuition for how MCMC bias/variance propagate through proximal updates and affect step sizes and sample/iteration complexity.  
- Recommend stating final complexity explicitly in terms of ε, condition numbers, mixing-time parameters, and dimension for both regimes.  
- Minor typos and wording fixes noted (several line edits and “An novel” → “A novel”).  
- Overall assessment: original and substantial theoretical contribution with rigorous guarantees under strong assumptions; leans positive but needs clearer assumptions, exposition fixes, and expanded experiments.
