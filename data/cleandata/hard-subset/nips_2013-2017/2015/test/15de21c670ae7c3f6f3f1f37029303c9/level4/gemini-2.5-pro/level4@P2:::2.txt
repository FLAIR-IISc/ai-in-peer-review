### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families, particularly those corresponding to high-treewidth graphical models where inference is typically intractable. The standard approach to tractability often involves restricting the model structure (e.g., to trees). In contrast, this work proposes a fundamentally different notion of a tractable parameter set: one where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing."

The core contribution is a proof that, for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, constraining the parameters to a fast-mixing set enables a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood estimate within that set. The analysis validates a common practical heuristic—using MCMC samples to approximate the gradient in a gradient descent procedure. The authors provide rigorous convergence guarantees by analyzing this procedure, drawing upon established results for projected gradient descent with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011). The paper separately analyzes the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, deriving convergence guarantees in terms of log-likelihood error and parameter distance, respectively, along with explicit schedules for setting algorithmic parameters. A simple experiment on an Ising model demonstrates the theory.

### **Evaluation**

This is a high-quality, theoretically substantial paper that makes an original and valuable contribution to our understanding of learning in intractable models.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main idea—defining tractability through fast-mixing dynamics rather than graphical structure—is a significant conceptual shift. It offers a new perspective on why and when MCMC-based learning methods might succeed. By formally connecting the speed of MCMC convergence to the complexity of maximum likelihood learning, the authors provide a rigorous foundation for a widely used class of algorithms. This theoretical link is an important contribution that could inspire future work.

2.  **Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears solid and rigorously executed. The proofs are a non-trivial synthesis of concepts from MCMC mixing time analysis, concentration inequalities, and convex optimization literature. The paper is a complete piece of work, providing not just asymptotic guarantees but also explicit, non-asymptotic bounds on the total computational effort (in terms of MCMC transitions) required to achieve a desired accuracy.

3.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is generally well-written and well-motivated, effectively situating the work within the context of previous approaches like pseudolikelihood and low-treewidth learning. I particularly commend the authors for the thoughtful "Discussion" and "Conclusions" sections, where they honestly address the limitations of their analysis (e.g., the cost of projection, sample initialization) and provide insightful connections to related methods like contrastive divergence. This demonstrates a mature understanding of the work's scope and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses (Constructive Feedback for Improvement):**

1.  **Practical Applicability:** The primary weakness, which the authors acknowledge, is that the framework replaces one very hard problem (intractable MLE) with another (defining a useful set of parameters `Θ` for which fast mixing can be proven and onto which one can efficiently project). Proving mixing time bounds is notoriously difficult for all but the simplest models. While this limits the immediate practical utility of the results, the paper's value is primarily theoretical. It formalizes an important intuition and could be a stepping stone for future, more practical developments. This is a weakness in terms of immediate impact, but not in terms of scientific quality.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Presentation:** While generally clear, the exposition could be improved by making some key assumptions more explicit.
    *   A crucial premise of the entire optimization framework is the convexity of the negative log-likelihood objective, `f(θ)`. While this is a standard result (the Hessian of `A(θ)` is the covariance of the sufficient statistics, which is positive semidefinite), explicitly stating and briefly justifying this in Section 2 would strengthen the paper's logical flow and make it more self-contained for the reader.
    *   Similarly, the requirement that the parameter set `Θ` must be convex is essential for the projection step but is not highlighted. It would be beneficial to clearly list all major assumptions (bounded sufficient statistics, fast mixing on a convex set `Θ`, etc.) in one place.

**Minor Comments and Typos:**

*   l. 185: "w here" should be "where".
*   l. 192: "this paper that" -> "this paper assumes that".
*   l. 207: "simple example fast-mixing" -> "simple example of a fast-mixing".
*   l. 281: "of of" -> "of".
*   l. 311-312: The sentence "A similar proof strategy could be used..." is slightly convoluted. Consider rephrasing for clarity. For instance: "An alternative proof strategy for the convex case would be to bound the error at each step using a Hoeffding-type inequality and a union bound over all `K` steps. However, this approach yields a slightly weaker result than the one presented in Theorem 2, which uses the Efron-Stein inequality to bound the sum of errors directly."

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a novel, original, and theoretically sound contribution to the field. It provides the first rigorous proof of convergence for a general class of MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning algorithms by introducing the concept of a "fast-mixing parameter set." While the practical challenge of identifying such sets remains, the theoretical insight is significant. The work is well-executed and provides a valuable new perspective on a classic problem. I recommend acceptance.
