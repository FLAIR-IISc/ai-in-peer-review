A key problem in Markov networks is weight learning, which is often defined as finding a setting of weights (attached to the features) such that the likelihood is maximized. Typically, a gradient descent algorithm is used to learn the weights, and a key sub-task in this algorithm is computing the gradient. Since the latter is NP-hard in general, Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms are often employed in lieu of exact algorithms to approximate it.

This paper derives theoretical results showing that if the weights (parameters) are constrained to a set that allows for fast MCMC convergence to the stationary distribution, then the weight learning algorithm – using MCMC to compute the gradient – provides a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). The paper considers both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, deriving separate bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, with a few minor typos. While I was unable to verify all the proofs in detail, they appear plausible and well-structured. 

Despite the impressive theoretical results, their practical relevance is limited – a point the authors acknowledge. A crucial question is whether it’s possible to ensure the parameters satisfy the necessary conditions for fast mixing. Have the authors considered this limitation, and whether it’s possible to enforce this condition artificially – perhaps as a form of regularization? Combining ridge regression with this constraint might lead to over-regularization, but exploring this direction could significantly strengthen the paper. If this practical challenge can be addressed alongside the theoretical findings, the work would represent a substantial contribution.

Overall, this is a reasonably well-written paper that formalizes the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, the requirement of fast mixing presents a significant practical hurdle. Addressing this, and demonstrating a pathway towards a practically accurate algorithm for weight learning, would be necessary for a stronger contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the convergence of a commonly used learning strategy (gradient descent with MCMC gradients).
*   **Novel Approach:** The work introduces a different notion of tractability – fast mixing – as a criterion for efficient learning, moving beyond traditional treewidth constraints.
*   **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, providing sufficient detail for understanding the main results.
*   **Well-Justified Analysis:** The analysis considers both regularized and unregularized cases, providing a comprehensive theoretical framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practical Relevance:** The fast mixing constraint is difficult to enforce in practice, potentially limiting the applicability of the results.
*   **Open Question:** The paper does not fully address the challenge of enforcing the fast mixing condition, leaving a significant gap in translating the theory into a practical algorithm.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the results are solid, they build on existing techniques (gradient descent, MCMC) and may not represent a revolutionary advance in the field.
