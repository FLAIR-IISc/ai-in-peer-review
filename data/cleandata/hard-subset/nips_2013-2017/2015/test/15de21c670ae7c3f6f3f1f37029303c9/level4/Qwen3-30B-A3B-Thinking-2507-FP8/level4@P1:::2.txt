

Summary: This paper presents a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. The authors address the fundamental challenge that exact maximum likelihood estimation is generally intractable due to the need to compute integrals with respect to the current parameter settings. By assuming that Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution for all parameters within a constrained set Θ, they demonstrate that the required integrals for gradient descent can be approximated with sufficient precision. When combined with the results of Schmidt et al. (2011), this yields a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within Θ. The paper provides complementary guarantees for both convex and strongly convex settings, with the former providing bounds on likelihood error and the latter on parameter distance. A simple empirical demonstration validates the theoretical findings.

The paper makes a compelling case for considering fast-mixing parameter sets as an alternative to traditional treewidth-based approaches. The theoretical analysis appears sound, though I haven't verified all proofs in detail. The authors successfully combine established techniques—MCMC mixing assumptions, concentration inequalities, and results from Schmidt et al.—in a non-trivial manner to establish their main results. The introduction is well-written with substantial references to relevant prior work, though it would benefit from a clearer statement about the convexity of the negative log-likelihood function, which forms the foundation of the entire analysis. Specifically, while the paper mentions the convexity of the second and third terms in Equation 1, it does not explicitly state that A(θ) is convex (as its Hessian is the covariance of sufficient statistics, hence positive semi-definite), which is crucial for the optimization framework.

The authors demonstrate commendable honesty in discussing limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully analyzes weaknesses, such as the computational cost of projection operations and the arbitrary initialization of MCMC chains, while also noting that theoretical assumptions may be overly conservative. The discussion of connections to contrastive divergence methods, though not directly analyzing that algorithm, provides valuable theoretical insight into how such approaches might approximate maximum likelihood solutions.

I have two minor technical concerns that should be addressed. First, the paper assumes Θ is convex without explicit mention, which is essential for the projection operator to be well-defined and for the optimization guarantees to hold. Second, while the paper's theoretical contribution is significant, I share the reviewer's concern that proving fast mixing for a given parameter set remains challenging in practice. This means the paper essentially replaces one hard problem (finding maximum likelihood) with another (establishing fast mixing), though the theoretical framework provides a valuable roadmap for future work.

Strengths:
- Well-motivated alternative to treewidth-based approaches
- Solid theoretical analysis that combines established techniques in novel ways
- Clear presentation of assumptions and their implications
- Thoughtful discussion of limitations and future directions
- Comprehensive treatment of both convex and strongly convex cases

Weaknesses:
- Missing explicit statement of log-likelihood convexity (critical for the optimization framework)
- Unstated assumption that Θ must be convex (essential for projection)
- Limited practical significance due to the difficulty of verifying fast-mixing conditions in practice

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of when maximum likelihood learning can be performed efficiently. While the practical applicability may be limited by the challenges of establishing fast mixing, the paper provides a valuable theoretical foundation for future work. The results are significant because they establish conditions under which maximum likelihood learning can be performed efficiently without restricting to low-treewidth graphical models.

The paper would benefit from minor revisions to clarify the convexity of the log-likelihood function and explicitly state the convexity assumption on Θ. These are minor points that would significantly enhance the paper's clarity and accessibility.

I find this work to be a substantial theoretical contribution to the field. The results are original, well-supported, and advance our understanding of tractable maximum likelihood learning. While the practical impact may be limited by the challenges of verifying fast mixing conditions, the theoretical framework provides valuable insights that will likely inform future research on efficient learning algorithms for exponential families. The paper deserves consideration for acceptance at NIPS.
