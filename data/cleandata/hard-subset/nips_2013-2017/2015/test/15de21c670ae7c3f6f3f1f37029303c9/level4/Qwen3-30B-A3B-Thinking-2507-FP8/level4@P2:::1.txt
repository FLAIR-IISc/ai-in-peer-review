

This paper proposes a method for maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth graphical models by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The authors prove that gradient descent with MCMC-sampled gradients approximates the maximum likelihood solution within this set with high probability, providing complexity bounds for both unregularized (cubic in 1/ε) and ridge-regularized (quadratic in 1/ε) cases, establishing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme.

**Quality**: The technical analysis is sound, with correct proofs for the main theorems. However, the assumptions made are unnecessarily restrictive. The authors require uniform ergodicity of the Markov chain (Equation 2) and fixed initialization at each iteration (using an arbitrary starting distribution r), which are not needed in the broader literature. Benveniste, Metivier, and Priouret (1990) provide a more general theory of stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics that does not require these assumptions, and their framework could have yielded similar results with weaker conditions. The authors acknowledge the fixed initialization is a simplification but fail to demonstrate why their approach is superior to the more efficient alternative of using previous samples as initialization (as discussed in Section 7).

**Clarity**: The presentation would benefit from greater clarity. The assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 are not explicitly stated upfront but are scattered throughout the text, forcing readers to piece together conditions. For instance, the "fast-mixing" assumption (Equation 2) is crucial but not clearly connected to the main results. The paper also lacks a clear statement of the relationship between the mixing parameters (C, α) and the desired accuracy (ε), making it difficult to apply the bounds in practice. A more structured presentation with assumptions clearly listed before theorems would significantly improve readability.

**Originality**: The main contribution appears to be a specific application of existing stochastic approximation theory to graphical models, rather than a novel theoretical advance. The authors' algorithm is fundamentally a variant of the Monte Carlo EM algorithm (Dempster et al., 1977), which has been extensively studied in the literature. The paper fails to properly contextualize its work within this established framework. For example, Andrieu et al. (2005) provide a comprehensive analysis of MCMC-based EM algorithms that covers more general scenarios, including non-uniform ergodicity and adaptive initialization strategies. The authors also overlook the work of Welling and Teh (2011) on scalable MCMC for Bayesian inference, which provides a more general theoretical foundation. The claim of novelty is undermined by the lack of meaningful comparison to these well-established approaches.

**Significance**: While the paper provides a theoretically interesting analysis, its practical significance is limited. The authors acknowledge they are not making a significant practical contribution, and their example (Section 6) shows the theoretical bounds are conservative compared to empirical performance. The approach does not advance the state of the art in maximum likelihood learning for graphical models, as the results can be obtained through more general and well-established techniques. The main value appears to be in the theoretical analysis itself, but this analysis is not novel enough to warrant publication at NIPS given the extensive related work.

**Recommendation**: The paper does not meet NIPS's standards for novelty and significance. While the technical results are correct, they are not sufficiently novel or impactful to justify publication. The authors have overlooked significant related work, particularly the extensive literature on Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics. The paper would require substantial revision to properly contextualize its contribution within this broader framework, address the limitations of their assumptions, and demonstrate how their results advance the field beyond existing work.

**Pro/Con Summary**:
- *Pro*: Provides a technically sound analysis of gradient descent with MCMC sampling for maximum likelihood learning; correctly analyzes both convex and strongly convex cases.
- *Con*: Fails to properly contextualize work within the extensive literature on Monte Carlo EM and stochastic approximation; makes unnecessarily strong assumptions; presentation lacks clarity in stating assumptions; results are not novel compared to existing theory.

The authors should revise the paper to:
1. Provide a thorough literature review that properly positions their work within the broader context of stochastic approximation and MCMC-based learning
2. Address why their specific assumptions (uniform ergodicity, fixed initialization) are necessary rather than simply convenient
3. Clearly state all assumptions before presenting theorems
4. Demonstrate how their results extend or improve upon existing theory

Without these revisions, the paper's contribution is marginal and fails to meet NIPS's standards for originality and significance.
