Summary: The paper proposes a novel method for computationally efficient maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families. In general, computing the MLE is intractable. From a convex optimization perspective, the main obstacle is evaluating an integral with respect to the current exponential-family parameter. Assuming the MCMC sampler is fast-mixing for all admissible parameters, the authors show that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be estimated accurately enough to yield, when combined with results from Schmidt et al. (2011), a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for computing the MLE. The analysis covers both convex and strongly convex settings, yielding different guarantees: in the convex case, bounds on likelihood error; in the strongly convex case, bounds on parameter error. A simple experiment illustrates the theory.

Overall assessment: This is a high-quality piece of work. The approach is well-motivated as an alternative to small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analysis appears solid (though I did not check the proofs in full detail). The proofs essentially combine the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and the results of Schmidt et al. While the technique is a synthesis of existing tools, the combination is nontrivial.

I appreciated the frank discussion of limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully analyzes weaknesses, notes where the assumptions are likely pessimistic, and outlines directions for future work. The paper also sheds some light on CD-type algorithms, even if the analyzed method is not exactly contrastive divergence.

Presentation: The introduction is well written and well referenced, but there are a few clarity issues. A small yet important omission is an explicit statement that the log-likelihood is convex: the second and third terms in Eq. 1 are clearly convex, and the convexity of A(theta) follows because its Hessian is the covariance of the sufficient statistics and thus PSD. Although seemingly minor, the paper’s logic rests on the convexity of f(theta), so stating this explicitly will help guide readers. I also recommend adding a clear summary of all assumptions; for example, the convexity of the parameter set Theta, which is required but not stated in the main text.

Practical impact: The contribution is original and substantial, but the practical value appears limited by the difficulty of proving fast mixing for MCMC. In effect, the work trades one hard problem (computing the MLE) for another (establishing a fast-mixing Markov chain). That said, such reductions are often valuable, and making this conversion explicit should benefit the community.
