This paper addresses the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, which is often intractable due to computational complexity. The core idea is to restrict parameters to a “fast-mixing” set – those for which Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference converges rapidly – and then demonstrate that gradient descent, using MCMC-approximated gradients, can achieve a fully polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) within this constrained parameter space. The authors analyze both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, providing convergence guarantees and computational complexity bounds for each.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though a few minor typographical errors are present. The theoretical results appear plausible, although a complete verification of all proofs was beyond the scope of this review. The contribution lies in providing a theoretical foundation for a commonly used, but previously lacking guaranteed, approach to maximum likelihood estimation leveraging MCMC. This work connects to previous research in graphical model learning, such as pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22], and composite likelihoods [20, 30], by offering an alternative with FPRAS guarantees – a stronger theoretical property than often available for these heuristics. It also builds on the notion of restricting parameters to tractable sets, like tree-structured distributions [1968, 2014], but defines tractability in terms of MCMC mixing speed rather than graph structure. The connection to work on mixing times, such as Jerrum & Sinclair [1993] and Hayes [2011], is also appropriately noted.

A key strength of the paper is the rigorous analysis of the convergence rate of gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients. The derivation of bounds for both convex and strongly convex cases is a significant achievement. The authors also acknowledge the practical limitations of their work, which is commendable.

However, the primary weakness of the paper – and the core issue impacting its overall significance – is the practical relevance of the “fast-mixing” constraint. While theoretically sound, enforcing this condition on parameters appears exceedingly difficult in real-world scenarios. The authors briefly acknowledge this limitation, but a more in-depth discussion of potential mitigation strategies would be beneficial. Specifically, exploring whether this fast-mixing condition can be enforced through regularization techniques (beyond the ridge regularization already considered), and analyzing the potential trade-offs involved, would greatly strengthen the paper.  Could adding this constraint as a penalty term lead to over-regularization, negating the benefits of the FPRAS?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for a widely used practice (MCMC-based gradient descent for maximum likelihood learning).
*   **Novel Definition of Tractability:** Defining tractability in terms of MCMC mixing speed offers a fresh perspective on parameter space restriction.
*   **FPRAS Guarantee:**  Establishing an FPRAS for learning within the fast-mixing parameter set is a significant result.
*   **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-structured and readable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practical Relevance:** The “fast-mixing” constraint is difficult to enforce in practice, diminishing the immediate applicability of the results.
*   **Lack of Exploration of Mitigation Strategies:** The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how to address the practical challenges of enforcing the fast-mixing condition.
*   **Incremental Advance:** While theoretically sound, the practical impact of the work remains uncertain without further investigation into real-world applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical paper with a potentially important contribution. However, the practical limitations related to enforcing the fast-mixing condition raise concerns about its overall impact. A strong rebuttal addressing the feasibility of enforcing this condition, or providing a convincing argument for why it's still a valuable theoretical benchmark, would significantly improve the paper's chances of acceptance. As it stands, I lean towards a **weak accept**, contingent on the authors providing a more compelling discussion of the practical challenges and potential solutions.
