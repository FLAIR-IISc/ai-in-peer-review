- Addresses maximum likelihood learning in intractable graphical models by constraining parameters to a convex "fast-mixing" set where MCMC converges geometrically.
- Proposes and analyzes projected gradient descent that uses MCMC samples to approximate the likelihood gradient.
- Main theoretical contribution: a formal proof that the algorithm yields an FPRAS for finding the maximum likelihood solution within the constrained set.
- Provides explicit non-asymptotic convergence rates for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases.
- Framing contrasts dynamic tractability (fast mixing) with structural tractability (e.g., low treewidth) and correlation decay assumptions.
- Fails to cite or compare to extensive stochastic approximation (SA) literature; the algorithm is a specific instance of SA.
- Omission of SA context makes it hard to assess novelty and whether results are rediscoveries of known SA theory.
- Strong technical assumptions: requires uniform geometric mixing across the entire parameter set Θ, which is restrictive compared to SA work.
- Impractical assumption that MCMC chains are restarted from a fixed distribution at each gradient step; ignores warm-start methods commonly analyzed in prior work.
- Unclear whether the derived convergence rates are novel without comparison to bounds obtainable from established SA frameworks.
- Strength: tackles an important problem and introduces an interesting perspective of learning via dynamic (fast-mixing) parameter constraints.
- Strength: rigorous, well-written analysis that quantifies total MCMC effort to reach target accuracy ε.
- Argument for acceptance: provides a complete, self-contained non-asymptotic analysis that may help readers unfamiliar with SA literature.
- Argument against acceptance: lack of engagement with decades of SA research and strong unrealistic assumptions limit originality and practical applicability.
- Overall summary: technically sound and clearly presented, but contribution is diminished by neglecting relevant prior work and by restrictive assumptions.
