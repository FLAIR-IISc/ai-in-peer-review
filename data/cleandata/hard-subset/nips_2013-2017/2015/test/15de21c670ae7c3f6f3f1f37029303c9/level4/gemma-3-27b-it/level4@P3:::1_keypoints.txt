- Studies maximum-likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set that guarantees rapid MCMC convergence.  
- Main technical contribution: proves an FPRAS for MLE in the fast-mixing set using gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients.  
- Analysis separates regularized and unregularized cases, yielding O(1/ε^2) (quadratic) and O(1/ε^3) (cubic) complexity in 1/ε respectively.  
- Offers a novel tractability perspective based on MCMC mixing times rather than treewidth constraints.  
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees (FPRAS) that meaningfully support the proposed approach.  
- Correctly highlights limitations of pseudolikelihood and composite likelihood methods, especially under model misspecification and for inference.  
- Problem formulation and parameter-set constraints are clearly defined.  
- Major oversight: limited engagement with stochastic approximation and Monte Carlo EM literature, despite strong similarities to those methods.  
- Neglects foundational work (e.g., Benveniste, Métivier, Priouret) that addresses related stochastic approximation results under broader conditions.  
- The paper’s requirement of uniform ergodicity and chain re-initialization may be unnecessarily strong compared to existing theory.  
- Presentation issues: assumptions are scattered and readers must cross-reference multiple sections (e.g., Theorems 1, 2, 6) to understand results.  
- Theorem 6 uses potentially overly restrictive assumptions not well justified given prior literature.  
- Discussion mentions improvements like sample pooling but fails to fully explore or connect to existing solutions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant FPRAS result, novel fast-mixing tractability perspective, and potential applicability beyond graphical models.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient literature engagement weakens the contribution, novelty is questionable given prior work, and presentation clarity needs improvement.  
- Overall verdict: weak reject — interesting theory but must better situate within stochastic approximation literature, clarify novelty, and improve exposition.
