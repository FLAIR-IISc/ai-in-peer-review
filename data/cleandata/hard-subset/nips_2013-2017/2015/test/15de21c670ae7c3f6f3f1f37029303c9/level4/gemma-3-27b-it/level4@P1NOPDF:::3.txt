A key problem in Markov networks is weight learning, which is often defined as finding weight settings – assigned to features – that maximize the likelihood. Typically, a gradient descent algorithm is used for this purpose, and a crucial sub-task within this algorithm is computing the gradient. Since exact gradient computation is NP-hard in the general case, MCMC algorithms are often used as an approximation.

This paper derives theoretical results demonstrating that if the weights (parameters) are such that MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution, then the weight learning algorithm – utilizing MCMC for gradient estimation – is a Fully Polynomial-Time Random Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The paper considers both an unregularized (convex) case and a ridge-regularized (non-convex) case, deriving separate bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, with a few minor typos. I was unable to verify all the proofs in detail, but they appear plausible.

While the theoretical results are impressive, their practical relevance is limited (and the authors appropriately acknowledge this). I question whether it is realistically possible to ensure that the parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems. Have the authors considered whether this condition could be enforced artificially? This condition could be viewed as a form of regularization, and combining it with ridge regression might lead to over-regularized models. If this issue can be addressed, alongside these theoretical results, the paper would be significantly strengthened.

Overall, this is a reasonably well-written paper that formalizes the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning—with and without regularization—yields a FPRAS. However, this condition currently lacks practical applicability. Further, non-trivial research challenges must be overcome before the results presented can lead to a practical and accurate weight learning algorithm.
