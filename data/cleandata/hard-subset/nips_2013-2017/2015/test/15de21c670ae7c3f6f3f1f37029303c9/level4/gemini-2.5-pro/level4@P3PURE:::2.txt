**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel and computationally efficient approach to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. In general, finding the maximum likelihood solution is intractable due to the difficulty of computing the log-partition function, which involves an integral over the sample space. The authors address this by assuming that a suitable MCMC sampler is fast-mixing for all allowed parameters. Under this assumption, they show that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be approximated with sufficient precision. By combining this result with the optimization framework of Schmidt et al. (2011), the paper establishes a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for calculating the MLE. The analysis considers both convex and strongly convex cases, which yield guarantees on the likelihood error and parameter error, respectively. A simple experiment is also included to demonstrate the theory.

**Review**

This is a high-quality and theoretically solid piece of work. The approach is well-motivated as an alternative to methods that rely on restrictive assumptions, such as small treewidth. While the theoretical analysis is a non-trivial combination of existing techniques—namely MCMC mixing bounds, concentration inequalities, and prior results on proximal gradient methods—the synthesis is novel and constitutes a significant contribution.

I appreciated the authors' honest and thoughtful discussion of the method's limitations. The conclusion, in particular, analyzes the weaknesses of the approach, considers how the theoretical assumptions might be overly pessimistic, and outlines valuable directions for future work. The paper also offers useful insights into Contrastive Divergence (CD)-type algorithms, even though the algorithm analyzed is not standard CD.

Despite the overall quality, the clarity of the technical presentation could be improved in a few areas:

1.  The paper is premised on the convexity of the negative log-likelihood, f(theta), yet this is never explicitly stated or justified. A brief explanation would help guide the reader. For instance, the authors could note that the first two terms in eq. 1 are linear (and thus convex) and that the convexity of the log-partition function, A(theta), follows from its Hessian being the covariance matrix of the sufficient statistics, which is always positive semidefinite.
2.  The paper would benefit from a clear, consolidated summary of all its underlying assumptions. For example, the requirement that the parameter set Theta must be convex is a key assumption that is never explicitly mentioned in the main text.

The primary limitation of this work, as the authors acknowledge, is its practical value. The core assumption that MCMC is fast-mixing is extremely challenging to verify in practice for the complex models where this method would be most useful. In a sense, the paper replaces one hard problem (finding the MLE) with another (proving a Markov chain is fast-mixing). That said, establishing this equivalence is in itself a valuable contribution. The ability to convert a difficult optimization problem into a sampling problem provides a new and useful theoretical perspective. For this reason, the results in this paper are an important addition to the literature.

Overall, I found the paper to be an original and substantial contribution. It presents a novel approach for developing conditions under which finding the approximate MLE is tractable and is well-written, despite its seemingly limited immediate practical utility.
