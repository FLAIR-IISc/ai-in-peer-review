

This paper presents a theoretically rigorous approach to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by leveraging fast-mixing Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) under constrained parameter sets. The core insight is that when parameters reside in a "fast-mixing set" (where MCMC mixes rapidly to the stationary distribution), gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood estimate (MLE). The authors establish complexity bounds: cubic in $1/\epsilon$ for the unregularized convex case and quadratic for ridge-regularized strongly convex cases. Crucially, this framework generalizes beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, avoiding the treewidth limitations of prior work (e.g., Chow-Liu trees or high-girth models). The paper builds on Schmidt et al. (2011) for gradient descent with noisy gradients and integrates MCMC mixing-time guarantees, providing a novel theoretical bridge between sampling efficiency and learning guarantees.

**Quality**  
The theoretical analysis is sound and well-executed. The authors correctly establish the Lipschitz continuity of the gradient (Theorem 1) and rigorously bound the convergence rates for both convex (Theorems 2–5) and strongly convex (Theorems 6–9) settings. The proof strategy—combining mixing-time assumptions, concentration inequalities, and existing gradient descent error bounds—is technically robust. A minor omission is the explicit justification for the convexity of the regularized log-likelihood $f(\theta)$ (required for the convex case), though this follows from the positive semi-definiteness of the Hessian (covariance of sufficient statistics). The conclusion thoughtfully addresses limitations: the need for a convex constraint set $\Theta$ (implied but not stated), the computational cost of projection (often negligible but potentially problematic for large models), and the gap between theoretical sampling requirements and practical CD-like algorithms. The example in Section 6 demonstrates practical feasibility but rightly acknowledges the theoretical conservatism of the bounds.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong introduction that contextualizes the work against prior methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, tree-structured constraints). The technical setup (Section 2) is precise, though the omission of the convexity argument for $f(\theta)$ slightly hinders readability. The summary of mixing-time results (Section 3.1) effectively connects prior work (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Liu & Domke, 2014) to the paper’s assumptions. Minor grammatical errors exist (e.g., "w here" in line 185, "of of" in line 281), but these do not impede understanding. The notation is consistent, and the proofs (though deferred to the appendix) are logically structured. The discussion of CD variants (Section 7) provides valuable insight into how the theory relates to common practice.

**Originality**  
This work makes a clear contribution by introducing *fast-mixing parameter sets* as a tractability criterion, distinct from treewidth-based constraints. While the combination of MCMC mixing, gradient descent, and concentration inequalities draws on established techniques, the synthesis is non-trivial and extends beyond prior NIPS work. The paper explicitly contrasts with Heinemann & Globerson (2014) and Abbeel et al. (2006), emphasizing that their approach avoids the "intractable inference" drawback of other methods. Crucially, the generalization to exponential families (not just graphical models) and the formal FPRAS guarantee represent significant advances over prior MCMC-based learning approaches (e.g., Tieleman, 2008; Steinhardt & Liang, 2012), which lack theoretical guarantees. The work is sufficiently distinct from recent NIPS papers on MCMC learning (e.g., Salakhutdinov, 2009; Steinhardt & Liang, 2012).

**Significance**  
The paper’s primary significance lies in its *theoretical contribution* rather than immediate practical impact. It formalizes a path to tractable MLE learning by shifting the computational burden from optimization to MCMC mixing analysis—a shift that, while not eliminating hardness, provides a precise roadmap for when MLE becomes feasible. As noted in the conclusion, proving fast mixing remains challenging (e.g., for Ising models, bounds require small interaction strengths), so the approach replaces one hard problem with another. However, this is a valuable theoretical contribution: it establishes conditions under which MCMC-based learning *can* be guaranteed to converge, which is rare in the literature. For practitioners, the results offer principled guidance for when to apply MCMC-based methods (e.g., for models where fast mixing can be verified) and highlight the limitations of heuristic approaches like contrastive divergence. The work is a strong foundation for future research on tractable learning in complex models.

**Recommendation**  
The paper excels in technical quality and originality, with a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of MLE learning. While its immediate practical utility is limited by the difficulty of verifying fast-mixing assumptions, the theoretical framework is valuable for the community. The clarity is high, and the discussion of limitations is exceptionally thorough. I recommend *acceptance* with minor revisions to explicitly state the convexity of $\Theta$ (a key assumption) and clarify the log-likelihood convexity argument in Section 1. These fixes would strengthen an already strong paper. The work advances the state of the art by providing the first FPRAS for MLE under MCMC-based gradient descent with theoretical guarantees, addressing a fundamental gap in the literature.
