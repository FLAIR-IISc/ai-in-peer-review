The paper introduces a novel and computationally efficient approach to maximum likelihood learning within exponential families. Obtaining the maximum likelihood estimate is generally intractable; from a convex optimization standpoint, the primary difficulty lies in computing an integral with respect to the exponential family parameter. By assuming rapid mixing of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for all permissible parameters, the authors demonstrate that these integrals, required for proximal gradient descent, can be approximated with sufficient accuracy. This, combined with the results of Schmidt et al. (2011), yields a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for calculating the MLE. The analysis covers both convex and strongly convex cases, resulting in distinct guarantees: likelihood error bounds for the former, and parameter error bounds for the latter. A basic experiment validates the theoretical findings.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution. The approach is well-justified as an alternative to methods relying on small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analysis appears robust, although a detailed verification of the proofs was not possible within the scope of this review. The proofs fundamentally involve combining the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and the results from Schmidt et al.; thus, the work represents a nontrivial combination of existing techniques. 

I particularly appreciated the candid discussion of the approach’s limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully analyzes the weaknesses, potential overstringency of the theoretical assumptions, and promising directions for future research. The paper also provides some insight into algorithms of the contrastive divergence type, despite analyzing an algorithm that is not strictly contrastive divergence.

The introduction is well-written and contains comprehensive references to prior work. However, there are minor issues affecting the clarity of the technical presentation. Notably, the paper lacks an explicit statement establishing the convexity of the log-likelihood function. Specifically, the convexity of the second and third terms in equation (1) is readily apparent, and the convexity of A(θ) follows from the positive semi-definiteness of its Hessian, which is equal to the covariance of the sufficient statistics. While seemingly minor, the entire paper rests on the convexity of f(θ), and acknowledging this would aid reader comprehension. A clear summary of the paper’s assumptions is also needed; for instance, the convexity of the parameter set Θ is never explicitly stated in the main text. 

In summary, I consider this to be an original and substantial contribution. However, the practical value of the results appears limited given the inherent difficulty in proving MCMC fast-mixing. In effect, the paper transforms one challenging problem (MLE computation) into another (finding a fast-mixing Markov chain). Nevertheless, such transformations can be valuable, and the availability of these results to the research community is worthwhile.
