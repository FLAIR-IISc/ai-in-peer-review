- Investigates maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by restricting to “fast-mixing parameters” that enable rapid MCMC convergence.
- Provides theoretical guarantees for gradient descent where gradients are approximated by MCMC sampling under the fast-mixing constraint.
- Establishes an FPRAS for ML estimation in the fast-mixing set: ε-accuracy in log-likelihood with effort ∝ (1/ε)^3 unregularized and ∝ (1/ε)^2 with ridge regularization (ignoring logs).
- Framework applies broadly to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models.
- Clearly defines notation and frames learning as minimizing a regularized log-likelihood with intractable exact gradients motivating MCMC.
- Connects to prior MCMC-based learning methods and emphasizes the existing lack of theoretical guarantees for those approaches.
- Reviews mixing-time background, total-variation distance, and the importance of mixing constants C and α for convergence rates.
- Provides Ising-model examples and discusses projection algorithms onto fast-mixing parameter sets.
- Proves Lipschitz continuity of the regularized log-likelihood gradient (Theorem 1).
- Gives convergence guarantees for the convex case (Theorem 2).
- Gives convergence guarantees for the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case (Theorem 6).
- Presents a computational lower bound (Theorem 7) and parameter schedules to achieve target accuracy (Theorems 8 and 9).
- Discusses trade-offs between convex and strongly convex convergence and implications for practical performance.
- Notes limitations: analysis omits projection algorithm complexity and assumes independent MCMC samples.
- Suggests future work including initialization from empirical distribution (contrastive divergence) and more sophisticated sampling strategies.
- Provides a 4x4 Ising-model example showing theoretical bounds are conservative compared to empirical performance.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-written, novel framing, and a significant theoretical contribution to learning in intractable models.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel MCMC-mixing-based tractable class, strong theoretical guarantees, broad applicability, clear presentation, addresses a fundamental problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: Theorem 7 is hard to interpret, practical impact not fully explored, and projection complexity not fully accounted for.
- Reviewer recommendation: accept.
