### Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models where exact inference is intractable. The authors propose an alternative to traditional methods that restrict the model structure (e.g., to trees). Instead, they advocate for constraining the model parameters to a "fast-mixing set," a convex set where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is guaranteed to converge rapidly to the stationary distribution.

The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a common practical algorithm: projected gradient descent where the gradient is approximated using MCMC samples. The authors prove that, under the fast-mixing assumption, this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within the constrained set. They derive explicit, non-asymptotic convergence rates for both the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, specifying the required number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and chain lengths to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly relevant problem in machine learning. Approximating gradients with MCMC is a widely used heuristic for learning intractable models, but it often lacks rigorous theoretical guarantees. Providing a formal analysis for this procedure is a valuable goal.

2.  **Formal Guarantees:** The primary strength is the derivation of explicit, non-asymptotic convergence guarantees. The paper provides a clear recipe for setting hyperparameters (iterations, samples, MCMC steps) to achieve a target accuracy (in log-likelihood or parameter distance) with a specified success probability. This level of rigor is commendable.

3.  **Interesting Perspective:** The concept of defining a tractable set via fast-mixing parameters, rather than structural properties like treewidth, is an interesting and powerful idea. The paper effectively connects theoretical results from the MCMC mixing time literature to the practical problem of learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Engagement with Prior Literature:** The most significant weakness is the paper's apparent lack of awareness of the extensive literature on stochastic approximation (SA) algorithms. The proposed method is a specific instance of a stochastic gradient algorithm with Markovian dynamics, a class of methods that has been studied for decades for optimizing intractable likelihood functions.

    *   The algorithm is strongly related to the Monte Carlo EM (MCEM) algorithm and other stochastic approximation schemes. A well-developed theory exists for these methods, often under much more general conditions than those assumed here. For instance, the classic work of Benveniste, Metivier, and Priouret ("Adaptive Algorithms and Stochastic Approximations", 1990) provides general convergence results for a broad class of such algorithms.

    *   The analysis relies on the assumption of geometric (uniform) ergodicity for all parameter settings within the constraint set Θ. While this enables the derivation of clean non-asymptotic bounds, the existing SA literature provides convergence results under weaker, non-uniform ergodicity assumptions, which are more broadly applicable.

2.  **Impractical Algorithmic Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis is built on assumptions that simplify the proof but are inefficient in practice.

    *   The requirement to re-initialize the MCMC chain from a fixed distribution `r` at each gradient step is a crucial simplifying assumption for the analysis. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion. However, this is not just a minor detail; it is a known inefficient practice. The standard and more effective approach is to use a "warm start" by initializing the current chain from the last state of the previous chain. This strategy has also been extensively analyzed in the SA literature, and the authors should compare their approach and results to this body of work. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the practical relevance of the derived bounds.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The presentation of the results could be improved for clarity and rigor. For example, key results like Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 do not formally state all the assumptions under which they hold. The reader must infer these from the surrounding text. Best practice would be to explicitly list all necessary conditions in the theorem statements themselves.

4.  **Novelty:** Given the extensive prior work on stochastic approximation, the novelty of the core results is limited. While deriving explicit, non-asymptotic rates is a contribution, its value is diminished by the reliance on strong assumptions (uniform mixing, chain restarts) and the lack of comparison to prior, more general theoretical frameworks. The authors need to more clearly articulate what their analysis provides that was not already understood from the existing SA literature.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses an important practical problem with a rigorous theoretical approach.
*   It provides explicit, non-asymptotic convergence rates for an MCMC-based learning algorithm, which is a valuable type of result.
*   The idea of constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing set" is conceptually appealing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper fails to connect with and build upon the substantial and highly relevant literature on stochastic approximation algorithms.
*   The novelty is questionable, as convergence for similar (and more general) algorithms has long been established.
*   The analysis relies on strong and practically inefficient assumptions (e.g., restarting the MCMC chain at each step) without adequately justifying them against more standard, analyzed techniques.

### Conclusion

In summary, while the paper tackles a relevant problem and the technical analysis appears sound within its own framework, its primary weakness is a significant lack of engagement with the extensive and well-developed literature on stochastic approximation. The results, therefore, seem only marginally novel. Before this work can be considered a significant contribution to the field, the authors must thoroughly situate their results within the context of prior work, clearly articulating what is new and why their more restrictive assumptions are necessary or lead to qualitatively stronger guarantees than existing analyses. Based on the limited originality and its disconnection from established literature, I lean towards rejection.
