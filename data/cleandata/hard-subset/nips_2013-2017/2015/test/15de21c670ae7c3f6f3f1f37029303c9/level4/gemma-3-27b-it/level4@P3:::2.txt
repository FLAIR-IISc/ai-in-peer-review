This paper proposes a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. In general, finding the maximum likelihood solution is intractable. The authors address this by demonstrating that, under the assumption of fast-mixing Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for all allowed parameters, the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be approximated with sufficient precision to obtain a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for calculating the MLE. Both the convex and strongly convex cases are considered, leading to different guarantees—the former on the likelihood error, the latter on the parameter error. A simple experiment demonstrating the theory is also included.

Overall, this is a high-quality piece of work. The approach is well-motivated as an alternative to relying on small-treewidth assumptions, a common strategy for tractability in graphical models. The theoretical analyses appear solid, though I haven’t checked the proofs in detail. The results primarily build upon existing techniques—combining the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and the results of Schmidt et al. (2011)—but this combination is nontrivial and provides a valuable theoretical contribution.

I particularly appreciated the honest discussion of the approach's limitations. The conclusion thoughtfully analyzes weaknesses, potential over-pessimism in the theoretical assumptions, and directions for future work. The paper also offers some insight into contrastive divergence (CD)-type algorithms, even though the algorithm analyzed is not strictly CD. 

The introduction is well-written and provides substantial references to previous work. However, there are a few points regarding clarity of technical presentation. A key omission is an explicit statement that the log-likelihood is convex. While the convexity is inherent in the form of the objective function (Equation 1), explicitly stating this fact—highlighting that the second and third terms are convex and the Hessian of A(θ) is positive semi-definite due to it representing a covariance—would improve the reader’s understanding and flow of the argument. Furthermore, a clearer summary of the paper’s core assumptions would be beneficial. For example, the convexity of the parameter set Θ is crucial but not explicitly stated in the main text.

Overall, I found this paper to be an original and substantial contribution. However, the practical value of the results may be limited. Proving fast mixing of MCMC is extremely challenging, potentially replacing one difficult problem (finding the MLE) with another (finding a fast-mixing Markov chain). Nevertheless, framing the problem in this way is often valuable, and making these results available to the community is worthwhile. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Presents a new perspective on tractable maximum likelihood learning, moving beyond treewidth constraints.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis appears sound and builds on established results.
*   **Honest Discussion:** The authors acknowledge limitations and potential areas for improvement.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and the motivation for the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality:** The reliance on proving fast mixing could limit the immediate applicability of the results.
*   **Clarity (Minor):** A few points in the technical presentation could be clarified (convexity of the log-likelihood, explicit statement of assumptions).
*   **Typographical errors:** Some minor typos exist (see below).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference.
*   The theoretical results are sound and contribute to our understanding of maximum likelihood learning.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented, despite minor areas for improvement.
*   The work provides a useful bridge between MCMC theory and optimization algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical impact of the results may be limited by the difficulty of verifying the fast-mixing assumption.
*   The paper is largely theoretical and lacks extensive empirical validation.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper. While the practical challenges of verifying the fast-mixing assumption are significant, the theoretical contribution is substantial and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. The work advances our understanding of maximum likelihood learning and provides a valuable foundation for future research.
