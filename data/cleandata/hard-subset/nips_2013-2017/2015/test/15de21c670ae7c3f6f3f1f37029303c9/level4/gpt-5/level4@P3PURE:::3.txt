A key problem in Markov networks is weight learning, typically defined as finding weights (attached to features) that maximize the likelihood. Gradient-based methods are commonly used, and a crucial subtask is computing the gradient. Because exact gradient computation is NP-hard in general, MCMC methods are often employed instead.

The paper derives theoretical results showing that if the parameters are such that MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution for those parameters, then the weight-learning algorithm that uses MCMC to compute the gradient is an FPRAS. The analysis covers two settings: the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized (non-convex) case, with separate bounds for each.

The paper is generally well written, with a few typos. I could not verify all the proofs, but they appear plausible.

Although the theoretical results are impressive, they seem to have little to no practical relevance (and I appreciate that the authors acknowledge this). In practice, it appears virtually impossible to ensure that the parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems. Have the authors considered whether these constraints can be enforced artificially? The condition can be viewed as a form of regularization, and using ridge regression in addition to it may yield over-regularized models. If this latter issue can be addressed, then together with these theoretical results the paper would be very strong.

Overall, this is a reasonably well-written paper that formalizes the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields an FPRAS. However, the central condition appears to have little practical relevance. Non-trivial research problems need to be addressed before the results in the paper can lead to a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning.
