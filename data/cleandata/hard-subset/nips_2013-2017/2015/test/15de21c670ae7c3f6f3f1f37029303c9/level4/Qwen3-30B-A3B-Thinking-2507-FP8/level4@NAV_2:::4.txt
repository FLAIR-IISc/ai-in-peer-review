

The paper introduces a novel framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing sets," where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. This approach contrasts with traditional tractable sets like tree-structured models (Chow-Liu [1968]) or well-specified model assumptions (pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22]), which often fail under model misspecification or yield intractable inference. The work generalizes prior mixing-time results (e.g., Domke & Liu [2013] for Ising models) to the exponential family setting, establishing theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. It builds upon foundational mixing-time analysis [19, 26] but extends it to learning contexts with explicit convergence rates, addressing a gap in the literature on MCMC-based learning for intractable models.  

**Quality:** The theoretical results are technically sound, with rigorous convergence bounds for both unregularized (Theorem 2–5) and ridge-regularized (Theorem 6–9) cases. The analysis correctly handles gradient approximation errors via mixing time assumptions and applies Schmidt et al. [26] for gradient descent with noisy gradients. The primary concern is Theorem 7’s lower bound, which uses abstract parameters (a, b, c, α) without clear interpretation for practical learning settings. An expanded discussion linking these parameters to model characteristics (e.g., interaction strengths, graph density) would strengthen the argument.  

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear definitions, logical flow, and effective use of examples (Section 6). The notation is consistent, and the proof sketches provide sufficient intuition without overwhelming detail.  

**Originality:** The framing of "fast-mixing parameter sets" as a tractable class for learning is novel. While mixing times have been studied in MCMC theory [19, 28], this work uniquely applies them to maximum likelihood learning with convergence guarantees, distinguishing it from prior approaches (e.g., contrastive divergence [5] or pseudolikelihood). The results extend beyond graphical models to general exponential families, a significant departure from related work on tree-structured learning [19, 20, 30].  

**Significance:** The paper fills a critical gap by providing the first theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth models. While the immediate practical impact may be limited (as noted in Section 6), the framework enables theoretically sound learning in scenarios where tree-structured approximations fail (e.g., misspecified models with dense graphs). The results could inspire future work on efficient MCMC-based learning in complex models (e.g., deep energy-based models [14]), though the submission would benefit from a stronger discussion of concrete applications (e.g., "This framework could facilitate learning in protein structure prediction [32] where treewidth is prohibitive").  

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel theoretical framework, rigorous convergence rates, addresses a well-identified gap in learning for intractable models, technically sound analysis.  
*Con:* Significance section lacks concrete impact discussion; Theorem 7’s lower bound is impractical for practitioners.  

The paper meets all NIPS quality standards. The minor weaknesses are easily addressable in revision, and the contribution is a meaningful advancement in theoretical learning for exponential families. Accept.
