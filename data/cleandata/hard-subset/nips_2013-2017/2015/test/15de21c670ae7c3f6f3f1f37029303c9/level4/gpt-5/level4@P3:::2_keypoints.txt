- Paper studies MLE in exponential families when exact inference is intractable by restricting parameters to a fast-mixing set Θ and using projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated expectations.  
- Main theoretical result: high-probability approximation guarantees to the MLE over Θ with explicit complexity schedules for convex and strongly convex cases.  
- Complexity: unregularized objective ε-accuracy with sampling work ~1/ε^3 (up to logs); ridge-regularized parameter ε-accuracy with work ~1/ε^2 (up to logs), yielding an FPRAS.  
- Novelty: framing learning via certified fast-mixing parameter sets and providing end-to-end MLE approximation guarantees beyond low-treewidth models.  
- Generality: applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not limited to specific graphical models.  
- Rigorous handling of biased, sampling-based gradients with clean schedules (K iterations, M samples, v chain length) for both convex and strongly convex regimes.  
- Thoughtful synthesis of mixing-time theory, concentration inequalities, and inexact gradient methods; connects to many prior works (pseudolikelihood, score matching, CD, MCMC-ML).  
- Discussion links to practice (e.g., relation to contrastive divergence and initialization choices) and acknowledges limitations.  
- Strength: conceptually principled alternative to low-treewidth assumptions by learning within certified fast-mixing regions.  
- Strength: clear, carefully executed proofs combining standard tools in a nontrivial way.  
- Weakness: practical impact limited by difficulty of certifying fast mixing and computing projections onto Θ for realistic models; constants C, α are hard to obtain and conservative.  
- Weakness: analysis requires independent fresh samples per iteration (no persistence), differing from effective heuristics like persistent CD.  
- Weakness: minimal experimental validation; lacks empirical trade-off studies and comparisons to baselines (pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood).  
- Weakness: a few clarity omissions and minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., R2 vs R2^2, roles of C, α, L).  
- Evaluation — Quality: technically sound with clear assumptions; main lever assumptions should be stated up front (bounded statistics, computable projection, mixing-rate form, sample independence).  
- Evaluation — Clarity: generally well written but should state convexity and Θ convexity early and unify notation/constants.  
- Evaluation — Originality: moderate-to-high; components exist but the fast-mixing set framing and resulting FPRAS are novel.  
- Evaluation — Significance: primarily theoretical; clarifies when MCMC-based learning can be made principled but practical deployment is constrained.  
- Suggestions: explicitly state key assumptions early (convexity, Θ convexity, bounded statistics, known mixing-rate constants) and summarize constants and how to obtain them.  
- Suggestions: provide more concrete Θ/projection examples beyond Ising, discuss robustness to approximate projections, and address dependence vs. fresh-sample assumptions.  
- Suggestions: expand empirical section to compare to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood and show realized vs predicted schedules.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel tractable-set perspective, strong theoretical guarantees with explicit schedules, relevant to community themes.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited practical applicability without mixing certification/projection tools, weak empirical validation, some clarity gaps.  
- Minor typos: several small editorial fixes suggested (e.g., “,w here” → “, where”; “this paper that” → “this paper assumes that”; other phrasing and duplication errors).  
- Overall recommendation: solid theoretical contribution, leans toward acceptance despite constrained practical impact due to certification/projection challenges.
