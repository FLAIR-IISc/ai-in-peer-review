

The paper introduces a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by leveraging fast-mixing Markov chains, offering a significant alternative to traditional treewidth-based constraints. Instead of restricting to tree-structured models (as in Chow-Liu), the authors propose constraining parameters to sets where MCMC converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. This work builds on foundational results in mixing times (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Domke & Liu, 2013) and combines them with gradient descent analysis (Schmidt et al., 2011) to establish theoretical guarantees for learning.

**Quality:** The technical quality is strong. The authors rigorously prove that for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, gradient descent with MCMC approximation yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation. The analysis covers both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (regularized) cases, with distinct guarantees (likelihood error vs. parameter error). The proofs combine established techniques (mixing time bounds, concentration inequalities) in a nontrivial way, with sufficient proof sketches to convey the core ideas. The paper carefully identifies and justifies key assumptions, including bounded sufficient statistics and a convex constraint set Θ (though the convexity of Θ is not explicitly stated in the main text, it's used in the projection operator definition).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, with a strong introduction that properly contextualizes the work relative to pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihood methods. However, two technical points could be clarified: First, the convexity of the negative log-likelihood f(θ) is fundamental to the analysis but isn't explicitly stated (though implied in the proof sketches). A brief explanation that f(θ) is convex because A(θ) is convex (as its Hessian is the covariance of sufficient statistics, hence positive semidefinite) would strengthen the argument. Second, the convexity of Θ is assumed but not explicitly stated in the main text, which would help readers understand the projection operation.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant original contribution by proposing a fundamentally different notion of tractability (fast-mixing parameters) rather than relying on structural constraints like small treewidth. While the techniques used (mixing times, gradient descent with errors) are known, their combination to address maximum likelihood learning is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by addressing the well-specified model limitation of methods like pseudolikelihood and composite likelihood, and by providing theoretical guarantees for when MLE becomes tractable.

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is valuable, though practical applicability is limited by the challenge of verifying fast mixing for specific models. The paper acknowledges this limitation, noting that it replaces one hard problem (MLE) with another (finding fast-mixing parameter sets). However, the theoretical framework provides a clear path for when MLE becomes tractable and offers insights into contrastive divergence algorithms, which are widely used in practice. This work advances our understanding of the conditions under which maximum likelihood learning can be efficiently performed, making it a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and includes a thoughtful discussion of limitations. The authors honestly address the practical challenges of verifying fast mixing and provide directions for future work. The connection to contrastive divergence algorithms is particularly insightful, offering theoretical justification for a widely used practical approach.

**Weaknesses:** The limited practical value due to the difficulty of proving fast mixing is a genuine limitation. The example section, while illustrative, doesn't strongly demonstrate practical utility. Additionally, the paper would benefit from explicitly stating the convexity of f(θ) and Θ to improve clarity.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of tractable maximum likelihood learning. While the practical applicability is constrained by the challenge of verifying fast mixing, the theoretical framework is valuable and well-executed. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly written (with minor clarity improvements suggested). I recommend acceptance for its substantial theoretical contribution to the field.
