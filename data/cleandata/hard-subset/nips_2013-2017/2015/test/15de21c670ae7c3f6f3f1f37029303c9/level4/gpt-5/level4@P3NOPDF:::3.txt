This paper studies weight learning in Markov networks when gradients are approximated by MCMC. The main contribution is a set of theoretical results showing that, under assumptions guaranteeing rapid mixing of the MCMC sampler at the current parameters, the standard gradient-based learning procedure becomes a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for optimizing the learning objective. The paper treats two settings—unregularized and ridge-regularized—and provides separate bounds for each.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: exact gradient computation is NP-hard; MCMC is the common workaround.
- Clean theoretical statement: if MCMC mixes quickly for the parameters encountered during learning, then the overall procedure admits polynomial-time accuracy guarantees.
- The separation of unregularized and ridge-regularized cases is helpful, and the derivations appear technically sound. While I did not fully verify every proof, the arguments look plausible and align with standard tools in Markov chain analysis.

Limitations and concerns
- Practical relevance is limited by the central assumption: ensuring that the iterates remain in a fast-mixing region is typically infeasible to certify or enforce in realistic models. The authors note this, but the issue is fundamental for applicability.
- The results rely on properties (e.g., spectral gap, contractivity, or high-temperature conditions) that are rarely checkable from data or known a priori during learning. Without a verifiable or enforceable condition, the FPRAS guarantee is mainly of theoretical interest.
- It is unclear how sensitive the bounds are to constants hidden in the mixing-time assumptions. Even if the dependence is polynomial, large constants could make the method impractical.
- The presentation could better clarify exactly what the FPRAS is for (the objective value, the parameters within ε of optimum, or both), and the precise dependence on model size, graph structure, feature norms, and accuracy/confidence parameters.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Enforcing fast mixing: Can one design a constrained or projected optimization scheme that keeps parameters in a known fast-mixing set (e.g., defined by a Dobrushin-type condition, bounded interaction strengths, or contractive couplings)? A projection step onto an L∞-bounded region that implies uniqueness/high temperature would be a concrete direction. Please discuss feasibility and the trade-off with statistical accuracy.
- Regularization: If the fast-mixing constraint acts like an implicit regularizer, how does it interact with ridge regularization? Is there a principled way to tune both so as to avoid over-regularization? Any guidance (theoretical or empirical) would be valuable.
- Diagnostics and adaptivity: Are there practical diagnostics to detect violations of the fast-mixing assumption during learning (e.g., autocorrelation tests, multiple-chain Gelman–Rubin statistics, or coupling-based heuristics), and adapt step sizes or constraints accordingly? Even heuristic safeguards could broaden applicability.
- Scope of the results: Do the bounds extend to other regularizers (e.g., L1 for sparsity) or to approximate objectives like pseudolikelihood? Clarifying the boundary of applicability would strengthen the paper.
- Complexity details: Please state explicitly the polynomial dependence of runtime/sample complexity on 1/ε, log(1/δ), number of variables, maximum degree, and any norm bounds on features/parameters. If warm starts materially affect the guarantees, note this as well.
- Empirical illustration: A small synthetic experiment demonstrating that a projection or constraint that implies fast mixing (e.g., bounded couplings) does not destroy predictive performance would help bridge theory and practice.

Minor comments
- The writing is generally clear; a careful proofread would fix a few typos and minor grammatical slips.
- It would help to collect and restate the mixing assumptions in one place, with concrete sufficient conditions (e.g., Dobrushin’s condition) and how they relate to parameter norms.

Overall assessment
The paper offers a well-written and conceptually clean analysis: if MCMC mixes rapidly along the learning trajectory, then gradient-based weight learning in Markov networks admits FPRAS-type guarantees, with results stated for both unregularized and ridge-regularized settings. However, without a practical mechanism to verify or enforce fast mixing during training, the results have limited immediate impact on practice. If the authors can (i) propose and analyze a tractable constraint/projection scheme that keeps parameters in a provably fast-mixing region, or (ii) provide practical diagnostics and heuristics that reliably maintain mixing without excessive bias, the work would become significantly stronger. As it stands, the paper is a solid theoretical contribution that clarifies when MCMC-based learning can be made computationally robust, but further nontrivial work is needed to translate these insights into practical, accurate algorithms.
