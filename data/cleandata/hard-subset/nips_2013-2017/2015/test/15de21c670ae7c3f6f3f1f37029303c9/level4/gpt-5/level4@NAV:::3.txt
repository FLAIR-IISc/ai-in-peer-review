A central challenge in Markov networks is weight learning, typically framed as selecting feature weights that maximize the likelihood. In practice, gradient-based optimization is used, and a crucial subroutine is computing the gradient. Because this is NP-hard in general, MCMC methods are often employed instead of exact computation.

The paper establishes theoretical guarantees showing that if the parameters are such that MCMC mixes rapidly to the stationary distribution under them, then the weight-learning algorithm (which uses MCMC to estimate the gradient) constitutes an FPRAS. It examines two settings: the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized (non-convex) case, deriving distinct bounds for each.

The manuscript is generally well written, with a few typographical errors. I could not check all proofs in detail, but they appear plausible.

Although the theoretical results are impressive, their practical relevance seems limited (and I appreciate the authors acknowledging this). It appears virtually impossible to guarantee that the parameters satisfy the theorems’ assumptions. Have the authors considered this? Specifically, can the condition be enforced artificially? One can view the condition as a form of regularization, and combining it with ridge could lead to over-regularized models. If this latter issue can be resolved, together with these theoretical results, this would be a very strong paper.

Overall, a reasonably well-written paper that delineates the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields an FPRAS. However, this condition appears to have little practical relevance. Non-trivial research problems must be addressed before these results can lead to a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning.
