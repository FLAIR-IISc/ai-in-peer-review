### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families, particularly those with high treewidth where inference and learning are typically intractable. The primary computational bottleneck in maximum likelihood estimation for these models is the calculation of the gradient of the log-partition function, which requires computing an expectation over the model's distribution. This paper tackles this challenge by constraining the model parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are guaranteed to converge quickly to the stationary distribution.

By leveraging this assumption, the authors demonstrate that the likelihood gradient can be approximated with sufficient precision using a practical number of MCMC samples. They then connect this to existing results on the convergence of gradient descent with inexact gradients [Schmidt et al., 2011] to prove that this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood estimate within the constrained set. The analysis is provided for both the standard convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, yielding guarantees on the sub-optimality of the log-likelihood and the parameter error, respectively. The theoretical results are supported by a small-scale experiment on an Ising model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Perspective on Tractability:** The paper provides a valuable and fundamentally different notion of tractability for learning in graphical models. Instead of relying on structural constraints like low treewidth, it focuses on dynamic properties of the model, i.e., the mixing time of an associated MCMC sampler. This is a creative and well-motivated approach.

2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's main contribution is a solid theoretical analysis that formally justifies a widely used heuristic: using MCMC to approximate gradients for maximum likelihood learning. While the analysis builds upon a combination of established techniques—namely, MCMC mixing time bounds, concentration inequalities, and convergence results for stochastic gradient methods—the synthesis is novel, non-trivial, and provides a valuable new perspective. It rigorously specifies the required number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and burn-in iterations to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.

3.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a strong motivation by situating the work within the existing literature. The authors are commendably transparent about the limitations of their work, offering a thoughtful discussion in the conclusion on the challenges of projection, sample initialization, and ways in which the theoretical assumptions might be conservative.

4.  **Relevance to Existing Heuristics:** The analysis provides formal insight into related algorithms like Contrastive Divergence (CD), even though the algorithm analyzed is not identical. It helps build a theoretical foundation for understanding why and when such MCMC-based learning procedures might succeed.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Practical Applicability:** The primary conceptual limitation is that the work effectively trades one difficult problem (intractable inference for MLE) for another (defining and projecting onto a set of parameters that guarantees fast mixing). Proving fast-mixing bounds for non-trivial models is notoriously difficult. While the authors provide some examples where this is possible (e.g., Ising models with bounded interaction strengths), the practical applicability of the framework seems limited to cases where such sets are already known. That said, formally connecting these two problems is a valuable scientific contribution in itself.

2.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The technical presentation could be slightly improved by explicitly stating all key assumptions upfront.
    *   The entire optimization framework relies on the convexity of the negative log-likelihood function `f(theta)`. While this is a standard result (the Hessian of the log-partition function A(theta) is the covariance matrix of the sufficient statistics and thus positive semi-definite), explicitly stating and briefly justifying this property in Section 2 would strengthen the paper's logical flow and make it more accessible.
    *   The requirement that the constraint set `Theta` must be convex is crucial for the projection step in the algorithm but is not mentioned in the main body of the paper until Section 3.1. A summary of all assumptions (bounded sufficient statistics, fast-mixing convex parameter set, etc.) early in the paper would be beneficial.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes an original and substantial theoretical contribution. It formalizes the intuition that if MCMC mixes quickly, it can be used for provably-correct maximum likelihood learning. While the practical value may be limited by the difficulty of identifying fast-mixing parameter sets, the framework itself is elegant and important. The paper establishes a valuable connection between the dynamics of sampling and the complexity of learning, which could inspire future work. I recommend acceptance.
