This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where exact inference for computing the likelihood gradient is intractable. A common practical solution is to approximate the gradient using samples from a Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm. However, this widely-used heuristic generally lacks theoretical guarantees of convergence.

The main contribution of this work is to provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for this approach. The authors prove that if the model parameters are constrained to a "fast-mixing set"—a set where MCMC is guaranteed to converge rapidly to its stationary distribution—then projected gradient descent using MCMC-approximated gradients is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within that set. The analysis provides explicit convergence bounds for two distinct scenarios: the unregularized objective (a convex problem) and the ridge-regularized objective (a strongly convex problem).

The paper is well-written and the theoretical results are impressive. The core arguments appear plausible, though I have not verified every detail of the proofs. Providing a formal justification for a popular and practical learning method is a significant contribution.

The primary weakness of the paper, however, lies in the practical applicability of its core assumption. The theoretical guarantees hinge on the ability to constrain and project parameters onto a known fast-mixing set. For many complex models of practical interest, defining this set and performing the projection is likely to be extremely difficult, if not impossible. The authors are commendably upfront about this limitation, positioning the work as a primarily theoretical contribution.

This leads to a key question: have the authors considered how one might enforce these fast-mixing constraints in a more general setting? The paper discusses projection for Ising models where such sets are well-characterized, but the extension to other models is not obvious. One could view the constraint itself as a form of regularization. If so, how does it interact with an explicit regularizer like the L2 penalty used in the strongly convex case? It seems possible that their combination could lead to an over-regularized model. If this challenge of practically enforcing the fast-mixing property could be solved, it would elevate this work from a strong theoretical paper to one with significant practical impact.

In summary, this is a well-executed paper that formalizes the conditions under which MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning is provably efficient. The theoretical results are novel and significant. However, the strong assumptions about knowing and being able to project onto a fast-mixing parameter set currently limit the direct practical relevance of these findings. Substantial further research is needed to bridge the gap between these theoretical results and a broadly applicable learning algorithm.
