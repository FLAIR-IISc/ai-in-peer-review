

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in undirected graphical models: maximum likelihood learning is computationally intractable due to the difficulty of computing partition functions. While previous work has explored tractable approaches like tree-structured parameter constraints (Chow-Liu algorithm), pseudolikelihood, score matching, and high-girth models, these methods often suffer from limitations in sample complexity, require well-specified models, or result in distributions where inference remains intractable.

The authors propose a novel approach based on "fast-mixing parameter sets" - parameter constraints that guarantee Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) convergence to the stationary distribution in polynomial time. They prove that when parameters are constrained to such a set, gradient descent using MCMC approximations for the gradient yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning. The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, establishing precise bounds on the computational complexity required to achieve desired accuracy.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with consistent notation and thorough mathematical treatment. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem within existing literature, particularly referencing seminal works on partition function complexity (Jerrum and Sinclair [1993]) and tractable learning approaches (Chow-Liu [1968], Heinemann and Globerson [2014]). The technical contributions are significant, as they establish the first theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning under specific parameter constraints.

The theoretical results are impressive and address an important gap in the literature. The authors provide a detailed analysis of how to balance the number of gradient descent steps, MCMC samples, and Markov chain transitions to achieve desired accuracy. The convex and strongly convex convergence analyses are particularly strong, with careful attention to the interplay between mixing rates, gradient errors, and optimization convergence.

However, the paper's main limitation is the practical relevance of its theoretical results. The authors rightly acknowledge that the fast-mixing parameter constraints are highly restrictive in practice. While the paper demonstrates that these constraints enable theoretical guarantees, it does not provide a practical mechanism for enforcing them in real-world applications. The reviewer is concerned that attempting to enforce these constraints (e.g., through additional regularization) could lead to over-regularized models that significantly degrade predictive performance.

The paper's theoretical contribution is valuable for understanding the theoretical boundaries of MCMC-based learning, but the conditions required for the fast-mixing parameter sets appear nearly impossible to satisfy without substantial model simplification. The authors do not address how to identify or enforce these constraints in practical scenarios, nor do they provide evidence that such constraints can be maintained while still learning meaningful models. The example in Section 6, while illustrative, relies on an artificial constraint (|θij| ≤ 0.2) that would likely not generalize to real-world problems.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to our understanding of when MCMC-based learning is theoretically sound, but the practical implementation challenges are not adequately addressed. The theoretical results would be significantly strengthened if the authors could demonstrate a practical method for enforcing the fast-mixing constraints without introducing excessive regularization. Without addressing this gap, the results remain largely of theoretical interest rather than providing a practical algorithm for maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth models.

The paper's strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis, clear presentation of complex mathematical concepts, and significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning. The main weakness is the limited practical relevance of the theoretical results, as the required constraints appear difficult to enforce in real-world applications without compromising model quality.

In terms of the four review criteria:
- Quality: The paper provides sound theoretical analysis with well-defined assumptions and thorough mathematical treatment. The proofs appear plausible and address important gaps in the literature.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written with clear organization and consistent notation. The mathematical exposition is accessible to experts in the field.
- Originality: The concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" represents a novel approach to addressing the intractability of maximum likelihood learning, distinct from previous tractable approaches.
- Significance: While the theoretical contribution is significant, the practical significance is limited by the restrictive nature of the required constraints. The paper does not provide a pathway to practical implementation.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding the conditions under which MCMC-based learning methods can be guaranteed to converge. However, the practical relevance of these results is questionable without a method to enforce the fast-mixing parameter constraints in real-world applications. The paper would benefit from addressing this gap, as it would transform a theoretically interesting result into a practically useful framework for maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth models.
