### Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models with high treewidth, where exact inference is intractable. While prior work often restricts the model structure to be tractable (e.g., tree-structured models via the Chow-Liu algorithm), this paper proposes a fundamentally different notion of tractability. It defines a tractable parameter set as one where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing," meaning the chain rapidly converges to its stationary distribution.

The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the widely used heuristic of performing projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. The authors prove that if the model parameters are constrained to a convex, fast-mixing set, this procedure is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within that set. The analysis provides explicit convergence rates and schedules for setting the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples (M), and MCMC transitions (v). The paper presents separate results for the standard convex (unregularized) case and the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case, demonstrating faster convergence for the latter.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and valuable piece of theoretical work. The results provide formal guarantees for a common and practical algorithm that has, until now, largely lacked a strong theoretical foundation.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The theoretical analysis is rigorous, carefully bounding the error introduced by biased MCMC gradient estimates and relating it to the overall convergence of the optimization procedure.
    *   The results are meaningful, providing explicit schedules for the algorithm's hyperparameters (K, M, v) required to achieve a desired accuracy (ϵ) with high probability (1-δ).
    *   The authors are careful to distinguish between the convex and strongly convex cases, deriving appropriate bounds for each that highlight the benefits of regularization.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   While the upper bounds on convergence (Theorems 5 and 9) are the main results, the corresponding lower bound for the strongly convex case (Theorem 7) is difficult to interpret. The theorem statement is dense, and the paper would benefit from an expanded discussion of its implications and how it relates to the derived upper bounds. A more intuitive explanation of the fundamental limits of this learning problem would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature, effectively contrasting the proposed "algorithmic tractability" with traditional "structural tractability."
    *   The notation is established clearly in Section 2, which serves as a helpful reference.
    *   The separation of the analysis into convex and strongly convex sections makes the theoretical development easy to follow.
    *   The example in Section 6, while simple, serves as a useful sanity check and illustrates how the theoretical bounds can be instantiated in practice.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is highly original.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The central concept of defining a tractable model class based on the mixing time of MCMC samplers is a novel and powerful idea. It reframes the problem of learning intractable models in a very insightful way.
    *   To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide rigorous, non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning by constraining the parameter space to ensure rapid mixing. This provides a formal justification for a long-standing heuristic.

**Significance:** The paper's significance lies in its potential to bridge the gap between the theory and practice of learning intractable models.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The results are important for the community because they provide a formal language and a set of theoretical tools to analyze a very common class of learning algorithms. Researchers who use MCMC to approximate likelihood gradients can now better understand the conditions under which their methods are guaranteed to succeed.
    *   The work opens up new research directions. It encourages the development of new models and parameter constraints that are explicitly designed to have good mixing properties, rather than relying on structural simplicity alone.

*   **Weaknesses/Suggestions for Improvement:**
    *   The paper could be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of its potential impact and practical implications. For instance, while several examples of fast-mixing parameter sets are given, the task of identifying or designing such sets for novel, complex models remains a major challenge. A discussion of future work on how to construct or learn these projections for more general model classes would be valuable. The current conclusion feels slightly brief and could be expanded to better outline opportunities for others to build on this work.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** The paper introduces a new and insightful definition of a "tractable" model class based on fast MCMC mixing, shifting the focus from structural to algorithmic properties.
2.  **Important Theoretical Guarantees:** It provides the first rigorous, non-asymptotic analysis for the widely-used heuristic of learning with MCMC-approximated gradients, filling a significant gap in the literature.
3.  **High Technical Quality:** The analysis is sound, detailed, and leads to explicit and meaningful convergence rates.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Practicality:** The paper focuses heavily on the theory and could do more to address the practical challenges of identifying and projecting onto fast-mixing parameter sets for new and complex models.
2.  **Clarity of Lower Bounds:** The presentation and interpretation of the lower bounds (specifically Theorem 7) could be improved to provide more intuition.

### Final Recommendation

The arguments in favor of acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. The paper's conceptual novelty and its significant theoretical contribution of providing guarantees for a widely used practical algorithm make it a clear accept for NIPS. It provides a solid foundation that future theoretical and practical work can build upon.
