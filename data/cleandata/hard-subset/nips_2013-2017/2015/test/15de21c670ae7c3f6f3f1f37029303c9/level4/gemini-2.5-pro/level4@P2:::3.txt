This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models with high treewidth. The standard approach involves gradient-based optimization, but computing the true gradient is often infeasible as it requires calculating expectations over the model's distribution. A common and pragmatic solution is to approximate these expectations using samples from a Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method. While widely used in practice, this heuristic approach lacks formal guarantees, as the MCMC samples are drawn from a distribution that only approximates the true stationary distribution, introducing bias into the gradient estimate.

The main contribution of this work is to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of this procedure. The authors introduce the novel concept of a "fast-mixing parameter set"—a set of parameters for which an MCMC sampler is guaranteed to converge to its stationary distribution at a geometric rate. By constraining the learning problem to such a set, the paper proves that gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within that set. This result is significant because it shifts the notion of tractability from graphical structure (e.g., low treewidth) to algorithmic properties (fast mixing). The analysis is general, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, and provides separate convergence guarantees for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem at the intersection of theory and practice. The idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets, rather than structural properties like treewidth, is a novel and insightful contribution. It provides a formal justification for a widely used heuristic and establishes a new framework for thinking about tractable learning in complex models. This work bridges a significant gap between the practical application of MCMC-based learning and its theoretical understanding.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are impressive and appear to be sound. The authors provide a detailed analysis deriving explicit bounds on the total computational effort required to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. The proofs, while not verified line-by-line, follow a clear and logical structure, building upon established results in convex optimization and concentration inequalities. The separate treatment of the convex and strongly convex cases is thorough and highlights the different convergence behaviors.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly articulates the problem, the proposed approach, and the main results. The motivation is established effectively by contrasting the proposed method with previous work on tractable learning (e.g., Chow-Liu, pseudolikelihood). The setup and notation are defined precisely, and the theorems are stated clearly.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Practical Relevance and Assumptions:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the strong assumption that one can efficiently identify and project onto a meaningful "fast-mixing parameter set" Θ. While the authors acknowledge this and provide examples for the Ising model where such projection is possible, the broader applicability of this assumption is a major concern. For many complex models of interest, defining such a set and performing the projection may be as difficult as the original learning problem. The practical significance of the results is therefore conditional on solving this potentially non-trivial prerequisite.

2.  **Exploration of Alternative Formulations:** The constraint `θ ∈ Θ` is treated as a hard constraint enforced by projection. This raises the question of whether this condition could be relaxed. For instance, could the fast-mixing property be encouraged via a regularization term rather than a strict projection? This might lead to a more flexible and practical algorithm that still benefits from the theoretical insights of this work. Exploring such alternatives, perhaps in the discussion, would strengthen the paper by connecting its theoretical core to more practical algorithmic possibilities. The current formulation also risks creating over-regularized models, as it is used in conjunction with a standard ridge penalty.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that provides the first formal convergence guarantees for the common practice of using MCMC to approximate gradients in maximum likelihood learning for exponential families. The conceptual framing around "fast-mixing parameter sets" is novel and significant.

The main limitation is the strong assumption regarding the feasibility of projecting onto such sets, which raises questions about the immediate practical impact of the results. However, as a foundational theoretical contribution that formalizes and provides insight into a widely used heuristic, the paper is of high value to the NIPS community. It lays the groundwork for future research that may bridge the gap to more practical applications. The strengths in originality and technical quality substantially outweigh the concerns about immediate practicality. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
