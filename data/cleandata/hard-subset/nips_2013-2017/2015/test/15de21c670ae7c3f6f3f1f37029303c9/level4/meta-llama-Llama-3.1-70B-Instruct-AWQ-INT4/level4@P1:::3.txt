This paper addresses a key problem in Markov networks: weight learning, which involves finding a setting of weights that maximizes the likelihood. Typically, a gradient descent algorithm is used to learn the weights, and a crucial sub-task in this algorithm is computing the gradient. Since the latter is NP-hard in general, Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms are often employed to compute it.

The paper derives theoretical results showing that if the weights (parameters) are such that MCMC converges quickly to the stationary distribution on them, then the weight learning algorithm (that uses MCMC to compute the gradient) is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). The paper considers two cases: unregularized case (convex case) and ridge-regularized case (strongly convex case), and derives separate bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, although there are a few typos. The proofs appear plausible, but verifying all of them is a non-trivial task. The theoretical results are impressive, but their practical relevance is limited. Ensuring that the parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems is virtually impossible. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it is unclear whether they have given much thought to enforcing this condition artificially. This condition can be seen as a form of regularization, and using ridge regression in addition to it may yield over-regularized models. If this problem can be solved, together with these theoretical results, the paper would be significantly strengthened.

Overall, the paper formalizes the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, this condition is of little to no practical relevance. Non-trivial research problems need to be addressed before the results derived in the paper can yield a practical/accurate algorithm for weight learning.

Strengths:

* The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the conditions under which MCMC can be used to compute the gradient in weight learning algorithms.
* The results are impressive, and the paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation of the theoretical framework.

Weaknesses:

* The practical relevance of the results is limited, as ensuring that the parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems is virtually impossible.
* The paper does not provide a clear solution to the problem of enforcing the condition artificially, which is necessary to make the results practically relevant.
