Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ on which MCMC converges rapidly. Within this set, the authors run projected gradient descent using Monte Carlo approximations to the gradient. For exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, they derive non-asymptotic, high-probability guarantees: (i) in the convex (possibly unregularized) case, the suboptimality in objective scales with total sampling effort KMv roughly as O(ε−3) up to logarithms; (ii) with ridge regularization (strong convexity), the parameter error scales as O(ε−2) up to logarithms. They obtain a Lipschitz bound on the gradient, adapt inexact projected gradient results (Schmidt et al.), and provide concrete schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and Markov steps (K, M, v). They also connect to mixing-time bounds for Ising/Potts models and projection onto norm-bounded interaction sets, and give a small Ising example.

This line of work is closely related to a substantial literature on maximum-likelihood with MCMC-generated gradients and stochastic approximation with Markovian noise, including Markov chain Monte Carlo maximum likelihood (e.g., Geyer 1991; Gu & Zhu 2001), Monte Carlo EM/SAEM (Delyon, Lavielle & Moulines 1999), and stochastic approximation with controlled Markov chains (e.g., Benveniste, Métivier & Priouret; Andrieu & Moulines; Fort et al.). Much of that literature establishes convergence (often almost sure and sometimes with rates) without uniform ergodicity, and analyzes algorithms that reuse chains across iterations (“persistent” or warm-started chains) instead of restarting from a fixed distribution at each iteration. The novelty here appears to be the explicit non-asymptotic complexity in terms of mixing parameters for fast-mixing sets and the coupling to projection oracles for such sets (e.g., Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke). Positioning the contribution against these prior results would strengthen the paper.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation of “fast-mixing parameter sets” and how to project onto them in important cases (Ising, general pairwise MRFs).
- Non-asymptotic, high-probability bounds with explicit dependence on K, M, v, mixing constants (C, α), and Lipschitz/strong convexity parameters.
- Useful scheduling guidance for practice and a careful separation of the convex vs strongly convex cases.
- The analysis applies to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not only graphical models.
- The discussion section is candid about limitations and points to variants (e.g., warm starts, contrastive divergence).

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Literature coverage. The paper should engage more deeply with stochastic approximation and MCMCML/MCEM/SAEM. Results on stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics (e.g., Benveniste–Métivier–Priouret; Andrieu & Moulines; Fort et al.) treat dependent samples, persistent chains, and weaker ergodicity conditions. Please compare assumptions (e.g., uniform geometric ergodicity across Θ, bounded sufficient statistics, projection oracle) and guarantees (almost sure convergence, rates) and clarify what is new: e.g., explicit finite-sample, complexity-style bounds via fast-mixing sets and projection.
- Restarted chains. The analysis requires reinitializing each chain from a fixed distribution r. There is extensive prior analysis of algorithms that reuse chains across iterations (persistent CD, MCMCML with warm starts), which can reduce bias and cost. It would be valuable to either extend the theory to warm starts or discuss how existing Markov-noise SA results apply here.
- Assumptions in theorem statements. Theorems 1–2 (and others) should state all assumptions locally and precisely (e.g., bounded sufficient statistics with norm bound R2; convex Θ; existence of projection; uniform mixing inequality with constants C, α; step sizes; independence of samples at a given iteration). Currently, some assumptions are only in surrounding text.
- Strength of mixing assumptions. The guarantee ∥Mvθq − pθ∥TV ≤ Cαv uniformly over θ ∈ Θ and initial q is strong. Please discuss whether uniform geometric ergodicity is essential, whether weaker drift/minorization conditions would suffice, and how C and α are obtained or estimated in practice.
- FPRAS claim. The “FPRAS for ML in Θ” depends on access to a projection oracle and known mixing parameters. The statement should be qualified accordingly and the oracle/model-size complexity of projection discussed.
- Experimental support. The single 4×4 Ising example is illustrative but too limited to assess practical impact. Consider adding experiments comparing restarted vs persistent chains, verifying scaling with K, M, v, and testing projection to different fast-mixing sets.
- Clarity/minor presentation issues. Define all symbols where they first appear in the main text. Ensure consistent notation for R2 vs R. Specify the exact Lipschitz constant used in algorithms (the example uses L smaller than the theoretical bound; clarify whether line search or backtracking is used).

Quality:
- The technical approach is sound under the stated (strong) assumptions, and the proofs appear to follow standard inexact-gradient and concentration tools. Clarifying assumptions and connections to established SA theory would improve rigor and credibility.

Clarity:
- Generally readable, but theorem statements should be self-contained. A consolidated assumptions section or an “Assumptions A1–A5” list referenced by each theorem would help.

Originality:
- Moderate. The combination of fast-mixing parameter sets with non-asymptotic complexity bounds for projected MCMC-based gradient descent is interesting, but closely related to established SA with Markov noise and MCMCML/SAEM frameworks. The paper’s unique angle is the explicit rate/complexity expressed via mixing constants and projection to mixing sets.

Significance:
- Potentially useful for guiding algorithm design under mixing constraints and for framing complexity in high-treewidth models. Practical impact may be limited by the strength of assumptions (uniform mixing, availability of projection and mixing constants) and the narrow experimental validation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, finite-sample guarantees under explicit mixing assumptions.
- Bridges mixing-time bounds with learning complexity.
- General exponential-family treatment; constructive in settings where projection to mixing sets is feasible.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient engagement with extensive prior literature on SA with Markov noise, MCMCML, and MCEM/SAEM; novelty appears incremental.
- Strong assumptions (uniform geometric ergodicity; restarts) and limited empirical validation.
- Theorems not fully self-contained; some presentation issues.

Actionable recommendations:
- Add a related work section comparing assumptions and guarantees with MCMCML (Geyer; Gu & Zhu), MCEM/SAEM (Delyon et al.), and SA with Markov noise (Benveniste–Métivier–Priouret; Andrieu & Moulines; Fort et al.).
- State assumptions explicitly in each theorem and discuss how to estimate or upper bound C, α in practice.
- Extend the analysis or experiments to persistent chains/warm starts, or explain precisely why restarts are required here.
- Qualify the FPRAS statement and discuss projection complexity.
