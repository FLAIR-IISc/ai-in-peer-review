This paper addresses the problem of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where inference is typically intractable. The authors propose an alternative approach to restricting parameters to a tractable set, such as tree-structured parameters, by considering a set of "fast-mixing parameters" where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the convergence properties of a gradient descent algorithm that uses MCMC to approximate the gradient of the likelihood function. The authors derive separate bounds for the unregularized case (convex case) and the ridge-regularized case (non-convex case), showing that the algorithm is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) when the parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear to be sound. However, the practical relevance of the results is limited, as it is challenging to ensure that the parameters satisfy the constraints required by the theorems. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it is unclear whether it is possible to enforce this condition artificially, for example, by using regularization techniques in addition to the fast-mixing condition.

The paper's contribution is primarily theoretical, providing a formal analysis of the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning yields a FPRAS. While the results are impressive, they are of little practical relevance, and non-trivial research problems need to be addressed before the results can be used to develop a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning.
