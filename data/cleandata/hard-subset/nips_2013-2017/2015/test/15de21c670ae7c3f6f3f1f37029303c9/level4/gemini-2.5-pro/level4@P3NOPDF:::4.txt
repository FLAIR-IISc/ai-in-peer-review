This paper addresses the problem of learning intractable probabilistic models by introducing a novel model class defined by the "fast mixing" property of an associated MCMC sampler. The authors provide theoretical results on the rates of convergence for learning algorithms within this framework, connecting these rates directly to the mixing times of the sampler.

**Quality and Technical Soundness**

The paper's primary contribution is the theoretical analysis of convergence rates for learning models that are amenable to MCMC-based inference. The bounds derived by the authors appear to be both meaningful and useful, establishing a clear link between the efficiency of the sampler (mixing time) and the efficiency of the learning algorithm. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for understanding when and why MCMC-based learning methods for intractable models are expected to succeed.

The main area for improvement concerns the lower bound presented in Theorem 7. As written, the theorem is difficult to interpret, and its practical implications are not immediately apparent. The submission would be significantly strengthened by an expanded discussion of this result. Providing more intuition, perhaps through a simplified example or a corollary for a special case, would help readers grasp its meaning. Furthermore, a more general discussion of the landscape of lower bounds applicable to this problem class would provide valuable context and better situate the paper's contribution.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed "fast mixing" model class is defined precisely, and the main theoretical results are stated clearly. The overall structure of the paper is logical and easy to follow.

**Originality and Significance**

The work appears to be highly original in two respects. First, the framing of the problem through the lens of a "fast mixing" model class is a novel and insightful way to characterize a set of tractable-by-approximation models. Second, the specific theoretical results on convergence rates are new and contribute directly to the literature on learning with intractable distributions.

The paper makes a significant contribution by filling a noticeable gap in the literature on learning intractable models with MCMC. It provides a formal basis for a class of models where learning is provably efficient. However, the broader significance and potential impact of the work could be articulated more effectively. To improve the paper, the authors should consider adding a discussion on the work's potential impact. This could include exploring future extensions, outlining opportunities for other researchers to build on these results, or speculating on which classes of practical models might fall into this "fast mixing" framework.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong submission that should be accepted. The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound framework for analyzing the learnability of models via MCMC. The convergence rate results are interesting and represent a solid contribution to the field. While the paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of the lower bound and a more thorough discussion of its broader significance, the core ideas are original and valuable. The suggested revisions would further enhance the paper's clarity and impact.
