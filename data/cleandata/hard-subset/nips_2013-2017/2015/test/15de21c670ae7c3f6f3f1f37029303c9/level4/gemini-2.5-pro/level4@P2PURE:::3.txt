A central problem in Markov networks is weight learning, where the goal is to find a setting for the feature weights that maximizes the data likelihood. This is typically accomplished using gradient-based optimization. A key subroutine in this process is the computation of the gradient, which is generally NP-hard. Consequently, MCMC algorithms are often employed to approximate the gradient instead of exact methods.

This paper derives theoretical results demonstrating that if the model parameters (weights) are such that the MCMC sampler converges rapidly to its stationary distribution, then the gradient-based learning algorithm is a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The analysis considers two settings: the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized case, deriving separate performance bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, containing only a few minor typos. While I was not able to verify all the proofs in detail, the arguments appear plausible.

Although the theoretical results are impressive, their practical relevance is limited, a point the authors commendably acknowledge. The primary condition required by the theorems—that the parameters ensure rapid MCMC mixing—seems virtually impossible to guarantee in practice. It would be interesting to know if the authors have considered whether this condition could be enforced algorithmically. For example, one might treat the rapid-mixing requirement as a form of regularization. However, this raises the question of how such a constraint would interact with existing methods like ridge regression, potentially leading to over-regularized models. If this issue could be resolved, it would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that formally establishes the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning can be proven to be a FPRAS. The main weakness is that this central condition has little to no practical applicability at present. Bridging the gap between these theoretical results and a practical algorithm will require solving non-trivial research problems.
