

Summary: This paper introduces a novel method for computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families, where standard approaches are typically intractable. The authors address the core challenge of computing the integral required for gradient estimation by assuming that Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling mixes rapidly for all parameter values within their constrained set. By leveraging this assumption along with concentration inequalities and the results of Schmidt et al. (2011), they establish a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation. The analysis covers both convex and strongly convex cases, providing distinct guarantees: the former on log-likelihood error and the latter on parameter distance error. A simple illustrative example demonstrates the theoretical findings.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution with a well-motivated approach that offers a compelling alternative to traditional small-treewidth assumptions in graphical models. The theoretical analysis appears robust, though I have not verified all proofs in detail. The results effectively combine existing techniques—specifically, MCMC mixing properties, concentration inequalities, and Schmidt et al.'s work—into a non-trivial framework that advances the understanding of maximum likelihood learning in exponential families.

The paper excels in its honest discussion of limitations, with a thoughtful conclusion that thoroughly analyzes the approach's weaknesses, identifies overly pessimistic theoretical assumptions, and suggests promising directions for future work. Additionally, it provides valuable insights into contrastive divergence-type algorithms, even though the specific algorithm analyzed differs from standard contrastive divergence.

The introduction is well-written and contains substantial references to relevant prior work. However, there are minor clarity issues in the technical presentation. Most notably, the paper omits a straightforward but crucial observation that the negative log-likelihood function f(θ) is convex, which is central to the entire analysis. The convexity follows directly from the PSD nature of the Hessian of A(θ), which is the covariance matrix of the sufficient statistics. A brief discussion of this would greatly aid readers in following the paper's logical flow. Additionally, the paper lacks a clear summary of its key assumptions, such as the requirement that the constraint set Θ must be convex.

The paper represents an original and substantial contribution to the field. While the practical value of the results is somewhat limited—since verifying fast mixing for MCMC is often as challenging as solving the original maximum likelihood problem—the paper successfully transforms one hard problem into another, which can be valuable for theoretical understanding. The availability of these results to the research community is therefore meaningful, even if the immediate practical application requires further development.
