Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies maximum likelihood learning for exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting to low-treewidth models (e.g., trees via Chow–Liu), the authors propose constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ, where an associated MCMC sampler mixes geometrically fast. Within this set, they analyze projected gradient descent that uses MCMC-generated samples to approximate gradients. They prove that, for bounded sufficient statistics, this procedure yields a fully polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained maximum likelihood problem. They provide separate analyses for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases: roughly cubic total sampling effort in 1/ε for ε-accurate log-likelihood in the convex case, and quadratic effort in 1/ε for ε-accurate parameter error in the strongly convex case, up to logarithmic factors. The results are stated in the general exponential family framework and are not limited to graphical models.

The work builds on mixing-time theory (e.g., Jerrum–Sinclair; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes), on projected parameter sets that guarantee fast mixing for Ising and general pairwise MRFs (Domke–Liu; Liu–Domke), and on optimization with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al.). It contrasts with alternatives such as pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods, and correlation-decay/high-girth approaches, and is related to fast-mixing learning via restart chains (Steinhardt–Liang). The paper also clarifies the typical bias/noise introduced by MCMC-based gradient estimates and provides concrete schedules for the number of optimization steps, samples, and chain transitions.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution: rigorous conditions under which MCMC-based gradient descent approximates constrained MLE with high probability.
- Generality: applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not only specific graphical models.
- Separation of convex versus strongly convex settings and explicit complexity bounds and schedules for iterations, samples, and mixing steps.
- Careful use of concentration inequalities and established convergence results for inexact gradients.
- The paper is generally well written, and the claims are plausible (though I could not check all proofs in detail).

Weaknesses and practical relevance:
- Practical relevance appears limited. It seems very difficult in practice to ensure that parameters satisfy the required fast-mixing conditions, or to verify that a given model lies in Θ. The constants (C, α) are rarely known, and the bounds can be very conservative, leading to large K, M, and v.
- While some projections onto Θ are feasible in special cases (e.g., simple clamping for bounded Ising couplings; spectral-norm projections for pairwise models), for general models the projection step may be nontrivial or computationally heavy, and could induce significant bias relative to the unconstrained MLE.
- Enforcing both a fast-mixing constraint and ridge regularization risks over-regularization. The authors should discuss whether these constraints can be tuned jointly (e.g., adaptively selecting Θ based on data) to avoid overly biased models.
- The analysis assumes fresh independent MCMC samples per iteration and does not cover practical heuristics such as persistent chains, sample pools, or contrastive divergence–style initializations, which are common in large-scale learning. Bridging this gap would substantially improve practical impact.

Specific questions and suggestions:
- Can the authors propose principled, data-driven ways to choose or adapt the constraint set Θ (e.g., target a spectral-norm bound on pairwise couplings) that balance statistical accuracy and mixing guarantees?
- How robust are the results to inaccurate or unknown mixing constants? Can one use empirical diagnostics to adapt v during training with retained guarantees?
- What is the computational complexity of the projection step for the most general settings considered, and how does it scale with model size?
- Could the analysis be extended to cover persistent sampling or warm-start strategies (e.g., maintaining a sample pool) that reduce practical mixing time?
- Would combining contrastive divergence initializations with the fast-mixing constraint meaningfully reduce v in practice, and could a theory be developed for this hybrid?
- Please fix minor typos and consider clarifying the Lipschitz constant derivation and the dependence on dimensionality N in the bounds.

Quality: The technical development appears sound and is carefully structured. I could not verify every proof, but the arguments seem consistent with the cited tools.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized, with helpful definitions and assumptions. Minor typos exist.

Originality: The idea of defining a tractable set via fast mixing and providing an FPRAS for constrained MLE is original and distinct from tree-structured or correlation-decay approaches.

Significance: Theoretical significance is solid, as the results formalize guarantees for a widely used but poorly understood practice (MCMC-based gradient learning). Immediate practical significance is limited by the difficulty of enforcing/verifying fast-mixing constraints and by conservative bounds.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a clean theoretical framework and guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning under explicit, verifiable (in some cases) constraints.
- General results applicable to broad exponential families.
- Clarifies the interaction between sampling error, bias, and optimization in a principled way.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited practical impact at present: fast-mixing constraints are hard to enforce or certify for realistic high-dimensional models, and the prescribed schedules are likely too conservative.
- Projection may be computationally demanding or distort the model substantially, and the analysis does not accommodate common practical training schemes (persistent chains, sample pools).
- The empirical section is minimal and does not demonstrate practical feasibility at scale.

Overall evaluation:
A well-written theoretical paper that formalizes when MCMC-based gradient descent yields an FPRAS for constrained maximum likelihood estimation. However, without a compelling pathway to enforce fast-mixing constraints in realistic settings and without accommodation of standard practical training heuristics, the immediate utility is limited. Addressing these open problems would considerably strengthen the paper’s practical relevance.
