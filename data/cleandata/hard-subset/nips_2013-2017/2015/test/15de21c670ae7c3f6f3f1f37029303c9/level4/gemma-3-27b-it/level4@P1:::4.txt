This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by considering a set of “fast-mixing parameters” – parameters for which Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference converges quickly. While standard approaches restrict parameters to tractable sets like tree-structured distributions, this work provides theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with gradients approximated by MCMC sampling when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set. Specifically, it establishes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within this set, achieving ε-accuracy in log-likelihood with effort cubic in 1/ε (ignoring logarithmic factors) in the unregularized case, and quadratic in 1/ε with ridge regularization.

**1 Introduction**
The paper begins by highlighting the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in general undirected graphical models, referencing the #P-completeness result of Jerrum and Sinclair [1993] for the Ising model. It positions its approach as an alternative to existing methods like pseudolikelihood [3], score matching [16, 22], and composite likelihoods [20, 30], which often suffer from inferior sample complexity or lack guarantees when the model is misspecified.  The authors argue these methods also often lead to intractable inference, whereas restricting parameters to a tractable set – traditionally tree-structured distributions (Chow-Liu algorithm [1968]) – offers a solution.  This work proposes a fundamentally different notion of tractability, focusing on fast mixing of MCMC, and demonstrates that this guarantees an FPRAS. The key result applies broadly to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models.  The paper analyzes gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, establishing convergence guarantees for both unregularized and ridge-regularized cases.

This section provides necessary background on mixing times and their relation to fast-mixing parameter sets. The definition of total-variation distance and its connection to Markov chain convergence is clearly explained. The paper emphasizes the importance of knowing constants C and α that govern the mixing rate, referencing the Convergence Theorem [19]. Examples, specifically for the Ising model, demonstrate how mixing time bounds can be established under certain conditions. The discussion of projection algorithms onto the set of fast-mixing parameters, particularly for the Ising model [12, 19, 20], is relevant and provides context. 

**4 Main Results**
The core results are presented in this section, beginning with a Lipschitz continuity result for the regularized log-likelihood gradient (Theorem 1). Theorems 2 and 6 establish convergence guarantees for the convex and strongly convex cases, respectively. The detailed proofs are deferred to the appendix, but helpful proof sketches are provided. The paper explores lower bounds on the computational effort (Theorem 7) and proposes parameter schedules to achieve desired accuracy (Theorems 8 and 9). The discussion of the trade-offs between convex and strongly convex convergence, and the implications for practical performance, is valuable.

**5 Discussion**
The discussion section acknowledges limitations of the analysis, such as the neglect of projection algorithm complexity and the independence assumption in the MCMC sampling. It also points to potential future directions, including exploring the benefits of initializing samples from the empirical distribution (contrastive divergence [5]) and investigating more sophisticated sampling strategies.

**6 Example**
The included example with the Ising model on a 4x4 grid provides a concrete illustration of the proposed approach. The comparison of theoretical and practical convergence rates suggests a degree of conservatism in the theoretical bounds.



**Overall Assessment:**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with clear definitions, well-established theoretical results, and a rigorous analysis. The convergence guarantees for learning with fast-mixing parameters are a significant contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and results. The notation is consistently used, and the proof sketches provide intuition for the main theorems.

**Originality:** The framing of the problem in terms of fast-mixing parameter sets and the corresponding theoretical results appear novel. This work offers a new perspective on learning in intractable models.

**Significance:** While the immediate practical impact might not be immediately obvious, the paper addresses a fundamental question in statistical inference and machine learning: how to learn efficiently in models where exact inference is impossible. The establishment of theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based learning algorithms is a significant step forward and opens up opportunities for further research in this area. The paper fills a gap in the literature and could potentially lead to the development of more efficient and reliable learning algorithms for complex models.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framework for tractable model classes based on MCMC mixing times.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees for convergence of gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients.
*   Broad applicability to exponential families beyond graphical models.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lower bound (Theorem 7) is difficult to interpret and could benefit from further clarification.
*   The practical significance is not fully explored; a more detailed discussion of potential applications and impact would be beneficial.
*   The complexity of the projection step is not fully accounted for in the analysis.



**Overall Recommendation:**  I recommend acceptance. This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of statistical learning. The originality, technical soundness, and potential impact of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses identified. The paper offers a valuable new perspective on learning intractable models and provides a solid foundation for future research.
