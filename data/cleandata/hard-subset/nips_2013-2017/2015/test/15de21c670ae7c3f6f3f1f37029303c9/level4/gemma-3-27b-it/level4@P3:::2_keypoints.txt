- Proposes a novel approach for computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families using fast-mixing MCMC to approximate proximal gradient integrals, yielding a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS).  
- Treats both convex and strongly convex cases, providing likelihood-error guarantees for the former and parameter-error guarantees for the latter.  
- Includes a simple experiment that demonstrates the theoretical claims.  
- Theoretical contribution arises from a nontrivial combination of MCMC mixing assumptions, concentration inequalities, and results from Schmidt et al. (2011).  
- Provides useful insight into contrastive-divergence–type algorithms, though the analyzed algorithm is not strictly CD.  
- Well-motivated alternative to small-treewidth assumptions common in graphical-model tractability work.  
- Analysis appears rigorous and well-presented; introduction and literature context are strong.  
- Honest and thoughtful discussion of limitations, potential over-pessimism in assumptions, and directions for future work.  
- Major limitation: reliance on a fast-mixing MCMC assumption, which is difficult to prove and may limit practical applicability.  
- Practical impact is potentially limited since proving fast mixing can be as hard as the original MLE problem.  
- Clarity issues: the paper omits an explicit statement that the log-likelihood is convex and does not clearly summarize core assumptions (e.g., convexity of parameter set Θ).  
- Minor presentation issues and typographical errors (examples listed at l185, l192, l207, l281, l311–312).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem, provides sound theoretical advances, and bridges MCMC theory with optimization methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical applicability due to strong assumptions and insufficient empirical validation.  
- Recommendation: accept.
