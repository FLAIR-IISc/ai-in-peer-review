- Paper studies ML learning in exponential families by restricting parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ and using projected gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients.  
- Proves high-probability convergence within Θ and gives FPRAS-style complexity: O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)) (unregularized) and O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) (ridge-regularized).  
- Analysis assumes bounded sufficient statistics, access to an MCMC sampler with geometric mixing (parameters C, α), and a projection oracle onto Θ.  
- Technical arguments use Lipschitz gradient bounds, concentration inequalities, and inexact projected gradient descent results; treatment of MCMC gradient bias is careful.  
- Main theorems appear correct with sensible dependence on problem and mixing parameters; schedules are near-tight and require fresh independent MCMC samples per iteration.  
- Paper does not cover persistent-sample methods (PCD) or shared sample pools; analysis relies on independent fresh samples.  
- Practical challenges: identifying/estimating mixing constants (C, α) and computing projections onto Θ (easy for simple constraints, expensive for spectral constraints).  
- Theorem 7 (lower bound) is hard to interpret; needs more intuitive discussion on scaling with ε, α, and strong convexity.  
- Writing is generally clear and well organized but would benefit from consolidating assumptions early and fixing notation inconsistencies (e.g., R2 vs R22).  
- Algorithm details need clarification: explicitly state f (negative log-likelihood), λ’s role, step sizes, and projection specifics in Algorithm 1.  
- Provide intuition for Theorem 7’s β1/β2/β3 schedule parameters and possibly a table mapping them to computational budgets.  
- Fix typos and incomplete bibliography entries (e.g., “Inferning” → “Inferring”; missing years/venues).  
- Originality: defining tractable classes by fast-mixing parameter sets is novel and the FPRAS-type guarantees for general exponential families are a significant contribution.  
- Significance: fills a theoretical gap for MCMC-based likelihood gradient learning and can guide design of mixing-promoting regularizers and sample/chain/iteration budgeting.  
- Limitations: relies on known mixing constants, potentially expensive projections for complex Θ, and excludes persistent-chain/hidden-variable settings.  
- Pros: rigorous high-probability convergence, general applicability to bounded exponential families, insightful schedules, and near-matching lower bounds.  
- Cons: practical reliance on mixing estimates and projections, hard-to-interpret lower bound, notation issues, and omission of PCD-style algorithms.  
- Suggestions: provide methods or robustness analysis for estimating C and α, discuss constructing Θ beyond pairwise models and projection complexity, and comment on extensions to persistent chains.  
- Ask for clarification on dimension dependence (via R and mixing bounds) and feasibility of adaptive schedules based on mixing diagnostics.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — technically solid, well-motivated, and a valuable theoretical advance despite practical limitations.
