

Summary: This paper introduces a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in exponential families (EFs), addressing the intractability of direct MLE computation. The core insight leverages the assumption that Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) mixes rapidly for all valid EF parameters. This allows the integrals required for proximal gradient descent to be approximated with sufficient precision, enabling a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for MLE via combination with Schmidt et al. (2011). The analysis covers both convex and strongly convex settings, yielding distinct guarantees: likelihood error bounds in the convex case and parameter error bounds in the strongly convex case. A simple empirical demonstration validates the theoretical claims.

The work is a high-quality, substantial contribution. It is well-motivated as a viable alternative to restrictive small-treewidth assumptions common in prior work. The theoretical framework is sound, effectively combining MCMC mixing assumptions, concentration inequalities, and existing results. While the technical synthesis is nontrivial, the proofs appear to rely on established techniques, and the overall argument holds. The paper excels in its transparent discussion of limitations: it critically evaluates the practical challenges of verifying fast mixing, acknowledges the theoretical assumptions as potentially pessimistic, and offers clear directions for future work. This honesty significantly strengthens the contribution.

The introduction is well-written and contextualizes the work effectively. However, two technical clarifications would enhance accessibility: (1) the convexity of the log-likelihood (central to the entire argument) should be explicitly stated. While the second and third terms in Eq. 1 are convex and the log-partition function \(A(\theta)\) is convex due to its positive semi-definite Hessian, this point warrants brief mention to guide readers through the optimization framework. (2) A concise summary of key assumptions (e.g., the requirement that the parameter set \(\Theta\) is convex) would improve clarity and help readers assess the scope of the results.

The paper’s primary limitation is the practical dependency on verifying fast MCMC mixing—a notoriously difficult task. As the author acknowledges, this replaces one hard problem (MLE computation) with another (establishing fast mixing). While this may limit immediate applicability, the theoretical conversion is valuable for algorithmic design and community reference. The analysis also provides useful insights into contrastive divergence (CD)-type algorithms, though the proposed method differs from standard CD.

The paper is well-written overall, with minor grammatical and typographical errors requiring correction (e.g., "l185: ',w here' → ', where'; l192: 'this paper that' → 'this paper assumes that'; l207: 'simple example fast-mixing' → 'simple example of a fast-mixing'; l281: 'of of' → 'of'; l311–12: 'An novel approach' → 'A novel approach'). The sentence "An novel approach to developing conditions..." is nonsensical and must be corrected to "A novel approach to developing conditions..."

In conclusion, this is a significant theoretical advance that rigorously connects MCMC mixing to tractable MLE approximation. While the practical utility hinges on the feasibility of verifying fast mixing, the paper provides a crucial framework for future algorithmic development. The work is original, well-executed, and makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on efficient inference in exponential families. It deserves publication in NIPS.
