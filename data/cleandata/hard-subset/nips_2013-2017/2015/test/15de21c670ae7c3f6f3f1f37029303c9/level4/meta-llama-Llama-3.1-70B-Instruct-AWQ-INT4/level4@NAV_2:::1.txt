I think that the main issue of the manuscript is a lack of awareness of the literature on the topic. The authors' algorithm is strongly related to the Monte Carlo EM algorithm (and in fact the general class of stochastic approximation algorithms with Markovian dynamics which have been proposed in various forms to optimize intractable likelihood functions). There is a well-developed literature on the topic, and the authors should compare what they have been doing to existing work. A theory which includes some of the authors' results already exists and covers much more general scenarios.

The paper explores an alternative notion of a tractable set, namely a set of “fast-mixing parameters” where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference can be guaranteed to quickly converge to the stationary distribution. This concept is interesting and has potential applications in inference for high-treewidth undirected graphical models. However, the authors should discuss how their work relates to previous research on MCMC methods and stochastic approximation algorithms.

The presentation of the results is far from optimal. For example, for Theorem 1 and 2, it is not clear what the assumptions are. One can refer to the text before and after, but the reader should not have to do that. The authors should clearly state the assumptions and provide more context for the theorems.

The paper provides a guarantee on the convergence of the algorithm, but the assumptions used are quite strong. The authors should discuss the limitations of their approach and how it compares to existing methods. Additionally, the paper lacks a clear discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm and how it scales with the size of the problem.

The example provided in the paper is useful for illustrating the concept, but it is not clear how the algorithm performs on more complex problems. The authors should provide more experimental results to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, but it lacks clarity and context. The authors should revise the paper to address the issues mentioned above and provide a more comprehensive discussion of their work in relation to existing research.

In terms of originality, the paper provides a new perspective on the concept of tractable sets, but the algorithm itself is not entirely new. The authors should discuss how their work builds upon existing research and what novel contributions they are making.

In terms of significance, the paper has potential applications in inference for high-treewidth undirected graphical models, but the authors should provide more context and discussion of the impact of their work.

Overall, the paper has some interesting ideas, but it needs to be revised to address the issues mentioned above. The authors should provide more context, clarify the assumptions, and discuss the limitations of their approach.
