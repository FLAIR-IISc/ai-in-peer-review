Summary: The paper proposes a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. In general, finding the maximum likelihood solution is intractable. From a convex optimization perspective, the sticking point is the need to calculate an integral with respect to the current exponential family parameter. By assuming that Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is fast-mixing for all allowed parameters, the authors are able to show that the integrals needed for projected gradient descent can be calculated with sufficient precision that, when combined with the results of Schmidt et al. (2011), a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for calculating the MLE can be obtained. Both the convex and strongly convex cases are considered, which lead to different types of guarantees: the former on the likelihood error, the latter on the parameter error. A simple experiment demonstrating the theory is also included.

Overall, this is a high-quality piece of work. The approach is well-motivated as an alternative to relying on small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analyses appear solid, though I haven't checked the proofs in detail. The proofs are essentially a matter of combining the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and the results of Schmidt et al. The paper is thus a nontrivial combination of existing techniques.

I appreciated the honesty with which the limitations of the approach were discussed. In particular, a thoughtful conclusion analyzed weaknesses of the approach, ways in which the theoretical assumptions might be overly pessimistic, and directions for future work. The paper also offers some insight into contrastive divergence (CD)-type algorithms, even though the algorithm analyzed is not strictly contrastive divergence.

The introduction was well-written with substantial references to previous work. However, there were a few issues with the clarity of the technical presentation. One small but important omission was simply stating that the log-likelihood is, in fact, convex: the second and third terms in Eq. 1 are clearly convex, and the convexity of A(θ) follows from the fact that its Hessian is the covariance of the sufficient statistics, and thus is positive semi-definite. While seemingly minor, the entire paper is predicated on the convexity of f(θ), so discussing this will help guide the reader through the logic. A clearer summary of the assumptions made in the paper would also be helpful. For example, the assumption that the parameter set Θ must be convex is never explicitly stated in the main text. 

Overall, I found the paper to be an original and substantial contribution. The results seem to have limited practical value, however, due to the fact that proving fast mixing of MCMC is extremely challenging. So, in a sense, the paper replaces one hard problem (finding the MLE) with another (finding a fast-mixing Markov chain). That said, being able to transform one problem into another is often useful, and thus making the results of this paper available to the community is valuable.
