### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where computing the gradient of the log-likelihood is typically intractable. The standard approach to this problem is to restrict the model parameters to a structurally simple set, such as tree-structured models, where inference is efficient. This work proposes a fundamentally different notion of a tractable parameter set: a "fast-mixing" set Θ, within which Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is guaranteed to converge rapidly to the stationary distribution.

The authors analyze the widely used heuristic of performing gradient descent where the gradient is approximated using samples from an MCMC chain. They provide a rigorous theoretical proof that this procedure constitutes a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within the constrained set Θ. The analysis is general, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. The paper presents two main results:
1.  For the unregularized (convex) case, it derives a convergence guarantee for the objective function value, showing that the total computational effort to achieve an ε-accurate solution scales cubically with 1/ε (ignoring logarithmic factors).
2.  For the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case, it provides a convergence guarantee in terms of parameter distance, with the total effort scaling quadratically with 1/ε.

The paper carefully derives schedules for setting the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples per step, and MCMC chain length to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The core idea of defining tractability through the lens of MCMC mixing time, rather than graphical structure (like treewidth), is both novel and insightful. This reframing of the problem opens up a new avenue for research in learning intractable models and provides a formal basis for learning models that may have dense, high-treewidth structures, so long as their parameters promote rapid mixing.

2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Foundation for a Common Heuristic:** The practice of using MCMC to approximate gradients in likelihood-based learning is widespread (e.g., in training Restricted Boltzmann Machines), but it often lacks theoretical guarantees due to the biased nature of the gradient estimates from finite-length chains. This paper provides the first formal proof, to my knowledge, that this procedure is not just a heuristic but can be an FPRAS under well-defined conditions. This is a strong and valuable contribution to the field.

3.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and mathematically precise. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their work relative to prior art (e.g., pseudolikelihood, score matching, and low-treewidth methods), and transparently stating their assumptions. The provided proofs, though not all verified in detail, appear plausible and are sketched out intuitively in the main text. The authors are also commendably upfront about the practical limitations of the analysis in the discussion section.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Practical Applicability of the Core Assumption:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its central assumption: the existence of a known, convex, fast-mixing set Θ onto which one can efficiently project. While the paper provides concrete examples from the literature (e.g., Ising models with bounded interaction strengths or specific matrix norm constraints), it is unclear how a practitioner would define such a set for a new or more complex model. The difficulty has been shifted from intractable inference to the challenging task of characterizing and enforcing a fast-mixing parameter space. How can these sets be constructed in practice beyond the well-studied cases?

2.  **The Projection Oracle:** The analysis assumes access to an efficient projection operator ΠΘ. The computational cost of this projection, which must be performed at each gradient step, is not included in the final complexity bounds for the learning algorithm. As the authors acknowledge, if the projection is itself computationally expensive (e.g., scales super-linearly with model size, as the SVD-based projection for the spectral norm constraint does), it could dominate the runtime and challenge the overall efficiency of the proposed scheme. It would be helpful if the authors could comment further on the complexity of projection for the examples they cite.

3.  **Interaction with Regularization:** The proposed constraint θ ∈ Θ can be seen as a form of implicit regularization. The paper analyzes its combination with explicit ridge regularization (λ > 0). Could the authors elaborate on the interplay between these two? For instance, could enforcing a strict fast-mixing constraint render additional explicit regularization unnecessary, or could their combination lead to an over-regularized and less expressive model?

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution by formalizing the conditions under which MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning is provably efficient. The conceptual shift from structural to dynamic tractability is a powerful idea. The primary concern is the practical feasibility of identifying and projecting onto the required fast-mixing parameter sets. While the immediate practical impact may be limited until methods for constructing such sets are developed, the theoretical foundation laid by this work is strong and likely to inspire important follow-up research. The paper's strengths in originality and technical rigor outweigh its practical limitations, making it a solid contribution to the NIPS community.
