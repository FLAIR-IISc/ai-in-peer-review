A central problem in Markov networks is weight learning, typically formulated as choosing feature weights to maximize the likelihood. Gradient descent is commonly used, with a crucial subtask being gradient computation. Because this subtask is NP-hard in general, MCMC methods are often used in place of exact computation.

The paper presents theoretical results showing that if the parameters are such that MCMC mixes rapidly to the stationary distribution, then the weight-learning algorithm that uses MCMC to estimate the gradient is an FPRAS. The analysis treats two settings separately: the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized (non-convex) case, providing distinct bounds for each.

The paper is generally well written, with a few typos. I could not verify all proofs, but they appear plausible.

Although the theoretical results are impressive—and I appreciate that the authors acknowledge this—their practical relevance seems minimal. It appears virtually impossible in practice to guarantee that the parameters satisfy the required conditions. Have the authors considered this issue? In particular, is it feasible to enforce the condition artificially? The condition itself can be interpreted as a form of regularization, and combining it with ridge regression may lead to over-regularized models. If this latter concern can be addressed, then together with these theoretical results the paper would be very strong.

Overall, this is a reasonably well-written paper that formalizes the conditions under which using MCMC for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields an FPRAS. However, the key condition appears to have little practical relevance, and nontrivial research challenges must be resolved before the results can lead to a practical and accurate weight-learning algorithm.
