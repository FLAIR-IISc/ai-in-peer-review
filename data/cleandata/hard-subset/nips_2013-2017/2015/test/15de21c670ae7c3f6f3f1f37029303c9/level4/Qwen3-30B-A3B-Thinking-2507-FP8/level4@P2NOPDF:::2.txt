

Summary: This paper introduces a novel approach for computationally efficient maximum likelihood estimation in exponential families (EFs), addressing the intractability of exact MLE computation. The authors leverage the assumption that MCMC mixes rapidly for all valid EF parameters to approximate integrals required for proximal gradient descent. Combining this with Schmidt et al. (2011), they establish a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for MLE, with distinct guarantees for convex and strongly convex cases—likelihood error bounds versus parameter error bounds. A simple empirical demonstration validates the theoretical claims.

The work is a high-quality, original contribution that effectively motivates its approach as an alternative to restrictive small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical framework is robust, integrating MCMC mixing assumptions, concentration inequalities, and prior results. While the techniques are largely combinatory, their synthesis is nontrivial and well-executed. The authors demonstrate commendable transparency in discussing limitations: a thoughtful conclusion critically evaluates the practicality of fast-mixing assumptions, identifies overly pessimistic theoretical constraints, and outlines promising future directions. The paper also provides valuable insights into contrastive divergence (CD) algorithms, even though the analyzed method differs slightly from standard CD.

The introduction is well-crafted with comprehensive references, but technical exposition could be clearer. A critical omission is the explicit statement that the log-likelihood function is convex—a foundational premise of the paper. The convexity of the second and third terms in Equation (1) is evident, but the log-partition function \(A(\theta)\)’s convexity (derivable from its Hessian being the covariance matrix of sufficient statistics, hence PSD) should be explicitly noted to guide readers through the argument. Additionally, the paper should clearly state that the parameter set \(\Theta\) is assumed convex, as this is essential for the optimization framework yet omitted from the main text.

The primary limitation is the practical utility of the approach, as proving fast-mixing MCMC for arbitrary EF parameters remains exceptionally challenging. The paper thus replaces one hard problem (MLE computation) with another (verifying fast mixing), which constrains immediate applicability. However, this theoretical conversion is valuable for the community, offering a framework to assess tractability under specific conditions. The results are not "seemingly limited" but rather constrained by a critical assumption whose verification is problem-dependent.

Overall, this is a substantial contribution that advances the theoretical understanding of efficient MLE in EFs. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and responsibly contextualized. While practical deployment hinges on the feasibility of verifying fast-mixing, the paper provides a crucial stepping stone for future research. I recommend acceptance.
