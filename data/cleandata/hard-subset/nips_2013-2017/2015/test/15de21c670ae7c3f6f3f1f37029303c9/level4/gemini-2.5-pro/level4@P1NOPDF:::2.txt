**Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach for computationally efficient maximum likelihood estimation in exponential families, a problem that is generally intractable. The primary bottleneck from a convex optimization standpoint is the need to compute an integral with respect to the current parameter estimate. The authors show that by assuming a fast-mixing MCMC sampler is available for all parameters, the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be approximated with sufficient precision. This, combined with the analysis of Schmidt et al. (2011), yields a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for computing the MLE. The paper considers both the convex and strongly convex cases, leading to guarantees on the likelihood error and parameter error, respectively. A simple experiment is included to illustrate the theoretical results.

**Overall Assessment and Strengths**

This is a high-quality piece of work. The approach is well-motivated, offering an alternative to methods that rely on small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analysis appears solid, although I have not verified the proofs in detail. The core theoretical contribution is a nontrivial synthesis of existing techniques, primarily combining the assumption of fast MCMC mixing with concentration inequalities and the convergence results for stochastic proximal-gradient methods from Schmidt et al. (2011).

A major strength of the paper is its thoughtful and honest discussion of the method's limitations. The conclusion provides a nuanced analysis of the approach's weaknesses, considers how the theoretical assumptions might be overly pessimistic in practice, and outlines valuable directions for future research. The paper also offers useful insights into contrastive divergence (CD)-type algorithms, even though the analyzed algorithm is distinct from standard CD.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the introduction is well-written and provides substantial context, the technical presentation could be improved in a few areas for clarity.

1.  One small but important omission is an explicit statement and justification of the log-likelihood's convexity. The entire optimization framework is premised on this property. A brief explanation—for example, noting that the Hessian of the log-partition function A(theta) is the covariance matrix of the sufficient statistics and therefore positive semidefinite—would better orient the reader and strengthen the paper's logical flow.

2.  The paper would benefit from a centralized, clear summary of all its assumptions. For instance, the assumption that the parameter set Theta must be convex is crucial for the optimization problem but does not appear to be explicitly stated in the main text.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper is an original and substantial contribution. Its immediate practical value may be limited, as the core assumption of a fast-mixing MCMC sampler is itself extremely difficult to verify for most models of interest. In essence, the paper replaces one hard problem (computing the MLE) with another (proving fast mixing). Nevertheless, establishing this formal connection between two fundamental problems is a valuable theoretical result. The ability to reframe the MLE problem in this way is a useful addition to the literature and could inspire future work.
