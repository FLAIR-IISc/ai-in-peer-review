Summary
The paper proposes an algorithm for optimizing intractable likelihood functions using a stochastic-approximation scheme with an embedded Markov chain. While the topic is important and the approach is of practical interest, the contribution appears to overlap substantially with established work on Monte Carlo EM (MCEM) and, more broadly, stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics. The current draft does not adequately situate the method within this literature, and several theoretical and presentation issues limit its impact.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and challenging class of problems (intractable likelihood optimization).
- Employs a principled stochastic-approximation framework.
- The intent to provide convergence guarantees is commendable.

Major Concerns
1) Limited novelty and insufficient positioning within the literature:
- The proposed method is closely related to MCEM and to stochastic-approximation EM variants (e.g., approaches that combine Robbins–Monro updates with Markovian noise). There is a substantial body of theory for such algorithms that appears not to be fully acknowledged or leveraged.
- Existing results already establish convergence under assumptions more general than those used here, and for algorithms that do not require the Markov chain to be reinitialized at each iteration.

2) Overly strong assumptions:
- Uniform ergodicity of the Markov chain is not necessary in this context. Standard drift/minorization conditions or weaker ergodicity assumptions suffice in much of the prior work on stochastic approximation with Markovian noise.
- Reinitializing the Markov chain at a fixed distribution r at each iteration is not required and is often undesirable in practice. Using a persistent chain (e.g., reinitializing from the previous state) is common and has been analyzed extensively; the paper acknowledges this but dismisses it as a simplification choice for analysis. This significantly undermines practical relevance and does not reflect the state of the art.

3) Presentation and clarity of the theoretical results:
- For Theorems 1 and 2, the assumptions are not clearly and explicitly stated adjacent to the statements. Readers should not need to infer assumptions from surrounding text.
- The assumptions used for Theorem 6 are stronger than necessary. More general convergence results are available in the literature on stochastic approximation with Markovian perturbations (e.g., the framework of Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret), which could subsume this theorem with weaker conditions.

Detailed Comments and Suggestions
- Literature review and positioning:
  - Clearly articulate how the proposed algorithm differs from classical MCEM and from stochastic-approximation EM variants that incorporate MCMC (sometimes referred to as MCMC-SAEM or related schemes).
  - Discuss prior theory on stochastic approximation with Markovian noise and its implications for your setting. In particular, compare your assumptions and results to those in standard references such as Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret, and related developments that relax uniform ergodicity and allow state-dependent Markov kernels.
  - Explain whether your analysis offers any genuinely new guarantees (e.g., nonasymptotic rates, sharper constants, or novel state-space assumptions). If the novelty is primarily in algorithmic design, emphasize the practical gains and provide empirical evidence.

- Assumptions on the Markov chain:
  - Replace uniform ergodicity with weaker, verifiable conditions (e.g., drift and minorization) where possible.
  - Allow persistent chains rather than requiring reinitialization from a fixed r. If you retain reinitialization for some reason (e.g., parallel tempering or control variates), justify it both theoretically and empirically, and quantify the computational trade-offs.

- Theorem statements and proofs:
  - Restate all assumptions immediately before each theorem, including conditions on step sizes, regularity of the objective, properties of the Markov kernels, and noise conditions. Ensure that symbols (e.g., r, transition kernels, parameter domains) are defined where they first appear.
  - Where your proofs mirror standard stochastic-approximation arguments, reference the exact lemmas/theorems you rely on and specify any problem-specific adaptations. This will help readers understand what is new versus what follows from established results.
  - For Theorem 6, clarify which parts of the result are genuinely new. If existing theorems already imply your claim under weaker assumptions, either relax your assumptions accordingly or reframe the contribution.

- Empirical evaluation:
  - If experiments are included or planned, compare against established baselines such as MCEM and persistent-chain SAEM-type methods. Evaluate sensitivity to:
    - Step-size schedules and batch sizes of MCMC updates per iteration.
    - Chain initialization strategy (fixed r versus persistent).
    - Mixing properties of the kernels, especially in higher dimensions.
  - Report computational cost per effective update to substantiate any practical advantages.

- Clarity and presentation:
  - Improve notation consistency and define all quantities on first use.
  - Add a concise “Assumptions” subsection before the main results that groups common conditions to avoid repetition and ambiguity.
  - Provide a brief conceptual discussion of why reinitialization is not necessary and when persistence helps, with citations.

Overall Assessment
The paper targets an important problem, but in its current form the contribution appears marginal relative to established work on MCEM and stochastic approximation with Markovian noise. The analysis relies on unnecessarily strong assumptions, the connection to prior literature is incomplete, and the theorem statements need to be made self-contained and aligned with general results known in the field. I recommend substantial revision: broaden and deepen the literature comparison, relax assumptions using standard tools, allow persistent chains, clarify theorems and proofs, and provide empirical comparisons to established baselines. With these improvements, the paper would be much clearer about its novelty and potential impact.
