

The paper addresses weight learning in Markov networks, a key challenge defined as optimizing feature-associated weights to maximize likelihood. Standard approaches employ gradient descent, requiring gradient computation—a task that is NP-hard in general. Consequently, MCMC methods are frequently used to approximate gradients. The authors establish theoretical conditions under which an MCMC-based weight learning algorithm becomes a Fully Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). Specifically, they prove that if the model parameters permit rapid MCMC convergence to the stationary distribution, the learning algorithm achieves FPRAS guarantees. The analysis covers both the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (non-convex) cases, deriving distinct theoretical bounds for each.

The paper is generally well-written, with only minor typographical errors. While I was unable to fully verify all proofs, they appear plausible and logically structured. However, the theoretical contributions, though mathematically sound, face a critical limitation: the required conditions for rapid MCMC convergence are almost entirely impractical to satisfy in real-world scenarios. The authors rightly acknowledge this gap, but the question remains whether such conditions can be enforced artificially. For instance, could the constraints be embedded as an additional regularization term? If so, combining this with ridge regularization might lead to excessive regularization, potentially degrading model performance. The authors do not explore this trade-off, which is pivotal for practical applicability.

Given that the core theoretical condition lacks empirical feasibility, the results currently offer little value for developing practical weight learning algorithms. The paper formalizes a meaningful theoretical boundary, but this boundary is too narrow to guide algorithmic design. Before these results can inform real-world applications, significant theoretical and methodological challenges must be resolved—specifically, establishing how to reliably satisfy the rapid mixing condition without compromising model accuracy. Without addressing this, the work remains an interesting but disconnected theoretical exercise. While the writing is clear and the mathematical framework is rigorous, the absence of a viable path to practical implementation significantly diminishes the paper’s impact. A stronger contribution would emerge if the authors could demonstrate how to operationalize their conditions or propose a modified algorithm that bridges the gap between theory and practice. As it stands, the paper’s primary value lies in its precise characterization of the theoretical limits of MCMC-based learning, rather than in providing actionable insights for practitioners.
