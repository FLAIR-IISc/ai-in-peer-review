Summary
The paper studies maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ, i.e., a convex set for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes to the model’s stationary distribution at a geometric rate. Within this set, the authors analyze a standard projected gradient descent algorithm that uses MCMC samples to estimate gradients. They prove that, for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, the procedure converges to the maximum-likelihood solution within Θ with high probability, yielding a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). They treat both the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, obtaining complexity O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)) in log-likelihood suboptimality and O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) in parameter error, respectively.

The work is placed against tractable classes based on structure (e.g., treewidth) and related learning strategies such as Chow–Liu trees, pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods, learning in high-girth graphs (correlation decay), and MCMC-based learning (Geyer; Tieleman; Salakhutdinov). It connects to mixing-time bounds for Ising/Potts models (e.g., Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes; Jerrum–Sinclair) and to fast-mixing projections (Domke–Liu; Liu–Domke). The convergence analysis leverages Lipschitz continuity of the gradient, concentration inequalities (Hoeffding, Bernstein/Efron–Stein), and inexact projected gradient descent guarantees (Schmidt et al.).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The assumptions are clearly stated: bounded sufficient statistics; access to a sampler whose mixing rate on Θ is bounded by parameters (C, α); and a projection oracle onto Θ. The Lipschitz gradient bound, the convex/strongly convex convergence results, and the sample/steps/transition scheduling are consistent with standard tools. The treatment of bias due to approximate MCMC gradients is careful, and the separation of convex vs. strongly convex cases is appropriate.
- Correctness and completeness: The main theorems appear correct, with sensible dependence on problem and mixing parameters. The explicit schedules show near-tightness vis-à-vis lower bounds. The analysis transparently requires fresh, independent MCMC samples per iteration; the paper correctly notes that “persistent” sample pools (as in PCD) are not covered.
- Assumptions and practicality: The key practical challenges are (i) identifying or estimating C and α for the chosen MCMC kernel on the chosen Θ; (ii) computing the projection ΠΘ. For Ising models with simple bound constraints, projection is trivial; spectral-norm constraints require iterative SVD-based projections (Domke–Liu). The example illustrates feasibility but also suggests conservative constants (e.g., a smaller L is used in practice). These limitations are acknowledged.
- Lower bound interpretability: Theorem 7’s lower bound is hard to parse in its current form. A more intuitive discussion of how the bound scales with ε, α, and the strong convexity parameter, and how tight it is compared to the constructive schedule (Theorem 8/Corollary 9), would improve accessibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and clearly written. Suggestions:
- Consolidate assumptions early: define all conditions on Θ, the sampler, and the projection operator in one place.
- Notation hygiene: R2 is used as the norm bound constant but expressions like R22 appear; consider renaming the bound to R or B and using B2 for its square to avoid confusion.
- Be consistent about f (negative log-likelihood) and the role of λ; explicitly state step sizes and projection details in Algorithm 1.
- Provide intuition for Theorem 7 and the β1, β2, β3 schedule parameters, possibly with a short table mapping to computation budgets.
- Fix typos and incomplete references (e.g., “Inferning” → “Inferring”; missing years/venues in the bibliography).

Originality
The notion of a tractable class defined by guaranteed fast mixing is a compelling alternative to structure-based tractability. The convergence guarantees for MCMC-based approximate gradients within fast-mixing parameter sets are, to my knowledge, novel in scope (general exponential families) and form (FPRAS-type bounds with explicit K, M, v schedules). The work complements Steinhardt–Liang’s restart-chain formulation by analyzing more standard MCMC learning with projection to fast-mixing sets.

Significance
The paper fills a theoretical gap by providing end-to-end guarantees for a widely used but often heuristic practice: using MCMC to approximate likelihood gradients in high-treewidth models. The results may influence:
- The design of regularizers/projections that promote fast mixing (beyond Ising models).
- Practical budgeting of samples vs. chain length vs. iterations to achieve a target accuracy.
- Understanding the trade-offs between fast-mixing constraints and model expressivity.

Limitations temper immediate practical impact: reliance on known mixing constants, potential expense of projection for complex Θ, and exclusion of persistent chains/contrastive divergence settings with shared sample pools or hidden variables. Nonetheless, as a theoretical contribution, it is strong and likely to be built upon.

Pros
- Clear, rigorous analysis yielding high-probability convergence and FPRAS guarantees.
- General applicability to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Insightful schedules and near-matching lower bounds.
- Bridges MCMC mixing-time theory with learning guarantees.

Cons
- Practical use requires knowledge or estimates of mixing parameters (C, α) and efficient projections.
- Theorem 7 is difficult to interpret; more intuition is needed.
- Some notational inconsistencies and incomplete references.
- Does not cover persistent MCMC/PCD-style algorithms commonly used in practice.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide guidance on estimating C and α in practice, or discuss robustness to misspecified mixing rates.
- Elaborate on how to construct Θ for models beyond pairwise Ising/Potts, and on the complexity of the projection operator for those Θ.
- Discuss extensions to persistent chains or sample-pool methods, even if only as conjectures or partial results.
- Clarify the dependence on dimension N (e.g., via R and mixing-time bounds) and whether adaptive schedules for K, M, v based on observed mixing diagnostics are feasible within your framework.
- Tighten and complete the bibliography, and standardize notation (especially the constant denoting the bound on ||t(x)||).

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a well-motivated and technically solid contribution that advances our theoretical understanding of maximum likelihood learning with MCMC in high-treewidth models, introducing a useful tractable class and delivering meaningful convergence guarantees.
