This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, enabling efficient inference via Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). The core idea – leveraging fast mixing to obtain theoretical guarantees for gradient-based learning – is a valuable contribution. The authors prove a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood estimation within this set, providing convergence rates for both unregularized and ridge-regularized cases. The work extends beyond graphical models, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a key challenge in learning with complex models. The mathematical treatment appears rigorous, and the theorems are clearly stated (though, see Clarity comments below). The demonstration of an FPRAS is a significant theoretical result. The connection to existing work on related approximation schemes (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993) is explicitly made, establishing the context within the broader field of intractable inference. The example provided at the end is helpful in illustrating the practical potential of the approach.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough engagement with the existing literature on stochastic approximation and Monte Carlo EM algorithms. While the authors acknowledge related work, the connection to established results feels superficial. The manuscript does not adequately position its contributions relative to the extensive body of literature on optimizing intractable likelihoods using iterative methods with Markovian dynamics. For instance, the work of Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret offers a broader theoretical framework that may encompass the results presented here, and should be discussed.  The assumption of uniform ergodicity, and the re-initialization of the Markov chain, warrant more detailed justification in light of prior work that explores alternatives like re-initializing with the previous sample. 

Regarding clarity, while the theorems themselves are well-stated, the assumptions underpinning them could be made more explicit within each theorem's statement.  Currently, readers are required to refer back and forth between the theorem and the preceding text to fully grasp the conditions for its validity.  A more self-contained presentation would enhance readability.

In terms of originality, while the core idea of leveraging fast mixing for gradient-based learning is valuable, the novelty of the results is somewhat diminished by the limited comparison to existing literature. The work doesn’t fully establish how it advances beyond, or differs significantly from, already established techniques.

Overall, the significance of this work lies in providing theoretical guarantees for a commonly used practical approach (MCMC-based gradient descent). The FPRAS result is a strong theoretical contribution. However, a more comprehensive literature review and a clearer articulation of the work’s novelty would significantly strengthen the manuscript.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical guarantees (FPRAS) for MCMC-based learning.
*   Applies to a broad class of exponential families, not just graphical models.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in statistical inference.
*   Clear mathematical presentation of the core results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited engagement with and acknowledgement of relevant literature on stochastic approximation and Monte Carlo EM.
*   Novelty of the results is not fully established in relation to prior work.
*   Presentation could be improved by making assumptions more explicit within the theorem statements.
