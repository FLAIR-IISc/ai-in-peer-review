

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical model learning: maximum likelihood estimation in high-treewidth undirected models. The authors propose a novel approach to this problem by shifting focus from tractable parameter spaces (like tree-structured models) to "fast-mixing parameter sets" where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. This work extends beyond graphical models to the general exponential family framework, making it more broadly applicable.

The paper makes two key theoretical contributions: (1) it proves that when parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning; (2) it establishes precise computational complexity bounds for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases. The authors show that for the unregularized case, achieving ε-accuracy in log-likelihood requires O(1/ε³) total computational effort, while the ridge-regularized case achieves ε-accuracy in parameter distance with O(1/ε²) effort.

The paper relates well to prior work in several important ways. It builds upon the foundational work of Jerrum and Sinclair (1993) on computational complexity of graphical models, while contrasting with standard approaches like the Chow-Liu algorithm for tree-structured models. The authors also engage with recent work on fast-mixing Markov chains (e.g., Liu and Domke, 2014; Domke and Liu, 2013) and MCMC-based learning methods (e.g., Steinhardt and Liang, 2014). Unlike previous methods that assume well-specified models or focus on specific graphical structures, this work provides a general framework applicable to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.

The paper excels in quality and technical rigor. The theoretical results are well-motivated, carefully proved (with detailed proof sketches provided), and the complexity bounds are derived with appropriate mathematical care. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature, particularly in the mixing time theory for Markov chains. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized exposition, clear notation, and effective use of figures (Fig. 1 and Fig. 2) to illustrate key concepts. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results, satisfying the clarity criterion.

The originality is significant. While previous work has explored restricting parameters to tree-structured spaces (Chow-Liu, Heinemann and Globerson), this paper introduces a fundamentally different notion of tractability based on fast-mixing properties. This approach offers a theoretically grounded alternative to existing methods that typically assume well-specified models or have inferior sample complexity. The paper properly contextualizes its contribution relative to recent NIPS work, particularly distinguishing itself from methods like Abbeel et al. (2006) and Mizrahi et al. (2014).

The significance of this work is primarily theoretical, though the authors appropriately acknowledge its limited immediate practical impact. The paper establishes a theoretical foundation for understanding when MCMC-based learning algorithms can be guaranteed to converge to good solutions, which is a valuable contribution to the theory of graphical models and learning algorithms. While the practical applicability is limited by the difficulty of enforcing the fast-mixing constraints in real-world applications, the theoretical results provide a benchmark for future work on MCMC-based learning methods.

The main weaknesses are in practical relevance and the feasibility of enforcing the fast-mixing constraints. As the authors note, ensuring that parameters lie within a fast-mixing set is challenging in practice. While they acknowledge this limitation, the paper does not sufficiently explore how such constraints might be enforced or whether they could be incorporated into learning algorithms. The draft review correctly identifies this as a major limitation, but the authors might have more thoroughly investigated whether their theoretical constraints could be approximated through additional regularization or other practical techniques. The paper's example in Section 6 demonstrates a theoretically sound but highly conservative implementation, suggesting the theoretical bounds may be overly pessimistic for practical applications.

The draft review incorrectly characterizes the ridge-regularized case as "non-convex" when it is actually strongly convex (a key aspect of the paper's analysis). This mischaracterization weakens the review's credibility. The paper's theoretical contribution is more substantial than the draft suggests, as the strongly convex case yields significantly better convergence rates (O(1/ε²) vs. O(1/ε³)).

In summary, this is a technically strong paper that makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding the conditions under which MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning can be guaranteed to work. While the practical relevance is limited by the difficulty of enforcing the fast-mixing constraints, the theoretical results are rigorous and well-motivated. The paper is well-written, properly contextualized, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. The main weakness is the lack of practical guidance for enforcing the fast-mixing constraints, but this is a common challenge in theoretical work that may be addressed in future applications.

I recommend acceptance as a theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of MCMC-based learning in graphical models. The paper's clear exposition and rigorous proofs make it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, particularly for researchers interested in the theoretical foundations of learning algorithms.
