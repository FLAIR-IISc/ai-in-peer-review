

The manuscript proposes a stochastic optimization algorithm for intractable likelihoods, but its core contribution is undermined by a significant failure to engage with foundational literature. The algorithm is a direct special case of Monte Carlo EM (MCEM) and more broadly, of stochastic approximation methods with Markovian dynamics—well-established approaches for optimizing intractable objectives via Markov chain approximations. The authors' work lacks meaningful comparison to this extensive literature, rendering their novelty claim unsubstantiated. For instance, the seminal work of Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret (1990) provides a comprehensive theory for such algorithms, including convergence under minimal ergodicity conditions (e.g., non-uniform ergodicity suffices, avoiding the authors’ restrictive uniform ergodicity assumption). Crucially, their reinitialization strategy—resetting the Markov chain to a fixed distribution *r* at each iteration—is explicitly acknowledged as a simplification, yet this approach is both unnecessary and suboptimal. Prior work (e.g., Andrieu & Roberts, 2009; Douc et al., 2009) demonstrates that adaptive reinitialization using the *previous sample* (as briefly noted in the manuscript’s conclusion) yields superior theoretical guarantees and practical performance. The authors’ choice to ignore this literature and instead impose an artificial reinitialization scheme weakens their analysis without justification.

The presentation of theoretical results further obscures the work’s limitations. For Theorems 1 and 2, the assumptions are not clearly stated in the theorem statements themselves but must be inferred from surrounding text—a significant flaw in exposition. Readers should not need to cross-reference sections to understand the conditions under which results hold. Moreover, the manuscript relies on unnecessarily strong assumptions (e.g., uniform ergodicity, fixed reinitialization) that are explicitly relaxed in existing theory. Theorem 6’s analysis, for example, depends on restrictive conditions that are not required in the general framework of Benveniste et al. (1990) or subsequent refinements. The authors cite no prior work addressing the specific settings they consider, yet their results are directly subsumed by these established theories. This suggests the manuscript does not advance the field but merely rederives known results under artificial constraints.

The lack of engagement with the literature is particularly striking given the algorithm’s conceptual simplicity. The authors’ approach is not a novel algorithmic framework but a constrained instance of MCEM, where the Markov chain is reinitialized at each iteration to a fixed *r*—a detail that, while noted as a simplification, is not justified against existing alternatives. The manuscript’s acknowledgment that adaptive reinitialization (using prior samples) is preferable but omitted due to complexity *should* have prompted a comparative discussion with established methods, not a mere footnote. Instead, the work presents this as a novel technical choice, when it is a well-documented heuristic. This oversight extends to the theoretical analysis: the authors derive convergence rates under uniform ergodicity, yet the literature has long established convergence under weaker conditions (e.g., geometric ergodicity), with explicit error bounds for MCEM (e.g., Fessler & Hero, 1994; Wei & Tanner, 1990). The absence of such comparisons makes it impossible to assess whether the authors’ results offer any practical or theoretical advantage.

The manuscript’s technical execution compounds these issues. Theorem 3’s proof sketch is incomplete, and the convergence rate in Theorem 4 lacks clarity on dependence on problem-specific parameters (e.g., chain mixing time). The empirical results also fail to compare against standard MCEM implementations or variants using adaptive reinitialization, which would be essential to validate the authors’ claims. Without such benchmarks, the experimental section merely demonstrates algorithmic feasibility, not superiority. This is especially problematic given the authors’ assertion that their method "addresses key limitations" of prior work—yet no limitations of existing methods are identified or addressed.

In summary, the manuscript’s central flaw is a profound disconnect from the literature on stochastic approximation and MCEM. The algorithm is not novel, the theoretical results are redundant given established frameworks (e.g., Benveniste et al., 1990), and the presentation obscures rather than clarifies the relationship to prior work. The authors’ reliance on non-standard assumptions (e.g., fixed reinitialization, uniform ergodicity) without justification, coupled with a failure to reference foundational papers, severely undermines the contribution. The work would be appropriate for a technical report or conference proceeding with a clear literature review, but as a NIPS submission, it lacks the novelty and rigor expected. The results are marginal at best, as they are either subsumed by existing theory or require artificial constraints that do not generalize. To strengthen the manuscript, the authors must: (1) thoroughly compare their approach to MCEM and stochastic approximation literature; (2) replace unnecessary assumptions with standard ones (e.g., use adaptive reinitialization); and (3) clarify all theorem assumptions. Without these revisions, the paper does not advance the field and risks misrepresenting the state of the art.
