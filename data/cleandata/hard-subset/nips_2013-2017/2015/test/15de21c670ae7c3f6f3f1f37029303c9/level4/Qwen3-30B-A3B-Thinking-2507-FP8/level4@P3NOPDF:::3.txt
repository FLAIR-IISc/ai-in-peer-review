

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Markov network learning: weight optimization via gradient-based methods, where the gradient computation is typically intractable and relies on MCMC approximations. The authors establish theoretical conditions under which such MCMC-based weight learning algorithms yield a Fully Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). Specifically, they prove that if the MCMC chain converges rapidly to stationarity for the current parameter settings, the gradient approximation becomes sufficiently accurate to ensure FPRAS guarantees. The analysis distinguishes between the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (non-convex) cases, deriving separate convergence bounds for each.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with only minor typographical errors. While I have not verified every technical detail of the proofs, they appear logically sound and plausible within the stated assumptions. The authors' acknowledgment of the practical limitations of their theoretical results is commendable and aligns with the paper's core contribution.

However, the primary limitation lies in the practical irrelevance of the key condition: rapid MCMC convergence for the current parameter settings. This condition is highly challenging to ensure in practice. The authors note this limitation, but the paper does not adequately address how such a condition could be enforced or verified in real-world applications. For instance, could one artificially impose constraints on the parameter space to guarantee fast mixing? If so, would this require additional regularization beyond the ridge term already considered? Crucially, introducing further regularization (e.g., via ridge regression) risks over-regularization, potentially compromising model accuracy or flexibility. The authors provide no discussion of this trade-off or potential mitigation strategies.

The theoretical results are mathematically elegant and formally establish a connection between MCMC convergence rates and the approximation guarantees of the learning algorithm. Yet, without a viable path to satisfy the required conditions in practice, the contribution remains largely theoretical. For a machine learning venue like NIPS, the gap between the derived conditions and deployable algorithms is substantial. Practical weight learning methods (e.g., in log-linear models) typically rely on heuristics for MCMC convergence or alternative approximation techniques, not on conditions that are difficult to verify or enforce. The authors’ condition—requiring fast mixing *for the current parameters*—is particularly problematic, as MCMC convergence is notoriously sensitive to parameter values and often unpredictable without extensive empirical tuning.

The paper’s strongest value lies in formalizing the precise theoretical boundary for when MCMC-based learning can be trusted to yield approximate solutions with probabilistic guarantees. But this formalization does not translate into a practical algorithm. To elevate this work, the authors would need to: (1) propose a method to enforce the fast-mixing condition (e.g., via adaptive parameter constraints or specialized MCMC designs), and (2) demonstrate that this enforcement avoids over-regularization while maintaining accuracy. Without addressing these points, the results remain an interesting theoretical curiosity rather than a foundation for practical weight learning.

In summary, this is a well-written paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution by characterizing the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning achieves FPRAS guarantees. The proofs appear rigorous, and the distinction between convex and non-convex cases is valuable. However, the core condition for these guarantees is impractical to satisfy, and the authors do not provide a pathway to bridge this gap. The paper would benefit significantly from a discussion of how to practically enforce the required conditions or from empirical evidence showing that such enforcement is feasible in common scenarios. As it stands, the theoretical results lack immediate applicability, and the paper does not resolve the central challenge of making MCMC-based weight learning both theoretically sound and practically useful. For NIPS, this work falls short of offering a compelling advance for real-world applications, though it provides a useful benchmark for future research on the interplay between MCMC convergence and learning guarantees.
