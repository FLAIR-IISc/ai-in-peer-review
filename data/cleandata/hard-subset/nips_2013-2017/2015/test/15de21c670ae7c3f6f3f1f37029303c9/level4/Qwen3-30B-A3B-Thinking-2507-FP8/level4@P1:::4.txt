

This paper introduces a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by focusing on "fast-mixing parameter sets" rather than traditional tractable structures like trees. The authors propose that when model parameters are constrained to a set where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) mixing time is guaranteed to be fast, gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximations can efficiently converge to the maximum likelihood solution. This work extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, establishing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for learning within these constrained parameter sets.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC approximation, which is widely used in practice but lacks formal justification. The authors establish bounds for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, showing that the total computational effort scales as O(1/ε³ log(1/ε)) and O(1/ε² log(1/ε)) respectively. This is a significant theoretical advance that fills an important gap in the literature on learning intractable models using MCMC.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature. It builds on the long-standing challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth models (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993), while offering a fundamentally different approach from traditional methods like pseudolikelihood (Besag, 1974) or score matching (Hyvärinen, 2005). The authors' framing of tractability through mixing times rather than structural constraints (e.g., tree structures) represents a meaningful conceptual shift from prior work, including Heinemann & Globerson (2014) on high-girth models and Steinhardt & Liang (2014) on fast-mixing Markov chains.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical results are technically sound and rigorously proven, with careful attention to the interplay between mixing times, gradient approximation errors, and convergence rates. The analysis of both convex and strongly convex cases is thorough, and the explicit parameter schedules (Theorems 4-5 and 8-9) provide concrete guidance for practitioners. The paper also demonstrates the practical relevance of the theory through a well-chosen Ising model example (Section 6), showing that the theoretical bounds, while somewhat conservative, are reasonable in practice.

The main weakness lies in the presentation of lower bounds (Theorem 7), which are difficult to interpret without additional context. While the authors provide a detailed theoretical derivation, a more intuitive discussion of why these lower bounds matter, and how they relate to the upper bounds, would significantly strengthen the paper. The current presentation makes it challenging for readers to assess the tightness of the authors' results or understand how they compare to potential alternative approaches.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and precise mathematical exposition. The notation is consistently defined, and the proofs are logically structured. The connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation is well-maintained throughout, making the paper accessible to both theoretical and applied researchers.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a fundamental limitation in the practical application of MCMC for learning intractable models. By establishing theoretical guarantees for a widely used approach, it provides a foundation for more reliable and efficient learning algorithms in high-treewidth settings. While the paper acknowledges the theoretical nature of its contribution, it would be strengthened by more explicit discussion of potential applications and future research directions. For instance, how might these results inform the design of more efficient contrastive divergence variants, or extend to models with latent variables? Such discussion would better highlight the paper's impact on the broader machine learning community.

The originality of the work is clear: the concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" as a tractable class for learning is novel, and the theoretical results establishing convergence rates for learning within this class represent a significant advancement. The authors properly contextualize their work against related literature, including the recent NIPS 2014 paper by Steinhardt & Liang on fast-mixing models.

In summary, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature on learning intractable graphical models. The results are technically sound, the presentation is clear, and the approach offers a new perspective on tractability that complements existing work. While the lower bound discussion could be improved, the paper's core contributions are significant and well-supported. I believe this work would be valuable to the NIPS community and should be accepted.
