

Summary: The paper proposes a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. Finding the maximum likelihood solution is generally intractable. From a convex optimization perspective, the core challenge lies in computing an integral with respect to the current exponential family parameter. By assuming that Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) mixes rapidly for all parameters in the domain, the authors demonstrate that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be computed with sufficient precision. When combined with the results of Schmidt et al. (2011), this yields a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for computing the maximum likelihood estimate (MLE). The analysis covers both convex and strongly convex cases, leading to distinct guarantees: the former bounds the likelihood error, while the latter bounds the parameter error. A simple experimental demonstration of the theory is also provided.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution. The approach is well-motivated as a viable alternative to relying on small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analyses appear sound, though I have not verified the proofs in detail; they essentially combine the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and Schmidt et al.'s results. While the paper synthesizes existing techniques, the combination is nontrivial. I appreciate the authors' honesty in discussing the approach's limitations, including a thoughtful analysis of the weaknesses, the overly pessimistic nature of some theoretical assumptions, and promising directions for future work. The paper also provides valuable insights into contrastive divergence (CD)-type algorithms, even though the analyzed algorithm is not precisely CD.

The introduction is well-written and contains substantial references to prior work. However, the technical presentation has minor clarity issues. Specifically, the convexity of the log-likelihood (a premise of the entire paper) should be explicitly stated: the second and third terms in Equation (1) are clearly convex, and the convexity of $A(\theta)$ follows because its Hessian is the covariance of the sufficient statistics, hence positive semidefinite. A clear summary of all assumptions would also be beneficial; notably, the convexity of the parameter set $\Theta$ is never explicitly mentioned in the main text.

While the results represent an original and substantial theoretical contribution, their practical value appears limited due to the extreme difficulty of verifying the fast-mixing assumption for MCMC. The paper thus replaces one hard problem (computing the MLE) with another (establishing fast mixing for the MCMC chain). Nevertheless, converting one hard problem into another is often useful, making the paper's results valuable to the community.
