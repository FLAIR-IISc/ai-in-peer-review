A key challenge in Markov network learning is weight estimation, which aims to maximize the likelihood by finding optimal weight settings for the network’s features. Gradient descent is commonly employed for this task, with Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) methods often used to approximate the gradient due to the intractability of exact computation. 

This paper presents theoretical results demonstrating that, under the condition of rapid MCMC convergence on the model’s parameters, the resulting weight learning algorithm – leveraging MCMC for gradient estimation – constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS). The analysis is conducted for both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) scenarios, with distinct bounds derived for each.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured, though a few minor typos were noted. While a complete verification of all proofs was not possible, the presented arguments appear plausible and logically sound. The paper effectively connects the concept of fast-mixing parameters to the theoretical guarantees of gradient-based learning.

A significant limitation, acknowledged by the authors, is the practical relevance of the core assumption – ensuring MCMC mixes rapidly on the learned parameters. It remains unclear how easily this condition can be enforced in real-world scenarios. It would be valuable to explore strategies for artificially imposing this constraint, perhaps through regularization techniques, and assess the potential for over-regularization when combined with existing methods like ridge regression. Addressing this challenge would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.

In summary, this is a solid theoretical contribution that formalizes the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning achieves FPRAS guarantees, both with and without regularization. However, the practical applicability of these conditions remains a substantial open question. Further research is needed to bridge the gap between the theoretical results and the development of practical, accurate weight learning algorithms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents rigorous theoretical results establishing FPRAS guarantees under specific conditions.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The concepts are explained clearly and the paper is well-organized.
*   **Novelty:** The work explores a novel notion of “fast-mixing parameters” for tractable learning.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately situates itself within the existing literature on graphical model learning and MCMC methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practical Relevance:** The key assumption of fast mixing is difficult to ensure in practice and limits the immediate applicability of the results.
*   **Limited Practical Evaluation:** While the authors acknowledge the lack of practical contribution, some preliminary exploration of enforcing the fast-mixing condition could strengthen the work.
