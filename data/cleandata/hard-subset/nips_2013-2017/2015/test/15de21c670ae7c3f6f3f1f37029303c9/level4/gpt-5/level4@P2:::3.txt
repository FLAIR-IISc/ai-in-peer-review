Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies maximum likelihood learning for exponential families (including undirected graphical models) when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting to tree-structured models (e.g., Chow–Liu) or using surrogate objectives such as pseudolikelihood (Besag), score matching (Hyvärinen), or composite likelihood (Varin, Lindsay), the authors constrain parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ for which a chosen MCMC chain mixes rapidly. Under bounded sufficient statistics and an ability to project onto Θ, they analyze projected gradient descent that estimates gradients via MCMC samples, and show it constitutes an FPRAS for the constrained MLE. They provide separate results for the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case. For the unregularized likelihood, attaining ε suboptimality in objective value requires total sampling effort roughly cubic in 1/ε (up to logs); with ridge regularization, achieving ε accuracy in parameter distance requires effort quadratic in 1/ε (up to logs). The analysis gives concrete schedules for the number of iterations, samples per iteration, and chain length, and near-matching lower bounds. The development leverages mixing-time bounds for Gibbs and related chains (e.g., Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum, Jerrum–Sinclair) and connects to prior work on projecting to fast-mixing sets (Domke & Liu) and learning with restart chains (Steinhardt & Liang).

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: reframing tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets rather than graph structure or surrogate losses, and showing MCMC-based gradient descent can be made theoretically sound under these constraints.
- Generality: the results apply to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not only specific graphical models.
- Technical contributions: clear convergence guarantees for both convex and strongly convex settings; explicit schedules and near-matching lower bounds; use of standard tools (concentration inequalities, inexact gradient descent) in a careful way.
- Relation to literature: situates the work among Chow–Liu, pseudolikelihood/score/composite likelihood, correlation-decay approaches, and mixing-time results; acknowledges connections to contrastive divergence variants.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Practical relevance: verifying that parameters lie in a fast-mixing set Θ and obtaining usable constants (C, α) is difficult in realistic models; projecting to Θ may be computationally costly and can significantly bias the model (potential over-regularization).
- Assumptions: bounded sufficient statistics and known projection oracles are strong; many useful models (e.g., with hidden variables) are not covered.
- Algorithmic mismatch with practice: the analysis assumes independent fresh chains per iteration and does not capture persistent chains or sample pools commonly used in practice; the theory does not quantify benefits of data-dependent initialization (e.g., contrastive divergence).
- Empirics: only a small illustrative example is shown; no systematic experiments to assess trade-offs between projection strength, accuracy, and runtime, nor comparison to pseudolikelihood/score matching under similar constraints.
- Constants and chain choice: guidance on selecting the MCMC kernel and estimating mixing rates in practice is limited; the complexity bounds hide potentially large constants.

Quality:
The technical development appears careful and builds on standard results; proofs seem plausible, though I did not verify all details. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the limitations are candidly discussed. There are some typos and minor presentation issues, but the paper is overall technically sound.

Clarity:
Generally well written and organized. It would help to:
- Emphasize that ridge regularization yields strong convexity (the “non-convex” phrasing should be avoided).
- Make the projection requirement more concrete beyond specific Ising examples; summarize available projection algorithms and their complexity.
- Explain how C and α are obtained or estimated in practice for different chains.
- Discuss the computational cost of projection in the overall complexity, at least at a high level.

Originality:
Moderately high. While fast-mixing constraints and mixing-time bounds are known, using them to provide FPRAS-style guarantees for MLE with MCMC-estimated gradients across general exponential families, together with explicit schedules and lower bounds, is a valuable contribution.

Significance:
Primarily theoretical. The results clarify when common MCMC-in-the-loop learning strategies are principled. However, the strong assumptions and difficulty of enforcing/estimating fast-mixing constraints limit immediate practical impact. With further development on how to construct and project onto Θ in realistic settings, the approach could influence practice.

Suggestions and questions for the authors:
- Provide practical guidance for constructing Θ beyond Ising (e.g., spectral-norm or block-wise constraints for general pairwise MRFs), and discuss projection algorithms and their scaling.
- Explore adaptive or data-driven ways to enforce fast-mixing constraints (e.g., gradually tightening norm bounds, surrogate penalties) and examine the bias–variance trade-off.
- Discuss estimating or upper-bounding mixing rates online (e.g., via coupling diagnostics) to set K, M, v without relying on unknown constants.
- Evaluate persistent-chain variants or sample-pool methods; can the analysis be extended to dependent samples?
- Include empirical comparisons with pseudolikelihood/score matching under comparable constraints to illustrate accuracy vs. tractability trade-offs on moderate-size graphs.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, general theoretical framework tying MCMC mixing to provable learning guarantees.
- Concrete convergence rates with explicit schedules and near-matching lower bounds.
- Broad applicability to exponential families with bounded statistics.
- Careful discussion of assumptions and limitations.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited practical applicability; constructing/projecting onto Θ and obtaining mixing constants is challenging.
- The algorithmic setting diverges from common practice (persistent chains, CD), and the theory does not cover these variants.
- Empirical validation is minimal and does not demonstrate benefits over strong baselines.
- Potential over-regularization from enforcing fast-mixing constraints remains unresolved.

Overall assessment:
A well-executed theoretical paper that formalizes when MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning admits FPRAS-type guarantees under fast-mixing parameter constraints. The contribution is conceptually interesting and technically solid, but practical relevance is currently limited by strong assumptions and the difficulty of enforcing/estimating fast mixing in real models.
