The primary weakness of this manuscript is its limited engagement with the extensive literature on stochastic approximation algorithms for optimizing intractable likelihoods, particularly Monte Carlo EM (MCEM). The proposed algorithm is closely related to, and appears to be a specific instance of, the general class of stochastic approximation algorithms with Markovian dynamics. A comprehensive body of work exists on this topic, and the authors must situate their contribution within this context by providing a thorough comparison to existing methods.

Furthermore, a well-established theoretical framework already exists for analyzing such algorithms, often under much weaker conditions than those presented here. This existing theory already encompasses some of the authors' results while covering much more general scenarios. For example:

1.  **Ergodicity Conditions:** Existing analyses do not typically require the underlying Markov chains to be uniformly ergodic, which is a strong assumption.

2.  **Chain Initialization:** The authors' strategy of re-initializing the Markov chain from a fixed distribution `r` at each iteration is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this is done to simplify the analysis, this simplification comes at the cost of generality and potentially practical performance. Standard practice, supported by extensive analysis in the literature, often involves "warm-starting" the chain from the previous iteration's state, which can lead to faster convergence. These variants have also been thoroughly analyzed.

The presentation of the results also detracts from the paper's clarity. For instance, the assumptions for Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 are not clearly stated within the theorems themselves. While these assumptions may be found in the surrounding text, forcing the reader to search for them is not ideal and hinders a clear understanding of the claims.

Finally, the theoretical analysis relies on unnecessarily strong assumptions. The conditions required for results like Theorem 6, for instance, are much stricter than what is known to be necessary. More general results for similar settings can be found in foundational texts on stochastic approximation, such as Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret's "Adaptive Algorithms and Stochastic Approximations."

In summary, the manuscript's primary shortcomings are its failure to connect with a large and relevant body of prior work and, as a consequence, the marginal novelty of its contributions. The proposed algorithm and its analysis appear to re-derive results for a specialized case of a well-understood class of methods, without acknowledging the more general and powerful frameworks that already exist.
