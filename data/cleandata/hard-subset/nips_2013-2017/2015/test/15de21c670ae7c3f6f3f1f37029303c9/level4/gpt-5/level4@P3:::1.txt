Summary
The paper studies maximum-likelihood (ML) learning for exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting to low-treewidth structures, the authors constrain parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ: for any θ ∈ Θ, a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast with known constants (C, α), and there is an oracle to project onto Θ. They analyze projected gradient descent where the likelihood gradient is approximated by MCMC samples that are reinitialized from a fixed distribution at each iteration. Under bounded sufficient statistics and fast mixing, they prove high-probability convergence to the ML solution within Θ. In the unregularized (convex) case they give an effort bound cubic in 1/ε (up to logs) for ε-suboptimality in objective value; with ridge regularization (strong convexity), they achieve quadratic dependence in 1/ε for parameter error. They also provide matching lower bounds up to constants/logs and explicit schedules for the number of iterations, samples, and mixing steps. The theory is presented for general exponential families, with examples and projection constructions for Ising models.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to a large body of work on MCMC-based likelihood optimization and stochastic approximation with Markovian noise, including MCMC maximum likelihood (Geyer, 1991; Gu & Zhu, 2001), Monte Carlo EM/SAEM for latent-variable models (Delyon, Lavielle & Moulines, 1999), and extensive theory on stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics (Benveniste, Métivier & Priouret, 1990; Kushner & Yin, 2003; Andrieu & Moulines, 2005). For energy-based models, persistent sampling schemes and their analyses (e.g., Younes, 1999; related to persistent contrastive divergence) address the dependence across iterations that arises when samples are not reinitialized. Compared with this literature, the main novelty here is to (i) formalize a projection-based restriction to fast-mixing parameter sets and (ii) provide complexity upper/lower bounds that depend explicitly on mixing rates and projection oracles, yielding an FPRAS-style statement for constrained ML. However, many of the technical tools (error decomposition for biased MCMC gradients, uniform geometric ergodicity, step-size/schedule selection, strong/weak convexity arguments) are standard in the stochastic approximation literature and available under weaker conditions than the paper assumes.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of “fast-mixing parameter sets” and the role of a projection oracle; the Ising examples (spectral/induced norms) help ground the concept.
- Generality: analysis applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.
- End-to-end complexity guarantees with explicit schedules, and lower bounds that nearly match the upper bounds (up to constants/logs).
- Separation of convex vs. strongly convex regimes and explicit dependence on mixing constants.
- The results help rationalize widely used “MCMC-in-the-loop” ML procedures and clarify how to tune K, M, and v to achieve target accuracy.

Weaknesses
- Limited awareness and positioning relative to prior theory on stochastic approximation with Markovian noise. Similar convergence guarantees (and under weaker assumptions than uniform geometric ergodicity and per-iteration reinitialization) have been established (e.g., Benveniste–Métivier–Priouret; Kushner–Yin; Andrieu–Moulines). The paper should clarify what is new beyond casting these results into a fast-mixing-projection framework and deriving the specific FPRAS-style complexity.
- The assumption that chains are reinitialized from a fixed distribution at each iteration, with independent samples per iteration, is mainly for analysis convenience and is at odds with practical algorithms that recycle states (“persistent” chains). Existing analyses cover Markovian dependence across iterations; the paper could substantially strengthen its contribution by leveraging those tools.
- Theorem statements are underspecified: key assumptions (bounded sufficient statistics, existence of projection oracle onto Θ, geometric mixing with known C, α, choice of MCMC kernel, step-size rules) should be stated explicitly within each theorem. As written, readers must hunt for conditions across sections.
- Practicality concerns: the guarantees depend on mixing constants that are rarely known or tight in realistic models, and on nontrivial projection oracles for general exponential families (beyond specific Ising cases). The complexity of projection is largely ignored in the overall effort accounting.
- Empirical validation is minimal (a small illustrative Ising example), offering limited evidence about practical gains over pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood or persistent MCMC-ML in realistic settings.

Quality
- Technical development appears sound, and the upper/lower bound structure is coherent. However, the analysis uses stronger assumptions than necessary given existing SA theory, and the paper would benefit from tighter integration with that literature.
- Proof clarity is acceptable, but key assumptions should be front-loaded in theorem statements. Algorithmic details (Algorithm 1) and parameter schedules should be made fully explicit.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but the presentation can be improved by:
  - Stating all assumptions at the top of each theorem.
  - Clearly specifying the projection oracle and its computational cost.
  - Distinguishing what is assumed known (C, α) vs. what is selected by the practitioner (K, M, v, step sizes).

Originality
- Moderate. The fast-mixing projection viewpoint and FPRAS framing are interesting, but much of the convergence machinery is well known in the SA/MCML literature and can be invoked under weaker conditions.

Significance
- Potentially useful as a conceptual and pedagogical contribution clarifying the role of fast mixing in ML learning and offering explicit complexity trade-offs. Practical impact is limited without broader empirical validation and without addressing persistent chains and weaker mixing assumptions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand and integrate related work: MCML (Geyer, 1991), SAEM (Delyon et al., 1999), stochastic approximation with Markovian noise (Benveniste et al., 1990; Kushner & Yin, 2003; Andrieu & Moulines, 2005), and analyses of persistent sampling in energy-based models (e.g., Younes, 1999). Clearly delineate what is new here.
- Relax assumptions by adopting SA tools that handle Markovian dependence across iterations and non-uniform ergodicity; analyze the “sample pool”/persistent-initialization strategy that practitioners use.
- State all assumptions within Theorems 1, 2, and 6; define the MCMC kernel, mixing notion, and projection oracle explicitly at the point of use.
- Discuss how C and α would be estimated or bounded in practice, and how sensitive the schedules are to misspecification.
- Provide more substantive experiments comparing against pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood, MCML with recycled chains, and persistent CD, measuring both accuracy and wall-clock effort, and report the cost of projection.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean formalization of learning within fast-mixing parameter sets.
- General exponential-family analysis with explicit complexity bounds and near-matching lower bounds.
- Could help bridge practice and theory in MCMC-based ML optimization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established SA/MCML theory; stronger assumptions than necessary.
- Analysis excludes the practically important persistent sampling regime despite existing tools to handle it.
- Sparse empirical support and limited guidance on obtaining/using mixing constants and projection oracles in real problems.
