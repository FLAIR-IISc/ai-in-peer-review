## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission: [Paper Title - *Please fill in*]

This paper presents convergence rate guarantees for learning algorithms applied to a novel class of tractable models defined by their efficient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) approximability. The authors demonstrate meaningful and useful bounds on the convergence rates, framing the problem in terms of “fast mixing” model classes. Overall, the work appears original and fills a gap in the literature concerning learning intractable models using MCMC methods. I recommend acceptance.

**Quality:**

The core results – convergence rates expressed in terms of mixing times – are a significant contribution. The bounds themselves appear well-motivated and potentially valuable for analyzing and designing learning algorithms for models within this framework. However, Theorem 7, presenting a lower bound, requires further clarification. Its current presentation is difficult to interpret, and a more in-depth discussion of its implications would significantly strengthen the paper. Expanding on the relationship between this lower bound and existing lower bounds applicable to similar problems would be particularly beneficial. While the technical aspects appear sound, increasing the accessibility of the lower bound analysis is crucial.

**Clarity:**

The presentation is generally clear and well-structured. The authors effectively convey the core ideas and the mathematical derivations are reasonably easy to follow, given the complexity of the subject matter. 

**Originality:**

The framing of the problem through the lens of a “fast mixing” model class is a novel and insightful approach.  This provides a principled way to define tractability in the context of MCMC. Furthermore, the theoretical results – specifically the convergence rate guarantees – appear genuinely original. The work does not simply apply existing techniques but instead develops a new theoretical framework for analyzing learning in this setting.

**Significance:**

The significance of this work, while present, could be better articulated. The paper addresses a clear need within the literature: a rigorous understanding of learning in models where exact computation is intractable but MCMC provides reasonable approximations. This is a common scenario in many complex machine learning applications. The introduction of this new class of tractable models – those based on good MCMC approximability of expectations – is a valuable contribution.

To enhance the paper's impact, I recommend a more thorough discussion of its potential applications and broader implications. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on how these theoretical results can guide the design of practical learning algorithms. Furthermore, exploring potential future extensions of this work would be beneficial. For example, could the framework be extended to handle more complex MCMC algorithms beyond Metropolis-Hastings, or could it be applied to problems beyond parameter estimation? Discussing opportunities for building upon this work would strengthen the perception of its long-term significance. 

**Summary:**

This submission presents a novel and interesting theoretical framework for understanding learning in models amenable to MCMC approximation. The theoretical results are original and the paper fills an important gap in the literature. While the presentation of the lower bound (Theorem 7) could be improved and a more detailed discussion of potential impact would be beneficial, the overall quality of the work is high.  I believe the paper warrants acceptance to NIPS 2015 and has the potential to stimulate further research in this area.
