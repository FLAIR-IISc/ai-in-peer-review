### **Summary**

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where exact inference is typically intractable. Instead of restricting model parameters to structurally simple sets (e.g., tree-structured graphs), the authors propose a novel and compelling alternative: constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is guaranteed to converge rapidly.

The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the widely used but previously heuristic approach of performing gradient descent using gradients approximated by MCMC samples. The paper proves that this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within the fast-mixing set. The analysis is presented for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, making the results broadly applicable beyond graphical models. Two cases are considered: the unregularized (convex) case, where achieving ε-accuracy in log-likelihood requires effort cubic in 1/ε, and the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case, where achieving ε-accuracy in parameter distance requires effort quadratic in 1/ε.

### **Quality**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and rigorous piece of work. The core contribution lies in the theoretical guarantees (Theorems 2 and 6) that formalize the convergence of MCMC-based gradient descent, a method that is popular in practice but has lacked a solid theoretical foundation due to the biased nature of the gradient estimates. The proofs appear correct, building upon existing results on projected gradient descent with errors and concentration inequalities to bound the deviation from the true gradient.

The analysis is careful and detailed, providing explicit schedules for setting the number of gradient iterations (K), MCMC samples (M), and chain length (v) to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. This provides a clear, actionable recipe that connects the abstract theory to concrete algorithmic parameters.

A minor weakness is the presentation of the lower bound in Theorem 7. The result is complex and presented with minimal intuition, making it difficult for the reader to appreciate its tightness or its direct implications for the optimality of the schedules derived in Theorem 8. A more detailed discussion on the relationship between the derived upper bounds and this lower bound would strengthen the paper by contextualizing the results.

### **Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, contrasting the proposed approach with existing methods like pseudolikelihood and structurally-constrained learning. The problem is formally defined in Section 2, and the helpful table of notation greatly aids readability. The main results are stated clearly, and the proof sketches provide valuable intuition for the more involved derivations in the appendix. The example in Section 6, while simple, serves as a useful sanity check and illustrates the application of the theoretical results.

### **Originality**

The work is highly original in two respects. First, the conceptual framing of a "tractable" model class based on an algorithmic property (fast MCMC mixing) rather than a structural one (e.g., low treewidth) is a novel and powerful idea. This shifts the focus from graph structure to the dynamics of inference.

Second, while many have used MCMC to approximate gradients for learning, this paper is, to my knowledge, the first to provide rigorous, non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for this process in the form of an FPRAS. It formally bridges the gap between the theory of mixing times and the practice of learning intractable models, addressing the critical issue of biased gradient estimates arising from finite-length Markov chains. The work is well-situated within the literature and clearly articulates how it differs from and builds upon previous contributions.

### **Significance**

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a firm theoretical foundation for a widely-used class of algorithms, justifying the intuition that if MCMC mixes quickly, MCMC-based learning should converge correctly. This result is important for both theorists and practitioners.

For researchers, it opens up a new avenue for designing tractable models: instead of solely focusing on graphical structures that permit exact inference, one can focus on designing parameterizations or regularizers that provably induce fast mixing. This could inspire new research into identifying and projecting onto novel fast-mixing sets for various models of interest.

While the authors claim no significant practical contribution, the theoretical insights could guide practitioners in setting hyperparameters for MCMC-based learning more principledly. The paper's discussion of future work and limitations is honest, acknowledging issues like the complexity of the projection step and the inability of the current analysis to cover common heuristics like "sample pooling."

### **Arguments for Acceptance**
*   **Novel Conceptualization:** Introduces an original and insightful definition of tractability based on fast MCMC mixing, shifting the paradigm from structural to algorithmic constraints.
*   **Rigorous Guarantees:** Provides the first formal FPRAS guarantees for the common practice of learning with MCMC-approximated gradients, filling a crucial theoretical gap in the literature.
*   **Technical Strength:** The analysis is technically sound, comprehensive (covering both convex and strongly convex cases), and provides concrete schedules for algorithmic parameters.
*   **High Potential Impact:** The work bridges the theory of mixing times and the practice of machine learning, and is likely to inspire follow-up research on designing and learning fast-mixing models.

### **Arguments Against Acceptance**
*   **Clarity of Lower Bounds:** The interpretation and implications of the lower bound (Theorem 7) could be explained more thoroughly to better contextualize the main results.
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Impact:** The paper could benefit from a deeper discussion on how these theoretical findings might guide the design of practical learning systems beyond the provided example.

### **Recommendation**

The paper's strengths overwhelmingly outweigh its minor weaknesses. It makes a fundamental and significant theoretical contribution to the problem of learning in intractable graphical models. The novel framing of tractability and the rigorous convergence guarantees for a widely used heuristic method represent a major advance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
