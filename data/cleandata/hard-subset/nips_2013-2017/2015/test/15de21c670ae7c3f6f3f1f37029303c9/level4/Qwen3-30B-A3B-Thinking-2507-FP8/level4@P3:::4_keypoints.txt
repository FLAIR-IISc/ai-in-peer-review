- Introduces constraining parameters to "fast-mixing sets" so MCMC inference mixes rapidly, enabling tractable maximum likelihood learning in exponential families.  
- Proves an FPRAS for the maximum likelihood solution using gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximations for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.  
- Establishes convergence rates: unregularized case scales cubicly in 1/ε, ridge-regularized case scales quadratically in 1/ε.  
- Generalizes beyond graphical models to arbitrary exponential families, not limited to tree-structured models.  
- Provides explicit parameter schedules (K, M, v) to achieve ε-accuracy and bounds total computational effort (KMv).  
- Builds on prior work (Jerrum & Sinclair 1993; Domke & Liu 2013) but significantly extends theoretical guarantees for learning.  
- Technical quality is strong: rigorous proofs for Lipschitz continuity and convergence (Theorems 1–6) using Bernstein’s inequality and standard gradient analysis.  
- Only notable weakness: Theorem 7’s lower bound is mathematically sound but lacks intuitive interpretation and comparison to the upper bounds.  
- Suggests adding a short discussion and a small example to illustrate Theorem 7’s meaning and tightness.  
- Notes some theoretical bounds are conservative (illustrated by the Ising model example).  
- Presentation and clarity are excellent: consistent notation, well-explained algorithm and proof sketches; minor suggestion to compare "fast-mixing" sets to other tractable sets (e.g., high-girth models).  
- Highly original: reframes fast-mixing chains as a tractable parameter class for learning and provides novel FPRAS guarantees absent in prior work.  
- Significant: enables theoretically guaranteed learning where tree-structured approximations fail, with implications for dense models and heuristic methods like contrastive divergence.  
- Recommends acceptance with minor revisions to clarify Theorem 7 and expand the significance discussion (2–3 sentences) highlighting practical potential and extensions (e.g., latent-variable models, RBMs).
