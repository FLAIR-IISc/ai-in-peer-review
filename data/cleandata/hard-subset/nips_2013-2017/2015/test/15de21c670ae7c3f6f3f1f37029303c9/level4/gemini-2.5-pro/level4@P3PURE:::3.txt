This paper addresses the problem of weight learning in Markov networks, which is typically formulated as a likelihood maximization problem. Gradient-based algorithms are standard for this task, but they require computing a gradient that is NP-hard in the general case. Consequently, MCMC methods are often used to produce an approximation.

The main contribution of this paper is a set of theoretical results demonstrating that if the model parameters (weights) are in a region that ensures rapid convergence of the MCMC sampler to its stationary distribution, then the MCMC-based weight learning algorithm constitutes a Fully Polynomial-Time Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The authors derive separate performance bounds for two settings: the unregularized (convex) case and the L2 ridge-regularized (non-convex) case.

The paper is generally well-written, with a few minor typos. While I was not able to verify all the proofs in detail, the arguments appear plausible. The theoretical results are impressive; however, their practical relevance is limited, a point the authors commendably acknowledge. The primary concern is that the conditions on the model parameters required by the theorems—namely, that they induce rapid mixing—are virtually impossible to guarantee or enforce in practice.

This raises a critical question: have the authors considered methods to artificially enforce this rapid-mixing condition during the learning process? Such a constraint could be interpreted as a form of regularization. If so, its interaction with the existing ridge regression penalty would need to be carefully studied, as their combination could potentially lead to over-regularized models. If this challenge of enforcing the rapid-mixing condition could be solved, it would transform these theoretical results into a very strong and practical contribution.

Overall, this is a reasonably well-written paper that formalizes the conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning is a FPRAS. However, the reliance on this strong and impractical condition is a major limitation. Non-trivial research problems must be addressed before the results derived in this paper can yield a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning.
