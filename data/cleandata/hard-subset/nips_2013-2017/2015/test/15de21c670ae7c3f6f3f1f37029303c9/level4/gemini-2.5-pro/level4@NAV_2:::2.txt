This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in exponential families where exact inference is intractable, a common scenario for graphical models with high treewidth. Rather than relying on structural constraints like low treewidth, the authors introduce a novel notion of tractability by restricting parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where MCMC inference is guaranteed to converge rapidly. The primary contribution is a rigorous proof that projected gradient descent, with gradients estimated via MCMC, constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the MLE within this constrained set. The analysis carefully treats both the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases, providing convergence guarantees on the log-likelihood and parameter error, respectively. This work connects to a long line of research on making MLE tractable, but offers a distinct alternative to methods based on pseudolikelihood, score matching, or restricting models to tree-like structures.

**Strengths**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a high-quality theoretical analysis. The core results, establishing an FPRAS for MLE under fast-mixing assumptions, appear solid and well-supported by the provided proofs. The authors skillfully combine established results from MCMC mixing time theory, concentration inequalities, and the convergence of optimization algorithms with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011).
*   **Originality:** The approach is highly original. While using MCMC to approximate gradients is a common heuristic, this paper provides the first formal theoretical guarantees for this procedure in the context of MLE, by explicitly linking optimization convergence rates to MCMC mixing rates. This reframing of tractability—from graphical structure to MCMC dynamics—is a novel and valuable conceptual contribution.
*   **Significance:** The work is significant as it formally bridges the gap between MCMC theory and optimization for learning. Although its direct practical application may be limited (see weaknesses), the theoretical framework is valuable. It provides a rigorous lens for analyzing widely used heuristic methods like contrastive divergence and may inspire future research into designing models and parameterizations with provably fast-mixing properties. The authors are also to be commended for the transparent and thoughtful discussion of the work's limitations.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the clarity of the technical presentation could be improved in a couple of key areas:
    1.  The entire analysis is predicated on the convexity of the negative log-likelihood objective function, f(θ). While this is a standard result, the paper never explicitly states or justifies it. A brief mention that the Hessian of the log-partition function A(θ) is the covariance of the sufficient statistics, and thus positive semidefinite, would make the paper more self-contained and better guide the reader through the logic.
    2.  The analysis relies on several assumptions that could be more clearly consolidated for the reader. For instance, the requirement that the parameter constraint set Θ must be convex (to allow for efficient projection) is a crucial detail that is not explicitly highlighted in the main setup. A clear, itemized list of assumptions would be beneficial.

*   **Practical Significance:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its practical applicability. The framework effectively replaces one hard problem (intractable MLE) with another (identifying a non-trivial, convex set of parameters Θ that is provably fast-mixing and onto which one can efficiently project). Certifying fast mixing is extremely difficult for most models of interest, which the authors acknowledge. Therefore, while theoretically elegant, the direct utility of the proposed scheme for practitioners is likely limited at present.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an original and substantial theoretical contribution that provides a new perspective on achieving tractable maximum likelihood learning. The connection it establishes between MCMC mixing rates and optimization convergence is novel and important. While the practical utility is constrained by the difficulty of proving fast-mixing properties for parameter sets, the work is a valuable addition to the literature. It provides a solid foundation for future work and offers rigorous insights into related, more heuristic learning algorithms. I recommend acceptance.
