

A central challenge in Markov networks is weight learning, defined as determining feature weights to maximize likelihood. Gradient descent is commonly employed for this purpose, with gradient computation as a critical subtask. Since exact gradient computation is NP-hard in general, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms are typically used as approximations.  

The paper establishes theoretical results demonstrating that if the model parameters ensure rapid MCMC convergence to the stationary distribution, the resulting weight learning algorithm (using MCMC for gradient estimation) constitutes a Fully Polynomial Randomized Approximation Scheme (FPRAS). The authors analyze two distinct cases: the unregularized (convex) setting and the ridge-regularized (non-convex) setting, deriving separate convergence bounds for each.  

The paper is generally well-written, though it contains minor typographical errors. While I was unable to fully verify all proofs, they appear plausible.  

Although the theoretical contributions are impressive, they lack practical relevance—acknowledged by the authors—which is a significant limitation. It appears virtually impossible to guarantee the parameter conditions required by the theorems in real-world scenarios. The authors should address whether these conditions can be enforced artificially (e.g., as a form of regularization), and whether incorporating ridge regression alongside this condition risks over-regularization. Resolving this issue, combined with the presented theory, would substantially strengthen the paper.  

Overall, the paper provides a clear formalization of conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning (with and without regularization) yields a FPRAS. However, these conditions are of negligible practical utility. Substantial non-trivial research must be undertaken before the results can inform a practical or accurate weight learning algorithm.
