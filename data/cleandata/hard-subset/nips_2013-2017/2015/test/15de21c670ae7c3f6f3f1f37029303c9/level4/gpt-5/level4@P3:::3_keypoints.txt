- Summary: Studies ML for exponential families with parameters constrained to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ, analyzes projected gradient descent with MCMC-estimated gradients, and proves FPRAS bounds: unregularized Õ(1/ε^3) work for objective and ridge-regularized Õ(1/ε^2) for parameter error; instantiates for Ising/pairwise MRFs and gives an example.  
- Relation to prior work: Unifies MCMC-biased-gradient ML, surrogate estimators, tractability assumptions, and prior fast-mixing projection results; novelty is a unified FPRAS analysis with explicit iteration/sample/chain-length schedules.  
- Strength: Technical care — bounds gradient bias via TV mixing, controls sampling noise with concentration, proves stability of projected gradient descent, and gives explicit schedules and mixing-constant dependence.  
- Strength: Original formulation of fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable alternative to low treewidth, with broad applicability across exponential families.  
- Strength: Clear organization, stated assumptions, and an illustrative example; clarifies when MCMC-in-the-loop learning has end-to-end guarantees.  
- Weakness: Practical enforceability of Θ — projecting onto a provably fast-mixing convex set and knowing mixing constants (C, α) is often hard or loose; projections beyond Ising-like cases are not detailed.  
- Suggestion: Provide concrete projection algorithms and complexity for broader models (e.g., Potts, general discrete/continuous families) and discuss/estimate C and α or give adaptive schedules (e.g., chain-length doubling).  
- Weakness: Conservatism of bounds — constants appear loose in the example; request discussion of typical magnitudes and empirical tightness.  
- Weakness: Interplay with regularization — projection plus ridge can double-regularize and hurt statistical efficiency; suggest alternatives (spectral-norm penalties) and comparisons of projection vs penalty.  
- Weakness: Initialization and sample reuse — analysis assumes independent fresh samples per iteration and omits persistent chains, sample pools, and contrastive-divergence practices; extend or comment on these.  
- Weakness: Scope of MCMC kernels — need clarification how results vary with different kernels (Glauber, Swendsen–Wang, etc.) and whether Θ depends on kernel choice.  
- Minor clarity issues: Fix notation (R vs R2 / “R22”), restate assumptions before key lemmas (e.g., Section 4.1), and correct wording that implies ridge makes the problem non-convex.  
- Evaluation: Technically sound and original with clear presentation, but practical impact limited by mixing-constant dependence and projection availability; significance moderate.  
- Questions for authors: Recommend concrete Θ/projection procedures beyond Ising, adaptive schedules without (C,α), sensitivity of statistical accuracy vs regularizers, and whether analysis can handle persistent chains/sample pools.  
- Pros for acceptance: Establishes general FPRAS for ML under fast-mixing constraints, bridges MCMC mixing theory and biased-gradient optimization, and extends prior fast-mixing projection work.  
- Cons against acceptance: Limited practical relevance currently due to difficulty enforcing/estimating fast-mixing constraints, model-specific/expensive projections, and minimal experimental validation.  
- Overall: Well-written, technically solid contribution that advances theory of MCMC-based ML under fast-mixing constraints but needs more guidance on constructing Θ and stronger empirical evidence to increase practical impact.
