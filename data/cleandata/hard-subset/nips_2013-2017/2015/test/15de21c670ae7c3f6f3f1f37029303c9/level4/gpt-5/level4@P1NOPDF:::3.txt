A core problem in Markov networks is weight learning, typically defined as choosing feature weights to maximize the likelihood. Gradient-based methods are standard, and a key subtask is computing the gradient. Because this is NP-hard in general, MCMC is often used as a substitute for exact computation.

This paper presents theoretical results showing that if the parameters are such that MCMC mixes rapidly to the stationary distribution for those weights, then a weight-learning algorithm that uses MCMC to estimate the gradient constitutes an FPRAS. The paper analyzes two settings separately: the unregularized (convex) case and the ridge-regularized (non-convex) case, providing distinct bounds for each.

The paper is generally well written, with a few typos. I could not verify all the proofs in detail, but they appear plausible.

Despite the impressive theory, the practical relevance seems minimal (and I appreciate that the authors acknowledge this). In practice, it appears virtually impossible to verify or guarantee that the learned parameters satisfy the required constraints. Have the authors considered ways to enforce these constraints during learning? One could view the condition itself as a form of regularization; however, combining it with ridge regularization risks over-regularization and degraded performance. If this issue can be addressed—e.g., by devising a mechanism to enforce the mixing condition without excessive regularization—then, together with the theoretical results, the paper would be much stronger.

Overall, this is a reasonably well-written paper that formalizes the conditions under which MCMC-based gradient estimation for weight learning (with and without regularization) yields an FPRAS. However, the key condition currently appears to have little practical relevance. Nontrivial research challenges must be solved before these results can lead to a practical and accurate algorithm for weight learning.
