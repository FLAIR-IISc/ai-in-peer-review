A central issue in Markov networks is parameter learning, typically framed as choosing feature weights that maximize the likelihood. Gradient-based optimization is standard, but computing exact gradients is NP-hard in general, so MCMC is commonly used as a surrogate.

This paper presents theoretical guarantees showing that if the parameters lie in a set for which MCMC rapidly converges to the stationary distribution, then gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient estimates constitutes an FPRAS for maximum-likelihood learning. The analysis treats two regimes—unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized—and derives separate bounds for each.

The paper is mostly clearly written, with a few minor typos. I did not check every proof in full detail, but they appear plausible.

Although the theoretical results are impressive (and the authors appropriately note the limitations), their direct practical relevance seems limited. It appears very difficult in practice to ensure that the learned parameters satisfy the fast-mixing constraints required by the theorems. Have the authors considered ways to impose these constraints during training? One could view them as a form of regularization, but combining them with ridge penalties may lead to over-regularization. If this challenge were addressed, together with the existing theory, the result would be a very strong contribution.

Overall, this is a reasonably well-written paper that formalizes conditions under which MCMC-based weight learning (with and without ridge regularization) yields an FPRAS. However, the central condition seems to have limited practical applicability. Nontrivial research questions remain before these results can underpin a practical and accurate learning method.

A clear and careful formalization of when MCMC-driven learning gives an FPRAS, but the key assumption appears impractical; resolving this gap is necessary before the approach can be recommended for real-world parameter estimation.
