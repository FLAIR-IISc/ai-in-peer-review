

The paper introduces a novel computational approach for efficient maximum likelihood estimation in exponential families. Standard maximum likelihood estimation is typically intractable due to the need to compute an integral with respect to the current exponential family parameter. By assuming fast-mixing MCMC for all permissible parameters, the authors demonstrate that integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be evaluated with sufficient precision. Combined with Schmidt et al. (2011), this yields a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for the MLE. Both convex and strongly convex settings are analyzed, providing distinct guarantees: likelihood error bounds for the convex case and parameter error bounds for the strongly convex case. A simple experimental validation of the theory is included.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution. The approach is well-motivated as a viable alternative to restrictive small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analysis appears sound, though the proofs were not verified in detail; they essentially combine the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and Schmidt et al.'s results. While the paper synthesizes existing techniques, the integration is nontrivial.

The authors' candid discussion of limitations is commendable, including a thoughtful analysis of the approach's weaknesses, overly pessimistic theoretical assumptions, and future work directions. The paper also provides useful insights into contrastive divergence-type algorithms, though the analyzed algorithm differs slightly from standard contrastive divergence.

The introduction is well-written with comprehensive references, but the technical presentation has minor clarity issues. A critical omission is the explicit statement that the log-likelihood is convex: the second and third terms in Eq. 1 are clearly convex, and convexity of $A(\theta)$ follows from the positive semi-definiteness of its Hessian (the covariance of sufficient statistics). Given the paper's foundation on the convexity of $f(\theta)$, this discussion would significantly aid reader comprehension. A clear summary of all assumptions, including the unmentioned requirement that the parameter set $\Theta$ must be convex, would also be beneficial.

The results represent an original and substantial contribution. However, their practical utility is limited since proving fast mixing for MCMC is notoriously difficult, effectively replacing one hard problem (MLE computation) with another (establishing fast mixing). Despite this, the paper's value lies in providing a formal framework for such problem transformations.
