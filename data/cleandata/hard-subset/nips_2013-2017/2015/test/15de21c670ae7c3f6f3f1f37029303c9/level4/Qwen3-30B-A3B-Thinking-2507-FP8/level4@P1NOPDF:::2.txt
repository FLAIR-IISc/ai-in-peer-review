

Summary: This paper introduces a novel approach to computationally efficient maximum likelihood learning in exponential families. Finding the maximum likelihood estimate (MLE) is generally intractable, with the main computational bottleneck being the need to evaluate an integral with respect to the current exponential family parameter. By assuming that Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) mixes rapidly for all parameters in the allowed set, the authors demonstrate that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be computed with sufficient precision. Combined with the results of Schmidt et al. (2011), this yields a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme for the MLE. The paper analyzes both convex and strongly convex settings, providing distinct guarantees: likelihood error bounds for the former and parameter error bounds for the latter. A simple experiment validates the theoretical claims.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution. The approach is well-motivated as a viable alternative to restrictive small-treewidth assumptions. The theoretical analysis appears sound, though I have not verified the proofs in detail. The proofs essentially combine the MCMC mixing assumption with concentration inequalities and Schmidt et al.'s results, making the paper a nontrivial synthesis of existing techniques.

The authors' candid discussion of the approach's limitations is particularly commendable. The conclusion thoughtfully addresses weaknesses, identifies overly pessimistic theoretical assumptions, and suggests concrete future directions. The paper also provides valuable insights into contrastive divergence (CD)-type algorithms, even though the analyzed algorithm differs slightly from standard CD.

The introduction is well-written and well-referenced, but some technical points require clarification. A minor but important omission is the explicit statement that the log-likelihood is convex. While the second and third terms in Equation (1) are clearly convex, and the convexity of the log-partition function \(A(\theta)\) follows from the positive semi-definiteness of its Hessian (the covariance of sufficient statistics), explicitly stating the convexity of \(f(\theta)\) would better guide readers through the paper's core argument. Additionally, a clear summary of the paper's key assumptions would be beneficial; notably, the requirement that the parameter set \(\Theta\) be convex is never explicitly stated in the main text.

While the results are theoretically significant, their practical utility is limited by the extreme difficulty of proving fast mixing for MCMC chains—a challenge that essentially replaces the original MLE problem with another hard problem. Nevertheless, the ability to reframe the problem in this way is valuable, and the paper's results will be a useful addition to the community's toolkit.
