### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel and computationally efficient approach to maximum likelihood learning in exponential families where, in the general case, finding the MLE is intractable. The primary computational bottleneck from a convex optimization perspective is the calculation of an integral with respect to the current parameter estimate. The authors address this by assuming that a suitable MCMC sampler is fast-mixing for all parameters in the allowed set. Under this assumption, they demonstrate that the integrals required for proximal gradient descent can be approximated with sufficient accuracy. By integrating this result with the analysis of Schmidt et al. (2011), the paper establishes a fully-polynomial randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for computing the MLE. The analysis considers both the general convex and the strongly convex cases, providing guarantees on the likelihood error for the former and on the parameter error for the latter. The theory is supported by a simple illustrative experiment.

### **Detailed Comments**

This is a high-quality and well-executed piece of theoretical work. The approach is well-motivated as a compelling alternative to methods that rely on restrictive structural assumptions, such as small treewidth. While I have not verified the proofs in detail, the theoretical analysis appears solid. The technical contribution is a nontrivial synthesis of existing techniques, primarily by connecting the fast-mixing MCMC assumption with concentration inequalities and the optimization framework of Schmidt et al.

A notable strength of the paper is its frank and thoughtful discussion of the approach's limitations. The conclusion, in particular, provides a nuanced analysis of the method's weaknesses, identifies areas where the theoretical assumptions may be overly pessimistic in practice, and outlines promising directions for future research. Furthermore, the analysis provides valuable insights into contrastive divergence (CD)-style algorithms, even though the specific algorithm analyzed is not CD itself.

Despite the paper's strengths, the clarity of the technical presentation could be improved in a few areas. While the introduction provides a good overview of previous work, some key details are omitted, which could hinder the reader's understanding.

1.  **Justification of Convexity:** A small but crucial omission is an explicit statement that the negative log-likelihood function is convex. The entire optimization framework is predicated on this property. A brief justification—noting that the first two terms in Equation 1 are affine and the log-partition function A(θ) is convex because its Hessian is the covariance matrix of the sufficient statistics (and thus positive semidefinite)—would greatly help guide the reader through the logic of the paper.

2.  **Consolidation of Assumptions:** The paper would benefit from a clear, consolidated summary of all the assumptions made. For example, the assumption that the parameter set Θ must be convex is fundamental to the optimization problem but does not appear to be explicitly stated in the main text. Centralizing these requirements would improve the paper's self-containedness and rigor.

In conclusion, this paper presents an original and substantial contribution. Its immediate practical value may be limited, as proving that an MCMC sampler is fast-mixing for an entire family of distributions is an extremely challenging problem in its own right. In this sense, the paper effectively replaces one hard problem (computing the MLE) with another (proving fast mixing). Nevertheless, establishing such theoretical connections is valuable, as it reframes a difficult problem and may open new avenues for research. Therefore, this work is a worthwhile addition to the literature.
