This paper addresses the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where exact inference is intractable. The authors propose a novel approach: rather than restricting parameters to a tractable set like tree-structured parameters, they consider a set of “fast-mixing parameters” for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference converges quickly. They prove a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within such a set, analyzing both unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases. This work provides theoretical guarantees for a commonly used practice – approximating likelihood gradients with MCMC samples – which typically lacks such guarantees.

The paper is well-structured and generally clearly written, although dense at times. The notation is introduced incrementally, and the upfront summary of symbols is helpful. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work in graphical models and statistical learning, situating their contribution within the broader landscape of approaches to intractable learning (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods, etc.). The proofs, while not fully verified by this reviewer, appear logically sound and well-motivated. The discussion of related mixing time bounds for Ising models and other graphical models is thorough and provides useful context.

The main strength of this work lies in its theoretical rigor. Establishing an FPRAS under the fast-mixing assumption is a significant contribution, formally justifying a widely used, yet previously unanalyzed, approach to approximate learning. The separate analyses for the convex and strongly convex cases provide a nuanced understanding of the algorithm’s behavior and convergence rates. The paper also thoughtfully acknowledges the limitations of its results and discusses potential directions for future work.

However, the primary weakness, as the authors themselves concede, is the practical relevance of the fast-mixing parameter set constraint. The core assumption – that one can guarantee fast mixing and efficiently project parameters onto this set – is a substantial hurdle. While the paper provides examples of how to enforce this condition for specific models like the Ising model, it remains unclear whether this is feasible or effective for more complex, real-world graphical models. The discussion of this challenge, while honest, feels somewhat brief and could be expanded. Specifically, exploring the trade-offs between the fast-mixing constraint and other regularization techniques (like the ridge regularization already analyzed) would be valuable. Could enforcing fast mixing lead to over-regularization, hindering the model's ability to fit the data?

Furthermore, the paper focuses almost exclusively on theoretical results. While a detailed experimental evaluation might not be the primary goal, including a small-scale empirical demonstration of the algorithm’s performance, even on synthetic data, could help to illustrate its potential and limitations. This could involve comparing the performance of the proposed algorithm to existing methods under controlled conditions, and analyzing the impact of the fast-mixing constraint.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The FPRAS result is a significant theoretical advancement in the field of approximate learning in graphical models.
*   **Rigor and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, mathematically rigorous, and provides a clear presentation of the results.
*   **Addresses a Practical Issue:** The work tackles a common practice (using MCMC for gradient estimation) and provides theoretical justification for it.
*   **Acknowledges Limitations:** The authors honestly acknowledge the practical challenges associated with enforcing the fast-mixing constraint.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practical Relevance:** The fast-mixing parameter set constraint may be difficult to satisfy in practice for complex models.
*   **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The absence of even a small-scale empirical evaluation hinders the assessment of the algorithm’s performance and potential.
*   **Potential for Over-Regularization:** The interplay between the fast-mixing constraint and other regularization techniques is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of learning in graphical models. While the practical relevance of the core assumption is a concern, the theoretical results are significant and the paper is well-written and insightful. I lean towards **accepting** the paper, with the caveat that the authors address the practical limitations and potential for over-regularization in a more thorough manner in the camera-ready version.  A brief discussion of how the fast mixing condition relates to existing regularization strategies would significantly strengthen the paper.
