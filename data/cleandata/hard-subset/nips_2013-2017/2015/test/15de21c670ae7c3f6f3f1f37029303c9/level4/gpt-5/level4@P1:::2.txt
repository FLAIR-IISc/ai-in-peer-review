Summary
This paper proposes a novel route to computationally efficient maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics. Instead of restricting to low-treewidth models (e.g., trees) to make inference tractable, the authors restrict parameters to a set Θ that guarantees fast mixing of a chosen MCMC algorithm. Under this assumption, they analyze projected (proximal) gradient descent where the likelihood gradient is approximated by MCMC samples. Combining mixing-time bounds with concentration inequalities and convergence results of Schmidt et al. (2011), they obtain fully polynomial randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) for the MLE within Θ. Two regimes are treated: the unregularized (convex) case, where the algorithm achieves ε-accuracy in log-likelihood with total effort O(1/ε3) up to logarithmic factors, and the ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case, which achieves ε-accuracy in parameter distance with total effort O(1/ε2) up to logs. The analysis is presented in the exponential-family framework and applies beyond graphical models. A small Ising-model experiment illustrates the theory.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned as an alternative to treewidth-based tractability (e.g., Chow–Liu, high-girth models) and to surrogates such as pseudolikelihood, score matching, and composite likelihood, which may have inferior sample complexity and do not target the M-projection under misspecification. It also connects to MCMC-based maximum-likelihood methods and recent work on fast-mixing chains via restarts (Steinhardt & Liang). The contribution is a nontrivial synthesis: fast-mixing conditions from the mixing-time literature, concentration bounds (Efron–Stein, Bernstein, Hoeffding), and inexact projected gradient descent (Schmidt et al.).

Strengths
- Clear and compelling motivation to replace structural tractability with algorithmic tractability (fast mixing).
- Generality: results apply to arbitrary exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, not only specific graphical models.
- Solid theoretical development (I did not verify every proof in detail), with transparent assumptions and explicit schedules for iterations (K), samples (M), and mixing steps (v).
- Useful discussion of limitations and connections to contrastive-divergence–type algorithms, even though the analyzed method is not exactly CD.
- The example, while simple, helps ground the theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions for clarity
- The paper implicitly relies on the convexity of the negative log-likelihood f(θ) = A(θ) − θ·t̄ + (λ/2)∥θ∥2 but does not state it explicitly early on. It would help readers to note that A(θ) is convex because its Hessian is the covariance of t(X), hence PSD; the other terms are clearly convex. Since convexity underpins the whole analysis, stating this up front would improve readability.
- The assumption that Θ is convex is essential (projection and mixing guarantees hold on Θ); this should be clearly stated in the main text.
- Some presentation could be tightened. For example, Theorem 1 (Lipschitz gradient) is important and worth highlighting earlier. When discussing mixing assumptions, it would help to clearly separate algorithm-agnostic assumptions from those specific to Gibbs or Swendsen–Wang, and to emphasize the requirement that one can efficiently project onto Θ.
- Practical guidance: while the theory provides precise schedules for K, M, v, acknowledging that constants (e.g., L = 4R2^2 + λ) may be pessimistic—as seen in the example—would better set practitioner expectations.
- The algorithmic analysis assumes independent samples at each iteration and does not cover sample-pool heuristics commonly used in practice; noting this limitation in the main body (not only in the discussion) would help.

Quality
The work is technically sound under its assumptions. Claims are supported by careful use of concentration inequalities, mixing-time bounds, and inexact gradient methods. The approach cleanly distinguishes convex and strongly convex regimes and provides explicit complexity bounds.

Clarity
Generally well written and well organized, with a strong introduction and extensive references. The few missing explicit assumptions noted above can be easily remedied. Some minor typos exist (see below).

Originality
While composed of known components, their combination to yield an FPRAS for MLE within fast-mixing parameter sets is novel and conceptually valuable. The perspective of “algorithmic tractability via fast mixing” is a fresh alternative to structural tractability.

Significance
The practical impact is limited by the difficulty of establishing fast mixing and efficiently projecting onto Θ for real-world models. In that sense, the paper may seem to replace one hard problem (exact ML) with another (proving fast mixing and computing projections). Nevertheless, formalizing this trade-off is useful, and the results may inform the design of regularizers and constraints that promote fast mixing.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and well-motivated framework.
- General theoretical results with explicit complexity bounds.
- Careful, transparent discussion of limitations.
- Relevant to the NIPS community’s interests in learning and inference in graphical models and exponential families.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited practical applicability without verifiable fast-mixing conditions and efficient projection methods.
- Dependence on potentially loose constants; schedules may be conservative.
- Does not address common practical heuristics (sample pools, CD with hidden units).

Minor comments and typos
- l185: “,w here” → “, where”
- l192: “this paper that” → “this paper assumes that”
- l207: “simple example fast-mixing” → “simple example of a fast-mixing”
- l281: “of of” → “of”
- l311–312: sentence appears nonsensical; please revise.
- Consider explicitly stating early that f(θ) is convex and that Θ is assumed convex.

Overall
A novel approach to developing conditions under which finding the (approximate) MLE is tractable. Well written and theoretically substantive, though of seemingly limited practical value given the difficulty of certifying fast mixing in practice.
