- Tackles maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models where exact inference is intractable.
- Proposes optimizing over a set of "fast-mixing parameters" so MCMC inference converges quickly instead of restricting to tractable parameter families.
- Proves a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within the fast-mixing set.
- Provides separate convergence analyses for the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases.
- Gives theoretical justification for the common practice of approximating likelihood gradients with MCMC samples.
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written overall, with helpful incremental notation and symbol summary.
- Strong situating in related work; thorough discussion of existing approaches (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods).
- Proofs appear logically sound and well-motivated, with useful discussion of mixing-time bounds (e.g., Ising models).
- Main strength is theoretical rigor and the significant FPRAS contribution under fast-mixing assumptions.
- Primary weakness is practical relevance: guaranteeing fast mixing and projecting parameters onto that set is challenging in real-world models.
- Examples for specific models (Ising) are provided, but broader feasibility and effectiveness remain unclear and discussion is brief.
- Potential issue that enforcing fast mixing could over-regularize and harm model fit; trade-offs with other regularizers need exploration.
- Lacks empirical evaluation; even small-scale synthetic experiments would help demonstrate practical performance and limitations.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, rigor and clarity, addresses a practical practice, and acknowledges limitations.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical relevance of the core assumption, absence of empirical results, and unclear interaction with regularization.
- Overall recommendation: lean toward accepting, with a request to expand discussion of practical limitations, over-regularization risks, and relation to existing regularization strategies.
