

The paper introduces a novel framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to "fast-mixing sets," where MCMC inference converges rapidly to stationarity. This approach contrasts with prior work that restricts to tree-structured models (e.g., Chow-Liu) or assumes well-specified models (e.g., pseudolikelihood). The authors prove that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximations achieves a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within such sets. The key contributions include: (1) establishing convergence rates for both unregularized (cubic in 1/ε) and ridge-regularized (quadratic in 1/ε) cases, (2) generalizing beyond graphical models to arbitrary exponential families, and (3) providing explicit parameter schedules (K, M, v) to achieve ε-accuracy. The results build on foundational work by Jerrum & Sinclair (1993) on intractability and Domke & Liu (2013) on fast-mixing projections, but significantly extend the theoretical guarantees for learning.

**Quality**  
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The proofs for Lipschitz continuity (Theorem 1) and convergence rates (Theorems 2–6) are rigorous and well-structured, leveraging standard tools like Bernstein’s inequality and Schmidt et al.’s gradient descent analysis. The unregularized case (convex) and regularized case (strongly convex) are carefully distinguished, with clear bounds on total computational effort (KMv). The only notable weakness is Theorem 7’s lower bound, which is mathematically sound but lacks intuitive interpretation. The authors should expand the discussion to clarify *why* this bound is meaningful (e.g., by comparing it to the upper bounds in Theorems 4–5) and illustrating its tightness with a small example. This would strengthen the theoretical completeness without adding significant length. Otherwise, the analysis is thorough and addresses both strengths (e.g., explicit parameter schedules) and weaknesses (e.g., conservative theoretical bounds, as shown in the Ising model example).

**Clarity**  
The presentation is exceptionally clear. Key notation is consistently defined (e.g., Θ, α, C for mixing rates), and the core algorithm (Figure 1) is well-explained. The proof sketches provide sufficient intuition without sacrificing rigor, and the example (Section 6) effectively demonstrates practical feasibility. Minor improvements could include a brief comparison of the "fast-mixing" constraint to existing tractable sets (e.g., high-girth models in Heinemann & Globerson 2014) to contextualize the novelty.

**Originality**  
This work is highly original. While prior NIPS papers (e.g., Steinhardt & Liang 2014) explored fast-mixing chains for inference, this paper uniquely frames them as a *tractable parameter class* for *learning*, bridging a critical gap in the literature. The exponential family generalization (beyond graphical models) and the FPRAS guarantee for MCMC-based learning are novel contributions not present in recent NIPS work (e.g., Domke & Liu 2013 focuses on projection, not learning). The paper properly references related work (e.g., Jerrum & Sinclair 1993 on intractability, Abbeel et al. 2006 on well-specified models) and clearly articulates how this approach differs from them.

**Significance**  
The significance is substantial but requires better articulation. The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in learning high-treewidth models: MCMC-based learning lacks theoretical guarantees, while tree-structured approximations fail when the target distribution is mis-specified. By enabling *theoretically guaranteed* learning in fast-mixing sets, this work opens avenues for practical applications where tree-structured models are inadequate (e.g., dense Ising models with strong interactions). The significance could be further emphasized by discussing: (1) how this framework enables learning in models where current methods (e.g., contrastive divergence) are heuristic, (2) potential extensions to latent-variable models (e.g., RBMs), and (3) a brief mention of computational savings in the Ising example (Section 6). While the paper claims "no significant practical contribution," the theoretical foundation directly supports future work on scalable learning in complex models—a key NIPS focus.

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a clear, rigorous contribution to the theory of tractable learning. The results are novel, technically sound, and address a gap in the literature on MCMC-based learning. The minor weakness (Theorem 7’s interpretation) is easily fixable, and the significance, though understated, is demonstrable. I recommend **acceptance**. The authors should incorporate the suggested clarification for Theorem 7 and expand the significance discussion (2–3 sentences) to highlight the framework’s potential for enabling theoretically grounded learning in previously intractable settings. This would elevate an already strong paper to a top-tier NIPS contribution.
