

The manuscript proposes an algorithm for optimizing intractable likelihood functions via stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics, but fails to adequately contextualize its contribution within the well-established literature. The core method closely aligns with the Monte Carlo EM algorithm and broader classes of stochastic approximation algorithms designed for intractable likelihoods. Crucially, the authors do not engage with foundational work in this area, leading to a significant oversight in their theoretical development and novelty claim.

Specifically, the authors' approach—particularly their requirement to reinitialize the Markov chain at a fixed distribution $r$ at each iteration—is not novel and has been extensively analyzed in prior work. While the authors acknowledge this reinitialization simplifies their analysis, they do not justify why this choice is preferable to alternative strategies, such as reinitializing from the previous sample (which they themselves note in the manuscript as potentially superior). This technique, along with the underlying stochastic approximation framework, has been thoroughly studied in the literature (e.g., by Andrieu, Doucet, and Holenstein, 2007; and in the seminal text by Benveniste, Métivier, and Priouret, 1990). The authors’ omission of this context weakens their contribution, as their results are subsumed by more general existing theory. For instance, established results do not require uniformly ergodic Markov chains or fixed reinitialization, covering significantly broader scenarios.

The presentation of theoretical results further undermines the manuscript’s clarity and rigor. The assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 are not explicitly stated within the theorems themselves, forcing readers to infer them from surrounding text—a practice that violates standard academic conventions. This lack of precision obscures the conditions under which the results hold and reduces reproducibility. Similarly, the strong assumptions employed in Theorem 6 (e.g., uniform ergodicity, fixed reinitialization) are unnecessary given the general results in Benveniste et al. (1990), which establish convergence under weaker conditions. The authors’ reliance on these restrictive assumptions appears motivated by analytical convenience rather than necessity, and they fail to demonstrate why their framework offers advantages over the established theory.

The manuscript’s novelty is therefore questionable. The algorithmic approach is derivative of existing methods, the theoretical analysis does not advance beyond well-known results, and the presentation lacks the clarity expected for NIPS. While the authors’ specific implementation may have practical utility, the work does not provide new theoretical insights or significantly improve upon the state of the art. The absence of a thorough comparison to relevant literature—particularly the Monte Carlo EM framework and stochastic approximation literature—prevents a meaningful assessment of their contribution. 

In summary, the manuscript requires substantial revision to properly situate its work within the existing body of research. The authors must: (1) cite and contrast their method with key prior work on stochastic approximation with Markovian dynamics; (2) justify their reinitialization strategy (or adopt a more standard approach like using the previous sample); (3) restate theorems with explicit assumptions; and (4) demonstrate how their results extend beyond the established theory of Benveniste et al. Without these changes, the paper’s contribution remains marginal and its theoretical claims unsupported by the broader literature. As currently presented, the work does not meet NIPS’s standards for novelty or technical rigor.
