This paper presents a fascinating and technically sound approach to maximum likelihood learning in graphical models by focusing on “fast-mixing parameters.” Instead of restricting to tree-structured parameters, the authors consider parameters where MCMC inference converges quickly, providing a theoretical guarantee for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. This is a significant contribution, extending beyond graphical models to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The core idea is that if parameters are constrained to a set where MCMC mixes quickly, a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the maximum likelihood solution within that set exists. This contrasts with previous work that either restricts the model structure (e.g., Chow-Liu algorithm for tree-structured models) or uses alternative estimation methods like pseudolikelihood or score matching, which often lack the same theoretical guarantees and can yield intractable inference distributions. The paper explicitly addresses the limitations of these alternative approaches and positions its work as providing a more robust theoretical foundation for learning when exact inference is difficult. The authors appropriately cite key papers in graphical model learning (Abbeel et al., Chow-Liu, Jerrum & Sinclair) and related MCMC analysis (Hayes, Dyer et al.). The extension to general exponential families is clearly articulated and distinguishes this work from earlier studies primarily focused on graphical models.  The connection to recent work utilizing MCMC for learning (Steinhardt & Liang) is also noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the proposed approach, demonstrating an FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning under the fast-mixing parameter constraint. The analysis distinguishes between convex and strongly convex cases, providing different convergence rates.
* **Generality:**  The results aren't limited to graphical models, but apply to the broader class of exponential families, enhancing the paper's impact.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. Notation is introduced clearly, and the key ideas are explained in a reasonably accessible manner. The inclusion of a "cartoon" illustrating the gradient descent strategy is helpful.
* **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The paper tackles a core challenge in statistical modeling—efficient learning when exact inference is intractable.
* **Practical Relevance (Demonstrated):** The example Ising model application, though not a central contribution, shows the framework can operate in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality Concerns:** While theoretically elegant, the practical challenges of verifying the fast-mixing property for a given parameter set and efficiently projecting onto the set are significant. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn't delve deeply into algorithmic approaches for projection beyond citing existing work. The computational cost of projection, especially for high-dimensional parameters, could be a bottleneck.
* **Tightness of Bounds:** The theoretical bounds might be conservative. The example demonstrates this, where a less stringent Lipschitz constant resulted in faster convergence. A more refined analysis exploring the tightness of the bounds would be beneficial.
* **Mixing Rate Assumption:** The assumption of a known mixing rate (Equation 3) is a strong one. Determining this rate in practice can be difficult. The paper acknowledges existing bounds but their applicability to a learning setting needs further exploration.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The paper does not explore the impact of the initial parameter estimate on convergence speed. While it acknowledges arbitrary initialization, a discussion of practical initialization strategies (e.g., contrastive divergence) would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The single Ising model example is limited. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation on diverse datasets and model structures would strengthen the paper's claims.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-supported approach to maximum likelihood learning.
* The generality of the results, extending beyond graphical models, is a significant strength.
* The work addresses a fundamental problem in statistical modeling with a rigorous analysis.
* The clarity of the presentation and the logical structure of the paper are commendable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Practical concerns regarding verifying fast-mixing properties and efficient projection may limit the immediate applicability of the proposed approach.
* The theoretical bounds might be conservative and require further refinement.
* The limited empirical evaluation leaves open questions about the method’s performance in real-world scenarios.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. While practical challenges remain, the rigorous analysis and generality of the results make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. The paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the practicality of verifying fast mixing and providing a more thorough discussion of potential challenges in real-world applications. A slightly expanded empirical evaluation would also be beneficial.

**Rating:** Accept (7/10)
