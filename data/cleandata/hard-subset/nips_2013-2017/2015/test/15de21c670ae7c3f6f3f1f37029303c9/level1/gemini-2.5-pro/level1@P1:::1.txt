### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in undirected graphical models with high treewidth, where exact inference and gradient computation are intractable. The standard approach to tractability is to restrict the model's graphical structure (e.g., to a tree, as in the Chow-Liu algorithm). This paper proposes a fundamentally different notion of tractability: restricting the model's parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` for which a given Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampler is provably "fast-mixing" (i.e., converges geometrically to its stationary distribution).

The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a common learning heuristic: projected gradient descent where the intractable gradient of the log-likelihood is approximated using samples from an MCMC chain. The authors prove that this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the MLE within the constrained set `Θ`. This result holds for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, making it broadly applicable. The paper provides two main convergence results:
1.  For the convex (unregularized) case, it provides a schedule for the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and chain length to achieve an ε-accurate solution in log-likelihood with total computational effort scaling as O(1/ε³), ignoring log factors.
2.  For the strongly convex (ridge-regularized) case, it provides a similar schedule to achieve an ε-accurate solution in parameter distance with effort scaling as O(1/ε²).

This work connects the theoretical literature on MCMC mixing times with the practical problem of learning intractable models, providing the first formal guarantees for a widely used class of algorithms that previously lacked a solid theoretical foundation.

### Relation to Previous Work

The paper is well-situated within the literature on learning graphical models. It correctly identifies the limitations of classic tractable methods like Chow-Liu [6], which are restricted to tree structures, and popular approximate methods like pseudolikelihood [3] and score matching [16], which do not optimize the true likelihood and lack guarantees under model misspecification.

The core idea of using MCMC to approximate likelihood gradients is central to the training of many modern energy-based models, most famously in the context of Restricted Boltzmann Machines via Contrastive Divergence (CD) [5, 29]. However, as the authors correctly state, these methods are largely heuristic and lack formal convergence guarantees to the MLE, partly due to the biased nature of the gradient estimates from short MCMC runs. This paper provides a landmark result by formally analyzing a variant of this procedure (using longer chains and projection) and proving its convergence.

The notion of achieving tractability through parameter constraints has been explored before. Heinemann and Globerson [13] learn high-girth models where correlation decay enables approximate inference. More closely related is the work of Steinhardt and Liang [28], who also propose learning fast-mixing models, but do so by designing a specific fast-mixing chain. The present work is more general, providing a framework that can leverage any known fast-mixing conditions from the rich literature on MCMC theory [19, 12, 9], such as bounds on interaction strengths in Ising models.

### Strengths

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper's main contribution is highly original and significant. It bridges a critical gap between the theory of MCMC and the practice of machine learning. By framing tractability in terms of fast-mixing parameter sets rather than graph structure, it offers a new and powerful perspective. Proving that the MCMC-based gradient descent procedure is an FPRAS for the constrained MLE is a major theoretical breakthrough for a problem of long-standing interest.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The proofs build upon established results in convex optimization with noisy gradients [26] and concentration inequalities, carefully accounting for the bias and variance introduced by finite-length MCMC sampling. The separate analyses for the convex and strongly convex cases are thorough and provide explicit schedules for the algorithm's hyperparameters.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem is formally defined, and the main results are stated precisely before delving into proof sketches. The inclusion of these sketches provides valuable intuition without overwhelming the reader.

4.  **Generality:** The theoretical results are developed for general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics. While motivated by graphical models, this generality means the contributions are potentially useful for a much wider range of models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Practicality of Fast-Mixing Sets:** The framework's applicability hinges on being able to define a useful and non-trivial convex set `Θ` of fast-mixing parameters. The examples given for Ising models rely on constraining the magnitude of interaction parameters, which may significantly limit the expressive power of the learned models. A deeper discussion on the trade-off between the strictness of the mixing-time conditions and the resulting model quality would be beneficial.

2.  **Complexity of Projection:** The overall complexity analysis focuses on the number of MCMC transitions (`KMv`), largely omitting the cost of projecting onto the set `Θ` at each of the `K` gradient steps. While the paper argues this cost is often small (and it is trivial for the box constraints in the experiment), for more complex sets, such as those defined by spectral norms [8], the projection can be computationally expensive and may not be negligible.

3.  **Conservatism of Theoretical Bounds:** As acknowledged by the authors in the experimental section, the derived bounds are likely quite conservative. The dependence on problem constants like the Lipschitz constant `L` and the sufficient statistic norm bound `R₂` can lead to hyperparameter schedules (e.g., number of samples `M`) that are impractically large. While establishing the correct polynomial dependence is the main goal, the looseness of the bounds may limit their use as direct practical guidance.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first formal convergence guarantees for the widely used MCMC-MLE paradigm.
*   Introduces a novel and powerful concept of tractability based on fast-mixing parameter sets.
*   The results are technically strong, significant, and presented with exceptional clarity.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning and is likely to inspire follow-up work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The practical utility is somewhat constrained by the restrictiveness of known fast-mixing conditions.
*   The analysis overlooks the potentially significant computational cost of the projection step.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to the problem of learning intractable probabilistic models. Its primary strength lies in providing the first rigorous proof of convergence for a popular but poorly understood class of algorithms. The conceptual shift from structural tractability to parameter-based tractability is both novel and insightful. While the practical implications are tempered by the limitations of known fast-mixing conditions and the conservatism of the bounds, the theoretical significance is undeniable. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a topic of great importance to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
