

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in exponential families by constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing" set, where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. Unlike traditional approaches that restrict to tree-structured parameters (e.g., Chow-Liu), the authors consider parameter sets where mixing time is bounded, proving that gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation can find ε-accurate solutions with high probability. The work extends beyond graphical models to general exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, providing computational guarantees for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases.

**Strengths**

The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant theoretical contribution. The authors successfully establish rigorous bounds on the computational complexity required to achieve ε-accuracy in log-likelihood (O(1/ε³)) or parameter distance (O(1/ε²)), which is a significant advancement over the common practice of using MCMC-based learning without theoretical guarantees. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from previous tractable learning methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, high-girth models) by focusing on the mixing properties of MCMC rather than graph structure. The proof techniques are sound, with careful handling of the bias from MCMC sampling and the variance from gradient estimation. The connection to existing mixing time literature (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair, Domke and Liu) is well-established, and the example with Ising models provides a concrete illustration of the framework's applicability. The paper also demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of how to balance the trade-offs between gradient steps, samples, and Markov chain transitions.

**Weaknesses**

The main limitation is the strong assumption that the fast-mixing parameter set Θ is known and that projection onto Θ is efficient. While the paper provides examples for Ising models, it does not address how to identify such a set for more complex models or how to handle cases where Θ is not convex. This is a critical gap because in practice, determining Θ for arbitrary models would be challenging, and the paper does not discuss how to estimate the mixing parameters C and α. The computational complexity results (O(1/ε³)) are higher than those for some alternative tractable learning methods (e.g., pseudolikelihood typically achieves O(1/ε²) complexity), and the paper acknowledges the theoretical bounds may be conservative (as evidenced by the example where the algorithm converged faster than predicted). The discussion of practical implementation is limited, with only a simple Ising model example provided. The paper also doesn't address how to handle cases where the model is misspecified (i.e., the true distribution is not in the assumed family), which is a significant limitation for practical applications.

**Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance**

*Quality:* The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and clear definitions. The paper carefully addresses the interplay between MCMC bias, gradient estimation error, and optimization convergence. However, the assumption about the known fast-mixing set weakens the practical applicability of the results.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-written and logically organized. The notation is consistent, and the proof sketches provide good intuition for the main results. A minor improvement would be to include a more detailed discussion of how to practically identify fast-mixing sets for different model classes.

*Originality:* The core idea of using fast-mixing parameter sets for maximum likelihood learning is novel. While there are previous works on fast-mixing MCMC (e.g., Domke and Liu 2013), this paper is the first to leverage such sets for theoretical guarantees on maximum likelihood learning. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work on tractable inference and learning.

*Significance:* The paper makes an important theoretical contribution by providing the first rigorous guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning. This is significant because MCMC-based learning is widely used in practice but lacks theoretical backing. However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the high computational complexity and the strong assumptions about the fast-mixing set.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a theoretically important contribution that advances our understanding of MCMC-based learning. While the practical applicability is constrained by the assumption about the fast-mixing set, the theoretical framework is sound and general. The paper makes a valuable addition to the NIPS literature on tractable learning methods, providing a principled alternative to tree-structured parameter constraints.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the following points: (1) Expand the discussion of how to practically identify fast-mixing sets for different model classes; (2) Provide a more detailed comparison of computational complexity with other tractable learning methods; (3) Include a brief discussion of how to handle misspecified models.

The paper is strong in its theoretical contribution and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, though it should be noted that the practical utility is limited by the strong assumptions. The theoretical guarantees provided are significant for understanding the fundamental limits of MCMC-based learning.
