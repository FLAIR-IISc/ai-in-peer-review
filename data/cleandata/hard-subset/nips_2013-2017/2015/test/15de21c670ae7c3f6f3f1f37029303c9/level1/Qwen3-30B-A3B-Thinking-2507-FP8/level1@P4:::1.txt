

**Review**

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in maximum likelihood learning for undirected graphical models: inference is intractable in high-treewidth models, making learning difficult. While the standard approach restricts to tree-structured parameters (e.g., Chow-Liu algorithm), the authors propose an alternative notion of tractability: constraining parameters to a "fast-mixing set" where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference converges quickly to the stationary distribution. They prove that for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients converges to the maximum likelihood solution within the fast-mixing set with high probability. They establish computational complexity bounds: cubic in 1/ε for unregularized learning and quadratic for ridge-regularized learning, providing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS).

The work builds on previous NIPS contributions including tree-structured learning (Chow-Liu [1968], Heinemann and Globerson [2014]), methods for well-specified models (pseudolikelihood, score matching), and mixing time results (Jerrum and Sinclair [1993], Domke and Liu [2013]). It extends these by introducing the fast-mixing parameter set concept and providing the first theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based learning when MCMC is fast-mixing. The paper relates to Steinhardt and Liang [28] who proposed learning based on stationary distributions from fast-mixing chains, but focuses specifically on maximum likelihood learning with theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths:**
- **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proofs (sketched for main results) for both convex and strongly convex cases. The complexity bounds are carefully derived and compared to lower bounds. The analysis handles both the unregularized (convex) and ridge-regularized (strongly convex) cases with appropriate mathematical treatment.
- **Novelty:** The fast-mixing parameter set concept represents a fundamentally different approach to tractable learning compared to the standard tree-structured constraint. The theoretical analysis for MCMC-based learning under fast-mixing conditions appears novel in the NIPS literature, as previous work lacked such guarantees.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured, with clear notation, logical flow, and helpful example (Section 6). The proof sketches provide intuitive understanding without sacrificing mathematical rigor. The example demonstrates the practical feasibility of the theoretical results.
- **Significance:** The results provide theoretical justification for a widely-used practical approach (MCMC-based learning) and establish computational complexity bounds for a broader class of models than tree-structured ones. This advances the theoretical understanding of learning in graphical models.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Limited Practical Demonstration:** While the authors acknowledge the paper makes no significant practical contribution, the example in Section 6 is minimal and doesn't demonstrate practical utility compared to existing methods.
- **Projection Complexity:** The paper notes that projection onto fast-mixing sets might be non-trivial for large models, but doesn't provide guidance on handling this in practice (though it acknowledges this might not be the bottleneck).
- **Initialization Assumptions:** The analysis assumes arbitrary initialization for the Markov chain, while practical implementations (e.g., contrastive divergence) use specific initialization strategies that might reduce required mixing time.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a novel theoretical framework for MCMC-based learning with strong guarantees
- Establishes computational complexity bounds for a broader class of models than traditional tree-structured approaches
- Rigorous mathematical analysis with careful treatment of both convex and strongly convex cases
- Well-written and clearly organized, with helpful example and discussion of limitations

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited practical demonstration or comparison with existing methods
- Theoretical results might be conservative compared to practical implementations (as noted in the example)
- The "fast-mixing" constraint might be restrictive for some practical applications

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding MCMC-based learning in graphical models. It provides a rigorous foundation for a common practical approach and extends the scope of tractable learning beyond tree-structured models. While the practical impact is limited (as the authors acknowledge), the theoretical results are sound and significant for the field. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the literature on graphical models and optimization.

The paper meets NIPS criteria as follows:
- Quality: High - rigorous theoretical analysis with well-supported claims
- Clarity: High - clear structure, notation, and explanations
- Originality: High - novel concept of fast-mixing parameter sets with theoretical guarantees
- Significance: Medium-High - provides theoretical justification for a widely-used method, advancing theoretical understanding

I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
