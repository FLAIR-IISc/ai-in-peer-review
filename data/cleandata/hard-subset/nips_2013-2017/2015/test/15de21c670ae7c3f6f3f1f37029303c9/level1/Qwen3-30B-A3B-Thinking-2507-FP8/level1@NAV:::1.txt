

This paper proposes a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing sets" where MCMC convergence is guaranteed. The authors prove that when parameters are constrained to such a set, gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation yields a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme for the maximum likelihood solution. For unregularized problems, the total effort required is cubic in 1/ε (disregarding logarithmic factors), while ridge-regularized problems achieve quadratic scaling. The work generalizes beyond graphical models to exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for a commonly used practical approach.

The paper builds on NIPS work such as Jerrum & Sinclair (1993) on intractability of partition functions, Chow & Liu (1968) on tree-structured models, and Steinhardt & Liang (2014) on fast-mixing models. It significantly extends previous approaches that either restrict to tree-structured parameters (Chow & Liu) or rely on approximate methods without theoretical guarantees.

Strengths: The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, providing clear complexity bounds for both convex and strongly convex cases. The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding when practical MCMC-based learning methods can be theoretically justified. The distinction between convex and strongly convex cases is particularly well-executed.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks substantial empirical validation beyond a small Ising model example with only 5 training samples on a 4x4 grid, which doesn't demonstrate practical utility. The projection step onto the fast-mixing set is assumed to be efficient without justification, and the paper doesn't address how to identify such sets for arbitrary models. The connection to practical implementation is limited, and the example in Section 6 doesn't convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages over existing approaches.

Originality: The concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" represents a significant departure from traditional tractability notions based on treewidth. While related to Steinhardt & Liang (2014), the theoretical framework and complexity analysis are novel contributions.

Significance: The theoretical results provide a foundation for justifying MCMC-based learning methods, which are widely used in practice. However, the limited empirical validation and lack of demonstration of practical advantages over existing methods (such as pseudolikelihood or score matching) somewhat diminish the impact.

Overall, this is a technically sound theoretical contribution with strong quality and originality, but its significance would be substantially enhanced with more substantial empirical validation and discussion of practical implementation challenges. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution but would benefit from stronger empirical grounding.
