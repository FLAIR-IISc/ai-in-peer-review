- Problem: studies ML learning in exponential families constrained to a fast-mixing parameter set Θ so MCMC-based gradient estimates converge rapidly.  
- Main result: projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients converges w.h.p. to the MLE within Θ for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics.  
- Complexity (convex/unregularized): ε-accurate log-likelihood requires O((1/ε)^3 polylog(1/ε)) total Markov transitions.  
- Complexity (strongly convex/ridge): ε-accurate parameter solution requires O((1/ε^2) polylog(1/ε)) total transitions.  
- Practical schedules: provides explicit iteration counts, samples per iteration, and chain lengths as functions of mixing constants (C, α).  
- Connection to inference work: links to projection-onto-fast-mixing results for Ising/MRFs (e.g., spectral-norm constraints) and includes a small Ising example.  
- Relation to prior learning methods: builds on MCMC-MLE, CD/PCD, pseudo/composite likelihood, score matching; closely related to prior “projecting for fast mixing” NIPS work but advances to end-to-end learning guarantees and an FPRAS.  
- Complementary to restart-based fast-mixing methods (Steinhardt & Liang): provides general exponential-family guarantees without modifying the chain.  
- Technical approach: convergence analysis leverages inexact gradient descent theory (Schmidt et al., NIPS’11) and gives high-probability bounds with explicit mixing dependence.  
- Quality: theory is sound and carefully reasoned, but relies on strong assumptions (known geometric mixing constants and feasible projection).  
- Clarity: paper is well organized and readable; would benefit from discussion on estimating/bounding C and α in practice, projection computational costs, and broader empirical validation.  
- Originality: framing an FPRAS for MLE over fast-mixing sets and synthesizing mixing-time theory with biased stochastic optimization is novel.  
- Significance: conceptually important—shifts tractability from graph structure/treewidth to mixing behavior and rigorizes MCMC-based learning—but practical impact may be limited by unknown/large constants and conservative bounds.  
- Pros: clear, general theoretical guarantees; explicit complexity schedules; broad exponential-family applicability and connections to prior fast-mixing projection work.  
- Cons: requires known/usable fast-mixing sets and constants; projections may be costly; bounds likely loose with large constants; limited empirical validation; assumes fresh independent samples each iteration (no sample reuse); does not address hidden-variable models or misspecification beyond Θ.
