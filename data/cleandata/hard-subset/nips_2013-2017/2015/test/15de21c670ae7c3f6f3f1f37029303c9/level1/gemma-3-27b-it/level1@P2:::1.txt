This paper investigates maximum likelihood learning in graphical models, addressing the intractability of inference in high-treewidth models. The core idea is to restrict parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, enabling efficient gradient-based optimization via MCMC sampling. The authors prove a fully polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within such sets, offering convergence guarantees for both unregularized and ridge-regularized cases.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods, Chow-Liu) and the challenges associated with directly applying gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. It correctly positions its contribution as a theoretical justification for a common practical strategy, providing guarantees previously lacking. The connection to the broader field of FPRAS and computational complexity (mentioning #P-completeness) provides valuable context. The exponential family framework is a good generalization beyond graphical models.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a solid theoretical analysis, providing FPRAS guarantees for a crucial learning problem. The proof sketches provide intuition, and referencing Schmidt et al.’s work appropriately grounds the results.
*   **Novelty:** The concept of “fast-mixing parameters” as a tractable set is a novel and potentially useful alternative to traditional approaches like tree-structured parameters.
*   **Generality:**  Extending the results beyond graphical models to the broader exponential family enhances the impact and applicability of the work.
*   **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions of notation and a logical flow of ideas. The inclusion of a cartoon figure illustrating the gradient descent strategy is helpful.
*   **Detailed Background:** The extensive background section on mixing times, Ising models, and related work demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.
*   **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper thoroughly analyzes both the convex and strongly convex cases, providing distinct convergence results and computational complexity bounds.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Relevance:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper acknowledges limited practical contribution. The example provided, while illustrative, does not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.  The reliance on strong assumptions about the mixing rate (C and α) might be restrictive in practice.
*   **Tightness of Bounds:** The derived bounds on computational complexity appear relatively loose. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion, pointing out potential conservatism in the theoretical analysis.
*   **Projection Complexity:** The paper downplays the potential computational cost of projecting onto the fast-mixing parameter set Θ.  While it’s noted the complexity may be small in some cases, it’s a crucial factor, and a more thorough discussion is warranted.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** The influence of the initial distribution 'r' on MCMC convergence isn’t fully explored. The paper notes it's arbitrary but mentions contrastive divergence, implying it *could* matter and suggesting a direction for future work but doesn’t integrate it into the analysis.
*   **Overly Detailed Background:** While comprehensive, the background section (especially regarding mixing times for specific models) is exceptionally long and detailed. Some of this could be condensed or moved to the appendix to improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to maximum likelihood learning with strong convergence guarantees.
*   The generalization to exponential families broadens the applicability of the results.
*   The work addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical models and offers a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCMC-based learning.
*   The paper is well-written and demonstrates a deep understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical impact of the results is limited, and the computational complexity bounds are relatively loose.
*   The assumptions regarding mixing rates may be restrictive in real-world scenarios.
*   The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the projection complexity and its impact on overall performance.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. While the practical relevance is currently limited, the theoretical contribution is significant. The paper rigorously establishes a valuable result – an FPRAS for maximum likelihood learning with fast-mixing parameters – and fills a gap in the existing literature.  The weaknesses are mostly related to practical considerations and the tightness of bounds, which do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The detailed background and careful analysis demonstrate a high level of expertise and make this a worthwhile addition to the NIPS proceedings.
