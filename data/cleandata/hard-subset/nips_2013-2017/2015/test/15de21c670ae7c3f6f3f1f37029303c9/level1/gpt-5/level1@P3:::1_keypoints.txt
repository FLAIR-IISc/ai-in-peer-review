- Paper studies ML in exponential families constrained to a “fast-mixing” parameter set Θ where a chosen MCMC kernel has geometric mixing with known (C, α).  
- Algorithm: projected gradient descent on negative log-likelihood with MCMC-approximated gradients and projection back to Θ.  
- Main technical contribution: high-probability convergence analysis handling biased, noisy MCMC gradients to yield an FPRAS for ML within Θ.  
- Unregularized (convex) case: ε-accuracy in objective needs total MCMC effort O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)).  
- Ridge-regularized (strongly convex) case: ε-accuracy in parameter distance needs O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) MCMC effort.  
- Analysis components: Lipschitz gradient from bounded sufficient statistics, mixing-time control for bias, and concentration inequalities for sampling noise.  
- Convergence proof builds on projected GD with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., NIPS’11).  
- Positioned relative to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood/score matching and tractable-structure learning; extends prior fast-mixing learning work.  
- Novelty: unified exponential-family guarantees and explicit sample/mixing schedules yielding FPRAS under fast-mixing constraints.  
- Analysis is careful and technically sound under assumptions; combines standard tools in a nontrivial way.  
- Lipschitz constant and continuity bounds appear conservative (e.g., L = 4R2^2 + λ) and could likely be tightened.  
- FPRAS claim is meaningful only if Θ admits polynomial-time projection and polynomial mixing parameters in problem size.  
- Clarity: paper is well written, notation and algorithmic schedule are explicit, and convex vs strongly convex regimes are well distinguished.  
- Clarity suggestions: state how α and C scale with dimension, specify MCMC kernel assumptions (irreducible/aperiodic/reversible), and discuss step-size choice and knowledge of L.  
- Significance: reframes tractability as fast mixing rather than low treewidth and provides theoretical grounding for MCMC-based likelihood gradients.  
- Practical impact depends on availability/expressiveness of Θ with provable mixing and efficient projection; may be limited beyond pairwise discrete MRFs.  
- Strengths: solid theoretical contribution, general exponential-family setting, explicit schedules, and good connection to prior fast-mixing/projection work.  
- Weaknesses: bounded sufficient-statistics assumption excludes many continuous families; extending to sub-Gaussian would broaden scope.  
- Weaknesses: projecting onto Θ and certifying (C, α) are nontrivial beyond specific models, limiting applicability.  
- Weaknesses: analysis requires independent samples and chain restarts each iteration, not covering persistent MCMC used in practice.  
- Weaknesses: conservative constants likely make prescribed budgets impractical; empirical section is minimal and lacks comparisons.  
- Questions for authors: can the TV continuity bound be tightened or justified with explicit dimension dependence?  
- Questions: what concrete Θ and projection algorithms exist beyond Ising/Potts, and what are their α, C?  
- Questions: can analysis extend to biased but correlated gradients from persistent MCMC or to sub-Gaussian t(X) (e.g., Gaussian graphical models)?  
- Acceptance arguments: conceptually novel reframing, rigorous analysis likely to influence future work, first general guarantees for biased MCMC gradients within Θ.  
- Rejection arguments: limited practical impact without richer empirical validation and concrete Θ/mixing estimates; strong assumptions and conservative constants reduce usability.
