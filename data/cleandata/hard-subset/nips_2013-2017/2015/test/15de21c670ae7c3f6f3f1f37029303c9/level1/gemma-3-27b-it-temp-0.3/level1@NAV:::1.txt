## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models with high treewidth, where traditional inference is intractable. The authors propose a novel approach: instead of restricting parameters to tractable sets like trees, they focus on “fast-mixing parameters” – those for which MCMC inference converges quickly. They demonstrate a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within such sets, analyzing both regularized and unregularized cases. This is a significant contribution as it provides theoretical guarantees for a widely used practice (gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients) that previously lacked such backing.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly situating the work within the existing literature on maximum likelihood learning, including pseudolikelihood, score matching, and tree-structured parameter restrictions. The authors effectively highlight the limitations of these existing methods, particularly their inferior sample complexity and inability to guarantee tractable inference post-learning. The connection to mixing times and the rigorous analysis of gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients are strong points. The results are presented with mathematical clarity, and the inclusion of a proof sketch for the main theorems is helpful. The example provided at the end, while simple, helps to ground the theoretical results.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the generality to exponential families is a strength, the practical implications for complex graphical models aren't fully explored. The assumption of known mixing rates (C and α) is a significant one; determining these in practice can be difficult. The discussion of initialization strategies (like contrastive divergence) feels somewhat cursory, acknowledging their potential benefit without a detailed analysis. Furthermore, the paper leans heavily on existing results (e.g., Schmidt et al.’s gradient descent convergence analysis) without providing substantial novel theoretical contributions beyond adapting them to the fast-mixing parameter setting. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides theoretical guarantees for a common and practical learning strategy.
*   Introduces a novel and potentially powerful notion of "tractability" based on mixing times.
*   Rigorous mathematical analysis and clear presentation.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in graphical model learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on strong assumptions (known mixing rates).
*   Practical implications for complex models are not fully explored.
*   Limited novel theoretical contributions beyond adaptation of existing results.



**Overall:** I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field. While not a groundbreaking leap, it provides valuable theoretical grounding for a widely used practice and opens up interesting avenues for future research. I recommend **acceptance**, with a minor revision request to expand the discussion on practical considerations for estimating mixing rates and exploring the potential benefits of different initialization strategies.
