Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies maximum-likelihood learning for exponential families when exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting to low-treewidth structures (e.g., Chow–Liu trees), it proposes constraining parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ for which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes at a uniform geometric rate ∥Mvθq − pθ∥TV ≤ Cαv across Θ. Under bounded sufficient statistics, the authors analyze projected gradient descent where each gradient is approximated by MCMC sampling. They prove high-probability convergence to the MLE within Θ with explicit effort bounds: in the unregularized convex case, ε-accuracy in objective value requires total Markov transitions KMv scaling as O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)); with ridge regularization (strong convexity), ε-accuracy in parameter distance requires O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)). They present lower bounds matching their upper bounds up to constants and log factors and provide concrete instantiations for Ising and pairwise MRFs where projection onto fast-mixing sets is known (e.g., via bounding spectral norm of interaction matrices). The analysis leverages Lipschitz continuity of the gradient (with L = 4R2^2 + λ), concentration bounds, and convergence of inexact projected gradient descent (Schmidt et al., 2011).

This connects to: pseudolikelihood and score matching alternatives (Besag; Hyvärinen) which can have inferior sample complexity and may not yield tractable learned models; structure-restricted MLE (Chow–Liu; high-girth models); MCMC-based MLE (Geyer; Gu & Zhu) and CD (Tieleman; Hinton) which often lack guarantees; fast-mixing parameter projection for Ising/MRFs (Domke & Liu 2013; Liu & Domke 2014); and learning fast-mixing models via restart chains (Steinhardt & Liang 2015). The contribution is a general, model-agnostic optimization guarantee for MLE over provably fast-mixing parameter sets with explicit complexity bounds.

Strengths:
- Generality: applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not limited to specific graphical structures.
- Clear theoretical guarantees: high-probability bounds for both convex and strongly convex cases, with explicit schedules for iterations K, samples M, and chain length v; accompanying lower bounds indicate near-optimality up to polylog factors.
- Careful use of mixing-time theory and inexact gradient descent analysis; clean decomposition of error into sampling noise and bias from incomplete mixing.
- Concrete instantiation for Ising/MRFs, including examples of convex fast-mixing sets and projection procedures.
- Clarifies a widely used practice (sampling-based gradients) by placing it on rigorous footing under fast-mixing constraints.

Weaknesses:
- Practicality hinges on the availability of a convex Θ with known uniform mixing constants C, α and an efficient projection operator. For many models/algorithms, obtaining tight C, α is difficult, and existing bounds can be extremely conservative.
- The total work dependence (ε−3 for unregularized case) may be large in practice; even the ε−2 scaling in the regularized case includes unfavorable constants and logarithms.
- Empirical validation is minimal (a single 4×4 Ising example), with no comparison to standard baselines (pseudolikelihood, CD) or to persistent CD/persistent chains.
- The analysis assumes independent sampling per iteration (no sample pools/persistent chains), which differs from common practice; the paper notes this but does not address it theoretically.
- The “FPRAS” terminology is used informally for an optimization target; precise approximation notion (additive vs multiplicative) could be clarified to align with standard complexity definitions.
- The cost of projection is excluded from the total effort; for some Θ (e.g., spectral-norm constraints in large models), projection may be nontrivial.

Quality:
- Technical soundness is high. Assumptions are clearly stated (bounded t(x), uniform geometric mixing over Θ, convexity/strong convexity via λ, ability to project). The Lipschitz gradient bound and error decomposition are standard and correctly applied. The schedules for K, M, v and the accompanying lower bounds are consistent with the form of the inexact gradient descent bounds and concentration inequalities.
- One minor notational issue: R2 denotes a bound on ∥t(x)∥2, so terms like 4R2^2 may confuse; a clearer symbol (e.g., R for the norm bound) would improve readability.
- The reliance on known C, α uniform over Θ is a strong assumption; the paper discusses examples (Ising, pairwise MRF) where such sets and projections are available.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized: setup, mixing-time background, main theorems, schedules, and a small example. Proof sketches offer intuition; full proofs are deferred.
- Referencing is adequate and situates the work within both ML and mixing-time literature.
- Some cross-references (e.g., Algorithm 1) are not included in the excerpt; ensuring the algorithm is explicit would help reproducibility.
- Suggestions: clarify the FPRAS definition for optimization; tighten notation around R2; discuss how to estimate or bound C, α in practice; add guidance on selecting MCMC kernels (Gibbs vs cluster methods).

Originality:
- The notion of learning within fast-mixing parameter sets exists (Domke & Liu; Liu & Domke), but this paper’s contribution is a general convergence and complexity theory for MLE with sampling-based gradients under such constraints, plus near-matching lower bounds. The analysis seems novel in its generality and explicit effort bounds.

Significance:
- The theoretical contribution is meaningful: it provides the first general, high-probability optimization guarantees for MLE with MCMC-based gradients subject to fast-mixing constraints, applicable beyond specific graph families.
- Practical impact may be limited by the strength and conservativeness of mixing-time bounds and projection costs; nevertheless, the framework could motivate principled algorithm design where fast mixing is enforced.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, general theoretical results with clear assumptions and near-optimal effort bounds.
- Bridges a gap between practice (MCMC-based gradient estimates) and theory.
- Connects and unifies strands of work on fast mixing, projection, and inexact optimization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical support; unclear practical benefits given potentially loose constants and strong assumptions (uniform C, α and efficient projection).
- Does not handle common practices like persistent sampling; scope of applicability in real-world models may be narrow.
- Some terminological and notational clarity issues (FPRAS definition; R2 notation) and omission of algorithm details in the text.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include comparisons to pseudolikelihood and CD on moderate-size problems; report actual mixing diagnostics and realized constants.
- Clarify the FPRAS claim, specifying the approximation notion for optimization.
- Discuss strategies for estimating or adaptively controlling C, α (e.g., spectral gap proxies) and for choosing MCMC kernels to improve constants.
- Extend the theory or provide bounds for persistent chains or sample-pool strategies.
- Analyze projection complexity for different Θ and its impact on total runtime.

Overall assessment:
A strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of MLE with MCMC under fast-mixing constraints. The paper is clear and well-situated in the literature, with novel and technically sound results, but empirical validation and practicality are limited.
