## Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models with high treewidth. It proposes a novel approach: instead of restricting parameters to tractable sets like trees, constrain them to a “fast-mixing” set, guaranteeing efficient convergence of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference. The core contribution is proving a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning within such sets, achieved via gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients. This builds upon existing work like Chow-Liu’s algorithm [Chow & Liu, 1968] and recent methods for learning high-girth models [Heinemann & Globerson, 2014], offering a more general framework. The work is firmly grounded in the theoretical foundations of MCMC convergence [Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993] and builds on existing gradient-based learning techniques using sampling [e.g., Geyer, 2009].

**Strengths:** The paper presents a theoretically sound approach with strong guarantees. The FPRAS result is significant, as it addresses a longstanding issue in learning with complex models. The analysis is rigorous, with detailed proofs (deferred to the appendix) and clear sketches within the main text. The authors demonstrate awareness of related work, referencing key papers in the field of graphical models and MCMC methods. Expanding the scope beyond graphical models to encompass general exponential families is a notable generalization, increasing the broader applicability of the results. The provided example, though simple, offers some practical validation.

**Weaknesses:** While theoretically strong, the practical implications are less clear. The constant factors hidden within the polynomial runtime could be substantial, potentially limiting real-world applicability. The paper acknowledges this and notes that the parameter projection complexity is not considered. The assumption of a known mixing rate and ability to project onto the fast-mixing set, while stated, might be non-trivial to verify in practice. Further, the discussion around initialization strategies (e.g., contrastive divergence) feels somewhat cursory, and the potential benefits are not fully explored. The writing, while generally clear, occasionally gets bogged down in technical details.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel theoretical result: FPRAS for learning within fast-mixing sets.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees and rigorous analysis.
*   Generalization to exponential families.
*   Addresses a challenging and important problem.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Potential practical limitations due to hidden constants and projection complexity.
*   Assumption of known mixing rate and projection algorithm.
*   Limited exploration of practical considerations like initialization.



**Overall:** This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. While practical limitations exist, the rigorous analysis and novel results justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and briefly discuss potential strategies for mitigating the practical challenges.
