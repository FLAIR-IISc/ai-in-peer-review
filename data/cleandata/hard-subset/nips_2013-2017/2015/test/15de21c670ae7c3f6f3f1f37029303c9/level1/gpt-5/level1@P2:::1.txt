Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies maximum-likelihood (ML) learning in undirected graphical models (more generally, exponential families) when exact inference is intractable. Rather than restricting to low-treewidth structures (e.g., Chow–Liu trees) or using surrogate objectives (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihood), the authors restrict parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ where a chosen Markov chain Mθ provably mixes rapidly to pθ. They then analyze projected gradient descent that uses MCMC to approximate the likelihood gradient, showing that, for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, one can reach an ε-accurate solution (inside Θ) with high probability and fully-polynomial time in 1/ε: cubic in 1/ε for the unregularized convex case (in terms of suboptimality in f) and quadratic in 1/ε for ridge-regularized (in terms of parameter error). They provide explicit schedules for the number of iterations K, samples M, and mixing steps v, and lower bounds that justify near-optimality up to log factors.

This work connects to: (i) fast-mixing parameter projections for Ising models (Domke & Liu, NIPS 2013) and mixing-time bounds via Dobrushin/spectral norms (Hayes; Dyer et al.); (ii) MCMC-ML methods (Geyer; Gu & Zhu) and inexact proximal-gradient analysis (Schmidt et al.); (iii) alternative “tractable” modeling choices like trees (Chow–Liu) or correlation decay/high-girth (Heinemann & Globerson); (iv) fast-mixing design via restarts (Steinhardt & Liang). The key difference is a general, principled FPRAS-style complexity guarantee for ML within a fast-mixing set across arbitrary exponential families with bounded statistics, using a standard yet previously unguaranteed MCMC-gradient workflow.

Quality (soundness and support):
- The analysis is careful and builds on standard tools: Lipschitz gradient bounds via total variation control under bounded sufficient statistics; concentration (Hoeffding/Efron–Stein/Bernstein) for sampling error; Schmidt et al.’s inexact gradient descent bounds; mixing-time assumptions expressed as geometric decay ∥Mvθq − pθ∥TV ≤ Cαv. The convex and strongly convex cases are treated separately with appropriate measures of error (objective gap vs parameter distance) and matching parameter schedules and lower bounds.
- Assumptions are explicit: (a) bounded ∥t(x)∥; (b) existence of a convex fast-mixing set Θ with known constants C, α and a projection oracle ΠΘ; (c) independence of samples within and across iterations (fresh chains, not persistent pools); (d) chosen sampler consistent with the mixing bound. Under these, the results are technically sound.
- Potential technical caveats: (i) The TV-Lipschitz bound in θ-space used to derive L may hide constants that can be quite loose; ensuring the claimed ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ c∥θ − φ∥ requires the boundedness of t and careful control; the authors say proofs are in the appendix. (ii) The practicality hinges on availability and tightness of C, α and on the ability to project onto Θ efficiently; this is feasible for certain Ising/MRF settings (spectral/induced norm constraints) but less clear for arbitrary exponential families. The paper acknowledges projection cost but does not quantify it.
Overall, the theoretical contribution is rigorous within its assumptions.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written and organized. Notation is heavy but consistent, and the main symbols are summarized early. Theorems state assumptions and conclusions cleanly, and proof sketches convey the core ideas.
- Suggestions: provide more guidance on how to construct Θ and projection oracles beyond Ising/pairwise MRFs; clarify which samplers Mθ the bounds apply to in each example (Gibbs vs Swendsen–Wang vs subgraphs world) and implications for C, α; include a short illustrative calculation of L in a simple model to demystify constants; expand the empirical example or add a second one demonstrating sensitivity to α, C and gains from projection.

Originality:
- Prior work projected parameters to ensure mixing (Domke & Liu) and designed fast-mixing learning objectives (Steinhardt & Liang), but a general FPRAS-style guarantee for ML with biased MCMC gradients over a mixing-constrained Θ across general exponential families appears novel. The explicit complexity schedules and lower bounds strengthen the contribution. The framing of “tractable by fast mixing” as an alternative to low-treewidth is a valuable perspective.

Significance:
- Conceptual significance: clarifies when the ubiquitous “MCMC for likelihood gradients” strategy can be made provably correct and efficient by constraining parameters to fast-mixing regions, and quantifies the compute–accuracy tradeoffs.
- Practical significance: moderated by the difficulty of obtaining/using tight C, α, by potentially expensive projection for general models, and by requiring many fresh chains per iteration rather than persistent chains. Nevertheless, for classes where Θ and projection are known (Ising/Potts with norm constraints), this could inform algorithm design and regularization strategies and may motivate learning objectives that directly enforce Dobrushin-like conditions.

Strengths:
- Clear, general theoretical framework covering arbitrary exponential families with bounded statistics.
- Concrete convergence and complexity guarantees with explicit schedules and matching lower bounds (up to logs).
- Bridges mixing-time theory and inexact gradient optimization in a principled way.
- Situates contribution against a broad literature and acknowledges limitations.

Weaknesses:
- Reliance on known/tight mixing constants and projection oracles limits applicability; construction of Θ is only concrete for certain models.
- Complexity and guarantees do not account for projection cost; ignoring persistent chains may be overly conservative relative to practice.
- Empirical evaluation is minimal and does not validate constants or compare to alternatives (e.g., pseudolikelihood, CD, PCD).
- Guarantees for unregularized case are in objective space, while regularized are in parameter space; practitioners may want direct bounds on test likelihood or marginals.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a novel, rigorous answer to a long-standing practical heuristic (MCMC-ML with biased gradients) under meaningful constraints.
- Generalizes beyond specific graphical model classes and unifies several strands of theory.
- Theorems are technically solid and potentially influential for how we think about tractable learning beyond treewidth.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical impact uncertain due to unavailability/tightness of mixing constants and nontrivial projections in general; the method may be hard to instantiate broadly.
- Empirical support is too limited to assess usefulness or sharpness of constants.
- Some assumptions (fresh independent chains each iteration) deviate from common practice; extension to persistent chains is left open.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper makes a clear and principled theoretical contribution with good novelty and clarity, even if practical adoption will require further work on constructing Θ and estimating/relaxing mixing constants.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate.
