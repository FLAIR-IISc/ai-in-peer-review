This paper studies maximum-likelihood learning in exponential families under a novel tractability notion: constraining parameters to a fast-mixing set Θ so that MCMC-based gradient estimates converge rapidly. The authors prove that, for any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, projected gradient descent using MCMC-approximated gradients converges with high probability to the MLE within Θ. They derive complexity bounds: in the unregularized (convex) case, an ε-accurate log-likelihood solution requires O((1/ε)3 polylog(1/ε)) total Markov transitions; with ridge regularization (strong convexity), an ε-accurate parameter solution requires O((1/ε2) polylog(1/ε)). The analysis provides explicit schedules for iterations, samples per iteration, and chain length as functions of mixing constants (C, α). The paper also connects to projection onto fast-mixing sets via existing results for Ising/MRFs (e.g., spectral-norm constraints), and illustrates with a small Ising example.

Relation to prior work: It builds on MCMC-MLE (Geyer), CD/PCD (Hinton; Tieleman), and alternatives like pseudo/composite likelihood and score matching. It is closely related to “projecting for fast mixing” (Domke & Liu, NIPS’13; Liu & Domke, NIPS’14) but advances from feasibility of inference to end-to-end learning guarantees and an FPRAS for the constrained MLE. It also complements Steinhardt & Liang’s fast-mixing chains with restarts by providing general exponential-family results without modifying the chain. The convergence analysis leverages inexact gradient descent (Schmidt et al., NIPS’11).

Quality: The theoretical development is sound and carefully reasoned, with appropriate high-probability bounds and explicit dependence on mixing. Assumptions are clearly stated, though some are strong (known geometric mixing with constants, availability and cost of projection).

Clarity: The paper is well organized (clear notation, theorem statements, proof sketches) and readable. Minor suggestions: discuss how to estimate/upper bound C and α in practice; clarify the computational cost of projection in common settings; expand empirical section beyond a toy example.

Originality: The FPRAS framing for MLE over fast-mixing sets and the general exponential-family treatment appear novel, synthesizing mixing-time theory with biased stochastic optimization guarantees.

Significance: Conceptually important—shifts tractability from graph structure/treewidth to mixing behavior; provides rigorous foundations for widely used MCMC-based learning. Practical impact may be tempered by unknown constants and conservative bounds.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, general theoretical guarantees; FPRAS for constrained MLE
- Bridges MCMC mixing and learning with explicit complexity schedules
- Broad applicability to exponential families; connects to prior NIPS work on fast-mixing projections

Cons for acceptance:
- Requires known/usable fast-mixing sets and constants; projections may be costly
- Bounds likely loose with large constants; limited empirical validation
- Assumes independent fresh samples each iteration; excludes common sample-reuse strategies
- Does not address hidden-variable models or misspecification beyond the Θ constraint
