### Summary of the Main Ideas

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in high-treewidth undirected graphical models, where exact inference for computing the log-likelihood gradient is intractable. While MCMC-based approximations of the gradient are commonly used in practice (e.g., contrastive divergence and its variants), these methods have largely lacked theoretical guarantees. The key idea of this paper is to reframe the notion of a "tractable" model class. Instead of restricting the graph structure to be low-treewidth (as in the Chow-Liu algorithm), the authors propose restricting the model parameters `θ` to a convex set `Θ` for which MCMC methods are guaranteed to be "fast-mixing," i.e., converge geometrically fast to the stationary distribution.

The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of projected gradient descent using MCMC-based gradient estimates under this fast-mixing assumption. The paper proves that this common practical strategy constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the MLE solution within the constrained set `Θ`. The analysis is general, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics. Two cases are considered:
1.  For the general convex case (unregularized MLE), the paper shows that achieving an `ε_f`-accurate solution in log-likelihood requires a total computational effort (number of MCMC transitions) of `O(1/ε_f^3)`, ignoring logarithmic factors.
2.  For the strongly convex case (ridge-regularized MLE), a more efficient rate is established, where achieving an `ε_θ`-accurate solution in parameter distance requires `O(1/ε_θ^2)` effort.

For both cases, the authors provide explicit schedules for setting the number of gradient steps, MCMC samples, and MCMC chain lengths to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. This work provides a formal foundation for a widely used class of learning algorithms, connecting the theory of MCMC mixing times with the theory of inexact convex optimization.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis appears sound and rigorous. The authors carefully break down the gradient estimation error into a bias term (from finite MCMC steps) and a variance term (from finite sampling) and bound both appropriately. The bias is controlled by the fast-mixing assumption, while the variance is handled using concentration inequalities (Bernstein's and Hoeffding's). These bounds are then integrated with existing convergence results for gradient descent with inexact gradients from Schmidt et al. [2011] to derive the final complexity guarantees. The claims are well-supported by the proofs provided in the appendix. The paper is a complete piece of theoretical work.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem setup, notation, and assumptions are clearly defined at the outset. The main results (Theorems 2 and 6) are stated precisely, and the proof sketches provide valuable intuition before deferring the full details to the appendix. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their work, explaining how known mixing time results for models like the Ising model can be used to define the required "fast-mixing" parameter sets `Θ`. The single illustrative example, while simple, effectively demonstrates the application of the theory.

**Originality:** The core idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets, rather than structural properties like treewidth, is a novel and insightful contribution. While practitioners have implicitly favored parameters that lead to better MCMC performance for years (e.g., via weight decay), this paper is the first to formalize this notion and use it as the foundation for a provably correct learning algorithm. It establishes a novel and important bridge between the fields of MCMC theory and machine learning optimization, providing the first convergence guarantees for MCMC-based MLE that account for both bias and variance in the gradient.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. It addresses a fundamental gap between the theory and practice of learning intractable graphical models. By providing an FPRAS for MLE in these models, the paper moves MCMC-based learning from a heuristic to a principled and guaranteed algorithmic framework. Researchers and practitioners can use these results to reason about hyperparameter choices (e.g., number of Gibbs steps in contrastive divergence) and understand the trade-offs between computational effort and approximation accuracy. While the derived bounds may be loose in practice, they provide the correct scaling relationships and a formal justification for a popular class of methods. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research aimed at tightening these bounds, extending the analysis to other sampling schemes (like persistent CD), and exploring richer classes of fast-mixing models.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Practicality of Fast-Mixing Sets:** The major assumption is the existence of a known, convex, fast-mixing set `Θ` onto which one can efficiently project. The examples given (based on Dobrushin-type conditions for Ising models) often lead to very conservative bounds on interaction strengths, potentially limiting the expressive power of the learnable models. The practical utility of the framework depends heavily on the discovery of broader, less restrictive, and still projectable fast-mixing sets for more complex models.

2.  **Conservatism of Theoretical Bounds:** As the authors note in their experiment, the derived schedules for `K`, `M`, and `v` are likely to be quite conservative, leading to computational costs that are much higher than what is used in practice. This is a common feature of high-probability theoretical bounds but does limit the direct prescriptive value of the formulas. A discussion on which aspects of the analysis contribute most to this looseness would be beneficial.

3.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's contribution is primarily theoretical, but the single small-scale experiment feels insufficient. A more thorough empirical study, even on synthetic data, could have compared the proposed guaranteed approach against standard heuristics like PCD or alternative methods like pseudolikelihood. This would help quantify the practical trade-offs in computational cost and solution quality (e.g., when the true model is outside `Θ` or the data-generating distribution is misspecified).

4.  **Scope of MCMC Analyzed:** The analysis assumes that at each gradient step, MCMC chains are run independently from a fixed starting distribution. This model does not cover the popular and often more efficient persistent contrastive divergence (PCD) algorithm, where chains are initialized from the samples of the previous iteration. While analyzing PCD is a known hard problem, acknowledging this limitation more explicitly and discussing the challenges of extending the current framework would be valuable.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novel conceptual contribution:** Reframes "tractability" in terms of fast-mixing parameter sets, a powerful alternative to structural constraints.
*   **First theoretical guarantees:** Provides the first formal proof that MCMC-based MLE can be an FPRAS, bridging a major gap between theory and practice.
*   **High technical quality:** The analysis is rigorous, sound, and builds upon established results in optimization and probability.
*   **High significance:** The results are important for the machine learning community, providing a foundation for principled learning in intractable models and guiding future research.
*   **Excellent clarity and presentation.**

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The practicality of the required fast-mixing sets and projection operators may be limited beyond simple models.
*   The derived theoretical bounds are likely very loose, limiting their direct use for setting hyperparameters in practice.
*   The empirical evaluation is minimal.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant and original contribution to our understanding of learning in intractable graphical models. The weaknesses are primarily related to the gap between theory and practice, which is common for foundational work of this nature. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the core idea and the rigor of the analysis, far outweigh these limitations. The paper is a clear fit for NIPS and is likely to have a lasting impact on the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
