

# Peer Review

This paper proposes a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth undirected graphical models by restricting parameters to "fast-mixing sets" where MCMC inference is guaranteed to mix quickly. The authors prove that when parameters are constrained to such a set, gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation can find a maximum likelihood solution inside the set with high probability, providing theoretical guarantees for a method commonly used in practice without theoretical backing.

The paper builds on the standard approach of restricting to tractable parameter sets (e.g., tree-structured parameters via Chow-Liu), but introduces a fundamentally different notion of tractability based on mixing time guarantees. This is distinct from prior work on tractable learning, such as pseudolikelihood (Besag, 1974), score matching (Hyvärinen, 2005), or Abbeel et al.'s (2006) method, which typically require well-specified models or yield intractable inference. The authors' approach is more general than recent work on fast-mixing models (Steinhardt and Liang, 2014; Liu and Domke, 2014), as it applies to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just specific graphical models.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Quality:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for both convex and strongly convex cases, establishing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme. The proofs are well-structured and address the key challenge of bounding errors from MCMC approximation.

2. **Novelty:** The concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" represents a significant conceptual advance over traditional tractability notions based on treewidth. The paper clearly differentiates this from prior work, and the results are substantially different from those in recent NIPS proceedings.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with detailed notation and sufficient explanation of key concepts. The example in Section 6 helps ground the theoretical results.

4. **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental problem in graphical models with clear theoretical implications. The approach could enable practical maximum likelihood learning in models where inference was previously intractable, and the results apply broadly to exponential families.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Practical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical results demonstrating the approach's practical utility. The small example in Section 6 (4x4 grid with 5 samples) is insufficient to evaluate scalability or practical performance.

2. **Projection Complexity:** The paper acknowledges that the computational cost of projection onto the fast-mixing set is not addressed, though this could be significant for large models. This limitation is noted in the Discussion but isn't explored in depth.

3. **Implementation Details:** The paper doesn't address practical considerations like initialization strategies for MCMC (contrastive divergence vs. uniform initialization) or how to choose the fast-mixing set in practice.

4. **Comparison to Alternatives:** While the paper mentions existing methods, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity or practical performance against alternatives like pseudolikelihood or variational methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper makes a theoretically significant contribution by establishing the first rigorous guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning.
- The novel concept of fast-mixing parameter sets is both elegant and generalizable beyond graphical models.
- The results provide a strong theoretical foundation for a common practice that previously lacked guarantees.
- The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a fundamental problem in the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The purely theoretical nature of the paper without empirical validation limits its practical impact.
- The computational cost of the projection step is not addressed, which could be a significant bottleneck in practice.
- The small-scale example doesn't demonstrate scalability or real-world applicability.
- The paper doesn't address how to practically select the fast-mixing set for a given model.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in graphical models with significant potential impact. While the lack of empirical validation is a notable limitation, the paper's theoretical rigor, novelty, and significance make it a strong candidate for NIPS. The authors have clearly distinguished their work from prior contributions and provided a complete theoretical analysis.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written and organized (Clarity), introduces a novel concept of tractability (Originality), and addresses a fundamental problem with broad applicability (Significance). The theoretical guarantees fill an important gap in the literature for a method widely used in practice.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should acknowledge the limitations of the purely theoretical approach in the final version, and potentially address the projection complexity issue in future work. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical foundations of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models.
