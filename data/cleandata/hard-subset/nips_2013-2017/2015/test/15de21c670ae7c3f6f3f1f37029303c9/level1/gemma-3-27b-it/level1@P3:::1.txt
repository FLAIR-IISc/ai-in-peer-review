This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximum likelihood learning in graphical models with high treewidth, offering a novel approach based on restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set and leveraging MCMC for gradient estimation. The core contribution is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for ML estimation within such sets, with provable convergence rates for both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases. 

The motivation is well-presented, contrasting the limitations of existing methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching, composite likelihoods) with the tractability offered by fast-mixing parameter sets. The paper correctly identifies the core challenge – theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based gradient estimation – and provides a solid theoretical foundation for addressing it. The connection to exponential families is a clever generalization, extending the applicability beyond traditional graphical models.  The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, building upon established results in Markov chain convergence and optimization. The inclusion of both convex and strongly convex analyses is a strength, providing different guarantees under varying conditions. The clear presentation of results, alongside lower bounds and practical considerations (as illustrated in the example section), adds significant value.

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications remain somewhat unclear. The definition of "fast-mixing" (Equation 2) relies on knowing constants (C, α) characterizing the mixing rate, which are often difficult to obtain in practice. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t offer much guidance on estimating these constants for real-world models.  The connection to existing work on mixing time bounds (Section 3.1) feels a bit superficial; while references are provided, the discussion lacks depth regarding the practical applicability of these bounds for different graphical model structures.

The example section, while helpful, feels slightly disconnected from the theoretical development. It’s not entirely clear how the chosen parameters (.2 for θij bound) directly relate to the theoretical guarantees presented earlier. The statement about L=10 converging faster than the theoretically predicted L=97 is interesting, but lacks explanation. Is this due to a tighter constant hidden within the theoretical bound, or is it an indication that the theory is overly conservative?

Furthermore, the discussion of limitations in the conclusion, while honest, feels a bit brief. The issue of initializing samples (e.g., using contrastive divergence) is mentioned, but not explored in detail. A deeper discussion of the trade-offs between different initialization strategies and their impact on convergence would be valuable.  The projection step, crucial for the algorithm, is mentioned but its computational cost isn’t thoroughly examined.  For complex parameter spaces, the projection itself could become a bottleneck.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of utilizing fast-mixing parameter sets for tractable ML learning is a novel and promising direction.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method, with provable convergence guarantees.
*   **Generality:** Extending the results to the broader exponential family framework enhances the applicability of the work.
*   **Significance:**  An FPRAS for ML learning in high-treewidth models is a significant result, potentially enabling applications previously intractable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Practical Applicability:**  The dependence on knowing mixing rate constants (C, α) poses a practical challenge.
*   **Limited Discussion of Mixing Time Bounds:** The connection to existing mixing time results could be more in-depth.
*   **Briefness of Limitations Discussion:** The conclusion’s discussion of limitations feels somewhat superficial.
*   **Projection Cost:** The computational cost of the projection step isn’t adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are strong and the proposed approach offers a promising alternative to existing methods for ML learning in high-treewidth models. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas. While further work is needed to address the practical challenges and fully explore the limitations, the novelty and theoretical rigor justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised above regarding the practical estimation of mixing rate constants, a more detailed discussion of mixing time bounds, and a more thorough examination of the projection cost.  A slightly expanded discussion of limitations and potential future directions would also be beneficial.
