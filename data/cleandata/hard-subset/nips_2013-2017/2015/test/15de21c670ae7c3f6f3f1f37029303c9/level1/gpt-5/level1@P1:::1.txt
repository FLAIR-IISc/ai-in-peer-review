Summary
The paper studies maximum likelihood learning in exponential families under intractable inference by constraining parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ—i.e., a convex subset of parameters for which a chosen MCMC kernel Mθ mixes geometrically fast with uniform constants (C, α) over Θ. The main result shows that projected gradient descent, using gradients approximated by MCMC samples obtained after v transitions from arbitrary restarts, converges to an approximate MLE constrained to Θ with high probability. For bounded sufficient statistics t(x), the authors prove a Lipschitz gradient bound and give explicit complexity schedules. In the unregularized (convex) case, they achieve ε-accuracy in objective value with total MCMC effort scaling as ~ (1/ε)^3 up to logarithmic factors; with ridge regularization (strongly convex), ε-accuracy in parameter distance scales as ~ (1/ε)^2 up to logs. They also provide near-matching lower bounds (up to logs), yielding FPRAS-type guarantees. The analysis applies to general exponential families (not only graphical models) provided bounded statistics and a projection onto Θ exist. The paper complements prior work on tree-structured learning (Chow-Liu), pseudolikelihood/score matching (Besag; Hyvärinen; Marlin & de Freitas), composite likelihood (Varin et al.), and projections to mixing sets (Domke & Liu NIPS’13; Liu & Domke NIPS’14), and relates to fast-mixing model learning (Steinhardt & Liang) and MCMC-MLE/SA (Geyer; Gu & Zhu; Descombes et al.). It clarifies when widely-used MCMC-based gradient approximations are provably reliable.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The framework is coherent: bounded statistics imply Lipschitz gradients via TV bounds; geometric mixing controls bias in gradient estimates; concentration bounds (Hoeffding/Bernstein/Efron–Stein) control sampling noise; convergence follows from inexact projected gradient results (Schmidt et al.). The complexity schedules and lower bounds align and are carefully derived. Assumptions are clearly stated (uniform mixing over Θ, ability to project).
- Limitations: The need for known, uniform mixing constants (C, α) over Θ and a tractable projection operator is strong. In many models, establishing such Θ with useful size and computing C, α is nontrivial; known constructions (Dobrushin/spectral norm constraints for Ising/MRFs) can be conservative. The analysis assumes independent fresh chains every iteration (no sample persistence/pools), potentially overstating required effort relative to common practice. Bounds can be loose (e.g., the Lipschitz constant L = 4R^2_2 + λ; notation aside, likely pessimistic).
- Empirics: A didactic 4×4 Ising example illustrates feasibility but does not validate tightness or practical scalability. No comparison to pseudolikelihood/CD or to persistent sampling strategies.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with helpful background on mixing times and a clear setup. However:
- Notation around R2/Ra and “R22” is confusing; define a single bound R = supx ||t(x)||2 and keep it consistent.
- Algorithm 1 is referenced but not shown in the excerpt; ensure pseudo-code with all hyperparameters and projection step.
- Be explicit about per-iteration computational costs, including projection complexity for the different Θ instantiations (e.g., spectral-norm projection via SVD).

Originality
The novelty lies in combining projections to certified fast-mixing parameter sets with a full convergence and complexity analysis for MCMC-approximated gradient descent, yielding FPRAS-like guarantees and near-matching lower bounds. Prior NIPS work addressed either tractable structures (trees), projections for mixing, or learning fast-mixing models, but not end-to-end guarantees for MLE within Θ with explicit schedules and complexity. The lower-bound/upper-bound pairing is a nice contribution.

Significance
Conceptually significant: it provides a principled route to make MCMC-based maximum likelihood optimization polynomial-time under explicit tractability assumptions, and formalizes a common heuristic practice. Practically, impact may be limited by the strength of the assumptions (certified uniform mixing, feasible projection) and the conservative constants. Nonetheless, the work could influence future algorithm design (e.g., regularizers projecting toward certifiably mixing regions; adaptive schedules that don’t need known C, α).

Pros
- Clear, general framework for learning with MCMC bias/noise under fast-mixing constraints.
- Provable convergence with explicit complexity schedules; near-matching lower bounds.
- Applies to general exponential families with bounded statistics.
- Connects and extends several lines of prior work; good positioning within literature.

Cons
- Strong assumptions: known, uniform geometric mixing constants over Θ; tractable projection.
- Conservative bounds and limited empirical validation; unclear practical competitiveness.
- Excludes common practices like persistent chains/pools; independence across iterations may be unnecessary.
- Notational rough edges (R2/Ra), and some proofs deferred, making verification harder.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and unify notation; provide complete Algorithm 1 and per-iteration cost analysis including projection.
- Discuss how to handle unknown C, α in practice (e.g., doubling/halving schedules for v, adaptive stopping, or using certifiable upper bounds via Dobrushin/spectral norms).
- Extend discussion to persistent initialization and show how to adapt proofs (e.g., mixing from warm starts, contractive couplings) or at least bound improvements under small ||θk−θk−1||.
- Provide larger-scale experiments comparing against pseudolikelihood and CD with/without regularization, showing trade-offs between modeling accuracy and certified tractability.
- Explore other MCMC kernels (e.g., Swendsen–Wang) and corresponding Θ, and comment on projection feasibility.
- If possible, separate optimization error from statistical estimation error to give end-to-end guarantees on excess risk.

Overall assessment
A solid theoretical paper with clear contributions to the understanding of MCMC-based learning under mixing constraints. While practical impact is curtailed by assumptions and limited experiments, the conceptual clarity and completeness of the analysis make it a worthwhile NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance, conditional on clarifying notation and expanding the practical discussion.
