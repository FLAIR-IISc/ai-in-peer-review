### Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the intractable problem of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) in high-treewidth undirected graphical models. Instead of the common approach of restricting the model structure (e.g., to trees as in the Chow-Liu algorithm), the authors propose a novel form of tractability: restricting the model parameters to a convex set Θ for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are guaranteed to be "fast-mixing."

The core contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of a widely used but poorly understood heuristic: performing projected gradient descent on the negative log-likelihood, where the intractable expectation in the gradient is approximated using MCMC samples. The paper proves that if the parameters are constrained to a fast-mixing set, this procedure constitutes a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the MLE solution within that set. This result is general, applying to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models.

The authors provide two distinct analyses. For the general convex case (unregularized MLE), they show that achieving an ε-accurate solution in log-likelihood requires a total computational effort cubic in 1/ε (ignoring log factors). For the strongly convex case (ridge-regularized MLE), they leverage strong convexity to show that an ε-accurate solution in parameter distance can be found with effort quadratic in 1/ε. The analysis explicitly provides schedules for setting the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples per step (M), and MCMC transitions per sample (v) to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability.

This work builds a crucial bridge between the theory of MCMC mixing times (e.g., Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Hayes, 2006) and the practice of machine learning. While many have used MCMC to approximate gradients for learning (e.g., in Boltzmann machines), this was done without formal convergence guarantees due to the biased nature of the gradient estimates from finite-length chains. This paper provides the first such non-asymptotic guarantees by formalizing the intuition that fast mixing enables efficient learning. It is also related to work on stochastic optimization with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011) and methods for finding and projecting onto fast-mixing parameter sets (Domke and Liu, 2013).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed analysis, with clear proof sketches provided in the main text. The work is complete, providing explicit complexity bounds and hyperparameter schedules. The authors are careful to state their assumptions (e.g., bounded sufficient statistics, existence of a geometric mixing rate, ability to project onto Θ) and to analyze the convex and strongly convex cases separately, which require different proof techniques and yield different convergence properties.
*   **Weaknesses:** The derived bounds likely have large constants and may be conservative, as suggested by the experiment in Section 6. The practical utility of the proposed schedules is limited by the fact that they depend on problem-specific constants (like the Lipschitz constant L, mixing rate parameters C and α) which are often unknown or difficult to estimate. The analysis also assumes the cost of projection onto Θ is negligible compared to the sampling cost, which may not hold for complex constraint sets.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:** The introduction provides excellent motivation and context, clearly positioning the work against alternatives like pseudolikelihood and structurally-constrained models. The notation is introduced clearly, and the problem setup is unambiguous. The main theorems are stated formally and are easy to understand. The inclusion of intuitive proof sketches is very helpful for the reader. The overall narrative flows logically from the problem definition to the theoretical results and their implications.
*   **Weaknesses:** I have no significant complaints regarding clarity. The presentation is a model of how theoretical papers should be written.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original.
*   **Strengths:** The central idea of defining tractability via MCMC mixing speed, rather than graphical structure, is a novel and powerful concept. While the algorithm itself (projected gradient descent with MCMC gradients) is not new, the rigorous analysis providing the first FPRAS guarantee for it is a groundbreaking contribution. It establishes a formal connection between two previously disparate fields—MCMC mixing time analysis and optimization for machine learning—and provides a new theoretical foundation for a whole class of practical algorithms.
*   **Weaknesses:** The novelty lies almost entirely in the theoretical analysis rather than in the algorithm. However, given the long-standing gap between theory and practice in this area, this analytical novelty is a major strength.

**Significance:** The paper's theoretical significance is very high.
*   **Strengths:** This work provides a foundational theoretical justification for a common class of learning algorithms that previously relied only on heuristics and empirical success. It answers a fundamental question: under what conditions, and how efficiently, can we learn models where inference is performed by MCMC? This is a major advance. The results are likely to inspire follow-up work on analyzing more complex MCMC-based learning procedures (e.g., contrastive divergence variants, methods using persistent chains) and on designing new models and projection operators for different fast-mixing sets. The generality of the framework (any exponential family) broadens its impact beyond the initial motivation of graphical models.
*   **Weaknesses:** The immediate practical impact is somewhat limited, as the authors acknowledge. The derived hyperparameter schedules are likely too conservative for direct use, and practitioners will probably continue to use heuristics for setting learning rates and sample sizes. The requirement of an efficient projection algorithm may also limit the choice of fast-mixing parameter sets.

### Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** Provides the first rigorous, non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for maximum likelihood learning using MCMC-approximated gradients.
2.  **High Originality:** Introduces a novel concept of tractability based on mixing speed, bridging the gap between MCMC theory and practical ML.
3.  **Theoretical Significance:** Establishes a foundational result that could spur significant new research in learning intractable models.
4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a complex theoretical argument accessible and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Direct Practical Impact:** The derived hyperparameter schedules are likely too conservative and dependent on unknown constants to be used directly in practice.
2.  **Strong Assumptions:** The analysis relies on the existence of an efficient projection operator for the fast-mixing set Θ, which may not always be available.

In my opinion, the profound theoretical significance and originality of this work far outweigh its limited immediate practical applicability. It addresses a long-standing open problem with a rigorous and elegant analysis. This is precisely the kind of foundational paper that NIPS should be publishing.
