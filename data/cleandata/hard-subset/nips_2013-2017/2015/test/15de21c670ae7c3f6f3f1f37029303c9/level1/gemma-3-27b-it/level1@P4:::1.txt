This paper explores a novel approach to maximum likelihood learning in graphical models by restricting parameters to a “fast-mixing” set, where MCMC inference converges quickly. It provides theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients, establishing a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood solution within this set.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth graphical models. It clearly distinguishes its approach from existing methods like pseudolikelihood or composite likelihoods, highlighting their limitations in sample complexity and guarantees when the model is misspecified. The connection to existing work on mixing times and the applicability to a broader exponential family framework are well-articulated. The theoretical results – FPRAS guarantees for both unregularized and ridge-regularized cases – are significant and clearly stated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of using "fast-mixing" parameter sets as a tractable constraint for maximum likelihood learning is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the proposed approach, establishing FPRAS with well-defined complexity bounds.
*   **Generality:** The results are not limited to graphical models but apply to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, broadening the scope of impact.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The setup and notations are clearly defined, and the proof sketches provide useful intuition.
*   **Completeness:** The analysis covers both convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, providing a comprehensive understanding of the algorithm’s behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Relevance:** While theoretically sound, the practical implications of the approach are not fully explored. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the challenges in identifying and projecting onto fast-mixing sets in real-world scenarios. The example provided feels somewhat contrived.
*   **Computational Cost of Projection:** The paper touches on the computational complexity of projecting onto the fast-mixing set but does not provide a detailed analysis of this cost, especially for complex models. This cost could potentially outweigh the benefits of faster MCMC convergence.
*   **Assumptions on Mixing Rate:** The analysis relies on knowing the mixing rate constants (C and α) for the MCMC algorithm. In practice, these constants are often difficult to determine accurately, potentially limiting the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical example is limited and doesn’t demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods. A more extensive empirical evaluation with diverse datasets and models would strengthen the paper.
*   **Literature Review Depth:** While references are provided, the coverage of recent work, particularly in variational inference and alternative approaches to approximate learning in graphical models, could be more comprehensive. For example, recent advancements in score-based generative models, which also rely on MCMC for inference, aren't discussed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-founded approach to maximum likelihood learning.
*   The FPRAS guarantees are a significant contribution to the field.
*   The generality of the results makes them potentially applicable to a wide range of problems.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical relevance of the approach is uncertain, and the paper lacks a strong empirical validation.
*   The computational cost of projection and the difficulty of determining mixing rate constants could limit the applicability of the method.
*   A more comprehensive literature review would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong theoretical contribution with a novel idea and rigorous analysis. However, the limited empirical evaluation and potential practical challenges raise some concerns. While not immediately applicable to a wide range of real-world problems, the theoretical results are significant and warrant acceptance. The reviewers should push for a more thorough discussion of the practical limitations and potential avenues for future research that address these limitations.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions, requesting a more detailed discussion of practical considerations and a more extensive empirical evaluation.
