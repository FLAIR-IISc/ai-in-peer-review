

# Review of "Maximum Likelihood Learning With Arbitrary Treewidth via Fast-Mixing Parameter Sets"

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models by shifting the focus from treewidth constraints to fast-mixing parameter sets. The authors argue that instead of restricting to tree-structured models (as in Chow-Liu or high-girth approaches), one can constrain parameters to sets where Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) mixing is guaranteed, enabling theoretical guarantees for gradient descent with MCMC-based gradient approximation. The key insight is that for exponential families with bounded sufficient statistics, when parameters are constrained to such fast-mixing sets, gradient descent with MCMC approximations provides a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for maximum likelihood learning.

The paper builds on foundational work in graphical models (Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993; Chow & Liu, 1968) and MCMC mixing theory (Levin et al., 2009), but makes a significant conceptual shift by connecting mixing time guarantees to learning guarantees rather than inference guarantees. Previous work (e.g., Abbeel et al., 2006; Heinemann & Globerson, 2014) typically focused on tractable inference structures or approximate learning methods without theoretical guarantees, while this paper establishes the first theoretical guarantees for a widely-used practical approach.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound and well-justified. The authors provide rigorous theoretical guarantees with clear proof sketches for all main results. The key theorems establish that for unregularized learning, achieving ε-accuracy in log-likelihood requires effort cubic in 1/ε, while ridge-regularized learning achieves ε-accuracy in parameter distance with effort quadratic in 1/ε. The proofs carefully address the bias and variance in gradient estimates due to MCMC sampling, and the authors appropriately acknowledge limitations (e.g., the complexity of projection, initialization of MCMC chains). The theoretical bounds are well-motivated and supported by the example in Section 6, which demonstrates that the algorithm performs better than the conservative theoretical bounds predict.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide clear definitions of all key terms and notation, and the logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results is excellent. The proof sketches provide intuitive explanations without sacrificing rigor. The example in Section 6 is particularly helpful for understanding the practical implications of the theoretical results. The only minor weakness is that some readers unfamiliar with mixing time theory might find Section 3.1 challenging, but the paper provides sufficient background to follow the main results.

**Originality**: This is a highly original contribution. While previous work focused on tractable inference structures (trees, high-girth), this paper introduces the novel concept of "fast-mixing parameter sets" and establishes the first theoretical guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning. The connection between mixing time guarantees and learning guarantees is fundamentally new, and the results apply to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work, including the tree-structured parameter sets (Chow-Liu) and the well-specified model assumptions of other methods (pseudolikelihood, score matching).

**Significance**: The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical models: the intractability of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth models. By providing theoretical guarantees for a practical approach (MCMC-based gradient descent), it bridges a critical gap between theory and practice. The results demonstrate that learning can be performed efficiently even in high-treewidth models, as long as parameters are constrained to fast-mixing sets. The complexity bounds (cubic vs. quadratic in 1/ε) provide a theoretical foundation for understanding the computational cost of such approaches. While the paper acknowledges limited practical contribution, the theoretical framework is significant for guiding future algorithm design and understanding the limits of MCMC-based learning.

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Strong theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental problem in graphical models
- Novel perspective connecting mixing time guarantees to learning guarantees
- Rigorous proofs with clear intuition provided through proof sketches
- General framework applicable to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics
- Well-written with clear organization and helpful example

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- Limited empirical validation (the example is synthetic and doesn't compare to other methods)
- The paper acknowledges that the projection step might be expensive in some cases, though it's not thoroughly analyzed
- The theoretical bounds are conservative (as demonstrated in the example), which might limit practical appeal

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models and maximum likelihood learning. While it lacks empirical validation, its theoretical rigor and novelty more than compensate for this limitation. The results provide a solid theoretical foundation for a widely-used practical approach, and the framework is general enough to potentially inspire future work in learning with intractable models. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the theoretical understanding of a fundamental problem in machine learning with clear connections to practical methods.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is substantial, the writing is clear and precise, and the results significantly advance our understanding of when and how MCMC-based learning can be guaranteed to work well. The paper is a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings and will likely influence future work in graphical models and learning with intractable inference.
