Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies maximum likelihood learning in exponential families under an alternative tractability notion: instead of restricting to low-treewidth structures, constrain parameters to a “fast-mixing” set Θ in which a chosen MCMC kernel mixes geometrically fast with known constants (C, α). The algorithm is projected gradient descent on the negative log-likelihood (with optional ridge regularization), where gradients are approximated by MCMC sampling at each iterate and then projected back onto Θ. The core technical contribution is a high-probability convergence analysis that explicitly accounts for biased, noisy MCMC gradients, yielding fully polynomial-time randomized approximation schemes (FPRAS) for the maximum-likelihood solution within Θ. For the unregularized (merely convex) case, achieving ε-accuracy in the objective requires total MCMC effort O(ε−3 polylog(1/ε)); with ridge regularization (strong convexity), achieving ε-accuracy in parameter distance requires O(ε−2 polylog(1/ε)) effort. The analysis leverages (i) a Lipschitz gradient bound that follows from bounded sufficient statistics, (ii) mixing-time control to bound bias, and (iii) concentration inequalities to control sampling noise; it then plugs into convergence results for projected gradient descent with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., NIPS’11).

The work is well situated relative to: pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, score matching, and local/statistical recovery methods that avoid likelihood intractability but may not converge to the M-projection under misspecification; Chow–Liu and other tractable-structure learning that restricts treewidth; and prior attempts at learning fast-mixing models, notably projection to fast-mixing Ising parameter sets (Domke & Liu, NIPS’13) and restart-based fast-mixing constructions (Steinhardt & Liang, ICML’15). The paper’s novelty is to provide general exponential-family guarantees for MCMC-based likelihood optimization under fast-mixing constraints, with explicit schedules and complexity bounds.

Quality
The overall analysis is careful and technically sound under the stated assumptions. The decomposition of gradient error into mixing bias and sampling variance, the use of Efron–Stein/Bernstein (convex case) and vector Hoeffding plus union bounds (strongly convex case), and the projection-based argument are standard but nontrivial to combine; the paper does so cleanly. The Lipschitz gradient bound hinges on bounded t(x) and a TV continuity bound for exponential families; the constants look conservative (e.g., L = 4R2^2 + λ), which is typical but could be tightened. The FPRAS characterization is meaningful conditional on Θ having polynomial-time projection and polynomial mixing parameters in problem size. Proof sketches are plausible; full proofs (appendix) will be important to verify the total variation continuity step and the dependence of α, C on model size and Θ.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The setup, notation, and algorithmic schedule are explicit. The distinction between convex and strongly convex regimes is clear, and the role of Θ, C, α is explicit. The example is modest but illustrative. Some clarity suggestions:
- Be explicit about how α and C scale with dimension N for the concrete Θ you propose (e.g., spectral-norm bounded Ising), and summarize known bounds to reassure readers that 1/(1−α) remains polynomial.
- State assumptions on the MCMC kernel (irreducible, aperiodic, reversible?) and whether the TV bounds are uniform over all initializations.
- Discuss how one chooses step sizes and whether knowledge of L is required; the example uses smaller-than-theory L, which should be acknowledged as heuristic.

Originality
Confining learning to fast-mixing parameter sets is not entirely new (Domke & Liu; Steinhardt & Liang), but this paper advances the state of the art by:
- Providing a unified exponential-family treatment with bounded sufficient statistics.
- Proving convergence rates with explicit sample and mixing budgets that yield an FPRAS within Θ.
- Bridging the gap between widely used practice (MCMC-based likelihood gradients) and theory by handling bias rigorously.

Significance
The work is conceptually significant: it reframes “tractability” for learning as fast mixing rather than low treewidth, and turns a ubiquitous heuristic into a method with guarantees. Practically, impact hinges on the availability and expressiveness of Θ with provable mixing and efficient projection. For Ising/Potts with spectral/induced-norm constraints, the approach seems implementable; for richer exponential families, constructing Θ and obtaining α, C may be challenging. Still, the result provides a principled baseline and may influence future design of regularizers that enforce fast mixing.

Strengths
- Solid theoretical contribution establishing FPRAS-type guarantees for MCMC-based ML under clear assumptions.
- General exponential-family setting; analysis separates convex vs strongly convex regimes with appropriate rates.
- Explicit schedules for iterations/samples/chain length and lower bounds that clarify near-optimality up to logs.
- Good connection to prior work on fast mixing and projections for Ising/MRFs.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Strong assumptions: bounded sufficient statistics exclude common continuous families (e.g., Gaussians) unless truncated; extension to sub-Gaussian tails would broaden scope.
- Practical feasibility: projecting onto Θ and, more importantly, certifying or even estimating (C, α) are nontrivial beyond specific models; without concrete Θ beyond pairwise discrete MRFs, applicability is narrow.
- Independence of samples across iterations and restarting chains each time may be inefficient; analysis does not cover persistent chains or sample pools, which are common in practice.
- Crude constants (e.g., L, TV continuity bound) likely make prescribed budgets overly conservative.
- The empirical section is minimal; no comparison to pseudolikelihood/composite likelihood or to unconstrained CD on moderate-size problems to illustrate tradeoffs.
- FPRAS claim depends on α scaling; if α degrades with N for the chosen Θ, the “polynomial” may hide unfavorable exponents.

Questions for the authors
- Can the TV continuity bound ∥pθ − pφ∥TV ≤ 2R2∥θ − φ∥2 be tightened or justified via KL and Pinsker with explicit constants, and does it introduce dimension dependence?
- Beyond Ising/Potts, what concrete Θ and projection algorithms do you envision (e.g., higher-order factors, conditional random fields), and what are their α, C?
- Can the analysis be extended to biased but correlated gradients from persistent MCMC, perhaps via mixing-within-iterations and stability of projected GD?
- Is it possible to replace boundedness by sub-Gaussian assumptions on t(X) to cover Gaussian graphical models?

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel reframing with rigorous analysis and clear position relative to NIPS literature.
- Technically correct and methodologically clean; likely to influence future work on fast-mixing regularization.
- Provides the first general guarantees for biased MCMC gradients in likelihood learning within a meaningful constraint set.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical impact is limited without richer, empirically validated Θ and estimates of mixing parameters.
- Assumptions (bounded stats, independence, known L and α, C) and constants reduce immediate usability.
- Minimal experiments; lacks comparative study to situate tradeoffs empirically.

Overall, a solid theoretical contribution; I lean toward acceptance for its clarity, rigor, and conceptual impact, while encouraging the authors to strengthen the empirical narrative and broaden the scope of Θ in a revision.
