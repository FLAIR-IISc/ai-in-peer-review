- Studies MLE in exponential families under intractable inference by restricting parameters to a convex “fast-mixing” set Θ with uniform geometric mixing constants (C, α).
- Main result: projected gradient descent with MCMC-approximated gradients (v transitions, fresh restarts) converges to an approximate MLE over Θ with high probability.
- For bounded sufficient statistics, authors prove Lipschitz gradients and give explicit complexity schedules.
- Unregularized case: ε-accuracy in objective with total MCMC cost ~ (1/ε)^3 (up to logs); ridge-regularized case: ε-accuracy in parameter distance with cost ~ (1/ε)^2 (up to logs).
- Near-matching lower bounds provided, yielding FPRAS-style guarantees.
- Analysis applies to general exponential families with bounded statistics and a computable projection onto Θ.
- Technical soundness: coherent use of TV bounds, geometric mixing to control bias, concentration inequalities for sampling noise, and inexact projected gradient theory for convergence.
- Strong assumptions: requires known uniform mixing constants over Θ and tractable projection operators, which can be hard to obtain or conservative in practice.
- Analysis assumes independent fresh MCMC chains each iteration, excluding persistent sampling strategies commonly used in practice.
- Bounds may be loose/pessimistic (e.g., Lipschitz constant estimates), limiting practical tightness.
- Empirics are limited to a didactic 4×4 Ising example and do not assess scalability, tightness, or comparisons to pseudolikelihood/CD/persistent chains.
- Clarity issues: inconsistent notation for statistic norms (R2/Ra/R22) and Algorithm 1 missing in the excerpt; per-iteration computational cost and projection details need clarity.
- Novelty: combines projection-to-mixing sets with full convergence and complexity analysis for MCMC-approximated gradient descent, plus paired lower bounds.
- Significance: conceptually important for making MCMC-based MLE polynomial-time under explicit assumptions, but practical impact limited by strong assumptions and conservative constants.
- Pros: clear general framework, provable convergence with schedules, near-matching bounds, applicability to general exponential families, and good literature positioning.
- Cons: strong/possibly unrealistic assumptions, conservative bounds, limited empirical validation, exclusion of persistent-chain methods, and some notational/proof clarity issues.
- Suggested improvements: unify notation, include full Algorithm 1 and per-iteration cost, discuss handling unknown C/α (adaptive schedules), analyze persistent/warm-start chains, add larger-scale comparisons (pseudolikelihood/CD), and explore other MCMC kernels and projection feasibility.
- Overall assessment: solid theoretical contribution with clear analysis and value for NIPS, but acceptance conditioned on improved notation and expanded practical discussion.
