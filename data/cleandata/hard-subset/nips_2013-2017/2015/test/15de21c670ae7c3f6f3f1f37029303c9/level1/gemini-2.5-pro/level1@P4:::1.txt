### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenge of maximum likelihood learning in undirected graphical models with high treewidth, where exact inference is intractable. Instead of restricting the model structure (e.g., to a tree), the authors propose a novel approach: restricting the model parameters to a convex set Θ for which Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing." The core contribution is a theoretical analysis of projected gradient descent where the gradient is approximated using MCMC samples. The paper proves that this common heuristic is, in fact, a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for finding the maximum likelihood parameters within the constrained set Θ.

This work is situated in the broader context of learning intractable models. It contrasts its approach with methods like Chow-Liu that enforce tractable structures, and with approximate learning objectives like pseudolikelihood or score matching, which may not converge to the M-projection under model misspecification. The authors build upon the rich literature on MCMC mixing times (e.g., Jerrum and Sinclair, 1993) and recent results on the convergence of optimization algorithms with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011).

The main results provide explicit schedules for the number of gradient steps (K), MCMC samples per step (M), and MCMC chain lengths (v) to achieve a desired accuracy with high probability. For unregularized (convex) maximum likelihood, the total computational effort (KMv) to achieve ε-accuracy in log-likelihood is shown to be O(1/ε³), ignoring log factors. For ridge-regularized (strongly convex) problems, the effort to achieve ε-accuracy in parameter distance is O(1/ε²). These results apply generally to any exponential family with bounded sufficient statistics, not just graphical models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proofs are rigorous and build upon established results in convex optimization and probability theory. The analysis carefully decomposes the error in the gradient estimate into a bias term (from finite MCMC chain length) and a variance term (from finite samples), and bounds their impact on the overall optimization procedure. The assumptions, such as the existence of a convex fast-mixing set and the ability to project onto it, are clearly stated. The authors are honest about the limitations of the analysis, for example, by acknowledging that the complexity of the projection step is not included in the main complexity results. The work represents a complete theoretical contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation by clearly outlining the intractability of the problem and the shortcomings of existing approaches. The problem setup, algorithm, and main theoretical results are presented with precision. The use of a table of notation is helpful for the reader. The proof sketches in the main body provide valuable intuition before the full proofs in the appendix. The illustrative example, while simple, effectively demonstrates the concepts and highlights the conservative nature of the theoretical bounds in practice.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this paper is its conceptual novelty. The idea of defining tractability in terms of parameter-dependent mixing times, rather than graph structure (treewidth), is a significant and original perspective. While using MCMC to approximate gradients is a standard heuristic in practice (e.g., in training Restricted Boltzmann Machines), this work is one of the first to provide formal convergence guarantees for this procedure in the context of maximum likelihood estimation. It successfully bridges the gap between the theory of rapid mixing for Markov chains and the theory of stochastic convex optimization, which is a novel and valuable synthesis.

**Significance:** The results are of high theoretical significance. They provide a formal justification for a widely used class of algorithms and a principled framework for analyzing their convergence. This work could inspire further research into characterizing fast-mixing parameter sets for different model classes and developing more efficient projection algorithms. It also provides a theoretical basis for setting hyperparameters (K, M, v) that are often tuned heuristically.

However, the immediate practical significance may be somewhat limited. The main weaknesses are:
1.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** The requirement of knowing a convex set Θ with a uniform geometric mixing guarantee for all parameters within it is strong. While the paper provides examples for Ising models based on bounding interaction strengths, establishing such sets and their associated mixing constants (C, α) for more complex, state-of-the-art models may be very difficult.
2.  **Projection Oracle:** The analysis assumes access to an efficient projection oracle. For complex definitions of Θ, this projection can itself be a computationally expensive optimization problem, and its cost is not accounted for in the final complexity bounds.
3.  **Conservative Bounds:** As the authors' own experiment suggests, the derived bounds for K, M, and v are likely to be quite loose. While theoretically important, they may not provide tight, practical guidance for algorithm configuration compared to empirical tuning.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The paper introduces a new and insightful way to think about tractable learning, shifting focus from graph structure to parameter-based MCMC mixing speed.
*   **Important Theoretical Guarantees:** It provides the first formal FPRAS guarantee for the common practice of MCMC-based maximum likelihood learning under the specified conditions.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous, correct, and builds a strong connection between two important theoretical areas.
*   **High Clarity:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and clearly situates its contributions within the relevant literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Practical Applicability:** The assumptions regarding the knowledge of a fast-mixing set and an efficient projection oracle may limit the method's direct applicability to simple models.
*   **Conservative Nature of Results:** The theoretical schedules for the algorithm's parameters are likely too conservative for practical use, making the contribution primarily of theoretical interest.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is minimal, serving only as a proof of concept rather than a demonstration of practical utility on a challenging problem.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant and original contribution to our understanding of learning in intractable graphical models. The central idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing parameter sets is novel and compelling. The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for a widely used heuristic, which is an important step forward. While the practical applicability of the specific results may be limited by strong assumptions and conservative bounds, the conceptual and theoretical value is high. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and significance far outweigh the weaknesses. This work is likely to be influential and stimulate new research at the intersection of MCMC theory and machine learning.
