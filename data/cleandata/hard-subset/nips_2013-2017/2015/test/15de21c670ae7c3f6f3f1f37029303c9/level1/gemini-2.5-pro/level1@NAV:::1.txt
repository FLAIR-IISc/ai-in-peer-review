This paper addresses the fundamental challenge of maximum likelihood learning in high-treewidth graphical models, where exact gradient computation is intractable. The authors analyze the widely used heuristic of approximating the log-likelihood gradient with samples from MCMC. While this approach is popular (e.g., Tieleman, 2008), it has lacked formal guarantees. The paper's central idea is to constrain the model parameters to a convex "fast-mixing set," where MCMC is guaranteed to converge geometrically. Within this framework, the authors prove that projected gradient descent using MCMC-approximated gradients is a fully-polynomial time randomized approximation scheme (FPRAS) for the constrained maximum likelihood objective. This provides a formal connection between the rich literature on MCMC mixing times (e.g., Jerrum & Sinclair, 1993) and the practical problem of learning intractable models, offering an alternative notion of tractability to structural restrictions like the Chow-Liu algorithm.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous analysis that carefully accounts for both the sampling error (from a finite number of samples) and the mixing error (from a finite MCMC chain length). The authors provide a complete analysis covering both the convex (unregularized) and strongly convex (ridge-regularized) cases, building upon established results in optimization with inexact gradients (Schmidt et al., 2011).

*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated, the notation is precise, and the main theorems are stated lucidly. The inclusion of proof sketches provides excellent intuition for the formal results, making a complex topic accessible.

*   **Originality:** The contribution is highly original. While using MCMC for learning is common, this is the first work to my knowledge that provides non-asymptotic convergence guarantees for this procedure by explicitly defining tractability in terms of mixing rates. This conceptual framing is both novel and powerful.

*   **Significance:** The work is very significant. It provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for a common class of algorithms, offering clear insights into the trade-offs between optimization steps, sample sizes, and MCMC transitions. While the derived schedules may be conservative in practice (as the authors honestly demonstrate), the theoretical framework is a major advance and is likely to inspire follow-up research on designing fast-mixing models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** A technically strong and original paper that provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCMC-based maximum likelihood estimation. It is exceptionally well-written and has high potential for impact by adding rigor to a popular heuristic.
*   **Con:** The practical utility of the specific parameter schedules might be limited by loose theoretical bounds. The complexity of projecting onto the fast-mixing set is not fully incorporated into the final complexity results.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its originality, technical soundness, and significance—far outweigh its minor limitations. It is an excellent contribution to the field.
