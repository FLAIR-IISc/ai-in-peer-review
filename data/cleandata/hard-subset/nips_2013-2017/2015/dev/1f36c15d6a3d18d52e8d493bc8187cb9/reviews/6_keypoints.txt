Paper studies sample complexity for testing whether an unknown discrete distribution p lies ε-away from shape-constrained classes (monotone, log-concave, unimodal, monotone hazard rate).  
Motivation unclear: imposing monotone or log-concave constraints on discrete data requires an ordinal structure and a metric, which limits applicability to many discrete settings.  
Theorem 1 proof implicitly requires independence between the observations Xi and the constructed distribution q; the manuscript should explicitly state that samples used to estimate q must be discarded.  
Lemma 5 gives only an expectation bound E[χ^2(p,q)] ≤ ε^2/500, but Theorem 1 requires a (high-probability or deterministic) χ^2(p,q) ≤ ε^2/500, creating a gap.  
Birgé (1987) is cited repeatedly but concerns continuous density estimation; the exact Birgé result used should be stated.  
The claim “any log-concave distribution is unimodal” is false under the paper’s discrete definition (counterexample provided); proofs relying on this must be corrected.  
The stated sample complexity for testing log-concavity, O(√n/ε^2), only holds when ε > n^{-1/4}; the claim must be qualified for regimes with smaller ε.  
Clarify connections to statistics and cite discrete-specific literature on monotone/log-concave estimation/testing, e.g., Jankowski & Wellner (2009) and Balabdaoui et al. (2013).  
Main contribution is theoretical and builds on Valiant & Valiant (2014), but several proofs and assumptions need greater rigor and explicit clarification.
