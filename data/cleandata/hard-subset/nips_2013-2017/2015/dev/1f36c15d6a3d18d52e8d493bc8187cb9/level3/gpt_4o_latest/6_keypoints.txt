- Problem: Tests whether a discrete distribution p belongs to a class C or is far from it, focusing on small-sample regimes.  
- Main contribution: Computationally efficient, sample-optimal testers for monotonicity, independence, log-concavity, unimodality, and monotone hazard rate.  
- Technique: Novel use of the χ²-statistic in the small-sample regime combined with structural decompositions and convex programming.  
- Optimality: Provides matching lower bounds (extensions of Paninski’s construction) to demonstrate sample-optimality.  
- Strength: Significant theoretical advance with improved sample complexity and removal of logarithmic factors over prior work.  
- Strength: Innovative technical toolkit (χ²-statistic, extended Birgé decomposition, convex programming) for handling complex classes.  
- Strength: Rigorous proofs for upper and lower bounds, with particularly compelling lower-bound arguments.  
- Strength: Broad applicability across several fundamental distribution classes and relevance to property testing/sublinear algorithms.  
- Weakness: Poorly motivated — insufficient explanation of why shape constraints on discrete distributions are practically important.  
- Weakness: Critical independence assumption (between observations and newly drawn q) in Theorem 1 is neither clearly stated nor justified.  
- Weakness: Logical gap between conditions in Lemma 5 and requirements for invoking Theorem 1, weakening proof flow.  
- Weakness: Frequent reliance on Birgé (1987) is unclear because that work treats continuous densities; the discrete connection is not explained.  
- Weakness: Incorrect claim that “any log-concave distribution is unimodal” under the paper’s definitions, undermining some theoretical foundations.  
- Weakness: Sample complexity statement in the abstract is imprecise for regimes where ε decreases with fixed n, potentially misleading readers.  
- Weakness: Lacks references and connections to discrete statistical literature, limiting interdisciplinary impact.  
- Recommendation: Major revisions — address clarity, missing assumptions, logical gaps, errors, and precision before acceptance.  
- For acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, optimal sample complexity with matching lower bounds and broad relevance.  
- Against acceptance: Insufficient clarity in motivations/assumptions, gaps in rigor, factual errors, and missing statistical connections.
