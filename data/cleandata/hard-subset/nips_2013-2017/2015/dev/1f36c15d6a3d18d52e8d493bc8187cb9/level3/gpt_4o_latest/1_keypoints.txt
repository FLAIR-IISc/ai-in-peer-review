- Problem addressed: testing whether a discrete distribution belongs to a class (monotone, log-concave, unimodal) or is far from it.  
- Main contribution: sample-optimal, computationally efficient testers that significantly improve sample complexity over prior work.  
- Technique novelty: novel use of the χ² statistic in the small-sample regime and extensions like Birgé’s decomposition to higher dimensions and Kolmogorov’s max inequality for unimodality.  
- Theoretical completeness: provides matching lower bounds, establishing optimality in the small-sample regime.  
- Algorithm design clarity: presents a clear three-step framework—near-proper learning, computing distance to the class, and χ² testing—applied across classes.  
- Practical relevance concern: focus on discrete cube sample spaces limits applicability to many real-world problems involving continuous variables.  
- Engagement with statistics literature: critique that the paper dismisses statistical work and overlooks finite-sample results and null-distribution simulation approaches.  
- Sparse referencing: fails to adequately cite relevant statistics literature (e.g., Dümbgen and Walther 2008 on shape-constrained inference).  
- Finite-sample claims questioned: although claimed finite-sample, implementations still require large samples, weakening distinction from asymptotic tests.  
- Clarity/accessibility: technical strength is high but exposition could be clearer for readers unfamiliar with property testing and χ² nuances.  
- Recommendation to authors: provide real-world examples where discrete-cube testing is applicable.  
- Recommendation to authors: engage and cite statistical literature, including finite-sample methods.  
- Recommendation to authors: better clarify how their finite-sample guarantees differ from traditional asymptotic approaches.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contributions with optimal sample complexity and novel techniques with broader potential.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical relevance due to discrete focus and insufficient engagement with related statistical literature.  
- Overall conclusion: technically strong and interesting but needs improved practical motivation and better situating within statistical work to increase impact.
