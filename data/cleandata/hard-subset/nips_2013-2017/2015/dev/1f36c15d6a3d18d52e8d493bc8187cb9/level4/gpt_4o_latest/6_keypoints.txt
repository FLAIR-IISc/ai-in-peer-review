- Motivation: Need clearer justification for imposing shape constraints on discrete distributions since monotone/log-concave assumptions require an ordering or metric and may not apply to all discrete data.  
- B1 (Thm 1): The proof implicitly requires independence between observations and the constructed distribution q, which only holds if samples used to estimate q are excluded—this must be stated.  
- B2 (Lemma 5): Lemma 5 gives an expected χ^2 bound E[χ^2(p,q)] ≤ ε^2/500, but Theorem 1 needs a deterministic χ^2(p,q) ≤ ε^2/500, creating a gap to resolve.  
- B3 (Birgé 1987): Clarify which specific result of Birgé (1987) is being used, since that work concerns continuous density estimation while the paper treats discrete distributions.  
- B4 (Log-concavity vs unimodality): The claim that “any log-concave distribution is unimodal” is false under the paper’s discrete definition; provide correction (counterexample given).  
- B5 (Rate for log-concavity testing): The abstract’s O(√n/ε^2) sample claim for log-concavity is imprecise—Theorem 4 only yields this rate when ε > n^−1/4.  
- Connections to statistics: Emphasize that the discrete [n] growing-support setting differs from continuous literature and may be unfamiliar to statisticians.  
- References: Recommend citing discrete-focused works (Jankowski & Wellner 2009; Balabdaoui et al. 2013) for estimation/testing of discrete monotone and log-concave distributions.  
- Overall: While building on Valiant & Valiant (2014) with novel theory, the reviewer is unconvinced of proof rigor and requests addressing the listed theoretical concerns.
