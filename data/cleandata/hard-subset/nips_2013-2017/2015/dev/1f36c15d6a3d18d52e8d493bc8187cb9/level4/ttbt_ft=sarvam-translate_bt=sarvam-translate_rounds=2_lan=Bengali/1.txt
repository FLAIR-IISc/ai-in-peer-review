H1: P is not a monotone.
I liked the paper.
The results are quite interesting. However, I have some concerns:

1. The sample space you used is a discrete cube.
Why would anyone want to test monotony in this space?
In reality, people use monotony tests for continuous random variables.
Could you give an example of a real data analysis problem where your settings are of scientific interest?

2. You have discarded statistical literature that focuses on large sample sizes.
This is somewhat misleading: many tests can be simulated and validated using the null distribution. Asymptotics are often used to obtain specific theoretical results regarding the power of the test. By specific, I mean limits, not any limits.
And there are statistical papers that provide guarantees for limited samples.
An example is:
Dumbgen, Lutz, and Günter Walter.
"Multiscale Inference About a Density." Annals of Statistics (2008): 1758-1785.
However, your reference to vast statistical literature on this topic is very limited. You need to have more references to statistical literature.

3. Also, I am not sure if your test is really a limited sample test.
Suppose I want to use your test and I want to make sure that the type 1 error is less than alpha. (You assume alpha = 1/3, but I assume you can change alpha to any user-specific level.) Your results say: there exist some N0 where the type 1 error is less than alpha, if the sample size N is greater than N, the type 1 error is less than alpha. Asymptotic statistical tests say: the type 1 error of the test is close to alpha for large N. I don't see any real difference. If I use your test with real data, there is no way to know if N is greater than N0. We just have to assume that N is large enough. So it seems to me that at a practical level, it is similar to an asymptotic test.

Interesting paper. But I am not sure if this problem actually occurs in practice. Also, the connections with statistics have been dismissed very easily.
