This manuscript aims to determine the number of observations required to distinguish an unknown distribution $p$ from a set of distributions $\mathcal{C}$, where the distributions of $\mathcal{C}$ are at least $\epsilon$ away in terms of total variance distance. The different classes of $\mathcal{C}$ considered are the monotone, log-concave, unimodal, and monotone hazard rate distributions.

Below are some questions and comments that may help authors improve their manuscript:

A. Purpose: I believe the objectives of imposing size-related constraints on discrete distributions remain to be further explained in this manuscript. Discrete distributions are primarily useful for modeling (i) the frequency of observed objects (e.g., IP addresses) and (ii) data computation. Applying the monotone constraint to (i) means imposing an ordinal structure on the objects; furthermore, using the log-concave constraint requires a metric related to the ordinal structure. These effects somewhat limit the use of the proposed method, as there is no natural ordering of all objects.

B. Theory: B1. Proof of Theorem 1.
To obtain (1) and (2), independence is required between $X_i$ observations and the newly drawn distribution $q$ (which satisfies properties 1 and 2). This is not always true, unless the observations used to estimate $q$ are discarded for the rest of the analysis. This requirement should be more explicitly stated.

B2. Use of Lemma 5.
In the monotone case, Lemma 5 states that a $q$ can be found such that $\mathbb{E}[\chi^2(p,q)]\le \epsilon^2/500$. However, to apply Theorem 1, $\chi^2(p,q) \le \epsilon^2/500$ is required. So there is a discrepancy here.

B3. Continuous and discrete domains.
Birjees (1987) has been cited several times in the development of the theory. As far as I know, Birjees' work is only related to the assumption of density. Can the authors cite specific results from Birjees (1987) that are mentioned multiple times in the manuscript?

B4. Log-concavity vs. Unimodal.
In the proof of Lemma 7, as well as in the H.2 section of the appendix, it is stated that `any log-concave distribution is unimodal'. However, this is false according to the definition given in Section 2 (i.e., $f{i-1}f{i+1} \le fi^2$). For example, consider $n=7$, $f1 = f4 = f7 = 1/3$ and $f2 = f3 = f5 = f6 = 0$.

B5. Log-concavity test rate.
The summary states that $O(sqrt{n}/\epsilon^2)$ samples are required for log-concavity testing. However, in terms of Theorem 4, this is true when $\epsilon > n^{-1/4}$. Clearly, this statement is not correct when we are in the region of reducing $\epsilon$ while keeping $n$ constant.

C. Connection to statistics. It is worth mentioning here that the authors assume that the distribution of interest, i.e., $p$, is discrete. Furthermore, in the development of the theory, $p$ is assumed to have support in $[n]$ (i.e., $\{1,2,\ldots,n\}$), which increases with respect to $n$. This distribution is quite popular in computer science, although not very well known within statistics. The authors cite works such as Hall and Van Keelag (2005) and Kullé and Samworth (2010). However, the cited works are only related to the continuous domain. For more relevant works for estimating/testing a discrete unimodal and log-concave distribution, see Jankowski and Wellner (2009) and Balabdaoui et.al (2013).

References
Jankowski, H. and Wellner, J.A. (2009). Estimation of a discrete unimodal distribution. Electronic Journal of Statistics, 3, 1567--1605.
Balabdaoui, F., Jankowski, H., Rufibach, K. and Pavlides, M. (2013). Asymptotic distribution of discrete log-concave MLE and related applications, Journal of the Royal Statistical Society Series B, 75, 769-790.

The main method depicted in this manuscript is based on Valiant and Valiant (2014), although the new distributions are quite different, which leads to the development of new theories (as well as new lemmas in the appendix). I believe the main contribution of this manuscript is its theory (which seems interesting), although I am still not sure that the proofs are rigorous enough.
