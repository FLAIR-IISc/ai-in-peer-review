H0:
P is uniform.
vs.
H1: P is not uniform.
I liked this paper.
The results are interesting. However, I have some concerns:

1. The sample space you used is a different cube.
Why would anyone want to test for uniformity in this space?
In practice, people use a uniformity test for a continuous random variable.
Can you cite a real data analysis problem where your setting is of scientific interest?

2. You dismiss statistical literature to focus on a large sample regime.
This is a bit misleading: many tests can be made precise by simulating a zero distribution. Asymptotics are used to obtain precise theoretical results about the strength of the tests. By precise, I mean limits, not constraints.
And there are statistical papers that provide finite sample guarantees.
One example is:
Dumbgen, Lutz, and Günther Walther.
"Multiscale Estimation of Density." The Year in Statistics (2008): 1758-1785.
At any rate, your references to the vast statistical literature on this topic are very few. You should have more references to statistical literature.

3. Also, I'm not sure if your test is actually a finite sample test.
Suppose I want to use your test and I want to make sure that the type I error is less than alpha. (You take alpha = 1/3 but I assume that you can make alpha any user-specified level by changing things.) Your results say: there are some N0s such that the type I error is less than alpha if the sample size N is greater than N, then the type I error is less than alpha. Asymptotic statistical tests say: the type I error is close to alpha in the test for large N. I don't see any real difference. If I use your test with real data, I have no way of knowing whether N is greater than N0 or not. We just have to assume that N is large enough. So it seems to me that, on a practical level, it is similar to an asymptotic test.

Interesting paper. But I'm not sure if this problem actually comes up in practice. Also, statistical connections are dismissed too easily.
