This manuscript aims to determine the number of observations required to separate an unknown distribution $p$ from a set of classes $\mathcal{C}$ of distributions, which are at least $\epsilon$ away in the total variance distance. The classes considered in $\mathcal{C}$ include the monotonic, log-concave, unimodel, and monotonic hazard rate distributions.

Below are some questions and comments that are expected to help the author(s) improve the manuscript:

A. Motivations. I think motivations for imposing shape-related constraints on discrete distributions need to be further explained in this manuscript. Discrete distributions are mainly useful for modeling (i) the frequencies of observed objects (e.g., IP addresses) and (ii) data counting. In (i), applying the monotonic constraint means that an ordered structure must be imposed on the objects; moreover, using the log-concave constraint requires a metric associated with the ordered structure. These implications somewhat limit the use of the proposed method, as not all objects have a natural order.

B. Theory. B1. Proof of Theorem 1.

To obtain (1) and (2), independence between observations $X_i$ and the newly drawn distribution $q$ (which satisfies properties 1 and 2) is required. This is not always the case, unless the observations used to estimate $q$ are discarded for the rest of the analysis. This requirement should be stated more explicitly.

B2. Use of Lemma 5.

In the monotonic case, Lemma 5 states that one can find a $q$ such that $\mathbb{E}[\chi^2(p,q)]\le \epsilon^2/500$. However, to apply Theorem 1, $\chi^2(p,q) \le \epsilon^2/500$ is required. So there is a difference in between.

B3. Continuous case vs. Discrete case.

Birje (1987) was quoted a few times in the development of the theory. To my knowledge, Birje's work only relates to density estimation. Can the author(s) cite specific results from Birje (1987) that are mentioned several times in the manuscript?

B4. Log-concavity vs. Unimodal.

In the proof of Lemma 7, as well as in Section H.2 in the appendix, it is stated that `any log-concave distribution is unimodal'. However, this is incorrect given the definition given in Section 2 (i.e., $f{i-1}f{i+1} \le fi^2$). For example, consider $n=7$, $f1 = f4 = f7 = 1/3$ and $f2 = f3 = f5 = f6 = 0$.

B5. Rate for testing log-concavity.

The abstract states that testing log-concavity requires $O(sqrt{n}/\epsilon^2)$ samples. However, given Theorem 4, this is true when $\epsilon > n^{-1/4}$. Clearly, this statement is not accurate when we are in the realm of `fixing $n$ and reducing $\epsilon$'.

C. Connection to statistics. It is appropriate to point out that here the author(s) assumed that the distribution of interest, i.e., $p$, is discrete. Furthermore, in the development of the theory, the support of $p$ is considered to be $[n]$ (i.e., $\{1,2,\ldots,n\}$), which increases with respect to $n$. This setting is quite popular in computer science, although perhaps not very well known in statistics. The author(s) cited works such as Hall and Van Keelegom (2005) and Kule and Samworth (2010). However, the cited work only relates to the continuous case. For more relevant work on estimating/testing discrete monotonic and log-concave distributions, see Jankowski and Wellner (2009) and Balabdaoui et al. (2013).

References
Jankowski, H. and Wellner, J.A. (2009). Estimation of a Discrete Monotonic Distribution. Electronic Journal of Statistics, 3, 1567--1605.
Balabdaoui, F., Jankowski, H., Rufibach, K. and Pavlids, M. (2013). Asymptotic Distribution of Discrete Log-Concave MLE and Related Applications, Journal of the Royal Statistical Society Series B, 75, 769-790.

The main method depicted in this manuscript is based on Valiant and Valiant (2014), although the new settings are quite different, leading to the development of new theories (as well as new lemmas in the appendix). I think the main contribution of this manuscript is its theory (which seems interesting), although I still have to be convinced that the proofs are rigorous enough.
