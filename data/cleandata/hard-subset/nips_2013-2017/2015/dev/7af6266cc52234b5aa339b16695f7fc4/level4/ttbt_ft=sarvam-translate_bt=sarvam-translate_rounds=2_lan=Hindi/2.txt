Summary: The author proposes optimizing tools from the design and analysis of predictive markets to solve the problem of learning a good hypothesis when training data is distributed across multiple parties. The author also proposes modifications to guarantee differential privacy. In more detail, the author proposes maintaining a current hypothesis, allowing participants to update this hypothesis (considering various "betting languages" to do so), and charging/rewarding participants after applying the final hypothesis to a test data point using a family of convex cost functions (parameters by domain element x). (The selection of cost functions is determined by the loss function.) Differential privacy is added by adapting state-of-the-art techniques to the "continuous observation" model (where privacy is required at each step of time, paid linearly for the number of time steps). Quality: The combination of different models and techniques is done efficiently. The paper on motivation seems a bit weak. The results have a taste of "here we know how to do it," rather than the more traditional "here is a well-motivated problem" and "here is our solution and why it is better than previous/obvious solutions" narrative. Clarity: The quality of writing is reasonably good. Originality: None of the tools used are original. Some combinations of them seem original here. Significance: I find the results marginally significant.
