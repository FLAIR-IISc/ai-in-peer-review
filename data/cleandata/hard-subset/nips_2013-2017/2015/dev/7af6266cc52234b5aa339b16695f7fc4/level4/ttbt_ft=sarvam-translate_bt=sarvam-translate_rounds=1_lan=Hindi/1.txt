The authors identify a strategy to compensate agents for revealing private and relevant data. This strategy is based on interesting ideas from machine learning literature, including the use of stochastic gradient descent algorithms to determine payouts for data. This allows agents to be compensated effectively while maintaining a limited budget. The authors also include mechanisms to maintain the privacy of agents and identify various "profit maximization" strategies based on the trust agents place in their data. A significant contribution seems to be identifying a mechanism that compensates agents for their data while maintaining a limited budget.

The usefulness of this family of algorithms depends on timely, accurate information. If "true samples" are only generated in the distant future, Section 2.3 is not viable (see below), and Section 2.1 does not provide greater benefits on traditional betting markets (except for generalizations of more complex prediction functions).

Section 3 does not seem to fundamentally address the serious problem: depending on the type of privacy, there seem to be simple solutions to prevent agent i from knowing about agent j: (a) anonymous participation (b) updates and anonymous relationships between agents (which agent makes what trade is not available to other agents).

A truly useful contribution here is the generalization to using sophisticated prediction tools from prediction markets, thus allowing prediction markets to extend far beyond betting on single binary or scalar variables.

This is great, but in practice, it seems that this will provide very little incentive for participation unless the market runs with a continuous stream of true samples, so that rewards can be evaluated quickly.

(If I don't know what my data is bidding on, how much credit it can get, and when the rewards will come, why should I play?)

Section 2.3 (offering to buy data) seems to be based on knowing the loss function of the predictions, which means that the correct answers are known.

In that case, what does it mean to bid on data?

If the answers are not known, how does one get marginal benefits from the given data point?

This seems to be a very fundamental dilemma.

There are some possible ways out of this, but they all seem to open the system up to gaming: - Use previous true samples to evaluate the value of new training data.

However, then one can offer a lot of training data to match any known previous samples, and obtain rewards by generating fake data to overfit the previous data. - Use some prediction deviation measure to evaluate the usefulness of new data (i.e., all the data that changes my predictions is good).

This clearly means that gaming can be done by generating crazy data to wildly derail predictions.

Thus, it seems that the only way to establish a cost function is in light of future true samples, and this seems to be a serious limitation because the future is far away.

This completely stops the "selling" plan in Section 2.3. The authors propose a family of market algorithms that expand prediction markets by rewarding incremental improvements in predictions from contributed data. This is indeed a very good idea, however, its practical application seems very limited because the market requires a known loss function, so if the purpose of the prediction is not known, there is no way to establish an exact incentive.
