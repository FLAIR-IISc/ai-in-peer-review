The authors have identified a strategy to compensate agents for revealing personal and relevant data. This strategy is based on interesting concepts from machine learning literature, including the use of stochastic gradient descent algorithms to determine the value of data. This helps to effectively compensate agents while maintaining a limited budget. The authors have identified various "profit maximization" strategies for selecting agents' data based on different processes to protect their privacy and their confidence in the data. It seems that the main contribution here is to identify a process for compensating agents for their data while maintaining a limited budget. The usefulness of this family of algorithms depends on timely and accurate information. If a "true sample" is available in the future, then section 2.3 will not be effective (see below), section 2.1 does not offer more benefits than the traditional betting market (without generalization of more complex prediction functions). Section 3 does not seem to solve a fundamental serious problem: depending on the type of privacy, there are easier solutions to prevent agent i from knowing about agent j: (a) anonymous participation (b) updates and anonymous relationships between agents (which agent has traded with is not available to other agents).

The truly useful contribution here is a generalization of the prediction market to use state-of-the-art prediction tools, which can extend the prediction market beyond betting on single binary or scalar variables.

This is great, but in reality, it seems to offer very little incentive to participate, unless the market is running with a true sample of the future, so that the reward can be quickly evaluated. (If I don't know what my data is bidding on, how much credit it can get, and when the reward will come, then why should I play?)

Section 2.3 (Data purchase offer) is based on knowing the loss function of the prediction, which means knowing the correct answers. In that case, what does it mean to bid on data? If the answers are not known, how will the marginal gain come from a specific data point?

This seems to be quite a fundamental dilemma. There are several possible ways to get out of this, but it seems that they all open the system up to gaming: - Use past true samples to evaluate the value of new training data. However, then someone can simply provide a lot of training data to match only known past samples and create fake data to overfit past data and get rewards. - Use some prediction divergence measures to evaluate the usefulness of new data (i.e., all data that changes my prediction is good).

Of course, this can be played with strange data to mislead the prediction. So, it seems that the only way to set up a cost function in light of a true sample of the future, and this is a serious limitation, because the future is far away. This completely negates the "selling" scheme of section 2.3. The authors have proposed a family of market algorithms that expand the prediction market by rewarding incremental improvements in predictions from contributing data. This is a really great idea, but its practical application seems very limited, because a known loss function is required for the market, so if the object of prediction is not known, there is no way to set up the correct incentive.
