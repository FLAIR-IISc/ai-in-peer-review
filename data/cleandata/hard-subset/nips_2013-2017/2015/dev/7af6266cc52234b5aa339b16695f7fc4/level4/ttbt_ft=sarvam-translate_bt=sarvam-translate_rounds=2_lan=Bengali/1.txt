The authors have identified strategies for compensating agents for revealing personal and relevant data. This strategy is based on the appealing concept of machine learning literature, which includes the use of stochastic gradient descent algorithms to determine the value of data. This helps to effectively compensate agents while maintaining a limited budget. The authors have identified various "profit maximization" strategies based on different processes for agents to select data, in order to protect their privacy and their trust in the data. The main contribution here seems to be identifying a process for compensating agents for data while maintaining a limited budget. The usefulness of this family of algorithms depends on timely and accurate information. If a "true sample" is obtained in the future, then section 2.3 will not be effective (see below), section 2.1 does not give more advantages than the traditional betting market (except for the generalization of more complex prediction functions). Section 3 does not seem to solve a fundamental serious problem: depending on the type of privacy, there is a simpler solution to prevent agent i from knowing about agent j: (a) anonymous participation (b) updates between agents and anonymous relationships (who agent has dealt with is not available to other agents).

The really useful contribution here is a generalization of the prediction market to use state-of-the-art prediction tools, which can expand the prediction market beyond betting on single binary or scalar variables.

That's great, but in reality, it seems to provide very little incentive to participate, unless the market is running with a true sample of the future, so that the reward can be quickly evaluated. (If I don't know what my data is bidding on, how much credit it can get, and when the reward will come, then why should I play?)

Section 2.3 (data purchase offer) is based on knowing the prediction loss function, which means knowing the correct answer. In that case, what does it mean to bid on data? If the answer is not known, how will the marginal profit come from a specific data point?

This seems to be quite a fundamental dilemma. There are several possible ways to get out of this, but it seems that they all open up the system for gaming: - Use past true samples to evaluate the value of new training data. However, then one can simply provide a lot of training data to match only with known past samples and overfit the past data and create fake data to get a reward. - Use some prediction divergence measures to evaluate the usefulness of new data (i.e., all data that changes my prediction is good).

Of course, this can be played with strange data to confuse the prediction. So, it seems that the only way to set up an expenditure function in light of a true sample of the future, and this is a serious limitation, because the future is very far away. This completely negates the "selling" scheme of section 2.3. The authors have proposed a family of market algorithms that expand the prediction market by rewarding incremental improvements in predictions from contributing data. This is really a great idea, but its practical application seems to be very limited, because a known loss function is required for the market, so if the object of prediction is not known, there is no way to set up the correct incentive.
