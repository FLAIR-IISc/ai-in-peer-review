In game theory, it can be shown that under certain well-behaved assumptions, the so-called price of anarchy of a complete information game is finite. The price of anarchy is the ratio of the optimal social welfare to the worst-case social welfare under some kind of equilibrium. The literature on this subject is quite extensive, and limits on the price of anarchy have been shown for a large class of coarse-connected equilibrium. However, in the world of incomplete information games, these results do not exist, and only limits on the price of anarchy of the Bayes-Nash equilibrium exist.

The main contribution of this paper is to provide a limit on the price of anarchy for coarse-connected equilibrium. This is done by reducing these games to many complete information games.

I think the main point of the paper comes from Lemma 10. The hypothesis of the Lemma is not entirely clear to me. First, they say that D^T is an empirical distribution of plays, and they say that if D^T converges to D in a sub-sequence distribution then... However, D^T is a random measure, so is the convergence a.s. in the distribution? Should the sub-sequence be the same for all values of D^T? Similar questions arise after taking the limit on equation (10). In a way, they are taking the limit of the form
E{s \sim D^T}[fT(s)]
And they claim that because fT \right arrow f a.s. and D^T \rightarrow D in the distribution (which again does not mean much), the above term will converge to E{s \sim D} [f(s)]. It is not entirely clear whether this will apply in general, and if so, the authors should provide a better explanation.

The rest of the paper seems correct, so I would consider accepting the paper if the authors could address my comments on Lemma 10. Still, I don't think the subject of the paper will be of much interest to the NIPS community.
