This paper attempts to demonstrate that the price of randomness obtained through the property of ease of games extends to the results on the quality of learning outcomes in Bayesian games. To achieve this, the authors interpret a Bayesian game as a stochastic game of complete information where there is a large population of potential participants. Once this assumption is made, two important results are shown: First, the outcome converges to a form of Bayesian correlated equilibrium when all players use a form of un-regret learning in the stochastic game under consideration. Second, the stochastic game under consideration is also smooth if the game or mechanism itself is smooth. These two results show that the price of randomness obtained through the ease technique extends to the results on the quality of learning outcomes in Bayesian games. This paper is very difficult to read and evaluate; it is more convenient for the "Game Theory" community than the "NIPS" community (as indicated by its references). Furthermore, it does not seem to have been written specifically for the NIPS conference; it is simply a "cut and paste" from an extended version. This is reflected in the final section where the authors are saying: "In this section, we analyze convergence..." and nothing is specified about this convergence. Some questions, comments, and suggestions: (a) Why does the additional material only provide proof for theorems 10 and 13? What about other theorems/lemmas? (b) Why is correlated equilibrium one of the Forges? (c) Theorem 11 is not clear; do you mean it converges to BCE? Please explain this in more detail. (d) It is difficult to manage selfish players, except when we add a central entity as is the case with correlated EQ. With this type of equilibrium, the problem is easier to solve (in general), but does this represent a realistic approach?
