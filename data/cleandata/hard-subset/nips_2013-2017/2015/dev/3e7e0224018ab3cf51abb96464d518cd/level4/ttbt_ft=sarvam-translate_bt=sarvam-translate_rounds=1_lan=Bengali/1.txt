This paper attempts to show that the value of randomness achieved through the smoothness property of a game extends to the quality of learning outcomes in Bayesian games. To achieve this, the authors interpret a Bayesian game as a stochastic game with complete information, where there is a large population of potential participants. Once this assumption is made, two important results are shown: Firstly, the result converges to a form of Bayesian Cores Correlated Equilibrium when all players use a form of no-regret learning in the considered stochastic game. Secondly, the considered stochastic game will also be smooth if the game or process itself is smooth. These two results show that the value of randomness obtained through the smoothness technique extends to the quality of learning outcomes in Bayesian games. This paper is very difficult to read and evaluate; it is more convenient for the "Game Theory" community than the "NIPS" community (as its references reflect). Furthermore, it does not seem to have been written specifically for the NIPS conference; it appears to be simply "cut and pasted" from an extended version. This is reflected in the final section where the authors state: "In this section, we analyze the convergence..." and nothing specific is stated about this convergence. Some questions, comments, and suggestions: (a) Why does the additional material only provide proof of theorems 10 and 13? What about other theorems/lemmas? (b) Why is the Cores Correlated Equilibrium a forgery? (c) Theorem 11 is not clear; do you mean it converges to BCCE? Please elaborate on this. (d) It is difficult to manage selfish players unless we add a central entity, such as through Cores Correlated EQ. With this type of equilibrium, the problem (in general) is easier to solve, but does it reflect a realistic view?
