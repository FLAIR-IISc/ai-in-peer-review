Game theory can show that under certain well-behaved assumptions, the so-called price of chaos in a complete information game is finite. The price of chaos is the ratio of the optimal social welfare under some kind of equilibrium to the social welfare in the worst-case scenario. The literature on this subject is quite extensive, and limits on the price of chaos have been shown for a large class of broadly connected equilibrium. However, in the world of incomplete information games, these results do not exist, and only limits on the price of chaos of the Bayes-Nash equilibrium exist.

The main contribution of this paper is to provide a limit on the price of chaos for broadly connected equilibrium. This is done by reducing these games to several complete information games.

This paper is indeed difficult to read and seems to lack generality. Even the setup was not entirely clear to me. What do the authors mean by "Fi is an empirical distribution of population Pi values," which is being used to talk about empirical distribution?

I think the main point of the paper comes from Lemma 10. The hypothesis of the Lemma is not entirely clear to me. First, they say that D^T is an empirical distribution of plays, and they say that if D^T converges to D in a sub-sequence distribution... However, D^T is a random measure, so is the convergence a.s. in the distribution? Should the sub-sequence be the same for all values of D^T? Similar questions arise after taking the limit on equation (10). In a way, they are taking the limit of the form
E{s \sim D^T}[fT(s)]
And they claim that because fT \right arrow f a.s. and D^T \rightarrow D in the distribution (which again does not mean much), the above term will converge to E{s \sim D} [f(s)]. It is not entirely clear whether this will apply in general, and if so, the authors should provide a better explanation.

The rest of the paper seems correct, so I would consider accepting the paper if the authors could address my comments on Lemma 10. Still, I don't think the topic of the paper will be of much interest to the NIPS community.
