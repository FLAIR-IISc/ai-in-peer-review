In game theory, one can show that under certain smooth assumptions, the so-called indifference value of a complete information game is bounded by the indifference value. The indifference value is the ratio of the optimal social welfare to the minimum social welfare achievable under a given type of equilibrium. The literature on this subject is quite extensive and broadly shows the limits of the indifference value for large classes of weakly connected equilibria. However, in the world of incomplete information games, these results do not exist, and only the indifference value limits of Bayes-Nash equilibria exist.

The main contribution of this paper is to provide a limit on the indifference value for broadly connected equilibria. The way to do this is to reduce these games to a few complete information games.

I think the main problem with this paper comes from Lemma 10. The assumptions in the lemma are not entirely clear to me. Firstly, they say that D^T is an empirical distribution of plays, and they say that if a sub-distribute of D^T converges to D in a distribution then... However, D^T is a random measure, so is convergence a.s. for all values of D^T? Does the sub-distribute have to be the same for all values of D^T? This same kind of question arises after taking the limit on equation (10). In one way, they are taking a limit of the form E{s \sim D^T}[fT(s)], and they are claiming that since fT \right arrow f a.s. and D^T \rightarrow D in the distribution (which again does not make much sense), the upper term will converge to E{s \sim D} [f(s)]. It is not entirely clear whether this will apply in general, and if it does, the authors should give a better explanation.

The rest of the paper seems correct, so I would consider accepting the paper if the authors could resolve my comments on Lemma 10. Still, I don't believe that the subject of the paper will be of much interest to the NIPS community.
