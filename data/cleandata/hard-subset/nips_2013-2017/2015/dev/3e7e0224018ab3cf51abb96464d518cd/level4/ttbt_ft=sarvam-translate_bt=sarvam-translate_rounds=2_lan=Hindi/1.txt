This paper attempts to demonstrate that the price of randomness obtained through the property of an easy game extends to the quality of learning outcomes in a Bayesian game. To achieve this, the authors interpret a Bayesian game as a stochastic game of complete information where there is a large population of potential participants. Once this assumption is made, two important results are shown: first, the outcome converges to a Bayesian equilibrium in the form of a balance when all players use an unpredictable form of learning in the stochastic game under consideration. Second, the stochastic game under consideration is also smooth if the game or mechanism itself is smooth. These two results show that the price of randomness obtained through the easy technique extends to the quality of learning outcomes in a Bayesian game. This paper is very difficult to read and evaluate; it is more convenient for the "Game Theory" community than the "NIPS" community (as indicated by its references). Furthermore, it does not seem to have been written specifically for the NIPS conference; it is simply a "cut and paste" from an extended version. This is reflected in the final section where the authors are saying: "In this section, we analyze convergence..." and nothing is specified about this convergence. Some questions, comments, and suggestions: (a) Why does the additional material only provide proof for theorems 10 and 13? What about other theorems/lemmas? (b) Why is the equilibrium of correlation one of the forgeries? (c) Theorem 11 is not clear; do you mean it converges to BCE? Please explain it in more detail. (d) It is difficult to manage selfish players, except when we add a central entity as is the case with the equilibrium of correlation. With this type of equilibrium, the problem is easier to solve (in general), but does it represent a realistic approach?
