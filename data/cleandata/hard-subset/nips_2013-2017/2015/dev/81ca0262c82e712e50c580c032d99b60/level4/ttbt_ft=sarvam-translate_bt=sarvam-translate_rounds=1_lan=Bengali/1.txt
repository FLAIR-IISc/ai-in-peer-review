Summary

This research paper presents a new approach to path integral control. The proposed method is based on the fact that the system control matrix G is often known and that an uncontrolled dynamics (or dynamics under a reference controller) can be learned using a Gaussian process. The reward function's constraint takes on a more general form compared to the previous PI method, which means that noise and control can operate in various sub-spaces, among other things. The authors also show how their framework can be used to generalize from known work to new work and evaluate the method on three simulated robotics problems, where their method is more favorable than SOTA reinforcement learning and control methods.

Quality
In general, the derivations seem correct and principled (although I am not sure about task generalization, see below).
Related work is adequately discussed, the authors mention similarities and differences with related work, and compare it with state-of-the-art methods. The experiments are convincing, although some details are missing (see below).
Task generalization seems odd: (13) states that the reward function's index will linearly combine to create the reward function's index for the new task. However, it is not clear to me how this turns the value function's index into a similar linear combination (14). However, it should be explained, but I think there may actually be an error here ((14) also states that psi is the index of the cost function, which is only applicable for the last time step as shown in the equation around line 247).
As defined by the authors, task generalization is specific to the case where the task is defined by a target, although it seems that other task variations are also possible if a suitable task similarity kernel can be defined.
The experiments consider relevant and realistic tasks and compare them with SOTA methods. The technical details in the experimental section are somewhat vague: the paper should mention what the dynamics and reward function are, and how long the trajectory is sampled. (Possibly in the appendix). A baseline is repeated PI control, is it one of the methods in Table 1? It would be insightful to see how it compares on these qualitative aspects.
The comparison plots in Figures 1 and 2 should show error bars. The reported score is the value fc, the index of psi.
Is this psi calculated by the robot? What if the robot is overly optimistic in calculating psi? Reporting the average (or increasing (pruned)) reward would be more objective (which determines whether to optimize the algorithm).

Clarity
In general, the paper is well-written and explains the proposed methods quite well. Compared to other methods, such as Table 1, it helps to understand the relationship with SOTA. There are some grammatical and formatting errors here, see below under minor points. A confusing point is that there are different regression targets in GP-s (2) (line 094) and line 208, which do not include the reference control. This should be consistent or explained. The authors should explain how the hyperparameters of the GP are set. What does "posteriour distribution can be obtained by limiting the joint distribution" mean - is conditioning meant?
Algorithm 1 is confusing - here, it looks like an open-loop control sequence is being optimized, while the rest of the paper discusses learning a feedback controller.
If it is really an open-loop sequence u1 ... uT that returns in line 13 of the algorithm, how can the algorithm deal with noise? This should be clarified or corrected.

Basics and Significance
Although the main components of the proposed algorithm have been used in previous algorithms (such as spread through a learned stochastic forward model [15], path integral control), the algorithm combines them in a new way. As far as I know, it is a fundamental formula that seems to achieve very good performance. Compared to strong benchmarks, the proposed algorithm can be considered a significant improvement over previous work, given its evaluation on realistic problems.
