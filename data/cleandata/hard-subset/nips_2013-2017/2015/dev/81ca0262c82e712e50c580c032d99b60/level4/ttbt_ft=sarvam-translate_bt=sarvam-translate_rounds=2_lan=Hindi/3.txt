This paper presents a new PI-related method for Stochastic Optimal Control. The main idea is to use a Gaussian process to model the trajectories obtained as a result of applying an unconstrained dynamics to a proposed control $u^old$. The proposed Bellman equation is defined to be optimized for the control update $\delta u$ at each iteration. This method leverages analytical methods for GP estimation to efficiently update the GP parameter and has been experimentally evaluated in three simulated tasks.

The idea is interesting, and the authors have a working method that could compete with existing methods. I generally like the paper, but I think the following points need to be addressed:

1. The problem formulation section is a bit confusing, as it includes two optimizations: one to find an optimal control over $u$, and the other that optimizes $\delta u$. From 2.1, it seems that the optimal $u$ is given with a proposed control $u^old$, which is clearly not the case, as finding the optimal $u$ is precisely the main task. The authors need to clarify this point.

2. The organization of the paper could be much better: since the proposed approach is not presented in 3.1, but rather before it, I would move sub-section 3.1 after sub-section 2.1. This would also improve readability, as equation (9) includes the estimation step explained in sub-section 3.1.

3. I also found section 3.1 confusing, which defines the GP for an unconstrained process (u=0) (line 208), contradicting the definition in equation (2) that depends on the current proposed control. Furthermore, if the GP is initialized using sample data from an unconstrained process, the authors should also clarify how the poor conditioning of the unconstrained process in terms of effective sample size affects this initialization.

4. Regarding the method for updating the GP model, it seems that the formulation ignores the cost of the current control sequence, see equation (5). This term enters the equation as a Radon-Nikodim derivative [16,17] in the iterative PI control. Can the authors point to a corresponding term in their formulation? Can we still talk about a generalization of the PI control, or is this another policy search, a PI-related method?

5. The use of confidence spread for forward estimation can fail when the dynamics around the reference suddenly change, i.e., they are non-Gaussian. This occurs in the presence of constraints, for example, which does not occur in the presented experiments. The authors should clarify whether this is a potential problem or not.

6. Regarding the notation, although the authors present the method as model-based, it is not clear whether a model of the dynamics is used (they only have a probability distribution over the trajectories through the GP). I suggest separate notation for clarity.

7. The authors do a good job of relating their approach to existing PI-related methods, which is a difficult task given the (already large) current literature on PI control. In this regard, I have the following comments:

- The constraint is not ignored in PI^2 [8], but PI^2 is an open-loop policy search method, so the constraint is meaningless and the same argument of PI^2-CMA applies.
- The response PI [17] uses a parameterized response term (second-order correction), but not a parameterized policy.
- PI-REPS [11] is model-based (although the model can be learned, as in guided policy search [23]).
- Iterative PI [16] is the original PI with significant samples and therefore: model-based, with the same structural constraint as PI and without a parameterized policy.

These comments include not only Table 1, but the text is spread throughout the paper.

The paper presents a new PI-related method for Stochastic Optimal Control that uses a Gaussian process to model trajectories. The approach is promising, but it is not clear whether it is a generalization of PI control or whether it is a policy search, a PI-related method. The presentation should be improved and some other points need to be clarified.
