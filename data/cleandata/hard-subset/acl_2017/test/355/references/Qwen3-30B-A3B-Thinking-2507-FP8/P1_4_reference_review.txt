

# Peer Review for ACL

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes neural models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenging problem of zero pronoun resolution (where arguments are omitted in Japanese sentences). The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that captures interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The key contribution is demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic parsers, which previous approaches heavily depended on and suffered from error propagation.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1. A neural architecture (Grid-RNN) that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic information.
2. Empirical demonstration that neural models can outperform previous state-of-the-art approaches, particularly for zero argument identification (a major challenge in Japanese PAS).
3. A compelling argument that context in the entire sentence (captured by RNNs) is beneficial for zero argument resolution, which previous approaches didn't fully leverage.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Performance Improvement on a Challenging Task**: The paper demonstrates clear and statistically significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art models, particularly for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 44.09% F1). This is a substantial gain (3 points) for a task that has been notoriously difficult in Japanese PAS analysis. The authors provide strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of their approach, which is the most compelling aspect of the paper.

2. **Novel Application of Grid-RNN Architecture**: While Grid-RNNs have been used in other contexts (Graves and Schmidhuber, 2009; Kalchbrenner et al., 2016), their application to Japanese PAS analysis for capturing multi-predicate interactions is novel and well-motivated. The paper effectively demonstrates how this architecture can model the semantic relationships between predicates without relying on syntactic information, which is a significant contribution to the field.

3. **Strong Experimental Design and Evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments, including ablation studies on network depth, residual connections, and detailed analysis per case role. The comparison with multiple baseline models on the same dataset (NAIST Text Corpus 1.5) is fair and provides clear evidence of the model's superiority. The results for dative arguments (which appear infrequently) are particularly impressive, showing the robustness of their approach.

4. **Clear Demonstration of the Value of Context**: The paper effectively shows that modeling context in the entire sentence (using RNNs) is beneficial for zero argument identification, which has been a longstanding challenge. The authors provide evidence that their models can capture long-distance dependencies necessary for resolving zero pronouns.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Architecture**: While the application to Japanese PAS is novel, the Grid-RNN architecture itself is not new. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain how their specific implementation differs from or improves upon the original Grid-RNN work (Graves and Schmidhuber, 2009; Kalchbrenner et al., 2016). It's unclear what specific adaptations were made for the Japanese PAS task, which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches**: The paper mentions previous neural approaches to Japanese PAS (Shibata et al., 2016; Iida et al., 2016) but doesn't provide a clear comparison showing how their model differs from or improves upon these. It's unclear whether their Grid-RNN approach is fundamentally different from or better than these prior neural approaches, which weakens the significance of their contribution.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Why the Model Works**: While the paper shows the model performs better, it lacks qualitative analysis of *how* the model captures multi-predicate interactions. Providing specific examples where the multi-sequence model correctly resolves zero pronouns by leveraging interactions between predicates would greatly strengthen the paper's contribution.

4. **No Computational Efficiency Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity or training time of their model compared to previous approaches. Given the increased complexity of Grid-RNNs, it's important to know if the performance gain is worth the additional computational cost, especially for practical applications.

5. **Limited Context in Broader NLP Literature**: The paper focuses solely on Japanese PAS analysis but doesn't situate their work within the broader context of semantic role labeling (SRL) in other languages. A comparison to recent English SRL models that use neural networks (e.g., Zhou and Xu, 2015) would provide more context for the significance of their contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your Grid-RNN model specifically differ from the Grid-LSTM model proposed by Kalchbrenner et al. (2016)? What adaptations did you make for the Japanese PAS analysis task?

2. Could you provide a comparison of your model's performance with the neural approaches of Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016) when they are also provided with syntactic information? Would the performance gains be similar or different?

3. Could you provide specific qualitative examples showing how your multi-sequence model correctly resolves zero pronouns by leveraging multi-predicate interactions, particularly in cases where the single-sequence model fails?

4. What is the computational complexity and training time of your model compared to previous state-of-the-art approaches? Is the performance gain worth the additional computational cost?

5. The paper shows that the multi-sequence model improves for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 46.10%) but shows minimal improvement for direct dependency arguments (88.17% vs. 88.10%). Why do you think multi-predicate interactions are particularly beneficial for zero arguments but less so for Dep arguments?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written paper that makes a significant contribution to Japanese PAS analysis by demonstrating that neural models can outperform previous state-of-the-art approaches without relying on syntactic parsers. The empirical results are strong and the approach is well-motivated. While the novelty of the Grid-RNN architecture itself is limited, its application to this specific problem is novel and impactful. With the authors addressing the weaknesses mentioned above (particularly the lack of comparison to recent neural approaches and insufficient analysis of why the model works), this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
