

# Peer Review: Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenging problem of zero pronoun resolution (omitted arguments). The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that explicitly models interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The key contribution is demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic parsers, thereby avoiding error propagation in pipeline approaches.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Demonstration that neural models can outperform syntactic-dependent approaches** for Japanese PAS analysis without requiring syntactic information.
2. **Grid-RNN architecture for modeling multi-predicate interactions** that provides statistically significant improvements for zero argument resolution.
3. **Thorough analysis of performance by case role**, showing particular effectiveness for the challenging dative case and zero arguments.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful problem with practical impact**: Japanese PAS analysis is hampered by argument omission (zero pronouns), which is particularly challenging for zero arguments lacking syntactic dependencies. Previous state-of-the-art approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015) relied on syntactic parsers, introducing error propagation. The authors successfully demonstrate that neural models can achieve better results without syntactic information, which is a significant practical contribution to the field.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments with statistically significant results (p<0.05). The multi-sequence model achieves 81.42% F1 compared to Ouchi et al.'s 79.23%, with particularly notable improvements for zero arguments (47.12% vs 44.09%). The analysis by case role (Table 3) demonstrates the model's particular effectiveness for the dative case (32.35% vs 4.80% in Ouchi et al.), which is challenging due to its low frequency.

3. **Well-motivated Grid-RNN architecture**: The paper provides a clear explanation of how Grid-RNNs enable inter-sequence connections between multiple predicate sequences, capturing multi-predicate interactions. The residual connections implementation is well-justified and improves performance for deeper networks, which is a valuable technical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty**: While the authors claim their Grid-RNN approach for multi-predicate interactions is novel, Grid-RNNs were first introduced by Graves and Schmidhuber (2009) for image captioning, and Kalchbrenner et al. (2016) applied them to text. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their use of Grid-RNNs for Japanese PAS from previous applications. The core innovation is applying an existing architecture to a new problem domain, but the novelty claim isn't adequately qualified.

2. **Modest performance gains**: The absolute improvement over the state-of-the-art (0.27% F1) is modest despite statistical significance. The single-sequence model already outperforms Ouchi et al. (2015) by 2.23% on zero arguments. This raises questions about whether the Grid-RNN architecture specifically provides the gains, or whether simply using a neural model (rather than feature-based) is the main contributor.

3. **Lack of comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper cites Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016) as neural approaches but doesn't compare directly with them. These approaches also use neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis. A comparison with these methods would better establish the relative contribution of the Grid-RNN architecture.

4. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't address how the model performs on sentences with long-distance dependencies or multiple zero pronouns. It's unclear how the Grid-RNN handles cases where multiple predicates could share the same argument, which is common in Japanese. The authors should discuss these limitations.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your Grid-RNN model specifically differ from the neural approach of Shibata et al. (2016), which also used a neural network for Japanese PAS analysis? Can you provide a more direct comparison with their method?

2. Given that the single-sequence model (which doesn't use Grid-RNNs) already achieves significant improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015), what specific advantages does the Grid-RNN architecture provide beyond the general neural approach? Could the improvements be explained by deeper networks or residual connections alone?

3. The paper mentions that the neural models learn long-distance dependencies, but doesn't provide evidence of this. Could you provide examples of sentences where the multi-sequence model correctly resolves zero pronouns that are far apart in the sentence?

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid contribution that demonstrates the viability of neural models for Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic information. The Grid-RNN approach for modeling multi-predicate interactions is well-motivated and provides statistically significant improvements, particularly for the challenging zero argument resolution task. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the performance gains, while significant, are modest. The paper would be strengthened by a more careful comparison with recent neural approaches and a clearer discussion of the specific contributions of the Grid-RNN architecture versus general neural modeling.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to Japanese NLP by showing that error propagation from syntactic parsers can be avoided with neural models, which is an important practical advancement. The results on zero argument resolution are particularly compelling for the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the novelty claims and provide clearer comparisons with recent neural approaches.
