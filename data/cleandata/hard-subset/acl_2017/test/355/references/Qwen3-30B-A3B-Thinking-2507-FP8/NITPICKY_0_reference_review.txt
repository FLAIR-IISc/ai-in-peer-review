

# Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically focusing on capturing multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information. The authors introduce a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs and extend it to a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. They evaluate their models on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 and claim state-of-the-art results, particularly for zero argument identification.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural model that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information without requiring syntactic parsing
2. A Grid-RNN based multi-sequence model that captures interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence
3. Demonstration that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results for Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic information

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in Japanese PAS analysis (zero argument identification), which is particularly challenging due to the pro-drop nature of Japanese. The authors demonstrate that their neural approach effectively captures contextual information for zero argument identification, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. The authors provide a clear comparison with previous state-of-the-art approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015), showing consistent improvements across multiple metrics, especially for zero arguments. The results for dative case (DAT) are particularly noteworthy, with the neural models achieving around 30-point improvement over previous work.

3. The paper makes a strong case for the necessity of capturing multi-predicate interactions, which is well-supported by the experimental results showing significant improvements for zero arguments (47.12% vs 46.10% for single-sequence model).

## Weaknesses

1. **Methodological inconsistency regarding "no syntactic information" claim**: The paper claims to "automatically induce features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information" without syntactic information, but Section 3.1 describes a "PRED" feature that requires "word index of the target predicate and words around the predicate." This implicitly requires identifying the predicate position in the sentence, which likely requires some form of syntactic or semantic analysis. The authors do not explain how they identify predicates without syntactic information, contradicting their main claim. This is a fundamental methodological flaw that undermines the paper's core contribution.

2. **Critical typographical and formatting errors**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that suggest a lack of careful proofreading:
   - "ap-proach" should be "approach" (line 5 of abstract)
   - "GridRNNs" should be "Grid-RNNs" (line 14 of abstract)
   - In Figure 2 caption, "multi-sequence" is written as "multi-sequence" (inconsistency with the rest of the paper)
   - In Section 4.2, the equation has unbalanced parentheses: "h (ℓ) m,t= { g(ℓ)(h (ℓ−1) m,t ⊕ h (ℓ) m−1,t,h (ℓ) m,t−1) (ℓ = odd)" (missing closing parenthesis)
   - In Table 1, the significance mark † is not explained in the table itself (only mentioned in the text)

3. **Insufficient experimental details for reproducibility**:
   - The paper claims "standard data splits" but doesn't provide the exact train/dev/test splits, making it difficult to reproduce their experiments.
   - The paper states "We do not use any external resources" but doesn't clarify what constitutes "external resources" (e.g., word embeddings, pre-trained models).
   - The paper doesn't explain how they handle out-of-vocabulary words or word segmentation in Japanese, which is critical for Japanese NLP.

4. **Overstated significance of small improvements**: The paper claims that the multi-sequence model "significantly outperformed" the single-sequence model with F-measure of 81.42% vs 81.15% (a difference of only 0.27%). This small improvement is not statistically significant in most contexts, and the paper doesn't provide enough detail on the statistical tests to justify the claim of significance. The paper also doesn't discuss the practical significance of this small improvement.

5. **Inconsistent corpus version usage**: The paper uses NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 for evaluation but compares with results from NAIST Text Corpus 1.4β in Table 3. The paper doesn't explain why different corpus versions are used for comparison, making the comparison potentially invalid. The differences between versions (particularly the revision of dative case annotation) significantly impact results, as noted in the paper itself.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you identify target predicates in the sentence without using syntactic information? Your "PRED" feature requires knowing the position of the predicate relative to other words, which implies you need some form of predicate identification, likely requiring syntactic or semantic analysis.

2. You claim your model uses "word sequence information" without syntactic information, but your PRED feature explicitly requires knowledge of the predicate's position. How do you handle cases where the predicate is omitted (zero predicate), as is common in Japanese?

3. The paper states "We do not use any external resources," but word embeddings are typically considered external resources. Do you consider word embeddings as external resources? If not, how do you handle unknown words, and how does this affect your model's performance?

4. The improvement from single-sequence to multi-sequence model is only 0.27% in F-measure. Given that this is a small improvement, why is it considered significant? Can you provide more details on the statistical tests you performed to claim significance?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in Japanese PAS analysis and demonstrates promising results, the methodological inconsistencies (particularly the contradiction between the claim of "no syntactic information" and the use of a predicate position-based feature), critical typographical errors, and insufficient experimental details for reproducibility are substantial flaws that undermine the paper's credibility.

The most fundamental issue is the contradiction between the paper's main claim ("without syntactic information") and the technical details provided (the PRED feature requires knowing the predicate position). This is a critical methodological flaw that cannot be overlooked, as it directly challenges the paper's core contribution. The other issues (formatting errors, insufficient experimental details, overstated significance of small improvements) further compound this problem and suggest a lack of attention to detail that is expected in ACL publications.

Even though the paper demonstrates state-of-the-art results for Japanese PAS analysis, the methodological inconsistencies and lack of clarity regarding the "no syntactic information" claim are too substantial to overlook. These issues significantly reduce the paper's scientific contribution and reproducibility.
