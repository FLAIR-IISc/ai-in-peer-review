

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on modeling multi-predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs. The authors claim that their models achieve state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic information from parsers, which is a common limitation in previous approaches. They demonstrate significant improvements, especially for zero argument identification (omitted arguments), which is a challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. Novel application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis, avoiding error propagation from syntactic parsers
2. Demonstration that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information
3. Significant improvements in zero argument identification (3.0 points F-measure over previous state-of-the-art)

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application of Grid-RNNs for Multi-Predicate Interactions**: The core contribution of applying Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis is genuinely novel. While Grid-RNNs have been used in other contexts, this is a fresh application to a specific problem in Japanese NLP that has not been addressed before. The paper correctly identifies that existing approaches suffer from error propagation due to their reliance on syntactic parsers, and this neural approach cleanly avoids that limitation. This is a bold approach that directly addresses a known problem in the field.

2. **Significant Improvement for Zero Arguments**: The paper demonstrates a particularly strong result for zero argument identification (47.12% F-measure vs. 44.09% for the previous state-of-the-art), which is one of the most challenging aspects of Japanese PAS analysis. The paper correctly identifies that zero arguments lack direct syntactic dependencies, making them difficult to identify, and shows that multi-predicate interactions are particularly beneficial for this task. This is a significant contribution to a long-standing problem in the field.

3. **State-of-the-Art Results Without Syntactic Information**: The paper successfully demonstrates that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic information, which is a major claim. Previous approaches heavily relied on syntactic information from parsers, which introduces error propagation. The fact that the authors' neural models can automatically induce effective features from word sequences alone is a significant advancement.

4. **Clear Evidence of Multi-Predicate Interactions' Benefits**: The paper provides strong evidence that the multi-sequence model (with Grid-RNNs) outperforms the single-sequence model, especially for zero arguments (47.12% vs 46.10% F-measure), which directly supports the claim that multi-predicate interactions are beneficial for PAS analysis. This is a compelling demonstration of the value of the proposed approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches**: The paper compares to older baseline models (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015) but fails to compare with more recent neural approaches for Japanese PAS analysis, such as Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016), which are mentioned in the related work but not directly compared. This is a significant weakness because the paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results, yet doesn't show how it compares to the most recent neural approaches.

2. **Relatively Small Overall Improvement**: While the improvement for zero arguments is significant (3.0 points), the overall F-measure improvement is relatively small (81.42% vs 79.23% for Ouchi et al., 2015). In the context of NLP, such a small improvement might not be considered groundbreaking, especially without a thorough comparison to other recent neural approaches.

3. **Lack of Analysis of Grid-RNN Advantages**: The paper describes the Grid-RNN architecture but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why it works better than other neural architectures for this task. For example, it doesn't compare Grid-RNNs to Transformers, which are now standard for capturing long-range dependencies, or provide an ablation study of different components of the Grid-RNN architecture.

4. **No Error Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any error analysis to understand where the model is making mistakes. This would be valuable to understand the limitations of the approach and guide future work, especially for the challenging zero argument identification task.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your model compare to Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016), who also used neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis? The paper mentions these works but doesn't provide a direct comparison.

2. The paper claims that the neural models achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information. Can you provide more details about how your model compares to other neural approaches that might have used some syntactic information?

3. The paper mentions that Grid-RNNs are used to capture multi-predicate interactions. Can you provide more insight into why Grid-RNNs are particularly well-suited for this task compared to other neural architectures?

4. The improvement in overall F-measure is relatively small (about 2 points). Given this, why do you believe this work represents a significant step forward rather than an incremental improvement?

## Overall Recommendation
Accept

This paper introduces a novel application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis, which directly addresses a known limitation in the field (error propagation from syntactic parsers). The results for zero argument identification are particularly strong and significant, demonstrating a 3.0-point improvement over the previous state-of-the-art. While the overall F-measure improvement is modest, the paper's novel approach and its successful application to a challenging problem make it a valuable contribution to the field. The minor methodological weaknesses (lack of comparison to recent neural approaches, small overall improvement) are outweighed by the novelty and significance of the core idea for a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological perfection.
