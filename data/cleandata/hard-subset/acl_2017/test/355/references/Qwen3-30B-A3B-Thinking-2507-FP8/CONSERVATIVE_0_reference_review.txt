

# Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

## Summary of Contributions (as I see them)

1. The paper demonstrates that neural sequence models can achieve state-of-the-art results on Japanese PAS analysis without relying on syntactic information, particularly for zero argument identification (which has been a long-standing challenge).
2. The paper shows that considering multi-predicate interactions through a Grid-RNN architecture provides a small but statistically significant improvement over single-sequence models for zero argument identification.
3. The paper provides thorough experimental validation on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 dataset with rigorous comparison to previous state-of-the-art approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Rigorous empirical validation on standard dataset**: The paper provides a thorough comparison with previous state-of-the-art approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015) on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 dataset, with proper statistical significance testing. The results demonstrate meaningful improvements for zero argument identification (3-point F-measure improvement), which is the most challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis. This is a significant contribution as it shows neural approaches can effectively handle the zero pronoun problem without syntactic dependency parsing.

2. **Practical advantage of avoiding syntactic pipeline**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their neural approach achieves better results without requiring syntactic parsing, which avoids error propagation issues common in pipeline approaches. This is a practical contribution that addresses a real limitation in previous Japanese PAS systems.

3. **Comprehensive ablation study**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of different model configurations (network depth, residual connections, single vs. multi-sequence models) and their impact on performance. This careful analysis strengthens the claims about what contributes to the improved performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty in the core architecture**: The paper's main contribution is a "Grid-RNN" architecture for capturing multi-predicate interactions. However, Grid-RNNs have been previously used in other NLP tasks (Graves and Schmidhuber, 2009; Kalchbrenner et al., 2016), and the paper fails to clearly demonstrate how their specific application to Japanese PAS is novel or significantly different from prior work. The authors cite these papers but don't adequately explain their specific innovations, making the claimed contribution seem incremental rather than novel.

2. **Inadequate comparison with relevant neural approaches**: The paper claims to achieve "state-of-the-art" results without syntactic information, but fails to compare with other neural approaches that also work without syntactic information (e.g., Zhou and Xu, 2015 for English SRL). The authors mention these in related work but don't actually compare with them, making it impossible to assess whether their approach is truly better than existing neural methods that also avoid syntactic parsing.

3. **Misleading claim about "without syntactic information"**: The authors claim their model "automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information" without syntactic information, but their input features (word indices, predicate positions, etc.) are essentially linguistic features that encode syntactic information. This is a common misconception in neural NLP papers that undermines the significance of their claimed contribution.

4. **Lack of qualitative analysis to explain success**: The paper shows quantitative improvements but provides no qualitative analysis to explain *why* the multi-sequence model works better. Without examples showing specific cases where the multi-sequence model successfully resolves a zero pronoun that the single-sequence model missed, it's difficult to be convinced that the Grid-RNN architecture is genuinely capturing meaningful multi-predicate interactions.

5. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison with simpler alternatives that might achieve similar results. For example, they don't compare with an attention-based model that could capture interactions between predicates without the complexity of a grid structure, or with a single-sequence model that processes all predicates in sequence rather than using a grid. This weakens their claim that the Grid-RNN architecture is specifically beneficial.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your Grid-RNN architecture differ from previous uses in Graves and Schmidhuber (2009) and Kalchbrenner et al. (2016)? What specific innovations do you introduce that make your application to Japanese PAS analysis novel?

2. Why didn't you compare your approach with Zhou and Xu (2015), which also achieved state-of-the-art results for English SRL without syntactic information? How would your results compare if you applied their approach to Japanese PAS?

3. Your input features (word indices, predicate positions, etc.) are essentially linguistic features that encode syntactic information. How do you respond to the criticism that your model is not truly "without syntactic information," and what would be the impact of using purely word sequence information (e.g., only word embeddings without any positional or syntactic features)?

4. Can you provide specific examples where your multi-sequence model successfully resolves a zero pronoun that the single-sequence model failed to resolve? What kind of multi-predicate interactions does the model appear to be capturing in these examples?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-executed empirical study that demonstrates neural approaches can achieve state-of-the-art results for Japanese PAS analysis, particularly for zero argument identification. However, the claimed novelty of the Grid-RNN architecture is insufficiently justified, and the paper fails to adequately compare with relevant neural approaches. The misleading claim about "without syntactic information" further undermines the contribution.

I recommend **Rejection** for the following reasons:
1. The core methodological contribution lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing Grid-RNN work.
2. The paper fails to provide adequate comparison with other neural approaches that work without syntactic information.
3. The claim of "without syntactic information" is misleading and weakens the significance of the contribution.
4. The paper lacks sufficient qualitative analysis to explain why the Grid-RNN approach works better.

While the empirical results are solid and the problem being addressed is important, the paper fails to make a convincing case for the novelty and significance of the proposed approach. The contribution would be much stronger if the authors had demonstrated the novelty of their Grid-RNN architecture more clearly and had compared their approach with other neural methods that work without syntactic information.
