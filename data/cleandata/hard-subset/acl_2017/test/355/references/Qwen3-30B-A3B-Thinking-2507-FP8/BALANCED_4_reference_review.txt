

# Review for ACL Conference

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a specific focus on addressing the challenging problem of zero argument identification (arguments that are omitted in the sentence). The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model that processes each predicate independently using RNNs, and a multi-sequence model that extends this approach using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. 

My assessment of the main contributions (ordered by strength):

1. **Novel neural approach to Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic parsing**: The paper presents a significant methodological shift from previous state-of-the-art approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015; Shibata et al., 2016) that heavily relied on syntactic information from parsers. The authors demonstrate that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance without this error-prone pipeline component.

2. **Grid-RNN architecture for multi-predicate interactions**: The paper introduces a grid-type neural architecture that explicitly models interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence, which is particularly beneficial for identifying zero arguments (which lack direct syntactic dependencies).

3. **Empirical demonstration of significant improvements for zero argument identification**: The experiments show that the proposed models, particularly the multi-sequence model, achieve substantial improvements (up to 3.0 points F-measure) over previous state-of-the-art for zero argument identification, which has been a persistent challenge in Japanese PAS analysis.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to a well-established problem**: The paper makes a significant methodological contribution by addressing a longstanding challenge in Japanese PAS analysis (the zero argument problem) through a neural approach that avoids reliance on syntactic parsing. Previous approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015; Shibata et al., 2016) suffered from error propagation due to their dependency on syntactic parsers, while this paper demonstrates that high-quality results can be achieved directly from word sequences. The authors provide compelling evidence through their results (Table 1), where both neural models outperform the previous state-of-the-art, with the multi-sequence model achieving statistically significant improvements for zero arguments (47.12% vs 44.09% F-measure).

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough and well-structured evaluation on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 dataset, with appropriate baselines (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015). The use of bootstrap resampling for statistical significance testing is appropriate, and the detailed breakdown by case role (Table 3) reveals particularly strong improvements for the dative case (DAT), which has been historically challenging due to infrequent usage and annotation complexities. The paper also demonstrates the effectiveness of residual connections and network depth (Table 2), providing valuable insights for model design.

3. **Clear theoretical grounding and linguistic motivation**: The paper is well-grounded in linguistic theory, specifically the observation that predicates in a sentence are semantically related and that multi-predicate interactions can serve as a clue for PAS analysis. This is supported by previous work (Ouchi et al., 2015; Yang and Zong, 2014), and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their Grid-RNN architecture captures these interactions (Figure 6). The distinction between Dep, Zero, and Inter-Zero arguments (Section 2.1) is well-explained and sets up the problem clearly.

4. **Effective model architecture design**: The Grid-RNN architecture is a clever adaptation of existing neural network techniques to the specific needs of Japanese PAS analysis. The authors clearly explain the architecture (Figure 6) and demonstrate how it extends the single-sequence model to capture multi-predicate interactions. The use of residual connections (Section 4.2) is particularly well-justified and contributes to the model's performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inadequate comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information, but fails to compare with Iida et al. (2016), which also used neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis (specifically for zero anaphora resolution). This is a significant omission, as Iida et al. (2016) already demonstrated the effectiveness of neural networks for this task. The paper should either include this as a baseline or provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from and improves upon Iida et al. (2016).

2. **Lack of qualitative analysis and model interpretation**: While the paper provides strong quantitative results, it lacks any qualitative analysis to understand *why* the neural models work better. The authors don't provide examples of sentences where the multi-sequence model successfully identifies zero arguments that the single-sequence model misses, or where the Grid-RNN architecture makes a specific difference. This would greatly enhance the paper's contribution by providing insights into the linguistic patterns captured by the model.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations and failure cases**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For example:
   - How does the model handle sentences with many predicates?
   - What is the performance on long sentences or sentences with complex zero anaphora?
   - How does the model perform on sentences where the zero pronoun refers to an antecedent outside the immediate sentence (Inter-Zero arguments, which the authors mention but don't analyze)?
   - What are the specific failure cases, and why do they occur?

4. **Limited exploration of the Grid-RNN architecture's contribution**: The paper claims that the Grid-RNN architecture is key to capturing multi-predicate interactions, but doesn't provide an ablation study to isolate the contribution of the grid connections. It's possible that using multiple sequences without the grid connections (i.e., processing each predicate's sequence independently) would have been sufficient, or that the grid connections are less important than the authors suggest. An ablation study would strengthen the paper's claims.

5. **Lack of discussion about scalability and computational efficiency**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational requirements of the proposed models compared to previous approaches. Given that the Grid-RNN architecture processes multiple sequences in parallel, it may have different computational characteristics than the previous pipeline-based approaches. This information would be valuable for practitioners.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information, but doesn't compare with Iida et al. (2016), which also used neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis. How does your approach differ from Iida et al. (2016), and why does your model achieve better results despite using similar neural techniques?

2. In Table 3, your model achieves significantly higher F-measures for the dative case (DAT) compared to previous work. However, the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 has revised annotation criteria for DAT (as noted in the paper). Could you clarify how your model's performance on DAT compares to the previous work that used the same corpus (NAIST Text Corpus 1.5), rather than comparing with work that used an older corpus version?

3. The paper mentions that the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model for zero arguments (47.12% vs 46.10%). Could you provide a few qualitative examples showing specific sentences where the multi-sequence model correctly identifies a zero argument that the single-sequence model misses, along with a brief explanation of why the multi-predicate interactions were helpful in these cases?

4. The paper states that the model parameters were learned without external resources. Given the success of transfer learning in NLP, how do you think incorporating external resources (e.g., large unlabeled corpora, multilingual resources) could further improve the model's performance, and what would be the most promising direction for future work?

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that high-quality Japanese PAS analysis can be achieved without syntactic parsing, which addresses a key limitation of previous approaches. The experimental results are strong, and the proposed Grid-RNN architecture is a thoughtful adaptation of neural techniques to the specific needs of Japanese PAS analysis.

However, the paper has several important weaknesses that need to be addressed before publication. The most critical issue is the omission of comparison with Iida et al. (2016), which is a recent neural approach to the same task. The lack of qualitative analysis and limited discussion of limitations also reduce the paper's impact.

With revisions addressing these concerns (particularly the comparison with Iida et al., 2016, and the addition of qualitative examples), this paper would be a strong candidate for ACL. The core idea is valuable, and the experimental results are solid, but the paper would be significantly strengthened with more thorough comparisons and analysis.
