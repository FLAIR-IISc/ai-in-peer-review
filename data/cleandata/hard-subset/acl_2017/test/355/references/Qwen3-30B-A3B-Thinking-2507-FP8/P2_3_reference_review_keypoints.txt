- Proposes two neural models (single-sequence bidirectional RNN and multi-sequence Grid-RNN) for Japanese PAS zero-pronoun identification without syntactic parsers.  
- Claims automatic feature induction sensitive to multi-predicate interactions.  
- Reports state-of-the-art results on NAIST Text Corpus for zero argument identification (47.12% F1).

- Correctly targets zero pronoun identification as a key challenge in Japanese PAS and motivates multi-predicate interactions linguistically.  
- Achieves statistically significant empirical improvements, notably for zero argument identification.  
- Ablation study shows modeling predicate interactions materially improves performance.  
- Thorough comparison with prior parser-based SOTA (Ouchi et al., 2015) under identical experimental settings.

- Fails to compare against more recent neural approaches (e.g., Iida et al., 2016), making the "state-of-the-art" claim questionable.  
- Grid-RNN novelty is limited since similar architectures exist (Kalchbrenner et al., 2016); paper underexplains task-specific adaptations.  
- Lacks analysis of failure modes and limitations (e.g., long-distance dependencies or specific constructions).  
- Absolute F1 for zero arguments remains low despite improvements, leaving open major challenges.

- Why was Iida et al. (2016) not used for comparison, and how does this model outperform their multi-column CNN?  
- What specific modifications distinguish this Grid-RNN from Kalchbrenner et al.'s implementation for Japanese PAS?  
- What are the most common error types in zero argument identification and remaining challenges?

- Overall, the paper makes a solid empirical contribution demonstrating value of multi-predicate modeling without parsers.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to include comparisons to recent neural work, clarify Grid-RNN novelties, and analyze failure cases.
