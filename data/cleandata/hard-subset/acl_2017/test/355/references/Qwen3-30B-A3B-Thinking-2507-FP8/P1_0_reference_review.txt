

# Peer Review: Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

## Summary
This paper proposes neural models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that automatically induce features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information from parsers. The authors introduce a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs that independently estimates arguments for each predicate, and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that captures interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. Their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, particularly for zero argument identification (omitted arguments in Japanese), improving by 3 points over the previous state-of-the-art.

## Main Contributions
1. A multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions without syntactic information, which represents the most significant contribution of the paper.
2. Demonstration that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance for Japanese PAS analysis without relying on syntactic parsers, challenging the common pipeline approach in the field.
3. Thorough experimental evaluation showing the effectiveness of their approach specifically for the challenging zero argument identification task.

## Strengths

1. **Significant improvement for zero argument identification**: The paper addresses a well-known challenge in Japanese PAS analysis where arguments are often omitted (zero pronouns), making identification difficult without syntactic dependencies. Their multi-sequence model achieves a 3-point improvement in F-measure for zero arguments over the previous state-of-the-art (47.12% vs. 44.09%), which is substantial in this domain. This is particularly valuable as zero argument identification is a major bottleneck in Japanese PAS analysis.

2. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The authors provide comprehensive ablation studies comparing single-sequence vs. multi-sequence models, with/without residual connections, and different network depths. The results consistently show that the multi-sequence model with Grid-RNNs and residual connections achieves the best performance, providing strong evidence for their approach.

3. **Clear contribution to the field**: The paper makes a clear contribution by demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information, which has been a common requirement in previous Japanese PAS systems. This shifts the paradigm away from error-prone pipeline approaches that rely on syntactic parsers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of Grid-RNN architecture**: While Grid-RNNs were introduced in previous work (Graves and Schmidhuber, 2009; Kalchbrenner et al., 2016), the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this specific architecture is particularly suited for multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis. The authors cite Grid-RNNs as a "neural architecture that effectively models multi-predicate interactions," but don't provide a strong theoretical or empirical justification for this choice over other neural architectures like transformers or attention-based models.

2. **Insufficient comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper compares primarily with older baseline models (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015), but doesn't directly compare with more recent neural models for Japanese PAS analysis that might have been published after these works. The cited work by Iida et al. (2016) using multi-column CNNs for zero anaphora resolution is mentioned but not directly compared, which weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.

3. **Misleading claim about "no syntactic information"**: The paper claims their model "does not rely on syntactic information," but their input features (like word position relative to the predicate) are inherently syntactic in nature. The PRED feature extracts "words around the predicate" using a window, which is a syntactic feature. This blurs the line between their approach and traditional methods, making the claim somewhat misleading.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify why Grid-RNNs are particularly suitable for modeling multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS compared to other neural architectures like transformers or attention-based RNNs? What specific properties of Grid-RNNs make them better suited for this task?

2. How does your multi-sequence model compare to more recent neural approaches for Japanese PAS analysis that might have been published after the cited works (e.g., after 2016)? Have you considered comparing with models that use contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT) that have become popular in the field?

3. The paper states that their model doesn't use syntactic information, but the PRED feature (which uses a window around the predicate) is clearly syntactically motivated. How do you reconcile this claim with your feature design, and what specific syntactic information does your model avoid using compared to previous approaches?

## Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable contribution to Japanese PAS analysis by demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic parsers, particularly for the challenging zero argument identification task. The multi-sequence model with Grid-RNNs is well-motivated and shows significant improvements. However, the novelty claim regarding Grid-RNNs could be strengthened, and the comparison with more recent neural approaches should be expanded.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the novelty of Grid-RNNs for this specific task and to provide a more precise characterization of the "no syntactic information" claim. The paper is well-written, well-structured, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Japanese NLP.
