

# Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of argument omission (zero pronouns) in Japanese. The authors claim their models achieve state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic information, which is a common approach in previous work. 

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1. The demonstration that neural sequence models can achieve competitive results on Japanese PAS analysis without using syntactic information (though this claim needs careful validation)
2. The introduction of a Grid-RNN-based multi-sequence model to capture multi-predicate interactions
3. Thorough experimental evaluation on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with comparisons to previous state-of-the-art approaches

## Strengths

1. **Solid experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough comparison against previous state-of-the-art approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015) on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 dataset. The results clearly show improvements in F-measure, particularly for zero arguments (the most challenging aspect of the task), which is a meaningful contribution to the field.

2. **Clear problem statement**: The paper effectively identifies and explains the core challenge of argument omission in Japanese PAS analysis, which has been recognized in previous literature (citing Iida and Poesio, 2011; Sasano and Kurohashi, 2011). The authors correctly identify that previous approaches relying on syntactic parsers suffer from error propagation, which is a valid concern.

3. **Reproducibility**: The paper provides sufficient implementation details, including hyperparameters, network architectures, and experimental settings, making the work reproducible. This is particularly important for neural approaches where implementation details can significantly affect results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the proposed architecture**: The Grid-RNN architecture is not novel - the authors cite Graves and Schmidhuber (2009) and Kalchbrenner et al. (2016) as the original sources. The paper fails to provide sufficient justification for why Grid-RNNs are particularly suitable for this task compared to more standard sequence modeling approaches (like LSTMs or Transformers), which are widely used in similar NLP tasks. The claim that Grid-RNNs "effectively capture multi-predicate interactions" is not sufficiently supported with comparative analysis against alternative architectures.

2. **Questionable claim about "no syntactic information"**: The paper claims their model uses "word sequence information" without syntactic information, but previous neural approaches for Japanese PAS analysis (Shibata et al., 2016; Iida et al., 2016) also used neural networks and likely incorporated syntactic information through feature engineering (as they mention "exploiting syntactic and selectional preference information as atomic features"). The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how their model truly differs in terms of syntactic information usage from previous work, making this key claim questionable.

3. **Inadequate ablation study**: The paper lacks a thorough ablation study to demonstrate that the specific Grid-RNN architecture is responsible for the performance gains. The authors claim that "the multi-sequence model significantly outperformed the single-sequence model," but they don't provide evidence that Grid-RNNs specifically are the key factor rather than just using a more complex neural model. They could have compared their Grid-RNN approach with standard RNNs or LSTMs on the same task to show the specific benefit of their architecture.

4. **Limited analysis of why the approach works for zero arguments**: The paper states that "capturing multi-predicate interactions is particularly effective for zero arguments," but provides no case studies or examples to illustrate this. A deeper analysis of specific examples where the multi-sequence model correctly identifies zero arguments would strengthen their claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify exactly what "syntactic information" means in the context of your claim that your model doesn't use it? Previous neural approaches (Shibata et al., 2016; Iida et al., 2016) also used neural networks and likely incorporated some form of syntactic information through their feature engineering. How is your approach truly different?

2. Why did you choose Grid-RNNs specifically for this task? How does it compare to other sequence modeling approaches (e.g., standard RNNs, LSTMs, Transformers) on the same task? A comparison with these more common architectures would better support your claim that Grid-RNNs are particularly suitable for capturing multi-predicate interactions.

3. Can you provide specific examples of sentences where your multi-sequence model correctly identifies a zero argument by leveraging multi-predicate interactions, and where the single-sequence model fails? This would help demonstrate why multi-predicate interactions are particularly valuable for zero argument identification.

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper presents a solid experimental evaluation showing improvements over previous work, the claimed novelty of the Grid-RNN architecture is questionable, and the key claim about "no syntactic information" requires stronger justification. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison with alternative architectures and a clearer explanation of how their approach truly differs from previous neural approaches. As a skeptical reviewer, I find the contribution to be incremental rather than transformative, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the novelty of their approach. 

I would recommend rejection, as the paper fails to provide compelling evidence that their specific architectural choice (Grid-RNNs) is truly novel and necessary for the performance gains they report. The results are interesting, but the contribution lacks sufficient methodological novelty to justify publication in ACL.
