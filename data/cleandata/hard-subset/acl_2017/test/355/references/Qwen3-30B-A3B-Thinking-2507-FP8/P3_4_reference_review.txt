

# Peer Review: Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on handling argument omission (zero pronouns) through multi-predicate interactions. The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that captures interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The key contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that high-performance Japanese PAS can be achieved without syntactic parsing, eliminating error propagation from pipeline processing
2. Proposing a Grid-RNN architecture that effectively models multi-predicate interactions for zero argument identification
3. Achieving state-of-the-art results on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, with particularly significant improvements (3 points) in zero argument identification

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Impact on a Challenging Problem**: The paper successfully addresses one of the most difficult aspects of Japanese PAS analysis—zero argument identification. The 3-point improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (Ouchi et al., 2015) for zero arguments is substantial, given that this task typically has F-measures around 40-50%. This represents a meaningful advance in the field.

2. **Strong Evidence for Multi-Predicate Interactions**: The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that multi-predicate interactions are crucial for zero argument identification, which aligns with linguistic intuition. The multi-sequence model's 1-point improvement over the single-sequence model specifically for zero arguments (47.12% vs 46.10%) is particularly convincing.

3. **Simplification of Pipeline Architecture**: By eliminating the need for syntactic parsers, the authors significantly simplify the PAS analysis pipeline. This is a major practical contribution, as previous joint approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015) suffered from error propagation through the pipeline. The demonstration that word sequence information alone suffices for high performance is valuable for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inadequate Comparison with Recent Neural Approaches**: The paper fails to properly compare with Shibata et al. (2016), which already used neural networks (feed-forward) for Japanese PAS. The authors mention this work but don't provide a clear comparison, making it difficult to assess whether their Grid-RNN approach offers a genuine improvement over existing neural methods. This is a significant oversight given that Shibata et al. (2016) is the most recent neural approach to Japanese PAS.

2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies**: The paper lacks critical ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components. For instance, it's unclear how much of the 0.27-point improvement (81.42% vs 81.15%) comes from Grid-RNNs versus deeper networks or residual connections. The results in Table 2 show that deeper networks with residual connections improve performance, but the authors don't quantify the specific contribution of the Grid architecture.

3. **Ambiguous Evaluation Methodology**: The paper references "NAIST Text Corpus 1.5" but doesn't clarify how it differs from the more commonly used NAIST Text Corpus 1.4β (mentioned in Table 3). The annotation criteria for the dative case differ between these versions, which significantly impacts results (as shown in Table 3). Without clear explanation, it's difficult to properly contextualize their results.

4. **Lack of Failure Analysis**: The paper provides no analysis of where the model fails. For Japanese PAS, particularly for zero arguments, understanding the failure cases would be valuable for identifying the limitations of the approach and guiding future work. This omission limits the paper's contribution to the field.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your Grid-RNN approach specifically improve upon Shibata et al.'s (2016) neural model for Japanese PAS? Shibata et al. used a feed-forward neural network for the score calculation part of the joint model, while your approach uses Grid-RNNs. What specific aspects of their architecture are overcome by your approach?

2. In Table 3, your model achieves 7.52 F-measure for DAT zero arguments, while Ouchi et al. (2015) achieved 4.80. Given that dative arguments are relatively rare in Japanese (only 3-4% of arguments), why is this improvement so substantial? Is there something specific about dative zero arguments that your model handles better?

3. The paper claims "our neural models realize the state-of-the-art performance by using word sequence information of a sentence" without syntactic information. However, the feature extraction (Section 3.1) uses "word index of the target predicate and words around the predicate" as a feature. How is this different from using syntactic information, and why is it acceptable to include this form of "local syntactic" information?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to Japanese PAS analysis by demonstrating that high-performance can be achieved without syntactic parsing, and by providing strong evidence for the importance of multi-predicate interactions. The improvements in zero argument identification are particularly significant. However, the paper would be substantially strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing a proper comparison with Shibata et al. (2016) and including more detailed ablation studies. The paper has strong potential but requires additional methodological rigor to fully establish its claims.

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions (addressing the weaknesses noted above). The work represents a meaningful advance in the field, but needs to better contextualize its contribution relative to recent neural approaches and provide more detailed analysis of the model's components and limitations.
