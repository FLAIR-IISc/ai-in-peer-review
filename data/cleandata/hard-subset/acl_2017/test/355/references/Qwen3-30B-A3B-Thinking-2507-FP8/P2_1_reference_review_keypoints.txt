- Summary: Proposes BiRNN single-sequence and Grid-RNN multi-sequence models for Japanese PAS, improving zero-pronoun resolution and achieving SOTA on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5.  
- Contribution: Introduces a Grid-RNN multi-sequence architecture to capture multi-predicate interactions without syntactic information.  
- Contribution: Shows neural models outperform prior SOTA, especially on zero arguments.  
- Contribution: Provides empirical evidence that multi-predicate interactions notably help zero-argument identification.  
- Strength: Achieves a substantial (≈3-point) F-measure gain on zero arguments over Ouchi et al. (2015), addressing long-distance dependencies.  
- Strength: Novel architecture models predicate interactions directly from word sequences and outperforms a single-sequence baseline.  
- Strength: Reaches state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic parsers, avoiding error propagation from pipelines.  
- Strength: Provides comprehensive evaluation (per-role analysis, ablations, argument-type breakdowns) supporting claims.  
- Weakness: Novelty justification is weak — Grid-RNN use is not clearly distinguished from prior neural multi-predicate/interactions work.  
- Weakness: Fails to directly compare with recent neural work (Shibata et al., 2016), undermining the SOTA claim.  
- Weakness: No discussion or analysis of computational complexity or scalability for sentences with many predicates.  
- Weakness: Limited discussion of generalization to other corpora or domains beyond NAIST 1.5.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to better justify novelty, include comparison to Shibata et al., and address complexity/generalizability.  
- Question: How does your Grid-RNN specifically differ from other neural approaches to modeling multi-predicate interactions?  
- Question: Why was there no direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016)?  
- Question: Please provide details on the computational complexity of the multi-sequence model for multi-predicate sentences.
