- Summary: Proposes neural sequence models for Japanese PAS—a bidirectional RNN per predicate and a Grid-RNN multi-sequence model—to resolve zero pronouns and achieve SOTA without syntactic parsers.  
- Contribution 1: Neural PAS approach that bypasses syntactic parsers, avoiding pipeline error propagation.  
- Contribution 2: Introduces a Grid-RNN architecture to model multi-predicate interactions for zero pronoun resolution.  
- Contribution 3: Empirical validation showing significant improvements, especially on zero arguments.  
- Strength 1: Effectively tackles the difficult zero pronoun problem with a notable F-measure gain (47.12% vs. 44.09%).  
- Strength 2: Novel Grid-RNN design motivated by predicate semantic relations, with empirical support for multi-predicate modeling.  
- Strength 3: Thorough ablation studies showing benefits of depth, residual connections, and multi-sequence modeling.  
- Weakness 1: Fails to compare directly with prior neural works (Shibata et al. 2016, Iida et al. 2016) on the same corpus.  
- Weakness 2: Lacks analysis of scalability and computational cost for long sentences or many predicates given Grid-RNN complexity O(M×T×L).  
- Weakness 3: Provides minimal linguistic analysis or concrete examples demonstrating learned multi-predicate interactions.  
- Weakness 4: Insufficient discussion of dataset/annotation limitations (NAIST Text Corpus 1.5), e.g., dative case handling and generalization.  
- Weakness 5: Overstates novelty relative to prior work (e.g., Zhou & Xu 2015) without clear architectural comparisons.  
- Question 1: How does the multi-sequence model scale and what are computational trade-offs for sentences with >5 predicates?  
- Question 2: Can the authors provide concrete examples where the multi-sequence model resolves zero pronouns that the single-sequence model fails on, explaining the interactions used?  
- Question 3: How do results compare directly with Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016) on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5?  
- Overall: Solid, well-motivated contribution with convincing results and a novel Grid-RNN; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to add comparisons and deeper analysis.
