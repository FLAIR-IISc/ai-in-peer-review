

# Review for ACL Submission: "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenging problem of predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, with a specific focus on identifying zero arguments (omitted arguments referred to by zero pronouns). The authors propose two neural sequence models: a single-sequence model that processes each predicate independently and a multi-sequence model that captures interactions between multiple predicates using Grid-RNNs. The main contributions claimed by the authors are:

1. A neural approach that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information without relying on syntactic information
2. A Grid-RNN-based multi-sequence model that effectively captures interactions between all predicates in a sentence
3. State-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, particularly for zero argument identification

In my assessment, the strongest contribution is the demonstration that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results for Japanese PAS without syntactic information, which addresses a key limitation of prior work. The second strongest contribution is the Grid-RNN-based multi-sequence model that captures multi-predicate interactions. The third contribution is the empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of these models, particularly for zero argument identification.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant problem**: The paper effectively identifies and motivates the challenge of zero argument identification in Japanese PAS analysis, which is a well-known difficulty in the field. The authors clearly explain why this is challenging (lack of direct syntactic dependencies) and why it matters for semantic analysis. This is a strong foundation for the paper.

2. **Strong empirical results for a challenging task**: The paper demonstrates that their neural models achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, with particularly significant improvements for zero argument identification (47.12% F1 vs. 44.09% for the previous state-of-the-art). This is a meaningful improvement in a difficult task and provides strong evidence that neural approaches can be effective for Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic information.

3. **Effective use of Grid-RNNs for multi-predicate interactions**: The paper's core technical contribution (the Grid-RNN-based multi-sequence model) is well-explained and justified. The results showing that the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model (81.42% vs. 81.15% F1) provide convincing evidence that capturing multi-predicate interactions is beneficial for PAS analysis, particularly for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 46.10% F1).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core neural architecture**: The Grid-RNN architecture used in the paper is not novel - it was introduced in Graves and Schmidhuber (2009) and later used in Kalchbrenner et al. (2016). The paper does not clearly explain how their application of Grid-RNNs differs from previous work or why this specific architecture is particularly well-suited for the task. The paper states that their model is "an extension from Zhou and Xu (2015)" but doesn't provide sufficient justification for why Grid-RNNs were chosen over other approaches to capture multi-predicate interactions.

2. **Inadequate comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper only compares with older baseline models (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015), but doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches to Japanese PAS, such as those by Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016), which also use neural networks. The paper mentions these works but doesn't provide a direct comparison. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the authors' approach is truly state-of-the-art or simply as good as older neural approaches.

3. **Lack of in-depth analysis of why the model works**: While the paper shows that the multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence model, it doesn't provide sufficient analysis of *why* this is the case. The paper would benefit from error analysis showing specific examples where the multi-sequence model correctly identified a zero argument that the single-sequence model missed, or an analysis of which types of multi-predicate interactions are most helpful for the task.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your multi-sequence model compare to the neural approaches by Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016), which also use neural networks for Japanese PAS? The paper mentions these works but doesn't provide a direct comparison. Given that Shibata et al. (2016) also used neural networks for PAS, how does your approach differ from theirs?

2. The paper claims that the Grid-RNN architecture "effectively captures multi-predicate interactions," but doesn't provide evidence beyond the overall F1 improvements. Could you provide specific examples where the multi-sequence model correctly identified a zero argument due to capturing interactions between predicates, and the single-sequence model failed to do so?

3. Why did you choose Grid-RNNs specifically for capturing multi-predicate interactions rather than other architectures that might handle multi-sequence data (e.g., transformers, attention mechanisms)? How would your approach compare to these alternatives?

## Additional Comments

1. The paper lacks ablation studies for key components of the model (e.g., residual connections, number of layers, window size). These would help understand the contribution of different design choices.

2. The paper mentions that their models achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information, but doesn't discuss the computational cost of their approach compared to previous methods. It would be useful to know if the neural models require significantly more training time or resources.

3. The paper doesn't discuss how the model would scale to sentences with more than two predicates, which is common in Japanese. This is an important limitation to address.

4. The paper states that "the multi-sequence model significantly outperformed the single-sequence model in F-measure in total (81.42% vs 81.15%)" but doesn't provide statistical significance for these results beyond the bootstrap resampling mentioned in Table 1. It would be helpful to have more details on the statistical significance of these improvements.

## Overall Assessment

This paper addresses an important problem in Japanese PAS analysis and demonstrates that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic information. The results are solid, and the focus on zero argument identification is particularly relevant to the challenges of Japanese semantic analysis.

However, the core technical contribution (the Grid-RNN-based multi-sequence model) is not sufficiently novel, and the paper lacks adequate comparison with recent neural approaches to the problem. The lack of in-depth analysis of why the multi-sequence model works better than the single-sequence model is another significant weakness.

With substantial revisions to address these weaknesses (particularly adding a comparison with recent neural approaches and providing more detailed analysis of why the multi-sequence model works), this paper could make a stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper's contribution is somewhat incremental.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

The paper has important contributions in demonstrating the effectiveness of neural models for Japanese PAS analysis, but the core technical contribution lacks sufficient novelty and the paper doesn't sufficiently compare with recent work in the field. The paper would need substantial revisions to strengthen the novelty claim and provide more thorough analysis before it could be considered for publication at ACL.
