- Paper proposes neural sequence models (single-sequence BiRNN and multi-sequence Grid-RNN) for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on zero pronoun resolution.  
- Main claim: neural models can achieve state-of-the-art PAS results without syntactic parsers, avoiding pipeline error propagation.  
- Contribution: Grid-RNN architecture to model interactions between multiple predicates, improving zero argument resolution.  
- Empirical result: multi-sequence model achieves 81.42% F1 vs. Ouchi et al.'s 79.23%; zero-argument F1 improves from 44.09% to 47.12%.  
- Analysis by case role shows strong gains for the low-frequency dative case (32.35% vs. 4.80%).  
- Strength: addresses a meaningful, practical problem (zero pronouns) and reduces dependency on syntactic parsers.  
- Strength: thorough empirical validation with statistical significance (p<0.05).  
- Strength: well-motivated Grid-RNN design with residual connections improving deeper network performance.  
- Weakness: novelty overstated — Grid-RNNs and their use in text were previously introduced (Graves & Schmidhuber 2009; Kalchbrenner et al. 2016).  
- Weakness: absolute F1 gains are modest (0.27% overall), raising questions whether gains stem from architecture or general neural modeling.  
- Weakness: lacks direct comparison to recent neural approaches (e.g., Shibata et al. 2016, Iida et al. 2016).  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion/evidence of limitations, e.g., long-distance dependencies, multiple zero pronouns, and shared arguments.  
- Questions for authors: clarify differences from Shibata et al. (2016) and provide direct comparisons; explain specific advantages of Grid-RNN beyond general neural gains; provide examples demonstrating long-distance zero-pronoun resolution.  
- Overall: solid contribution demonstrating neural PAS without syntax and improved zero-argument resolution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to temper novelty claims and add comparisons/limitations.
