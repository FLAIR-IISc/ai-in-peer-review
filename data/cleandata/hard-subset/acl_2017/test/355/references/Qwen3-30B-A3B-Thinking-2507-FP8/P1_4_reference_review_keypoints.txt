- Contribution: Proposes neural models (BiRNN single-sequence and Grid-RNN multi-sequence) for Japanese PAS, focusing on zero pronoun resolution and avoiding syntactic parsers.
- Contribution: Introduces Grid-RNN to capture multi-predicate interactions and shows neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results.
- Contribution: Argues that whole-sentence RNN context improves zero argument identification.

- Strength: Significant and statistically meaningful improvement on zero-argument identification (47.12% vs. 44.09% F1).
- Strength: Novel application of Grid-RNN to model multi-predicate semantic interactions without syntactic information.
- Strength: Strong experimental design with ablations, per-role analysis, and fair baselines on the NAIST Text Corpus.
- Strength: Clear evidence that sentence-level context and long-distance dependencies aid zero pronoun resolution.

- Weakness: Core Grid-RNN architecture is not novel and paper lacks detail on task-specific adaptations or differences from prior Grid-RNN work.
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to recent neural approaches (e.g., Shibata et al., Iida et al.) to clarify relative improvements.
- Weakness: Lacks qualitative analysis and concrete examples showing how multi-predicate interactions enable correct zero pronoun resolution.
- Weakness: No discussion of computational complexity or training time to assess practical cost of Grid-RNNs.
- Weakness: Limited positioning within broader SRL literature (e.g., comparisons to neural English SRL models).

- Question: How does your Grid-RNN differ specifically from Kalchbrenner et al.'s Grid-LSTM and what task-specific adaptations were made?
- Question: Can you compare performance against Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016), especially when those models have syntactic input?
- Question: Can you provide qualitative examples where the multi-sequence model succeeds and the single-sequence model fails?
- Question: What is the computational complexity and training time compared to prior state-of-the-art—are gains worth the cost?
- Question: Why do multi-predicate interactions help zero arguments more than direct dependency (Dep) arguments?

- Overall recommendation: Strong paper with impactful empirical gains; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address comparisons, analysis, and efficiency.
