- Contribution: Achieves high-performance Japanese PAS without syntactic parsing, avoiding pipeline error propagation.  
- Contribution: Proposes a Grid-RNN architecture to model multi-predicate interactions for zero argument identification.  
- Contribution: Reaches state-of-the-art on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, with a ~3-point gain on zero argument identification.

- Strength: Significant practical impact—substantial 3-point F improvement on the difficult zero argument task.  
- Strength: Provides strong empirical evidence that multi-predicate interactions improve zero argument identification (multi-sequence > single-sequence).  
- Strength: Simplifies pipeline by removing dependency on syntactic parsers, showing word sequence information can suffice.

- Weakness: Inadequate comparison with recent neural approach (Shibata et al., 2016), making relative gains unclear.  
- Weakness: Insufficient ablation studies to isolate the Grid-RNN’s contribution versus depth or residual connections.  
- Weakness: Ambiguous evaluation methodology—unclear differences between NAIST 1.5 and commonly used 1.4β and their impact on results.  
- Weakness: No failure analysis provided to characterize model errors, especially for zero arguments.

- Question: How exactly does Grid-RNN improve over Shibata et al.’s feed-forward neural model?  
- Question: Why is the DAT zero-argument F-measure improvement so large given the rarity of dative arguments?  
- Question: How do features like target-predicate index and surrounding words differ from syntactic information, and why are they acceptable?

- Overall: Valuable advance showing PAS without parsing and the importance of multi-predicate interactions, but needs clearer comparisons and more analyses.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
