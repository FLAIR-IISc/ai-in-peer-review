

# Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

## Summary
The paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenging problem of zero pronoun resolution (arguments omitted in Japanese sentences). The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model that processes each predicate independently, and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The paper demonstrates that these models achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without relying on syntactic information.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Demonstrating that neural models can achieve competitive results for Japanese PAS without syntactic information (though previous work by Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016) had already shown this)
2. Proposing a Grid-RNN architecture to model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS
3. Showing marginal improvements over state-of-the-art baselines, particularly for zero argument identification

## Strengths
1. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper provides detailed results across multiple metrics (F-measure for Dep, Zero, and all arguments) and different case roles (NOM, ACC, DAT) on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 dataset. The authors report statistical significance using bootstrap resampling, which is good practice.

2. **Clear ablation studies**: The authors conduct useful ablation studies showing the effects of network depth, residual connections, and the multi-predicate consideration. For example, Table 2 demonstrates how deeper networks with residual connections improve performance.

3. **Addressing a real problem**: Zero pronoun resolution is indeed a well-known challenging problem in Japanese PAS, and the paper attempts to tackle it with a neural approach. The focus on intra-sentential argument analysis is appropriate given the difficulty of inter-sentential analysis.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach**: The Grid-RNN architecture is not convincingly shown to be necessary or superior to simpler alternatives. The paper doesn't compare to standard RNNs or attention mechanisms that could potentially model multi-predicate interactions without the complexity of Grid-RNNs. The justification for using Grid-RNNs seems to be based on citing previous work (Graves and Schmidhuber, 2009; Kalchbrenner et al., 2016) without demonstrating why this specific architecture is better for their task. This is particularly concerning as the authors claim it "effectively captures multi-predicate interactions" without showing it's better than simpler approaches.

2. **Insufficient evidence that neural approach is better**: The paper claims that their neural models "realize the state-of-the-art performance by using word sequence information of a sentence" without sufficient evidence that this is due to the neural approach rather than better feature engineering. Previous work (Shibata et al., 2016; Iida et al., 2016) had already shown that neural networks can achieve good results with syntactic features. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why a neural approach without syntactic features is better than using syntactic features with a simpler model. This is a significant oversight for a paper claiming to advance the field.

3. **Marginal improvements over baselines**: The improvements over the state-of-the-art (Ouchi et al., 2015) are relatively small (about 1-3 F1 points). While statistically significant (Table 1), these improvements might not be practically significant for real-world applications. In my view as a skeptical reviewer, this marginal gain doesn't justify the novelty claim of the Grid-RNN approach. The paper would have been stronger with larger improvements or a more compelling argument for why these small gains matter.

4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any error analysis to understand why their model works better, especially for zero arguments. A detailed analysis of cases where their model succeeded or failed would be valuable to understand the model's strengths and limitations. Without this, it's difficult to assess whether the improvements are due to the model architecture or just better training.

5. **Inadequate comparison to simpler alternatives**: The paper only compares to previous PAS approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015; Imamura et al., 2009) but doesn't compare to simpler neural architectures that could model multi-predicate interactions. For example, they could have compared to a model that uses standard RNNs over all predicates or a model with attention mechanisms between predicates. Without these comparisons, it's hard to justify the complexity of the Grid-RNN approach.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that their neural models achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information. However, previous work (Shibata et al., 2016; Iida et al., 2016) had already shown that neural networks can achieve good results with syntactic features. Can the authors provide a more convincing argument for why a neural approach without syntactic features is better than using syntactic features with a simpler model?

2. Why is Grid-RNN specifically needed for modeling multi-predicate interactions? Could standard RNNs or attention mechanisms achieve similar results with less complexity? The paper doesn't provide a comparison to these simpler alternatives, which is a significant omission.

3. The improvements over the state-of-the-art are relatively small (about 1-3 F1 points). Can the authors provide a more detailed error analysis to understand the specific cases where their model improves and why these improvements matter for real-world applications?

## Overall Recommendation
I am not convinced that this paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The improvements over the state-of-the-art are marginal, and the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the Grid-RNN architecture is necessary or superior to simpler alternatives. The authors claim to "automatically induce features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information," but the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this is due to the neural approach rather than better feature engineering. The paper would have been stronger with a more compelling argument for the novelty of their approach and more substantial improvements over baselines.

The paper is well-written and the experiments are thorough, but the marginal gains and lack of convincing evidence for the novelty of the Grid-RNN approach make it difficult to justify the main contribution. I recommend rejection, as the paper doesn't present a sufficiently novel or impactful contribution to warrant publication in ACL.
