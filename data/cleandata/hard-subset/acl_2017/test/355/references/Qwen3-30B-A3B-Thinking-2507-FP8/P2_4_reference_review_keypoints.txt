- Proposes neural sequence models that induce multi-predicate interaction features for Japanese PAS without relying on syntactic parsers, addressing zero pronoun challenges.  
- Main contributions: novel syntax-free neural approach, Grid-RNN multi-sequence model for explicit predicate interactions, and state-of-the-art results on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5.  
- Strength: Addresses a meaningful problem by avoiding parser-dependent error propagation, improving zero argument identification.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results and ablation studies showing multi-sequence model and residual connections materially improve performance.  
- Strength: Novel application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions with clear linguistic motivation.  
- Strength: Clear comparison and contextualization against relevant prior Japanese PAS work.  
- Weakness: Overstates novelty of Grid-RNN use; lacks differentiation from prior Grid-RNN applications.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison with English SRL work (e.g., Zhou and Xu 2015) to show generality.  
- Weakness: Lacks detailed error analysis and illustrative examples explaining multi-sequence gains on zero arguments.  
- Weakness: Unclear what “without syntactic information” precisely means versus features used by prior work.  
- Weakness: Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability relative to sentence length and predicate count.  
- Question: How does this Grid-RNN application differ from previous NLP uses and why is it particularly suited to Japanese PAS?  
- Question: Can the approach be applied to English SRL without syntax, and how would it compare to Zhou and Xu (2015)?  
- Question: Can the authors provide specific zero-argument examples where multi-sequence succeeds but single-sequence fails?  
- Question: What exact features are used and how do they differ from previous syntactic or joint models (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015)?  
- Question: How does model complexity scale with number of predicates per sentence and would long sentences be prohibitive?  
- Overall assessment: Strong, well-motivated paper with convincing results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify novelty, provide error analysis, and discuss features and complexity.
