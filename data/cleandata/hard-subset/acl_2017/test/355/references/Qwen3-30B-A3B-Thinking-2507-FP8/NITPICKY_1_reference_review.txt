

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on capturing multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information. The authors introduce a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that processes all predicates and their argument candidates simultaneously. They claim their models achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, with the multi-sequence model showing particular improvements for zero argument identification.

## Main Contributions (as claimed by authors)
1. A neural model that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information (without relying on syntactic information).
2. A grid-type neural architecture (Grid-RNN) for capturing multi-predicate interactions.
3. Experimental results showing state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS analysis.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in Japanese PAS analysis (zero argument identification), which is challenging due to the lack of direct syntactic dependencies in pro-drop languages.
2. The approach of using neural models to capture multi-predicate interactions without syntactic information is novel and potentially valuable for Japanese NLP.
3. The results show clear improvements over previous state-of-the-art models, particularly for zero arguments (3.0-point improvement in F-measure).

## Weaknesses
1. **Critical technical error in reference**: The paper cites "Graves and Schmidhuber, 2009" for Grid-RNNs, but the actual paper (Graves and Schmidhuber, 2009) introduces "Grid LSTM," not Grid-RNN. This is a significant technical inaccuracy that undermines the paper's credibility. The paper should have cited "Kalchbrenner et al., 2016" for Grid-RNN, which is correctly cited later in the paper.

2. **Numerous formatting inconsistencies**: The paper contains multiple inconsistent mathematical notations throughout (e.g., "x arg t" instead of "x_{arg,t}", "x pred t" instead of "x_{pred,t}", "xmarkt" instead of "x_{mark,t}"). There are also missing parentheses in equations (e.g., in the Grid Layer section, the equation for h^(ℓ)_m,t is missing a closing parenthesis). These inconsistencies make the paper difficult to follow and appear unprofessional for an ACL submission.

3. **Incomplete description of Grid-RNN implementation**: The paper claims that Grid-RNNs effectively capture multi-predicate interactions but fails to provide sufficient detail about why Grid-RNNs are specifically needed for this task instead of other architectures that could capture multi-predicate interactions. The paper also doesn't explain how the Grid-RNN architecture differs from standard RNNs in this specific context.

4. **Reference formatting inconsistencies**: The reference list contains numerous formatting errors (e.g., "Bastien et al.,? 2012" should be "Bastien et al., 2012", "Carreras and Màrquez.,? \Q2005\E, Carreras and Màrquez.," has strange formatting, "Collobert et al\.,? 2011" has a backslash in "et al\."). These errors are unprofessional for an ACL submission.

5. **Missing experimental details**: The paper claims "significant improvements" but doesn't provide full statistical evidence for all comparisons (only mentions significance for Single-Seq vs. Multi-Seq in Table 1). The values for Table 2 are missing in the provided text, making it impossible to verify the claimed performance improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper cites "Graves and Schmidhuber, 2009" for Grid-RNNs, but that paper actually introduces "Grid LSTM," not Grid-RNN. Could you please clarify the correct reference for Grid-RNN and explain why this reference is used in the paper?

2. The paper states "the grid-type neural architecture effectively captures multi-predicate interactions" but doesn't provide qualitative analysis (e.g., example outputs showing how the model captures interactions). Could you provide such examples to support this claim?

3. In the Grid Layer section, you mention "the blue curve lines represent the inter-sequence connections" but there's no figure with blue curves provided in the paper. Could you clarify what these blue curves refer to or provide the appropriate figure?

4. The paper claims "the neural models realize the state-of-the-art performance by using word sequence information of a sentence" but doesn't compare directly to other neural models that use word sequence information without syntactic information (e.g., Zhou and Xu, 2015). Could you clarify how your approach differs from these existing neural models?

5. In Table 3, you compare your results to models using "NAIST Text Corpus 1.4β" with external resources. Could you clarify how your experiments differ from these previous studies (especially since you claim to use "no external resources")?

## Overall Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and shows promising results, the numerous technical inaccuracies, formatting inconsistencies, and missing details are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The critical error in citing "Grid-RNN" when the reference is for "Grid LSTM" is particularly concerning, as it suggests a lack of careful attention to detail. For an ACL submission, which is known for its high standards in technical accuracy and presentation, these issues significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The paper would require substantial revisions to address these fundamental problems before it could be considered for publication.
