

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenging problem of zero pronoun resolution. The authors introduce a single-sequence model using RNNs and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information. They evaluate their models on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 and claim state-of-the-art results.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. The multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis
2. Demonstration that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information
3. Improvement in zero argument identification, a challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis

## Strengths
1. **Focus on a meaningful problem**: The paper addresses a well-documented challenge in Japanese PAS analysis (zero pronoun resolution), which is recognized as one of the most problematic issues in the field (cited by Iida and Poesio, 2011; Sasano and Kurohashi, 2011).

2. **Thorough evaluation on standard dataset**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, with appropriate data splits and comparison with previous state-of-the-art approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015).

3. **Clear demonstration of multi-predicate interactions benefit**: The results show that the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model (81.42% vs 81.15% F1), particularly for zero arguments (47.12% vs 46.10% F1), which aligns with the linguistic intuition that multi-predicate interactions are beneficial for zero argument identification.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Technical Inconsistencies in Grid-RNN Architecture (Major flaw)
The paper's description of the Grid-RNN architecture (Section 4.2) contradicts the standard Grid-RNN architecture described in the cited literature (Graves and Schmidhuber, 2009; Kalchbrenner et al., 2016). The paper states that "odd-numbered layers" use "h^(ℓ)_m-1,t" (previous sequence) and "h^(ℓ)_m,t-1" (previous word), while "even-numbered layers" use "h^(ℓ)_m+1,t" (next sequence) and "h^(ℓ)_m,t+1" (next word). This alternating direction per layer (odd/even) is inconsistent with the Grid-RNN architecture described in the literature, which typically maintains consistent directionality across layers. This fundamental misunderstanding of the Grid-RNN architecture makes the proposed model difficult to understand and replicate, significantly undermining the technical contribution.

### 2. Confusing Description of Residual Connections (Major flaw)
The paper states: "the input vector h^(ℓ-1)_m,t of the ℓ-th layer is added to the output vector h^(ℓ)_m,t" (Section 4.2). This description is technically incorrect. Residual connections should be between the input and output of the *same* layer, not between different layers (h^(ℓ-1) and h^(ℓ)). The correct residual connection would be h^(ℓ)_m,t = h^(ℓ)_m,t + h^(ℓ-1)_m,t, not as described. This confusion in basic neural network concepts suggests a lack of technical rigor.

### 3. Formatting and Reference Inconsistencies (Significant flaw)
The paper contains multiple formatting issues that undermine its credibility:
- References to "Figure 1" appear multiple times in Sections 2.1 and 2.2, but the figure is not included in the paper.
- Figure numbering appears inconsistent (e.g., references to "Figure 2" before "Figure 3" and "Figure 4" are described).
- "Figure 6" is referenced in Section 4.2 but not provided.
- The placeholder URL "http:xxx" for the source code is clearly incomplete.
- Inconsistent reference formatting (e.g., "Bastien et al\.,? 2012" instead of "Bastien et al., 2012").
- The paper claims "the whole document" for Inter-Zero arguments but then states they focus on "intra-sentential" arguments, creating a logical inconsistency.

### 4. Impractical Numerical Claims (Minor flaw)
The paper states "the multi-sequence model improved around 3.0 points in F-measure" for zero arguments, but the actual improvement is 3.03 points (47.12 - 44.09 = 3.03). While this might seem minor, it reflects a lack of precision in the paper's presentation. The paper claims "around 3.0 points" but provides an exact figure of 3.03, creating a minor contradiction.

### 5. Inadequate Comparison with Relevant Neural Approaches (Moderate flaw)
The paper claims "our neural models realize the state-of-the-art performance by using word sequence information of a sentence" but fails to provide a fair comparison with other neural approaches that might have used similar word sequence information without syntactic information. For example, Zhou and Xu (2015) achieved 81.07% F1 on English SRL using bidirectional RNNs without syntactic information, but the paper doesn't discuss how their approach differs from this or why it would be expected to perform better on Japanese PAS. This omission weakens the claim of novelty.

## Questions to Authors

1. The Grid-RNN architecture described in Section 4.2 alternates between using previous and next sequences based on layer parity (odd/even). This contradicts the Grid-RNN architecture described in Graves and Schmidhuber (2009) and Kalchbrenner et al. (2016), which maintains consistent directionality. Can you clarify why your architecture differs from the standard Grid-RNN and provide evidence that this alternating direction improves performance?

2. The residual connection description states "the input vector h^(ℓ-1)_m,t of the ℓ-th layer is added to the output vector h^(ℓ)_m,t." This implies a connection between layers rather than within a layer. Could you provide the correct mathematical formulation for the residual connection in your model?

3. The paper references "Figure 1" multiple times but does not include it. Can you provide this figure or clarify what it's supposed to illustrate? Additionally, Figure 6 is referenced but not included.

4. Zhou and Xu (2015) achieved 81.07% F1 on English SRL using bidirectional RNNs without syntactic information. How does your approach differ from theirs, and why would it be expected to perform better on Japanese PAS (which is dependency-based rather than constituent-based)?

5. The paper claims "the neural models realize the state-of-the-art performance by using word sequence information of a sentence" but doesn't compare with other neural approaches that might have used similar word sequence information. Can you provide a comparison with the neural approaches in Shibata et al. (2016) or Iida et al. (2016) that used neural networks for Japanese PAS?

## Final Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in Japanese PAS analysis, the technical flaws in the Grid-RNN architecture description, the confusing residual connection explanation, and the formatting inconsistencies are too severe to ignore. The fundamental misunderstanding of the Grid-RNN architecture (a core component of the proposed method) and the lack of precision in technical descriptions significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The formatting issues also detract from the paper's professionalism. These flaws, while perhaps minor in isolation, collectively constitute a substantial barrier to understanding and replicating the proposed method. Given the perfectionist standards of ACL, these issues warrant rejection despite the paper's potentially valuable focus on zero pronoun resolution in Japanese PAS analysis.
