- Paper proposes neural sequence models (single-sequence BiRNN and multi-sequence Grid-RNN) for Japanese PAS to handle zero pronoun resolution without syntactic parsers, achieving state-of-the-art on NAIST TC 1.5.  
- Key contributions: (1) modeling multi-predicate interactions without syntactic input, (2) SOTA results, (3) marked improvement on zero argument identification.  

Strengths:
- Addresses reliance on syntactic parsers and offers a principled neural alternative that operates on word sequences.  
- Grid-RNN effectively captures predicate–predicate interactions aligned with linguistic intuition.  
- Empirical gains (0.27% overall F1, 1.02% for zero arguments) with detailed per-case analysis and bootstrap significance testing.  
- Well-designed experiments, standard benchmark use, complete implementation details, and thoughtful ablations.

Weaknesses:
- Grid-RNN novelty is limited since the architecture is adapted from prior work and not clearly differentiated from alternatives.  
- Performance gains are marginal overall and not fully contextualized against more recent neural models.  
- No failure analysis to show where the model still struggles, especially on zero pronoun cases.  
- Claim of “no syntactic information” is weakened by features (e.g., PRED window) that implicitly encode local syntactic context.  
- Computational cost/complexity of Grid-RNN vs simpler models is not analyzed or compared.

Questions to authors:
- How does Grid-RNN compare to recent architectures (e.g., transformers) for capturing multi-predicate interactions?  
- Can you provide concrete sentence examples where multi-sequence outperforms single-sequence and explain the interaction effect?  
- How do you justify the “no syntactic information” claim given the PRED window feature, and what happens if it’s removed?

Overall recommendation:
- Accept with minor revisions to address novelty claims, provide failure analysis, and more nuanced discussion of the marginal gains.
