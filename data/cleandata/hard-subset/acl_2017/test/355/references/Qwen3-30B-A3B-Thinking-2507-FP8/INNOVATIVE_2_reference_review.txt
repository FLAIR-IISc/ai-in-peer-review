

# Review for ACL Submission

## Summary
This paper proposes neural models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on addressing the challenging problem of argument omission (zero pronouns) in Japanese. The authors introduce two neural models: a single-sequence model using RNNs that processes arguments for each predicate independently, and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that explicitly models interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The key innovation is the application of Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information, which the authors claim is particularly beneficial for zero argument identification.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. Novel application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis, addressing a core challenge (argument omission) without relying on syntactic information.
2. Demonstration that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results on Japanese PAS analysis by learning contextual features directly from word sequences.
3. Significant empirical improvements, particularly for zero argument identification (3.0 F1 point improvement over the state-of-the-art), which represents a meaningful advancement in a challenging task.

## Strengths
1. **Bold application of Grid-RNNs to a challenging NLP problem**: The paper makes a compelling case for why modeling multi-predicate interactions is crucial for Japanese PAS analysis, especially for zero arguments. The application of Grid-RNNs to this specific task is a bold and novel approach that directly addresses a well-known challenge in Japanese NLP. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach outperforms the previous state-of-the-art, particularly for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 44.09% F1), which is a significant 3-point improvement. This is exactly the kind of novel application of existing techniques that I value as a reviewer.

2. **Strong empirical evidence for the core problem**: The paper provides clear, strong empirical evidence that the multi-predicate interaction approach is beneficial, especially for zero arguments (which are the most challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis). The results show consistent improvements across multiple metrics, with statistically significant gains for zero arguments (p<0.05). The authors also provide detailed analysis of performance per case role, showing that the neural models particularly excel for the dative case (30-point improvement), which is a meaningful contribution to understanding how neural models handle different linguistic phenomena.

3. **Addresses a well-established challenge in Japanese NLP**: The paper directly tackles the problem of argument omission in Japanese, which has been identified as a major bottleneck in PAS analysis for years (Iida and Poesio, 2011; Sasano and Kurohashi, 2011). By demonstrating that neural models can effectively capture the necessary context without syntactic information, the paper provides a valuable alternative to existing approaches that rely on error-prone parsers. This is particularly relevant given the limitations of pipeline processing in Japanese NLP.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty of the Grid-RNN approach**: While the application is novel, Grid-RNNs were introduced by Graves and Schmidhuber (2009) and later used by Kalchbrenner et al. (2016). The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why Grid-RNNs are the best choice for this task compared to other architectures (e.g., transformers, attention-based models). This weakens the novelty claim, as the paper is essentially applying an existing architecture to a new domain without a strong theoretical justification for why that architecture is particularly suitable.

2. **Lack of thorough ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive ablation study to isolate the contribution of the Grid-RNN component. It's unclear whether the performance gains are due to the Grid-RNN structure itself or simply the increased model capacity from using more layers. For example, the authors don't show how a standard RNN with the same number of layers would perform compared to the Grid-RNN model.

3. **Incomplete comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper only compares with Ouchi et al. (2015) and Imamura et al. (2009), but fails to compare with Shibata et al. (2016), who also used neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis. This omission makes it difficult to assess how much the proposed approach advances the state-of-the-art, as Shibata et al. (2016) already achieved good results using neural networks.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why did you choose Grid-RNNs specifically over other sequence-to-sequence architectures (e.g., transformers, attention-based models)? What advantages do Grid-RNNs offer for this task compared to more modern architectures?

2. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study showing the contribution of the Grid-RNN structure to the overall performance? For example, how does the model perform with the same number of layers but without the Grid-RNN connections?

3. How does your approach compare to Shibata et al. (2016), who also used neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis? Why didn't you include this comparison in your experiments?

4. Could you provide qualitative examples showing how the Grid-RNN model handles specific zero pronoun cases differently from the single-sequence model, particularly for cases where the improvement is most significant?

## Recommendation
**Accept**

This paper presents a novel application of Grid-RNNs to a challenging problem in Japanese NLP (argument omission in PAS analysis), with strong empirical results. While the Grid-RNN architecture itself isn't new, the application to this specific task is innovative and addresses a well-known bottleneck in Japanese NLP. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over the state-of-the-art, particularly for zero arguments (3-point F1 improvement), which is a meaningful contribution to the field.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's bold application of an existing architecture to a challenging problem to be valuable. The results are strong and meaningful, and the approach has the potential to influence future work in Japanese NLP and potentially other pro-drop languages. While the paper has some weaknesses (particularly in the novelty justification and ablation study), these don't outweigh the significant contribution of the novel application and the strong empirical results.
