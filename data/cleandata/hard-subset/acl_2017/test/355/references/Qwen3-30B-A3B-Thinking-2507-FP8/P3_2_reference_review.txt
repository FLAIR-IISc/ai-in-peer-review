

# Peer Review: Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenging problem of zero pronoun identification (omitted arguments). The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs that processes each predicate independently, and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that captures interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The main contributions are: (1) a neural approach to PAS analysis that avoids reliance on syntactic parsers (solving error propagation issues), (2) a novel Grid-RNN architecture to model multi-predicate interactions, and (3) state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, particularly for zero argument identification.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a fundamental limitation in existing approaches**: The paper correctly identifies that previous state-of-the-art Japanese PAS systems (Ouchi et al., 2015; Shibata et al., 2016) heavily rely on syntactic parsers, suffering from error propagation. The authors' solution of using neural networks that work directly on word sequences is well-motivated and addresses a real problem in the field. This is a significant contribution as it removes a major bottleneck in the pipeline.

2. **Demonstrating meaningful improvement on the zero argument task**: The multi-sequence model achieves a 1.03% absolute improvement in F-measure for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 46.09%), which is particularly impressive given that zero argument identification is one of the most challenging aspects of Japanese PAS. This represents a concrete, measurable improvement on a specific, well-identified problem.

3. **Clear architectural innovation**: The Grid-RNN approach to model multi-predicate interactions is well-explained and represents a thoughtful adaptation of existing neural network architectures. The paper clearly distinguishes this approach from previous work (e.g., Zhou and Xu, 2015) by addressing the multi-predicate aspect that was missing in English SRL models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated "state-of-the-art" claim**: The paper claims "state-of-the-art" results, but the improvements are relatively modest (only 2.19% absolute F-measure improvement over Ouchi et al., 2015). More critically, the paper doesn't compare with Iida et al. (2016), which also used neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis (specifically for zero anaphora resolution). The absence of this comparison weakens the claim of being state-of-the-art.

2. **Lack of analysis for "why" the model works**: While the authors demonstrate that their model outperforms previous approaches for zero arguments, they don't provide a detailed analysis of *how* the model identifies zero arguments. For example, they don't analyze which specific multi-predicate interactions are most informative for zero argument resolution. This limits the theoretical contribution of the work.

3. **Insufficient comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper mentions Zhou and Xu (2015) as a baseline but doesn't compare with their model directly on the Japanese PAS task. Given that Zhou and Xu achieved 81.07% F-measure on English SRL, it would be valuable to see how their approach would perform on Japanese data, especially since the authors claim their model is an extension of this work.

4. **No scalability analysis**: The paper doesn't address how the Grid-RNN architecture scales with sentence complexity. For sentences with many predicates (e.g., 5+), the Grid-RNN's computational complexity might become prohibitive. The paper should discuss the practical limitations of their approach.

5. **Inadequate discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't acknowledge potential weaknesses, such as the model's performance on very long sentences (where RNNs typically struggle with long-range dependencies) or its ability to handle complex predicate relationships beyond the ones in the training data.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions Iida et al. (2016) used neural networks for zero anaphora resolution but doesn't compare with their results directly. How does your model compare with their approach, and why does your method achieve better results?

2. The paper claims multi-predicate interactions are particularly helpful for zero arguments. Can you provide specific examples from your results showing which predicate relationships (e.g., temporal, causal) are most informative for zero argument identification?

3. The Grid-RNN architecture has O(M^2T) complexity where M is the number of predicates and T is sentence length. How does this affect your model's performance on longer sentences or sentences with many predicates?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach to Japanese PAS analysis that successfully addresses a key limitation in previous work. The incremental improvements on zero argument identification are meaningful, and the Grid-RNN architecture is a clever adaptation of existing neural network concepts. However, the paper's claims of "state-of-the-art" results are overstated due to the lack of comparison with relevant recent neural approaches, and the lack of deeper analysis into why the model works well for zero arguments limits the theoretical contribution. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with major revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above)
