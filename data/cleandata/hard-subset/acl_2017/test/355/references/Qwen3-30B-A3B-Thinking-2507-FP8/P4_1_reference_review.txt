

# Review for "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes neural network models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of zero anaphora resolution (identifying omitted arguments). The authors argue that existing approaches rely heavily on syntactic information from parsers, which suffers from error propagation. They introduce two models: a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs to capture context around a single predicate, and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The paper demonstrates that these neural models achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without relying on syntactic information.

Main contributions in order of strength:
1. A neural architecture (Grid-RNN based) that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis without relying on syntactic information (the most significant contribution)
2. Demonstration that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results for Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic information, representing a significant departure from prior work
3. Empirical evidence showing that modeling multi-predicate interactions is particularly beneficial for zero argument identification, which is the most challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evidence for addressing a well-defined problem**: The paper provides clear, statistically significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art approaches, particularly for zero arguments (the most challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis). The multi-sequence model achieves 81.42% F1 compared to 79.23% for the previous state-of-the-art, with a particularly significant improvement of 3.0 points for zero arguments (47.12% vs 44.09%). This demonstrates that the authors' approach effectively addresses a long-standing problem in Japanese PAS analysis.

2. **Effective use of Grid-RNNs for multi-predicate interactions**: The paper provides a clear architectural description of how Grid-RNNs enable the capture of multi-predicate interactions. The results clearly show that the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model (81.42% vs 81.15% F1), with particularly significant improvements for zero arguments (47.12% vs 46.10%). This validates the authors' core claim that modeling multi-predicate interactions is beneficial for Japanese PAS analysis.

3. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies (comparing different network depths, with/without residual connections) and detailed analysis per case role. Notably, the neural models achieve much higher results for the dative case (improvement of ~30 points), demonstrating robust learning of rare argument types. The evaluation on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 dataset with proper data splits provides strong evidence for the model's effectiveness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the Grid-RNN architecture**: Grid-RNNs were introduced by Graves and Schmidhuber (2009) for other tasks (e.g., image recognition), and the paper doesn't provide a strong theoretical justification for why this architecture is particularly suitable for capturing multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS. The paper doesn't compare with other sequence-to-sequence approaches that could potentially achieve similar results (e.g., transformers, attention mechanisms), making it difficult to assess whether Grid-RNNs are truly necessary or just a reasonable choice.

2. **Insufficient comparison with recent neural approaches**: While the paper mentions Zhou and Xu (2015) for English SRL, it doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches for Japanese PAS that might have been published after their baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015). The authors should clarify how their work relates to these more recent neural approaches and why their specific architecture is superior.

3. **Unclear distinction from previous joint approach**: The paper doesn't sufficiently explain how their approach to multi-predicate interactions differs from the previous joint approach (Ouchi et al., 2015). Both approaches model multi-predicate interactions, but the previous approach relied on syntactic features while the authors' approach uses neural networks. The paper should provide a more detailed comparison of how their neural approach to multi-predicate interactions improves upon the previous joint approach.

4. **Lack of implementation details for practical assessment**: The paper lacks sufficient detail on computational complexity, training time, and inference time compared to previous approaches. These metrics are essential for assessing the practical utility of the proposed method, especially since the authors claim it avoids error propagation from pipeline processing.

5. **No discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't address limitations of their approach, such as performance on longer sentences, handling of more complex predicate interactions, or scalability to larger datasets. This omission weakens the paper's claim of being a state-of-the-art solution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your Grid-RNN architecture specifically address the limitations of previous approaches that relied on syntactic features for multi-predicate interactions? Can you provide a more detailed comparison with the previous joint approach (Ouchi et al., 2015)?

2. Have you considered alternative architectures for capturing multi-predicate interactions (e.g., transformers, attention mechanisms) that might be more efficient or effective than Grid-RNNs?

3. Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of your model compared to previous approaches, including training time and inference time?

4. How do you expect your model to perform on longer sentences or sentences with more complex predicate interactions that might require understanding beyond the current window size (C=5)?

5. The paper mentions that their approach can be applied to SRL, but doesn't provide any results for English SRL. Could you share any preliminary results or plans for applying this approach to English SRL?
