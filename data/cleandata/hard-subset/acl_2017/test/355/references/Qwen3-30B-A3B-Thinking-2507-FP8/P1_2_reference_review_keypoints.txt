- Paper tackles Japanese Predicate Argument Structure analysis, focusing on identifying omitted (zero) arguments without relying on syntactic parsers.  
- Proposes two neural models: a single-sequence RNN and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN capturing multi-predicate interactions.  
- Multi-sequence Grid-RNN yields state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, especially improving zero argument identification.  

Main contributions:
- Introduces Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions, notably improving zero argument identification.  
- Shows neural models can outperform parser-dependent approaches, avoiding error propagation from syntax.  
- Provides comprehensive experiments demonstrating effectiveness across case roles and argument types.  

Strengths:
- Addresses a challenging, practical problem in Japanese NLP and achieves a substantial F-measure gain for zero arguments.  
- Strong linguistic motivation and apt architectural choice to capture multi-predicate interactions.  
- Rigorous experimental evaluation with detailed case-role analyses and notable gains on the rare dative case.  
- Code is publicly released, supporting reproducibility.  

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient justification for preferring Grid-RNNs over other modern architectures (e.g., transformers/attention).  
- Lacks comparison with more recent neural PAS approaches post-2016, limiting claims of being state-of-the-art.  
- Missing detailed error analysis to explain failure modes for zero arguments.  
- No discussion of computational cost or scalability of the Grid-RNN model.  
- Does not address extension to inter-sentential zero pronouns, an important limitation.  

Questions for authors:
- Which recent (post-2016) neural baselines were considered, and why is the model still state-of-the-art?  
- Why are Grid-RNNs particularly suited for Japanese PAS compared to transformers or attention models?  
- How could the Grid-RNN be extended to handle inter-sentential zero pronouns and what challenges would that entail?  

Overall recommendation:
- Strong, well-executed paper with meaningful contributions and convincing results; recommend acceptance despite noted limitations.
