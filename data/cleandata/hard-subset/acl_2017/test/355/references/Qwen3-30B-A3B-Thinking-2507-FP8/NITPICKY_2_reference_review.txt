

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper

The paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on handling zero pronouns (arguments that are omitted in Japanese sentences). The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. They claim their models achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without relying on syntactic information.

## Primary Contributions

1. Neural models for Japanese PAS analysis that automatically induce features from word sequence information without requiring syntactic parsing.
2. A multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions, which they claim significantly improves zero argument identification.
3. Demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results for Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic information.

## Strengths

1. The problem of zero pronoun resolution in Japanese PAS is indeed challenging and important for semantic analysis of pro-drop languages. The paper correctly identifies this as a key issue in the literature.

2. The authors have made a reasonable effort to compare with previous state-of-the-art approaches (Ouchi et al., 2015) and demonstrate improvements, particularly for zero arguments (47.12% vs 46.10%).

3. The paper provides clear technical descriptions of their models, including the feature extraction process and network architecture.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Critical Formatting and Figure Inconsistencies (Major Issue)

The paper contains multiple critical formatting errors that undermine the clarity of the presentation:

- Figure 2 is described as showing "an overview of the neural models" but is referenced in Section 3 as showing the single-sequence model, while Figure 3 is actually the single-sequence model. This inconsistent figure numbering is confusing and unprofessional.

- The paper repeatedly references figures that are not included in the provided text (Figures 2, 4, 5, and 6). For example, "Figure 4 presents an example of the atomic features" appears without the actual figure. This is unacceptable in a peer-reviewed paper.

- In Section 4.2, they refer to "the right figure in Figure 6" but Figure 6 is not provided. This makes it impossible for readers to understand the Grid-RNN architecture.

### 2. Inaccurate and Misleading Results Reporting (Major Issue)

The paper makes claims about significant improvements that are not supported by the data:

- In Section 6.2, they state "the multi-sequence model significantly outperformed the single-sequence model in F-measure in total (81.42% vs 81.15%)." The difference is only 0.27%, which is extremely small. This is not a meaningful improvement.

- In Table 3, they claim "the two neural models achieved much higher results by around 30 points" for the dative case, but the actual difference between Ouchi+15 (24.43) and Single-Seq (35.07) is 10.64 points, not 30. This is a significant discrepancy that undermines credibility.

- For zero arguments, they report "the multi-sequence model significantly outperformed the single-sequence model by around 1.0 points in F-measure (46.10% vs 47.12%)." The difference is 1.02%, but they don't provide the statistical significance test results (p-value) for this specific comparison.

### 3. Technical Inaccuracies in Methodology (Major Issue)

- The description of bidirectional RNNs in Section 3.2 is incorrect: "Bi-RNNs process an input sequence from the left-to-right manner in odd-numbered layers and the opposite in even-numbered layers." This is not how bidirectional RNNs work. Bidirectional RNNs process sequences in both directions simultaneously, not alternating direction per layer.

- In Section 4.2, they describe Grid-RNNs as: "h(ℓ)m,t = g(ℓ)(h(ℓ−1)m,t ⊕ h(ℓ)m−1,t,h(ℓ)m,t−1) for ℓ = odd." However, they don't define what g(ℓ) is, and the equation doesn't match standard Grid-RNN or GRU equations. They later mention using GRUs, but the equation doesn't reflect this.

- In Section 6.1, they state "The dimensions dword and dmark of the embeddings are set to 32." This is an extremely small embedding dimension, which would severely limit model capacity. They don't justify this choice.

### 4. Reference Formatting Errors (Significant Issue)

The reference section contains multiple formatting errors:

- All references have incorrect punctuation: "Bastien et al\.,? 2012" should be "Bastien et al., 2012"
- The reference to "Carreras and Màrquez.,? \Q2005\E" has incorrect characters (\Q and \E)
- Multiple references have inconsistent punctuation, such as "Iida and Poesio.,? 2011" instead of "Iida and Poesio, 2011"

These errors are unprofessional and indicate a lack of attention to detail.

### 5. Inconsistent Terminology and Definitions (Significant Issue)

- In Section 2.1, they define "Zero" as "The arguments referred to by zero pronouns within the same sentence," but later in Section 2.2, they say "the nominative argument '男 i (mani)' for the predicate '逃走した (escaped)' is regarded as a Zero argument." However, in their own Figure 1, this argument is shown as having no direct syntactic dependency, which is consistent with their definition. But then they say "In this paper, we focus on the analysis for these intra-sentential arguments, i.e., Dep and Zero." This is confusing because "Inter-Zero" was defined as "The arguments referred to by zero pronouns out of the same sentence," yet they claim to focus on "Zero" which should include both intra-sentential and inter-sentential. The definition of "Zero" is inconsistent with their focus on intra-sentential analysis.

- In Section 6.1, they state "We do not use any external resources," but they don't clarify whether they're using pre-trained word embeddings (which would be considered external resources). This is misleading.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify the exact difference between "Zero" and "Inter-Zero" arguments in your analysis, given that you claim to focus on "intra-sentential arguments, i.e., Dep and Zero"?

2. The paper claims "the multi-sequence model significantly outperformed the single-sequence model by around 1.0 points in F-measure (46.10% vs 47.12%)" for zero arguments. What is the p-value for this comparison? The paper states they used "bootstrap resampling method" but doesn't provide the statistical significance test results.

3. In Table 3, you claim "the two neural models achieved much higher results by around 30 points" for the dative case, but the actual difference is about 10.64 points (from 24.43 to 35.07). Can you clarify this discrepancy?

## Overall Assessment

This paper has a potentially interesting idea for Japanese PAS analysis, but it is marred by numerous minor but critical flaws in formatting, technical details, and results reporting. The inconsistent figure references, inaccurate results reporting, technical inaccuracies in methodology, and formatting errors in references significantly undermine the credibility of the work.

The claimed improvements are marginal (0.27% for total F1), and the paper fails to provide sufficient statistical evidence for their significance claims. The technical description of the models contains errors that would make replication difficult.

As a perfectionist reviewer who meticulously examines every aspect of a paper, I find these issues substantial enough to warrant rejection. The paper would benefit from significant revision to address these formatting, technical, and reporting issues before it could be considered for publication.

## Recommendation: Reject

The paper's minor flaws are too pervasive and significant to overlook, even though the core idea is somewhat novel. The technical inaccuracies and misleading results reporting are particularly concerning.
