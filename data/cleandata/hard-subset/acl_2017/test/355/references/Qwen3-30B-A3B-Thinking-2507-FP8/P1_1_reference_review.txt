

# Peer Review: "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenging problem of zero argument identification (arguments omitted in Japanese sentences). The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that explicitly models interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The key claim is that modeling multi-predicate interactions significantly improves performance, particularly for zero arguments, without relying on syntactic information from parsers.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The multi-sequence model with Grid-RNNs that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis, demonstrating significant improvements (especially for zero arguments) over previous state-of-the-art approaches.
2. Demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance for Japanese PAS analysis without relying on syntactic information from parsers, which previous approaches heavily depended on.
3. Providing a detailed analysis showing that multi-predicate interactions are particularly beneficial for zero argument identification, which is one of the most challenging aspects of Japanese PAS.

## Strengths
1. **Compelling empirical results for zero arguments**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that their multi-sequence model achieves 47.12% F1 for zero arguments compared to 44.09% for the previous state-of-the-art (Ouchi et al., 2015), a significant improvement of 3.03 points. This directly addresses a well-known challenge in Japanese PAS analysis, and the authors provide a clear explanation of why multi-predicate interactions help specifically for zero arguments (which lack direct syntactic dependencies).

2. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, comparing against strong baselines, analyzing performance by case role (NOM, ACC, DAT), and using statistical significance testing (bootstrap resampling). The results are well-organized in Tables 1-3, with clear evidence that their approach outperforms previous work across all metrics.

3. **Clear architectural innovation**: The Grid-RNN architecture is well-explained and well-motivated for the specific task of capturing interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The paper clearly demonstrates how the Grid-RNNs connect between different predicate sequences, which is crucial for modeling multi-predicate interactions without syntactic information.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty of Grid-RNN architecture**: The Grid-RNN architecture itself (Graves & Schmidhuber, 2009) has been previously proposed, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain what novel aspects of Grid-RNNs they're applying to this specific task. The authors don't clearly differentiate their Grid-RNN implementation from prior work, making it difficult to assess the novelty of their architectural contribution.

2. **Insufficient comparison with related neural approaches**: While the paper discusses prior neural approaches (Shibata et al., 2016; Iida et al., 2016) in Section 5.3, it doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs in terms of architecture or feature engineering. The authors claim their model doesn't require syntactic information, but they don't sufficiently explain why previous neural approaches that did use syntactic information (like Shibata et al.) couldn't have achieved similar results without it.

3. **Lack of analysis on why Grid-RNNs work better than alternatives**: The paper claims Grid-RNNs are necessary for capturing multi-predicate interactions, but doesn't adequately compare with simpler alternatives (e.g., a single RNN over the entire sentence). Without this comparison, it's unclear whether the Grid-RNN structure is truly necessary or if a simpler approach would work just as well.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations and generalizability**: The paper doesn't address the limitations of their approach (e.g., performance on longer sentences, domain adaptation to non-newspaper text, computational complexity) or discuss how their approach might scale to other languages with similar zero pronoun phenomena.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your Grid-RNN implementation specifically differ from Graves and Schmidhuber's (2009) original Grid-RNN? What novel aspects of Grid-RNNs are you leveraging for Japanese PAS analysis?

2. Why not use a single RNN over the entire sentence (instead of multiple sequences with Grid-RNNs) to capture context across the whole sentence? How does the Grid-RNN approach specifically capture multi-predicate interactions better than a single sequence approach?

3. Have you considered testing your model on other Japanese corpora (e.g., novels, web text) beyond the NAIST Text Corpus (newspaper text)? How might your model's performance differ in domains with more complex syntax or longer sentences?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a well-motivated approach to a challenging problem in Japanese PAS analysis with compelling experimental results. The multi-sequence model with Grid-RNNs significantly improves zero argument identification, which is the most problematic aspect of Japanese PAS analysis. While the novelty of the Grid-RNN architecture itself is somewhat limited, the paper's application of this architecture to the specific problem of multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS is well-executed and demonstrates clear value. The thorough experimental evaluation and clear analysis of results make this a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the novelty concerns and provide more detailed comparisons with alternative architectures.
