

# ACL Review: "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenging problem of zero argument identification. The authors claim to introduce two models: a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that captures multi-predicate interactions. The main contributions as I see them are:

1. Proposing a neural model that avoids syntactic dependency parsing by learning from word sequences alone
2. Introducing a Grid-RNN architecture to model multi-predicate interactions
3. Demonstrating improved performance on zero argument identification compared to previous work

## Strengths

1. The problem of zero argument identification in Japanese PAS is well-motivated and important for the field. The authors correctly identify the challenge of argument omission in pro-drop languages and the limitations of current approaches that rely on syntactic information.

2. The experimental results show consistent improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods, particularly for zero arguments (3.03 points improvement for multi-sequence model), which validates their core claim that modeling multi-predicate interactions is beneficial for this specific challenge.

3. The paper makes a good effort to clarify the distinction between Japanese PAS analysis (dependency-based) and English SRL (constituent-based), which is important for readers familiar with SRL.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical missing figures and inconsistent figure references**: The paper repeatedly references figures (Figures 1-6) that are not included in the manuscript. This is a major issue for a technical paper that relies on visual explanations of the architecture (Figures 2, 3, 5, 6) and examples (Figures 1, 4). For instance, Section 1 mentions "Figure 2: An overview of the neural models" but no Figure 2 appears in the text. The absence of these figures makes it impossible to properly understand the proposed models, especially the Grid-RNN architecture.

2. **Inconsistent and messy equation formatting**: The paper contains numerous formatting errors in mathematical equations that undermine the technical rigor of the work. For example:
   - Equation (1): "xt = x arg t ⊕ x pred t ⊕ xmarkt" (should be x_{arg,t} ⊕ x_{pred,t} ⊕ x_{mark,t})
   - Equation (3): "h (ℓ) t = { g(ℓ)(h (ℓ−1) t , h (ℓ) t−1) (ℓ = odd) g(ℓ)(h (ℓ−1) t , h (ℓ) t+1) (ℓ = even)" (incorrect parentheses and inconsistent spacing)
   - Equation (5): "h (ℓ) m,t= { g(ℓ)(h (ℓ−1) m,t ⊕ h (ℓ) m−1,t,h (ℓ) m,t−1) (ℓ = odd)..." (inconsistent formatting of subscripts and superscripts)

3. **Inaccurate technical references and terminology**: The paper claims to use "Grid-RNNs" but references Graves and Schmidhuber (2009) who introduced "Grid Long Short-Term Memory" (Grid-LSTM), not Grid-RNN. This is a significant technical inaccuracy. Additionally, the paper states "we use the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) (Cho et al., 2014) as the function g(ℓ)" but then says "the parameters of GRUs are set to 32×32" - GRUs don't have parameters set to "32x32" (this is confusing and incorrect; it should refer to the hidden size being 32).

4. **Inconsistent and incorrect reference formatting**: The reference list contains numerous formatting errors, including:
   - "Bastien et al\.,? 2012" instead of "Bastien et al., 2012"
   - "Zhou and Xu.,? 2015" instead of "Zhou and Xu, 2015"
   - "Theano: new features and speed improvements, Frédéric Bastien, Pascal Lamblin, Razvan Pascanu, James Bergstra, Ian J. Goodfellow, Arnaud Bergeron, Nicolas Bouchard, Yoshua Bengio., Deep Learning and Unsupervised Feature Learning, Bastien et al\.,? 2012, Bastien et al\.,"
   - The reference to "Grid Long Short-Term Memory" (Kalchbrenner et al., 2016) is incorrectly cited as "Grid-RNNs" throughout the paper.

5. **Inaccurate claim of "state-of-the-art" results**: The paper claims to achieve "state-of-the-art" results but only compares against non-neural baselines (Ouchi et al., 2015; Imamura et al., 2009). The authors mention that "Shibata et al. (2016)" used neural networks, but they don't compare against this neural baseline. The paper states "We demonstrate that our neural models exceed the accuracy of the state-of-the-art Japanese PAS analyzer (Ouchi et al., 2015)," but Ouchi et al. (2015) is not a neural model, so this is misleading.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify the relationship between "Grid-RNNs" and the "Grid Long Short-Term Memory" (Grid-LSTM) introduced by Graves and Schmidhuber (2009)? The paper consistently refers to "Grid-RNNs" but the cited work is about Grid-LSTM.

2. Could you provide the missing figures (Figures 1-6) to properly illustrate the models and examples? Without these, it's impossible to evaluate the proposed architecture.

3. The paper states "the multi-sequence model significantly outperformed the single-sequence model in F-measure in total (81.42% vs 81.15%)." However, the difference of 0.27 percentage points is very small, and the paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for this difference. Could you clarify whether this difference is statistically significant?

4. The paper claims "our neural models realize the state-of-the-art performance by using word sequence information of a sentence" but doesn't compare against other neural approaches (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016). Could you explain why this comparison is missing?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem in Japanese PAS analysis and presents some promising results, the numerous technical and formatting flaws undermine the credibility of the work. The missing figures, inconsistent equation formatting, inaccurate technical references, and incorrect claims about state-of-the-art results are serious issues that would require significant revisions before the paper could be considered for publication. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance given the fundamental issues with the paper's presentation and technical accuracy.

The paper's contribution is potentially valuable, but the execution and presentation are so flawed that the contribution cannot be properly evaluated. The authors would need to completely revise the paper, fix all formatting issues, provide missing figures, and properly compare against all relevant neural baselines before it could be reconsidered.
