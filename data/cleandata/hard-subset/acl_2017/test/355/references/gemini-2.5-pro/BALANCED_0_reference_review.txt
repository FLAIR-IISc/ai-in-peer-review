### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a particular focus on identifying omitted arguments (zero anaphora), a key challenge in pro-drop languages. The authors propose two neural sequence models that operate directly on word sequences, avoiding the need for syntactic features and thus mitigating error propagation from parsers. The first model, a "single-sequence" model, uses stacked Bi-RNNs to analyze each predicate independently. The main contribution is the "multi-sequence" model, which employs a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly model all predicates in a sentence. This novel architecture is designed to capture semantic interactions between multiple predicates, which is hypothesized to be a crucial source of information for resolving difficult PAS cases.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel Grid-RNN architecture for joint PAS analysis**: The primary contribution is the application of Grid-RNNs to simultaneously model all predicate-argument structures within a sentence. This provides a principled, end-to-end method for learning and leveraging inter-predicate dependencies directly from text.
2.  **State-of-the-art results without syntactic features**: The paper demonstrates that a purely sequence-based neural approach can outperform previous state-of-the-art systems (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015) which rely heavily on syntactic information. This is a significant finding that points towards simpler and more robust models for this task.
3.  **Strong empirical evidence for the value of multi-predicate modeling**: The experiments provide compelling evidence that explicitly modeling interactions between predicates yields significant performance gains, particularly for the challenging task of identifying omitted ("Zero") arguments.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture**: The proposal to use Grid-RNNs to model the interactions between predicates is both novel for this task and linguistically well-motivated. The architecture elegantly extends a standard 1D sequence model (processing words) into a 2D grid (processing words and predicates), allowing information to flow both along the sentence and across different predicate contexts. The strong empirical results, especially the significant F-measure improvement on "Zero" arguments, strongly support the claim that this architecture effectively captures these useful interactions.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Results**: The paper achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5. The proposed multi-sequence model outperforms the previous best system by over 2 F1 points overall. Crucially, the gains are most pronounced on the difficult "Zero" argument subtask (+3.03 F1 over Ouchi et al., 2015), directly validating the paper's core hypothesis. The improvements are shown to be statistically significant, adding confidence to the results.
3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation**: The experimental setup is clear and comprehensive. The models are compared against strong and relevant baselines. The ablation study contrasting the single-sequence and multi-sequence models effectively isolates the benefit of the joint modeling component. Furthermore, the analysis of model depth and the inclusion of residual connections add to the paper's empirical value and provide useful insights for practitioners. The detailed breakdown of results by argument type and case role (Tables 1 and 3) strengthens the analysis.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation**: The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, and the differences between the single-sequence and multi-sequence models are explained effectively, aided by clear diagrams (e.g., Figure 6).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis**: While the quantitative results are excellent, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a qualitative analysis that provides insight into *how* the multi-sequence model is using the inter-predicate information. The central claim is that the model captures "multi-predicate interactions," but the paper does not offer concrete evidence of this beyond the aggregate performance numbers. Including an example sentence where the single-sequence model fails but the multi-sequence model succeeds in identifying a challenging zero argument would make the contribution much more tangible and convincing.
2.  **No Discussion of Computational Cost and Scalability**: The multi-sequence model processes a `T x M` grid for a sentence with `T` tokens and `M` predicates. This appears to be substantially more computationally expensive than the single-sequence baseline, especially during training. The paper does not discuss the practical trade-offs in terms of training time or inference speed. This is an important consideration, as the number of predicates in a sentence can vary widely, and efficiency is a key factor for real-world applications.
3.  **Potential Sensitivity to Predicate Ordering**: The Grid-RNN processes the predicate dimension sequentially (from `m-1` to `m`), which implies that the order of predicates matters. The paper does not specify how this order is determined (e.g., is it their linear order of appearance in the sentence?). The model's performance could be sensitive to this design choice, as the information flow across predicates is directional. This aspect of the model should be clarified, and if possible, its impact should be discussed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a qualitative analysis of one or two examples where the multi-sequence model correctly identifies a "Zero" argument that the single-sequence model misses? This would be very helpful for understanding the concrete benefits of modeling predicate interactions.
2.  Could you comment on the computational complexity and practical inference speed of the multi-sequence model compared to the single-sequence model, especially for sentences with a large number of predicates?
3.  The Grid-RNN architecture processes predicates in a specific sequence. How is the order of predicates for the grid (`m=1...M`) determined? Have you investigated the model's sensitivity to this ordering?
