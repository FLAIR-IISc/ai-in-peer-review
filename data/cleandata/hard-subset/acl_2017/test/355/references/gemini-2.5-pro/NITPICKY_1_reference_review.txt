### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes two neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, a task complicated by frequent argument omission. The primary contribution is a "Multi-Sequence Model" that uses a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly model arguments for all predicates within a sentence, aiming to capture inter-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic features. This is compared against a simpler "Single-Sequence Model" using a standard Bi-RNN for each predicate independently. Both models are evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus, where they are shown to outperform previous state-of-the-art methods that depend on syntactic parsers.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that a purely sequence-based neural model, foregoing syntactic features, can achieve state-of-the-art results on Japanese PAS, thereby avoiding the error propagation inherent in syntax-reliant pipelines.
2.  The application of a Grid-RNN architecture to explicitly model interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence, which yields a small but statistically significant improvement over a strong single-predicate neural baseline.
3.  A comprehensive set of experiments on a standard benchmark, including comparisons against strong baselines and ablations on model depth and the use of residual connections.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves new state-of-the-art results on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 benchmark. The improvements over the previous best model (Ouchi et al., 2015) are substantial, particularly for the challenging task of identifying `Zero` arguments (+3.03 F1 for the Multi-Sequence model). This is a clear and valuable contribution.
2.  **Elimination of Syntactic Dependency:** A key strength is achieving these results without any syntactic information. By relying solely on word sequences, the model is simpler, more end-to-end, and avoids the well-known problem of error propagation from upstream parsers, a weakness of prior work that the authors correctly identify.
3.  **Well-Motivated Architecture:** The core idea of modeling multi-predicate interactions is linguistically well-founded and has been explored in prior work. The application of a Grid-RNN to implement this idea neurally is an elegant and appropriate architectural choice. The paper does a good job of building intuition by first presenting a simpler single-sequence model and then extending it to the multi-sequence case.
4.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The experiments analyzing the effect of network depth and residual connections (Section 6.2, "Effects of Network Depth") add value to the paper, demonstrating that deeper models with residual connections perform better and confirming that the authors have properly explored the model space.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Methodological Precision:** Several critical details of the methodology are either missing or unclear, hindering reproducibility.
    *   **Predicate Ordering:** The Grid-RNN in the Multi-Sequence model processes M predicate-specific sequences in a grid. The hidden state `h(ℓ)m,t` depends on `h(ℓ)m−1,t`, implying a fixed order of predicates. The paper never specifies how predicates are ordered (e.g., by appearance in the sentence, alphabetically). This is a crucial, unstated implementation detail that could affect performance.
    *   **Feature Representation:** The description of the input features in Section 3.1 is vague. For the `PRED` feature, it is unclear how the "word index of the target predicate and words around the predicate" are combined into the vector `x_pred_t` for each word `w_t` in the sequence. Is it a concatenation of C+1 embeddings? This lack of clarity is a significant flaw.
    *   **Figure Clarity:** Figure 2 is poorly labeled and highly abstract, failing to effectively communicate the architectural differences between the models. Figure 6 is similarly difficult to parse; the "blue curve lines" are not sufficiently illustrative of the information flow.

2.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of formatting errors, suggesting a lack of careful preparation.
    *   **Missing Table:** The text explicitly refers to "Table 2" in the "Effects of Network Depth" subsection, but this table is entirely absent from the manuscript. This is a major omission that leaves key results unsubstantiated.
    *   **Bibliography:** The reference section is riddled with errors. Multiple entries contain question marks, garbled BibTeX commands (e.g., `Bastien et al\.,?`, `\Q2005\E`), and inconsistent formatting. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference publication.
    *   **Excessive/Poor Use of Footnotes:** Critical information is often relegated to footnotes. For instance, the fact that the function `g(l)` is a GRU is hidden in footnote 3. Footnote 6 contains a lengthy explanation about the dataset versions that would be better integrated into the main text. This makes the paper difficult to read smoothly.

3.  **Overstated Significance of Key Result:** While the Multi-Sequence model's improvement over the Single-Sequence model is statistically significant, the absolute gain is quite small (81.15 to 81.42 F1). The authors claim this "demonstrates that the grid-type neural architecture can effectively capture the multi-predicate interactions." This conclusion feels overstated given the modest gain. The paper would be stronger with a qualitative analysis or examples showing exactly how the multi-predicate modeling helps correct errors made by the single-sequence model.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide the missing Table 2, which shows the results for models with different network depths and with/without residual connections?
2.  How are the M predicates in a sentence ordered before being fed into the Grid-RNN? Have you experimented with different ordering schemes, and does the model's performance depend on this choice?
3.  Could you provide a concrete example of how the feature vector `x_t` is constructed, particularly the `x_pred_t` component, for a word `w_t` that is not the predicate and is not within the predicate's context window?
4.  Given the small F1-score improvement of the Multi-Sequence model over the Single-Sequence model, can you provide a qualitative example of a sentence where the Multi-Sequence model correctly identifies an argument that the Single-Sequence model misses, and attribute this success to the modeling of inter-predicate interactions?
