### Summary of the paper and main contributions:

This paper proposes a neural approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenging problem of identifying omitted (zero) arguments. The authors argue that previous state-of-the-art methods rely heavily on syntactic features, making them vulnerable to parser errors. To address this, they propose two models that operate directly on word sequences. The first is a baseline stacked Bi-RNN model that analyzes each predicate independently. The primary contribution is the second model, a "multi-sequence" architecture based on a Grid-RNN, which jointly processes all predicates in a sentence simultaneously. This grid structure allows information to flow between the predicate-specific processing streams, aiming to capture multi-predicate interactions without explicit syntactic information. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that both models outperform the previous state-of-the-art, with the Grid-RNN model achieving the best results, particularly on zero argument identification.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  The novel application of a Grid-RNN to explicitly model multi-predicate interactions for PAS analysis in a fully end-to-end, non-syntactic framework. This provides a new and effective architectural approach to a known linguistic challenge.
2.  A new state-of-the-art result on a standard Japanese PAS benchmark (NAIST Text Corpus 1.5), achieved without relying on any syntactic features. This result challenges the prevailing assumption that high-quality syntactic parsing is a prerequisite for this task.

### Strengths:

1.  **Elegant and Principled Method for Modeling Interactions:** The core idea of using a Grid-RNN is a very compelling way to operationalize the linguistic intuition of multi-predicate interactions. Previous joint models (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015) captured these dependencies through complex feature engineering and joint inference over a syntax tree. This paper's approach is more direct and integrated into the network architecture itself. By creating parallel sequences for each predicate and connecting them via the grid's recurrent updates, the model has a clear mechanism to learn and propagate interaction information, while simultaneously avoiding the brittleness of a pipeline system.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear and significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art system. The head-to-head comparison in Table 1 shows a >2 point F1 improvement overall, which is substantial. Crucially, the gains are most pronounced for zero arguments (+3 F1), the very phenomenon the model was designed to address. The controlled comparison between the Single-Sequence and Multi-Sequence models provides strong evidence that the grid architecture is indeed the source of the improvement, validating the central hypothesis of the paper.
3.  **Clarity and High Potential for Impact:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The models are explained with clarity, supported by helpful diagrams (Figures 3 and 6). The motivation is clear, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are presented transparently. By demonstrating that a complex semantic task can be tackled effectively without syntactic parsers, this work could influence future research not only in Japanese PAS but also in SRL and related semantic analysis tasks for other languages, especially those that are pro-drop or have relatively free word order. The authors' plan to release the code further enhances its potential impact.

### Weaknesses:

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper offers no insight into *what* kinds of multi-predicate interactions the Grid-RNN model is actually learning. The central claim is that the grid architecture "effectively captures" these interactions, but this is only supported by the aggregate F1 score. A qualitative analysis showing a concrete example where the Multi-Sequence model corrects an error made by the Single-Sequence model would be invaluable. For instance, does it learn that arguments are shared between related verbs? Visualizing attention or hidden state differences could provide concrete evidence and move the contribution beyond "this architecture works" to "this is *how* and *why* it works."
2.  **Scalability of the Grid-RNN Architecture is Not Discussed:** The computational complexity of the Grid-RNN is proportional to the sentence length multiplied by the number of predicates (M). While M might be small on average in the newspaper corpus used, the paper does not discuss the model's behavior or limitations as M grows. What is the performance and computational cost for sentences with 5, 8, or more predicates? This is a practical concern that limits the understanding of the model's general applicability. A brief discussion of these complexity/scalability trade-offs would make the work more complete.
3.  **Input Feature Representation is Potentially Under-explored:** The model achieves impressive results with very minimal input features (word, predicate position, predicate marker). This is a strength, but it also raises questions. Japanese has overt case-marking particles (e.g., が -ga for NOM, を -wo for ACC) that provide strong local cues for argument roles. While the RNN can implicitly learn from them as part of the word sequence, it's not clear if this is optimal. It would be interesting to know if explicitly incorporating features for these particles or other morphological information (like PoS tags) was considered or tested. A brief ablation or discussion on this point would help clarify whether the current minimal feature set is truly sufficient or if there is still untapped potential from simple, non-syntactic linguistic features.

### Questions to Authors:

1.  Could you provide a qualitative analysis of a specific example from the development or test set where the Multi-Sequence model correctly identifies a zero argument that the Single-Sequence model misses? It would be particularly insightful to see how the inter-sequence connections in the Grid-RNN might have contributed to the correct prediction.
2.  Regarding scalability, what is the distribution of the number of predicates per sentence in the NAIST corpus? Have you analyzed the model's performance or computational time on the subset of sentences with a high number of predicates? Is there a practical upper limit on the number of predicates the model can handle effectively?
3.  You successfully replaced the complex syntactic features of prior work with learned representations. Did you experiment with including any simple, non-parsed linguistic features, such as embeddings for case-marking particles or part-of-speech tags, in the input layer? Do you believe such features would be redundant given the Bi-RNN's ability to model sequence context, or could they offer further improvements?
