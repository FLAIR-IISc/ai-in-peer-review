**Summary of the paper**
This paper addresses the task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenge of identifying omitted arguments (zero arguments). The authors propose two neural sequence models that operate directly on word sequences, thereby avoiding reliance on syntactic parsers and the associated error propagation. The first model, a "single-sequence model," uses a stacked bidirectional RNN to identify arguments for each predicate independently. The primary contribution is the "multi-sequence model," which employs a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly model all predicates in a sentence. This architecture is designed to capture interactions between predicates by propagating information not only along the time axis of the sentence but also across the different predicate-specific sequences. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that both models outperform previous syntax-based state-of-the-art systems, with the multi-sequence model achieving the best results, particularly on challenging zero arguments.

**Contributions**
From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  The application of a Grid-RNN to explicitly and jointly model interactions among all predicates within a sentence for PAS analysis. This presents a novel neural architecture for capturing a linguistically motivated phenomenon.
2.  A demonstration that a purely sequence-based neural model, without any syntactic features, can achieve state-of-the-art performance on a standard Japanese PAS benchmark. This is a valuable result, as it offers a simpler, more robust alternative to complex pipeline systems.

**Strengths**
1.  **Well-motivated and Novel Architecture:** The core proposal of using a Grid-RNN to model multi-predicate interactions is conceptually elegant. It directly operationalizes the linguistic intuition that predicates in a sentence are semantically related. The architecture, which passes hidden states between parallel predicate-specific sequences, is a sound and novel approach for this specific problem.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates strong results, surpassing the previous best-performing model on a standard dataset (NAIST 1.5). The gains are consistent across argument types (Dep, Zero) and are particularly pronounced for zero arguments, which is the key challenge the work aims to address. This provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed methods.
3.  **Independence from Syntactic Parsers:** By achieving state-of-the-art results using only word sequence information, the work successfully decouples PAS analysis from syntactic parsing. This is a significant advantage, as it eliminates a major source of error propagation found in previous systems and simplifies the overall processing pipeline.
4.  **Clear Analysis:** The authors provide a helpful breakdown of performance by argument type and case role (Tables 1 and 3). The analysis correctly highlights the model's particular strength in zero argument identification and connects this improvement to the architecture's ability to capture long-distance context and predicate interactions.

**Weaknesses**
Despite the interesting contributions, this submission is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from critical omissions to a general lack of polish, which collectively undermine my confidence in the work.

1.  **Severe Presentation and Formatting Deficiencies:** The paper exhibits a concerning lack of careful preparation.
    *   **Missing Table:** The text in Section 6.2 ("Effects of Network Depth") explicitly refers to and discusses "Table 2," which is supposed to show the impact of network depth and residual connections. This table is completely absent from the paper. This is a critical omission, as these results are used to justify key architectural choices and cannot be verified.
    *   **Malformed References:** The bibliography is unusable and appears to be an uncorrected output from a malfunctioning BibTeX process. Entries contain stray characters (`?`), un-rendered LaTeX commands (`\Q`, `\E`), and bizarre repetitions (e.g., "Bastien et al\.,? 2012, Bastien et al\.,"). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Minor Errors:** There are numerous typos and grammatical mistakes throughout the text (e.g., "as an solution" in Sec 2.2, "significent test" in Sec 6.2). Key model details, such as the use of GRUs, are relegated to a footnote (Footnote 3) instead of being stated clearly in the main text. The source code URL is an unprofessional placeholder (`http:xxx`).

2.  **Ambiguous or Unjustified Methodological Choices:**
    *   The "stacked Bi-RNN" described in Section 3.2 is non-standard. The layers alternate between forward and backward processing, which is less expressive than the conventional approach of having each layer consist of both a forward and a backward pass whose outputs are concatenated. This design choice is not explained or justified.
    *   The dimensionality of the embeddings and hidden states (`d_word`=32, GRU hidden size=32) is exceptionally small compared to typical values in contemporary NLP research. While this may be sufficient, it raises questions about whether the model's capacity is a limiting factor. A justification for these choices or an ablation study would be beneficial.

3.  **Limitations of the Interaction Model:** The Grid-RNN architecture propagates information between predicates in a fixed, linear order (presumably their order of appearance in the sentence). This imposes a potentially arbitrary sequential structure on predicate interactions, which may not reflect their true semantic relationships. The authors do not discuss this limitation or investigate the sensitivity of the model to predicate ordering.

4.  **Incremental Performance Gains:** While the results represent a new state-of-the-art, the improvement of the main multi-sequence model over its own single-sequence baseline is quite small (81.42% vs. 81.15% F1). Though statistically significant, this narrow margin slightly tempers the impact of the central claim regarding the benefit of the grid architecture.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please provide the missing Table 2? This is essential for evaluating your claims about the benefits of deeper networks and residual connections.
2.  What is the justification for your non-standard implementation of stacked Bi-RNNs (alternating directions per layer) as opposed to the more common architecture where each layer is fully bidirectional? Did you experiment with the latter?
3.  The Grid-RNN processes predicates in a fixed linear order. Have you analyzed the effect of this ordering on your results? For instance, does reversing or shuffling the predicate order impact performance?
