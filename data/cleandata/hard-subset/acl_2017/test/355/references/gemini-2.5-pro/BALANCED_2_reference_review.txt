### Reviewer Comments

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a particular focus on identifying omitted "zero" arguments, a key challenge in pro-drop languages. The authors propose two neural sequence models that operate directly on word sequences, thereby avoiding the error propagation inherent in previous state-of-the-art systems that rely on syntactic parsers. The first model, a "Single-Sequence" Bi-RNN, analyzes each predicate independently. The main contribution is the second model, a "Multi-Sequence" architecture based on Grid-RNNs, which jointly analyzes all predicates in a sentence. This architecture is designed to capture interactions between predicates by allowing information to flow both along the word sequence and across the different predicate-specific sequences. Experiments on the standard NAIST Text Corpus show that both models outperform previous syntax-based systems, and that the proposed Multi-Sequence model achieves a new state-of-the-art, with significant gains on the difficult task of zero argument identification.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Application of Grid-RNNs for Multi-Predicate Interaction:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture that uses Grid-RNNs to jointly model all predicate-argument structures within a sentence. This provides an elegant, end-to-end method for capturing the dependencies between predicates, a linguistic intuition that previous work has shown to be important but has typically addressed using complex, feature-engineered joint models.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance without Syntactic Features:** The paper demonstrates that a purely sequence-based neural model can surpass previous state-of-the-art results on a standard Japanese PAS benchmark. This is a significant finding, as it shows that explicit syntactic information, while helpful, is not necessary to achieve top performance and that its benefits can be outweighed by the costs of pipeline-induced errors.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The work is supported by a strong set of experiments. The inclusion of the Single-Sequence model serves as a perfect ablation study, clearly isolating and quantifying the benefit of the multi-predicate modeling in the Grid-RNN. The detailed analysis, breaking down results by argument type (Dep vs. Zero) and case role, provides convincing evidence for the model's effectiveness on the most challenging aspects of the task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and New SOTA:** The paper sets a new state-of-the-art on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5. The improvement over the previous best model (Ouchi et al., 2015) is substantial, especially on the most difficult part of the task: identifying zero arguments (an F1-score improvement of ~3 points, from 44.09 to 47.12). This is a clear and compelling reason for acceptance.
2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The core idea of using a Grid-RNN to model the interactions between predicates is conceptually elegant. It directly operationalizes the linguistic insight that predicates in a sentence are semantically related. The model design, which processes M parallel sequences for M predicates and allows information to flow between them, is a natural fit for the problem and avoids the brittleness of manually engineered features for joint inference.
3.  **High-Quality Analysis and Experimental Design:** The paper's experimental design is very strong. Comparing the Multi-Sequence model to the Single-Sequence model is a crucial ablation that provides direct evidence for the benefits of modeling predicate interactions. Furthermore, the detailed breakdown of performance on Dep vs. Zero arguments, and across different case roles (Table 3), lends significant weight to the authors' claims. For instance, the large gains on zero arguments confirm that modeling sentence-wide context and predicate interactions is particularly beneficial where local syntactic cues are absent.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to understand. The model architectures are explained well, aided by clear diagrams. The authors' commitment to releasing their source code is a significant strength that will benefit the community and ensure the work is reproducible.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong and support the claim that the Grid-RNN captures multi-predicate interactions, the paper would be more insightful with a qualitative analysis. A case study showing an example sentence where the Multi-Sequence model succeeds but the Single-Sequence model fails would provide a concrete illustration of the kinds of interactions being learned. This would help the reader understand not just *that* the model works better, but potentially *why* it works better from a linguistic perspective.
2.  **Limited Discussion of Alternative Architectures:** The Grid-RNN is a clever choice, but it is not the only way to model interactions between parallel sequences. A discussion of alternatives, such as using attention mechanisms (e.g., allowing each word in a predicate's sequence to attend to the hidden states of other predicate sequences) or graph neural networks, would strengthen the paper. A brief justification for choosing the Grid-RNN over these other possibilities would help contextualize the architectural contribution more broadly.
3.  **Dependence on Predicate Ordering:** The Grid-RNN architecture processes the predicate dimension sequentially (from `m=1` to `M` in the odd layers). This implies that the order in which predicates are presented to the model could affect performance. The paper does not specify what ordering is used (e.g., linear order of appearance in the sentence) or whether this choice was investigated. If the model is sensitive to this ordering, it would be an important detail to discuss.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a qualitative example from your test set that highlights the difference between the Single-Sequence and Multi-Sequence models? Specifically, a case where the Multi-Sequence model correctly identifies a difficult zero argument that the Single-Sequence model misses, and if possible, hypothesize about what interaction the model might have captured.
2.  The Grid-RNN imposes a specific, recurrent structure on the predicate dimension. Did you consider or experiment with other mechanisms for modeling inter-predicate interactions, such as an attention layer applied across the predicate dimension? What do you see as the primary advantages of the Grid-RNN for this specific task?
3.  How is the order of the M predicates for the M input sequences determined? Is it simply their linear order of appearance in the sentence? Did you investigate the sensitivity of the model to this ordering? Given the directional recurrence in the Grid-RNN's predicate dimension, it seems this could have an impact on the results.
