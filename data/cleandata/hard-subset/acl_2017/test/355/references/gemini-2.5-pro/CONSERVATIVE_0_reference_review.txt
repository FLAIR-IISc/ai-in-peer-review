### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural sequence modeling approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis. The primary goal is to address the challenge of identifying omitted arguments (zero arguments) in a pro-drop language without relying on syntactic features from external parsers, thereby avoiding error propagation.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The successful application of a stacked Bi-RNN model (Single-Sequence) to the Japanese PAS task, which, despite its relative simplicity, achieves state-of-the-art results by operating solely on word sequences.
2.  The introduction of a novel "Multi-Sequence" model using a Grid-RNN architecture. This model aims to explicitly capture interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence by processing them jointly in a grid structure.
3.  An empirical demonstration on the standard NAIST Text Corpus that these neural models, particularly the multi-sequence variant, outperform previous state-of-the-art systems that rely heavily on syntactic features. The improvements are most pronounced for the difficult task of identifying zero arguments.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results on a standard benchmark. The proposed models outperform the previous state-of-the-art joint model (Ouchi et al., 2015) by a considerable margin (+2.19 F1 points for the Multi-Sequence model). Achieving a new state-of-the-art is a significant accomplishment.

2.  **Elimination of Syntactic Dependency:** The models achieve these strong results without any syntactic information (e.g., dependency parses). This is a valuable contribution, as it simplifies the processing pipeline and makes the system robust to parsing errors, which are a known source of performance degradation in traditional systems. The success of this approach suggests that for this task, deep neural networks can effectively learn relevant structural cues directly from word sequences.

3.  **Effective Handling of Zero Arguments:** The paper demonstrates a notable improvement in identifying zero arguments, which is arguably the most challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis. The Multi-Sequence model improves F-measure on this subtask by over 3 points compared to the previous SOTA. This shows the model's effectiveness in capturing the long-range contextual information necessary for anaphora resolution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Marginal Improvement from the Main Technical Contribution:** The central technical novelty of the paper is the multi-sequence Grid-RNN model for capturing multi-predicate interactions. However, the performance gain of this complex model over the much simpler and more established single-sequence Bi-RNN model is very small (81.42 vs. 81.15 F1). A gain of 0.27 F1 points, while statistically significant, is practically minor. This result significantly weakens the claim that the Grid-RNN architecture is a key factor for success. It appears that the vast majority of the improvement over prior work stems from applying a standard Bi-RNN architecture, rather than the proposed multi-predicate interaction mechanism.

2.  **Insufficient Justification and Analysis of the Grid-RNN:** The paper claims that the Grid-RNN "effectively captures the multi-predicate interactions," but this claim is not supported by any analysis beyond the small F1-score improvement. The mechanism for interaction is under-explained. For instance, the predicates are fed into the grid based on their linear order in the sentence (`m=1...M`). This imposes an arbitrary sequential dependency between predicates. The authors do not justify this design choice or investigate potential biases it might introduce. The paper would be much stronger if it included a qualitative analysis or case study demonstrating *how* the inter-sequence connections in the grid help resolve a difficult case that the single-sequence model gets wrong. Without this, the proposed architecture remains an un-interrogated black box.

3.  **Limited Novelty of the Baseline Model:** The single-sequence model, which provides most of the performance gains, is a direct application of the stacked Bi-RNN architecture, very similar to the model of Zhou and Xu (2015) for English SRL. While applying an established method to a new language and task is a valid contribution, the paper frames the Grid-RNN as its primary innovation, which, as noted above, has a limited empirical impact. The paper's contribution could be more accurately described as demonstrating the power of standard RNNs on this task, with a minor extension that provides a marginal boost.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The multi-sequence model processes predicates in their linear order of appearance in the sentence. Have you explored whether this ordering impacts performance? For example, would reversing the order or using a random order during training change the results? This would help clarify whether the model is learning meaningful interactions or simply benefiting from a more heavily parameterized architecture.

2.  Given that the complex Grid-RNN architecture provides only a marginal 0.27 F1 improvement over the simpler Bi-RNN, how do you justify the added complexity? Does this small gain suggest that explicit modeling of multi-predicate interactions, at least in this manner, is less crucial than strong contextual encoding within a single predicate's scope, as provided by the standard Bi-RNN?

3.  Could you provide a concrete example from the test set where the multi-sequence model correctly identifies an argument (preferably a Zero argument) that the single-sequence model fails on? A brief analysis of what information might have been passed between the predicate sequences to enable this correction would provide much-needed evidence for your central claim about capturing multi-predicate interactions.
