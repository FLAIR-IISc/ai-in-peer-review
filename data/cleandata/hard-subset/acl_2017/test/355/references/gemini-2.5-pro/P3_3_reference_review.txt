### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis for Japanese, with a particular focus on resolving omitted arguments (zero anaphora), a known challenge in pro-drop languages. The authors argue that previous state-of-the-art methods rely on joint modeling of multiple predicates but are hampered by their dependence on syntactic parsers, which can introduce errors. To overcome this, the paper proposes two end-to-end neural models that operate directly on word sequences. The first is a baseline "Single-Sequence" model using stacked bidirectional RNNs (Bi-GRUs) to identify arguments for each predicate independently. The main contribution is the "Multi-Sequence" model, which employs a Grid-RNN to create a 2D computational grid over words and predicates. This architecture is designed to explicitly model the interactions between all predicates in a sentence by allowing information to flow not only along the word sequence but also across the predicate sequences. Experiments on the standard NAIST Text Corpus show that both models outperform previous syntax-based systems, and the Multi-Sequence model achieves a new state-of-the-art, with particularly strong improvements on identifying zero arguments.

### Contributions
1.  **A Novel Application of Grid-RNNs for Multi-Predicate Modeling:** The primary contribution is the elegant formulation of the multi-predicate PAS problem as a 2D grid (words × predicates) and the use of a Grid-RNN to process it. This is a well-motivated architectural choice that directly models the linguistic intuition of inter-predicate dependencies without needing explicit feature engineering or syntactic structures.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance without Syntactic Features:** The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration that an end-to-end neural model can surpass previous state-of-the-art joint models that heavily rely on gold or predicted syntactic parse information. This result is significant for the field of Japanese PAS analysis, as it demonstrates a path to building simpler, more robust systems that are not bottlenecked by parser performance.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The work is supported by a solid experimental setup, including a strong neural baseline (the Single-Sequence model), direct comparison with the previous SOTA on a standard benchmark, and insightful ablations. The analysis of performance by argument type (Dep vs. Zero) and case role clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed models, especially for the most challenging aspects of the task.

### Strengths
1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Architecture:** The core idea of using a Grid-RNN to capture interactions is compelling. It naturally maps the problem structure—multiple related sequence labeling tasks within a single sentence—onto a suitable neural architecture. The justification for moving away from syntax-dependent pipelines is clear and addresses a well-known problem of error propagation.
2.  **Significant Empirical Improvements:** The paper reports substantial gains over strong baselines. An overall F-measure improvement of over 2 points (79.23 to 81.42) on a standard dataset is a significant advance. Crucially, the largest improvements are seen on the most difficult part of the task: Zero argument identification (+3.03 F1 for Multi-Seq vs. Ouchi+15). This provides strong evidence that the proposed architecture is indeed capturing useful information for this phenomenon.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The models are described with sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is sound. The authors' decision to make their code publicly available is commendable and greatly contributes to the reproducibility and potential impact of the work.
4.  **Strong Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting top-line numbers. The breakdown of results by argument type (Table 1) and case role (Table 3) provides valuable insights. The experiment on network depth and residual connections (Table 2) is good practice and reinforces the soundness of the final model design.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results strongly suggest that the Grid-RNN is capturing multi-predicate interactions, the paper offers no qualitative analysis or examples to illustrate *how* this happens. The model remains a black box. A case study on a complex sentence, showing how the representations or predictions for one predicate's arguments are influenced by the presence of another, would have made the claims much more concrete and insightful.
2.  **Simple Input Features:** The model relies on randomly initialized embeddings for words and a few marker features. While achieving SOTA with such simple inputs is impressive, it is standard practice to leverage pre-trained word embeddings, which often provide a significant boost. The lack of experiments with pre-trained embeddings (e.g., from word2vec, fastText, or more recent contextual models for Japanese) is a missed opportunity. It's unclear if the gains from the architecture would hold, diminish, or increase when combined with stronger initial representations.
3.  **Ambiguity in Predicate Ordering:** The Grid-RNN processes predicates along an ordered dimension `m`. The paper does not specify how this order is determined. Is it the linear order of predicates in the sentence? Is it arbitrary? The performance of a directional RNN structure like this can be sensitive to the processing order. This methodological detail is important and its omission leaves a key aspect of the model underspecified.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding Weakness 3: How are the M predicates ordered when constructing the input for the Grid-RNN? Is the ordering based on their appearance in the sentence? Have you investigated whether the model's performance is sensitive to this ordering?
2.  Could you provide a concrete example from the test set where the Multi-Sequence model correctly identifies a Zero argument that the Single-Sequence model misses? A brief analysis of the hidden states or attention (if applicable) might help illuminate what kind of inter-predicate information the grid model is leveraging.
3.  Could you comment on the decision to train word embeddings from scratch rather than initializing with publicly available pre-trained Japanese embeddings? Do you believe the contextual information learned by the RNNs is sufficient to overcome the lack of pre-training, or do you see this as a promising avenue for further improvement?
