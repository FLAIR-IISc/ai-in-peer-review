### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural network model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, a task complicated by frequent argument omission (pro-drop). The authors argue that previous state-of-the-art methods rely heavily on syntactic parsers to model interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence, making them susceptible to error propagation. To address this, the paper introduces two syntax-free models that operate on raw word sequences. The first is a "Single-Sequence" model, which uses a stacked Bidirectional RNN (Bi-RNN) to identify arguments for each predicate independently. The second, and main contribution, is a "Multi-Sequence" model that uses a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly analyze all predicates in a sentence. This grid structure allows information to flow between the predicate-specific sequences, explicitly modeling multi-predicate interactions. Experiments on the standard NAIST Text Corpus show that both proposed models outperform previous syntax-based systems, and the Multi-Sequence model achieves a new state-of-the-art result, particularly improving the identification of omitted ("Zero") arguments.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel neural architecture for joint PAS analysis:** The primary contribution is the "Multi-Sequence" model, which employs a Grid-RNN to explicitly model the interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence. This architecture provides a principled, end-to-end neural method for joint modeling, moving beyond previous approaches that relied on feature engineering or pipeline-based joint decoding over syntactic structures.
2.  **A state-of-the-art syntax-free model for Japanese PAS:** The paper demonstrates that a purely sequence-based model can achieve state-of-the-art performance on a standard Japanese PAS benchmark. By operating directly on word sequences and forgoing syntactic features, the model simplifies the PAS pipeline and avoids potential error propagation from upstream parsers, a significant practical advantage.

### Strengths
1.  **Novelty and Technical Soundness of the Proposed Architecture:** The application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions is a novel and well-motivated approach for this task. It represents a clear evolution from standard sequence models (like the paper's Single-Sequence baseline) to a more complex architecture designed specifically for the joint nature of the problem. The model is clearly explained, and the progression from the simpler Bi-RNN model to the Grid-RNN model is logical and effectively illustrates the core contribution.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed models achieve significant improvements over established baselines on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5. The Multi-Sequence model surpasses the previous state-of-the-art (Ouchi et al., 2015) by over 2 F1 points overall (81.42 vs. 79.23). The gains are particularly pronounced for the challenging task of identifying Zero arguments (+3.03 F1), which strongly supports the hypothesis that modeling predicate interactions is crucial for resolving argument omission.
3.  **Elimination of Reliance on Syntactic Features:** A key strength is that the model achieves its strong performance without any syntactic information from parsers. This is a significant finding, as it suggests that deep neural networks can implicitly learn the necessary relational information from word sequences alone. This simplifies the overall system, makes it more robust to parsing errors, and potentially more adaptable to different domains or languages where high-quality parsers may not be available.
4.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare their models against strong, relevant baselines. The ablation study comparing the Single-Sequence and Multi-Sequence models clearly isolates the benefit of modeling predicate interactions. Furthermore, the analysis of performance across network depths (Table 2) demonstrates the effectiveness of deep architectures with residual connections, and the breakdown by case role (Table 3) provides deeper insight into where the model makes its largest gains.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Unclear Handling of Predicate Ordering in the Grid-RNN:** The Multi-Sequence model processes M predicate-specific sequences in a grid. The Grid-RNN architecture described (Eq. 5) has a directional flow, where information propagates from sequence `m-1` to `m`. This implies that the ordering of the predicates could significantly impact what the model learns. The paper does not specify how the predicates are ordered (e.g., by their linear position in the sentence, randomly). This is a critical, unstated implementation detail that could affect reproducibility and the interpretation of the model's learned interactions.
2.  **Limited Comparison with Contemporary Neural Baselines:** While the comparison to Ouchi et al. (2015) is appropriate for establishing a new state-of-the-art, the paper's neural baselines could be stronger. The related work section discusses Zhou and Xu (2015), who used a Bi-RNN with a CRF layer for a similar task. The authors dismiss the CRF layer by stating that Japanese arguments are infrequently adjacent. However, a CRF can capture global label constraints beyond simple adjacency and would constitute a stronger sequence-tagging baseline than the softmax-based Single-Sequence model. A more direct comparison would better situate the performance of the proposed models within the landscape of modern neural sequence labeling techniques.
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The Grid-RNN architecture processes an `M x T` grid, where `M` is the number of predicates and `T` is the sequence length. This is potentially much more computationally intensive than the Single-Sequence model, which processes `M` independent sequences of length `T`. The paper does not provide any analysis of the computational overhead (in terms of training or inference time) of the Multi-Sequence model, nor does it discuss how the model scales for sentences with a large number of predicates. This information is important for assessing the practical viability of the approach.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify how the M predicate-specific input sequences are ordered before being passed to the Grid-RNN in the Multi-Sequence model? Is the ordering based on the predicates' linear position in the sentence? Have you investigated whether different ordering schemes affect the final performance?
2.  The computational complexity of the Multi-Sequence model appears to scale with the number of predicates in a sentence. Could you provide details on the training and inference time of this model compared to the Single-Sequence model? How does the model perform on sentences with a high predicate count?
