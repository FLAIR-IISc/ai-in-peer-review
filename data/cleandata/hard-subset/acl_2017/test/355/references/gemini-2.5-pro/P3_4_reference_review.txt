### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on resolving zero arguments (omitted arguments). The primary goal is to effectively model interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence without relying on syntactic features from external parsers, thereby avoiding error propagation. The first model, a "Single-Sequence" model, uses a stacked Bidirectional RNN to analyze each predicate independently, serving as a strong baseline. The main contribution is the "Multi-Sequence" model, which employs a Grid-RNN architecture. This model processes all predicates in a sentence simultaneously, creating a grid where information flows both along the sentence sequence (time dimension) and across the different predicates (predicate dimension), explicitly modeling their interactions. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that both models outperform the previous state-of-the-art, and the Multi-Sequence model significantly surpasses the Single-Sequence model, particularly on the difficult task of identifying zero arguments.

### Contributions
1.  **A Novel Application of Grid-RNNs for Joint PAS Analysis:** The primary contribution is the elegant use of a Grid-RNN to jointly model all predicate-argument structures within a sentence. This architecture provides a principled, end-to-end mechanism for capturing inter-predicate dependencies, a phenomenon previously tackled by complex, feature-engineered joint models that relied heavily on syntactic parses.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Syntax-Free Japanese PAS:** The paper demonstrates that a purely sequence-based neural model can achieve state-of-the-art performance on a standard Japanese PAS benchmark. This is a significant result, as it challenges the long-held assumption that high-quality syntactic information is a prerequisite for high-performance semantic analysis in dependency-based languages.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Validation:** The proposed Multi-Sequence model achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the NAIST Text Corpus. Critically, the performance gains are most pronounced for zero-argument identification (+3.0 F1 points over the previous SOTA), which is the most challenging aspect of the task and the central motivation for the work. The statistically significant improvement of the Multi-Sequence model over the strong Single-Sequence baseline provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of modeling multi-predicate interactions via the grid architecture.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Architecture:** The paper's core idea is methodologically sound and well-motivated. The limitations of pipeline systems (error propagation) are a known problem, and the proposed end-to-end model is a direct solution. Using a Grid-RNN to explicitly share information across predicate analyses is a more direct and integrated approach than previous joint models that relied on decoding-time inference (e.g., ILP, reranking) over syntax-derived features.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Design and Analysis:** The inclusion of the Single-Sequence model is crucial, as it allows for a clean ablation that isolates the benefit of the multi-predicate modeling. The authors also investigate the effects of network depth and residual connections, demonstrating solid experimental practice. The breakdown of results by case role (Table 3) provides further insight, highlighting the model's particular strength in capturing long-distance dependencies necessary for zero nominative arguments.
4.  **Clear Positioning and Related Work:** The paper does an excellent job of situating itself within the literature. It clearly distinguishes its approach from prior syntax-based joint models (like Ouchi et al., 2015) and from syntax-free single-predicate neural SRL models (like Zhou and Xu, 2015), clearly articulating its novel contributions relative to both lines of work.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper is missing a qualitative analysis or case study. The central claim is that the Grid-RNN "effectively captures multi-predicate interactions," but it is unclear *what* kinds of interactions are being learned. Is the model resolving coreference between arguments of different predicates? Is it learning selectional preferences based on co-occurring predicates? An example from the dev/test set showing a sentence where the Multi-Sequence model succeeds and the Single-Sequence model fails would make the contribution much more concrete and interpretable.
2.  **Unclear Handling of Predicate Ordering:** The Grid-RNN processes M predicates along one of its dimensions (from m=1 to M). The paper does not specify how this order is determined. Is it simply the linear order of their appearance in the sentence? For sentences with complex clausal structures, this ordering could be non-trivial and might influence what information is propagated. A discussion of this choice and its potential impact on performance would strengthen the paper. For instance, reversing the order could potentially change the results, which would be an important finding.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Scalability and Limitations:** The model constructs an M x T grid, where M is the number of predicates and T is the sentence length. The computational complexity seems to be at least O(M*T), which could be demanding for long sentences with many predicates. A brief discussion of the model's computational complexity and how it scales with M would be valuable. Does performance degrade on sentences with a very large number of predicates? This practical consideration is important for real-world application.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the construction of the input grid for the Multi-Sequence model: How are the M predicates ordered along the "m" dimension? Is it based on their linear position in the sentence? Have you investigated if this ordering has a significant impact on model performance?
2.  Could you provide a concrete example of a sentence where the Multi-Sequence model correctly identifies a zero argument that the Single-Sequence model misses? This would help to qualitatively ground the claim that the model is capturing useful inter-predicate dependencies.
3.  The paper makes a strong case for being "syntax-free." However, even shallow and highly accurate syntactic information like Part-of-Speech (POS) tags are often used in neural models without introducing significant pipeline errors. Did you experiment with incorporating such features, and if so, what was the impact on performance? Would this compromise the main message of the paper?
