**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes two neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, a task complicated by the prevalence of zero pronouns (pro-drop). The primary goal is to model context and multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic features from external parsers, thereby avoiding error propagation. The first model, a "Single-Sequence" Bi-RNN, processes each predicate independently. The main proposed model, the "Multi-Sequence" model, uses a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly model all predicates in a sentence, aiming to capture their interactions. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that both models outperform the previous state-of-the-art, with the Multi-Sequence model achieving the highest scores, particularly on identifying challenging "Zero" arguments.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that a neural sequence labeling approach, without explicit syntactic features, can achieve state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS analysis. The significant improvement over a strong, syntax-reliant baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015) is the paper's most convincing result.
2.  The application of a Grid-RNN architecture to model interactions between multiple predicate-argument structures within a single sentence. While the performance gain from this specific architecture is modest, it presents an interesting, if not fully proven, direction for joint modeling.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark (NAIST Text Corpus 1.5). The proposed Multi-Sequence model improves the overall F-measure by over 2 points compared to the previous best result from Ouchi et al. (2015). More impressively, the improvement on the difficult "Zero" argument identification task is substantial (~3 F-points), addressing a well-known challenge in Japanese PAS. This is a solid empirical contribution.

2.  **Elimination of Syntactic Dependencies:** A significant advantage of the proposed method is its independence from syntactic parsers. Traditional methods for this task are often part of a pipeline that is susceptible to error propagation from parsing. By operating directly on word sequences, this model presents a simpler, more robust end-to-end system. This is a valuable engineering contribution.

3.  **Methodologically Sound Experiments:** The experimental setup is rigorous. The authors use standard data splits, compare against the recognized state-of-the-art baseline, and perform significance testing. The inclusion of the "Single-Sequence" model serves as a strong internal baseline and an effective ablation study for the Grid-RNN component, allowing for a clear assessment of the contribution of the multi-predicate modeling aspect.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Marginal Contribution of the Main Proposed Architecture:** The paper's primary technical novelty lies in the Multi-Sequence Grid-RNN model. However, the performance gain of this model over the much simpler Single-Sequence model is quite small (81.15% vs. 81.42% F-measure). The Single-Sequence model, which is a straightforward application of stacked Bi-RNNs (akin to Zhou and Xu, 2015), is responsible for the vast majority of the improvement over the prior state-of-the-art. This raises questions about the justification and added complexity of the Grid-RNN architecture. The results suggest that a powerful sequential model is sufficient, and the explicit "multi-predicate interaction" modeling provides only a minor benefit.

2.  **Lack of Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper claims that the Grid-RNN "effectively captures the multi-predicate interactions," but provides no analysis to substantiate this claim beyond the small F-measure improvement. The model is treated as a black box. There is no qualitative analysis, case study, or visualization to demonstrate *how* or *what* kinds of interactions are being learned. For example, are co-referent zero arguments being correctly linked across predicates? Does the model learn to avoid assigning the same argument conflicting roles for different predicates? Without such analysis, the central claim about modeling interactions is not convincingly proven; it is merely correlated with a slight performance bump.

3.  **Unconventional Modeling Choices Without Sufficient Justification:** The model uses a softmax output layer for what is fundamentally a structured prediction problem. While the authors note that arguments are infrequently adjacent in Japanese dependency-based PAS, this does not preclude other structural constraints (e.g., a predicate typically has only one NOM argument). Established models for sequence labeling, such as the BiLSTM-CRF used by Zhou and Xu (2015), are designed to handle such label dependencies. The paper dismisses the CRF with a brief justification, but a more thorough comparison or discussion would be necessary to establish that a simple softmax layer is an adequate or superior choice for this task.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The primary novelty of your work is the Multi-Sequence Grid-RNN model, yet it provides only a marginal (+0.27 F1) and computationally expensive improvement over the simpler Single-Sequence model. Could you provide a stronger justification for this architectural complexity? Is there a specific subset of phenomena or sentence structures where the Grid-RNN provides a more substantial advantage that is being diluted in the overall results?

2.  Your central claim is that the Grid-RNN captures multi-predicate interactions. Can you provide any qualitative evidence to support this? For instance, could you present an example sentence with multiple predicates and zero arguments where the Single-Sequence model fails and the Multi-Sequence model succeeds, and analyze the model's behavior to show that an inter-predicate dependency was indeed captured?

3.  Could you elaborate on the decision to use a softmax layer instead of a CRF? Given that your task involves assigning labels (NOM, ACC, DAT, etc.) where dependencies exist (e.g., constraints on the number of arguments per case), a CRF seems like a natural fit. Did you experiment with a CRF, and if so, how did it perform compared to the softmax?
