### Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural architecture for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, a task made particularly difficult by the prevalence of omitted arguments (zero pronouns) in the language. The authors' central thesis is that modeling the interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence can provide crucial clues for resolving these arguments, especially the omitted ones.

I see the main contributions of this work as follows:
1.  **A Novel Architecture for Multi-Predicate Interaction:** The primary contribution is the proposal of a "Multi-Sequence" model using a Grid-RNN to explicitly and end-to-end model the interactions between all predicates in a sentence. This moves beyond previous "joint" models that relied on feature engineering and complex inference, instead baking the interaction modeling directly into a novel neural architecture. This represents a new and conceptually elegant direction for modeling this type of linguistic phenomenon.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance Without Syntactic Features:** A significant secondary contribution is the demonstration that a purely sequence-based neural model can achieve state-of-the-art results on this task. By forgoing syntactic features from parsers, the model avoids the classic problem of error propagation from pipeline dependencies, presenting a simpler and more robust end-to-end approach.

### Strengths
1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Boldness:** The paper's core idea—using a Grid-RNN to create parallel recurrent pathways that communicate with each other—is a highly novel and compelling approach to modeling multi-predicate interactions. Traditional methods have either ignored these interactions or attempted to capture them with hand-crafted features in a joint inference framework. This work proposes learning these interactions directly from data through a specialized architecture. This is precisely the kind of bold, conceptually-driven research that pushes the field forward. The idea of information flowing both along the sentence timeline (for context) and across predicate "timelines" (for interaction) is intuitive and powerful.

2.  **Challenging Methodological Orthodoxy:** The decision to build a state-of-the-art system without any syntactic information is a strong and successful challenge to conventional wisdom in PAS analysis. For years, syntactic dependencies have been considered a cornerstone feature for this task. By demonstrating superior performance using only word sequences, the authors not only simplify the modeling pipeline but also suggest that deep sequence models are capable of implicitly learning the necessary relational information, rendering explicit syntactic parsing potentially redundant. This is a valuable finding with implications beyond just Japanese PAS.

3.  **Strong and Well-Analyzed Empirical Results:** The paper provides convincing evidence that the proposed model works. It significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art systems, which are themselves non-trivial baselines. Crucially, the gains are most pronounced on the most difficult aspect of the task: the identification of zero arguments (Table 1 shows a ~3 F1 point improvement over Ouchi et al., 2015). The ablation between the Single-Sequence and Multi-Sequence models clearly isolates the benefit of the Grid-RNN, confirming that the architectural novelty is responsible for the performance gain. The detailed breakdown by case role further strengthens the claims.

### Weaknesses
While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas that could be stronger. I view these as opportunities for future work rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis into Learned Interactions:** The paper convincingly shows *that* modeling multi-predicate interactions helps, but it offers little insight into *what* kinds of interactions the Grid-RNN is actually learning. Is it learning to share arguments? Is it capturing discourse-level relationships between events? A qualitative analysis with a few carefully chosen examples, showing a difficult case that the Single-Sequence model fails on but the Multi-Sequence model solves, would have made the contribution even more impactful. Without this, the performance gain feels a bit like a "black box" success, missing an opportunity to connect the model's internal workings back to linguistic theory.

2.  **Implicit Ordering of Predicates in the Grid:** The Grid-RNN architecture imposes a linear order on the predicates of a sentence (from m=1 to M). However, linguistically, the predicates in a sentence form an unordered set (or a graph). The authors do not discuss how this ordering is determined (e.g., by appearance in the sentence) or whether this arbitrary ordering affects performance. While the bidirectional nature of the grid likely mitigates this issue, it feels like a slight mismatch between the problem structure and the model architecture. A more natural fit might have been a graph-based neural network, though I appreciate the novelty of applying the Grid-RNN here. This is a minor methodological point, but one that clouds the conceptual purity of the approach.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The predicates `p_1, ..., p_M` are fed into the grid in a specific order. Could you clarify what this ordering is (e.g., linear order of appearance in the sentence)? Have you investigated whether shuffling this order impacts the model's performance?
2.  Could you provide a concrete example from the test set of a sentence where the Multi-Sequence model correctly identifies a zero argument that the Single-Sequence model misses? This would provide valuable qualitative evidence for the benefits of modeling predicate interactions.
