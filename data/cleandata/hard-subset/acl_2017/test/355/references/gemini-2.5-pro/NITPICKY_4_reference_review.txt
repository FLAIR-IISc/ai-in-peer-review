### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenge of identifying omitted arguments (zero pronouns). The authors propose two neural sequence models that operate directly on word sequences, thereby avoiding reliance on syntactic parsers and the associated error propagation. The first is a "single-sequence" model using stacked bidirectional RNNs to identify arguments for each predicate independently. The main contribution is the second model, a "multi-sequence" model, which employs a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly analyze all predicates in a sentence. This architecture is designed to explicitly capture interactions between predicates, which is hypothesized to be crucial for resolving ambiguous or omitted arguments. The authors demonstrate through experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus that their models, particularly the multi-sequence variant, achieve new state-of-the-art results, significantly outperforming previous syntax-based joint models.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel application of Grid-RNNs to jointly model multiple predicate-argument structures within a sentence, providing an end-to-end mechanism for capturing inter-predicate dependencies.
2.  An empirical demonstration that a syntax-free neural model can achieve state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS, offering a simpler and more robust alternative to traditional pipeline approaches.
3.  A detailed analysis confirming that the proposed multi-predicate interaction model is particularly effective for the difficult subtask of zero argument identification.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-Art Results on a Standard Benchmark:** The paper presents compelling empirical results. The proposed models significantly outperform strong, published baselines (Ouchi et al., 2015) on the standard NAIST Text Corpus dataset. The improvements are consistent across all metrics (Dep, Zero, All), with a particularly noteworthy ~3 point F-measure gain on Zero arguments for the Multi-Seq model. This is strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The core idea of using parallel RNN "streams" for each predicate and connecting them with a Grid-RNN is an intuitive and elegant solution to the joint modeling problem. It directly operationalizes the linguistic intuition that predicates in a sentence are semantically related. This approach is more integrated than previous joint models that often rely on complex scoring functions or inference procedures over a final combination of features.

3.  **Elimination of Syntactic Dependencies:** A significant advantage of this work is its independence from syntactic parsers. By learning directly from word sequences, the model avoids a major source of error propagation that affects many existing PAS systems. Achieving SOTA results without this information is a strong statement about the capabilities of modern neural sequence models for this structured prediction task.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The authors provide a solid experimental evaluation. The comparison between the single-sequence and multi-sequence models effectively isolates the contribution of the multi-predicate interaction mechanism. The breakdown of results by case role (Table 3) and the discussion of network depth and residual connections further strengthen the paper's conclusions. The use of significance testing is also commendable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Poor Presentation and Numerous Formatting Errors:** The manuscript is beset by a distracting number of presentation flaws that suggest a lack of care. The most egregious is the reference section, which is improperly formatted to the point of being nearly unreadable (e.g., "Bastien et al\.,? 2012, Bastien et al\.,"). The claim of publicly available code is undermined by a placeholder URL ("http:xxx"). Throughout the text, there are numerous typographical errors ("an solution," "significent test," "mani" for "man i"). Such a high density of errors is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission and severely detracts from the paper's professionalism.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Critical Methodological Details:** Several key implementation details are either missing or described opaquely, hindering reproducibility.
    *   **Feature Engineering:** The description of the "PRED" feature in Section 3.1 is ambiguous. It is unclear how features from a window around the predicate are incorporated into the input vector `xt` for *every* word `wt` in the sentence. A precise mathematical formulation or a clearer description is required.
    *   **Model Hyperparameters:** The explanation of GRU parameter sizes in Section 6.1 is cryptic. The statement "the parameters of GRUs related to the input values are set to 64 × 32, and others are 32× 32" is not interpretable without further definition of what "parameters related to input values" and "others" refer to. This is a critical detail for anyone attempting to replicate the work.
    *   **Missing Figures/Tables:** The text references Figures 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 and Table 2, none of which are present in the provided PDF. This makes it impossible to fully understand the model architectures and the results concerning network depth.

3.  **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** While the Grid-RNN model is novel in this context, the specific formulation of the update function in Section 4.2 (`g(ℓ)(h(ℓ−1)m,t ⊕ h(ℓ)m−1,t, h(ℓ)m,t−1)`) is presented without justification. The input to the GRU is a concatenation of the input from the layer below and the hidden state from the previous predicate's stream. Why was this specific formulation chosen over alternatives, such as using `h(ℓ)m-1,t` to modulate gates within the GRU? The lack of discussion on alternative designs makes the proposed architecture feel somewhat arbitrary.

4.  **Potentially Misleading Statement in Related Work:** In Section 5.3, the authors claim that "These previous studies used unpublished datasets..." when discussing comparisons. This seems incorrect. The English SRL work they cite (e.g., Zhou and Xu, 2015) uses public CoNLL shared task data, and the Japanese PAS work they compare against uses the same public corpus. This statement is confusing at best and should be corrected or clarified.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the "PRED" feature (Sec 3.1): For a single predicate `p`, is the feature vector derived from the C-word window around `p` concatenated to the input for every timestep `t` in the sequence? If so, is this vector identical for all `t`?

2.  Could you please elaborate on the GRU hyperparameter settings in Section 6.1 for the multi-sequence model? Specifically, which weight matrices correspond to the "parameters... related to the input values" (64x32) versus the "others" (32x32)?

3.  The reference list is not properly formatted. Will this be corrected in the final version? The same question applies to the placeholder URL for the source code.
