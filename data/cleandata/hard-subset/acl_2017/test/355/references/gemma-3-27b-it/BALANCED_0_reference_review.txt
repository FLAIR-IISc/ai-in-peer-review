This paper presents a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on handling argument omission, a significant challenge in the language. The authors propose a Grid-RNN architecture to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. They demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Multi-Predicate Interaction Modeling:** The core contribution lies in the successful application of Grid-RNNs to model interactions between predicates in Japanese PAS analysis. This approach avoids reliance on syntactic information, a common source of error in previous systems. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefit of considering these interactions, particularly for zero anaphora resolution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus, consistently outperforming previous methods including the strong baseline of Ouchi et al. (2015). This provides solid evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed models.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Analysis:** The authors conduct thorough experiments, including ablation studies with varying network depths and residual connections, and a detailed analysis of performance across different case roles. This strengthens the validity of the findings and offers valuable insights into the model's behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core Challenge:** The paper directly tackles the significant problem of argument omission in Japanese PAS, a well-recognized difficulty in the field.
2. **Novel Architecture:** The use of Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions is a novel approach in the context of Japanese PAS analysis and shows promising results.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods and experimental setup. The figures effectively illustrate the model architectures.
4. **Reproducibility:** The availability of source code is a significant positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research.
5. **Detailed Ablation Studies:**  The ablation studies regarding network depth and residual connections provide valuable insights into the model's components and their impact on performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents strong overall results, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still fails would be highly valuable for future improvements. For instance, are there specific types of zero anaphora or predicate interactions that consistently pose challenges?
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while standard, is relatively small and may not fully represent the diversity of Japanese text. The generalizability of the findings to other domains or more complex text types is unclear.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but doesn't provide extensive details about the search space or the sensitivity of the results to specific parameter choices. Understanding this sensitivity is important for practical application.
4. **Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison with Ouchi et al. (2015) is appropriate, exploring comparisons with more recent and advanced neural models for PAS (even those developed for other languages and adapted) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common failure cases of the model? Are there specific types of sentences or argument structures where the model performs poorly?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the size of the mini-batch?
3.  Have you explored transfer learning from other Japanese NLP tasks or larger, unlabeled corpora? If so, what were the results?
4.  The Grid-RNN architecture seems computationally intensive. Can you discuss the computational cost of the model and its scalability to larger datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "ALLCases Joint Model" is used in Table 1.  Clarify what this refers to (potentially in a footnote).
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the study and potential directions for future work in the conclusion.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to Japanese PAS analysis. The use of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions is a significant contribution, and the experimental results are compelling. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and its impact on the field. I recommend acceptance.
