This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of argument omission common in the language. The authors leverage Grid-RNNs to model interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence, aiming to overcome limitations of pipeline approaches reliant on syntactic parsing. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that this grid-type architecture effectively captures multi-predicate interactions, leading to state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus. A secondary contribution is the public release of source code, facilitating reproducibility. A third, more minor contribution, is the thorough empirical evaluation comparing single-sequence and multi-sequence models, as well as varying network depths with/without residual connections.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this paper is its demonstrated performance. The multi-sequence model consistently outperforms the baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015) and the single-sequence model on the NAIST Text Corpus, establishing a new state-of-the-art result. The improvements, particularly for zero arguments, are significant and highlight the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation study with residual connections further strengthens this finding.
2. **Relevant Problem Addressed:** Argument omission is a crucial challenge in Japanese PAS analysis. Addressing this problem with a neural approach that minimizes reliance on potentially noisy syntactic parsers is a valuable contribution. The motivation for circumventing error propagation is well-articulated.
3. **Thoughtful Model Design:** The Grid-RNN architecture is a well-chosen mechanism for capturing interactions between predicates. The extension of the single-sequence model to the multi-sequence model is logically sound and the experiments clearly demonstrate its benefits. The inclusion of residual connections is also a good practice and contributes to improved performance.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes a detailed experimental setup, including multiple baselines and ablation studies. The analysis of results per case role (Table 3) provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the model. This thoroughness strengthens the validity of the findings.
5. **Code Release:** Making the source code publicly available is a commendable practice that promotes reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination of Grid-RNNs and the overall architecture is effective, the individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are not novel. The novelty resides in applying these existing techniques to the specific task of Japanese PAS analysis with a focus on multi-predicate interactions. While effective, this is an incremental improvement rather than a groundbreaking conceptual advancement.
2. **Data Set Limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while a standard benchmark, is relatively small. The generalizability of the results to other datasets or real-world scenarios is not fully established. Evaluating the model on a larger, more diverse corpus would increase confidence in its robustness.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper presents quantitative results but lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Understanding *where* the model struggles (e.g., specific types of sentences, predicates, or argument types) could provide valuable insights for future improvements. Identifying common error patterns could guide further model refinement.
4. **Comparison to Recent Work:** The "Related Work" section could be more comprehensive, especially regarding recent advancements in neural SRL and PAS analysis. While the cited works are relevant, including more recent publications (e.g., Transformer-based models) would provide a broader context for the contribution of this paper.
5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but lacks details about the search process and the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings. Providing more information about this aspect would enhance reproducibility and understanding of the model's behavior.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of sentences where the model fails and the types of errors it makes?
2.  Have you experimented with Transformer-based architectures for this task? If so, how do they compare to the Grid-RNN model?
3.  How sensitive are the results to different hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the size of the mini-batch?
4.  Do you foresee any challenges in adapting this model to other pro-drop languages (e.g., Chinese, Italian)? What modifications might be necessary?
5. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the Grid-RNN architecture compared to the single-sequence model?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   Figure 2 could benefit from a more descriptive caption, explaining the purpose of each component of the models.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of Japanese PAS analysis. While the novelty is incremental, the strong empirical results, thoughtful model design, and comprehensive evaluation justify acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
