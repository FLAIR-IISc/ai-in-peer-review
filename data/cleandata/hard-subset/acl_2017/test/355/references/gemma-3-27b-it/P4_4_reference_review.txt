This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of argument omission common in the language. The core idea is to leverage grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs) to model interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence, without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. The authors present a single-sequence model as a baseline and a multi-sequence model incorporating Grid-RNNs. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus demonstrate that the proposed models outperform state-of-the-art methods.

The primary contribution of this work is the application of Grid-RNNs to Japanese PAS analysis for capturing multi-predicate interactions, leading to improved performance, particularly for zero argument identification. While joint modeling of predicate interactions isn't entirely novel in this area, the use of Grid-RNNs to achieve this *without* relying on syntactic information is a noteworthy improvement. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that neural sequence models, trained end-to-end, can achieve strong results in Japanese PAS analysis, competitive with or surpassing systems relying on hand-engineered features and pipeline processing. Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation and comparison against existing baselines on a standard dataset provide a solid validation of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Grid-RNNs:** The application of Grid-RNNs to model interactions between predicates in the context of Japanese PAS analysis, *without* relying on syntactic parsing, is a significant contribution. This addresses a known weakness of previous approaches (error propagation from parsers) and provides a more robust solution. The experimental results clearly demonstrate the benefit of this approach, particularly for zero anaphora resolution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed models achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, surpassing established baselines. The statistically significant improvements reported (particularly for zero arguments) strongly support the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors provide a thorough experimental setup, including comparisons with multiple baselines, ablation studies (network depth and residual connections), and detailed analysis of performance across different case roles. This strengthens the credibility of the findings and allows for a nuanced understanding of the model's behavior.
4. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of Japanese PAS analysis, specifically the problem of argument omission and the limitations of existing approaches. This provides strong motivation for the proposed work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core RNN Concepts:** While the application of Grid-RNNs to *this specific task* is novel, the underlying RNN and Grid-RNN architectures are well-established in the literature. The paper does not introduce any fundamental innovations to these architectures themselves. The novelty resides primarily in their application and demonstrated effectiveness within the context of Japanese PAS.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments mention various hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, L2 weight decay, number of layers) and how they were selected. However, a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the rationale behind specific choices would be beneficial.  It’s unclear how robust the performance is to changes in these hyperparameters.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents quantitative results broken down by case role, a more detailed qualitative error analysis would provide valuable insights.  Understanding *where* the model still struggles could inform future improvements. For example, what types of zero anaphora are particularly difficult to resolve?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter search process? Specifically, what ranges were explored for each hyperparameter, and what criteria were used to select the final values?
2.  Can you provide examples of cases where the model fails to correctly identify predicate-argument relations, particularly for zero arguments? This could help understand the limitations of the current approach.
3.  The paper focuses on intra-sentential arguments. Do you foresee any challenges or modifications needed to extend the model to handle inter-sentential arguments (Inter-Zero)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The code availability is a positive aspect.
*   The use of statistical significance testing strengthens the conclusions.
