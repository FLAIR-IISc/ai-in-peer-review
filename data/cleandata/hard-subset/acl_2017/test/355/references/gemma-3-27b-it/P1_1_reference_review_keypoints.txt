- Proposes a neural approach for Japanese PAS that omits explicit syntactic parsing, using only word sequences.
- Introduces Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions, improving zero anaphora resolution and achieving state-of-the-art on NAIST.
- Provides a single-sequence RNN baseline as a comparison and stepping stone to the Grid-RNN.
- Strength: strong empirical performance, notably for zero arguments, on a standard benchmark.
- Strength: clear motivation and problem definition around argument omission and parser noise in Japanese.
- Strength: well-designed Grid-RNN architecture with residual connections that suit multi-predicate modeling.
- Strength: comprehensive experiments and ablation studies showing component contributions.
- Strength: publicly released code for reproducibility.
- Weakness: limited novelty of individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs, residuals) despite their effective combination.
- Weakness: lacks detailed qualitative/error analysis to show where the model still fails.
- Weakness: restricted to intra-sentential arguments; no explored extensions to inter-sentential resolution.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and which hyperparameters mattered most.
- Question: please provide detailed error analysis with failure examples and frequent error types.
- Question: were different Grid-RNN grid sizes tried, and how did they affect performance?
- Question: any high-level ideas or plans for extending the model to inter-sentential argument resolution?
- Question: what is the computational cost (training and inference) of Grid-RNN vs. the baseline?
- Question: was the 32-d embedding size varied, and what impact did other sizes have?
- Additional comment: references section appears incomplete and needs correction.
- Additional comment: consider using conventional significance notation rather than “†”.
- Overall recommendation: accept — effective combination of components with strong results, but would benefit from deeper error analysis and discussions of extensions and sensitivity.
