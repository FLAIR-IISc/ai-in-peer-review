## Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

**Summary:** This paper presents a neural approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of argument omission. The authors propose a multi-sequence model employing Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence, without relying on traditional syntactic parsing. They demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective application of Grid-RNNs to Japanese PAS:** The paper successfully demonstrates the utility of Grid-RNNs for modeling multi-predicate interactions in a challenging, resource-poor language like Japanese.  This is a significant contribution as it moves away from pipeline-based approaches vulnerable to error propagation.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark, consistently outperforming previous methods, particularly in identifying zero arguments.
3. **Demonstration of Feature Learning:** The authors show that effective features for PAS analysis can be *learned* directly from word sequences, bypassing the need for explicit syntactic information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core Problem:** The paper tackles a central problem in Japanese NLP – argument omission. This is a well-motivated research direction with practical implications.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, using a standard dataset and comparing against strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art. The ablation study regarding residual connections is also insightful. The statistical significance tests further strengthen the results.
3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed models and experimental setup. The figures are helpful in understanding the architectures.
4. **Demonstrated Effectiveness of Multi-Predicate Modeling:** The improvement achieved by the multi-sequence model over the single-sequence model provides compelling evidence that modeling predicate interactions is beneficial for PAS analysis, especially for zero arguments. This is crucial given the motivation for the research.
5. **Publicly Available Code:** Releasing the source code encourages reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of Grid-RNNs and the specific application to Japanese PAS is valuable, the individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are not novel. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty *within* the context of the problem.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, a qualitative error analysis would provide deeper insights into the model's strengths and weaknesses. Understanding *what* types of errors the model still makes could inform future research. For example, are there specific types of zero anaphora that remain particularly challenging?
3. **Limited Discussion of Linguistic Motivation:** The paper states the linguistic intuition behind multi-predicate interaction modeling, but doesn't delve deeply into *why* these interactions are so crucial in Japanese.  More detailed linguistic analysis could strengthen the rationale for the proposed approach.  Connecting the model's behavior to specific linguistic phenomena would be valuable.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST corpus is relatively small. The paper doesn't fully address the generalizability of the results to larger, more diverse datasets.  While acknowledging the difficulty of obtaining larger datasets, discussing this limitation is important.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a few specific examples of sentences where your model significantly outperforms the baseline, and explain what aspects of the sentence structure or semantic relationships your model captures that the baseline misses?
2.  What types of errors does your model still make frequently?  Is there a specific subset of zero anaphora that proves particularly difficult for the model to resolve?
3.  Could you elaborate on the linguistic rationale behind focusing on multi-predicate interactions for resolving argument omission in Japanese? Are there specific linguistic theories or observations that directly motivate this approach?
4.  Do you have any plans to evaluate your model on larger datasets, or to explore techniques for improving its generalizability?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of related work in coreference resolution, as zero anaphora resolution shares similarities with that task.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. Despite some minor weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Japanese NLP. The strong experimental results, the effective application of Grid-RNNs, and the clear presentation make this a worthwhile addition to ACL. The authors have successfully demonstrated a promising approach to addressing a challenging problem in a resource-poor language.
