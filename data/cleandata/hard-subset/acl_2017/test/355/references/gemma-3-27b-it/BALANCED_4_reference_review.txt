This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on addressing the argument omission problem common in Japanese. The core idea is to model interactions between multiple predicates using Grid-RNNs, aiming to overcome the error propagation issues inherent in pipeline approaches relying on syntactic parsing. The authors present a single-sequence model as a baseline and demonstrate that their multi-sequence Grid-RNN model achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Multi-Predicate Interaction Modeling:** The primary contribution lies in the successful application of Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates in Japanese PAS analysis. This addresses a known challenge in the field and leads to performance improvements, particularly for zero arguments.
2. **State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed multi-sequence model achieves new state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed experimental analysis, including ablation studies on network depth and residual connections, and a per-case role breakdown, offering valuable insights into the model's behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of Japanese PAS analysis, specifically the argument omission problem, and provides a strong motivation for exploring multi-predicate interaction modeling.
2. **Well-Designed Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed, utilizing a standard dataset (NAIST Text Corpus) and comparing against strong baselines. The inclusion of statistical significance tests strengthens the validity of the results.
3. **Thorough Error Analysis:** The per-case role analysis in Table 3 is particularly insightful, revealing that the model excels at identifying dative arguments, which are often more challenging.
4. **Ablation Studies Provide Insight:** The ablation studies on network depth and residual connections provide valuable insights into the model's architecture and highlight the importance of these design choices.
5. **Publicly Available Code:** The availability of the source code promotes reproducibility and encourages further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the combination of Grid-RNNs for this specific task is effective, the individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are not novel. The paper's contribution primarily resides in the application and adaptation of these existing techniques to Japanese PAS analysis.
2. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** While quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a qualitative error analysis. Providing examples of cases where the model fails could offer valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.  What types of errors does the model make, and why?
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while standard, is relatively small and may not fully represent the complexities of real-world Japanese text. Evaluating the model on larger and more diverse datasets would further strengthen the findings.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter selection, the details are somewhat limited. A more detailed description of the hyperparameter search process would be beneficial.
5. **Comparison with More Recent Approaches:** The baseline models are somewhat dated. Comparing against more recent neural PAS models, even if not specifically for Japanese, would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed method's performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide some example sentences where the multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence model, and explain the reasoning behind the improvement?  A qualitative analysis would be beneficial.
2. What is the computational cost of the Grid-RNN model compared to the single-sequence model? Does the increased performance justify the added computational complexity?
3. Have you explored other methods for modeling multi-predicate interactions besides Grid-RNNs, such as attention mechanisms or graph neural networks? If so, what were the results?
4.  What is the impact of the window size *C* in the input layer? Did you experiment with different values, and how did they affect performance?

**Additional Comments:**

* The references are extensive and well-formatted.
* The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
* Consider adding a figure illustrating the multi-predicate interactions captured by the Grid-RNNs. This would help visualize the model's behavior.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Japanese PAS analysis. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the effective application of Grid-RNNs for modeling multi-predicate interactions and the strong experimental results justify acceptance. The weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed in a revision.
