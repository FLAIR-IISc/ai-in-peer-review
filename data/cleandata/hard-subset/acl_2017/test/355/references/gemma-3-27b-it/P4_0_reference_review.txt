This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of argument omission common in the language. The authors introduce a multi-sequence model utilizing Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence, aiming to improve upon existing approaches that rely heavily on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. The core contribution lies in the demonstration that multi-predicate interactions can be effectively modeled using a grid-type neural architecture *without* relying on syntactic information, achieving state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus. A secondary contribution is the publicly released source code, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The multi-sequence model demonstrably outperforms the state-of-the-art baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015) on the NAIST Text Corpus, particularly for zero arguments—the core focus of the paper. This improvement, statistically significant according to the authors, validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results in Table 1 clearly support this claim.
2. **Addressing a Key Challenge in Japanese NLP:** The paper tackles a significant challenge in Japanese NLP—argument omission. By focusing on multi-predicate interactions to resolve zero anaphora, the work directly addresses a known weakness of traditional PAS analysis methods. The motivation is well-articulated in the introduction and clearly links to existing literature (Iida & Poesio, 2011; Sasano & Kurohashi, 2011).
3. **Novel Application of Grid-RNNs:** While Grid-RNNs are not new, their application to the task of Japanese PAS analysis, specifically for modeling multi-predicate interactions, appears to be novel. The authors effectively leverage the grid structure to encode relationships between predicates, offering a potentially powerful alternative to syntax-based methods. The explanation of the Grid Layer and its connections (Figure 6) is reasonably clear.
4. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons with strong baselines, ablation studies on network depth with and without residual connections, and a detailed analysis of performance across different case roles. This rigorous evaluation strengthens the credibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Techniques:** The fundamental building blocks of the model – RNNs and Grid-RNNs – are well-established. The primary novelty resides in *how* these are combined and applied to the specific problem of Japanese PAS. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of existing approaches and a more detailed justification for the architectural choices.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall F-measures, a deeper error analysis could provide valuable insights. Identifying specific cases where the model fails could reveal limitations and directions for future research. For example, are certain types of predicate interactions particularly difficult to model? Are there specific linguistic phenomena that consistently cause errors?
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while a standard benchmark, may not fully represent the complexity of real-world Japanese text. Evaluating the model on a more diverse and challenging dataset could provide a more robust assessment of its generalizability. The reliance on a single dataset raises concerns about potential overfitting to its characteristics.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but the details are somewhat limited. A more comprehensive discussion of the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters could be beneficial. Were significant efforts made to optimize all hyperparameters, or were certain choices made based on intuition or prior work?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide specific examples of sentences where the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model, and explain *why* the grid structure is advantageous in those cases?
2.  What specific linguistic features or predicate types are most challenging for the model, and what potential modifications could address these challenges?
3.  Have you explored alternative architectures for modeling multi-predicate interactions, such as attention mechanisms or graph neural networks? If so, how did they compare to the Grid-RNN approach?
4.  How does the performance of your model degrade when tested on Japanese text from a different domain (e.g., news articles, novels) compared to the NAIST corpus?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 2 could be more clearly labeled to indicate the flow of information in each model.
*   The reference list is extensive but contains some formatting inconsistencies.
*   The use of "†" to denote significant results should be defined more precisely (e.g., specifying the p-value threshold).
