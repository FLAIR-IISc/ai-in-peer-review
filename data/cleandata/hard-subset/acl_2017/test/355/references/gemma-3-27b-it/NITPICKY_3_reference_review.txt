This paper presents a neural approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenge of argument omission. The authors propose a multi-sequence model utilizing Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates, aiming to improve upon existing pipeline-based methods. They demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the empirical demonstration that a Grid-RNN architecture can achieve competitive performance on Japanese PAS, even *without* relying on explicit syntactic parsing. While the idea of modeling predicate interactions isn't novel, the authors successfully implement and evaluate a neural approach that sidesteps the error propagation inherent in traditional pipeline systems. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimentation and comparison against existing state-of-the-art methods on a standard benchmark dataset. Finally, the publicly available source code is a valuable addition to the research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results on the NAIST Text Corpus demonstrably exceed previous state-of-the-art performance, particularly for zero argument identification. This is a significant practical achievement.
2. **Avoidance of Error Propagation:** The elimination of syntactic parsing as a prerequisite is a clear advantage. Pipeline systems are often limited by the accuracy of their initial stages; bypassing this issue is a valuable step forward.
3. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors present a detailed experimental setup, including ablation studies (network depth, residual connections), which contributes to the reproducibility and robustness of their findings.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed models and experimental methodology. The figures are helpful in understanding the network architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Idea:** The concept of modeling predicate interactions for PAS analysis is not new. The novelty resides primarily in the *implementation* using Grid-RNNs. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently highlight what specifically makes the Grid-RNN architecture uniquely suited to this task compared to other neural sequence modeling techniques (e.g., attention mechanisms). Why Grid-RNNs and not, say, a transformer?
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper reports overall F-measures, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying specific types of errors the model still makes (e.g., confusion between certain argument types, difficulties with long-distance dependencies) would provide valuable insights for future work. Simply stating that the model improves zero argument identification is insufficient; understanding *why* it improves is crucial.
3. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but doesn't provide much detail on the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings. A more comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter influence would enhance the robustness of the findings. Specifically, the motivation behind choosing the embedding dimensions (dword and dmark = 32) isn’t explained.
4. **Formatting Inconsistencies & Minor Errors:** There are several minor formatting issues throughout the paper. Figure 2 is referenced in the introduction but appears to be duplicated. Equation (2) is missing a full stop. The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., "Zhou and Xu (2015)" vs. "Iida et al. (2007)"). These inconsistencies, while small, detract from the paper's professionalism.
5. **Overstated Claims:** The conclusion states the models are applicable to SRL, but doesn't provide any justification or analysis for this claim beyond a superficial comparison to related work. This feels speculative and unsupported.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you elaborate on the specific advantages of using Grid-RNNs for modeling predicate interactions in this task compared to other neural sequence modeling architectures like transformers or attention mechanisms?
2. Could you provide a detailed error analysis, identifying the types of errors the model still makes and potential reasons for those errors?
3. What is the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings, particularly the embedding dimensions and the number of RNN/Grid layers?
4. Why was a window size of 5 chosen for the PRED feature? Was this parameter tuned, and if so, how?
5. Could you provide a more concrete justification for the claim that the models are applicable to general Semantic Role Labeling (SRL)?



**Additional Comments:**

* Please ensure consistent formatting and citation style throughout the paper.

* Equation (2) is missing a full stop.
* Check for any minor grammatical errors throughout the manuscript.



Despite achieving strong empirical results, the paper suffers from a lack of in-depth analysis and a degree of incremental novelty. The numerous minor flaws, while individually small, collectively detract from the overall quality of the submission. Therefore, I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The authors should focus on providing a more compelling justification for their architectural choices, conducting a thorough error analysis, and improving the overall presentation of the work.
