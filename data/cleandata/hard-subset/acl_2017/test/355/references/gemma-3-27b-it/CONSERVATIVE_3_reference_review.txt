This paper proposes a neural model, incorporating Grid-RNNs, for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of argument omission in pro-drop languages. The core claim is that their model, which learns multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing, outperforms existing state-of-the-art systems on the NAIST Text Corpus.

The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating competitive performance on a standard Japanese PAS task *without* explicit syntactic information. While neural approaches to SRL are common, adapting them effectively to Japanese, with its argument omission, is a worthwhile endeavor. A secondary contribution is the exploration of Grid-RNNs for modeling inter-predicate relationships, an architecture that, while not novel in itself, is applied in a relevant way to this problem.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results on the NAIST Text Corpus are encouraging. The model does indeed surpass previous state-of-the-art performance, particularly in the challenging area of zero anaphora resolution. This provides concrete evidence of the efficacy of their approach.
2. **Addressing a Core Challenge:** The paper directly tackles the key issue of argument omission in Japanese PAS, which is well-motivated and significant for practical applications. Focusing on this aspect distinguishes the work.
3. **Clear Presentation of Models:** The single-sequence and multi-sequence models are described clearly, with helpful diagrams illustrating the architecture. This makes the approach relatively easy to understand and potentially reproduce.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct experiments exploring network depth and the inclusion of residual connections, demonstrating a reasonable level of empirical investigation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core components – RNNs and Grid-RNNs – are established techniques. The primary novelty lies in *how* they are applied, not in the techniques themselves. The application, while reasonable, doesn't represent a substantial algorithmic breakthrough. The claim of "automatically inducing features" feels overstated; the network still learns representations based on predefined word embeddings and positional features.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While results are compared to Ouchi et al. (2015), a more comprehensive comparison with a wider range of recent approaches, including those utilizing different neural architectures, would strengthen the evaluation. Specifically, the authors should demonstrate a clear advantage over methods employing pre-trained embeddings (e.g., word2vec, fastText) *even with* syntactic features. Showing superiority over syntactically-informed baselines is crucial, given the authors’ emphasis on avoiding them.
3. **Data Set Limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while standard, is relatively small. The potential for overfitting and the generalizability of the results to larger, more diverse datasets are concerns. A more robust evaluation on multiple datasets would be desirable. The authors acknowledge the limitations of the dataset with respect to the dative case, but don't fully address how this might impact the overall conclusions.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions a search over hyperparameters, but lacks details on the specific search strategy and the range of values explored. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the justification for the chosen settings would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your model’s performance against recent state-of-the-art systems that *do* utilize syntactic information, perhaps with a re-implementation using the same dataset and evaluation metrics?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Did you experiment with pre-trained embeddings, and if so, how did they compare to the embeddings trained from scratch?
3. How was the learning rate schedule determined, and did you experiment with different schedules? Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter search process?
4. The paper focuses on intra-sentential arguments. Do you anticipate similar benefits from using Grid-RNNs for inter-sentential argument resolution, and what modifications would be necessary to adapt the model to that task?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive and generally well-formatted. The code availability is a positive aspect. The discussion of related work is adequate but could be more focused on the specific challenges of Japanese PAS and how existing approaches have addressed them.
