This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, addressing the argument omission problem common in pro-drop languages like Japanese. The authors introduce both a single-sequence model and a multi-sequence model utilizing Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. They report state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

The primary contribution, as *I* see it, is the demonstration that a grid-RNN architecture *can* improve PAS analysis performance in Japanese, specifically for zero-argument identification, *without* requiring explicit syntactic information. The empirical results, while modest, suggest a benefit from modeling multi-predicate interactions directly at the neural network level. A secondary contribution is the publicly available source code, allowing for reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art results:** The authors demonstrate a marginal but statistically significant improvement over existing methods on the NAIST Text Corpus. This provides initial evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.
2. **Addressing a key challenge:** The focus on argument omission, a significant difficulty in Japanese PAS analysis, is commendable. The results specifically highlight improvements in zero-argument identification, validating the model’s potential in tackling this problem.
3. **Novel architectural exploration:** The application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions is a reasonable and potentially valuable architectural choice. While grid-RNNs aren't novel *per se*, applying them to this specific task is a worthwhile investigation.
4. **Clear presentation of the models:** The descriptions of both the single-sequence and multi-sequence models are relatively clear and well-structured. The diagrams (Figures 2 and 3) are helpful in understanding the architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Modest Improvement:** The performance gains are relatively small. The improvement over the baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015) is less than 2% in F-measure overall, and the gains for 'Dep' arguments are negligible. Such a marginal improvement raises concerns about the practical significance of the contribution.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study to demonstrate the *specific* impact of the Grid-RNN component. It's unclear whether the improvement is solely due to the Grid-RNN, or whether other factors, such as the overall network depth or residual connections, contribute significantly.
3. **Feature Engineering:** While the authors state they aim to avoid reliance on syntactic information, the input features still include “PRED: Word index of the target predicate and words around the predicate.” This constitutes a form of syntactic awareness, albeit a limited one, and undermines the claim of complete independence from parsing. The window size 'C' of 5 is arbitrarily chosen and not justified.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The description of hyperparameter tuning is inadequate. Simply stating the range of values used for learning rate, L2 decay, and network depth isn't sufficient. A more detailed explanation of the search strategy (e.g., grid search, random search) and the rationale behind the selected values is needed. The choice of mini-batch size is also mentioned, but the justification for the selected sizes is missing.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still struggles would provide valuable insights for future improvements. A discussion of common error types and potential reasons for these errors is missing.
6. **Formatting inconsistencies:** There are numerous minor formatting issues throughout the paper, including inconsistent spacing after periods, and the references section could be better formatted. (e.g., inconsistent use of abbreviation such as "pages" vs "pp.").

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study to quantify the contribution of the Grid-RNN component, specifically compared to a comparable model *without* grid connections?
2. Could you provide a more comprehensive justification for the choice of the window size 'C' for the PRED feature?
3. What specific error types does the model still struggle with, and what are the potential reasons for these errors?
4. Please clarify the exact methodology used for hyperparameter tuning, including the search space and the selection criteria.
5.  How sensitive are the results to the initialization of the embedding matrices?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is quite extensive. A more concise list focusing on the most relevant related work would improve readability.
*   The link to the source code (“http:xxx”) is incomplete and needs to be corrected.



Overall, while the paper presents a reasonable approach and achieves marginal improvements, the lack of compelling evidence, the modest gains, and the numerous minor flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version.
