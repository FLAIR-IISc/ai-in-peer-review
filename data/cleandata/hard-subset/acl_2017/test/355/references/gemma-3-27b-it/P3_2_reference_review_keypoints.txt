- Summary: Proposes Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS, avoiding syntactic parsing and achieving SOTA on the NAIST corpus.  
- Contribution 1: Empirically shows Grid-RNNs effectively capture multi-predicate interactions and improve zero anaphora resolution.  
- Contribution 2: Establishes a strong single-sequence RNN baseline and conducts comprehensive experiments on depth and residual connections.  
- Contribution 3: Targets the challenging phenomenon of argument omission in Japanese and demonstrates a neural mitigation without noisy syntactic input.  
- Strength 1: Clear motivation and problem definition emphasizing limitations of pipeline syntactic approaches.  
- Strength 2: Effective adaptation of Grid-RNNs to model predicate interactions, supported by ablation studies.  
- Strength 3: Strong empirical results with notable gains for zero arguments.  
- Strength 4: Well-structured, readable paper with helpful architectural diagrams.  
- Strength 5: Publicly released code enabling reproducibility.  
- Weakness 1: Core model components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are not novel; novelty is mainly in application/combination.  
- Weakness 2: Lacks detailed qualitative error analysis to show remaining failure modes.  
- Weakness 3: Limited comparison to more recent Japanese NLP and neural SRL methods.  
- Weakness 4: Hyperparameter search and sensitivity are insufficiently detailed.  
- Question 1: Can the authors provide qualitative error analysis focused on zero anaphora failure cases?  
- Question 2: What are the computational costs (training/inference) of the multi-sequence model vs. single-sequence, and are gains justified?  
- Question 3: How sensitive is performance to input word boundary quality or noisy segmentation?  
- Question 4: Would the architecture generalize to other pro-drop languages, and what modifications are needed?  
- Additional comment 1: Reference list is extensive and appropriate.  
- Additional comment 2: Conclusion could be more concise and emphasize key takeaways and future work.  
- Additional comment 3: Discuss limitations of the NAIST corpus and generalizability to other datasets.  
- Overall recommendation: Recommend acceptance given strong empirical contribution despite limited architectural novelty.
