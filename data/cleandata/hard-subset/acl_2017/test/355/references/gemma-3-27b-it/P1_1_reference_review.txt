## Peer Review: Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

**Summary:**

This paper presents a neural approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of argument omission (zero pronouns). The authors propose a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates within a sentence, arguing this improves performance over existing methods which rely heavily on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. They demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, outperforming a strong baseline.  A single-sequence model using standard RNNs is also introduced as a baseline and stepping stone to the grid-RNN approach.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of Effective Neural Modeling for Japanese PAS without Explicit Syntax:** The paper successfully showcases that strong PAS performance can be achieved using only word sequence information, bypassing the need for potentially noisy syntactic parsers. This is particularly valuable for a language like Japanese where parsing can be difficult.
2. **Empirical Validation of Multi-Predicate Interaction Modeling:**  The paper provides compelling evidence that explicitly modeling interactions between predicates improves PAS accuracy, especially for zero anaphora resolution. The Grid-RNN architecture proves effective in achieving this.
3. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct thorough experiments on a standard benchmark, NAIST Text Corpus, with clear comparisons to strong baselines, including statistical significance testing.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark is a significant accomplishment. The improvements, particularly for zero arguments, are notable and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of Japanese PAS analysis, specifically the issue of argument omission and the limitations of existing pipeline approaches. This provides strong motivation for the proposed work.
3. **Well-Designed Architecture:** The Grid-RNN architecture appears well-suited for capturing multi-predicate interactions. The explanation of the architecture is clear, and the inclusion of residual connections is a sensible design choice.
4. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are well-structured and the ablation studies (e.g., with and without residual connections) provide valuable insights into the contributions of different components of the proposed model.
5. **Publicly Available Code:** Making the source code publicly available promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of Grid-RNNs and the specific application to Japanese PAS is valuable, the individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs, residual connections) are well-established in the literature. The paper doesn't present fundamentally new neural network architectures.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall F-measures, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still struggles (e.g., specific types of zero anaphora, complex sentence structures) would be helpful for identifying future research directions.  A qualitative analysis of misclassifications would be beneficial.
3. **Focus on Intra-Sentential Analysis:** The authors explicitly limit the scope to intra-sentential arguments. While understandable given the complexity of inter-sentential analysis, it feels limiting. A brief discussion of potential extensions to handle inter-sentential dependencies would be helpful.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experimental section details several hyperparameters that were tuned. A discussion of the sensitivity of the model to these hyperparameters would strengthen the results. Which hyperparameters had the biggest impact, and why?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of sentences where the model fails and a discussion of the reasons for the failures? What types of errors are most frequent?
2. **Ablation Study - Grid Size:**  Did you experiment with different grid sizes within the Grid-RNN architecture? What effect did different grid sizes have on performance?
3. **Inter-Sentential Extension:**  Have you considered potential approaches for extending the model to handle inter-sentential argument resolution? Even a high-level discussion would be appreciated.
4. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of the proposed model compared to the baseline?  Specifically, how does the Grid-RNN impact training and inference time?
5. **Impact of Embedding Dimensions:** You mention setting embedding dimensions to 32. Did you experiment with other embedding sizes, and if so, what impact did they have on performance?



**Additional Comments:**

* The references section seems incomplete (e.g., missing author details for some publications). Please ensure all references are complete.
* The use of “†” to denote significance is a bit unconventional. Consider using standard notation (e.g., * or ** for p < 0.05 and p < 0.01).




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the individual components are not novel, the effective combination of these components, the strong experimental results, and the clear demonstration of the benefits of modeling multi-predicate interactions make this a valuable contribution to the field of Japanese PAS analysis. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, especially providing a more thorough error analysis, would further strengthen the paper.
