This paper presents a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, addressing the challenge of argument omission in pro-drop languages. The authors propose a multi-sequence model utilizing Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates without relying on syntactic parsing. They claim to achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

The primary contribution is the application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis, demonstrating improved performance, particularly for zero-argument identification. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical validation of this approach on a standard dataset, providing a clear comparison against existing methods. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Results:** The reported performance improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods are noteworthy, particularly the gains in zero-argument identification. This is a significant contribution, as handling zero anaphora is a key challenge in Japanese PAS. The statistical significance testing provides some confidence in these results.
2. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the specific challenges of Japanese PAS analysis, focusing on argument omission and the limitations of pipeline approaches reliant on syntactic parsing. This provides a strong motivation for the proposed work.
3. **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and easy to follow. The architecture diagrams (Figures 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6) are helpful in understanding the proposed models.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation, including ablation studies (network depth, residual connections) and comparisons with baseline models.  The inclusion of results broken down by case role (Table 3) provides a more nuanced understanding of the model's performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the *combination* of Grid-RNNs for this specific task is reasonable, the individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are not novel. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why Grid-RNNs are *particularly* well-suited for capturing multi-predicate interactions compared to other methods like attention mechanisms or more sophisticated recurrent architectures.  A more detailed analysis of *how* the Grid-RNNs achieve improved performance would be valuable.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experimental details section is extensive, listing numerous hyperparameters. While commendable, this raises concerns about sensitivity to these parameters.  The paper lacks a proper analysis of hyperparameter robustness and the impact of different parameter settings on performance.  A sensitivity analysis is crucial, especially given the complexity of the models.
3. **Feature Engineering:** Although the aim is to avoid syntactic information, the input feature representation still relies on carefully crafted features (ARG, PRED, MARK) that require linguistic knowledge. The paper does not explore end-to-end learning with simpler input representations (e.g., word embeddings only). The dependence on these hand-engineered features diminishes the claim of fully automatic feature induction.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while standard, is relatively small and potentially limited in its diversity. Evaluating the models on larger and more diverse datasets would strengthen the findings and demonstrate generalizability.
5. **Minor Formatting Inconsistencies:** There are some minor formatting inconsistencies, such as varying spacing around equations and within tables. While not critical, these detract from the overall polish of the paper. Specifically, the citation format is not consistent.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* Grid-RNNs are expected to be effective for modeling multi-predicate interactions in this task, compared to other techniques like attention mechanisms?
2.  Have you conducted a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of your results? If so, please provide the findings.
3.  Did you explore simpler input representations (e.g., solely using pre-trained word embeddings) to assess the impact of the hand-engineered features?
4.  Could you discuss the computational cost of the proposed models, particularly the Grid-RNN architecture, and its scalability to larger datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is missing complete information for some entries (e.g., Bastien et al.).
*   The mention of "unpublished datasets" in the Related Work section is vague and should be clarified.
*   Figure 2 could be expanded to show the data flow more clearly within the single-sequence and multi-sequence models.



Overall, while the paper presents a valuable contribution through its empirical results, the lack of deeper analysis regarding the novelty of the approach, hyperparameter sensitivity, and feature engineering limits its impact. I lean towards **rejection** due to the cumulative effect of these weaknesses. A major revision addressing these points, with a more thorough analysis and justification of the design choices, would be required for reconsideration.
