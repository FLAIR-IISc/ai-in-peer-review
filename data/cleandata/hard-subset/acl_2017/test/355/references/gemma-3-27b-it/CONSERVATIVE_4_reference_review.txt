This paper presents a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the problem of argument omission. The authors propose a Grid-RNN based model to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. They demonstrate improved performance on the NAIST Text Corpus compared to existing methods.

The primary contribution is the application of a Grid-RNN architecture to the task of Japanese PAS analysis, specifically to address the challenges posed by argument omission. While Grid-RNNs are not a novel architecture *per se*, their application to this specific linguistic problem – and the reported gains – represent a noteworthy incremental improvement. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons against a strong baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015) and ablation studies examining the effects of network depth and residual connections.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported performance improvements over the state-of-the-art baseline on the NAIST corpus are compelling.  The gains, particularly for zero anaphora resolution, are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The consistent improvement across different metrics (Dep, Zero, All) lends credibility to the findings.
2. **Addresses a Core Problem:** Argument omission is a well-recognized challenge in Japanese PAS, and the focus on this aspect is valuable.  The motivation for exploring methods that bypass syntactic parsing, given its potential for error propagation, is sound.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:**  The authors provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning and evaluation metrics.  The inclusion of ablation studies regarding network depth and residual connections is commendable, as it offers insight into the model's behavior.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the proposed model and experimental results. The figures are helpful in understanding the architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core innovation – applying Grid-RNNs – is not groundbreaking.  Grid-RNNs have been explored in other NLP tasks, and the paper doesn’t present a substantial modification or theoretical advancement of the architecture itself. The claim of “automatically inducing features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions” feels overstated; this is a property of RNNs generally, and the specific benefit of the grid configuration isn't clearly articulated beyond the empirical results.
2. **Data Set Limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while standard, is relatively small and may not fully represent the complexity of real-world Japanese text.  The results may not generalize well to other datasets or domains.  The authors acknowledge this in referencing earlier work limiting evaluation to intra-sentential arguments, but a discussion of how the model might perform on more challenging data would be beneficial.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are positive, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still struggles – specific types of omissions, syntactic structures, or semantic ambiguities – would provide valuable insights and guide future research. Showing examples of the model's successes *and* failures would strengthen the evaluation.
4. **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While a comparison to Ouchi et al. (2015) is crucial, it would be valuable to include simpler baselines – perhaps a bi-LSTM without the grid structure – to isolate the contribution of the Grid-RNN component.  This would more directly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting specific cases where the model fails and explaining potential reasons for these failures?
2.  How do you anticipate the model’s performance would change if applied to a larger and more diverse dataset?
3.  Have you experimented with alternative methods for capturing multi-predicate interactions, such as attention mechanisms? If so, could you briefly discuss the results?
4. Can you quantify the computational cost of the grid-RNN compared to a standard RNN, and discuss potential scalability challenges?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are extensive and well-formatted. It’s good that source code is publicly available.
