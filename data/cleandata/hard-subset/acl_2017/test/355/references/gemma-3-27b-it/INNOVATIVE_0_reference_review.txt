This paper tackles the challenging problem of predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, specifically addressing argument omission. The authors propose a neural model leveraging Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. 

The primary contribution of this work, and the reason for its potential acceptance despite some limitations, is the *novel application of Grid-RNNs to the task of Japanese PAS analysis*. While RNNs have been applied to SRL, the explicit modeling of interactions *between* predicates using a grid architecture is a compelling and relatively unexplored direction, particularly for a language like Japanese where such interactions are crucial due to argument omission. The reported performance improvements, particularly on zero argument identification, support the effectiveness of this approach.

A secondary, though valuable, contribution is the thorough experimental validation. The authors demonstrate improvements over strong baselines on a standard dataset and provide ablation studies exploring the effect of network depth and residual connections.  This provides confidence that the observed gains aren’t simply due to chance or hyperparameter tuning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of utilizing Grid-RNNs to model inter-predicate relationships for Japanese PAS is genuinely novel. This is not a mere incremental improvement; it’s a shift in how the problem is approached, moving away from heavy reliance on syntactic information.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported gains over state-of-the-art methods, especially the significant improvement in Zero argument identification, offer strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model.  The statistically significant improvements reported are a key positive indicator.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, utilizing a standard benchmark and including ablation studies to analyze the impact of different components of the model. This provides a clear understanding of what contributes to the performance gains.
4. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of PAS analysis in Japanese, particularly the issue of argument omission, and positions the proposed approach as a direct solution to this problem.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates performance gains, it lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still fails (specific types of zero anaphora, complex sentence structures, etc.) would provide valuable insights for future work and bolster the claim that inter-predicate modeling is genuinely addressing the core challenges.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Recent, Strong SRL Models:** The baselines, while representative of previous work in Japanese PAS, don’t include comparisons to very recent, powerful SRL models developed for English and potentially adaptable to Japanese (e.g., transformer-based models).  A comparison would help contextualize the performance gains and show whether the Grid-RNN approach is competitive with current state-of-the-art techniques generally.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The description of hyperparameter selection is somewhat brief. While the ranges are provided, details about the search process or the rationale for specific choices are missing.  This raises a slight concern about whether the reported results are robust to different hyperparameter settings.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The reliance on a single dataset (NAIST Text Corpus 1.5) is a limitation. While standard, exploring performance on other datasets or different domains would strengthen the generalizability claim.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the types of errors the model still makes, particularly concerning zero anaphora resolution?
2. Have you considered comparing your model to more recent, transformer-based SRL architectures (even if requiring adaptation for Japanese)? If not, why?
3. Can you elaborate on the hyperparameter search process? What criteria were used to select the final hyperparameter settings?
4. Are there plans to explore the performance of the model on other Japanese datasets or in different application domains?
