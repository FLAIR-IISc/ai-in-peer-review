This paper proposes a neural model, utilizing Grid-RNNs, for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of argument omission. The authors aim to move beyond pipeline-based approaches that rely on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing, instead learning multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. The primary contribution is the demonstration of improved performance on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 compared to a strong baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015), suggesting the proposed grid-based architecture effectively captures these interactions. A secondary contribution is the exploration of residual connections within the network.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The paper presents clear experimental results demonstrating that the proposed models, particularly the Grid-RNN variant, achieve state-of-the-art performance on a standard Japanese PAS dataset. This is a crucial strength, as it provides concrete evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The statistical significance testing (bootstrap resampling) adds further credibility to the results.
2. **Relevant Problem Addressed:** Argument omission is a well-recognized challenge in Japanese PAS analysis, and addressing it is a worthwhile goal. The motivation for moving away from pipeline systems prone to error propagation is reasonable and well-articulated.
3. **Clear and Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The architecture diagrams (Figures 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6) are helpful for understanding the proposed models. The related work section provides a good overview of previous approaches.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The ablation study regarding residual connections demonstrates a conscientious effort to understand the contribution of different components of the model. The comparison to established baselines (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015) is appropriate.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the results are positive, the core idea of using RNNs to model sequential data and capture interactions between predicates is not entirely novel. The grid-RNN architecture, while a reasonable choice, is a relatively established technique and its application to this specific problem does not represent a significant breakthrough in architectural innovation. The paper lacks a strong argument for *why* a grid-RNN is particularly well-suited to this task beyond the intuitive notion of capturing interactions.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** Although the results show overall improvements, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the model succeeds and fails. Understanding the types of errors the model makes would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for future improvement. For example, a detailed look at the performance for different types of zero anaphora (e.g., those with closer or more distant antecedents) would be beneficial.
3. **Over-reliance on NAIST Corpus:** The evaluation is solely based on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5. While this is a standard benchmark, a more robust evaluation would involve testing the model on other datasets or performing cross-dataset generalization experiments. This would help assess the generalizability of the approach and prevent overfitting to the specific characteristics of the NAIST corpus.
4. **Lack of Comparative Analysis of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proposed models compared to the baselines. It is important to consider the trade-off between performance and efficiency, especially when dealing with complex neural architectures like Grid-RNNs.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on specific types of errors the model makes and potential reasons for those errors?
2. Have you considered any other neural architectures for capturing multi-predicate interactions besides Grid-RNNs (e.g., attention mechanisms)? If so, why did you choose Grid-RNNs over those alternatives?
3. What is the computational cost of the proposed models (training and inference time) compared to the baseline models?
4. Do you believe the performance gains are directly attributable to the grid architecture, or could similar results be achieved with carefully tuned single-sequence models, perhaps with attention mechanisms or more complex feature representations?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are formatted correctly. The availability of source code is commendable. The discussion of related work is adequate, but could be expanded to include more recent advancements in neural SRL and PAS analysis.
