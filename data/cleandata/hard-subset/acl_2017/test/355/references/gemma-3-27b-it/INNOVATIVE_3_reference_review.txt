This paper tackles the challenging problem of predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, specifically addressing argument omission. The authors propose a neural model leveraging Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates, aiming to move beyond pipeline-based approaches reliant on often-errorful syntactic parsing.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Novel Application of Grid-RNNs to PAS:** The core contribution lies in the application of Grid-RNNs to the task of Japanese PAS analysis. While Grid-RNNs aren't novel *per se*, their use to model multi-predicate interactions without explicit syntactic information is a genuinely interesting approach and a departure from standard pipeline methods.
2.  **Demonstrated Improvement over Strong Baselines:** The paper convincingly demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST corpus, exceeding previously published results. This empirical validation lends credence to the approach.
3.  **Focus on a Difficult Linguistic Phenomenon:** The paper explicitly addresses the challenge of zero anaphora resolution, a key difficulty in Japanese PAS, and shows improvements specifically on this subtask.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Bold Approach & Conceptual Innovation:** The move away from syntactic dependency parsing is significant. Relying solely on word sequence information and learning interactions directly from data is a promising direction, especially for languages where parsing accuracy is limited. This is a fundamentally different approach than incremental improvements on existing pipeline methods.
2.  **Empirical Results are Compelling:** The reported F1 scores represent a tangible improvement over existing methods, and the ablation study (Table 2) provides some insight into the benefits of network depth and residual connections.
3.  **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of Japanese PAS, particularly the issue of argument omission, providing a strong motivation for the proposed approach.
4.  **Reproducibility:**  The availability of source code is a substantial plus, allowing for verification and extension of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results demonstrate overall improvement, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still fails (e.g., specific types of zero anaphora, long-distance dependencies) would be crucial for future development.
2.  **Grid-RNN Architecture Detail:** The paper provides a high-level overview of the Grid-RNN, but lacks a detailed justification for specific architectural choices (e.g., layer sizes, number of layers, specific GRU configuration). A more in-depth explanation of the design rationale would be beneficial.
3.  **Comparison Limited to NAIST Corpus:**  The evaluation is solely based on the NAIST corpus.  Evaluating on other datasets or different domains would strengthen the generalizability claims.  Japanese PAS datasets are limited, but exploring other available options would be valuable.
4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The description of hyperparameter tuning is brief. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to different hyperparameter settings. A more systematic exploration of hyperparameters would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis? Specifically, what types of errors does the model still make, and are there particular linguistic phenomena that it struggles with?
2.  What motivated the specific architectural choices made for the Grid-RNN (e.g., layer sizes, number of layers)? Did you experiment with other configurations?
3.  Have you considered using pre-trained word embeddings? Would incorporating such embeddings improve performance?
4.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the Grid-RNN architecture compared to the single-sequence model?



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this is a strong and promising paper. The application of Grid-RNNs to Japanese PAS represents a novel and potentially impactful approach. The empirical results are encouraging and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The paper prioritizes innovation over incremental improvement, which I highly value. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above during the author response phase. The potential for future work building on this foundation is significant.
