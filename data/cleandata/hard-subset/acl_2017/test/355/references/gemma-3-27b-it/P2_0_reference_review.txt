## Review for "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a neural model, specifically using Grid-RNNs, to address the challenge of argument omission in Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis. The authors argue that existing approaches rely heavily on often-error-prone syntactic parsing, and their model aims to learn multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. They present both a single-sequence model (baseline) and a multi-sequence model (with Grid-RNNs), and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Grid-RNNs for Japanese PAS:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that Grid-RNNs can effectively capture multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS, leading to performance gains over existing methods and a strong baseline neural model. This is the most significant contribution.
2. **Focused Approach to a Core Japanese NLP Problem:** The work tackles a crucial and challenging problem in Japanese NLP – handling argument omission – with a well-motivated approach. The focus on intra-sentential arguments simplifies the problem but remains practically relevant.
3. **Clear and Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing their models with strong baselines and analyzing results per case role and network depth.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results are compelling. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST corpus, particularly the improvement in zero-argument identification, is a substantial achievement. The statistical significance tests further strengthen these claims.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The problem of argument omission in Japanese PAS is clearly articulated, and the authors convincingly argue that modeling multi-predicate interactions is crucial for addressing it. Bypassing the reliance on potentially noisy syntactic parsers is a smart move.
3. **Thorough Experimental Design:** The experiments are well-designed and clearly presented. The ablation study exploring network depth and the inclusion of residual connections provide valuable insights into the model's behavior. The per-case role analysis is also helpful.
4. **Clear and Detailed Model Description:**  The paper provides a detailed and understandable description of both the single-sequence and multi-sequence models, including the architecture and the equations. The diagrams are also helpful for understanding the flow of information.
5. **Publicly Available Code:** The availability of the source code is a major plus, allowing for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Components:** While the application of Grid-RNNs to this specific task is valuable, the individual components (RNNs, Bi-RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are not novel. The core innovation lies in the *combination* and application, but the architectural novelty is incremental.  This is not a fatal flaw, given the strong empirical results, but it should be acknowledged.
2. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the per-case role analysis is useful, a deeper dive into *what kinds* of errors the model makes would be helpful. Identifying specific linguistic phenomena that the model struggles with could guide future research.  For example, are certain types of zero anaphora particularly challenging?
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST corpus, while a standard benchmark, is relatively small.  Evaluating the model on a larger and more diverse dataset would further strengthen the claims of generalizability.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lists several hyperparameters that were tuned. A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, embedding dimension) would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors your model makes? Are there specific linguistic patterns or phenomena that consistently lead to incorrect predictions?
2. How sensitive are your results to the choice of hyperparameters? Did you perform a thorough hyperparameter search, and if so, can you provide some insights into the optimal ranges for key parameters?
3.  Have you considered incorporating any external knowledge resources (e.g., word embeddings trained on larger corpora) into your model? If so, did this improve performance?
4. While you focus on intra-sentential arguments, is there a pathway to extend the multi-sequence model to tackle inter-sentential anaphora resolution, and what challenges would that entail?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
* The referencing is thorough and up-to-date.
* Minor: There are a few instances where figure captions could be more descriptive.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept.**

Despite the incremental nature of the architectural novelty, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of Japanese NLP. The strong empirical results, well-motivated approach, and thorough experimental evaluation make it a worthy addition to ACL. The authors have convincingly demonstrated the effectiveness of Grid-RNNs for modeling multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis. Addressing the questions raised above would further strengthen the paper.
