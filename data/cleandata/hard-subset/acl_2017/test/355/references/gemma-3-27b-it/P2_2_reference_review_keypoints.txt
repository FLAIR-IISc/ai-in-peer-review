- Summary: Proposes single-sequence RNN and multi-sequence Grid-RNN models for Japanese PAS, achieving state-of-the-art results on the NAIST corpus by modeling multi-predicate interactions.
- Contribution 1: Empirically shows modeling multi-predicate interactions improves Japanese PAS, especially for omitted arguments.
- Contribution 2: Adapts and applies Grid-RNNs effectively to capture predicate interactions in PAS.
- Contribution 3: Establishes a strong baseline and provides comprehensive experiments, including ablations and per-role analysis.
- Strength 1: Clearly defines the problem and motivates the approach with linguistic intuition about argument omission.
- Strength 2: Strong experimental improvements over prior work, with significant gains for zero arguments and statistical significance testing.
- Strength 3: Well-designed Grid-RNN architecture with residual connections to support deeper networks.
- Strength 4: Thorough ablation studies that clarify the effects of depth and residual connections.
- Strength 5: Detailed per-case role analysis offering nuanced insight into model behavior.
- Weakness 1: Limited novelty in core components—the work adapts existing RNN/Grid-RNN rather than introducing new neural building blocks.
- Weakness 2: Insufficient error analysis detailing specific types of mistakes, particularly for zero arguments.
- Weakness 3: Reliance on the relatively small NAIST corpus; larger/diverse dataset evaluation is lacking.
- Weakness 4: Hyperparameter tuning details and search rationale are underreported, affecting reproducibility.
- Question 1: Can the authors provide qualitative error analysis and examples of challenging zero-argument cases?
- Question 2: What are the computational costs (training time, memory) of the multi-sequence/Grid-RNN model versus the single-sequence model?
- Question 3: How well would this approach generalize to other pro-drop languages (e.g., Chinese, Italian)?
- Additional comment 1: The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
- Additional comment 2: Code availability is appreciated and the references are comprehensive.
- Additional comment 3: Overall, the paper is a solid contribution to Japanese NLP and is recommended for acceptance.
