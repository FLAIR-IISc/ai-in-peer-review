- Solid contribution: uses Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions without syntactic parsing, achieving state-of-the-art on NAIST with informative ablation studies.  
- Contribution — multi-predicate modeling: Grid-RNNs improve PAS performance, especially zero-pronoun resolution.  
- Contribution — empirical results: achieves SOTA on the NAIST corpus.  
- Contribution — pipeline-free: strong performance without external parsers or complex feature engineering.  
- Strength — empirical strength: consistent improvements over baselines and clear ablation evidence for design choices.  
- Strength — architecture: well-motivated, effectively implemented Grid-RNN showing clear information propagation benefits.  
- Strength — robustness: avoids error propagation from syntactic parsers, improving real-world applicability.  
- Strength — presentation: clear, well-organized writing and helpful figures.  
- Weakness — related work: limited discussion of recent neural SRL work (attention, transformers) and comparisons.  
- Weakness — error analysis: lacks qualitative analysis of failure cases to explain model errors.  
- Weakness — hyperparameters: insufficient detail on hyperparameter sensitivity and robustness.  
- Weakness — dataset: reliance on the relatively small NAIST corpus raises generalization concerns.  
- Question — architecture choice: why Grid-RNNs instead of attention/transformers and expected advantages?  
- Question — qualitative examples: can authors show sentences where multi-sequence clearly outperforms single-sequence and what interactions are captured?  
- Question — pre-trained embeddings: were Word2Vec/FastText or other pre-trained embeddings tried to help small-data learning?  
- Question — PRED window size: was window size 5 chosen by experiment or linguistic intuition, and were alternatives tested?  
- Additional — reproducibility: code availability is excellent.  
- Additional — baselines: good comparison to reimplemented Imamura et al. (2009).  
- Additional — analysis: per-case-role performance table is informative.
