This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on handling argument omission, a significant challenge in Japanese. The core contribution lies in utilizing Grid-RNNs to model interactions between multiple predicates without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, showcasing the effectiveness of their approach, particularly for zero argument identification. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive experimental analysis, including ablation studies demonstrating the benefits of residual connections and network depth. Finally, the publicly released code adds to the reproducibility and potential impact of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods on the NAIST Text Corpus, particularly for zero arguments. This is a significant result, as accurately identifying omitted arguments is a core challenge in Japanese PAS analysis. The use of bootstrap resampling for significance testing strengthens the validity of the findings.
2. **Effective Architecture for Multi-Predicate Interaction:** The Grid-RNN architecture appears well-suited for capturing interactions between predicates. The ablation studies and comparative analysis between the single-sequence and multi-sequence models convincingly demonstrate the benefit of explicitly modeling these interactions, especially for the more challenging zero argument identification task.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a detailed experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies.  The analysis of performance per case role (Table 3) offers valuable insights into the model's strengths and weaknesses. The comparison with previous work, even acknowledging dataset differences, is helpful for contextualizing the results.
4. **Addressing a Core Problem:** The paper directly addresses the issue of argument omission in Japanese, a fundamental problem that hinders accurate semantic analysis.  The proposed approach offers a promising solution by leveraging the relationships between predicates to infer missing arguments.
5. **Code Availability:** Releasing the source code is a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility and encouraging further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of Grid-RNNs for this specific task is effective, the individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are not novel. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this specific application leverages the strengths of these established techniques to overcome challenges unique to Japanese PAS analysis.
2. **Lack of Analysis of Error Cases:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis of the errors made by the model. Examining specific examples of misidentified arguments, especially zero arguments, could reveal potential areas for improvement and provide insights into the model's limitations.  What types of zero arguments are particularly difficult to resolve?
3. **Dataset Specificity:** The evaluation is limited to the NAIST Text Corpus. While this is a standard benchmark, it is crucial to understand how well the model generalizes to other datasets or domains. A brief discussion of the potential challenges of adapting the model to different data sources would be beneficial.
4. **Complexity of Grid-RNN:** Grid-RNNs can be computationally expensive. The paper does not adequately discuss the computational cost of the proposed model and whether it is practical for large-scale applications. A comparison of training and inference time with simpler models would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide examples of sentences where the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model, and explain *why* the model is able to make the correct prediction in these cases?
2. What are the primary error types observed in the zero argument identification task? Are there specific linguistic phenomena or sentence structures that consistently lead to incorrect predictions?
3. How would you expect the performance of the model to be affected by a larger, more diverse dataset? Are there any potential challenges to adapting the model to other domains or genres?
4. Could you comment on the computational cost of the Grid-RNN architecture compared to the single-sequence model (e.g., training time, inference time)?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper references NAIST Text Corpus 1.4β in Table 3 but does not fully explain the difference between 1.4β and 1.5, other than mentioning the dative case annotation revision. A clearer explanation would be helpful.
* The writing is generally clear and well-organized, but some sentences are a bit verbose. Consider streamlining the language for improved readability.
* A brief discussion of related work in coreference resolution could be relevant, as zero anaphora resolution is closely related to this task.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of Japanese PAS analysis. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results and offers a promising approach for handling the challenging problem of argument omission. While the novelty of the individual components may be limited, the effective combination of these techniques and the thorough experimental evaluation justify acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
