- Proposes a Grid-RNN neural approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that models multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences to handle argument omission.

- Demonstrates Grid-RNN effectiveness on Japanese PAS, achieving state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

- Presents a pipeline-free approach that avoids reliance on syntactic parsing and potential error propagation.

- Provides comprehensive experimental evaluation, including ablation studies on network depth and residual connections.

- Strong empirical results, notably improved zero argument identification with statistical significance testing.

- Addresses a central problem in Japanese NLP (argument omission / zero anaphora resolution) with a well-motivated model.

- Clear, well-structured presentation with helpful model architecture figures.

- Thorough experiments and ablations that increase credibility and insight into model behavior.

- Source code is publicly available, supporting reproducibility.

- Limited novelty of individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs) and insufficient analysis of why Grid-RNNs capture interactions effectively.

- Lacks qualitative analysis of model successes and failures to reveal strengths, weaknesses, and learned patterns.

- Comparisons to more recent neural architectures (e.g., Transformers) are limited or missing.

- Hyperparameter tuning details and rationale are not fully explained.

- Asks for analysis of what interactions Grid-RNNs learn and whether hidden states reflect semantic relationships.

- Requests qualitative error analysis with illustrative failure cases and explanations.

- Inquires why Transformer-based models (e.g., BERT) were not considered and what challenges they pose for Japanese PAS.

- Asks about sensitivity to input window size (C) and results from different window sizes.

- Requests clarification of the exact Theano version used and standardization of citation style.

- Suggests highlighting the NAIST Text Corpus version differences more prominently in the main text.

- Overall: solid, empirically strong contribution to Japanese NLP; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
