This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, addressing the challenge of argument omission in pro-drop languages like Japanese. The core idea is to leverage Grid-RNNs to model interactions between multiple predicates without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. The authors present both a single-sequence model and a multi-sequence model (using Grid-RNNs) and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis, leading to improved performance, particularly in identifying zero arguments. While joint modeling of predicates isn’t novel, the specific implementation with Grid-RNNs and the demonstration of its effectiveness on this task is a valuable addition. A second contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation on a standard dataset, providing a clear benchmark for future research. The ablation studies comparing single vs multi-sequence models and models with/without residual connections are well-conducted and informative. Finally, the paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed models and experimental setup, facilitating reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art Performance:** The proposed multi-sequence model achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, exceeding previous approaches including Ouchi et al. (2015). This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed Grid-RNN architecture in capturing multi-predicate interactions and addressing the argument omission problem.  The improvement, particularly on zero arguments, is significant.
2. **Effective Use of Grid-RNNs:** The adaptation of Grid-RNNs for modeling predicate interactions is well-motivated and effectively implemented. The architecture allows for capturing relationships between predicates without the need for explicit syntactic information, mitigating error propagation from parsing.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive. The inclusion of ablation studies (different network depths, residual connections) provides valuable insights into the contribution of each component of the proposed model. Comparing the performance across different case roles further enriches the analysis.
4. **Clear and Detailed Description:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed models, including the input layer, RNN layer, and output layer. The figures and equations are helpful in understanding the model architecture. The implementation details are also sufficiently described to allow for replication.
5. **Addresses a Challenging Problem:** The paper tackles a core challenge in Japanese NLP – argument omission – and offers a novel approach to address it.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Concepts:** While the application of Grid-RNNs to this specific task is a contribution, the underlying concepts of RNNs, Grid-RNNs, and joint modeling are not entirely novel. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this work builds upon and differentiates itself from existing research in related areas.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The experiments are limited to the NAIST Text Corpus. While this is a standard dataset, its size and domain (newspaper text) may limit the generalizability of the findings. Evaluating the models on other datasets (e.g., broader domains, different genres) would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but doesn’t explicitly detail the process or provide a rationale for the chosen hyperparameters. More details on the hyperparameter search and the impact of different hyperparameter settings would be helpful.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  While the results are reported quantitatively, a qualitative error analysis would provide valuable insights into the types of errors made by the model and potential areas for improvement. Understanding *why* the model fails on certain examples could guide future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing Grid-RNNs over other architectures for modeling predicate interactions (e.g., attention mechanisms, graph neural networks)? What specific properties of Grid-RNNs were most beneficial for this task?
2.  The paper mentions that the models are applicable to SRL in other languages. Have you explored this application, or are there any anticipated challenges when adapting the models to languages with different syntactic structures?
3.  What impact does the window size 'C' have on the model's performance, and how was this value chosen?
4.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what ranges of values were explored for the learning rate, L2 regularization coefficient, and network depth?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list appears to be incomplete. Some references are cited in the text but not included in the bibliography.
*   The notation used in the equations could be slightly improved for clarity (e.g., explicitly defining the dimensions of the matrices).
*   The term “atomic features” feels slightly imprecise – perhaps “base features” would be more accurate.
