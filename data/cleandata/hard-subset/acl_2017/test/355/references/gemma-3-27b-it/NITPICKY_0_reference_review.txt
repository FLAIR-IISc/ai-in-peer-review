This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, addressing the challenge of argument omission. The authors introduce a single-sequence model and a multi-sequence model utilizing Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates without relying on syntactic parsing. They report state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the demonstration of competitive PAS performance *without* the traditional reliance on syntactic parsers, a common source of error propagation in this task. The use of Grid-RNNs to model predicate interactions is a reasonable approach. The empirical results, while incremental, do suggest a benefit to considering multi-predicate information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Elimination of Pipeline Dependency:** The most significant strength is the successful avoidance of a traditional parsing pipeline. This is a crucial step towards a more robust and potentially more accurate PAS system for Japanese, given the known limitations of Japanese parsers.
2. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-conducted, utilizing a standard dataset and comparing against strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art. The statistical significance testing is also appreciated.
3. **Clear Presentation of Models:** The architecture of both the single-sequence and multi-sequence models are clearly described and visualized with helpful diagrams (Figures 2 & 3). The explanation of the input feature engineering is also reasonably detailed.
4. **Detailed Ablation Study:** The inclusion of experiments exploring network depth and residual connections provides valuable insight into the model’s behavior and the importance of these design choices. This adds to the overall robustness of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the combination of Grid-RNNs and a sequence-based approach is suitable, the individual components – RNNs, Grid-RNNs, and feature concatenation – are not novel in themselves. The paper doesn’t offer significant algorithmic breakthroughs. The core idea feels like a sensible application of existing techniques rather than a fundamental innovation.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on overall F-measure. A more granular error analysis – categorizing the types of errors made by the system (e.g., incorrect argument identification, incorrect case role assignment) – would provide valuable insights and guide future improvements.  Specifically, understanding *why* the multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence model for zero arguments would be beneficial.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The description of hyperparameter tuning feels somewhat superficial.  It’s stated that hyperparameters were selected from specific sets, but the justification for these sets, and any exploration beyond them, isn’t provided.  This raises concerns about whether the reported results are robust to different hyperparameter settings.
4. **Formatting and Writing Quality:** The paper contains several minor inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., equation numbering, figure references) and writing (e.g., awkward phrasing, minor grammatical errors). While not major, they detract from the overall professionalism of the submission. For instance, the sentence "This confirms that our neural sequence models realize high-performance even without syntactic information" is unnecessarily verbose.  Also, the reference list is incomplete – some citations are referred to in the text but not fully listed in the bibliography.
5. **Lack of Discussion of Computational Cost:** The Grid-RNN architecture is computationally more expensive than a standard RNN. The paper doesn't discuss the computational overhead of the proposed model and its implications for scalability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by your models?
2. What was the rationale for choosing the specific hyperparameter search spaces (e.g., for λ, the number of RNN layers)? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of your results to different hyperparameter settings?
3. What is the computational cost of your multi-sequence model compared to the single-sequence model, and to the baseline models?
4. Can you elaborate on the specific linguistic intuitions that motivated the use of Grid-RNNs for capturing multi-predicate interactions in this context?

**Additional Comments:**

* Figure 6 is visually cluttered and could be improved for clarity.
* Please correct the formatting inconsistencies and writing errors throughout the paper.
* Ensure a complete and correctly formatted bibliography.



Despite the positive aspects, the incremental nature of the contribution and the presence of several minor flaws lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the work is technically sound and demonstrates a reasonable approach, it doesn't present sufficient novelty or depth to warrant acceptance in a highly competitive venue like ACL. Addressing the weaknesses, particularly the error analysis and hyperparameter tuning details, would significantly strengthen the paper.
