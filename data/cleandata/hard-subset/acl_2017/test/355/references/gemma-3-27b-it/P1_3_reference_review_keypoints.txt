- Presents neural approach to Japanese PAS, addressing zero pronouns with a single-sequence (BiRNN) and a multi-sequence (Grid-RNN) model; Grid-RNN attains state-of-the-art on NAIST 1.5.  
- Demonstrates effective neural PAS modeling without relying on syntactic parse information.  
- Empirically validates that modeling multi-predicate interactions (Grid-RNN) improves zero anaphora resolution.  
- Releases source code to support reproducibility.  
- Strong empirical results with statistically significant gains, notably for zero argument identification.  
- Well-motivated approach that connects linguistic intuition about argument omission to model design.  
- Clear and detailed model descriptions and illustrative figures.  
- Comprehensive experiments including ablation studies and per-argument-type analyses.  
- Good related-work discussion positioning the contribution within PAS and SRL literature.  
- Limited novelty of individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs); novelty is mainly in the application/adaptation.  
- Lacks qualitative error analysis to show where models still fail, especially for difficult zero anaphora types.  
- Reliance on the relatively small NAIST corpus raises concerns about generalizability and potential overfitting.  
- Hyperparameter tuning details are insufficiently described for full reproducibility.  
- Asks for qualitative error analysis and examples of challenging zero anaphora cases.  
- Requests experiments with alternative Grid-RNN connection patterns (e.g., only horizontal or vertical).  
- Queries the computational cost and efficiency trade-offs between Grid-RNN and single-sequence models.  
- Inquires whether pre-trained embeddings or large unlabeled data were explored and their potential effects.  
- References comprehensive; writing is clear and formatting is good.  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed paper with significant empirical contributions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
