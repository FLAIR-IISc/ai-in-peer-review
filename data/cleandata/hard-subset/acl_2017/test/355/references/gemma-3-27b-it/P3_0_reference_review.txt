## Review for "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid contribution to Japanese PAS analysis by effectively leveraging Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. The results demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST corpus, and the ablation studies provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach. However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in neural SRL and a more nuanced analysis of the limitations of the approach.

**Summary & Contributions:**

The paper addresses the challenge of predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, particularly the problem of argument omission. The authors propose a neural model based on Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence, circumventing the reliance on potentially noisy syntactic information.

I identify the following key contributions:

1. **Effective Multi-Predicate Interaction Modeling:** The Grid-RNN architecture demonstrably improves PAS analysis performance, specifically for zero-pronoun resolution, by effectively capturing semantic relationships between predicates. This is the primary contribution.
2. **State-of-the-Art Results:** The models achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST corpus, providing empirical evidence of their effectiveness.
3. **Pipeline-Free Approach:** The models achieve strong performance without relying on external parsers or complex feature engineering, offering a more robust and streamlined approach to Japanese PAS analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent improvement over baseline models, including the previous state-of-the-art, is a significant strength. The ablation studies (different network depths, residual connections) clearly demonstrate the benefit of the proposed architecture and design choices.
2. **Effective Grid-RNN Architecture:** The application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions appears well-motivated and effectively implemented. The clear explanation of how information propagates through the grid is helpful.  The improvement on zero-argument identification specifically highlights the benefit of this approach.
3. **Robustness to Error Propagation:**  By eliminating the dependency on syntactic parsing, the proposed model avoids the common issue of error propagation inherent in pipeline approaches. This is a crucial advantage for real-world applications.
4. **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The explanations of the models and experiments are clear and easy to follow. Figures are helpful in illustrating the architectures.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Related Work in Neural SRL:** While the paper cites relevant work in Japanese PAS analysis, it lacks a deeper discussion of recent advancements in *neural* Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) in English and other languages. There's a significant body of work utilizing attention mechanisms, transformers, and other advanced neural techniques that could provide valuable context and potential avenues for future research.  Comparing against these methods (even if not directly comparable due to language differences) would strengthen the contribution.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper primarily focuses on quantitative results. A qualitative error analysis, identifying specific cases where the model fails, would provide valuable insights into its limitations and areas for improvement. Understanding *why* the model makes mistakes is crucial for guiding future development.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions a hyperparameter search but doesn't provide extensive details.  Discussing the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters would be beneficial. Is the performance highly dependent on specific settings, or is the model relatively robust?
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST corpus, while a standard benchmark, is relatively small. Discussing the potential impact of dataset size on the model's generalization ability would be valuable. Would the model's performance hold up on a larger, more diverse dataset?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Could you elaborate on why you chose Grid-RNNs over other neural architectures for modeling multi-predicate interactions, such as attention mechanisms or transformers?**  What specific advantages did you anticipate Grid-RNNs would offer in this context?
2. **Can you provide some examples of sentences where the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model?**  What types of interactions are the Grid-RNNs capturing that lead to these improvements?
3. **Have you investigated the use of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText) in your models?**  Could pre-training improve performance, especially given the relatively small size of the NAIST corpus?
4. **The paper mentions using a window size of 5 for the PRED feature. Was this value determined through experimentation, or was it chosen based on linguistic intuition?**  Did you experiment with different window sizes?

**Additional Comments:**

* The code availability is excellent and will facilitate reproducibility.
* The comparison to the reimplemented Imamura et al. (2009) model is good for establishing a clear baseline.
* The table comparing performance across different case roles is informative.
