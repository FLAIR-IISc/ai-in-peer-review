## Peer Review: Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a neural network approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically tackling the challenge of argument omission common in the language. The authors argue that existing joint models rely heavily on potentially noisy syntactic information and propose a system based on Grid-RNNs that learns multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. They present a single-sequence model as a baseline and demonstrate that their multi-sequence model, employing Grid-RNNs, achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Grid-RNN effectiveness for Japanese PAS:** The paper provides compelling evidence that Grid-RNNs can effectively capture multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS, surpassing previous state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark.
2. **Pipeline-free approach to multi-predicate interaction:**  The work successfully moves away from pipeline approaches reliant on syntactic parsing, mitigating error propagation and demonstrating that interaction modeling can be achieved directly from the word sequence.
3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough comparison with existing methods, including ablation studies examining the impact of network depth and residual connections.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:**  The core strength of this paper is the performance improvement demonstrated on the NAIST corpus. Achieving state-of-the-art results, particularly for zero argument identification, is a significant contribution. The statistical significance testing further strengthens this claim.
2. **Addresses a crucial problem in Japanese NLP:** Argument omission is a central challenge in Japanese PAS, and the paper directly addresses this through modeling inter-predicate relationships. The focus on zero anaphora resolution is well-motivated and impactful.
3. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is logically organized, with clear explanations of the proposed models (single-sequence and multi-sequence with Grid-RNNs) and experimental setup. The figures illustrating the model architectures are helpful.
4. **Thorough experimentation and ablation studies:** The inclusion of experiments investigating the impact of network depth and residual connections adds credibility and provides insights into the model's behavior.
5. **Publicly available source code:** Making the code available promotes reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the *combination* of Grid-RNNs for this specific task is effective, the individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are not novel.  The paper doesn’t deeply explore *why* Grid-RNNs are particularly well-suited to this problem beyond stating they capture interactions. More analysis on the learned interaction patterns would be valuable.
2. **Lack of qualitative analysis:** The paper focuses almost exclusively on quantitative results. A qualitative analysis of cases where the model succeeds and fails would provide valuable insights into its strengths and weaknesses, and potentially suggest avenues for further improvement. Showing example sentences and the model’s reasoning (even at a high level) would be helpful.
3. **Limited comparison to recent advancements:** The comparison to related work feels somewhat dated. While the Ouchi et al. (2015) baseline is important, the paper doesn’t engage with more recent advancements in neural network architectures (e.g., Transformers) that might be relevant to this task. A brief discussion of why these weren't explored would be beneficial.
4. **Hyperparameter tuning details could be more thorough:** While the authors mention hyperparameter selection, the rationale behind the specific choices isn’t fully explained.  A more detailed description of the hyperparameter search process would increase confidence in the reported results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Grid-RNN Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of *what* types of interactions the Grid-RNNs are learning? Are there specific patterns in the hidden states that correspond to particular semantic relationships between predicates?  
2. **Error Analysis:** Could you present a qualitative error analysis of the model's predictions?  Specifically, can you illustrate a few examples where the model fails and discuss the potential reasons for the failure?
3. **Transformer Consideration:**  Why were Transformer-based architectures (e.g., BERT) not considered for this task? What challenges would be associated with adapting such models to Japanese PAS?
4. **Impact of Window Size:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of window size (C) in the input layer? Did you experiment with different window sizes, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*  It would be helpful to clarify the exact version of Theano used for implementation.
*  The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., sometimes “et al.” is used, sometimes not). Please standardize this.
* The statement about NAIST Text Corpus 1.4β and 1.5 revisions in Table 3 is good context but could be more prominently highlighted in the main text.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of Japanese NLP. The paper presents a well-motivated and empirically validated approach to addressing the challenging problem of argument omission in PAS analysis. While the individual components aren’t groundbreaking, the successful application of Grid-RNNs to this task and the strong empirical results make this a worthwhile submission. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing a more in-depth analysis of the learned interactions and considering recent advancements, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
