This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on addressing argument omission. The authors introduce both a single-sequence model and a multi-sequence model utilizing Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. They demonstrate improved performance over existing state-of-the-art methods on the NAIST Text Corpus.

The primary contribution lies in the application of Grid-RNNs to the Japanese PAS task, specifically to model multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. While the idea of utilizing interactions isn’t novel, the direct, end-to-end approach, bypassing traditional pipeline processing, is a reasonable step. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of improved performance on a standard benchmark dataset, indicating a viable approach to the argument omission problem.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Results:** The reported improvements over the state-of-the-art (Ouchi et al., 2015) on the NAIST corpus, particularly for zero arguments, are encouraging. The statistical significance testing lends some credibility to these findings.
2. **Direct Modeling of Interactions:** Avoiding reliance on syntactic parsing is a sensible direction, as errors in parsing can propagate and negatively impact PAS analysis. The Grid-RNN architecture offers a potential mechanism for directly capturing predicate interactions without this dependency.
3. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of PAS analysis in Japanese due to argument omission and effectively motivates the need for a model that can handle these challenges. The discussion of *Dep* and *Zero* arguments is well-defined.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors explore different network depths and the inclusion of residual connections, demonstrating a conscientious approach to model optimization.  The ablation study regarding residual connections is positive.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Components:** The individual components – RNNs, Bi-RNNs, Grid-RNNs – are well-established techniques. The paper doesn’t introduce any fundamentally new neural building blocks. The novelty is in the *combination* of these existing techniques applied to this specific task, but the degree of innovation feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper demonstrates improved overall performance, it lacks a detailed error analysis.  Understanding *what types* of errors the model still makes, and *why*, is crucial for guiding future work. Are there specific linguistic phenomena or sentence structures that consistently cause the model to fail? A qualitative analysis would be valuable.
3. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (NAIST Text Corpus 1.5). Generalizability to other datasets or text genres remains unaddressed.  The dataset is also relatively small by modern NLP standards.
4. **Feature Engineering Details:** While the authors claim to avoid relying on syntactic information, the input features (ARG, PRED, MARK) still implicitly encode some syntactic knowledge. The definition of the PRED feature, utilizing a window size of 5 around the predicate, is a form of syntactic context. A more rigorous discussion of what constitutes "syntactic information" is needed. Specifically, the effect of varying the window size C should be discussed.
5. **Formatting and Minor Errors:** There are several minor formatting inconsistencies. For example, Figure 2's caption is misaligned. Equations (2) and (3) are not clearly explained; it's unclear what "g(ℓ)" represents without looking at the surrounding text. Also, the repeated use of "Eq." is stylistically awkward. "Equation" should be used instead.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting common error types and potential reasons for these errors?
2. How sensitive is the model's performance to the window size *C* used in the PRED feature? Did you experiment with different values of *C*?
3.  Beyond the NAIST corpus, do you have any preliminary results or plans to evaluate the model on other Japanese datasets or different text genres?
4.  Can you clarify the role of the function g(ℓ) in Equations (2) and (3)? A more explicit explanation of its functionality would be helpful.
