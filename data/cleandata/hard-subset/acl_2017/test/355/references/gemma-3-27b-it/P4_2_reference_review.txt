This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on addressing the argument omission problem common in Japanese. The authors introduce both a single-sequence model and a multi-sequence model leveraging Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates without relying on syntactic information. The primary contribution is the demonstration that a Grid-RNN architecture can effectively model multi-predicate interactions and achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus. A secondary contribution lies in the successful application of neural sequence models to Japanese PAS analysis without the need for explicit syntactic parsing, mitigating error propagation issues. A third contribution is the publicly released source code.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art performance:** The multi-sequence model achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, results on the NAIST Text Corpus compared to existing methods, particularly for zero argument identification. The ablation study in Table 2 convincingly demonstrates the benefit of using residual connections with deeper networks. This suggests the proposed architecture is genuinely effective.
2. **Addressing a specific linguistic challenge:** The paper explicitly targets the challenging problem of argument omission in Japanese, which is a well-recognized issue in PAS analysis. The proposed approach directly addresses this challenge by modeling interactions between predicates. The focus on intra-sentential arguments, while a common practice, is clearly justified.
3. **Novel architectural choice:** The adoption of Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions is a noteworthy architectural choice. The authors provide sufficient justification for this design decision, and the experimental results support its effectiveness. The explanation of how grid-RNNs handle inter-sequence connections is clear and helpful.
4. **Comprehensive experimentation:** The paper includes a thorough set of experiments, including comparisons against strong baselines, analysis of network depth and residual connections, and a breakdown of performance by case role (Table 3). This rigorous evaluation strengthens the validity of the findings.
5. **Publicly available code:** The availability of the source code promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the combination of Grid-RNNs for this specific task is novel, the individual components (RNNs, Bi-RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are well-established in the field. The paper doesn't introduce fundamentally new building blocks.  The contribution lies more in the application and adaptation of existing techniques to a specific problem.
2. **Lack of detailed error analysis:** While the paper presents quantitative results, it lacks a qualitative error analysis. Examining specific examples where the model fails could provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement. Understanding the types of errors made (e.g., confusion between specific case roles) would be helpful.
3. **Limited discussion of hyperparameter tuning:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but provides limited detail on the process.  More information on how the hyperparameters were selected (e.g., using a validation set, grid search, random search) would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.
4. **Dataset limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while a standard benchmark, is relatively small.  Evaluating the model on larger and more diverse datasets could provide a more comprehensive assessment of its generalization ability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide some examples of sentences where the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model, illustrating the benefit of capturing multi-predicate interactions?
2. What are the major error types observed by the model? Is there a particular type of argument or predicate that consistently causes difficulties?
3. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the criteria used for selection?
4. Have you considered using pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) to initialize the embedding matrices?  If so, what was the impact on performance?
