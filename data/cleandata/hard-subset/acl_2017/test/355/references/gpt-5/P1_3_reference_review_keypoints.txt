- Proposes syntax-free neural models for Japanese PAS, focusing on zero arguments, using multi-sequence Grid-RNNs over a predicate-by-token grid.
- Introduces a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence, outperforming a strong single-sequence BiRNN baseline.
- Shows single- and multi-sequence variants achieve new SOTA on NAIST 1.5, with notable gains on Zero arguments and the DAT case.
- Demonstrates deeper RNN/Grid-RNNs with residual connections improve PAS performance, with useful training insights.
- Strong empirical improvements without syntactic input, especially on zero arguments (81.42 F vs. 79.23 baseline) and large DAT gains.
- Ablations clearly show multi-predicate interactions drive improvements; statistical significance is reported.
- Model is simple and general: uses only word embeddings, a predicate window, and a predicate marker, no external parsers.
- Standardized evaluation and per-case analyses (Dep vs. Zero) increase confidence in results.
- Limited architectural novelty: mainly a direct application of multidimensional/grid RNNs to joint predicate modeling.
- Lacks explicit structural constraints or decoding discussion (e.g., one-argument-per-case enforcement, CRF/ILP comparisons).
- Incomplete analysis of why multi-predicate modeling helps; missing qualitative examples, distance-based breakdowns, and simpler-interaction ablations.
- Comparisons may be unfair/incomplete: lacks syntax-agnostic baselines of prior work, fusion with syntax, and comparisons to recent neural PAS/SRL methods.
- Some experimental and reproducibility details are underspecified (predicate inclusion, average predicates per sentence, runtime/memory, embedding choices, bootstrap procedure).
- Clarify whether and how one-argument-per-case constraints are enforced and how violations affect scoring.
- Provide average number of predicates per sentence and runtime/memory overhead of multi-sequence vs. single-sequence, plus batching strategies for varying M.
- Break down gains by predicate-argument distance and by Zero vs. Dep for each case (NOM/ACC/DAT) to show where multi-predicate gains concentrate.
- Compare Grid-RNN to simpler interaction mechanisms (shared BiRNN with predicate markers, cross-predicate self-attention, message passing) via ablation.
- Report experiments with syntactic inputs and pre-trained embeddings to test complementarity and sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Clarify grid GRU formulation (what is treated as input vs. recurrent state) to aid reproducibility.
- Explain the role of the PRED label in the softmax and whether it affects inference.
- Discuss the large DAT gains in light of annotation differences between NAIST 1.4β and 1.5 and include an error analysis.
- Ensure code, trained models, and scripts are accessible for replication.
