Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes two neural sequence models that eschew explicit syntactic features: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN/GRU per predicate; and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, the models outperform prior systems, with stronger gains on Zero arguments and notable improvements for DAT.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple but effective syntax-agnostic neural formulation for Japanese PAS that achieves SOTA on NAIST 1.5, particularly improving Zero arguments.
2) A grid-style multi-sequence architecture to model multi-predicate interactions without a parser.
3) An empirical study indicating deep stacking with residual connections benefits PAS, and that modeling predicate interactions helps Zero cases.

Strengths
- Clear task focus with a strong motivation: Modeling Zero arguments without parser dependencies is important in pro-drop languages; demonstrating competitive performance without syntax is practically valuable.
- Solid empirical improvements on a standard benchmark: Both models beat strong baselines (Imamura et al. 2009; Ouchi et al. 2015) on NAIST 1.5. The gains for Zero (≈+2–3 F1) and especially DAT in Dep (≈+26–35 F1 over baselines in Table 3) are striking.
- Explicit modeling of multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN extension provides a principled way to share information across predicates and yields significant improvements over the single-sequence model for Zero (+1.0 F1) and small but significant gains overall.
- Ablation on depth and residual connections: The paper systematically explores layer depth and shows residual connections enable deeper, better-performing models, which increases confidence that capacity, not just a specific configuration, drives gains.
- Self-contained models with minimal feature engineering: The approach uses only word indices, a binary predicate marker, and a narrow predicate-centered context window, avoiding the fragile pipeline (syntax) used in earlier SOTA systems.

Weaknesses
- Missing decoding constraints and unclear prediction protocol: The model does a per-token softmax over labels {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null} for each predicate but does not describe how structural constraints are enforced (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate) or how ties and multiple predicted arguments per role are handled. Without a global decoding layer (CRF, ILP, or constrained inference), prediction could produce multiple arguments per role; the evaluation protocol for mapping token-wise labels to role assignments is not specified. This is a central methodological omission.
- Insufficient novelty relative to prior neural sequence models: The single-sequence model closely follows standard BiRNN tagging (akin to Zhou & Xu 2015) with a predicate marker. The Grid-RNN extension is a straightforward application of multidimensional RNNs (Graves & Schmidhuber 2009; Kalchbrenner et al. 2016). The conceptual novelty is modest; the main contribution is demonstrating these ideas for Japanese PAS. The paper would benefit from stronger baselines (e.g., a shared BiLSTM over the sentence with per-predicate predicate-mark features, without Grid-RNN; or simple cross-predicate attention) and ablations pinpointing what about Grid-RNN is crucial.
- Reporting and reproducibility issues: The code link is a placeholder (“http:xxx”), undermining reproducibility. Optimizer description is inconsistent (“SGD using mini-batch” and “learning rate automatically adjusted using Adam”); please clarify whether Adam or SGD was used. No variance across random seeds is reported. The significance test is used only to compare Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq; details (number of bootstrap samples, confidence intervals) are missing, and comparisons vs baselines are not marked. Important training details (dropout, gradient clipping, early stopping patience) are absent.
- Data and evaluation clarity gaps: It is unclear how argument candidates are defined at the token level in NAIST 1.5 without syntax; NAIST PAS roles are often annotated at the bunsetsu/head level, and the paper does not explain how heads are represented when using only word boundaries. The role “PRED” is included in the softmax label space, but evaluation generally excludes it; the paper does not specify whether PRED predictions are ignored at scoring time. The large DAT improvements (Table 3) warrant analysis; otherwise, they appear suspicious for a syntax-free model. No error analysis or per-distance breakdowns for Zero are provided, missing an opportunity to substantiate claims about long-distance dependencies.
- Presentation and citation quality: Numerous formatting and editing issues (e.g., broken figures/tables references, typos “significent,” inconsistent citation metadata with “?” and backslashes, footnotes inline, missing figure/table numbers, unclear dimensions “parameters of GRUs related to the input values are set to 64×32, and others 32×32”) reduce clarity. Residual connection details in Grid-RNN are underspecified (shape matching for addition across layers in multi-sequence setting).

Questions to authors
1) Decoding and constraints: How do you enforce role uniqueness per predicate (at most one NOM/ACC/DAT)? If you do not enforce it, how are predictions converted to final assignments and evaluated? Do you use thresholding or select the highest-scoring token per role?
2) Candidate representation: How are NAIST roles mapped to tokens without syntax? Are you using gold head tokens, bunsetsu heads, or some heuristic? Please detail the tokenization and head selection procedure.
3) PRED label: Why include PRED in the label space? Is it used for training only, and is it excluded in evaluation? What proportion of loss is attributable to the trivially predictable PRED class?
4) Baselines: Can you report a strong non-grid baseline that jointly processes all predicates via a shared BiLSTM with predicate markers (processing M predicates in parallel but without inter-sequence recurrence), and/or a self-attention across predicate sequences? This will help isolate the contribution of the Grid-RNN.
5) Ablations: Please provide ablations on (a) removing MARK or the predicate-centered window, (b) number of grid layers, (c) residual connections, and (d) using CRF or constrained decoding. Also report variance across 3–5 seeds.
6) Optimizer and training details: Clarify optimizer (Adam vs SGD), gradient clipping, dropout, batch size chosen on dev, and early stopping criteria.
7) Significance and error analysis: Provide confidence intervals, bootstrap settings, and error analyses, including distance-based Zero performance and analyses for DAT improvements.
8) Reproducibility: Please provide a working code link and configuration files, including how to prepare NAIST 1.5 inputs without syntax.

Overall, while the idea of syntax-free multi-predicate modeling is appealing and results are promising, the accumulation of methodological omissions, reporting inconsistencies, missing baselines/ablations, and presentation issues currently undermines confidence in the contribution.
