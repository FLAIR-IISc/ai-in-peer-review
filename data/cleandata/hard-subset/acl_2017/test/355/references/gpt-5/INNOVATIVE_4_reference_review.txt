Summary and contributions
This paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) without syntactic inputs, proposing two neural sequence models: (i) a single-sequence BiGRU that predicts case labels per predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence, enabling cross-predicate interactions. On NAIST 1.5 (standard split), both models surpass prior state-of-the-art systems that rely on syntax; the Grid-RNN yields the best overall, with notable gains on zero arguments.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A conceptually simple but bold architecture for modeling multi-predicate interactions via Grid-RNNs over the predicate-by-token grid, removing dependence on syntactic parsers while achieving SOTA.
2) Empirical evidence that jointly modeling predicates improves zero pronoun resolution and DAT case performance in Japanese PAS, with statistically significant gains over strong syntax-based joint baselines.
3) An assessment of depth and residual connections in this setting, showing deeper residual networks help.

Strengths
- Novel direction with practical impact: Demonstrates that syntax-free, end-to-end neural modeling can outperform syntax-dependent joint models for Japanese PAS. This is a substantial result for a pro-drop language where parser errors are a major bottleneck; eliminating pipeline dependencies is valuable beyond this task.
- Modeling multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN design that propagates information across predicates and time is an elegant instantiation of multi-dimensional RNNs for PAS. The improvements over the single-sequence model (overall and especially on Zero) support the core premise that cross-predicate signals help where dependencies are absent.
- Strong, clean empirical results: New SOTA on NAIST 1.5 without external resources or syntax, with significant gains on zero arguments (+~3 F1 over Ouchi et al. 2015) and large improvements on DAT. The evaluation uses standard splits and bootstrap significance tests, with per-case-role breakdowns and depth/residual analyses.
- Minimal feature engineering and small models: The method stays close to raw word sequences, using only predicate markers and local windows; results suggest the approach scales without heavy handcrafted features or selectional preferences.
- Analysis of depth/residuals: The study shows deeper stacks with residual connections are beneficial, giving practical guidance for model design.

Weaknesses
- Degree of novelty: Using multi-dimensional RNNs (Grid-RNN) to connect sequences is known; the main novelty is its application to PAS for multi-predicate interactions. Compared with alternative modern interaction mechanisms (e.g., cross-predicate attention, structured decoders), the architectural choice feels like a straightforward extension of prior grid RNNs rather than a fundamentally new modeling idea.
- Missing structural constraints: Decoding is independent softmax per token/predicate without enforcing case constraints (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate). The paper neither quantifies violations nor compares against simple constrained decoding. This could affect both precision and interpretability.
- Limited ablations on inputs and interactions: While depth/residual effects are explored, it is unclear how much each input component (PRED window, MARK indicator) contributes, or whether the gains stem largely from depth vs. grid interactions. A comparison to simpler multi-predicate couplings (pairwise features, cross-predicate attention) would strengthen the case for Grid-RNNs.
- Scope and generalization: Experiments are limited to intra-sentential arguments on a single corpus (NAIST 1.5). Claims about broader applicability (SRL, multilingual) are plausible but not tested. No cross-domain robustness or analysis of sentences with many predicates.
- Practical details: The approach scales with number of predicates M; the paper lacks discussion of training/inference time, memory, and behavior for sentences with many predicates. The code link is a placeholder, which limits reproducibility at review time.

Questions to authors (optional)
- How often does your model assign multiple NOM/ACC/DAT labels for a given predicate? Did you try constrained decoding (e.g., 0/1 constraints per case) and what was the impact on F1?
- Can you provide ablations removing the PRED window and MARK features, and/or using pre-trained word embeddings?
- How does training/inference time and memory usage scale with the number of predicates per sentence? What are typical runtimes on NAIST?
- Did you try simpler interaction mechanisms (e.g., cross-predicate self-attention or message passing) and how do they compare to Grid-RNN in accuracy and efficiency?
- Do gains on Zero correlate with longer predicate-argument distances? A breakdown by distance and sentence length would be helpful.
- Any error analysis for DAT improvements, given the annotation changes from 1.4β to 1.5?

Overall, I find the paper bold and impactful: despite some missing ablations and constraint handling, the core insight—jointly modeling predicates via a grid RNN to capture interactions without syntax—yields clear, significant improvements on a challenging task. I lean toward acceptance.
