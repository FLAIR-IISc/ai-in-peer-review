- Paper proposes syntax-free single-sequence BiRNN/GRU and multi-sequence Grid-RNN for Japanese PAS, focusing on intra-sentential zero arguments.
- Single-sequence model tags tokens per predicate using only word sequence and a predicate marker.
- Grid-RNN links per-predicate sequences along a predicate dimension to capture multi-predicate interactions without syntax.
- On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 both models outperform strong syntax-based baselines; Grid-RNN gives further gains, especially for zero arguments.
- Residual connections enable deeper networks and consistent performance improvements.
- Contribution: shows a syntax-free sequence model can match or slightly surpass prior state of the art with notable zero-argument gains.
- Contribution: introduces a grid-style recurrent architecture to share information across predicate-specific views.
- Contribution: provides systematic depth/residual ablations, per-case-role breakdowns, and significance testing.
- Strength: clear problem framing addressing pro-drop Japanese and pipeline error issues from parsing.
- Strength: strong empirical results without external resources; meaningful Zero and dative case F1 improvements.
- Strength: joint modeling across predicates is a natural approach and yields consistent improvements over single-predicate modeling.
- Strength: methodological clarity with described components, hyperparameters, training, and analysis choices.
- Weakness: limited novelty—Grid-RNN only mixes sequences at same token position, possibly missing broader inter-predicate structure.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to simpler alternatives (shared BiRNN with multiple markers, cross-predicate attention, learned pooling).
- Weakness: unclear decoding constraints—no description of enforcing at-most-one core argument per case per predicate or conflict resolution.
- Weakness: evaluation granularity unclear—word vs. bunsetsu/head-level mapping and treatment of multiple predictions per case not specified.
- Weakness: limited qualitative analysis showing what multi-predicate interactions the grid actually captures or examples where it helps.
- Weakness: scope limited to intra-sentential arguments and lacks comparisons to syntax-augmented or pretrained/contextual encoder models.
- Weakness: reproducibility gap—code URL is a placeholder, undermining public availability claim.
- Questions: do you enforce at-most-one argument per case per predicate, and how are conflicts/ties resolved?
- Questions: are gold arguments annotated at word or bunsetsu/head level, and how are token predictions mapped for scoring?
- Questions: do you have evidence the grid uses predicate co-occurrence/role compatibility vs. multi-tasking, with example cases?
- Questions: how does Grid-RNN compare to concatenated predicate markers, cross-predicate attention, or CRF/ILP global constraint layers?
- Questions: what is the computational cost and runtime scaling as the number of predicates per sentence increases?
- Questions: were loss weighting or sampling strategies used for rare classes (e.g., DAT, Zero), possibly explaining large DAT gains?
- Minor requests: provide actual code URL, clarify gradient clipping usage, report average predicates per sentence and average sentence length, and include a brief zero-argument error analysis.
