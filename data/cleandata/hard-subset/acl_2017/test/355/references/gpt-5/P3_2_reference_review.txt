Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential Dep and Zero arguments. It proposes two neural models without explicit syntactic features: (i) a single-sequence stacked BiRNN that predicts case labels for each predicate independently, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections enable deeper networks.
- On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, the models outperform strong syntax-based baselines (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015), with notable improvements on zero arguments. The multi-sequence model yields a small but significant gain over the single-sequence model overall and especially for Zero-NOM.
- The work suggests multi-predicate interactions can be learned from raw word sequences without parser-derived features, and explores depth/residual connections.

As I see them, the key contributions are:
1) A simple, syntax-free, sequence modeling approach that achieves new SOTA on NAIST 1.5 for intra-sentential PAS, especially for zero anaphora. 
2) A grid-style recurrent architecture across predicate and token dimensions to model multi-predicate interactions, with empirical gains over a strong single-predicate neural baseline.
3) An empirical study of depth and residual connections for PAS, showing deeper models benefit from residuals.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results without syntax: Despite using only word indices, predicate window, and a predicate mark, both models outperform the best prior syntax-heavy joint approach (Ouchi et al., 2015) on overall F1 and Zero F1. The Zero gains (~+3 F1 for multi-seq) are practically meaningful given the difficulty of zero anaphora without dependency links.
- Modeling multi-predicate interactions end-to-end: The grid-style RNN that connects sequences for different predicates is a clean, general mechanism for capturing cross-predicate signals without hand-crafted features or pipeline parsing. This contributes a reusable architectural idea beyond Japanese PAS (e.g., multipredicate SRL).
- Competitive single-sequence baseline and analysis of depth: The single-sequence model is already strong and isolates the value of sentence-level context encoding. The depth study with and without residual connections is informative; the benefit of residuals for 8-layer models improves credibility and replicability of the training recipe.
- Standard setting and significance testing: Evaluation follows the standard NAIST 1.5 splits and includes bootstrap significance tests. The comparison to widely cited prior work aids interpretability.
- Case-wise improvements: Large DAT gains over older baselines (on 1.5) suggest the network can learn less frequent patterns, countering the common concern that low-resource labels are neglected in purely neural setups.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and small multi-sequence gains: While the grid idea is neat, it is a straightforward adaptation of Grid-RNN/LSTM to this task. The overall gain of multi-sequence over single-sequence is modest (+0.27 F1 overall; ~+1.0 for Zero). Moreover, the per-case table shows mixed effects: the multi-sequence model improves Zero-NOM but degrades for Zero-ACC/DAT and for some Dep roles. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis explaining when and why interactions help or hurt.
- Missing stronger neural baselines and ablations: The paper does not compare to other syntax-free neural architectures that were contemporaneously strong (e.g., BiLSTM with richer input features, character/subword embeddings, pretrained word embeddings, global normalization or biaffine scoring). Nor does it ablate critical design choices (predicate window, mark feature, residuals beyond depth, inter-sequence connections vs simple parameter sharing, or attention-based cross-predicate interactions). This makes it hard to attribute gains specifically to the Grid-RNN mechanism.
- Under-specified architecture details: The definition of g for the grid cell is unclear. GRUs typically accept one recurrent input; here g takes concatenated inputs from the previous layer and two recurrent sources (time and predicate dimensions). How are gates parameterized, and are there separate gates per dimension? What is the scan order and does the model include reverse directions along both axes to remove order bias among predicates? Clarifying these is essential for reproducibility.
- Decoding constraints and training objective: The model uses per-token softmax per predicate without any global constraints (e.g., at-most-one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate). Prior joint models explicitly enforced such constraints. It is unclear whether any constraints are applied at inference, and how this affects F1 and error types (e.g., multiple NOMs). A constrained decoding layer or loss could be important.
- Analysis and interpretability: The central claim is that multi-predicate interactions help, yet the paper lacks qualitative examples or attention/activation analyses demonstrating what is being propagated across predicate sequences and how that resolves zero anaphora. Error analyses (especially for the observed regressions on Zero-ACC/DAT) would strengthen the claims.
- Practicality and complexity: No discussion of runtime, memory, or scaling with the number of predicates per sentence. A multi-dimensional RNN can be costly; reporting throughput or complexity and typical M on NAIST would help assess practicality.

Questions to authors
- How exactly is g implemented in the grid cell? Do you concatenate the two recurrent inputs and previous-layer state and feed them to a standard GRU, or do you use dimension-specific gates (as in Grid LSTM)? Are parameters shared across dimensions?
- Do you process the grid in both directions along token and predicate axes (e.g., odd/even layers reversing both t and m), and does this remove order bias with respect to predicate occurrence order?
- Are any hard constraints enforced at inference to prevent multiple arguments of the same case for a predicate? If not, how often do such violations occur, and would a simple constrained decoding improve results?
- What is the effect of adding common regularization (dropout, layer norm) or pretrained embeddings? Given your small embedding/hidden sizes, do such additions change the relative gains of the multi-sequence model?
- Can you provide qualitative examples where the multi-sequence model corrects errors made by the single-sequence model, especially for Zero-NOM?
- What is the average number of predicates per sentence (M), and what are training/inference runtimes for both models?

Additional comments
- Reporting micro vs macro F1 and per-sentence predicate counts could contextualize gains.
- Releasing code is important; please ensure the link is accessible and includes all training details.
