- Paper tackles Japanese PAS (Dep and Zero) with a syntax-agnostic single-sequence Bi-GRU and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that models multi-predicate interactions via an added predicate dimension and inter-sequence recurrence with residual connections.
- Single model: 81.15 F1; Grid-RNN: 81.42 F1 on NAIST 1.5, outperforming a strong joint syntactic baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015; 79.23 F1), with largest gains on Zero arguments (especially Zero-NOM).
- Contribution: a strong, simple syntax-agnostic neural baseline that beats a state-of-the-art joint syntactic model on NAIST 1.5.
- Contribution: a grid-style recurrent architecture for end-to-end multi-predicate interaction modeling that yields small but statistically significant gains over the single-predicate model.
- Contribution: empirical study showing depth and residual connections matter, with a detailed per-role breakdown highlighting where gains (mostly Zero-NOM) occur.
- Strength: demonstrates that careful sequence models over words can outperform models relying on predicted syntax, reducing pipeline error propagation.
- Strength: Grid-RNN provides an elegant way to share information across predicates without hand-designed joint features/constraints, improving Zero performance overall.
- Strength: experimental protocol is sensible—standard NAIST splits, bootstrap significance testing, and per-role (NOM/ACC/DAT; Dep/Zero) analyses.
- Strength: residual connections enable deeper RNN/Grid stacks, a practical insight for PAS/SRL modeling.
- Strength: architecture is generic and likely transferable to (multi-)predicate SRL in other languages.
- Weakness: limited algorithmic novelty—adapts known multi-dimensional RNN/Grid ideas to PAS without major new methods.
- Weakness: incomplete baselines and ablations—missing controlled comparisons to contemporary neural PAS work and ablations for inter-sequence connections, predicate features, position/distance, and global decoding.
- Weakness: mixed efficacy of multi-predicate modeling—overall Grid gains driven by Zero-NOM while Zero-ACC/Zero-DAT and Dep-DAT sometimes degrade; lacks analysis of when grid helps or hurts.
- Weakness: uses token-wise softmax without explicit global constraints (e.g., one filler per case role); unclear how contradictory predictions are resolved compared to joint/ILP systems.
- Weakness: limited feature exploration—only word embeddings, predicate window and marker used; no POS/morphology, subword, pretrained embeddings, or lightweight syntactic signals tested.
- Weakness: reproducibility/engineering details missing—code URL is placeholder; no runtime/memory scaling, predicate-per-sentence stats, variance across runs, or significance vs external baselines reported.
- Question: how are one-argument-per-case constraints enforced or handled at test time, and were constrained decoding or ILP tried?
- Question: how do multi-sequence gains vary with number of predicates per sentence and with antecedent distance for Zero cases?
- Question: were 2D bidirectional scans or attention-based inter-sequence connections experimented with, and how sensitive are results to the Grid-RNN design?
- Question: what are the ablation results for removing the predicate window or mark feature, and does adding distance/positional embeddings help Zero resolution?
- Question: can the authors provide controlled comparisons to Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016) on NAIST 1.5, and significance tests against Ouchi et al. (2015)?
- Question: are predicates gold or predicted, are tokenization/morphological assumptions standardized to NAIST, and is syntax used only for Dep vs Zero categorization at evaluation?
