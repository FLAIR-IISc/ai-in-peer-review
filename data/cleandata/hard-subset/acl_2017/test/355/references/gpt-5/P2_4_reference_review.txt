Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes two neural sequence models that do not use syntactic parses: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN model that predicts case labels for all tokens for a given predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. The models use simple token and predicate-marking features, optionally with residual connections, and are trained with a token-level softmax. On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, both models outperform strong prior systems; the Grid-RNN yields the best F1 overall (81.42) and especially improves Zero arguments (47.12), with significance over the single-sequence model.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A neural Grid-RNN architecture that jointly encodes multiple predicate-specific sequences to capture multi-predicate interactions without syntactic input, yielding state-of-the-art results on NAIST 1.5.
2) Demonstration that purely sequence-based neural modeling (no syntax) is competitive and superior to syntax-dependent prior SOTA for Japanese PAS, particularly for Zero arguments.
3) An analysis of depth and residual connections showing deeper models with residuals improve performance and training stability.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over strong baselines, especially on Zero arguments. The multi-sequence model achieves 81.42 overall F1 vs. 79.23 (Ouchi et al., 2015) and 78.15 (Imamura et al., 2009). For Zero arguments, improvements are substantial: 47.12 vs. 44.09 (Ouchi et al.) and 41.65 (Imamura). The authors also report a significant albeit smaller gain of the Grid-RNN over the single-sequence model (81.42 vs. 81.15 overall; 47.12 vs. 46.10 Zero), supporting the value of multi-predicate interactions.
- Methodologically sound and well-motivated modeling of multi-predicate interactions. Extending stacked BiRNNs to a grid over predicate sequences is a natural, principled way to allow inter-sequence information flow (via inter-sequence connections). This directly targets the key linguistic intuition leveraged in prior work (interactions among predicates) while removing dependence on predicted syntax and its error propagation.
- No reliance on syntactic parsers or external resources, yet achieving SOTA. Prior top systems relied on syntactic features and often suffered pipeline errors. Showing superior performance with only token sequence and predicate marking underscores the strength and potential robustness/portability of the approach (important for pro-drop languages and domains where parsers are weak).
- Ablations on depth and residual connections add credibility. The depth study (2/4/6/8 layers) with/without residuals shows consistent improvements with deeper networks when using residual connections, indicating careful engineering and training insights for sequence models in PAS.
- Comprehensive evaluation across case roles. The model improves not only overall and Zero but also Dep and per-role (NOM/ACC/DAT), with particularly large gains for DAT on NAIST 1.5, suggesting the learned representations generalize beyond the zero anaphora focus.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of what the model learns and why multi-predicate interactions help. While the Grid-RNN’s gains are statistically significant, the delta over the single-sequence model is modest overall. The paper would benefit from qualitative/error analysis and ablative studies of the inter-sequence connections (e.g., removing them, varying interaction radius, or visualizing attention/activation patterns) to strengthen the causal link between interactions and improvements, especially for Zero.
- Decoding and structural constraints are unclear. The model labels each token via softmax over {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null} per predicate. It is not specified whether constraints (e.g., at most one argument per case) are enforced at decoding. Without constraints, multiple tokens could be predicted with the same case for a predicate. Clarifying the evaluation protocol (token-level vs. argument-level selection) and whether global or local constraints are applied is important for interpretability and comparability.
- Reproducibility concerns. The paper claims public code, but the URL is a placeholder (“http:xxx”). Given reliance on implementation/training details (Theano, small embedding/hidden sizes, Grid-RNN schedule), reproducibility hinges on code availability. Also, some details are missing (e.g., exact hidden/state dimensions in Grid-RNN per gate; batching strategy across sentences with different M; runtime/memory).
- Scope limited to intra-sentential PAS and a single dataset. While aligned with prior work, the generality claim (e.g., to other pro-drop languages) is not empirically validated. Additional testing on other corpora or at least stronger cross-domain/robustness checks would bolster significance. Similarly, there is no comparison to a syntax-aware neural baseline (e.g., adding predicted dependencies or simple head features) to quantify the trade-off of discarding syntax.
- Computational complexity and scalability not discussed. Grid-RNN introduces O(MT) states with inter-sequence dependencies and a specific scan order, potentially limiting parallelism and increasing memory for sentences with many predicates. Reporting training/inference speed and memory consumption would help assess practicality.

Questions to authors
- Decoding constraints: Do you enforce uniqueness constraints per case for each predicate at inference time? If yes, how (e.g., selecting the argmax word per case, ILP, or post-hoc suppression)? How does this affect F1 compared to unconstrained token-level labeling?
- Evaluation protocol: Are the reported F-measures computed at token-level labeling (precision/recall over labeled tokens) or at argument-level selection per case per predicate? Please detail the exact scoring method to ensure comparability with Ouchi et al. (2015).
- Multi-predicate grid specifics: How do you handle sentences with different numbers of predicates during batching? What is the computational overhead vs. the single-sequence model (training time per epoch, GPU memory)?
- Feature ablations: What is the contribution of the predicate-window feature and the MARK feature? Have you tried removing/expanding them or using character/subword embeddings?
- Code availability: Will you release the code and scripts with the exact configurations used (including data preprocessing and evaluation) upon publication?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed paper with meaningful empirical gains and an elegant modeling idea. Addressing the decoding/evaluation clarity and reproducibility concerns would further strengthen it.
