Summary
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments, especially zero arguments in a pro-drop language. The authors propose two neural models that avoid explicit syntactic features: (i) a single-sequence stacked BiRNN that labels case roles for each predicate independently; and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections enable deeper networks. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, both models outperform strong baselines (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015), with the grid model yielding the best overall F1 and notable gains on zero arguments.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A syntax-free, joint neural architecture (Grid-RNN) for modeling multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS that achieves new SOTA on NAIST 1.5, particularly improving zero argument identification.
2) Empirical evidence that deep, residual BiRNNs over word sequences (without parsers or selectional preference features) capture long-distance dependencies sufficient for competitive PAS, simplifying pipelines and reducing error propagation.
3) An analysis showing depth and residual connections matter, and a decomposition of results by case and by Dep vs Zero.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical results without syntax. Both neural models outperform the best prior joint model that leverages syntactic/selectional features (Ouchi et al., 2015), with statistically significant gains overall and on zero arguments (+3.0 F1 over Ouchi+15 for Zero; single +2.0, multi +3.0). This demonstrates the viability of syntax-free PAS with learned sequence representations.

2) Modeling multi-predicate interactions improves zero arguments. The Grid-RNN brings consistent, significant gains over the single-predicate baseline (+0.27 F1 overall, +1.0 Zero), supporting the linguistic intuition that predicate interactions help zero anaphora where dependency clues are absent.

3) Architectural clarity and simplicity. The input features are minimal (word ids, predicate-centered window, predicate marker), yet the models perform strongly. The grid layer formulation is a principled instantiation of multidimensional recurrence for cross-predicate information flow.

4) Depth and residual connection study. The paper systematically varies the number of layers and shows residual connections enable effective training of deeper networks, yielding up to ~1 F1 improvement. This increases confidence in the engineering choices.

5) Case-wise breakdown and task relevance. The analysis by case (NOM/ACC/DAT) and Dep vs Zero is informative; large gains on DAT and zero-NOM suggest the models learn long-distance dependencies, which is central to the Japanese PAS problem.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty and modest grid gains. While applying Grid-RNNs to PAS is interesting, the core components (stacked BiRNNs, Grid-RNNs, residuals) are established. The added benefit of the grid over the single-sequence model is relatively small (+0.27 overall F1; +1.0 Zero), raising questions about whether the extra architectural and computational complexity is justified.

2) Lack of structural constraints or decoding. The model uses per-token softmax without CRF/ILP or explicit global constraints (e.g., uniqueness of an argument per case for a predicate). The paper does not clarify if multiple tokens can be labeled NOM/ACC/DAT for a predicate and how such violations are handled. This is a standard consideration in PAS/SRL and could affect precision.

3) Missing ablations on input features and training signals. No study of the predicate window size C, the impact of the MARK feature, or alternatives like pre-trained embeddings, character/subword features, or POS. Since the input is intentionally minimal, ablations are important to understand which parts are necessary vs superfluous.

4) Reproducibility and resource reporting. The code link is a placeholder; training details omit runtime, memory footprint, and complexity vs number of predicates. The Grid-RNN implies sequential dependencies along both sentence and predicate dimensions; scalability and batching for variable M are not discussed.

5) Scope limitations and baseline coverage. The work restricts to intra-sentential PAS with given predicates, which is standard but should be explicitly discussed in terms of end-to-end applicability. Also, comparison to Shibata et al. (2016) on NAIST 1.5 is missing (even if features differ), and broader comparisons to other neural baselines would strengthen claims.

Questions to Authors (optional)
1) Constraint handling: Do you enforce at most one argument per case per predicate? If not, how often do constraint violations occur, and how do they affect F1? Did you try simple post-processing (e.g., pick the top-scoring token per case)?

2) Computational cost: What are training/inference runtimes and memory for single vs grid models, and how do they scale with sentence length and number of predicates M? Any strategies for parallelization across M?

3) Ablations: What is the effect of removing the MARK feature or varying C? Did pre-trained embeddings or character/subword representations help? How critical are residual connections at shallow depths?

4) Error analysis: Can you provide qualitative/quantitative error analysis, especially for zero-NOM vs zero-DAT, and as a function of predicate-argument distance and number of predicates?

5) Output label set: Why include PRED in the label set if the predicate position is already marked in the input? Does predicting PRED help learning, and what is its accuracy?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with a clear idea and careful evaluation; addressing structural constraints, ablations, and reproducibility details would strengthen it further.
