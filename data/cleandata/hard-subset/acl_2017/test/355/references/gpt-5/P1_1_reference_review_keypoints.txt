- Task and focus: Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis targeting intra-sentential Dep and Zero arguments.
- Proposed models: single-sequence BiRNN predicting cases per predicate and multi-sequence Grid-RNN jointly modeling all predicates.
- Empirical result: Both models beat prior work on NAIST 1.5, with Grid-RNN achieving highest overall F1 and best Zero argument performance.
- Contribution 1: Effective syntax-free neural architecture achieving new SOTA on NAIST 1.5, notably for Zero arguments.
- Contribution 2: Grid-style recurrent architecture modeling multi-predicate interactions that yields significant gains over single-predicate modeling.
- Contribution 3: Empirical study showing benefits of deeper networks with residual connections for PAS.
- Strength: Strong empirical gains without parser-derived features, reducing pipeline error propagation.
- Strength: Substantial improvements on Zero argument identification, with notable per-case gains (e.g., DAT).
- Strength: Evidence that modeling multi-predicate interactions improves performance, aligning with prior joint work.
- Strength: Careful experimental setup using standard splits, per-type/case reporting, and significance testing.
- Strength: Useful analysis of network depth and residuals, showing ~+1 F1 from deeper stacks with residuals.
- Weakness: Methodological novelty is incremental—Grid-RNNs and multi-predicate ideas are known from prior work.
- Weakness: No explicit structural decoding constraints (e.g., at-most-one argument per case), leaving ambiguity about conflict resolution.
- Weakness: Ablations are incomplete—unclear if multi-sequence gains come from inter-sequence connections or increased capacity/parameterization.
- Weakness: Limited qualitative/diagnostic analysis demonstrating exactly how multi-predicate signals help disambiguate arguments.
- Weakness: Practical scalability and runtime/memory costs of Grid-RNN (which grows with predicates and length) are not reported; small batch sizes hint at high cost.
- Question: How are decoding constraints (uniqueness of core arguments per case per predicate) enforced or handled at inference?
- Question: Are single- and multi-sequence models matched for parameter count; can a parameter-matched comparison isolate inter-sequence effects?
- Question: Are improvements over Ouchi+15 statistically significant as well, beyond single vs multi significance?
- Question: What are training and inference times and how do they scale with number of predicates M and sentence length T?
- Question: Can the authors provide qualitative examples where multi-predicate interactions corrected single-sequence errors (especially Zero NOM)?
- Question: Are predicates assumed gold at test time, and how sensitive is the approach to predicate detection errors?
