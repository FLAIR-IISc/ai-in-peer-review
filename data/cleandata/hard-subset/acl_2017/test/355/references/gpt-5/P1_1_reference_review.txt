Summary and contributions
This paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). The authors propose two neural sequence models that avoid explicit syntactic inputs: (1) a single-sequence BiRNN that predicts case labels for each predicate independently, and (2) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models surpass prior work, with the Grid-RNN yielding the best overall F1 and notably improving Zero argument identification.

Key contributions (as I see them):
1) An effective syntax-free neural architecture for Japanese PAS that achieves new state-of-the-art performance on NAIST 1.5, especially for Zero arguments.
2) A principled, grid-style recurrent architecture to model multi-predicate interactions jointly, showing statistically significant gains over an otherwise strong single-predicate BiRNN.
3) Empirical analysis of network depth and residual connections in this task, demonstrating consistent benefits of deeper models with residuals.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains without syntactic features: The single-sequence and multi-sequence models outperform strong baselines (Imamura et al. 2009; Ouchi et al. 2015) while avoiding parser-derived inputs, reducing pipeline error propagation. The overall F1 improves from 79.23 (Ouchi+15) to 81.15 (single) and 81.42 (multi).
- Clear benefit on Zero arguments: The hardest subtask sees the largest gains: Zero F1 improves from 44.09 (Ouchi+15) to 46.10 (single) and 47.12 (multi), with the multi-sequence model significantly outperforming the single-sequence model (+1.0). Per-case breakdowns show notable improvements, including sizeable gains on DAT.
- Modeling multi-predicate interactions pays off: The Grid-RNN yields statistically significant improvements over the single-sequence model overall and on Zero. This supports the intuition (and prior non-neural joint work) that interactions among predicates are informative; here they are captured end-to-end without explicit syntactic scaffolding.
- Careful experimental setup and standard splits: The experiments use NAIST 1.5 with established splits and compare to recognized baselines. Results include per-type and per-case scores and a significance test (bootstrap) for single vs multi.
- Analysis of depth and residuals: The study of stacked layers and residual connections provides insight into optimization for sequence models in PAS; deeper stacks with residuals improve performance (~+1 F1), informing future model design.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling approach: Grid-RNNs are known, and multi-predicate interactions for PAS/SRL have been explored (e.g., Ouchi+15; Yang & Zong 2014). The main novelty is applying Grid-RNNs to Japanese PAS without syntax. While the empirical result is valuable, the methodological contribution is incremental.
- Lack of structural decoding constraints: The model uses token-wise softmax without explicit global constraints (e.g., at-most-one argument per case per predicate), unlike prior joint/ILP approaches. It is unclear how the method prevents multiple NOM/ACC/DAT predictions for a predicate or resolves ties/conflicts, and whether enforcing constraints would further improve accuracy.
- Incomplete ablations on the source of gains: The multi-sequence model differs from the single-sequence model both in architecture and (apparently) parameterization (different input dimensionalities are noted). It is unclear whether gains stem from inter-sequence connections per se, increased capacity, or training dynamics. A controlled ablation (e.g., parameter-matched models, shuffling inter-sequence connections, or removing residuals only in one) would strengthen the claim.
- Limited analysis of multi-predicate interaction effects: Beyond headline F1, the paper lacks qualitative or diagnostic evidence showing how one predicate helps another (e.g., case co-occurrence patterns, attention/activation analyses, or specific examples where inter-predicate signals disambiguate zero pronouns).
- Practical considerations and scalability: The Grid-RNN scales with number of predicates M and sentence length T, but training/inference complexity and runtime are not reported. Batch sizes are small (2–8), suggesting computational heaviness. Discussion of efficiency, memory footprint, and latency would be helpful, especially versus joint models with structured inference.

Questions to authors
- Decoding constraints: How do you enforce (or encourage) uniqueness of core arguments per case per predicate at inference? Have you observed multiple tokens labeled as the same case for a predicate, and if so, how are conflicts resolved?
- Parameter control: Are the single- and multi-sequence models matched in parameter count? If not, can you provide a parameter-matched comparison to isolate the effect of inter-sequence connections?
- Significance vs prior SOTA: You report significance between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq. Are improvements over Ouchi+15 also statistically significant?
- Runtime/complexity: What are training and inference times for both models, and how do they scale with M and T? How does the multi-sequence Grid-RNN compare to Ouchi+15’s joint model in runtime?
- Error analysis: Can you provide qualitative examples where multi-predicate interactions corrected errors (especially Zero NOM) that the single-sequence model made?
- Predicate identification: Do you assume gold predicates at test time? If so, how sensitive is the approach to predicate detection errors?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with a clear, well-motivated architecture and strong results, especially notable for being syntax-free. Addressing decoding constraints, providing tighter ablations, and adding qualitative analyses would considerably strengthen the work.
