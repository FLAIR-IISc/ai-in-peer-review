Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes two neural sequence models that do not use syntactic parses: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN that predicts case labels for each predicate independently, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections are used to train deeper networks.
- Main contributions (in order of strength):
  1) A grid-type recurrent architecture that jointly processes all predicate–candidate sequences to model multi-predicate interactions without syntactic features, yielding SOTA results on NAIST 1.5, particularly for zero anaphora.
  2) An empirical finding that sentence-level contextualization via RNNs and cross-predicate coupling improves Zero argument identification over strong joint baselines that rely on syntax (Ouchi et al., 2015).
  3) A practical, parser-free PAS pipeline with analysis of network depth and residual connections.

Strengths
- Empirical effectiveness without syntax: On the standard NAIST 1.5 split, both models outperform strong baselines; the multi-sequence Grid-RNN achieves 81.42 F overall vs 79.23 for Ouchi et al. (2015). The gains on Zero arguments are substantial (47.12 vs 44.09 F), directly addressing the hardest aspect of Japanese PAS. This supports the central claim that contextualized sequence modeling can reduce reliance on syntactic pipelines and mitigate error propagation.
- Joint modeling of multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN design that propagates information across predicates and time is well-motivated for PAS, where predicates in a sentence are interdependent. The comparison to the single-sequence baseline (81.42 vs 81.15 F overall; +1.0 F on Zero) isolates the benefit of multi-predicate coupling.
- Careful experimental setup on a standard benchmark: Uses the accepted NAIST 1.5 split, compares to strong reimplemented baselines, reports per-argument-type and per-case metrics, and employs bootstrap significance testing (for Single vs Multi). The depth/residuals study shows that residual connections enable deeper, better models.
- Simplicity and resource efficiency: Models use only word and predicate mark embeddings and small hidden sizes (32), no external resources, and still achieve SOTA. This increases practicality for languages/domains with weaker parsing tools.
- Clarity of the core method: The paper provides a clear description of the input representations (ARG/PRED/MARK), the stacked BiRNNs, and the Grid-RNN inter-sequence connections with residual links.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior art: Grid-RNNs are established, and multi-predicate interactions have been exploited in Japanese PAS (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015). The main novelty is the specific application of grid coupling without syntax. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis of what interactions are captured and why they help beyond prior joint models.
- Decoding/constraints are underspecified: The model assigns a case label per token per predicate via softmax, with no explicit global constraints (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate) or inference procedure. This can yield inconsistent outputs (multiple NOMs for the same predicate). It is unclear how such conflicts are handled or whether post-processing is applied. Given that prior joint models enforced structural constraints, this is a notable gap in the methodology description.
- Limited ablations and analysis: While depth/residuals are studied, there is no ablation of input features (e.g., impact of PRED window, MARK), no explicit ablation of the vertical (inter-predicate) connections vs. deeper single-sequence models, and no qualitative/error analysis to substantiate the claim that multi-predicate interactions drive the Zero gains. Without this, it is hard to disentangle benefits from added capacity vs. genuine interaction modeling.
- Scope and generalization: Evaluation is limited to one language and one corpus (NAIST 1.5), with no cross-domain or cross-lingual experiments, despite claims of applicability to SRL broadly. The paper references NAIST 1.4β but comparability is limited; also, significance vs. prior work is not reported.
- Reproducibility/engineering details: The code URL appears to be a placeholder (“http:xxx”). Runtime and memory costs of Grid-RNNs (O(MT) states per layer) are not discussed, nor are typical values of M (predicates per sentence) in the corpus. Theano implementation and small embedding sizes are noted, but practical training/inference efficiency vs. baselines is unclear.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- How do you enforce structural constraints at decoding time? Specifically, how do you ensure at most one argument per case per predicate, and how are ties/conflicts resolved? If no constraints are used, what is the empirical rate of inconsistencies?
- What is the marginal contribution of the inter-sequence (vertical) connections? Please provide an ablation that removes or shuffles vertical edges while keeping depth constant, and quantify the effect (especially on Zero).
- Input feature ablations: How much do the PRED window size (C), MARK feature, and ARG embeddings contribute? Is the PRED window essential? Please report performance for C=0 and without MARK.
- Can you provide qualitative examples where Grid-RNN changes a prediction relative to Single-Seq, illustrating the multi-predicate interaction used?
- Efficiency/scalability: What are average M and T on NAIST 1.5? What are training/inference times compared to the single-sequence model and to Ouchi et al. (2015)? How does performance/efficiency degrade with long sentences or many predicates?
- Statistical testing: Do the improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015) reach significance overall and for Zero? Please report p-values.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid, well-executed paper with clear motivation and strong empirical results, especially for zero anaphora, achieved without syntactic features. While methodological novelty is moderate and some important details (decoding constraints, ablations, qualitative analysis) are missing, the empirical contribution on a standard benchmark and the practical relevance of a parser-free approach warrant publication. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying decoding, adding key ablations, and providing qualitative evidence for the claimed multi-predicate interactions.
