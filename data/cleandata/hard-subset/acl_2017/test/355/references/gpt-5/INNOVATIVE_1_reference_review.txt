Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) that dispense with syntactic features and instead learn from word sequences. A single-sequence BiRNN model predicts arguments per predicate; a multi-sequence Grid-RNN jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST 1.5, both models outperform prior systems, with the grid model particularly improving zero arguments.
- Main contributions (as I see them):
  1) A grid-style multi-sequence recurrent architecture that jointly encodes predicates and their candidate arguments to capture inter-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic parses.
  2) Empirical demonstration that syntax-free sequence models can match or surpass syntax-heavy SOTA for Japanese PAS, with especially strong gains on zero anaphora.
  3) Analysis of model depth and residual connections for PAS, showing deeper grids with residuals are beneficial.

Strengths
- Novel and bold modeling of multi-predicate interactions: Adapting Grid/MD-RNN ideas to PAS is an original and compelling architectural choice for this task. Jointly encoding all predicates in a sentence in a grid and propagating information across predicate and token axes is a clean, end-to-end alternative to previous parser-dependent joint inference.
- Removes a major pipeline dependency while improving zero arguments: The model avoids syntactic parsers (a major source of error propagation), yet improves Zero F1 by ~2–3 points over the strongest prior system. This is precisely where eliminating parse errors should matter and aligns with the stated linguistic motivation.
- Solid empirical gains and significance tests: On NAIST 1.5, both single- and multi-sequence models beat strong baselines. The grid model’s gains over the single model are statistically significant, and improvements are consistent across case roles, with large benefits for DAT.
- Depth/residual ablations are informative: The paper shows deeper grids with residual connections improve performance and stabilize optimization—useful guidance for future work on deep sequence models for PAS/SRL.
- Simplicity and generality: The input features are minimal (word indices + predicate marker), no external resources, and no handcrafted syntactic features, suggesting good portability to other languages and SRL variants.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines for the multi-predicate interaction claim: The paper compares grid vs single-sequence but does not benchmark against simpler inter-predicate mechanisms (e.g., shared BiRNN with predicate-aware attention/cross-predicate attention, pooling across predicate-conditioned sequences, or a reranking/joint decoding layer). It’s hard to isolate if the grid per se is the main driver versus any reasonable inter-predicate information sharing.
- Methodological clarity on Grid-GRU is insufficient: The recurrence g(·) is said to be a GRU but is fed multiple recurrent states (h_{m−1,t}, h_{m,t−1}) plus the previous-layer state. It is unclear how these are integrated (concatenation into a single input? separate gates per dimension? parameter ties across directions?), whether there is dimension-specific gating (as in Grid LSTM), and how alternating directions mitigate order bias across both axes. A more precise definition is needed for reproducibility.
- Lack of structural decoding constraints: Japanese PAS typically has structural constraints (e.g., uniqueness of certain core roles per predicate). The model predicts via independent softmax per token per predicate without CRF/ILP or global constraints. It would be informative to show whether adding simple decoding constraints or consistency checks changes results, and whether the grid helps implicitly enforce them.
- Scalability/efficiency not addressed: The grid adds an extra recurrent dimension over predicates, which could be non-trivial for sentences with many predicates. There is no analysis of time/memory cost, typical M, and throughput relative to single-sequence.
- Scope and analysis: The work focuses on intra-sentential arguments only (standard, but still a limitation) and provides limited qualitative/error analysis to understand where inter-predicate signals help or fail. Also, while the paper emphasizes “no syntax,” it omits easily available morphological/subword features for Japanese; a brief ablation on adding POS/morphology or char/subword embeddings would strengthen the claim that syntax is unnecessary rather than that the model is under-featured.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Grid-GRU formulation: Please formalize g precisely. How are h_{m−1,t} and h_{m,t−1} combined with h^{(ℓ−1)}_{m,t}? Is there dimension-wise gating akin to Grid LSTM? Are parameters shared across directions and layers? Do odd/even layers reverse both m and t directions?
- Complexity and scalability: What are the typical values of M per sentence? What is the training/inference time and memory overhead of multi-sequence vs single-sequence given L layers? Any batching strategy across different M?
- Structural constraints: Do you enforce uniqueness constraints for NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate at decoding? If not, how often are constraints violated, and does post-hoc constraint enforcement affect F1?
- Baseline alternatives: How does the grid compare to an attention-based multi-predicate model (e.g., mutual attention between predicate-conditioned sequences) or a shared BiRNN with a joint CRF/ILP layer? Even small-scale comparisons would help isolate the value of the grid.
- Significance vs Ouchi et al. (2015): You report significance between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq. Are the improvements over Ouchi+15 also statistically significant on overall and Zero F1?
- Reproducibility: The code link appears placeholder. Will you release code and scripts, including seeds and bootstrap settings? Could you report variance across runs?

Overall, I find the idea of grid-style joint modeling across predicates both novel and well-motivated for PAS, with clear empirical benefits on zero anaphora and without relying on syntax. Despite some missing comparisons and methodological clarifications, the conceptual advance and results warrant acceptance.
