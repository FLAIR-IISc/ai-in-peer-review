Summary and main contributions
The paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero) without using syntactic parses. It introduces:
1) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture that jointly models interactions among all predicates in a sentence by connecting sequences of argument candidates across predicates, enabling multi-predicate interaction without syntactic information.
2) A strong syntax-free single-sequence baseline using stacked Bi-GRUs and simple token-level features (ARG, PRED-window, MARK) with a softmax output per token.
3) An empirical study on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 showing new state-of-the-art results without external resources, with notable gains on zero arguments; analysis of network depth and residual connections.

Strengths
- Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions without syntax: The Grid-RNN design explicitly propagates information across predicate-specific sequences (inter-sequence connections), operationalizing the intuition behind prior joint models but in an end-to-end, syntax-free way. The multi-sequence model improves overall F1 (81.42 vs 81.15) and especially Zero F1 (47.12 vs 46.10) over the single-sequence baseline with statistical significance (bootstrap, p<0.05). This supports the central claim that predicate–predicate interactions are beneficial even without parsed structures.

- Strong empirical performance and fair experimental setup: On NAIST 1.5, both proposed models outperform strong baselines including Ouchi et al. (2015) (joint model with syntax) and Imamura et al. (2009) (pointwise). The multi-sequence model achieves +2.19 F1 overall and +3.03 Zero F1 over Ouchi et al. Despite not using syntactic features or external resources, the models particularly improve difficult categories (e.g., DAT, Zero-NOM). Standard data splits and bootstrap significance are used, and per-role breakdowns clarify where gains arise.

- Clear architectural specification and sensible training choices: The paper details input construction (ARG/PRED/MARK), layer computation (stacked Bi-GRUs; grid connections along predicate and time dimensions), residual connections, and hyperparameters (depth, embeddings, optimization). The study of depth and residual connections shows consistent improvements for 8-layer models, indicating careful engineering and providing guidance for practitioners.

- Reduction of pipeline error propagation: By avoiding dependency parses while matching or exceeding syntax-enabled baselines, the work makes a compelling case for sequence-based PAS, especially for Zero arguments where parse information is less directly available.

Weaknesses
- Decoding and role uniqueness constraints are unclear: The model performs per-token softmax over labels including case roles and null, but PAS typically assumes at most one argument per case per predicate. The paper does not specify how predictions are converted to a valid set of arguments (e.g., selecting the highest-scoring token per role, tie-breaking, handling multiple tokens labeled as NOM). This omission affects technical soundness and reproducibility, particularly for comparing against joint models that enforce global constraints.

- Limited novelty beyond application: Grid-/multidimensional RNNs are established (Graves & Schmidhuber, Kalchbrenner et al.), and multi-predicate joint modeling for PAS is known (e.g., Ouchi et al.). The single-sequence model is a straightforward adaptation of standard BiRNN sequence labeling (e.g., Zhou & Xu). The contribution is primarily an application and engineering of Grid-RNN to Japanese PAS without syntax, rather than a fundamentally new modeling principle.

- Missing ablations and alternative baselines: The paper does not disentangle contributions of key design choices. Useful ablations would include removing inter-sequence connections (keeping parameter sharing only), varying the PRED-window and MARK features, or adding simple global constraints at decoding time. Comparisons to a syntax-free adaptation of prior SRL architectures (e.g., BiRNN with per-predicate max-over-words decoding) are absent. It is also unclear whether adding syntactic features to the proposed models would further help, which would contextualize the “syntax-free” advantage versus “syntax-augmented” performance.

- Some clarity and notation issues: The loss is presented as −∑t log P(yt|xt) (single sequence), but the multi-sequence extension is not formalized (ym,t). The scanning/order for even/odd grid layers is described briefly; the exact computation graph and boundary conditions could be clearer. References contain placeholders and inconsistencies, and figures critical to understanding are not visible here. While not fatal, these issues hinder precise replication.

- Scope and analysis: The work focuses on intra-sentential PAS (a standard choice) but would benefit from an error analysis to substantiate claims about long-distance dependencies and to characterize failure modes (e.g., which Zero cases remain difficult). Computational cost and scalability with number of predicates M are not reported.

Questions to authors
- Decoding: How do you ensure at most one argument per case per predicate at test time? If you classify each token with a softmax, do you subsequently select the single highest-scoring token per role, and how do you handle ties or the case where all tokens are near-null? Please detail the exact decoding and any constraints.

- Evaluation protocol: Are predicates given (gold) at test time? Are there any sentences with multiple valid arguments for the same role, and if so, how is that handled in scoring?

- Significance testing: You report significance for Multi-Seq vs Single-Seq. Did you also test significance vs Ouchi et al. (2015)? If so, please report.

- Ablations: What is the effect of removing inter-sequence connections (i.e., sharing parameters across predicates but no grid links)? How much do MARK and PRED-window features contribute?

- Complexity: What are the training/inference time and memory footprints as a function of sentence length T and number of predicates M? Any practical constraints for long sentences with many predicates?

- Syntax augmentation: Have you tried adding syntactic features to your models? This would help quantify how much of the baseline gap is due to parsing versus modeling.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, technically sound application of grid-type RNNs to Japanese PAS with strong empirical results, though details on decoding and stronger ablations would strengthen the contribution and reproducibility.
