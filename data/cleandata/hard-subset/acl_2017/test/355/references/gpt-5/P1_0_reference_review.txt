Summary and contributions
- The paper targets Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis with a focus on intra-sentential arguments, especially difficult zero anaphora. It proposes two neural models that avoid explicit syntactic features: (i) a strong single-sequence BiRNN/GRU model that predicts case labels for all tokens for a given predicate; and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly encodes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions via inter-sequence recurrent connections and residual links.
- On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, both models outperform strong syntax-based baselines, with the multi-sequence model achieving the best overall F1 (81.42 vs. 79.23 for Ouchi et al., 2015) and notable gains for zero arguments (+~3 F1 over Ouchi; +~1 F1 over the single-sequence baseline).
- Additional empirical contributions include: (a) showing that deep residual connections enable training of 6–8 layer RNN/Grid-RNNs and improve F1 by ~1 point; (b) detailed case-wise results (notably large gains for DAT), and significance testing.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful finding: strong syntax-free models. Despite using only word indices and a predicate mark (no parse trees or selectional preferences), both neural models surpass the best published syntax-based joint model on NAIST 1.5. This is important for robustness and portability across domains/languages where quality parsing is expensive or brittle.
- Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions. The Grid-RNN formulation is a principled way to let information flow across predicate-specific sequences. The multi-sequence model yields statistically significant improvements over an already strong single-sequence baseline, especially for zero arguments (e.g., Zero F1: 47.12 vs. 46.10).
- Strong zero anaphora performance. Zero arguments are the key pain point in Japanese PAS; the models improve Zero F1 by ~2–3 points over prior SOTA and by ~1 point via multi-predicate interactions, supporting the stated linguistic intuition with measurable gains.
- Depth and residuals matter. The paper demonstrates that deeper RNN/Grid-RNN stacks with residual connections improve performance (~+1 F1), contributing practical guidance to the community on training deep recurrent models for PAS/SRL.
- Careful experimental setup. Standard NAIST 1.5 splits; bootstrap significance testing; ablation on depth/residuals; comparison to both pointwise and joint baselines; no external resources, increasing reproducibility and isolating the effect of the architectures.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The single-sequence model is a straightforward BiRNN token classifier; the multi-sequence model is an application of Grid-RNNs to PAS. While the application is apt, the architectural contribution is incremental relative to existing multi-dimensional RNN ideas. The paper would be strengthened by comparing to other simple alternatives for multi-predicate interaction (e.g., shared encoders with cross-predicate attention, multi-task training, or late joint decoding).
- Modest aggregate gains of the multi-sequence model over the single-sequence baseline. The overall delta is small (81.42 vs. 81.15 F1), with mixed per-metric changes (e.g., small decreases for some Dep subcases). The paper does not quantify computational overhead of Grid-RNNs (time/memory scaling in M×T), leaving the practical trade-off unclear.
- No global structural constraints or analysis thereof. Predictions are made independently per predicate/token via softmax, without enforcing case-unique or argument-structure constraints commonly used in PAS/SRL (e.g., ILP, constrained decoding). A comparison showing whether simple constraints or CRF-style dependencies help or are redundant with Grid-RNN context would clarify the modeling choice.
- Limited analysis and ablation for why interactions help. The paper does not analyze when multi-predicate information yields gains (e.g., as a function of number of predicates per sentence, argument–predicate distance, or specific zero pronoun patterns). Similarly, ablations on inter-sequence connectivity directionality, window size C, or removing residuals are only partially explored; error analysis is lacking.
- Scope and generality claims are not empirically validated. The approach is tested on a single dataset/language (NAIST 1.5, Japanese), and excludes inter-sentential zero anaphora. Claims about applicability to broader SRL are plausible but untested. Also, the surprisingly large gains for DAT Dep (e.g., ~27–35 points over prior work) merit deeper explanation, given annotation changes from 1.4β to 1.5 and rarity of DAT arguments.

Questions to authors
- Computational efficiency: What are the training/inference time and memory costs of the multi-sequence model relative to the single-sequence model, as a function of number of predicates per sentence? How many predicates per sentence are typical in the dataset?
- Constraints: Did you try simple post-hoc constraints (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate) or a CRF/ILP layer? If so, how did they interact with the Grid-RNN; if not, can you comment on expected effects?
- Analysis of multi-predicate benefits: Can you provide breakdowns showing where Grid-RNN helps most (e.g., sentences with ≥2 predicates, long-distance zero anaphora, shared subjects/causatives)?
- DAT improvements: What drives the large gains for DAT Dep? Is it primarily recall? Any linguistic patterns you observed?
- External signals: While you intentionally avoided external resources, did you try POS/morphology or pre-trained embeddings? Do they complement your sequence/context modeling?
- Reproducibility: The code URL is a placeholder (“http:xxx”). Will you release code and trained models, and are there any non-trivial preprocessing steps beyond NAIST word boundaries?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with a clean idea and convincing results on a challenging task. The novelty is moderate, and the multi-sequence gains are modest but meaningful for zero anaphora. With stronger analysis, comparisons to alternative interaction mechanisms and/or structural constraints, and clarified efficiency trade-offs, this work would make a valuable contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
