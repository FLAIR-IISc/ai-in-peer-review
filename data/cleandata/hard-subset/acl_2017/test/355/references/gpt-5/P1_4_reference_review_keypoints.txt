- Paper addresses Japanese PAS, proposing a syntax-free single-sequence BiRNN and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN; both beat prior baselines on NAIST 1.5, especially for zero arguments.
- Contribution: a strong syntax-free BiRNN baseline achieves new SOTA and notably improves zero-argument identification.
- Contribution: a Grid-RNN that jointly models predicates gives small but statistically significant extra gains, particularly for zero arguments and DAT.
- Contribution: thorough empirical study with case-wise breakdowns, depth/residual ablations, and significance testing.
- Strength: single-sequence model outperforms a strong syntax-rich joint baseline, showing contextual representations can replace explicit syntax (+2–3 F1 on zero args).
- Strength: targeted improvements on hard phenomena (zero arguments, Zero-NOM, DAT) indicating better handling of long-distance dependencies.
- Strength: multi-predicate joint modeling provides consistent, statistically significant though modest gains (≈+0.27 F1), especially for zero arguments.
- Strength: clear methodological description (models, training, hyperparams), ablations, and bootstrap significance testing.
- Strength: simple, portable approach that avoids external resources, feature engineering, and parsers.
- Weakness: Grid-RNN’s added complexity yields limited gains and lacks comparisons to simpler multi-predicate alternatives or ablations isolating inter-sequence recurrence.
- Weakness: unclear inference/decoding constraints—no description of enforcing at-most-one-argument-per-case per predicate.
- Weakness: limited analysis of why multi-predicate modeling helps—no qualitative examples, probing, or visualizations.
- Weakness: scope/generalization limited to intra-sentential arguments on one dataset/language; missing cross-dataset/multilingual evidence and comparison to other neural+syntax work.
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns—placeholder code link and missing details on dropout, OOV handling, and token-to-argument alignment.
- Question: How are PAS constraints enforced at inference, how often do violations occur, and how are they handled in evaluation?
- Question: Are labels predicted at word or bunsetsu/head level, and how is NAIST’s multiword argument annotation mapped to tokens?
- Question: How does Grid-RNN compare to (i) a shared BiRNN with multiple predicate markers and independent heads and (ii) a multi-task setup without inter-sequence recurrence?
- Question: What are time/memory complexities as functions of number of predicates M and sentence length T; typical M and training/inference times?
- Question: Do gains from Grid-RNN persist when adding syntactic cues (gold or predicted)?
- Additional: provide qualitative examples illustrating zero-argument disambiguation via multi-predicate context.
- Additional: clarify whether F1 is micro-averaged over predicate-case instances and how null labels are handled.
- Additional: clarify ambiguous claims (e.g., “much higher by around 30 points” for DAT) with clearer context (Dep vs Zero).
