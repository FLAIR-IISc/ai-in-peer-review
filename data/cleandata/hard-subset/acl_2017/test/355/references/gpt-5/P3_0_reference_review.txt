Summary
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). The authors propose: (i) a syntax-agnostic single-sequence Bi-GRU model that labels each token with NOM/ACC/DAT/PRED/null for a given predicate; and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence by adding a predicate dimension and inter-sequence recurrence, thereby modeling multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections enable deeper stacks. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, the single model achieves 81.15 F1 and the grid model 81.42 F1, surpassing a strong joint syntactic baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015; 79.23 F1), with the largest gains on Zero arguments, especially Zero-NOM.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A strong, simple, syntax-agnostic neural baseline for Japanese PAS that surpasses a state-of-the-art joint syntactic model on NAIST 1.5.
2) A grid-style recurrent architecture that models multi-predicate interactions end-to-end and yields small but statistically significant gains over the single-predicate model.
3) An empirical study showing depth/residual connections matter for PAS and a detailed per-case breakdown that clarifies where gains (mostly Zero-NOM) arise.

Strengths
- Clear empirical advance without syntax: Demonstrates that carefully designed sequence models over word tokens can outperform a strong joint model that uses predicted syntax, reducing pipeline error propagation. The overall and Zero gains are non-trivial (Zero F1 +3.0 over Ouchi+15; overall +2.2), especially notable for Japanese zero anaphora.
- Modeling multi-predicate interactions end-to-end: The Grid-RNN is an elegant way to share information across predicates, avoiding hand-designed joint features/constraints. The multi-sequence model significantly improves over the single-sequence baseline (81.42 vs 81.15 overall; +1.0 for Zero), validating the core intuition.
- Sensible experimental protocol: Uses standard NAIST 1.5 splits, reports bootstrap significance, and provides per-role analyses (NOM/ACC/DAT; Dep/Zero), which increase transparency. The large improvements on DAT (vs baselines on 1.5) highlight robustness to rarer cases.
- Depth and optimization insights: The residual connections materially help deeper RNN/Grid stacks; this is a useful practical takeaway for the community working on PAS/SRL with RNNs.
- Broader applicability: The architecture is generic and should transfer to (multi-)predicate SRL across languages, complementing prior syntax-free SRL work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the core modeling: The approach adapts known multidimensional RNN/Grid ideas (MD-RNN/Grid-LSTM) to PAS. While the application and empirical validation are valuable, there is little algorithmic novelty beyond the architectural instantiation (Bi-GRU + residual + 2D recurrence).
- Incomplete baselines/ablations: Results are not compared under the same setting to contemporaneous neural PAS work (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016; Iida et al., 2016). Ablations are missing for key design choices: effect of inter-sequence connections vs simpler cross-predicate sharing (e.g., attention across predicate sequences; shared encoder with multiple predicate markers); effect of the predicate window (PRED) and mark features; position/distance features; replacing softmax with global decoding/constraints.
- Nuanced efficacy of multi-predicate modeling: The overall Zero improvement with Grid-RNN is driven by Zero-NOM; Zero-ACC and Zero-DAT degrade (Table 3), and Dep-DAT is slightly worse with the multi-sequence model. The paper should analyze when and why the grid helps or hurts (e.g., predicate count per sentence, distances, argument rarity).
- Structural constraints and decoding: The model uses token-wise softmax for each predicate without explicit global constraints (e.g., at most one filler per case role). It is unclear how contradictions (multiple NOMs) are avoided or handled at evaluation time; this omission matters for fair comparison to joint/ILP systems that enforce case-frame constraints.
- Feature economy vs missed opportunities: Using only word identity embeddings, a predicate window, and a predicate mark is commendably simple, but the paper does not explore common enhancements (POS/morphology, character/subword, pretrained embeddings), nor whether adding lightweight syntactic signals would further boost performance or close the remaining Zero gaps.
- Reproducibility/engineering details: The code URL is a placeholder. Important details are missing: runtime/memory scaling with M×T and depth; statistics on predicates per sentence; variance across runs; and significance vs the state-of-the-art baseline (only Single vs Multi significance is reported).

Questions to Authors
- Decoding/constraints: How do you enforce or handle one-argument-per-case constraints at test time? If multiple tokens are predicted as NOM for a predicate, how is the final assignment made? Did you try simple constrained decoding or ILP on top of your scores?
- Predicate multiplicity analysis: How does the multi-sequence gain vary with the number of predicates per sentence (M)? Please provide breakdowns by M and by antecedent distance for Zero cases.
- Directionality/design of Grid-RNN: Did you experiment with 2D bidirectional processing (four scanning orders) or attention-based inter-sequence connections? How sensitive are results to the specific inter-sequence recurrence design?
- Ablations: What is the impact of removing the predicate window (PRED) or the mark feature? Does adding distance-to-predicate or positional embeddings help Zero resolution?
- Comparisons: Can you provide a controlled comparison against Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016) under the same NAIST 1.5 setup? Also, please report significance against Ouchi et al. (2015), not only Single vs Multi.
- Resources and assumptions: Are predicates gold or predicted? Are morphological segmentation/tokenization and case markers standardized from NAIST? Do you use syntactic information only for categorizing predictions into Dep vs Zero at evaluation time?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with a clean idea and convincing improvements, but it would benefit from stronger comparative baselines, clearer decoding/constraints, and deeper analyses of when multi-predicate interactions help.
