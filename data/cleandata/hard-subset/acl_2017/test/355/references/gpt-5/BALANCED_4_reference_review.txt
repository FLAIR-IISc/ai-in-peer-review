Summary
The paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on intra-sentential arguments, including challenging zero arguments. It proposes two neural sequence models that avoid reliance on predicted syntactic parses: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN/GRU model that predicts case roles for each predicate independently by labeling all tokens, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections are explored to enable deeper networks. Experiments on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 show that both models outperform prior state of the art, with the Grid-RNN providing significant albeit modest gains over the single-sequence model, and larger gains on zero arguments. No external resources are used, and code is released.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A neural grid-style architecture (Grid-RNN) that jointly encodes multiple predicate-specific sequences in a sentence to model multi-predicate interactions for PAS, achieving SOTA on NAIST 1.5 without syntactic features.
2) A strong syntax-agnostic baseline for Japanese PAS using stacked BiRNNs with residual connections over token sequences, showing substantial improvements especially for zero arguments and DAT.
3) An empirical study showing that multi-predicate interactions help zero argument identification and that residual connections improve deeper PAS models.

Strengths
- Clear empirical advance without syntax: The models surpass strong syntax-based baselines (Ouchi et al., 2015) on NAIST 1.5, with +2.2 F1 overall and +3.0 F1 for zero arguments. This is a meaningful contribution because prior SOTA relied heavily on pipeline syntactic features and suffered error propagation; showing a competitive syntax-agnostic alternative is impactful.
- Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN significantly improves over the single-sequence model overall (+0.27 F1) and more on zero arguments (+1.0 F1). This supports the linguistic intuition that inter-predicate context informs argument recovery and provides a concrete, end-to-end neural mechanism to exploit it.
- Sound experimental methodology: The paper uses standard splits on NAIST 1.5, compares against strong baselines, reports significance via bootstrap, and analyzes per-case performance (NOM/ACC/DAT, Dep vs Zero). The depth/residual ablation is informative and shows benefits of deeper models with residual connections.
- Practical simplicity and reproducibility: The approach uses only word and predicate markers (no external resources, parsers, or handcrafted features), with reasonable hyperparameter transparency and released code, increasing the likelihood of adoption and further study.
- Notable gains on challenging categories: Dative arguments (especially zero DAT) and zero NOM see sizable improvements, aligning with the claim that sequence models capture longer-range dependencies better than local syntactic signals.

Weaknesses
- Limited structural constraints in decoding: The model performs per-token, per-predicate softmax classification without explicit global constraints (e.g., one argument per case per predicate). This can, in principle, yield multiple NOM/ACC/DAT assignments. The paper does not state whether any decoding constraints or postprocessing are applied. Prior work often used joint inference/ILP to enforce case uniqueness and compatibility; absence thereof could hinder precision and understates the potential of the approach.
- Modest gain of Grid-RNN over strong single-sequence baseline: While statistically significant, the overall improvement from multi-sequence modeling is small (+0.27 F1). Given increased architectural complexity and computational cost, stronger evidence (e.g., broader datasets, ablations isolating interaction pathways, qualitative analyses) would better justify the Grid-RNN’s added complexity.
- Incomplete comparisons and analysis: Although comparisons to Ouchi et al. (2015) and Imamura et al. (2009) are appropriate, the paper lacks comparisons to other non-syntactic or lightly-syntactic neural PAS/SRL models and does not explore simple pairwise predicate-argument architectures (e.g., attention or biaffine classifiers) as baselines. Additionally, there is no explicit runtime/memory analysis for Grid-RNN with varying predicate counts, which is relevant for practical deployment.
- Limited ablations and error analysis: Beyond depth/residuals, the paper does not ablate key input features (e.g., predicate window, mark embeddings) or assess robustness to OOVs. A qualitative/error analysis would help illuminate what types of zero-argument phenomena multi-predicate interactions resolve and where they fail.
- Scope restricted to intra-sentential arguments: This matches prior work, but the paper could more clearly quantify the coverage of the evaluated task (e.g., proportion of zero pronouns covered) and discuss implications for downstream applications.

Questions to authors
- Do you enforce any decoding constraints to ensure at most one argument per case per predicate? If not, how often does the model predict multiple candidates for the same case, and how does this affect precision? Have you tried simple post-hoc constraints or ILP decoding?
- How are predicates ordered along the “predicate” dimension in the Grid-RNN? Is the model sensitive to this order? Did you try alternative orderings or bidirectional flows in the predicate dimension?
- What is the computational footprint of the Grid-RNN compared to the single-sequence model (training time, memory), as a function of sentence length and number of predicates?
- Can you provide qualitative examples showing how multi-predicate interactions led to a correct zero argument that the single-sequence model missed?
- Did you evaluate the effect of adding lightweight linguistic features (POS/morphology) or pretrained/subword embeddings? Would such additions further close the gap on particularly sparse cases like DAT?
- Are the reported results averaged over multiple random seeds? If not, how sensitive are the models to initialization?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider adding global constraints (case uniqueness, case frames) at decoding time; this is likely to yield further gains with minimal architectural changes.
- An attention-based predicate-argument scoring baseline or a biaffine classifier could be a strong non-syntactic comparator.
- Character/subword embeddings may help with rare words and improve zero-argument recovery.
- A small qualitative study of dependency distance vs accuracy would substantiate the long-distance modeling claim.

Overall: This is a solid and timely contribution. Even if the Grid-RNN gain is modest, the paper’s main result—state-of-the-art PAS without syntax and improved zero argument recovery—is valuable. Strengthening decoding constraints and expanding analysis would further improve the work. I lean toward acceptance.
