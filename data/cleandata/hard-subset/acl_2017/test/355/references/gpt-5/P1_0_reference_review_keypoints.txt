- Paper addresses Japanese PAS focusing on intra-sentential arguments and zero anaphora, proposing a single-sequence BiRNN/GRU and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that models multi-predicate interactions.
- Both models outperform strong syntax-based baselines on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, with the Grid-RNN achieving the best overall F1 (81.42 vs. 79.23).
- Models yield notable gains on zero arguments (~+2–3 F1 over prior SOTA and ~+1 F1 for multi-predicate interactions vs. single-sequence).
- Deep residual connections enable training of 6–8 layer RNN/Grid-RNNs and improve F1 by roughly 1 point.
- Detailed case-wise results (notably large DAT gains) and statistical significance testing are provided.
- Strength: demonstrates strong syntax-free modeling that outperforms syntax-based joint models using only word indices and predicate marks.
- Strength: principled multi-predicate interaction via Grid-RNN yields statistically significant improvements, especially for zero arguments.
- Strength: improved zero anaphora performance addresses a core difficulty in Japanese PAS.
- Strength: shows practical benefit of depth and residuals for recurrent architectures in PAS/SRL.
- Strength: careful experimental setup—standard splits, ablations, and no external resources to isolate architecture effects.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty; models are incremental applications of existing sequence/Grid-RNN ideas.
- Weakness: modest aggregate gains of Grid-RNN over single-sequence (81.42 vs. 81.15) and some mixed subcase results; computational cost not quantified.
- Weakness: predictions are independent per predicate/token with no global structural constraints (e.g., ILP/CRF), and no comparison to such constraints.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of when and why multi-predicate interactions help (e.g., by predicate count, distance, or specific patterns) and limited ablation on interaction design choices.
- Weakness: scope limited to a single dataset/language and excludes inter-sentential zero anaphora; large DAT improvements need deeper explanation given annotation changes and rarity.
- Questions: request for computational efficiency metrics and typical predicates-per-sentence counts.
- Questions: whether simple post-hoc constraints or CRF/ILP were tried and how they interact with Grid-RNN.
- Questions: request for breakdowns showing where Grid-RNN helps most (≥2 predicates, long-distance zeros, etc.).
- Questions: what drives large DAT Dep gains—recall vs. precision and linguistic patterns.
- Questions: whether POS/morphology or pre-trained embeddings were tried and how they interact with the models.
- Questions: reproducibility—actual code release, trained models, and any non-trivial preprocessing beyond NAIST boundaries.
- Overall verdict: solid empirical paper with moderate novelty, meaningful zero-anaphora gains, and recommendation leaning toward acceptance pending stronger analysis and efficiency/constraint comparisons.
