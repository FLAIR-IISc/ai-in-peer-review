Summary
The paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments, including zero arguments (omitted arguments). It proposes two neural models that do not rely on syntactic parses: (i) a single-sequence stacked BiRNN that predicts arguments for one predicate at a time, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections enable deeper architectures. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong syntax-based baselines (notably Ouchi et al., 2015), with the Grid-RNN yielding the best overall F1 and especially improving zero anaphora. Significance tests and per-case-role analyses are provided.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A grid-type recurrent architecture that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions without syntax, yielding consistent gains over a strong single-sequence baseline.
- An empirical demonstration that syntax-free sequence models can surpass syntax-dependent SOTA on NAIST 1.5, with notable improvements on zero arguments and the dative case.
- An analysis of network depth and residual connections for PAS, showing deeper models with residuals help, plus a per-case-role breakdown.

Strengths
- Clear, targeted modeling of multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN design is a principled way to propagate information across predicate-specific sequences, aligning with linguistic intuition and prior joint modeling, yet avoiding parser error propagation. Gains over the single-sequence baseline (overall and especially on Zero) support the claim.
- Strong empirical results without syntax or external resources: Beating Ouchi et al. (2015) on NAIST 1.5 is nontrivial, particularly improving Zero F1 by ~3 points and overall F1 by ~2 points, while using simple features (word embeddings and a predicate marker).
- Careful experimental methodology: Standard splits, hyperparameter details, significance testing via bootstrap, and per-case-role breakdowns. The residual-connection study shows meaningful improvements for deeper models, indicating thoughtful engineering rather than one-off tuning.
- Practicality and generality: The approach avoids dependence on (error-prone) parsers and bespoke feature engineering. The architecture could transfer to SRL and other languages, as discussed.
- Reproducibility indicators: Implementation details are substantial, and code is promised (though the URL in the draft is a placeholder).

Weaknesses
- Limited evidence for the unique value of the grid interactions: The absolute gain from multi-sequence over single-sequence is modest (≈0.27 overall F1; ≈1 point on Zero). The paper would be stronger with ablations contrasting Grid-RNN against simpler cross-predicate sharing (e.g., shared BiRNN with predicate indicators; cross-predicate self-attention) or pointing to qualitative cases where predicate interactions decisively help.
- Decoding/formulation clarity: The model performs per-token softmax over {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null}, but the paper does not explain how role uniqueness constraints per predicate (e.g., at most one NOM) are enforced or how conflicts are resolved. Without global constraints/ILP, multiple assignments per case could occur; evaluation-time handling should be specified.
- Candidate granularity and alignment: It is unclear whether predictions are over words, bunsetsu heads, or other units. NAIST typically evaluates over dependency units; mapping from token-level labels to argument heads and how case particles are handled should be clarified to ensure comparability with prior work.
- Missing comparisons and analysis: No comparison to alternative neural architectures (e.g., LSTM vs GRU, CRF layer, biaffine scorers), nor to syntax-based models with predicted parses to quantify complementarity vs replacement. There is no error analysis or distance-based breakdown to substantiate the “long-distance dependencies” claim beyond aggregate F1.
- Scope limitations: Only intra-sentential arguments are considered (as in prior work), but the model’s applicability to inter-sentential zero anaphora is not explored. Runtime/memory characteristics of Grid-RNN as the number of predicates increases are not reported.

Questions to authors
- How do you enforce or post-process role uniqueness per predicate (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT)? If not enforced, how are multiple assignments treated during evaluation?
- What is the exact unit of labeling and evaluation (token, bunsetsu head, or other)? How do you map token-level predictions to argument candidates in NAIST, especially for particles vs content heads?
- Why include a PRED class in the output label set when the predicate is marked in the input? Does this improve learning, and what happens if it is removed?
- Can you report performance by predicate-argument distance bins to support the long-distance dependency claim?
- What are the time/memory costs of the multi-sequence Grid-RNN relative to single-sequence, and how does performance scale with the number of predicates in a sentence?
- Do your gains persist if you add simple decoding constraints (e.g., ILP) or a CRF layer? Did you try LSTM cells or attention across predicates?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Add an ablation comparing Grid-RNN to alternative multi-predicate interaction mechanisms (e.g., cross-predicate attention, shared encoders with predicate-specific heads). This would better isolate the contribution of the grid architecture.
- Provide a qualitative/error analysis illustrating successes/failures, especially zero anaphora cases where interactions help.
- If possible, report a variant with light global decoding constraints to ensure well-formed PAS outputs; many joint PAS/SRL systems benefit from such constraints.
- Clarify the evaluation protocol and unit alignment with NAIST to ensure fair comparison. If conversion steps are used, describe them.
- The promised code URL should be made concrete. If external pretrained embeddings are avoided by design, stating that explicitly will help contextualize results; otherwise, exploring contextualized embeddings could strengthen the empirical case.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a simple, effective, and parser-free model for Japanese PAS with a plausible and broadly applicable idea for modeling multi-predicate interactions. Strengthening the analysis/ablations and clarifying decoding and evaluation would make it more compelling.
