Summary
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, especially the challenging zero anaphora (intra-sentential) problem. The authors propose neural sequence models that avoid explicit syntactic features: (i) a single-sequence stacked Bi-GRU that labels each token with case roles for a given predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence by adding inter-sequence recurrences to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections are used to enable deeper models. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, the models outperform strong syntax-based baselines, with the Grid-RNN yielding the best overall and especially larger gains on Zero arguments.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A syntax-free, grid-type recurrent architecture that jointly models interactions across all predicates in a sentence for Japanese PAS, leading to improved zero argument identification over prior joint syntax-based systems.
2) A strong sequence labeling baseline for Japanese PAS using stacked Bi-GRUs and simple predicate-marked inputs, showing that much of the task can be handled from word sequences alone.
3) Empirical findings on depth and residual connections for PAS, and per-role breakdowns showing notable gains on DAT and Zero NOM.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains without syntax. Compared to Ouchi et al. (2015), the single-sequence model improves overall F by ~1.9 points (81.15 vs 79.23), and the Grid-RNN further to 81.42. For Zero arguments, gains are larger: +2.0 (single) and +3.0 (multi) F1 (44.09 to 47.12). This is compelling given the removal of parser features and the known difficulty of zero anaphora.
- Modeling multi-predicate interactions effectively. The multi-sequence Grid-RNN significantly outperforms the single-sequence model (81.42 vs 81.15 overall; Zero 47.12 vs 46.10), supporting the claim that capturing inter-predicate dependencies helps, especially for Zero arguments, consistent with prior linguistic intuitions.
- Simple, practical design. The approach uses only word indices and a predicate marker (plus small predicate-centered windows), no external resources, and achieves state-of-the-art results on NAIST 1.5. This reduces error propagation from parsing and lowers engineering effort.
- Depth and residual connections. The study of varying depth and residual links is useful; with residuals, deeper (8-layer) models yield ~+1 F1 over non-residual counterparts, indicating a viable path to stronger PAS models without hand-crafted features.
- Per-role analysis. The per-role breakdown (Table 3) shows substantial improvements, including large gains in DAT and meaningful improvements in Zero NOM, suggesting the model better handles sparse/long-distance phenomena.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing neural sequence labeling and grid RNNs. Grid-RNN/LSTM architectures and multi-predicate joint modeling are known; the main novelty is their application to Japanese PAS without syntax. From a methodological standpoint, the step from stacked Bi-RNNs to a 2D recurrence across predicates is incremental.
- Missing stronger or alternative baselines/ablations. The comparison omits: (i) a single-predicate Bi-RNN with a global decoding layer (e.g., CRF or simple ILP constraints) to enforce per-role uniqueness, (ii) attention/message-passing alternatives across predicates, and (iii) syntax-informed neural variants to quantify the benefit of removing syntax. An ablation isolating inter-sequence connections (e.g., shuffling or removing them, or replacing with learned predicate embeddings) would more directly support the claims.
- Inference constraints are unclear. The model labels each token per predicate independently via softmax; Japanese core roles typically have at most one filler per predicate. It is not stated whether uniqueness constraints or post-hoc selection are applied. Without constraints, duplicates or no-selection issues may occur. This also complicates comparisons with joint ILP baselines that enforce global constraints.
- Limited analysis and error characterization. The paper would benefit from qualitative analyses and quantitative breakdowns by distance between predicate and argument, number of predicates per sentence, and sentence length. It remains unclear where the grid connections help most beyond the aggregate Zero scores.
- Reproducibility/runtime details. While many hyperparameters are provided, there is no reporting of training/inference time, memory usage (which scales with T×M), or variance across random seeds. The significance test setup is not fully specified (number of bootstrap samples), and results appear to be single runs.

Questions to Authors
- How do you enforce per-role uniqueness per predicate at inference time? If multiple tokens are labeled NOM for a predicate, how is the final argument selected? Conversely, do you allow no argument for a role?
- What is the computational cost of the Grid-RNN (time and memory) as a function of sentence length T and number of predicates M? What are typical M in the corpus, and how does performance scale with M?
- Did you run multiple random seeds? What is the mean/std of F1 across runs? Please clarify the bootstrap setup (number of samples).
- Can you provide an ablation where inter-sequence connections are removed or replaced (e.g., concatenating predicate indicators, attention across predicate sequences) to isolate the benefit of the grid connections?
- How far are the Zero arguments (in tokens) from their predicates on average? Does the Grid-RNN help more at longer distances?
- Why include PRED as an output label given the MARK feature already signals the predicate? Does this aid learning or serve as an auxiliary task?

Additional comments
- Table 3 formatting is hard to parse; clarify which columns are Dep vs Zero per case and ensure comparability across datasets (1.4β vs 1.5).
- Please add a few qualitative examples illustrating successful zero resolution due to multi-predicate context.
- If anonymity permits, include the code URL; otherwise ensure final camera-ready contains it.
- Minor: specify window size rationale for PRED features; correct small typos and ensure all equations (e.g., g(ℓ) definition) are fully specified.

Overall, this is a well-executed application of grid-style recurrent modeling to Japanese PAS that achieves strong results without syntax and provides convincing evidence for multi-predicate interactions. Addressing the inference constraints, baselines/ablations, and analysis would strengthen the paper.
