Summary and claimed contributions
The paper tackles Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on intra-sentential arguments, especially zero arguments. It proposes two neural models that do not rely on syntactic parses: (i) a single-sequence Bi-GRU model that labels each token with case roles for a target predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates and their token sequences to model multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections are employed to stabilize deep networks. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines; the grid model further yields a statistically significant gain, especially on zero arguments.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) Empirical finding: A syntax-free neural sequence labeling approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS, with substantial gains on zero arguments and dative cases.
2) Architectural contribution: A grid-style recurrent model that jointly encodes multiple predicate sequences to capture multi-predicate interactions, yielding significant improvements over the strong single-sequence baseline.
3) Analysis: Depth/residual connections study and per-case breakdown; significance testing; code release.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results without syntax: The models outperform prior competitive systems (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015; Imamura et al., 2009) on NAIST 1.5, despite not using syntactic dependencies or external resources. This is practically valuable for robustness to parsing errors and for domains/languages lacking strong parsers.
- Clear benefit for zero arguments: The gains on Zero (≈+2–3 F1 over prior SOTA; +1 F1 multi- vs single-seq) are meaningful because zero anaphora is the core challenge in Japanese PAS. This supports the claim that sentence-level context and multi-predicate signals help resolve omissions.
- Modeling multi-predicate interactions: The grid-RNN formulation is a principled way to propagate information across predicates and tokens, aligning with linguistic intuition and extending prior joint approaches beyond feature engineering. The improvement over the single-sequence baseline is statistically significant.
- Careful experimental setup: Standard splits, bootstrap significance tests, per-case analyses, and a depth/residual study. The inclusion of residual connections to train deep recurrent stacks is technically sound and supported by results.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details and hyperparameters are given, and code is promised, facilitating replication.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: The single-sequence model is a fairly standard Bi-RNN sequence labeling setup; the multi-sequence model is a direct adaptation of Grid-RNNs. The work’s novelty lies more in applying known architectures to Japanese PAS than in proposing fundamentally new modeling principles.
- Small overall gain of the grid model: While statistically significant, the total F1 improvement of Multi-Seq over Single-Seq is modest (≈+0.27). The paper argues this evidences multi-predicate interactions, but stronger ablations/analyses are needed to convincingly isolate the source of gains (e.g., predicate-order invariance tests, removing inter-sequence connections, parameter-count controls).
- No global structural constraints at decoding: The model predicts case labels per token independently with a softmax, without ensuring per-predicate uniqueness constraints (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT). This departs from common joint PAS/SRL formulations and could yield inconsistent outputs. The paper does not explain whether post-hoc constraints or ILP/CRF layers are used; this is a potential source of errors and an unfair disadvantage to structurally constrained baselines.
- Limited qualitative/error analysis: The paper lacks qualitative examples or error breakdowns demonstrating what kinds of multi-predicate interactions are captured, or where the grid helps beyond the single-sequence model. Without such insight, it’s hard to assess generality and interpretability.
- Efficiency and scalability not discussed: Jointly processing all predicates in a grid increases computational cost O(MT) and introduces potential sensitivity to predicate ordering. The paper does not report runtime, memory, or training stability, nor assess sensitivity to ordering or sentence length.
- Scope limitations: The work focuses on intra-sentential PAS; while reasonable, the discussion of how the approach could extend to inter-sentential zero anaphora (e.g., via document-level grids or memory) is brief. Also, the model relies on gold predicate identification and pre-defined word segmentation; practical end-to-end performance is not evaluated.

Questions to Authors
- How do you enforce or evaluate case-role uniqueness per predicate (at most one NOM/ACC/DAT)? If not enforced, how often do violations occur, and how do they affect F1? Have you tried a simple constraint layer (e.g., per-case arg selection via max or ILP)?
- Predicate ordering: The grid processes predicates in a fixed order with alternating directional layers. How sensitive are results to the predicate order? Have you tried randomizing order during training or bidirectional propagation across the predicate dimension?
- Computational cost: What are training/inference times and memory usage for the single vs multi-sequence models? How does performance vary with sentence length and number of predicates?
- Ablations: Can you report ablations removing (i) the PRED window feature, (ii) the MARK feature, and (iii) residual connections (beyond depth)? Also, control for parameter count to ensure the grid gains are not due to increased capacity.
- Qualitative analysis: Please provide examples where the grid model corrects single-sequence errors, illustrating specific multi-predicate cues being leveraged.
- Decoding strategy: Do you perform any post-processing to select the final argument per case (e.g., selecting the highest-scoring token per case), or is evaluation done on token-wise labels directly?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider a light structured decoding layer to enforce per-case uniqueness (e.g., per-predicate per-case arg selection) or a simple ILP with constraints; this might further boost zero-arg precision.
- Compare with a syntax-augmented variant (gold and/or parsed) to contextualize the tradeoff between syntax-free robustness and potential gains from syntax.
- Report precision/recall for Zero and per-case roles, and an error taxonomy (e.g., long-distance vs local; coref-related; predicate competition).
- Discuss extending to inter-sentential zero anaphora (e.g., document-level grids or memory-augmented models).

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a solid, empirically validated syntax-free approach to Japanese PAS and a reasonable architectural extension for multi-predicate interactions. The novelty is moderate, but the improvements on zero arguments and the clean, reproducible setup make it a worthwhile contribution. Strengthening structural decoding and analyses would further solidify the impact.
