Summary and main contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, especially zero arguments, by proposing neural sequence models that avoid explicit syntactic features. The authors introduce:
1) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to model multi-predicate interactions over a predicate-by-token grid, leading to consistent gains over a strong single-sequence BiRNN baseline.
2) A competitive, syntax-free PAS model (both single- and multi-sequence variants) that achieves new SOTA results on NAIST 1.5, with especially notable improvements on Zero arguments and the DAT case.
3) An empirical study of depth and residual connections for PAS, showing deeper RNN/Grid-RNN with residuals improves performance.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements without syntax, especially on zero arguments. On NAIST 1.5 the multi-sequence model yields 81.42 F overall (vs. 79.23 for Ouchi et al. 2015), with largest gains on Zero (47.12 vs. 44.09). This is meaningful because zero arguments are the core challenge in pro-drop languages. The model also substantially improves the DAT case (+~30 F over baselines), which is rare and difficult.
- Clear demonstration of multi-predicate interactions. The single vs. multi ablation isolates the contribution of inter-predicate modeling. The multi-sequence Grid-RNN significantly outperforms the single-sequence BiRNN overall and on Zero, supporting the claim that joint modeling over all predicates helps. Statistical significance (bootstrap) is reported.
- Simplicity and generality. The model uses minimal features (word embeddings, a predicate window, and a predicate marker), no external resources, and no syntactic parser—yet outperforms a syntax-heavy prior SOTA. This suggests the approach is robust and potentially portable to other languages and SRL settings.
- Depth and training insights. The study shows deeper networks with residual connections perform better (notably at 8 layers). This is useful guidance for practitioners and indicates careful engineering to stabilize training of Grid-RNNs.
- Standardized evaluation and reasonable analyses. The work uses the standard NAIST 1.5 split; reports per-case, per-role, Dep vs. Zero breakdown; and significance testing, giving confidence in the robustness of results.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in architecture. The multi-sequence design is a fairly direct application of multidimensional/grid RNNs to joint predicate modeling, and the single-sequence baseline is a standard stacked BiRNN. Prior work already exploited multi-predicate interactions (e.g., Ouchi et al. 2015) and multidimensional RNNs exist (Graves & Schmidhuber; Kalchbrenner et al.). The paper’s main contribution is empirical: showing a clean, syntax-free grid formulation works well for Japanese PAS, rather than introducing a fundamentally new modeling idea.
- Missing structural constraints and decoding discussion. Japanese PAS typically enforces uniqueness constraints (e.g., one NOM per predicate). The proposed model uses per-token softmax without any global constraints or CRF/ILP decoding. It is unclear if multiple tokens can be predicted for the same case and how violations are handled. A comparison with constrained decoding (e.g., a simple post-hoc argmax-per-case constraint, CRF layer, or ILP) would clarify whether structure improves performance.
- Incomplete analysis of why multi-predicate modeling helps. While gains are shown, the paper lacks deeper analyses: qualitative examples, distance-based breakdown (e.g., long vs. short Zero cases), or attention/feature probing to show what information flows across predicates. Also missing are ablations comparing the grid to simpler interaction mechanisms (e.g., shared BiRNN with multiple predicate markers, cross-predicate self-attention, or pooling over predicate-specific states).
- Fairness and completeness of comparisons. The main baseline (Ouchi et al. 2015) leverages syntax and joint inference; the proposed model does not. While outperforming a syntax-heavy model without syntax is impressive, an apples-to-apples comparison would include: (i) a syntax-agnostic version of Ouchi et al., or (ii) augmenting the proposed model with syntactic features to test complementarity, and (iii) comparisons with recent neural PAS/SRL approaches (even if on different benchmarks, a discussion would help situate contributions).
- Reporting gaps and reproducibility details. Some experimental details are underspecified: whether all predicates with at least one intra-sentential argument are included equally across baselines; average number of predicates per sentence; model runtime/complexity vs. the single-sequence model; frequency thresholds’ impact; whether pre-trained embeddings were tried; handling of unknown words and segmentation; and the exact bootstrap procedure. The grid GRU formulation with two recurrent sources could be clarified further.

Questions to authors
- Do you enforce one argument per case per predicate during inference? If not, how often do violations occur and how are they handled in scoring? If yes, please describe the constraint mechanism.
- What is the average number of predicates per sentence M and the runtime/memory overhead of the multi-sequence model vs. single-sequence? Any batching strategies across sentences with different M?
- Can you provide a breakdown of gains by predicate-argument distance and by zero vs. dep for each case (NOM/ACC/DAT)? Do multi-predicate gains concentrate on long-distance zero-NOM as suggested?
- How does the model compare to simpler interaction architectures (e.g., a shared BiRNN with multiple predicate markers; cross-predicate self-attention; message passing between predicate positions)? An ablation here would strengthen the case for the Grid-RNN choice.
- Did you try adding syntactic inputs or pre-trained word embeddings? Are gains complementary to syntax, and how sensitive is the model to embedding/gru dimensions?

Additional comments
- Please clarify the definition of g in the grid: it seems the concatenation of h(m-1,t) with the previous layer’s state is treated as input x, and h(m,t-1) as the recurrent state; making this explicit would aid reproducibility.
- Minor: The label PRED in the softmax seems auxiliary; clarifying its role and whether it affects inference would help.
- The large DAT gains merit discussion given annotation differences between NAIST 1.4β and 1.5; a brief error analysis would be insightful.
- Please ensure the code link is accessible and release trained models and scripts to facilitate replication.
