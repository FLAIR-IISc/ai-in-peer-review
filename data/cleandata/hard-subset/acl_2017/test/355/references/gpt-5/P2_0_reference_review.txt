Summary and contributions
This paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). The authors propose two neural sequence models that do not rely on syntactic parses: (i) a single-sequence BiGRU that labels each token for a target predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections enable deeper stacks. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines, with the Grid-RNN yielding the best overall F1 and notably improving zero argument identification.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- An effective syntax-free PAS approach with strong empirical results: The models surpass a state-of-the-art syntax-based joint system (Ouchi et al., 2015) on NT C 1.5, particularly for zero arguments, demonstrating that sentence-level contextualization via RNNs can offset the lack of syntactic features.
- A practical, end-to-end architecture for multi-predicate interactions: Applying a Grid-RNN to jointly model all predicates’ candidate sequences is a clean alternative to pipeline/joint ILP models and avoids parser error propagation.
- A useful empirical study of depth and residual connections for PAS: The paper shows that deeper models with residual connections yield consistent gains, supporting the importance of depth for contextual encoding in PAS.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance without syntax or external resources. On NT C 1.5, overall F1 improves from 79.23 (Ouchi+15) to 81.15 (single-seq) and 81.42 (multi-seq). The Zero category improves by ~2.0 (single: 44.09→46.10) and ~3.0 points (multi: 44.09→47.12). These gains, achieved with only token embeddings, a predicate marker, and a small predicate window, indicate the models learn effective sentence-level features that mitigate the lack of dependency parses.
- Multi-predicate modeling yields consistent, statistically significant gains. Although modest in aggregate, the Grid-RNN improves overall F1 (81.15→81.42, p<0.05) and Zero F1 by ~1 point (46.10→47.12), aligning with prior findings that cross-predicate cues help zero anaphora resolution. This supports the paper’s central claim that multi-predicate interactions are beneficial, even without explicit syntax.
- Careful baselines and evaluation protocol. The paper uses the standard NT C 1.5 splits and compares against widely used baselines, including a reimplementation of Imamura+09 and Ouchi+15. Significance is tested via bootstrap resampling. The per-role breakdown (NOM/ACC/DAT, Dep/Zero) is informative; notably, DAT sees very large gains (e.g., Zero-DAT from 4.80 to 7.52–9.83), suggesting the models learn rarer cases better than previous feature-based systems.
- Depth and residual connections analysis. The study shows deeper stacks with residual connections yield roughly +1 F1, addressing typical optimization issues (vanishing gradients) and providing practical guidance for building stronger PAS architectures.
- Simplicity and practicality. Avoiding parser dependencies reduces pipeline error propagation and makes the approach broadly applicable and easier to deploy, especially for domains or languages where parsers are weak.

Weaknesses
- Marginal gains from the multi-sequence model relative to a strong single-sequence baseline. The headline improvement (81.15→81.42 overall F1, 46.10→47.12 Zero) is statistically significant but small, making it hard to assess the practical importance of Grid-RNN beyond depth and contextualization. Stronger ablations are needed to isolate the contribution of inter-sequence connections versus simply adding parameters or depth (e.g., parameter-matched deeper single-seq; multi-seq with inter-sequence connections disabled).
- Lack of structural constraints and decoding discussion. The model performs per-token softmax classification per predicate without explicit constraints (e.g., at most one NOM per predicate). Prior joint models often enforce such constraints via ILP/CRF. The paper does not quantify how often illegal assignments occur or whether post-hoc constraints were applied. This omission makes it hard to compare fairly to constraint-aware baselines and to understand potential error modes.
- Limited comparative scope and reproducibility concerns. While the paper cites Shibata+16 and Iida+16, it does not attempt to compare under harmonized settings (even partial). The code link is redacted/anonymized (“http:xxx”), and results are reported for a single run (no variance across random seeds), which weakens the reproducibility and robustness claims. Given small deltas for the Grid-RNN, run-to-run variance could matter.
- Insufficient analysis of when multi-predicate interactions help. The paper asserts benefits for zero arguments but provides no qualitative examples or stratified analysis (e.g., sentences with 1 vs >1 predicates; distance to antecedent; case frames). Without this, the claimed mechanism (capturing multi-predicate interactions) remains plausible but under-demonstrated.
- Some technical clarity gaps. The Grid-RNN computation order and boundary conditions are only briefly described; invariance to predicate ordering is unclear; the role of the PRED label in training and inference is not fully justified; initialization and padding strategies for the multi-axis recurrence are unspecified. These details matter to reproduce the multi-axis recurrence faithfully.

Questions to authors
- Constraints: Do you enforce any global constraints at decoding (e.g., at most one argument per role per predicate)? If not, how often do illegal assignments occur, and what is their impact on F1?
- Ablations: Can you report (a) a parameter-matched deeper single-seq model vs multi-seq; (b) multi-seq with inter-sequence connections disabled; (c) shuffling predicate order during training to test order invariance; and (d) performance stratified by number of predicates per sentence?
- Variance: What is the mean/std over multiple random seeds for both models? Are Grid-RNN gains robust across runs?
- Analysis: Can you provide qualitative examples illustrating how information from other predicates flips a zero-argument decision, and a breakdown by antecedent distance?
- Efficiency: What are training/inference costs as a function of number of predicates M and sentence length T? Any memory optimizations for longer sentences?

Overall assessment
- Novelty: Moderate. The application of Grid-RNNs to PAS and eliminating syntax is a useful, incremental advance rather than a fundamentally new modeling idea.
- Soundness: Generally sound, but the lack of constraints and limited ablations weaken causal attribution to multi-predicate interactions.
- Clarity: Good high-level exposition; some technical details would benefit from expansion.
- Significance: Solid empirical improvements on a standard benchmark without syntax; the approach is practical and likely to be of interest to PAS/SRL researchers.

With stronger ablations, constraint handling, and analysis, this would be a very compelling submission; as is, it is a solid and practical contribution.
