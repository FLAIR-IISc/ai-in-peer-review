- Paper proposes a syntax-free single-sequence BiRNN that labels tokens with case roles using only word-sequence features.
- Paper proposes a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes sequences for all predicates to capture multi-predicate interactions, with residual connections for depth.
- On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 both models beat strong baselines (Imamura+09, Ouchi+15); Grid-RNN further improves Zero argument detection (e.g., overall 81.42 vs 81.15; Zero 47.12 vs 46.10).
- Main contribution: empirical evidence that a simple syntax-free neural sequence model can outperform syntax-heavy SOTA on Japanese PAS, notably for Zero anaphora.
- Main contribution: joint multi-predicate modeling via a grid RNN yields significant gains, especially for Zero and dative cases.
- Main contribution: practical findings that deeper models with residual connections consistently help; per-case breakdowns show large gains for DAT and Zero.
- Strength: strong empirical performance on a standard benchmark with bootstrap significance testing supporting robustness.
- Strength: impactful result showing removal of predicted dependency parses can reduce pipeline error yet still outperform syntax-reliant methods.
- Strength: grid architecture is a well-motivated, effective, and clean mechanism to share information across predicates.
- Strength: sensible ablations on depth and residuals demonstrate careful engineering rather than a single lucky setting.
- Strength: minimal feature set and no external resources make the approach simple, reproducible, and potentially portable.
- Weakness: limited novelty — multidimensional/grid RNNs and BiRNN tagging are established; contribution is mainly empirical.
- Weakness: decoding uses per-token softmax without explicit structural constraints; unclear frequency/impact of multiple arguments predicted for same case.
- Weakness: possible capacity confound — Grid-RNN has larger effective capacity and no parameter-matched baseline or ablation to isolate interaction effects.
- Weakness: incomplete baselines — lacks direct comparison to contemporary neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM+global constraints/CRF) or neural results on NAIST 1.5.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis — no diagnostics by argument distance, sentence length, or number of predicates; missing error analysis of Zero cases fixed by Grid-RNN.
- Weakness: realism concerns — experiments assume gold word segmentation and gold predicate ID; sensitivity to predicted segmentation/predicate detection is not evaluated.
- Requested: clarify whether any structural decoding constraints are enforced and report frequency/effect of multiple same-case predictions.
- Requested: report parameter counts and include capacity-controlled baselines (wider/deeper single-sequence) or ablations removing inter-sequence links.
- Requested: report performance by argument distance and number of predicates per sentence to support claims about long-distance/predicate interactions.
- Requested: evaluate robustness to predicted word segmentation and predicate detection (even small-scale).
- Requested: test adding minimal syntactic features (e.g., predicted head/index or dependency label embeddings) to see if benefits are complementary.
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed empirical paper with clear gains but limited novelty and missing analyses/controls to fully attribute improvements to multi-predicate interactions.
