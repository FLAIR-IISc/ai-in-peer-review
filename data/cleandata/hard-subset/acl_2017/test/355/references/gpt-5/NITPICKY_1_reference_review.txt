Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that avoid explicit syntactic features. Two variants are presented: (i) a single-sequence BiGRU tagger that labels each token with case roles for a given predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes sequences for all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST 1.5 (intra-sentential arguments), the models outperform prior systems, with the Grid-RNN yielding the best F1, especially for zero arguments.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A grid-structured RNN architecture to capture multi-predicate interactions for PAS without syntactic inputs, yielding consistent improvements over a strong single-predicate neural baseline.
2) A competitive syntax-free PAS system on NAIST 1.5, notably improving zero argument F1.
3) An exploration of depth and residual connections for both architectures (albeit with limited detail).

Strengths
1) Clear demonstration that a syntax-free neural approach can be competitive for Japanese PAS. The overall F1 improvements over Imamura+09 and Ouchi+15 (test) are meaningful given the difficulty of zero anaphora. The zero argument gains (≈+3 F1 over Ouchi+15; +1 over the single-seq baseline) are particularly valuable.

2) Multi-predicate modeling in a neural framework. Extending PAS models to jointly consider all predicates is well-motivated by prior work; the Grid-RNN implementation is a clean, general mechanism that avoids pipeline parser errors and provides measurable benefits over the single-seq baseline.

3) Sensible design and comprehensive evaluation across roles/types. Reporting performance by Dep vs Zero and by case (NOM/ACC/DAT) is informative. The striking DAT gains (≈+30 F1 vs baselines using NAIST 1.5) suggest the approach captures distributional regularities even for sparse cases.

4) Practical training setup without external resources. The system relies only on the annotated corpus, simple embeddings, and GRUs, which improves accessibility and reduces reliance on parser quality.

5) Ablation on network depth/residuals (conceptually). The claim that residual connections aid deeper RNNs aligns with broader evidence and is useful for practitioners, even though the exposition is brief.

Weaknesses
1) Decoding/objective mismatch and constraint handling are unspecified. PAS typically requires at most one argument per case per predicate, but the model applies token-wise softmax independently per token. It is unclear how predictions are converted into a valid per-predicate, per-case assignment: Do you allow multiple NOM tokens? If not, how is uniqueness enforced (e.g., global normalization, ILP/CRF/constraints, or max-over-tokens post-hoc)? This is fundamental to correctness and reproducibility.

2) Incomplete and potentially unfair baselines. The comparisons omit Shibata et al. (2016) (a neural PAS model cited by the authors) and Iida et al. (2016) (CNN for zero anaphora). The claim that prior neural work uses “unpublished datasets” is not convincingly reconciled with Shibata+16, which appears to target NAIST 1.5. Without direct comparison or thorough explanation of incompatible settings, the SOTA claim is weakened.

3) Reproducibility gaps and inconsistent training details. The code link is a placeholder (“http:xxx”). Optimizer description is inconsistent (“SGD using mini-batch” vs “learning rate automatically adjusted using Adam”). Batch sizes are very small (2–8) without justification, and there is no mention of gradient clipping, dropout, or layer norm, despite vanishing/exploding concerns. Boundary conditions for the Grid-RNN (h(ℓ)m−1,t / h(ℓ)m,t−1 at sentence/predicate edges) and the exact dimensionalities (e.g., 64×32 inputs vs 32×32 others) are underspecified. Residual connection specifics (shape matching across layers, especially where inputs are concatenated) are unclear.

4) Order sensitivity and architectural choices in the Grid-RNN are under-explained. The inter-sequence pass is directional (odd layers: upper-left to lower-right; even: reverse). Results may depend on predicate ordering in the m dimension (not specified), and there is no analysis of order invariance or alternatives (e.g., four-directional MD-RNN). The choice of GRU vs LSTM, number of grid passes, and the effect of C (predicate-window) lack ablations.

5) Evaluation design and statistical testing are limited. Statistical significance is only reported for Single vs Multi (p<0.05), not against Ouchi+15. The overall F1 gain over the single baseline is small (81.42 vs 81.15), and Table 3 shows multi-seq underperforming single-seq for some Dep sub-cases (ACC, DAT), which is not discussed. Including NAIST 1.4β results with external resources in the same table may confuse comparability. The experimental protocol details are sparse (e.g., whether vocabulary is built only on train; how rare words and OOVs are handled at test; whether gold predicates are assumed; average number of predicates per sentence; runtime/memory).

Additional technical/editorial issues
- Figures referenced (2–6) are not shown; the narrative depends on them. Many reference entries have formatting errors (“?,” “\Q2005\E,” duplicated author tags), which is below ACL standards.
- The restriction to predicates “that have at least one argument in the same sentence” may bias evaluation; it is unclear if this assumption holds at test time or uses gold knowledge.
- The claim about avoiding error propagation from parsing is plausible but unsubstantiated without a controlled comparison adding predicted syntax to your neural model.

Questions to authors
1) How do you enforce per-predicate, per-case uniqueness in decoding? If you do not, how do you map token-level labels to PAS arguments for evaluation? Please provide the exact decoding algorithm and any constraints applied.

2) What is the predicate ordering along the grid’s m axis? Did you assess sensitivity to this order? Did you try multi-directional passes (e.g., MDLSTM-style) or permutation-invariant mechanisms?

3) Do you assume gold predicates at test time? How are predicates identified? Also, why limit to predicates with at least one intra-sentential argument—does this use gold knowledge at test time?

4) Please clarify the optimizer (SGD vs Adam), gradient clipping, dropout, and boundary handling in grid layers. Also reconcile the dimensionality settings with residual connections when inputs are concatenated.

5) Can you report significance vs Ouchi+15, ablations on ARG/PRED/MARK features and window C, and runtime/memory vs sentence length and number of predicates? Also, average predicates per sentence in NAIST 1.5?

6) Why is Shibata et al. (2016) not included as a baseline on NAIST 1.5? If settings differ, specify the differences and, if possible, provide a controlled comparison or reimplementation.
