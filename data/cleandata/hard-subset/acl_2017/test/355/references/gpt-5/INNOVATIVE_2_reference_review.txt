Summary and Contributions:
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes two neural sequence models that avoid explicit syntactic features: (1) a single-sequence BiRNN model that predicts arguments per predicate from word sequences; and (2) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, the approach achieves new state-of-the-art results without syntactic inputs, with notable gains for zero arguments.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A novel grid-style multi-sequence RNN architecture that jointly encodes all predicate-specific sequences to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntax, yielding significant improvements on Zero arguments.
2) Demonstration that syntax-free, sentence-level sequence modeling can surpass strong joint baselines for Japanese PAS (NAIST 1.5), including a substantial boost for the difficult DAT and Zero cases, together with a careful significance test.
3) Empirical insights on depth via residual connections enabling deeper RNN/Grid models and yielding consistent gains.

Strengths:
- Novel modeling of multi-predicate interactions without syntax: The Grid-RNN that connects predicate-specific sequences is a bold and elegant idea for this task, generalizing beyond prior joint/ILP formulations (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015). It is a clear conceptual contribution that can inspire similar designs for SRL and related tasks.
- Strong results on Zero arguments, a core challenge: On NAIST 1.5, the multi-sequence model improves Zero by ~3 F1 over Ouchi+15 (47.12 vs 44.09), and by ~1 F1 over the single-sequence variant (47.12 vs 46.10). This aligns with the paper’s motivation and supports the claim that sentence-level context and inter-predicate signals matter most for Zero.
- Competitive overall performance without syntactic features or external resources: Despite using only word/marker embeddings and a local predicate window, the models outperform well-engineered baselines, demonstrating the representational power of sequence models for PAS in pro-drop languages.
- Depth and training insights: The residual connections allow 8-layer RNN/Grid models to train effectively, with ~1 F1 improvements over non-residual deep variants. This is practically useful guidance for building deeper sequence models in low-resource settings (no pretraining).
- Solid experimental protocol: Standard data splits, bootstrap significance testing, and break-downs by case role are provided. The decision to focus on intra-sentential arguments is consistent with prior work and keeps the scope clear.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty in the single-sequence model and modest overall gains: The single-sequence model is a straightforward adaptation of BiRNN sentence encoders (akin to Zhou & Xu, 2015) to dependency-based PAS. The overall improvement of the multi-sequence model over single-sequence is small (81.42 vs 81.15), though Zero gains are more convincing. This may temper the perceived impact beyond the architectural idea.
- Potential ordering bias in Grid-RNN: The grid computes h in a fixed traversal order (upper-left to lower-right), which implies a dependence on the arbitrary ordering of predicates across the sequence dimension. The paper does not specify how predicates are ordered or test permutation invariance; this could affect robustness and makes the modeling assumption less clean than fully order-invariant joint encoders.
- Missing comparisons to stronger neural baselines: While the paper purposefully avoids syntactic information, it does not compare to syntax-free contextualized embeddings (e.g., pretrained language models) or to syntax-aware neural models (e.g., incorporating gold/predicted dependencies as soft features). Even light-touch baselines (e.g., BiRNN with subword or character features) could help calibrate the benefits of the grid interaction mechanism.
- Ablation gaps on the interaction mechanism: Beyond depth/residuals, there’s no ablation isolating the inter-sequence connections (e.g., shuffling predicates, removing cross-sequence inputs, or replacing with simple attention between predicate encodings). This makes it hard to attribute gains specifically to the grid structure versus shared encoders or increased capacity.
- Clarity/consistency issues in per-role results and analysis: Table 3 shows unusually large jumps for DAT (e.g., Dep/DAT improving by ~40 points over Ouchi+15), which merits deeper explanation (counts, new annotation criteria, error types). The paper mentions the 1.4β vs 1.5 differences, but the degree of improvement vs Ouchi+15 (also on 1.5) is surprising. No run-time or memory analysis is provided for the grid model, which could matter when M (predicates per sentence) is large.

Questions to Authors:
- How are predicates ordered along the grid’s sequence dimension? Have you tested permutation invariance (e.g., randomizing the order) or bidirectional traversal across predicate dimension to mitigate ordering bias?
- Can you provide an ablation where inter-sequence connections are removed or replaced by a simpler cross-predicate attention, to isolate the source of improvements?
- What is the distribution of number of predicates per sentence in NAIST 1.5, and how do gains vary by M (e.g., sentences with 1, 2–3, 4+ predicates)?
- Can you clarify the large DAT improvements in Table 3 (especially Dep/DAT)? Please include support counts, confusion matrices, and whether this is driven by annotation differences, data sparsity, or particular constructions.
- What are the computational costs (time/memory) relative to the single-sequence model as a function of sentence length T and number of predicates M?
- Do you use any POS/morphological segmentation beyond word boundaries? If not, could character/subword features further help Zero or DAT?
