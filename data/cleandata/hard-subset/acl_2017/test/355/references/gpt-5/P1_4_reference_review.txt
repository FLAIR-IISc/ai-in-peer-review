Summary
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments, especially zero arguments. The authors propose two neural sequence models that avoid explicit syntactic features: (1) a single-sequence BiRNN model that predicts case roles for each predicate independently using only word sequence information with predicate indicators; and (2) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model that jointly predicts arguments for all predicates in a sentence, aiming to capture multi-predicate interactions. Evaluated on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, both models outperform strong baselines, including the previous SOTA (Ouchi et al., 2015). The gains are largest for zero arguments, and residual connections enable deeper networks.

Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
1) Strong syntax-free baseline for Japanese PAS: A simple BiRNN sequence labeling model with minimal features (word embeddings and predicate markers) achieves new SOTA on NAIST 1.5, notably improving zero-argument identification without relying on parsers or external resources.
2) Joint modeling of multi-predicate interactions via a Grid-RNN: A grid-style recurrent architecture that connects sequences across predicates yields small but statistically significant additional gains, particularly for zero arguments and certain case roles (e.g., DAT).
3) Thorough empirical study: Case-wise breakdowns, depth/residual ablations, and significance testing provide a careful empirical characterization of where the gains come from.

Strengths
- Empirical significance without syntax: The single-sequence model surpasses a strong, syntax-rich joint baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015), demonstrating that contextual sentence representations can compensate for the absence of explicit syntactic dependencies. The overall F1 and, especially, zero-argument F1 gains (≈+2 to +3 points) are compelling.
- Targeted improvement on hard phenomena: Zero arguments are notoriously hard in Japanese PAS. The reported improvements, especially for Zero-NOM and improvements in DAT (for Dep), are meaningful for the community and suggest better handling of long-distance dependencies.
- Multi-predicate joint modeling adds value: Although the overall absolute gain from Grid-RNN is modest (+0.27 F1), it is statistically significant and consistent across metrics, with clearer benefits on zero arguments. This supports the claim that interactions among predicates are helpful.
- Methodological clarity and analysis: The paper clearly details model components, training, and hyperparameters, considers network depth and residual connections, and applies bootstrap resampling for significance. Case-wise results (NOM/ACC/DAT for Dep/Zero) help pinpoint where improvements occur.
- Simplicity and portability: The approach avoids external resources, feature engineering, and syntactic parsers, increasing robustness and portability to other domains/languages and making it a useful reference baseline.

Weaknesses
- Limited incremental gain of Grid-RNN and missing baselines: The added complexity of a grid architecture yields relatively small improvements over the already-strong single-sequence model. The paper lacks comparisons to simpler multi-predicate alternatives (e.g., a single shared BiRNN over the sentence with multi-predicate markers, attention across predicates, or multi-task sharing without inter-sequence recurrence). An ablation isolating the inter-sequence connections versus parameter sharing would strengthen the multi-predicate claim.
- Inference constraints unclear: The model predicts a case label per token per predicate with a softmax, but PAS typically enforces at most one argument per case per predicate. It is unclear whether any decoding constraints or post-processing (e.g., argmax with uniqueness, tie-breaking) are applied. Without this, multiple tokens could be labeled NOM for the same predicate, potentially harming precision or interpretability.
- Limited analysis of “why” multi-predicate helps: Beyond aggregate F1, there is little qualitative or diagnostic evidence that the Grid-RNN captures meaningful predicate interactions (e.g., examples where another predicate disambiguates a zero argument, or probing/visualizations). The claim would be more convincing with concrete analyses.
- Scope and generalization: Experiments are limited to intra-sentential arguments on a single dataset and language. While the paper positions the approach as applicable to SRL and other pro-drop languages, no cross-dataset/domain or multilingual evidence is provided. A comparison to Shibata et al. (2016) (neural with syntax) under consistent settings is also absent.
- Reproducibility details: The code link is a placeholder. While many hyperparameters are specified, details such as dropout/regularization beyond L2 and exact handling of OOV and tokenization-to-argument alignment could be clearer.

Questions to Authors
- Decoding: How do you enforce PAS constraints at inference (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate)? If you do not enforce them, how often do violations occur, and how are they handled during evaluation?
- Candidate granularity: Are labels predicted at the word level or bunsetsu/head level? How is NAIST’s annotation mapped to token labels, especially for multiword arguments?
- Multi-predicate alternatives: How does Grid-RNN compare to (i) a shared BiRNN over the sentence with multiple predicate markers (all predicates active) and independent softmax heads, and (ii) a multi-task setup without inter-sequence recurrence? An ablation comparing these would help isolate the benefit of inter-sequence connections.
- Computational cost: What is the time/memory complexity as a function of the number of predicates M and sentence length T? What are typical M values and training/inference times?
- Combining syntax: Have you tried adding syntactic cues (gold or predicted) to your models? Do the gains from Grid-RNN persist when syntax is available?

Additional comments
- Please provide qualitative examples illustrating successful zero-argument disambiguation via multi-predicate context.
- Clarify whether F1 is micro-averaged over predicate-case instances and how null labels are handled in evaluation.
- Some claims (e.g., “much higher by around 30 points” for DAT) would benefit from clearer context (Dep vs Zero).
