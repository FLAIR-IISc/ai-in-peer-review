Summary
The paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes two neural sequence models trained end-to-end without syntactic features: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN tagger that predicts case labels for each word with respect to a given predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to model multi-predicate interactions. On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines, including a state-of-the-art joint model with syntactic features (Ouchi et al., 2015). The Grid-RNN yields further gains, particularly for zero arguments.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A grid-structured recurrent model that captures multi-predicate interactions without explicit syntactic input, delivering state-of-the-art performance on NAIST 1.5 and improving zero argument identification (Zero), the hardest subtask.
2) An end-to-end RNN-based PAS framework that relies only on word sequence information and simple predicate markers, demonstrating that competitive performance is achievable without parsers or selectional preference resources.
3) Empirical analysis of network depth and residual connections for PAS, showing deeper models with residuals improve performance.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results without syntax: The single-sequence model (81.15 F1 overall) and the multi-sequence model (81.42 F1) both outperform Imamura et al. (2009; 78.15) and Ouchi et al. (2015; 79.23). Achieving SOTA without syntactic parses is a notable practical advance, mitigating error propagation and pipeline complexity.

- Clear benefit for Zero arguments: Compared to Ouchi et al. (2015), the single-sequence model improves Zero F1 by ~2.0 points (44.09 → 46.10) and the Grid-RNN by ~3.0 points (44.09 → 47.12). Gains are particularly notable for NOM zero arguments (Table 3), where modeling contextual and cross-predicate information is crucial.

- Multi-predicate interactions are effectively modeled: The Grid-RNN significantly outperforms the single-sequence model (81.42 vs 81.15 overall; Dep 88.17 vs 88.10; Zero 47.12 vs 46.10), supporting the core hypothesis that inter-predicate interactions aid PAS. The 2D recurrence with alternating scan directions is appropriate for propagating information across predicates and token positions.

- Sound experimental setup and significance testing: The paper uses standard NAIST 1.5 splits, compares to strong baselines, and reports significance via bootstrap resampling. Hyperparameters, training details (SGD/Adam, L2), and architectural dimensions are described with enough specificity to aid reproduction. Code availability is a plus.

- Depth and residual connection analysis: The paper shows performance improves with deeper RNN/Grid layers when residual connections are employed, indicating attention to training stability in deep recurrent settings.

Weaknesses
- Small overall improvement from Grid-RNN: While Zero gains are meaningful, the overall improvement of the multi-sequence over the single-sequence model is modest (81.42 vs 81.15 F1). The claim that grid architecture “effectively captures” interactions is plausible, but the net effect on overall F1 is limited.

- Missing decoding/constraint details: The model predicts per-token case labels via softmax (NOM/ACC/DAT/PRED/null) independently for each token and predicate. The paper does not describe how global structural constraints are enforced at inference (e.g., uniqueness constraints per case for a predicate, handling multiple tokens labeled NOM, or tie-breaking among candidates). This omission raises questions about output consistency and evaluation protocol.

- Limited ablations controlling for parameter count: The multi-sequence model introduces additional parameters (e.g., larger input dimensions and inter-sequence connections). There is no ablation equating parameter counts to isolate the effect of inter-sequence connectivity from capacity. A stronger causal attribution of gains to cross-predicate modeling would benefit from such controls.

- No analysis of syntax integration or error breakdown: While the contribution is “no syntax,” it remains unclear whether syntax would be complementary. There is no ablation or experiment combining the proposed models with light syntactic cues. Error analysis (e.g., by distance, ambiguity types, predicate types) is minimal; the paper reports per-case-role results but does not probe failure modes or where inter-predicate signals help most.

- Reproducibility gaps: Results are reported for the best dev epoch but not averaged over multiple random seeds; variance is unknown. Some preprocessing details are under-specified (e.g., morphological segmentation/tokenization method used for NAIST 1.5, predicate selection criteria beyond “at least one argument intra-sentential,” and handling of multiword predicates or nominal predicates if any). The choice to include a PRED label during training when predicates are known is not motivated.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Decoding constraints: How do you ensure structural well-formedness at inference (e.g., one argument per case per predicate)? If multiple tokens receive the same case label for a predicate, what resolution strategy is used? Is there any global decoding (e.g., ILP) or post-processing?

- Capacity vs interaction: Can you report an ablation where the single-sequence model is given a similar parameter budget as the Grid-RNN (e.g., wider layers) to isolate the benefit of inter-sequence connections?

- Variance and seeds: What is the variance across random seeds for Single-Seq and Multi-Seq (mean±std over multiple runs)? Are improvements robust?

- Preprocessing: What tokenizer/morphological analyzer was used to obtain word boundaries for NAIST 1.5? How are predicates defined (verbs only? verbal nouns? auxiliaries)? Are multiple-predicate sentences frequent, and what is the M distribution?

- Labeling design: Why include the PRED label in the output space when predicate positions are already known and marked via MARK features? Did removing PRED from the label set affect performance?

- Efficiency: What are training/inference time and memory costs for the Grid-RNN as a function of the number of predicates M? Any batching/truncation strategies for long sentences or many predicates?

Overall assessment
Clarity: Generally clear with helpful figures; some decoding and preprocessing details are missing.
Technical soundness: The architectures are standard and appropriate; training is sound; decoding constraints need clarification.
Novelty: Applying a Grid-RNN to jointly model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS without syntactic features is a meaningful and relevant contribution; the single-sequence model is not novel per se.
Significance: Moderate; the approach removes reliance on syntax while matching/exceeding SOTA, with notable gains on zero arguments, though overall gains from multi-sequence are modest.
