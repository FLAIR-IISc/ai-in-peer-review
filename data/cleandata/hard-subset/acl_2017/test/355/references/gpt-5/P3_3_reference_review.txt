Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential Dep and Zero arguments, and proposes neural sequence models that do not rely on predicted syntax. Two models are introduced: (i) a single-sequence stacked Bi-RNN that labels arguments for one predicate at a time using only word sequences and a small predicate-centric window, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines (Imamura et al., 2009 and Ouchi et al., 2015), with the Grid-RNN providing additional gains, especially for Zero arguments. Residual connections are shown to enable deeper networks.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Demonstration that syntax-free, sequence-based neural models can match and slightly surpass state-of-the-art syntax-reliant Japanese PAS systems, particularly for Zero arguments.
2) A grid-style recurrent architecture that operationalizes multi-predicate interactions in a fully neural, end-to-end manner and yields statistically significant, if modest, gains over a single-predicate model.
3) An empirical study on network depth and residual connections for PAS, showing deeper models benefit when residual links are used.

Strengths
- Compelling empirical result: syntax-free models surpass strong syntax-based baselines. The models outperform Imamura+09 and Ouchi+15 on NAIST 1.5, with clear improvements on Zero arguments (+2–3 F1) and competitive Dep performance. This supports the central claim that contextualized, sentence-level representation learning can compensate for (and sometimes beat) pipeline syntactic features in pro-drop PAS.
- Novel operationalization of multi-predicate interactions in a neural framework. While the linguistic idea is not new, the Grid-RNN instantiation that connects predicate-specific sequences and propagates information inter-sequentially is technically sound and conceptually neat. The statistically significant improvement over the single-sequence model, especially for Zero (46.10 → 47.12 F1), is aligned with prior intuition that interactions aid zero anaphora resolution.
- Clean, minimal input features and no external resources. Using only word indices, a predicate marker, and a small predicate-centered window reduces engineering and potential error propagation, yet achieves SOTA. This strengthens the methodological message and suggests portability to other languages/tasks.
- Sensible experimental protocol and significance testing. Standard NAIST 1.5 splits, bootstrap significance, and per-case-role breakdowns provide a reasonable evaluation picture. The discussion of NAIST 1.4β vs 1.5 dative annotation is helpful context.
- Depth and residuals analysis. The finding that deeper stacks benefit when augmented with residual connections is useful practical guidance for future PAS/SRL modeling.

Weaknesses
- Decoding/inference and structural constraints are unclear. The model performs token-level multi-class classification (NOM/ACC/DAT/PRED/null) independently per token/predicate, but PAS typically imposes at most one argument per case per predicate. The paper does not explain how global constraints are enforced at inference time (e.g., tie-breaking, argmax with constraints, or ILP/CRF layer). Without this, multiple words could be labeled NOM for the same predicate. Clarifying the decoding and its impact on evaluation is essential.
- Limited novelty beyond applying Grid-RNNs to PAS. The multi-predicate interaction concept is established (e.g., Ouchi+15; Yang & Zong, 2014), and multi-dimensional/grid RNNs are known. The core novelty is the specific application and minimal-input instantiation. While the empirical result is interesting, the conceptual/methodological contribution is incremental.
- Small absolute gains for multi-sequence model and limited analysis. The overall improvement over the single-sequence model is modest (81.15 → 81.42 F1), and case-wise effects are mixed (e.g., Dep-DAT decreases in Table 3). The paper would benefit from deeper analysis: when and why do interactions help? How does performance vary with the number of predicates, sentence length, or predicate ordering? An ablation removing inter-sequence connections or varying the grid traversal would strengthen the case.
- Comparison set is incomplete and fairness is not fully established. Shibata et al. (2016), a neural approach on NAIST 1.5 leveraging syntax, is discussed but not compared in results (authors note varying setups; still, a best-effort comparison or reimplementation would help). Additionally, the paper uses gold word boundaries and pre-specified “target predicates that have at least one argument in the same sentence.” It is not fully clear whether Ouchi+15 used identical predicate selection and segmentation conditions. Any divergence could bias results; this needs explicit confirmation.
- Reproducibility/engineering details. The code link is a placeholder (http:xxx). Important practical details are missing: dropout or other regularization (beyond L2), model capacity choices for Grid-RNN vs single-sequence to ensure fair parameter budgets, training time/complexity, and sensitivity to predicate ordering in the grid (the model only connects to m±1, so ordering could matter).

Questions to Authors
- How is decoding performed to enforce one argument per case per predicate? If no explicit constraints are used, how often do violations occur, and how are they resolved at evaluation time?
- What is the ordering of predicates along the grid dimension, and how sensitive are results to this ordering? Did you try full connectivity (e.g., attention) instead of only local m±1 connections?
- Are all experimental conditions (predicate set, segmentation/word unit, intra-sentential scope) strictly identical to Ouchi+15? If any differences exist, please quantify their impact.
- Why is Shibata+16 not included in the comparison table? Can you provide numbers or a discussion that normalizes for setup differences?
- What is the computational overhead of the multi-sequence Grid-RNN relative to the single-sequence model (time/memory as a function of M and T)? How often does M>3 in your test set?
- Did you explore dropout or layer normalization? Given the relatively small dataset, what steps prevent overfitting?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide an ablation that removes inter-sequence connections, varies the number of grid layers, and tests with/without residuals within the multi-sequence model.
- Consider adding a simple global constraint layer (e.g., ILP or a lightweight structured decoder) to enforce unique arguments per case; report whether this improves precision-recall trade-offs.
- Include error analysis for Zero cases (e.g., subject sharing, long-distance, case marker ambiguity) to make the contribution more interpretable.
- Clarify whether labels are assigned at word level or bunsetsu/phrase level; ensure evaluation is comparable to baselines.
- Release code and scripts to fully reproduce NAIST preprocessing and splits.
