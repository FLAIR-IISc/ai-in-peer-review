Summary and Contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on handling zero arguments and modeling interactions among multiple predicates within a sentence. The authors propose two neural sequence labeling models that do not use syntactic parses: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN model that labels case roles per predicate using only word sequence features, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model that jointly models all predicates in a sentence by allowing inter-sequence information flow. Experiments on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 indicate improvements over prior syntactic, joint models, especially for zero arguments.

Contributions (as assessed):
1) A grid-style multi-sequence RNN architecture that models multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic parses, yielding statistically significant gains over a strong single-sequence baseline and outperforming prior joint models.
2) A simple, syntax-free BiRNN baseline for Japanese PAS that achieves competitive accuracy, notably improving zero-argument identification using only word sequence features.
3) Empirical analysis of depth and residual connections, plus per-case evaluations, showing benefits of deeper models with residual connections and highlighting where zero-argument gains arise.

Strengths
- Eliminating reliance on syntactic parsers while improving accuracy: Both the single-sequence and multi-sequence models (81.15 and 81.42 F1 overall) outperform a strong joint baseline that uses syntax (79.23 F1; Ouchi et al., 2015). This directly addresses error propagation from parsing and shows the effectiveness of sequence-based neural representations for PAS.

- Modeling multi-predicate interactions effectively: The multi-sequence Grid-RNN shows significant improvements over the single-sequence model overall (81.42 vs. 81.15 F1) and on zero arguments (47.12 vs. 46.10 F1). This supports the central claim that inter-predicate information is useful and that Grid-RNNs can operationalize such interactions without explicit structure.

- Strong gains on zero arguments: Relative to Ouchi et al. (2015), zero-argument F1 improves by roughly 3 points (47.12 vs. 44.09) for the multi-sequence model, addressing a known challenge in Japanese PAS where syntactic dependencies are unavailable.

- Thorough experimental setup and analysis for depth/residuals: The study systematically varies network depth and shows residual connections substantially help (≈+1 F1 for 8 layers), supporting the technical soundness of the architecture choices. Per-role breakdowns (NOM/ACC/DAT, Dep/Zero) provide granular insight.

- No external resources: The models achieve reported gains using only the annotated corpus and word sequence features, suggesting the approach is resource-light and easily portable.

Weaknesses
- Decoding/constraints are under-specified: The output is a per-token softmax over labels (NOM/ACC/DAT/PRED/null). It is unclear how decoding enforces standard PAS constraints (e.g., at most one argument per case per predicate). If multiple tokens are assigned the same case, how is the final argument selected? The absence of a structured decoding step (e.g., constraints or ILP/CRF) may affect comparability to joint baselines that enforce such constraints.

- Inconsistency between aggregate and per-case zero results: Table 1 reports that the multi-sequence model improves zero-argument F1 over the single model (47.12 vs. 46.10). However, Table 3 shows multi-sequence lower than single for zero NOM, ACC, and DAT individually. This discrepancy requires clarification (e.g., macro vs. micro averaging, evaluation protocol differences, or a reporting error).

- Limited comparisons and significance testing vs. baselines: The paper compares to Imamura et al. (2009) and Ouchi et al. (2015), but indicates significance only between the two proposed models. Statistical significance relative to Ouchi et al. (2015) is not reported. Moreover, recent neural baselines (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016) are not included in the main comparison table, which makes the claim of state-of-the-art less conclusive.

- Reproducibility details are incomplete: The code link is a placeholder (“http:xxx”). Important details are missing or brief, such as random seed control, variance across runs, exact decoding protocol, and training time/memory. For the multi-sequence model, the computational complexity as a function of the number of predicates (M) is not analyzed.

- Ablations and analysis are limited: The contribution of specific design choices (e.g., PRED window, MARK feature, inter-sequence connections vs. simply deeper single-sequence models) is not isolated through ablations. Error analysis is limited, making it hard to understand where the Grid-RNN helps most beyond aggregate metrics.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- Decoding: How do you enforce that each case role (NOM/ACC/DAT) is assigned to at most one argument per predicate? If not enforced, how do you convert per-token predictions to final PAS structures for evaluation?

- Table discrepancy: Please reconcile the multi-sequence improvements on Zero in Table 1 with the lower per-case Zero scores in Table 3. Are different averaging schemes used?

- Significance vs. baselines: Are the improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015) statistically significant? Please report significance tests against that baseline.

- Reproducibility: Could you provide the actual code repository link, random seeds, and report variance over multiple runs? Also specify average M (number of predicates per sentence), and training/inference time and memory for single vs. multi-sequence.

- Candidate space: Are all tokens considered as potential arguments, or do you use any candidate filtering (e.g., excluding punctuation)? Do you allow multiple arguments per case in evaluation if the gold data has such instances?

- Loss formulation for multi-sequence: In Section 6.1, the loss is defined over t. For the multi-sequence model, is the loss summed over both m and t? Please clarify.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically plausible, and presents a novel grid-style architecture for modeling multi-predicate interactions without syntax, with empirical gains on a standard benchmark. Addressing the decoding specification, resolving the table inconsistency, and strengthening comparisons/significance vs. baselines would substantially improve the technical soundness and evidential support.
