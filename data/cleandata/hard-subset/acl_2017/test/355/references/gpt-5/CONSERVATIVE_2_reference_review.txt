Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes (i) a single-sequence BiRNN model that tags each token for a target predicate’s case role, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to model inter-predicate interactions, both without explicit syntactic features.
- On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines (Imamura et al., Ouchi et al.), with the multi-sequence model achieving the best overall F1 and notable gains on Zero arguments. The paper also studies depth and residual connections.
- Claimed contributions as I see them: (1) an empirical result that syntax-free sequence models can match/exceed established syntax-based PAS systems on NAIST 1.5, with solid gains on Zero arguments; (2) an application of Grid-RNNs to jointly capture multi-predicate interactions; (3) an analysis of depth/residuals for this task.

Strengths
- Competitive results without syntax. Demonstrating that a syntax-free model surpasses a strong syntax-based joint model (Ouchi et al.) on NAIST 1.5 is practically meaningful, especially for languages/domains where parsing is unreliable. The Zero F1 gains (+2–3 points) are non-trivial in a persistently difficult subtask.
- Explicit modeling of multi-predicate interactions. The multi-sequence Grid-RNN improves over the single-sequence baseline with statistical significance, and the benefit is larger on Zero arguments. This strengthens the long-standing intuition from joint PAS/SRL that predicates benefit from being modeled together.
- Clean, reasonably controlled setup. Training/dev/test splits follow prior work on NAIST 1.5; no external resources or pretraining simplifies the comparison and isolates the contribution of sequence modeling and inter-predicate connections. Implementation details, hyperparameter ranges, and use of residual connections are described, and there is an attempt at significance testing (bootstrap).
- Depth/residual study. The observation that deeper models with residual connections yield consistent gains adds engineering value and gives practical guidance to the community.
- Task relevance and clarity of motivation. Addressing Zero arguments in Japanese PAS is important and well-motivated; the paper positions itself relative to joint decoding and syntactic reliance in prior work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural SRL/PAS sequence models. The single-sequence component closely follows established BiRNN tagging approaches, and Grid-/multidimensional-RNNs are known. The main novelty is the specific application of Grid-RNN to multi-predicate PAS, which is incrementally new. The methodological contribution is modest given ACL expectations.
- Incomplete baselines and fairness of comparison. The paper does not reproduce or compare against contemporary neural PAS/SRL models on NAIST 1.5 that use syntax (e.g., Shibata et al. 2016) under matched conditions, claiming different settings. Given the strong claim “state-of-the-art without syntax,” more thorough baselines (e.g., a syntax-augmented version of the authors’ own model, or re-implementing a strong joint model with the same splits) would better substantiate the claim. The significance test is reported only between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq; statistical comparisons vs. Ouchi et al. are not shown.
- Methodological clarity and constraints. The output space allows labeling any token NOM/ACC/DAT for any predicate, but there is no mention of structural constraints (e.g., at-most-one argument per case per predicate, mutual exclusivity across predicates) or joint decoding to prevent conflicts. Prior joint models enforced such constraints explicitly. It is unclear whether the proposed models produce illegal or inconsistent assignments and how that affects evaluation.
- Loss design and class imbalance. The per-token softmax includes a PRED label and overwhelmingly many null labels; there is no discussion of class weighting, calibration, or sampling to mitigate severe imbalance. Including the trivial PRED class in the loss may distort optimization. Precision/recall trade-offs are not reported.
- Scope and generalization concerns. The focus is intra-sentential only (as in prior work), but the practical impact on full PAS (including inter-sentential) is unaddressed. The computational cost and scalability of Grid-RNNs with respect to number of predicates per sentence (M), sequence length (T), and depth (L) are not analyzed. No ablation contrasts Grid-RNN against simpler multi-predicate mechanisms (e.g., attention across predicates or shared self-attention encoders).
- Reproducibility/availability. The code URL is a placeholder (http:xxx). For a result largely based on empirical improvements, accessible code and precise evaluation scripts are crucial.

Questions to authors
- How do you enforce or handle structural constraints at inference time (e.g., one argument per case per predicate, conflicts across predicates)? If none are enforced, how often do illegal assignments occur, and how does that compare to joint ILP/CRF approaches?
- What is the effect of the PRED label in the softmax and the large null class on training? Did you try class weighting, focal loss, or excluding PRED from the loss to reduce imbalance?
- Can you report precision/recall for Dep vs. Zero and for each case? Also provide a distance-based analysis (e.g., Zero argument distance from predicate) to substantiate the “long-distance” claim.
- How does performance vary with the number of predicates per sentence? Please include runtime and memory comparisons between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq as M increases.
- Can you add ablations comparing Grid-RNN to simpler multi-predicate interaction mechanisms (e.g., cross-predicate attention, shared transformer encoder) to isolate the benefit of the grid topology?
- Are tokenization and predicate identification gold or predicted? If gold, what is the expected degradation with a realistic pipeline?

Overall recommendation
- Borderline reject. The empirical results are promising and practically relevant, especially for Zero arguments without syntax. However, the methodological novelty is limited, some key baselines/analyses are missing, and there are unanswered questions about constraints, loss design, and scalability. Strengthening the experimental comparisons and clarifying inference/constraint handling would significantly improve the paper.
