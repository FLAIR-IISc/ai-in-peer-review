Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). The authors propose:
- A syntax-free single-sequence BiRNN model that labels each token with a case role for a given predicate using only word sequence features (word embeddings, predicate context window, predicate indicator).
- A multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes sequences for all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions; residual connections enable deeper models.
On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, both models outperform strong baselines (Imamura et al. 2009; Ouchi et al. 2015), with the Grid-RNN further improving especially on Zero arguments. They also study depth and residual connections.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength:
1) Empirical result: a simple, syntax-free neural sequence model that surpasses syntax-heavy state of the art on NAIST 1.5, with sizable gains on Zero arguments. This challenges the prevailing assumption that predicted syntax is necessary and mitigates pipeline errors.
2) Joint modeling of multi-predicate interactions via a grid-type RNN over predicate sequences, yielding statistically significant improvements over the strong single-predicate baseline, especially for Zero and dative cases.
3) Practical insights on depth and residual connections for PAS: deeper models with residuals consistently help; per-case breakdowns show large gains for DAT and Zero.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance on a standard benchmark with fair splits: Both models outperform Imamura+09 and Ouchi+15; the Grid-RNN is significantly better than the single model in overall F1 and for Zero (81.42 vs 81.15 overall; 47.12 vs 46.10 Zero). Bootstrap significance testing is reported, which supports the robustness of the improvements.
- Impactful finding that syntax-free sequence models can excel at Japanese PAS: Removing reliance on predicted dependency parses addresses known error propagation and still beats syntax-reliant SOTA, which is particularly compelling for Zero anaphora where syntax is weaker.
- Joint multi-predicate modeling is well-motivated and effective: The grid architecture is a clean way to share information across predicates, aligning with prior evidence that predicates within a sentence interact; the observed gains on Zero support the intended mechanism.
- Sensible architectural choices and ablations: The paper explores varying depth and residual connections, showing consistent gains with deeper residual models, indicating careful engineering rather than a one-off setting.
- Simplicity and portability: The feature set is minimal; no external resources are used. This makes the approach easy to reproduce and potentially transferable to other languages/SRL settings.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Grid/Multidimensional RNNs are established; mapping them onto predicate-by-token grids is a natural extension. The single-sequence baseline closely follows standard BiRNN tagging setups. The contribution is primarily empirical, not conceptual.
- Output consistency and decoding: The model uses per-token softmax without explicit structural constraints (e.g., at-most-one argument per case). It’s unclear whether this yields multiple NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate or none, and how this impacts precision/recall relative to prior joint/ILP decoders. A comparison to constrained decoding or a simple post-hoc constraint would strengthen the claim.
- Attribution of gains to interactions vs capacity: The multi-sequence model has larger effective capacity (e.g., 64×32 GRU input vs 32×32), and concatenates neighbor-sequence states. There is no parameter-matched control (e.g., wider single-sequence) or ablation (e.g., removing inter-sequence links) to isolate the effect of multi-predicate interactions.
- Incomplete baselines and comparisons: Although Shibata et al. (2016) and other neural PAS work are cited, there is no direct comparison on NAIST 1.5 or a contemporary neural baseline like a BiLSTM with global constraints/CRF. Also missing is a comparison that augments your model with light syntactic cues to test complementarity rather than replacement.
- Analysis depth: Claims about long-distance dependencies and predicate interactions are plausible but not supported with diagnostics (e.g., performance by argument distance, sentence length, or number of predicates). An error analysis (e.g., typical Zero errors fixed by Grid-RNN) is absent, limiting interpretability.
- Practicality/realism: Experiments assume gold word segmentation and gold predicate identification (and focus only on intra-sentential arguments). The paper argues against pipeline error from parsers, but does not quantify sensitivity to predicted tokenization/predicate detection, which are realistic sources of noise.

Questions to authors
- Do you enforce any structural constraints at decoding time (e.g., at-most-one argument per case per predicate)? If not, how often does the model predict multiple candidates for the same case, and what is the effect on F1? Could simple constraints or ILP decoding improve results?
- Please report parameter counts and add a capacity-controlled baseline (e.g., a wider/deeper single-sequence model) or an ablation (Grid-RNN without inter-sequence connections) to confirm the gains come from multi-predicate interactions rather than added capacity.
- How does performance vary with argument distance from the predicate and with the number of predicates per sentence? Does the multi-sequence model help more in sentences with many predicates or long-distance Zero cases?
- What is the impact of using predicted word segmentation and predicate detection? Even a small-scale study would help assess robustness and the pipeline-error argument.
- Have you tried adding minimal syntactic features (e.g., predicted head index or dependency label embeddings)? Are the benefits complementary to your sequence-only approach?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed empirical paper with clear gains on an important task. The main concerns are novelty and missing analyses/controls to attribute improvements specifically to multi-predicate interactions and to address decoding consistency. With additional diagnostics and capacity-matched comparisons, this would be a strong accept; as is, it remains a promising contribution with room for strengthening.
