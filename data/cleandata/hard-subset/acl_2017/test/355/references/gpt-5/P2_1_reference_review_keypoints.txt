- Paper addresses Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) for intra-sentential Dep/Zero arguments, proposing a single-sequence BiRNN and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that avoid syntactic parses and use residuals for depth.
- Main contribution: a grid-style recurrent architecture that jointly models all predicate–candidate sequences and achieves SOTA on NAIST 1.5, notably improving Zero argument scores.
- Secondary contribution: empirical finding that sentence-level RNN contextualization and cross-predicate coupling improve Zero identification over strong syntax-based joint baselines.
- Practical contribution: a parser-free PAS pipeline with analysis of network depth and residual connections.
- Strength: strong empirical performance without syntax—Grid-RNN outperforms Ouchi et al. (2015) overall and substantially on Zero arguments, supporting reduced reliance on syntactic pipelines.
- Strength: multi-predicate joint modeling via Grid-RNN is well-motivated and shows measurable gains over the single-sequence baseline, isolating benefit of predicate coupling.
- Strength: careful experimental setup using standard NAIST 1.5 split, strong baselines, per-case metrics, and bootstrap significance testing for Single vs Multi.
- Strength: model simplicity and resource efficiency—only word/predicate-mark embeddings and small hidden sizes, no external resources, yet achieves SOTA.
- Strength: clear description of input representations (ARG/PRED/MARK), stacked BiRNNs, and Grid-RNN connections with residual links.
- Weakness: methodological novelty is incremental—Grid-RNNs and multi-predicate interactions are established and prior Japanese PAS work exploited joint modeling.
- Weakness: decoding and structural constraints are underspecified—no clear enforcement of “at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate” and conflict handling is unclear.
- Weakness: limited ablations and analysis—no isolation of vertical connections vs depth, no input feature ablations (PRED window, MARK), and little qualitative/error analysis.
- Weakness: limited scope and generalization—evaluation only on one language/corpus (NAIST 1.5) with no cross-domain or cross-lingual validation.
- Weakness: reproducibility and engineering details missing—placeholder code URL, no runtime/memory discussion, and unclear typical predicate counts (M) and costs of Grid-RNN’s O(MT) states.
- Rebuttal Q: How are structural constraints enforced at decoding and what is the empirical rate of inconsistencies if none are used?
- Rebuttal Q: What is the marginal contribution of inter-sequence (vertical) connections—please provide an ablation that removes or shuffles vertical edges.
- Rebuttal Q: Provide input feature ablations (PRED window size C, MARK, ARG embeddings) and results for C=0 and without MARK.
- Rebuttal Q: Provide qualitative examples where Grid-RNN changes predictions relative to Single-Seq to illustrate multi-predicate interactions.
- Rebuttal Q: Report efficiency/scalability: average M and T on NAIST 1.5, training/inference times vs Single-Seq and Ouchi et al., and behavior with long sentences or many predicates.
- Rebuttal Q: Do improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015) reach statistical significance overall and for Zero? Please report p-values.
- Overall: solid, well-executed paper with strong parser-free empirical gains—moderate novelty and missing analyses; reviewer leans toward acceptance pending clarified decoding, key ablations, and qualitative evidence.
