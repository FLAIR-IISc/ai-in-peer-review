Summary and contributions
This paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes (i) a stacked Bi-GRU sequence labeling model that predicts case labels for every token for a given predicate without syntactic features, and (ii) a Grid-RNN over predicates to model multi-predicate interactions by connecting the per-predicate sequences. On NAIST 1.5 with standard splits, both models outperform prior work, with the Grid-RNN yielding small but significant gains, particularly on Zero arguments.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical finding: a syntax-free, sentence-level Bi-GRU model achieves strong (claimed SOTA) performance on NAIST 1.5, with notable gains on Zero arguments.
2) Architectural extension: a Grid-RNN across predicates that modestly but significantly improves over the single-sequence model by modeling multi-predicate interactions.
3) Practical insight: deeper RNNs with residual connections help; depth ablation shows residuals are important.

Strengths
- Solid empirical results on a standard dataset and splits: The paper evaluates on NAIST 1.5 with the common partition and compares to strong published baselines (Imamura et al., Ouchi et al.), reporting bootstrap significance. The improvements on Zero arguments are meaningful for an acknowledged hard subtask.
- Competitive performance without syntax or external resources: Beating syntax-heavy baselines with sentence-level sequence models is practically interesting, suggesting contextualized sequential cues can substitute for parser features for intra-sentential PAS.
- Clear, reasonably simple models: The single-sequence Bi-GRU formulation is straightforward and well-described. The Grid-RNN extension is a natural way to pass information across predicates. Implementation details (optimizer, training, hyperparameter ranges) are provided, and the effect of residual connections and depth is quantified.
- Focus on multi-predicate interactions: The work aligns with prior intuition (e.g., Ouchi et al.) that predicate interactions help Zero arguments and provides a neural instantiation that is competitive.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and analysis of the claimed interaction modeling: The Grid-RNN is a direct application of existing multi-dimensional RNN ideas; the delta over the strong single-sequence baseline is small (e.g., +0.27 F1 overall). The paper does not include ablations to isolate that the inter-sequence connections (rather than extra depth/capacity) drive the gains. Stronger baselines are missing, e.g., shared BiRNN over the sentence with independent predicate predictions, simple cross-predicate pooling/attention, or parameter sharing without inter-sequence recurrence.
- Methodological concerns about decoding and constraints: The model does per-token softmax for each predicate and case without any explicit global constraints (e.g., uniqueness of core arguments per predicate). Prior joint models typically enforce such constraints. The paper does not discuss whether multiple tokens are predicted for a single case, whether any post-processing enforces consistency, or how this affects precision/recall. This raises comparability concerns with joint baselines optimized for legal structures.
- Incomplete evaluation and error analysis: Results are mostly aggregate F1. There is no P/R breakdown, no analysis by distance (critical for Zero arguments), sentence length, or number of predicates, and no study of where the Grid-RNN helps or fails. The ordering of predicates along the grid dimension and potential order sensitivity are not discussed. The surprisingly large improvements for some Zero sub-cases (e.g., NOM) warrant qualitative/error analysis.
- Scope and fairness of comparisons: While the paper claims SOTA on NAIST 1.5, it compares primarily to Ouchi et al. (2015) and Imamura et al. (2009). Other contemporary neural PAS/SRL approaches (e.g., Shibata et al., Iida et al.) are cited but not compared due to dataset/version issues; however, this limits the strength of the SOTA claim. The paper does not provide a simple non-grid multi-predicate baseline, making it hard to attribute gains specifically to “multi-predicate interactions.”
- Reproducibility and clarity issues: The code link is a placeholder (“http:xxx”). Some implementation choices are under-specified (e.g., how argument candidates are defined without syntax; how ties/multiple predictions per case are handled; exact predicate ordering in the grid). Runtime/memory characteristics and scaling with number of predicates per sentence are absent.

Questions to authors
- Do you enforce any structural constraints at inference (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate)? If not, how often does the model predict multiple tokens for a single case, and what is the impact on precision/recall?
- How are predicates ordered along the grid dimension? Did you try randomized orders or bidirectional grid passes? Is performance sensitive to this choice?
- Can you report precision/recall separately, and a breakdown by predicate-argument distance and sentence length? For Zero arguments, what proportion of improvements comes from long-distance cases?
- Can you provide ablations comparing: (a) shared sentence-level Bi-GRU features with independent per-predicate heads (no inter-sequence recurrence), (b) simple cross-predicate pooling/attention, and (c) Grid-RNN without residual connections? This would clarify whether “interaction” is the key factor.
- What is the average number of predicates per sentence in the test set, and how does runtime/memory scale with it? Any practical limits in training/inference?

Overall recommendation
While the empirical results are promising and the goal of modeling multi-predicate interactions without syntax is worthwhile, the methodological novelty is modest and the evaluation lacks critical analyses and ablations to substantiate the central claim. The absence of decoding constraints and unclear comparability to joint baselines further weaken the case. I lean toward rejection in the current form; a strengthened version with rigorous ablations, structural decoding considerations, richer analysis, and cleaner reproducibility would be much more compelling.
