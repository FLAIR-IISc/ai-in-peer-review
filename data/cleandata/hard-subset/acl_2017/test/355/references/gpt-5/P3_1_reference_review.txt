Summary and contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes two neural sequence models that avoid explicit syntactic features: (1) a single-sequence stacked BiRNN that labels each token for a given predicate, and (2) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections are used to enable deeper models. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, the models surpass strong prior systems, with the Grid-RNN giving the best results, especially for Zero arguments.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture that jointly models interactions among multiple predicates without syntactic inputs, improving Zero argument identification.
2) Strong empirical results on NAIST 1.5, surpassing prior state-of-the-art joint models that rely on syntax; analyses on depth and residual connections.
3) A simple, syntax-free setup (word and predicate window features) that nonetheless yields competitive performance, suggesting that sequence context and multi-predicate cues can replace some syntactic signals.

Strengths
- Empirical gains over strong baselines, including a widely used joint model. The multi-sequence model improves overall F and particularly Zero arguments (≈+3 pts over Ouchi et al., and ≈+1 pt over the single-sequence baseline), with statistical significance. This directly targets the hardest PAS subproblem (zero anaphora).
- Clear demonstration that multi-predicate interaction helps. The single vs. multi comparison isolates the effect of jointly modeling predicates; improvements on both Dep and Zero suggest the architecture is extracting complementary signal beyond single-predicate context.
- Depth and residuals analysis. Showing that deeper models with residual connections consistently help (+≈1 F) strengthens the engineering rigor and offers guidance for future work.
- Minimalistic feature set and no external resources. Results indicate that end-to-end sequence modeling can compensate for the absence of syntactic parses and selectional preferences (often brittle in pro-drop languages), supporting the paper’s motivation about reducing pipeline error propagation.
- Per-case breakdown and significance testing. Reporting NOM/ACC/DAT and Dep/Zero performance and performing bootstrap tests increases transparency of the gains.

Weaknesses
- Decoding/objective unclear with respect to PAS structural constraints. The model performs per-token softmax with labels {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null}. Japanese PAS typically enforces at most one argument per case per predicate. The paper does not describe how it enforces or decodes this constraint (e.g., selecting the top-scoring token per case, tie-breaking, handling multi-positives). Without this, the method may predict multiple NOMs for a predicate. This is a central omission, as evaluation is on predicate–argument pairs rather than token tags; unclear decoding could affect both precision and comparability with constrained baselines.
- Ambiguity in Grid-RNN formulation and implementation. The Grid layer feeds two recurrent neighbors (along token and predicate dimensions) into a GRU, but GRUs are designed for a single previous state. The paper does not specify how the two states are combined (concatenation, gating, parameter sharing), whether separate gates per dimension are used (as in Grid LSTM), or how scan-order bias is mitigated beyond alternating directions. This limits reproducibility and makes it hard to assess whether simpler cross-predicate fusion (e.g., attention, pooling) would suffice.
- Limited ablations for the interaction mechanism. While single vs. multi shows a benefit, we lack comparisons against simpler or more standard jointing mechanisms: (i) shared encoders with predicate-specific heads, (ii) cross-predicate self-attention, (iii) pairwise predicate coupling, or (iv) late fusion of single-predicate predictions. An ablation removing the inter-sequence connections (but keeping multiple sequences) would more directly quantify the Grid’s contribution.
- Comparisons to syntax-augmented or hybrid models are missing. The central claim is avoiding parser-induced errors. It would strengthen the paper to show whether adding lightweight syntactic cues (e.g., dependency heads, POS) to the neural model further improves results, and to what extent syntax still helps. Without this, it is unclear if the proposed approach is complementary or strictly superior to syntax-based pipelines.
- Analysis is shallow. There is no error analysis (e.g., by predicate distance, case ambiguity, sentence length, number of predicates), no qualitative examples, and limited discussion of where the Grid-RNN helps or hurts (e.g., DAT Zero decreases from single- to multi-seq in Table 3). Such analysis would substantiate the linguistic intuition behind multi-predicate interactions and guide future work.

Questions to authors
- Decoding constraints: How do you ensure at most one argument per case per predicate at inference? If multiple tokens receive the same case label, how are conflicts resolved? Is there a global normalization or post-hoc selection step? Please detail the exact decoding procedure used for scoring.
- Grid-GRU specifics: How is a GRU adapted to accept two recurrent inputs (time and predicate dimensions)? Do you concatenate both previous states into a single vector, or use separate gates per dimension? Are parameters shared across dimensions? Please provide equations or implementation details.
- Computational complexity: What is the time/memory overhead of the Grid-RNN versus the single-sequence model as a function of number of predicates M and sentence length T? What is the distribution of M in NAIST 1.5?
- Fairness/inputs: Are tokenization and predicate lists gold from NAIST or predicted? Do prior baselines use the same inputs and splits? Do you use any POS/morphology at all? If not, can you comment on performance sensitivity to morphological segmentation errors?
- Alternative interaction mechanisms: Did you try simpler cross-predicate fusion (e.g., concatenating per-predicate contextual representations, self-attention over predicate indices)? If yes, how did they compare? If not, could you comment on why Grid-RNN is preferable?

Overall, this is a timely and promising paper with solid empirical gains, but it would benefit from clarifying inference constraints, better specified modeling of the grid recurrence, and stronger ablations. Pending convincing answers to the decoding and implementation questions, I lean towards acceptance.
