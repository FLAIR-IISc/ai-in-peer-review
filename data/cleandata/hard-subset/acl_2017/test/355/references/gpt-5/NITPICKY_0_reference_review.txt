Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles Japanese Predicate-Argument Structure (PAS) analysis without syntactic features by (i) a single-sequence Bi-RNN model that labels tokens for each predicate independently and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Experiments on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 report improvements over a strong joint baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015), with notably larger gains for zero (intra-sentential) arguments.

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) A grid-style recurrent architecture to model multi-predicate interactions end-to-end from word sequences (no syntactic inputs), applied to Japanese PAS, with small but significant overall gains and larger gains for zero arguments.
2) An empirical finding that depth with residual connections helps sequence/grid models for PAS, and that reasonable performance can be achieved without syntactic parsers.

Strengths
- Improvement on zero arguments, where syntax-free modeling is most needed. The multi-sequence model gains ~1.0 F1 over the single model for Zero (47.12 vs 46.10) and ~3 points over Ouchi et al. (2015). This addresses a high-impact subproblem (intra-sentential zero pronouns), consistent with the motivation that long-distance and cross-predicate cues matter.
- Joint modeling across predicates without manual feature engineering. The Grid-RNN construction is a plausible and general way to pass information across predicate-specific sequences. It avoids hand-designed joint features and the error propagation of parser-based pipelines.
- Depth/residual connections matter. Although Table 2 is not shown here, the text states clear gains when stacking up to 8 layers with residuals (≈+1 F1), which is a useful practical takeaway for future PAS/SRL models.
- Clean setup and minimal supervision. No external resources or syntactic inputs; small, fixed-dimensional GRUs; simple word/mark embeddings; and a standard training protocol with Adam and L2. This lowers the barrier to reproduction and porting to other languages or SRL formulations (in principle).

Weaknesses
- Decoding and structural constraints are underspecified; possible mismatch with task definition. The output layer assigns a case label independently to each token per predicate via softmax over {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null}, but the paper never explains how the final argument set per predicate is constructed. PAS typically enforces at most one core argument per case; the current per-token labeling could produce multiple NOMs/ACC/DAT per predicate or none at all. There is no mention of global constraints, post-processing, or inference that selects a single candidate per case. This omission is critical to assess correctness and fairness relative to joint baselines that enforce constraints.
- Baseline and comparison coverage are incomplete. Only Ouchi et al. (2015) and Imamura et al. (2009) are compared on NAIST 1.5. Shibata et al. (2016) (cited) also report on NAIST 1.5; a direct comparison is expected. Further, a stronger neural baseline (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF per-predicate, or attention-based multi-predicate sharing) is missing; the paper argues CRF is unnecessary for dependency PAS but provides no empirical evidence. Given the overall gain is small (81.42 vs 81.15), stronger baselines are essential.
- Methodological/detail gaps in Grid-RNN definition and efficiency. The grid recurrence omits boundary conditions (what is h at m=0 or t=0; zero vectors, learned parameters, or carry-over?), update scheduling, and parallelization. Complexity scales with M×T×L; no runtime/memory analysis or statistics on number of predicates per sentence are provided. Without these, the practicality of the approach is unclear; real sentences with many predicates could be costly.
- Reproducibility issues and weak reporting. The code link is a placeholder (“http:xxx”). No random seeds, variance across runs, or multiple-trial averages are reported; selecting the best epoch on dev without variation can inflate reported gains, especially with small margins. Important ablations are missing: effect of MARK vs no MARK, predicate window size C, embedding size, dropout/regularization beyond L2, gradient clipping. The significance test is vaguely described (bootstrap resampling) with no details on resample size or unit of resampling.
- Formatting and reference quality are substandard. Numerous references have malformed years (“?”), broken fields (“\Q2005\E”), and missing details; figures/tables are inconsistently referenced (Table 2 is discussed but not shown; figure callouts lack sufficient captions here); and the paper contains typos (“ap- proach”, “significent”). These issues, while minor individually, are pervasive and detract from professionalism and credibility.

Questions to authors
- Decoding/constraints: How do you ensure a single argument (or none) per case per predicate? Do you apply any global constraints or post-processing to token-level predictions? If so, please describe the decoding and how it affects scores. If not, how do you reconcile this with the task’s evaluation protocol?
- Baselines: Why is Shibata et al. (2016) on NAIST 1.5 not included in the comparison table? Can you add results against that system and/or a BiLSTM-CRF baseline?
- Grid-RNN specifics: What are the boundary conditions for h(ℓ) at m=1 and t=1 (and at even layers for m=M, t=T)? Are these states zero-initialized or learned? How is the grid computed efficiently (scan order, batching)? Any gradient clipping?
- Efficiency: What are the average M (predicates per sentence), average T, training time per epoch, and inference throughput for single vs multi-sequence models? Memory footprint for L=8? How does performance scale with M?
- Variance/stability: Please report mean±std over multiple random seeds for key settings, and clarify the bootstrap test procedure (resample size, unit, confidence intervals).
- Ablations: What is the impact of removing the MARK feature, varying C, using larger embeddings, or adding dropout? Does CRF help despite arguments being sparse? Does adding syntax to your model further improve results?
- Data/filtering: You restrict to predicates with at least one intra-sentential argument. Do Ouchi et al. (2015) and other baselines use the exact same filtering and tokenization (word boundaries vs bunsetsu)? Please clarify to ensure fair comparability.
- Code: Please provide the actual repository URL and commit; list exact preprocessing/tokenization scripts to ensure replicability.
