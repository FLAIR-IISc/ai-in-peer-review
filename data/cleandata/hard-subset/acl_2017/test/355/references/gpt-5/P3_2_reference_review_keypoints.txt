- Paper tackles Japanese PAS (intra-sentential Dep and Zero) with two syntax-free neural models: single-sequence stacked BiRNN and multi-sequence Grid-RNN, using residual connections for depth.
- Claims state-of-the-art on NAIST 1.5 for intra-sentential PAS, especially for zero anaphora, with multi-sequence model improving Zero-NOM.
- Contribution: simple syntax-free sequence modeling achieves new SOTA on NAIST 1.5 for intra-sentential PAS, notably zero arguments.
- Contribution: grid-style recurrent architecture across predicate and token dimensions to model multi-predicate interactions, yielding empirical gains over a single-predicate baseline.
- Contribution: empirical study of depth and residual connections showing deeper models benefit from residuals.
- Strength: strong empirical results without syntactic features, outperforming prior syntax-heavy joint approaches and producing meaningful Zero F1 gains.
- Strength: end-to-end multi-predicate interaction modeling via Grid-RNN is a clean, general mechanism applicable beyond Japanese PAS.
- Strength: competitive single-sequence baseline and informative analysis of depth/residual effects that aids replicability.
- Strength: evaluation follows standard splits and includes bootstrap significance testing, aiding comparability.
- Strength: per-case improvements (especially DAT) indicate the network can learn less frequent patterns.
- Weakness: limited novelty and modest gains from multi-sequence over single-sequence, with mixed per-case effects and some regressions.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to stronger modern neural baselines and lacking ablations on key design choices and input features.
- Weakness: under-specified Grid-RNN cell definition (implementation of g, gate parameterization, scan order, directionality) hampers reproducibility.
- Weakness: unclear decoding constraints and training objective—no global constraints like at-most-one role per predicate are described.
- Weakness: insufficient qualitative/interpretability analysis demonstrating what cross-predicate signals are learned and why regressions occur.
- Weakness: no discussion of runtime, memory, or scaling with number of predicates; practicality of Grid-RNN is unquantified.
- Question: how is g implemented in the grid cell—concatenated GRU vs dimension-specific gates, and are parameters shared across dimensions?
- Question: Is the grid processed in both directions along token and predicate axes to avoid order bias, and how?
- Question: Are hard constraints enforced at inference to prevent multiple arguments of the same case, and how often do violations occur?
- Question: What is the effect of adding pretrained embeddings, dropout, or layer norm on results and relative gains?
- Question: Can authors provide qualitative examples where multi-sequence corrects single-sequence errors, particularly for Zero-NOM?
- Question: What is average number of predicates per sentence and the training/inference runtimes for both models?
- Additional comment: report micro vs macro F1 and per-sentence predicate counts to better contextualize gains.
- Additional comment: releasing accessible code with full training details is important for reproducibility.
