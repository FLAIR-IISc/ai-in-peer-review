Summary and Contributions
- The paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes two neural models that avoid explicit syntactic features: (i) a single-sequence Bi-GRU that predicts case labels token-wise for one predicate at a time, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture inter-predicate interactions. Residual connections are used to train deeper networks.
- Claimed contributions, as I see them:
  1) Empirical finding: a syntax-free, sequence-based neural model surpasses prior state-of-the-art results on NAIST 1.5 for intra-sentential PAS, with notable gains on zero anaphora.
  2) Architectural proposal: a grid-type RNN that connects predicate-specific sequences to model multi-predicate interactions, providing small but statistically significant gains over the single-predicate model.
  3) Depth/engineering study: evidence that deeper models with residual connections help, plus per-case-role breakdowns.

Strengths
- Competitive performance without syntax. On NAIST 1.5, both the single-sequence and multi-sequence models outperform established baselines (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015), with clear gains on Zero arguments (≈ +2–3 F1). This is practically meaningful given zero anaphora is the hard case in Japanese PAS.
- Joint modeling of predicates without parser dependency. The grid architecture is a reasonable way to operationalize multi-predicate interactions highlighted in earlier work, while avoiding pipeline errors from syntactic parsing. The setup is linguistically motivated and aligns with prior joint models.
- Standard splits and training protocol. The paper uses the conventional NAIST 1.5 splits, provides implementation and hyperparameter details, and reports a significance test (bootstrap) for Single vs Multi. The per-role breakdown (NOM/ACC/DAT; Dep/Zero) is informative and shows robust improvements, notably for DAT where neural models gain substantially.
- Depth/residuals help. The ablation over depth/residual connections is useful and supports a consistent observation from deep sequence models: residuals stabilize training and yield better performance.
- Simplicity. The feature set (word indices, predicate window, predicate mark) is minimalistic, making the contribution easy to reproduce and potentially extensible to other languages/tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established architectures. The single-sequence model is a standard Bi-RNN token classifier; the multi-sequence model is a straightforward application of Grid/MD-RNNs to PAS. Prior work already modeled multi-predicate interactions (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015), and multidimensional RNNs are not new. The architectural novelty is incremental.
- Inference and evaluation clarity. The paper uses per-token softmax over labels including NOM/ACC/DAT but does not explain how it enforces at most one argument per case per predicate, which is typically required in PAS. If no global constraints are enforced, how are multiple positive predictions handled? Without a clear decoding scheme and metric definition at the argument level, comparability to joint structured baselines is questionable.
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations. The comparison set omits competitive neural baselines on NAIST 1.5 (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016) or a syntax-informed neural variant in the authors’ own framework. There is no baseline that shares a single sentence encoder across predicates without grid connections (e.g., attention-based predicate conditioning) to isolate the effect of the inter-sequence links. Parameter counts are not matched between Single vs Multi, so small gains may partly reflect capacity. An ablation removing PRED/MARK features would clarify how much the model relies on simple predicate cues.
- Statistical rigor and robustness. Only Single vs Multi significance is reported. The absolute gain of the Multi-Seq over Single-Seq is small (0.27 F1 overall), and variance across random seeds is not reported. Confidence intervals and significance versus Ouchi et al. (2015) would strengthen claims. Early stopping by dev F is fine, but multiple runs would add robustness.
- Scope and analysis. The work is limited to intra-sentential PAS, which is consistent with some prior work but narrows the contribution. There is no qualitative analysis demonstrating concrete cases where multi-predicate interactions help, no error typology, and no discussion of runtime/complexity of Grid-RNN with respect to the number of predicates, which affects practicality. The code URL appears incomplete (“http:xxx”), raising reproducibility concerns.

Questions to Authors
- Decoding constraints: How do you ensure at most one argument per case (NOM/ACC/DAT) per predicate? If you do not enforce constraints, how are multiple assignments resolved for evaluation? Is the evaluation token-level or argument-level, and does it match the protocol of Ouchi et al. (2015)?
- Capacity control: What are the parameter counts of Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq at the reported settings? Can you provide a control where parameter counts are matched to show the grid connections (not extra capacity) drive the gains?
- Baselines: Can you include a shared-encoder baseline (encode the sentence once; condition on each predicate via attention/marker features) and/or a CRF layer, and a syntax-informed neural variant (e.g., predicted dependencies as features) to contextualize the benefits of Grid-RNN and syntax-free modeling?
- Robustness and significance: Please report mean±std over multiple seeds and significance against Ouchi et al. (2015). Are the gains consistent across sentence lengths and number of predicates?
- Computational cost: What are training and inference times, and how do they scale with the number of predicates M? What is the typical M in NAIST 1.5 and is there a practical upper bound?
- Task setup: Are predicates given as gold at test time? Do you consider only verbal predicates? Any candidate pruning (e.g., to heads) or are all tokens candidates? How are sentences with zero arguments handled?

Overall, the paper is a solid application study with modest architectural tweaks and clear empirical gains on a standard benchmark without syntax. However, the novelty is limited, and important methodological and comparative questions remain. Strengthening baselines, clarifying decoding/evaluation, and adding robustness and analysis would materially improve the submission.
