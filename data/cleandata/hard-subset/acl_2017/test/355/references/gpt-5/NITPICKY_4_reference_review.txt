Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep, Zero). It proposes two neural architectures that do not use syntactic parses: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN (GRU) that predicts case labels for each predicate independently, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions, with residual connections to ease training. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, the proposed models outperform prior work that relies on syntactic features, with the Grid-RNN showing small but statistically significant gains over the single-sequence model, especially for Zero arguments.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A grid-type RNN architecture to model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS without explicit syntactic inputs, yielding improvements for Zero arguments.
2) A syntax-free, end-to-end sequence model that achieves or exceeds prior reported results on NAIST 1.5, suggesting strong contextual encoding can partially replace explicit syntax.
3) An empirical study on depth and residual connections for PAS, indicating deeper networks with residuals help.

Strengths
- Clear task focus and motivation: The paper targets the hardest part of Japanese PAS—zero arguments—and explicitly tests whether multi-predicate interactions help, aligning with linguistic intuition and prior joint inference work.
- Competitive results without syntax: Both models outperform strong prior systems (Imamura+09; Ouchi+15) on NAIST 1.5 without syntactic or selectional preference features. This is meaningful given the common reliance on parsers for Japanese PAS and the known error propagation in pipelines.
- Modeling predicate interactions end-to-end: The Grid-RNN instantiation is a principled attempt to propagate information across predicates and time. The observed +1.0 F1 for Zero over the single-sequence model suggests the added dimension is beneficial.
- Simple, reproducible design choices (in principle): Small embedding sizes, GRUs, Adam, and standard cross-entropy ease reproduction; an analysis of depth/residuals (albeit briefly described) is useful for practitioners.
- Solid dataset protocol: Uses standard NAIST 1.5 splits and reports F1 for Dep/Zero and per case role; bootstrap testing is mentioned for the key intra-paper comparison.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient methodological clarity in core architecture: The Grid-RNN definition is ambiguous. The function g is “GRU” but is fed two prior hidden states (inter-sequence and intra-sequence). It is unclear whether one is treated as “state” and the other as “input,” how concatenations are handled dimensionally, and how boundary conditions for m=1 or t=1 are initialized. The ordering of predicates (m dimension) is unspecified and may affect results due to directed recurrences. These are central implementation details that must be precisely defined.
- Decoding and constraints are under-specified: With per-token softmax over {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null}, how are global constraints enforced (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate)? If not enforced, how are conflicting predictions evaluated? No mention of post-processing, loss re-weighting for severe class imbalance, or alternative decoding (e.g., CRF or ILP-style constraints) that are standard in PAS/SRL. This undermines reproducibility and may affect the fairness of comparisons.
- Limited baselines and ablations: While single vs multi-sequence is compared, there is no comparison to simpler interaction mechanisms (e.g., cross-predicate self-attention, pooling, or shared BiRNN with predicate indicator features), nor a biaffine token–predicate classifier baseline. Ablations of input features (ARG/PRED/MARK, window size C) are absent. The small overall F1 gain of the Grid-RNN over single-sequence (+0.27) warrants deeper analysis to justify the added complexity.
- Incomplete and inconsistent evaluation/analysis: Only F1 is reported; precision/recall are missing. Significance testing is only presented for Single vs Multi, not versus prior SOTA, yet the abstract claims exceeding SOTA. Table 3 headings are confusing and seem to mix Dep/Zero with NOM/ACC/DAT, leading to questionable statements (e.g., “around 30 points” improvements for DAT do not match the table). No qualitative/error analyses are provided to support claims about long-distance dependencies or multi-predicate interactions.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues: The code link is “http:xxx”; figures/tables are referenced but some are absent or incorrectly numbered (e.g., Table 2 is discussed but not shown; several figures are not included). Numerous typos and reference inconsistencies (e.g., “significent,” broken hyphenation, “?” in citations, incomplete bibliographic entries, mismatched RNN variants—Grid LSTM vs GRU) indicate poor polish. Critical preprocessing details for Japanese (tokenization/morphology, handling case markers) are insufficiently described. The vocabulary cutoff criterion is stated oddly (“frequency 2 or more”), and no seed or variance over runs is provided.

Questions to Authors
- Grid-RNN specifics: Precisely define g when using a GRU. What is x and what is h_prev in g(x, h_prev)? How are h(ℓ)_{m−1,t} and h(ℓ)_{m,t−1} combined? How are boundary states initialized? What is the predicate ordering along m, and did you test robustness to ordering?
- Decoding constraints: Do you enforce at-most-one argument per case per predicate? If yes, how? If not, how do you evaluate when multiple tokens are labeled NOM for the same predicate?
- Baselines: How does a strong biaffine token–predicate classifier (or a shared BiRNN with predicate indicators and cross-predicate self-attention) perform on NAIST 1.5? Please add/justify.
- Significance vs prior SOTA: Are your improvements over Ouchi+15 statistically significant on the test set? Please report precision/recall and significance.
- Preprocessing: What tokenization/morphological units are used (word boundaries in NAIST can mean morphemes)? Are case markers modeled explicitly in the ARG feature? Any use of character/subword embeddings?
- Training details: Did you tune class weighting or use focal loss to mitigate null dominance? What is the effect of window size C and the MARK feature? Report variance over seeds.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea of modeling multi-predicate interactions without syntax is interesting and results are promising, but the paper in its current form lacks critical architectural clarity, decoding details, and thorough baselines/ablations; presentation and bibliographic issues are substantial. Addressing these would significantly strengthen the submission.
