- Task & approach: Syntax-free neural models for Japanese PAS, introducing a single-sequence Bi-RNN and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN for intra-sentential Dep and Zero arguments.  
- Main empirical result: Both models outperform strong syntax-based baselines on NAIST 1.5, with Grid-RNN improving Zero argument F1.  
- Contribution 1: Shows sequence-based, syntax-free neural models can match/surpass syntax-reliant PAS systems, especially for Zero arguments.  
- Contribution 2: Proposes a Grid-RNN to model multi-predicate interactions end-to-end, yielding modest but significant gains over single-predicate modeling.  
- Contribution 3: Empirically analyzes network depth and residual connections, finding deeper models benefit with residual links.  
- Strength: Compelling empirical evidence that contextualized sentence representations can compensate for pipeline syntactic features.  
- Strength: Novel and technically sound Grid-RNN instantiation to propagate inter-predicate information.  
- Strength: Minimal input features (word indices, predicate marker, small window) and no external resources increase methodological simplicity and portability.  
- Strength: Sensible experimental protocol with standard NAIST 1.5 splits, bootstrap significance testing, and per-role breakdowns.  
- Strength: Practical insight that residual connections enable deeper PAS/SRL models.  
- Weakness: Decoding/inference unclear—no explanation of how global constraints (one argument per case per predicate) are enforced at inference.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty—multi-predicate interactions and grid RNNs are established; contribution is mainly application-specific.  
- Weakness: Small absolute gains from Grid-RNN and mixed case-wise effects; insufficient analysis of when/why interactions help.  
- Weakness: Incomplete comparisons and fairness concerns (e.g., Shibata+16 not included, potential differences in predicate selection/segmentation vs Ouchi+15).  
- Weakness: Reproducibility/engineering details missing (placeholder code link, dropout/regularization, parameter budgets, training complexity, predicate ordering sensitivity).  
- Question: How is decoding implemented to enforce one argument per case per predicate, and how often do violations occur?  
- Question: What predicate ordering is used on the grid, how sensitive are results to it, and did you try full connectivity/attention?  
- Question: Are experimental conditions (predicate set, segmentation, intra-sentential scope) identical to Ouchi+15, and if not, what is the impact?  
- Question: Why is Shibata+16 excluded from the results table; can you normalize or report comparable numbers?  
- Question: What is the computational overhead (time/memory) of Grid-RNN vs single-sequence as functions of M and T, and how often is M>3 in test data?  
- Question: Did you explore dropout or layer normalization and what measures prevent overfitting on the small dataset?  
- Suggestion: Add ablations removing inter-sequence connections, varying grid layers, and testing residuals within the multi-sequence model.  
- Suggestion: Consider adding a global constraint/structured decoder (e.g., ILP) to enforce unique arguments per case and report effects on precision/recall.  
- Suggestion: Provide detailed error analysis for Zero cases (subject sharing, long-distance, case marker ambiguity) to improve interpretability.  
- Suggestion: Clarify whether labels are assigned at word or bunsetsu/phrase level and ensure evaluation comparability with baselines.  
- Suggestion: Release code and preprocessing scripts to fully reproduce NAIST splits and experiments.
