Summary and contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) without relying on syntactic features, proposing two neural sequence models: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN/GRU model that predicts arguments per predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015), with larger gains on zero anaphora.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: A syntax-free, sequence-based neural model achieves competitive or better F1 than syntax-heavy SOTA on NAIST 1.5, particularly improving zero arguments.
2) Architectural instantiation: Adapting Grid-RNNs to jointly model multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS, yielding statistically significant gains over a strong single-predicate RNN baseline.
3) Some depth/optimization observations: Residual connections help train deeper (8-layer) RNN/Grid-RNN stacks.

Strengths
- Competitive results without syntactic features. The single-sequence model already exceeds Ouchi et al. (2015) on overall F and Zero F, and the multi-sequence model further improves by ~0.3 overall and ~1 point for Zero. Given the prominence of syntactic cues in Japanese PAS, this is a noteworthy empirical result that questions the necessity of predicted syntax in this setup.
- Clear modeling of multi-predicate interactions. The Grid-RNN formulation is a principled way to pass information across predicate-specific sequences, and the comparison against the single-sequence baseline isolates the effect of multi-predicate modeling (with significance testing between the two proposed models).
- Sensible training protocol and tuning. The paper uses standard splits, details optimizer, regularization, hyperparameter ranges, and shows that residual connections aid deeper models. This improves credibility of the reported numbers.
- Focus on a difficult subproblem. The improvements on Zero arguments (intra-sentential) are the most impactful part of PAS in Japanese; demonstrating consistent gains there is practically meaningful.

Weaknesses
- Missing decoding/constraint details. The output layer is a per-token softmax over labels {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null} for each predicate, but no mechanism is described to enforce the one-argument-per-case constraint typical in Japanese PAS or to select a single candidate per case. A per-token softmax can trivially yield multiple NOMs for a predicate. If a post-processing selection (e.g., pick top candidate per case) is applied, it must be specified; otherwise the formulation is methodologically underspecified.
- Limited and potentially unfair baseline comparisons. The paper compares only to Imamura (2009) and Ouchi (2015). It omits recent neural baselines on NAIST 1.5 such as Shibata et al. (2016) (and possibly others), and does not establish significance against Ouchi. Since Ouchi uses syntax and a joint model, a more thorough comparison (including models that also eschew syntax or hybridize with syntax) is needed to substantiate the central claim. At minimum, report significance vs Ouchi (2005 bootstrap) and include the strongest published results on NAIST 1.5 under comparable settings.
- Causal claim about parser error propagation is untested. The paper motivates removing syntax by citing error propagation, but provides no experiment validating this hypothesis (e.g., compare against the same neural model augmented with predicted dependencies; or with oracle/gold parse features to bound the potential of syntax; or a pipeline error analysis). As a result, the conclusion that “our models avoid pipeline errors” is speculative.
- Novelty is modest. The single-sequence BiRNN/GRU formulation follows well-established SRL architectures; Grid/MD-RNNs are known, and using them to link predicate-specific sequences is a relatively straightforward application. The contribution is primarily empirical within a specific dataset rather than conceptual/algorithmic.
- Incomplete reproducibility and ablation. The code link is a placeholder (http:xxx). There is no ablation on input features (ARG/PRED/MARK), window size, embedding initialization (no pretrained embeddings), or the role of residual connections in the multi-sequence setting beyond a brief table. Runtime/memory and scalability of Grid-RNNs with many predicates per sentence are not reported. Error analysis is minimal; we do not learn where the gains come from (e.g., distance to predicate, case distribution, discourse phenomena).

Questions to Authors
- Decoding and constraints: How do you enforce that each predicate has at most one argument per case? Is there a post-processing step that selects a single candidate per case (e.g., max over tokens per case label)? If so, please describe precisely. How are ties handled, and how are null cases decided?
- Significance vs baselines: Are the improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015) statistically significant (bootstrap over sentences)? Please report p-values. Why is the significance marker only shown for Single vs Multi?
- Comparison to recent neural PAS/SRL on NAIST 1.5: Can you include results for Shibata et al. (2016) and any other contemporary systems on the same split, even if they use syntax, to position your numbers clearly? If the settings differ, can you reproduce their setting or provide a matched comparison?
- Syntax ablation: Can you augment your single- and multi-sequence models with predicted dependency features (or gold parses) to quantify the effect of syntax and directly test the error-propagation hypothesis? This would substantially strengthen the paper’s central motivation.
- Efficiency/scalability: What are training and inference times for 8-layer Grid-RNNs, and how do they scale with the number of predicates per sentence? Any memory constraints for long sentences or many predicates?
- Reproducibility: Please provide the actual code link and specify random seeds, preprocessing (tokenization/segmentation), and exact handling of rare/unknown words. Are embeddings learned from scratch only, or did you try pretrained embeddings?

Additional comments
- A qualitative/error analysis (e.g., typical Zero cases improved, long-distance patterns, interaction examples across predicates) would help interpret the Grid-RNN’s gains.
- Consider reporting precision/recall trade-offs per case to understand whether improvements come from better recall on Zero.
- Given that CRF was avoided because adjacent arguments are rare, did you try simple global constraints (ILP or soft-constraints) to enforce case uniqueness? This might strengthen the single-sequence baseline too.

Overall, the paper presents a solid empirical result with a relatively straightforward application of established neural architectures. However, key methodological details (decoding constraints), limited comparisons, and untested claims about syntax/pipeline errors reduce my confidence. Strengthening these aspects in the rebuttal would materially improve the submission.
