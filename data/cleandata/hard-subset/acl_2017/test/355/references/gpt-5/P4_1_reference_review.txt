Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). The authors propose neural sequence models that eschew syntactic features: (i) a single-sequence Bi-GRU tagger that labels each token with case roles for a given predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections facilitate training deep stacks. On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, the models outperform strong baselines, including the joint syntax-based model of Ouchi et al. (2015), with notable gains on zero arguments.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical advance: A syntax-agnostic neural sequence model achieves state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS (intra-sentential) on NAIST 1.5, surpassing a syntax-heavy joint baseline (Overall F: 81.42 vs 79.23; Zero F: 47.12 vs 44.09).

2) Architectural contribution: A Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates, propagating information across predicate-specific sequences, yields significant improvements over an independent Bi-GRU tagger, especially for zero arguments (+1.0 F for Zero, 47.12 vs 46.10).

3) Analysis of modeling choices: Evidence that depth and residual connections improve performance; per-case analysis shows substantial improvements for dative arguments (e.g., Dep DAT: 64–66 F vs 38 F in Ouchi et al., 2015).

Strengths
- Strong results without syntax: The proposed models outperform both pointwise and joint baselines that rely on predicted syntax. Table 1 shows overall F gains of ~2.2 points over Ouchi et al. (79.23→81.42). Zero arguments, historically difficult, see ~3-point improvements (44.09→47.12). This supports the claim that sentence-level contextual modeling via RNNs can mitigate error propagation from parsing and is practically valuable.

- Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN improves over the single-sequence model (81.42 vs 81.15 overall; 47.12 vs 46.10 for Zero), with statistical significance reported between the two (bootstrap, p<0.05). This directly substantiates the central hypothesis that inter-predicate information benefits PAS.

- Depth and residual connections: The paper systematically varies network depth and shows residual connections enable deeper models to train effectively, yielding ~1 F improvement compared to no residuals. This strengthens the technical soundness and guides future model design.

- Per-role breakdown and difficult cases: The dative case, typically underrepresented and challenging, benefits markedly (Dep DAT: 65.9/64.4 vs 38.4 in Ouchi et al.). For Zero NOM, gains are also consistent (50.65 vs 48.11). Such detailed reporting indicates robustness across roles.

- Simplicity and generality: Input features are minimal (word identity, predicate window, predicate mark), no external resources are used, and the approach is potentially applicable to other pro-drop languages and SRL tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The key ideas—neural sequence labeling without syntax and exploiting multi-predicate interactions—are known in SRL/PAS. The Grid-RNN mechanism is adapted from prior work (Graves & Schmidhuber; Kalchbrenner et al.). The novelty lies mainly in applying a grid-style recurrence to Japanese PAS without syntax and demonstrating empirical gains, rather than in a new learning principle.

- Decoding/constraints not clearly specified: The model uses per-token softmax over {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null} for each predicate. PAS typically requires structural constraints (e.g., at most one argument per case per predicate). The paper does not describe how globally consistent argument sets are derived from token-level labels, nor whether constraints are enforced. This raises questions about possible illegal outputs and comparability to constrained baselines.

- Limited ablation on interaction mechanism: While single vs multi-sequence results are compared, there is no ablation isolating the inter-sequence connections (e.g., removing m-dimension connections, shuffling predicate order, bidirectional grid sweeps). Without these, it is hard to ascribe gains specifically to modeled interactions rather than added capacity.

- Significance and fairness of comparison: Statistical significance is reported only for single vs multi-sequence, not for comparisons against Ouchi et al. (2015). A test vs baselines would strengthen claims. Moreover, the study does not explore whether adding syntactic features to the proposed models further improves results (important to assess complementarity), nor compare to contemporary neural PAS/SRL systems with stronger embeddings.

- Scope and scalability: The work focuses on intra-sentential PAS and predicates with at least one in-sent argument. It is not clear whether baselines use identical predicate filtering, which may affect comparability. There is no analysis of computational cost or scalability with the number of predicates M per sentence; Grid-RNN may incur nontrivial overhead for long sentences with many predicates.

Questions to authors
1) Decoding/constraints: How do you derive final argument sets per predicate from token-level predictions? Do you enforce constraints such as one argument per case and uniqueness? If so, please specify the inference procedure; if not, how often do violations occur?

2) Predicate/candidate selection: Are all tokens considered argument candidates for each predicate? How are tokens with multiple possible roles handled? How do you evaluate when multiple tokens receive the same case label?

3) Predicate ordering and grid traversal: How is the predicate ordering along the grid’s m dimension defined? Is performance sensitive to this order? Did you experiment with bidirectional or permutation-invariant variants?

4) Attribution of gains: Can you provide an ablation that removes inter-sequence connections (keeping depth/parameters comparable) to quantify the pure effect of multi-predicate interaction?

5) Significance vs baselines: Did you run significance tests comparing your models to Ouchi et al. (2015)? Please report p-values.

6) Reproducibility: The code link is given as a placeholder. Will code, training scripts, and seeds be released? What is variance across runs?

7) Efficiency: What are training/inference times and memory footprints for single- vs multi-sequence models as a function of sentence length and number of predicates?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically sound, and demonstrates meaningful empirical gains with a simple, general approach; its main limitation is incremental novelty and missing details on decoding and ablations.
