Summary and Key Contributions
- A syntax-free neural architecture for Japanese PAS that jointly models all predicates in a sentence via a grid-type RNN (Grid-RNN). This aims to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on predicted syntax, mitigating pipeline error propagation.
- A strong single-sequence BiRNN baseline and an extension to multi-sequence Grid-RNN with residual connections; the latter yields consistent gains, particularly for zero arguments.
- State-of-the-art results on NAIST 1.5 without external resources, with notable improvements for Zero and DAT cases; analysis of depth and residual connections shows deeper models benefit from residuals.

Strengths
- Novel, bold modeling of predicate–predicate interactions without syntax: Moving from joint inference over hand-engineered, syntax-dependent features to a grid-style recurrent model that directly propagates information across predicates is a meaningful step that opens a new direction (end-to-end, syntax-free PAS). This is a principled architectural contribution, not just a feature tweak.
- Empirical gains where it matters (Zero): The multi-sequence model improves Zero F1 over a strong joint baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015) by ~3 points and over the single-sequence model by ~1 point; this addresses the core challenge in Japanese PAS (omission/zero pronouns). Improvements for DAT are also large, suggesting robustness even for rarer roles.
- Clean setup, no external resources or syntax: Achieving SOTA with only word sequences and lightweight predicate markers helps isolate the contribution of sequence modeling and multi-predicate interaction, and avoids confounds from pipeline errors.
- Depth and training insights: The study on network depth and residual connections is informative; residuals enable deeper stacks (8 layers) and yield ~1 point gains, which is practically relevant for the community.
- Generalizable idea: The multi-sequence layout and inter-sequence recurrence are straightforward to port to SRL and other structured prediction tasks where multiple targets in the same input interact.

Weaknesses
- Limited structural constraints in decoding: The model performs per-token softmax without global constraints (e.g., case uniqueness per predicate). This risks inconsistent outputs (multiple NOMs for one predicate). Prior work leveraged ILP/CRF/constraints; the paper neither enforces nor analyzes this, which is a substantive omission. An ablation with simple constraints or a structured layer would clarify the tradeoff.
- Novelty is partly incremental w.r.t. existing multidimensional RNNs: Grid/MD-RNNs are known; the main novelty lies in their application to PAS. While valuable, the architectural move is a relatively direct adaptation, and the paper lacks deeper analysis of what predicate-level signals are being captured (e.g., attention/attribution, case-level interaction patterns).
- Analysis and ablations are thin: Beyond depth/residuals, there’s little probing. Missing: effect of predicate window size C, removing MARK/PRED features, sensitivity to sentence length/number of predicates, distance-based performance for Zero, and qualitative error analysis showing concrete cross-predicate cues.
- Scalability and inference details: The multi-sequence grid scales with M×T×L and uses directional sweeps; the paper does not discuss computational cost, memory, or how performance scales with many predicates per sentence. The choice/order of inter-sequence recurrence (m,t sweep) may bias information flow; no comparison to alternate schedules or to simpler shared-context mechanisms (e.g., self-attention across predicates).
- Evaluation scope and fairness: Results are reported on a single in-domain corpus/split; no out-of-domain, no robustness across seeds, and unclear statistical testing granularity beyond one table. Also, while surpassing syntax-based baselines, a comparison to other syntax-free neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM+self-attention, CRF head, or reranking) would strengthen claims.

Questions to Authors
- Do you enforce any global constraints at inference (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate)? If not, how often are constraints violated, and do simple post-hoc constraints help?
- What is the computational complexity and memory footprint as a function of number of predicates M? What is the typical/maximum M in NAIST 1.5, and how does runtime compare to the single-sequence model?
- How sensitive is performance to the predicate window size C and to the MARK/PRED features? Please provide ablations.
- How robust are results across random seeds? Please report mean/stdev over multiple runs and clarify the bootstrap setup (number of samples, paired test).
- Can you provide qualitative cases where information from another predicate flips a Zero decision? Any analysis by predicate distance or number of predicates?
- Why choose the particular inter-sequence sweep order? Have you tried alternative orders or bidirectional inter-sequence propagation in a single layer?
- Could you compare against a simpler multi-predicate sharing mechanism, e.g., a shared BiRNN over the sentence plus predicate-specific attention, or a Transformer-based alternative, to isolate the benefits of the Grid connections?

Overall: Despite some missing structural constraints and limited analysis, the paper proposes a compelling, syntax-free approach to modeling multi-predicate interactions with clear gains on the core difficulty (Zero). The idea is sufficiently novel and promising to merit acceptance.
