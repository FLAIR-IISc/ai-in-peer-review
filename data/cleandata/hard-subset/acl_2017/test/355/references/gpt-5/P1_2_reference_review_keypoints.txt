- Summary: Proposes syntax-free single-sequence BiRNN and joint Grid-RNN for Japanese intra-sentential PAS, achieving SOTA on NAIST 1.5 with notable gains on zero arguments.

- Contribution 1: Grid-RNN jointly models multi-predicate interactions, improving zero argument identification and achieving new SOTA.

- Contribution 2: Deep residual BiRNNs over words (no syntactic features) capture long-distance dependencies and simplify pipelines.

- Contribution 3: Analysis shows depth and residuals matter; results decomposed by case and Dep vs Zero.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical gains without syntax, outperforming prior joint models with statistically significant improvements overall and on zero arguments.

- Strength 2: Multi-predicate modeling (Grid-RNN) yields consistent improvements, supporting the linguistic importance of predicate interactions for zero anaphora.

- Strength 3: Architecturally simple and principled: minimal input features and a clear grid-layer instantiation of multidimensional recurrence.

- Strength 4: Systematic depth and residual-connection study demonstrating improved training and up to ~1 F1 gain.

- Strength 5: Informative case-wise breakdown showing large gains on DAT and zero-NOM, indicating learning of long-distance dependencies.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty; core components are established and Grid-RNN’s gains over single-sequence are modest (+0.27 F1 overall).

- Weakness 2: No structural constraints or global decoding (CRF/ILP); unclear handling of multiple arguments per case and potential violations.

- Weakness 3: Missing input-feature and training-signal ablations (MARK feature, window size C, pre-trained embeddings, subword/POS).

- Weakness 4: Reproducibility/resource reporting lacking: code placeholder, no runtime/memory/complexity or batching/scalability discussion for Grid-RNN.

- Weakness 5: Scope limited to intra-sentential given predicates; missing comparisons to some relevant baselines (e.g., Shibata et al. 2016) and end-to-end applicability discussion.

- Question 1: Do you enforce at most one argument per case per predicate, and how often do violations occur or affect F1?

- Question 2: What are training/inference runtimes and memory for single vs grid models, and how do they scale with sentence length and predicate count M?

- Question 3: What are the effects of removing the MARK feature, varying window size C, and using pre-trained or character/subword embeddings?

- Question 4: Can you provide error analysis for zero-NOM vs zero-DAT, by predicate-argument distance and number of predicates?

- Question 5: Why include PRED in the label set if predicates are marked in input, does predicting PRED aid learning and what is its accuracy?

- Overall: Solid empirical work with clear evaluation; addressing structural constraints, ablations, and reproducibility would strengthen the paper.
