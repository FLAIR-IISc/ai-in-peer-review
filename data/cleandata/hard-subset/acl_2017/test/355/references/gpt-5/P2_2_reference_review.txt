Summary and key contributions
- The paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, especially intra-sentential zero arguments, by replacing syntax-heavy joint modeling with neural sequence models that rely only on word sequences. It proposes:
  1) A single-sequence BiRNN/GRU model that predicts case labels for each token with respect to a given predicate, learning contextual features without syntactic input.
  2) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence via inter-sequence connections to capture multi-predicate interactions, plus residual connections to enable deeper stacking.
- Empirically, on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits and no external resources, both models outperform prior syntactic baselines, and the Grid-RNN further improves especially on zero arguments.

Strengths
- Clear empirical advance without syntax or external resources: The single-seq model improves the “All” F1 from 79.23 (Ouchi et al., 2015) to 81.15, and the multi-seq Grid-RNN further to 81.42. For Zero arguments—a key pain point—the gains are larger: 44.09 → 46.10 → 47.12. These consistent, statistically significant improvements (bootstrap p<0.05) demonstrate that sequence-based context can replace expensive and error-prone syntactic pipelines while advancing the state of the art.
- Modeling multi-predicate interactions neurally: Prior SOTA joint models used parser-derived structures to capture predicate interactions. Here, the Grid-RNN provides a simple, general mechanism to pass information across predicates, yielding additional gains, particularly for Zero arguments (+1.0 F1 over the single model). This is a principled architectural contribution with a clear motivation grounded in earlier linguistic and modeling insights.
- Sensible and thorough experimental protocol: The work uses standard splits of NAIST 1.5, compares to strong baselines (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015), reports significance, and investigates depth and residual connections. The finding that residual connections enable 6–8 layer models with tangible improvements adds credibility and practical guidance.
- Practical significance: Removing dependency on syntax simplifies the PAS pipeline, avoids error propagation, and improves robustness in domains where parsing is unreliable. The models are task-agnostic enough to be extendable to SRL and potentially multilingual settings, as noted by the authors.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: Grid/multidimensional RNNs are known (Graves & Schmidhuber, 2009; Kalchbrenner et al., 2016). The contribution is mainly an application and instantiation to PAS; the inter-sequence connection design (neighboring predicates only, simple concatenation) is straightforward. The work’s value is more empirical/practical than conceptual.
- Decoding and structural constraints are under-specified: The output layer is token-wise softmax over labels (NOM/ACC/DAT/PRED/null). PAS typically requires at most one argument per case per predicate. The paper does not explain how uniqueness constraints are enforced, what happens if multiple tokens receive the same case label, or whether there is a final selection step (e.g., argmax per case across tokens) or global constraint satisfaction. This omission matters for both clarity and reproducibility.
- Limited baselines and ablations for multi-predicate modeling: While comparing single vs multi-sequence is useful, the paper lacks comparisons to simpler interaction mechanisms (e.g., sharing predicate-aware attention across predicates, pooling or gating across predicate-specific sequences) or to graph-based neural models that jointly encode predicate-token relations. An ablation on inter-sequence connectivity (e.g., full vs neighbor, removing PRED marker, varying window size C) would help isolate what drives the gains.
- Analysis depth is limited: Beyond aggregate F1 and a depth/residual study, there is little qualitative/error analysis (e.g., by distance, argument type frequency, predicate density). The per-case table is hard to parse and contains surprising claims (e.g., “around 30 points” improvement for DAT seems inconsistent with the shown numbers for Zero-DAT). A clearer breakdown (and sanity-checked table) would better support the claims about long-distance dependencies and rare cases.
- Reproducibility details are incomplete: The code link is a placeholder; hyperparameters are mostly provided, but important details are missing (e.g., decoding constraints as above, average number of predicates per sentence affecting complexity, batching for multi-predicate inputs, tokenization specifics for Japanese “word boundaries” and how unknowns are handled). Given modest model sizes, runtime/memory is likely fine, but reporting would help.

Questions to authors
- Decoding constraints: How do you ensure at most one argument per case per predicate? If multiple tokens are predicted as NOM, how is the final decision made? Is there a global normalization or post-hoc selection?
- Inter-sequence connectivity: Why restrict to immediate neighbor predicates in the grid? Did you try fully connected predicate interactions or different traversal orders? How sensitive are results to the predicate ordering?
- Role of the PRED label and MARK feature: Does labeling PRED in the output serve only as a training signal? Could it leak trivial cues between sequences in the multi-model? Did you ablate PRED/MARK to assess their impact?
- Efficiency and scaling: What is the average number of predicates per sentence (M), and what are the training/inference times relative to the single model? Any issues with vanishing/exploding gradients beyond what residuals fix?
- Error analysis: Do gains concentrate on long-distance Zero-NOM cases? Please provide performance by predicate-argument distance and by sentence predicate count.
- Table clarity: Please double-check Table 3 and the claim about ~30-point improvements for DAT; the numbers as printed do not seem to support that magnitude.

Overall evaluation
- Significance: Solid. Removing syntax while improving SOTA on NAIST 1.5, with stronger gains on zero arguments, is impactful for Japanese PAS.
- Originality: Moderate; the architectural idea is an application of known multidimensional RNNs, but the task-specific framing and empirical validation are valuable.
- Soundness and clarity: Mostly sound, but decoding constraints and certain details need clarification; analysis could be deeper.
- Recommendation: Lean accept, contingent on clarifying decoding and tightening analysis/ablations.
