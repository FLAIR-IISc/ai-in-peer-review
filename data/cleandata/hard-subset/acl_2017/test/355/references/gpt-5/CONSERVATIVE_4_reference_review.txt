Summary and contributions
The paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis with two neural sequence models that avoid explicit syntactic features: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN that labels each token with case roles for a given predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform older baselines; the Grid-RNN yields small but statistically significant gains over the single-sequence model, especially for zero arguments.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: A strong, syntax-free BiRNN sequence labeling baseline can match or surpass prior syntax-dependent joint models on NAIST 1.5, notably improving zero anaphora detection.
2) Joint modeling across predicates via Grid-RNN: A simple, general mechanism to share information across predicate-specific sequences produces modest but consistent gains over the single-predicate baseline.
3) Depth/residual study: Evidence that deeper stacks with residual connections improve performance, indicating the feasibility of training relatively deep recurrent architectures for PAS without external resources.

Strengths
- Solid empirical result without syntactic inputs: Despite relying only on word sequence features (plus predicate markers), the single-sequence model substantially improves over the reported Ouchi et al. (2015) numbers on NAIST 1.5, particularly for Zero. This is practically valuable given the error propagation issues of pipeline parsers they highlight and removes a dependency on high-quality parses.
- Multi-predicate interaction modeling is simple and effective: The Grid-RNN formulation is straightforward and end-to-end, and the observed +~1 F for Zero over the single-sequence baseline supports the premise that inter-predicate cues help zero anaphora.
- Sensible engineering and training: The paper covers training setup, reasonable hyperparameter sweeps (layers, weight decay, batch size), residual connections for deeper networks, and reports significance (albeit only for Single vs Multi). The approach is reproducible in spirit, and releasing code (claimed) is a plus.
- Targeted per-case analysis: Reporting per-case (NOM/ACC/DAT) breakdowns and Dep vs Zero is helpful; the improvements for Zero-NOM are aligned with the task’s main difficulty and with prior observations.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and modest gains for the new component: The single-sequence model is a direct adaptation of established BiRNN sequence labeling (e.g., Zhou & Xu, 2015) to PAS; the Grid-RNN is a known architecture applied in a fairly direct way. The incremental gains from the Grid-RNN over a strong BiRNN baseline are small (overall +0.27 F; Zero +1.02 F), raising questions about whether the additional complexity is warranted versus simpler multi-predicate sharing strategies.
- Missing and/or insufficient baselines: The comparison set omits strong recent neural baselines on NAIST 1.5 (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016) and syntax-augmented neural models. It also lacks simpler joint alternatives (e.g., attention-based cross-predicate sharing or feature concatenation) and structured decoding baselines (CRF/ILP) that enforce standard PAS constraints. Without these, it is hard to attribute the gains to the specific Grid-RNN design versus just having any joint mechanism.
- Methodological clarity on decoding constraints: The formulation assigns case labels to each token via a per-token softmax for each predicate. Standard PAS generally enforces at most one argument per case per predicate. The paper does not describe any constraint enforcement or decoding procedure to ensure such uniqueness. As presented, multiple tokens could be labeled NOM for the same predicate; it is unclear how final arguments are selected and how evaluation is computed. This is a major ambiguity that could affect both correctness and comparability.
- Evaluation and reporting gaps: Statistical significance is only reported for Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq. No significance testing versus Ouchi et al. (2015). The evaluation protocol lacks important details: exact tokenization/segmentation, definition of “word” in Japanese, how candidate arguments are filtered, whether all predicates (including those with no intrasentential argument) are included, and how precision/recall/F are computed (span vs token, uniqueness constraints). These omissions make it difficult to assess fairness and reproducibility.
- Limited analysis/ablations: Aside from depth/residuals, there is no ablation of input features (e.g., effect of the PRED window, MARK, removing predicate context), no error analysis, and no study of where multi-predicate interactions help (e.g., distance to antecedent, number of predicates, predicate types). Runtime/parameter counts and scalability with number of predicates per sentence are not reported.

Questions to authors
- How do you enforce standard PAS constraints at decoding time (e.g., at most one argument per case per predicate)? If you do not enforce them, how do you convert per-token softmax outputs to a valid set of arguments for evaluation?
- Please specify the exact evaluation protocol: What constitutes a “word” in segmentation? Are all predicates evaluated or only those with at least one intrasentential argument? How are precision/recall/F computed (token-level vs argument-level)? Are ties/multiple predictions handled?
- Why is there no comparison to Shibata et al. (2016) on NAIST 1.5? If incompatible settings preclude direct comparison, can you replicate a comparable setup or report your reimplementation results?
- Did you try simpler multi-predicate sharing mechanisms (e.g., cross-predicate self-attention, shared context encoders with predicate-specific heads) as baselines to the Grid-RNN? If so, how do they compare?
- What is the computational overhead of Grid-RNN versus the single-sequence model (parameters, training/inference time), and how does performance scale with the number of predicates per sentence?
- Have you tried adding predicted syntactic features to your models to test whether “no syntax” is a virtue or a limitation? Do such features help or hurt?

Overall, the paper shows a strong syntax-free baseline and a modestly beneficial joint mechanism. However, the lack of clarity about decoding constraints and missing baselines reduce the evidential strength for the proposed Grid-RNN as the preferred approach. Clarifying methodology and strengthening comparisons would significantly improve the paper.
