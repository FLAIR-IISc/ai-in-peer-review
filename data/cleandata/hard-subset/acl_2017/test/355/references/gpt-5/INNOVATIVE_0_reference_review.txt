Summary and contributions
This paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). The authors introduce:
1) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic parses.
2) A strong single-sequence BiRNN baseline (no syntax) that already surpasses prior SOTA, demonstrating that sentence-level sequential context is highly effective for PAS and zero anaphora.
3) Empirical evidence that residual connections and deeper stacks help, and that multi-predicate modeling yields significant gains for zero arguments.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: grid-style joint modeling across predicates without syntax. Prior work leveraged joint inference with heavy syntactic features; this work replaces that with a neural grid over the predicate-by-token lattice, a clean and general architectural idea. While multi-dimensional RNNs are known, applying them to jointly encode predicate interactions for PAS is a meaningful shift and opens a syntax-free path to joint modeling.
- Strong empirical results without syntax. Both the single-seq and multi-seq models outperform the best prior joint model with syntactic features (Ouchi et al., 2015) on NAIST 1.5. The multi-seq model achieves 81.42 F vs 79.23, and notably improves Zero identification (47.12 vs 44.09). This supports the central claim that sequence context and predicate interactions can substitute for explicit syntax for intra-sentential PAS.
- Focused gains on zero arguments. The multi-seq model’s advantage is clearest on Zero cases (+~3 F over Ouchi et al., +1 over single-seq), aligning with the paper’s linguistic motivation and demonstrating that multi-predicate information is particularly useful when dependencies are not syntactically realized.
- Practical, resource-lean setting. No external resources or syntactic parsers are used, reducing pipeline error and making the approach appealing for low-resource settings where parses are unreliable.
- Clear, reproducible training/evaluation protocol. Standard splits, significance testing via bootstrap, and analysis of depth/residual connections. The report of deeper networks with residuals helping (~1 F) is useful guidance.

Weaknesses
- Modest incremental gains from the key novelty. The multi-sequence Grid-RNN improves only slightly over the already-strong single-sequence baseline (81.42 vs 81.15 overall; +1.0 F for Zero). The primary accuracy jump seems to come from leveraging deep BiRNNs over word sequences rather than from the inter-predicate grid per se. A more thorough ablation would help isolate how much benefit arises specifically from inter-sequence connections vs added capacity.
- Limited analysis of what interactions are learned. The paper argues that multi-predicate interactions help zero anaphora, but provides little qualitative/error analysis (e.g., distance to antecedents, number of predicates per sentence, predicate co-occurrence patterns). Without case studies or probing, it’s hard to understand the mechanism or conditions under which the grid brings gains.
- Evaluation scope confined to one language/task variant. While the approach is positioned as broadly applicable to SRL, experiments are only on Japanese PAS (intra-sentential) from a single corpus. Even a small-scale check on another language or a constituent SRL benchmark (without syntax) would strengthen the generality claim.
- Comparisons to contemporary neural baselines are incomplete. The paper compares to Ouchi et al. (2015) and an older pointwise model, but not to strong neural baselines that also eschew syntax for PAS/SRL or to syntax-aware neural models on the same split. This leaves open whether the gains are competitive with modern alternatives beyond the chosen baselines.
- Some architectural/design choices are under-justified. For instance, directional sweeps in the grid, choice of GRU over other cells, omission of CRF (beyond the reasonable argument about Japanese dependency), and the role of the PRED label in the loss could be articulated and ablated more thoroughly.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide ablations that isolate the contribution of inter-sequence connections? For example: same parameter count but no inter-sequence edges; inter-sequence edges only in top layers; random predicate ordering; alternative multi-predicate fusions (e.g., cross-predicate attention).
- How sensitive is performance to the number of predicates per sentence (M)? Please report F by M-buckets and decoding/training time vs M. Does grid complexity become problematic for sentences with many predicates?
- Does the directionality/order of grid computation matter? Did you try multi-directional sweeps (as in MDLSTM) or averaging multiple traversal orders?
- How exactly is the PRED label used in training/inference? Are you predicting PRED for every predicate-specific sequence and masking loss for non-predicate tokens? Any effect on learning stability?
- Could you report performance by sentence length and by predicate-argument distance for Zero cases? This would substantiate claims about capturing long-distance dependencies.
- Have you tried integrating shallow syntactic cues (e.g., dependency heads) or pretrained embeddings to see if the grid still adds value beyond them?

Overall, I find the core idea—syntax-free, grid-style joint modeling of all predicates in a sentence—novel enough to merit attention, and the empirical results are solid, especially for zero anaphora. While the incremental improvement over the single-sequence baseline is small, it is consistent, significant, and aligned with the paper’s motivation. With stronger ablations and analysis, the work could be even more compelling. I lean positive given the conceptual direction and its potential to influence syntax-free joint modeling for PAS/SRL.
