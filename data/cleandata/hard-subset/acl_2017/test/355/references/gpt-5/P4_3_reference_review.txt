Summary
The paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, with emphasis on zero arguments, by proposing neural sequence models that do not rely on syntactic parses. A single-sequence model uses stacked Bi-RNNs over word sequences to produce context-sensitive representations and per-word case-role predictions for a given predicate. Extending this, a multi-sequence Grid-RNN jointly processes sequences for all predicates in a sentence, enabling information flow across predicates to model multi-predicate interactions. On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, both models outperform strong prior systems, with the Grid-RNN providing significant gains, especially for zero arguments. The authors also study network depth and residual connections.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A Grid-RNN architecture for jointly modeling interactions across multiple predicates in a sentence for Japanese PAS, yielding significant gains over a strong single-sequence RNN baseline and prior SOTA, particularly on zero arguments (Zero: 47.12 vs 44.09 F1 compared to Ouchi et al., 2015).
2) A strong syntax-agnostic sequence modeling baseline for Japanese PAS that, using only word sequence features and simple predicate markers, exceeds prior state-of-the-art systems that rely on syntactic information (All: 81.15/81.42 vs 79.23 F1).
3) An empirical study of depth and residual connections for PAS models, showing deeper networks with residuals improve performance.

Strengths
- Clear empirical improvement without syntactic features: Both proposed models outperform strong baselines that use syntax (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015). On the NAIST 1.5 test set, All F1: Single-Seq 81.15 and Multi-Seq 81.42 vs 79.23 (Ouchi+15), demonstrating that context learned from raw word sequences can match or surpass syntactic pipelines and avoid error propagation.

- Effective handling of zero arguments: The zero argument case is notably improved. Zero F1 increases from 44.09 (Ouchi+15) to 46.10 (Single-Seq) and 47.12 (Multi-Seq), with the latter significantly better than the single-sequence baseline. Per-case breakdown shows particularly strong gains for Zero-NOM (Multi-Seq 50.65 vs 48.11), indicating the models capture long-distance dependencies beneficial for zero anaphora.

- Modeling multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN achieves statistically significant gains over the single-sequence model overall (81.42 vs 81.15 All F1) and on Zero (47.12 vs 46.10), supporting the hypothesis that interactions among predicates are useful and can be learned from sequence data without explicit syntax.

- Depth and residual connections study: The paper systematically varies the number of layers and reports that deeper models with residual connections yield about 1.0 F1 improvement over their non-residual counterparts, indicating thoughtful optimization and robustness of the approach.

- Reproducibility: The paper provides implementation details (Theano, optimizer settings, embedding sizes, training protocol) and states that source code is publicly available, which supports verifiability.

Weaknesses
- Decoding constraints and label formulation are unclear: The output is a per-word softmax over {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null} for each predicate. It is not specified how global constraints typical in PAS (e.g., at most one core argument per case for a predicate) are enforced at decoding, if at all. Without structured inference or constraints, multiple words could be assigned the same case label, and the effect on precision/recall is not analyzed. This undermines comparability to joint/ILP models that enforce constraints.

- Incomplete ablations isolating the source of gains: The multi-sequence model has different parameterization (e.g., 64×32 GRU inputs) than the single-sequence baseline, so capacity differs. There is no capacity-controlled baseline (wider/deeper single sequence) nor an ablation that removes inter-sequence connections while matching parameter count. This makes it difficult to attribute gains strictly to multi-predicate interaction modeling versus additional capacity.

- Limited baseline coverage and comparability: While the paper compares to Imamura+09 and Ouchi+15 on NAIST 1.5, it does not include more recent neural baselines on the same splits (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016), and states that prior neural SRL works use different datasets. A discussion or results aligning settings where possible would strengthen claims of state-of-the-art performance.

- Evaluation and metric clarity: The paper reports F1 for Dep and Zero categories and per-case roles, but details of how candidate sets are constructed, how multiple predictions per case are handled, and how edges are scored for F1 are not fully specified. For example, are all words candidates for every predicate? How is PRED label used in training/evaluation when the predicate is known? Clearer definition of the evaluation protocol would aid reproducibility and fairness.

- Analysis depth: The paper lacks error analysis or qualitative examples demonstrating how multi-predicate interactions help, and does not report precision/recall trade-offs or performance by sentence length/number of predicates. Computational efficiency (O(M·T·L) complexity) and runtime/memory impact for sentences with many predicates are not discussed.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Decoding and constraints: Do you enforce any structural constraints at inference (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate), or is decoding purely per-word argmax? If unconstrained, how often do multiple words receive the same case for a predicate, and what is the effect on precision/recall?

- Evaluation protocol: Please clarify the exact scoring procedure. Is evaluation over (predicate, word, case) edges with standard F1? How is the PRED label used in training and is it ignored at evaluation?

- Candidate set: Are all tokens in the sentence considered candidates for all predicates, or is there pruning? If so, how is it done?

- Capacity control: Can you provide results for capacity-matched baselines (e.g., wider/deeper single-sequence models) to isolate the benefit of inter-sequence connections? Similarly, an ablation with Grid-RNN but inter-sequence connections removed (same parameters otherwise) would be informative.

- Efficiency and scalability: What are training/inference times and memory profiles for typical sentences, and how do they scale with the number of predicates M?

- Error analysis: Can you provide qualitative or categorical error breakdowns showing where multi-predicate interactions help (e.g., coreference-like cases, argument sharing)?
