**Summary**
This paper addresses the task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on resolving zero arguments (zero pronouns). The authors argue that previous state-of-the-art joint models rely heavily on syntactic features, making them vulnerable to error propagation from parsers. To overcome this, they propose purely neural, sequence-based models. The first model, a single-sequence Bi-directional RNN, serves as a strong baseline that processes each predicate independently. The main proposal is a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs, which jointly analyzes all predicates in a sentence simultaneously. This architecture is designed to capture interactions between predicates without any syntactic input. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that both proposed models outperform previous systems, with the Grid-RNN model establishing a new state-of-the-art, particularly improving the identification of zero arguments.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the novel and effective application of a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS. This provides an elegant, end-to-end method for capturing complex dependencies within a sentence without relying on external syntactic parsers.
2.  A significant empirical contribution is achieving new state-of-the-art results on a standard Japanese PAS benchmark. The work demonstrates convincingly that a model using only word sequence information can outperform prior methods that depend on syntactic features, thus avoiding a major source of pipeline errors.

**Strengths**
1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper presents strong empirical results, outperforming the previous best-performing system on the NAIST Text Corpus. The improvements are consistent across all argument types (Dep, Zero) and are particularly substantial for the challenging task of zero argument identification, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Model:** The use of a Grid-RNN to propagate information across parallel sequences representing different predicates is a clever and well-suited architecture for this problem. It directly models the linguistic intuition that predicates in a sentence are semantically related, and does so within a single, unified neural network.
3.  **Independence from Syntactic Features:** A key advantage of this work is its ability to achieve top performance without any syntactic information. This makes the model simpler, faster, and more robust, as it is not susceptible to errors from an upstream syntactic parser. This is a valuable direction for semantic analysis tasks.
4.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is well-written, and the models are explained clearly with the help of useful diagrams (e.g., Figure 6). The authors do a good job of making a task specific to a non-analytic language accessible to a broader NLP audience. The promise to release the source code further enhances the paper's value and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the multi-sequence model works better, but provides little insight into *why* or *in what specific cases*. The results in Table 3 indicate that the main advantage of the multi-sequence model is for identifying nominative (NOM) zero arguments. A qualitative analysis comparing the outputs of the single- and multi-sequence models on illustrative examples would significantly strengthen the paper by explaining the concrete benefits of modeling predicate interactions.
2.  **Absence of Linguistic Constraint Modeling:** The model independently predicts a label distribution for each word-predicate pair using a softmax function. This formulation does not enforce fundamental linguistic constraints, such as ensuring a predicate has at most one argument for a given case role (e.g., only one NOM) or that an argument candidate cannot fill multiple roles for the same predicate. An analysis of how often the model produces such invalid structures is needed to better understand its behavior.
3.  **Insufficient Context on Task Scope:** The motivation and problem definition could be clearer. While the paper focuses on intra-sentential zero anaphora, it doesn't provide statistics on how common this phenomenon is within the dataset relative to inter-sentential anaphora. This information is crucial for understanding the scope and overall difficulty of the task being addressed. More context on specific ambiguities in Japanese PAS would also help a general reader appreciate the challenges.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide statistics from the NAIST corpus on the proportion of zero arguments that are intra-sentential versus inter-sentential? This would help frame the importance and difficulty of the specific problem you are solving.
2.  How are inter-sentential zero arguments ("Inter-Zero") handled in your experimental setup? Are they mapped to the `null` label during training? Please discuss the potential implications of this choice on the model's ability to learn representations for the intra-sentential task.
3.  As your model does not explicitly enforce linguistic constraints, could you report how often the multi-sequence model violates them on the development set (e.g., by assigning multiple NOM arguments to a single predicate)?
4.  The improvement of the Multi-Seq model over the Single-Seq model is most pronounced for NOM zero arguments (Table 3). Do you have an analysis or intuition for why modeling multi-predicate interactions is particularly beneficial for this specific case? Providing a concrete example where the Multi-Seq model succeeds and the Single-Seq model fails would be very helpful.
