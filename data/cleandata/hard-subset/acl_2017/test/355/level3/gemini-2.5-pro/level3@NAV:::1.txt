**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a neural sequence model for Japanese Predicate-Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenge of identifying dropped arguments (zero pronouns). The authors introduce a multi-sequence architecture based on Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks (Grid-RNNs) to capture interactions between all predicates in a sentence. Critically, their model does not use explicit syntactic features, instead learning directly from word sequences. Experimental results on the NAIST Text Corpus show that this syntax-free model outperforms previous state-of-the-art systems, demonstrating significant gains in the identification of zero arguments.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the demonstration that a purely sequence-based neural model, without recourse to syntactic parsers, can achieve state-of-the-art performance on the complex task of Japanese PAS analysis. This provides a strong argument for simpler, end-to-end trainable models that are not susceptible to error propagation from upstream parsing components.
2.  The paper makes a notable contribution to the challenging problem of zero pronoun resolution in Japanese. It empirically validates that modeling multi-predicate interactions via a Grid-RNN architecture is particularly effective for this sub-task, a key finding for pro-drop languages.

**Strengths**
1.  The most compelling strength is the model's performance. By outperforming prior systems that rely heavily on syntactic information, the work successfully challenges the conventional wisdom that explicit syntax is a prerequisite for high-quality PAS analysis.
2.  The focus on zero pronoun identification is a significant asset. The authors tackle a well-known difficulty in Japanese NLP, and the performance gains shown by the multi-sequence model clearly highlight the benefits of their proposed architecture for this specific problem.
3.  The paper is commendably clear, detailed, and well-written. The model architecture, implementation details, and experimental setup are described thoroughly, ensuring the work is easy to understand and facilitating reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**
The paper’s weaknesses are minor and primarily related to presentation.
1.  The manuscript contains several typographical errors and minor English fluency issues that should be corrected in a final version. A thorough proofreading by a native speaker is recommended.
2.  The explanation of the PRED-context feature could be enhanced. The authors could better illustrate this point by using the example in Figure 6 to explain how including context like the accusative case marker with the verb provides a useful signal to the model.
3.  The use of boldface in Table 2 is not explained. Please clarify its meaning in the table caption or text.
