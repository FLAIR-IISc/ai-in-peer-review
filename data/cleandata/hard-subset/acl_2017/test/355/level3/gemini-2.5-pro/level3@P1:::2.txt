**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes two neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, a task complicated by the frequent omission of arguments (zero arguments). The first model, "Single-Seq," is a stacked bidirectional RNN that processes argument candidates for each predicate independently. This model is largely an adaptation of the approach by Zhou and Xu (2015) for English Semantic Role Labeling (SRL). The second and primary model, "Multi-Seq," extends the first by using a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly model all predicates within a sentence. The goal is to capture interactions between multiple predicates to better resolve arguments, particularly zero arguments. A key advantage of this approach is that it operates directly on word sequences, foregoing the need for syntactic features from external parsers, thereby avoiding potential error propagation. The authors evaluate their models on the NAIST Text Corpus, demonstrating that both models outperform the previous state-of-the-art system, with the Multi-Seq model achieving the best results.

**Contributions**

1.  **A New State-of-the-Art for Japanese PAS without Syntactic Features:** The primary contribution of this work is empirical. By successfully adapting and extending recent neural sequence modeling techniques, the authors establish a new state-of-the-art F-measure of 81.42 on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 benchmark. This result is achieved without relying on syntactic dependency information, which was a core component of previous leading systems. This demonstrates the power of end-to-end learning for this task and simplifies the overall system architecture.

2.  **A Grid-RNN Model for Multi-Predicate Interactions:** The paper proposes a novel application of Grid-RNNs to model the interactions between different predicate-argument structures within the same sentence. While the performance gain from this specific architectural choice is modest, it directly addresses a known linguistic phenomenon and provides a principled neural architecture for capturing these dependencies, showing particular effectiveness for the difficult case of zero-argument identification.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed models achieve a significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art system, Ouchi et al. (2015). The overall F-measure improves by over 2 points (79.23 to 81.42), which is a substantial gain on a standard benchmark. This clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed neural approach.

2.  **Significant Improvement on a Known Hard Problem:** The models show particularly strong performance on the identification of zero arguments, a key challenge in Japanese PAS. As shown in Table 1, the Multi-Seq model improves the F-measure on zero arguments by over 3 points compared to Ouchi et al. (2015) (44.09 to 47.12). This suggests that the contextual representations learned by the RNNs are highly effective for resolving these long-distance dependencies.

3.  **Elimination of Dependency on Syntactic Parsers:** A major strength of this work is that it achieves state-of-the-art performance using only word sequence information. Previous high-performing systems (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015) relied heavily on syntactic features, making them susceptible to error propagation from the parsing stage. This work presents a more robust, end-to-end system that is simpler to build and deploy.

4.  **Well-Written and Clearly Motivated Paper:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The motivation for moving from a single-predicate model to a multi-predicate interaction model is clearly explained, and the architectures of the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models are described in sufficient detail. The inclusion of experiments on network depth and residual connections provides a thorough analysis of the model's components.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Missing Comparison to a Key Contemporary Baseline:** The paper fails to compare its results against Shibata et al. (2016), who also applied a neural network model to Japanese PAS, building upon the same joint model from Ouchi et al. (2015). Since Shibata et al. (2016) represents another recent neural approach to the same problem, its omission is a significant weakness. Without this comparison, it is difficult to fully assess whether the performance gains are due to the specific RNN-based architecture proposed here or simply due to the general power of neural networks in this domain.

2.  **Limited Impact of the Multi-Sequence Model:** The main technical novelty of the paper is the use of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions. However, the performance difference between the Single-Seq model (81.15 F1) and the Multi-Seq model (81.42 F1) is quite small (+0.27 F1). While statistically significant, this gain is much smaller than the overall improvement over the baseline. It suggests that most of the performance gain comes from the strong sequential modeling of the Bi-RNN, and the explicit modeling of predicate interactions via the Grid-RNN provides only a marginal benefit. This is in contrast to the large gains reported by Ouchi et al. (2015) for their joint modeling approach, which raises questions about the effectiveness of this particular method for capturing predicate interactions.

3.  **Incremental Methodological Contribution:** The Single-Seq model is a straightforward application of the architecture from Zhou and Xu (2015) to a new language and task. The primary novelty lies in the Multi-Seq extension. As noted above, the empirical benefit of this extension is small. Therefore, the paper's contribution feels more like a strong piece of engineering and successful application of existing methods rather than a breakthrough in fundamental methodology.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your work and Shibata et al. (2016) both present neural approaches to Japanese PAS that were published around the same time. Could you please discuss the key differences between your model and theirs, and why you believe your approach is advantageous? A direct comparison would be ideal, but even a detailed discussion would help situate your contribution in the field.

2.  The performance gain from your primary contribution, the Multi-Seq model over the Single-Seq baseline, is only 0.27 F1. This is considerably smaller than the benefit of joint modeling reported in Ouchi et al. (2015). Do you have any insight into why the impact of modeling multi-predicate interactions appears to be smaller in your framework? Could it be that the stacked Bi-RNN in the Single-Seq model already implicitly captures some of these interactions through its large receptive field, leaving little room for improvement for the explicit Grid-RNN connections?
