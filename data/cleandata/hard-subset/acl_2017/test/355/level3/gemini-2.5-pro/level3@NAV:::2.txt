**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**
This paper presents two neural models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis. The first is a strong baseline that adapts the end-to-end RNN model from Zhou & Xu (2015) to Japanese PAS. The second, which is the main technical proposal, extends this model using Grid-RNNs to create a multi-sequence architecture designed to capture interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that the proposed models achieve a new state-of-the-art result without relying on syntactic features.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A successful demonstration that a syntax-free, end-to-end neural sequence model can significantly outperform previous state-of-the-art systems for Japanese PAS that rely on syntactic information.
2.  The application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions, which provides a novel approach to this problem and yields additional, albeit small, performance gains, particularly for identifying zero arguments.

**Strengths:**
1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper reports a substantial improvement over the prior state-of-the-art system (Ouchi et al., 2015), boosting the overall F1-score from 79.23 to 81.42. These strong empirical results on a standard benchmark are a compelling argument for acceptance.
2.  **Well-Structured and Clearly Motivated:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. It clearly motivates the need for multi-predicate modeling by highlighting the challenge of zero anaphora in Japanese and builds its argument logically from a single-sequence model to the more complex multi-sequence variant.
3.  **Elimination of Syntactic Dependency:** A key strength of the proposed approach is achieving top performance without requiring input from a syntactic parser. This avoids error propagation from a pipeline and simplifies the overall system, which is a valuable practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Missing Comparison to Key Related Work:** The paper fails to compare its model against Shibata et al. (2016), which is also a neural network-based model for Japanese PAS that extends the work of Ouchi et al. (2015). This is a critical omission, as a direct comparison is necessary to properly situate the paper's contribution within the context of other contemporary neural approaches.
2.  **Incremental Gain from the Main Technical Contribution:** The paper’s primary novelty lies in the Grid-RNN multi-sequence model. However, this model provides only a marginal improvement over the simpler single-sequence baseline (81.42 vs. 81.15 F1). The vast majority of the gains come from adapting the existing architecture of Zhou & Xu (2015), which raises questions about the practical impact and complexity trade-off of the Grid-RNN extension.
3.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core single-sequence model, which is responsible for most of the performance gains, is a direct application of a well-known architecture. While its successful adaptation is valuable, the work is more of an effective extension of existing methods rather than a fundamentally new technique.
