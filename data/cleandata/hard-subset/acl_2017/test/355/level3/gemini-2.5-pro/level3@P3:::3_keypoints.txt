- Proposes two neural sequence models (Single-Sequence and novel Multi-Sequence Grid-RNN) for Japanese PAS to handle pro-drop zero pronouns.  
- Models operate directly on word sequences without external syntactic parsers to avoid pipeline error propagation.  
- Multi-Sequence Grid-RNN jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture inter-predicate interactions.  
- Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show both models outperform prior state of the art, with Multi-Seq best overall and on zero arguments.  
- Contribution: novel and effective application of Grid-RNNs for joint PAS analysis, learning semantic dependencies from words alone.  
- Contribution: strong empirical results and neural baselines establishing a new SOTA on a standard benchmark.  
- Strength: significant performance gains, especially for intra-sentential zero pronouns (Zero arguments).  
- Strength: well-motivated architecture progression (Single-Seq → Multi-Seq) serving as an effective ablation.  
- Strength: paper is clear and accessible with helpful examples and illustrative diagrams.  
- Weakness: lacks qualitative analysis explaining why and in which contexts Multi-Seq outperforms Single-Seq; analysis limited to aggregate F1.  
- Weakness: insufficient contextualization of task difficulty (missing corpus statistics and clearer contrast with canonical SRL issues like NOM/ACC ambiguity).  
- Weakness: model does not enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per case role), and paper doesn’t analyze frequency of invalid outputs.  
- Question: please provide detailed comparisons and example sentences showing where Multi-Seq succeeds and Single-Seq fails, especially for NOM gains.  
- Question: how often does the best model violate basic linguistic constraints, and does it learn them implicitly from data?  
- Question: provide NAIST corpus stats on proportion of zero pronouns with intra-sentential vs inter-sentential antecedents.  
- Question: how are inter-sentential zero arguments represented in training—unannotated/null or explicitly represented?  
- Additional comment: paper needs proofreading (several typos) and reference/bibliography formatting fixes.
