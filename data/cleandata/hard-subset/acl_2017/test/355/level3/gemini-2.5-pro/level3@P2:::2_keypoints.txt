- Proposes two neural models for Japanese PAS: a single-sequence Bi-RNN and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates; both beat prior SOTA on NAIST, with the multi-sequence best on zero arguments.  
- Main contribution: a new SOTA PAS system that achieves strong results without using syntactic features, simplifying the pipeline and avoiding parser errors.  
- Main contribution: introduces a Grid-RNN to explicitly model multi-predicate interactions, yielding targeted gains on zero-argument detection.  
- Strength: Significant overall performance improvement — best model F1 = 81.42, ≈2.2 points above prior best (79.23).  
- Strength: Notable improvement on zero (omitted) argument identification — multi-sequence model raises F from 44.09 to 47.12.  
- Strength: Eliminates reliance on syntactic parsers while improving accuracy, making the approach more robust and self-contained.  
- Strength: Paper is clear, well-structured, and authors plan to release code for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Fails to compare experimentally with Shibata et al. (2016), a relevant recent neural baseline, which weakens the SOTA claim.  
- Weakness: The Grid-RNN’s overall gain over the strong single-sequence baseline is marginal (+0.27 F1), calling into question its practical impact given added complexity.  
- Weakness: The paper does not analyze why multi-predicate modeling yields much smaller gains here than in prior joint-model work (e.g., Ouchi et al.), nor whether Bi-RNNs implicitly capture those interactions.  
- Question: Please include experimental comparison to Shibata et al. (2016) to validate the SOTA claim.  
- Question: Provide analysis of the complexity vs. benefit trade-off for the Grid-RNN and qualitative/error analyses showing where multi-predicate modeling helps most.
