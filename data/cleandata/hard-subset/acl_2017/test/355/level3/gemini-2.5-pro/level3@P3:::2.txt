### Summary

This paper proposes two neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, a task complicated by frequent argument omission (zero pronouns). The first model, a "single-sequence" approach, adapts the state-of-the-art Bi-RNN architecture from English Semantic Role Labeling (Zhou & Xu, 2015) to Japanese PAS. This model processes each predicate independently. The second, more novel "multi-sequence" model extends this by using a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly model all predicates in a sentence simultaneously, explicitly capturing interactions between their argument structures. Evaluated on the standard NAIST Text Corpus, both models outperform the previous state-of-the-art system, which relied on syntactic features. The Grid-RNN model yields further, albeit small, improvements, particularly in the challenging task of identifying zero arguments. The work successfully demonstrates that end-to-end neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance on this task without relying on external syntactic parsers.

### Contributions

1.  **A new state-of-the-art for Japanese PAS analysis:** The paper's primary contribution is empirical. It successfully applies a deep Bi-RNN model to Japanese PAS, demonstrating that a purely sequence-based neural approach can significantly outperform previous state-of-the-art systems that rely on complex, feature-engineered, and syntax-dependent pipelines. This result is important as it aligns Japanese PAS with trends in other NLP tasks, showing the power of end-to-end learning while avoiding potential error propagation from parsers.
2.  **A novel neural architecture for modeling multi-predicate interactions:** The paper proposes using Grid-RNNs to create a multi-sequence model that jointly decodes the argument structures for all predicates in a sentence. While the performance gains from this specific architecture are modest, it presents a novel and principled way to explicitly model inter-dependencies between predicates within a unified neural framework, which is a valuable architectural contribution to the field of semantic analysis.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant performance improvement over the state of the art:** The proposed models achieve a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark. The single-sequence model improves the overall F1-score from 79.23% (Ouchi et al., 2015) to 81.15%, and the multi-sequence model further improves it to 81.42%. This is a clear and substantial gain, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2.  **Strong performance on the most difficult subtask:** The paper provides a convincing analysis showing that the models are particularly effective at identifying zero arguments, which is the key challenge in Japanese PAS. The multi-sequence model improves the F1-score for zero arguments by over 3 points compared to the previous state-of-the-art (44.09% to 47.12%), highlighting the practical value of modeling sentential context with RNNs.
3.  **Well-motivated and clearly presented work:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The motivation to move away from syntax-dependent models to avoid error propagation is compelling. The progression from a strong single-predicate baseline to a joint multi-predicate model is logical and allows for a clear analysis of the benefits of modeling predicate interactions. The model diagrams are clear and aid in understanding the proposed architectures.
4.  **No reliance on syntactic features:** A key strength is that the models achieve state-of-the-art performance using only word sequence information. This simplifies the system pipeline, removes the dependency on a parser, and avoids the problem of error propagation, making the model more robust and self-contained.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of the primary model:** The strongest results come from the single-sequence model, which is a direct and straightforward adaptation of the architecture proposed by Zhou & Xu (2015) for English SRL. While its successful application to Japanese PAS is a solid engineering contribution, the methodological novelty is limited. The paper's main architectural innovation, the Grid-RNN model, provides only a marginal improvement (+0.27 F1) over this very strong baseline.
2.  **Missing comparison with a key contemporary paper:** The paper fails to compare its results with Shibata et al. (2016), which is cited as another recent neural approach for Japanese PAS that also extends the work of Ouchi et al. (2015). This omission makes it difficult to fully assess the proposed model's standing relative to concurrent work. A direct comparison or, at a minimum, a detailed discussion of the differences in methodology and experimental setup is necessary to properly situate the paper's contribution.
3.  **Modest impact of the main technical contribution:** The central claim of the paper is that modeling multi-predicate interactions via a Grid-RNN is beneficial. However, the empirical gain from this component is quite small. The vast majority of the improvement over prior work comes from the powerful single-sequence Bi-RNN baseline. This raises the question of whether the added complexity of the Grid-RNN architecture is justified by the marginal performance gain.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper omits a comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), another recent neural network model for Japanese PAS. Could you please clarify why this comparison was not included? Given that their work also targets the NAIST Text Corpus, a discussion of how your method and results compare would be crucial for understanding the current state of the art.
2.  The performance gain from the multi-sequence model over the strong single-sequence baseline is statistically significant but small (+0.27 F1 overall). Could you comment on the computational cost (in terms of training time and/or decoding speed) of the Grid-RNN model compared to the single-sequence model? Does the modest performance gain justify the additional architectural complexity?
3.  The deep Bi-RNN in the single-sequence model already has access to the full sentence context for each predicate. To what extent do you believe this strong contextualization implicitly captures some of the multi-predicate interactions that the Grid-RNN is designed to model explicitly? Could this implicit modeling explain why the additional gains from the multi-sequence model are not larger?
