- Summary: Proposes Single-Seq (stacked Bi-RNN per predicate) and Multi-Seq (Grid-RNN jointly modeling predicates) for Japanese PAS, achieving state-of-the-art results without syntactic features.

- Contribution 1: Establishes a new SOTA F1 of 81.42 on the NAIST Text Corpus using only word-sequence information (no syntactic dependencies).

- Contribution 2: Introduces a Grid-RNN architecture to model multi-predicate interactions, showing particular benefit for zero-argument identification.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical gains—overall F1 improved by >2 points over prior SOTA (Ouchi et al., 2015).

- Strength 2: Notable improvement on zero-argument identification (~+3 F1), addressing a key challenge in Japanese PAS.

- Strength 3: Eliminates reliance on external syntactic parsers, reducing error propagation and simplifying deployment.

- Strength 4: Well-written paper with clear motivation and thorough analyses (network depth, residuals).

- Weakness 1: Omits comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), a relevant contemporary neural baseline.

- Weakness 2: Multi-Seq yields only a small improvement (+0.27 F1) over Single-Seq, questioning the practical impact of the Grid-RNN.

- Weakness 3: Methodological novelty is incremental—Single-Seq largely adapts Zhou & Xu (2015), and Multi-Seq’s benefits are modest.

- Question 1: Please compare/contrast your approach with Shibata et al. (2016) or provide reasons for not including it.

- Question 2: Why is the Multi-Seq gain over Single-Seq so small—does the stacked Bi-RNN already capture multi-predicate interactions?
