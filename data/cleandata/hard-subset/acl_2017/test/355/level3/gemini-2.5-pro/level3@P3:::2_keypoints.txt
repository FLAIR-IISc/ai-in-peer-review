- Summary: Proposes two neural models for Japanese PAS — a per-predicate single-sequence Bi-RNN and a joint multi-predicate Grid-RNN — both beat prior syntax-dependent SOTA, with Grid-RNN giving modest gains on zero pronouns, all without external parsers.

- Contribution 1: Establishes a new state-of-the-art for Japanese PAS using an end-to-end Bi-RNN approach (overall F1 from 79.23% to 81.15%).

- Contribution 2: Introduces a Grid-RNN multi-sequence architecture to explicitly model inter-predicate interactions, yielding a small additional gain (81.15% → 81.42%).

- Strength 1: Significant empirical improvement over prior work, demonstrating the effectiveness of a sequence-only neural approach.

- Strength 2: Stronger performance on the hardest subtask (zero arguments), improving zero-argument F1 from 44.09% to 47.12%.

- Strength 3: Well-motivated and clearly presented progression from single-predicate to joint modeling, with helpful diagrams and analysis.

- Strength 4: Eliminates dependence on syntactic features and parsers, reducing pipeline complexity and error propagation.

- Weakness 1: Limited methodological novelty — the best gains come from adapting Zhou & Xu (2015) rather than from a new core technique.

- Weakness 2: Missing experimental comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), another recent neural PAS model on the same corpus.

- Weakness 3: The Grid-RNN’s empirical benefit is marginal relative to its added architectural complexity.

- Question 1: Why was Shibata et al. (2016) not compared experimentally or discussed in depth?

- Question 2: What is the computational cost (training/decoding time) of the Grid-RNN versus the single-sequence model, and is the small F1 gain worth it?

- Question 3: To what extent does the deep Bi-RNN already capture inter-predicate interactions, possibly explaining the limited extra gain from the Grid-RNN?
