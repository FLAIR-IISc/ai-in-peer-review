### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis. The authors propose two models that operate directly on word sequences, removing the reliance on syntactic features that characterized previous state-of-the-art systems. The first model, a "single-sequence" model, adapts a strong Bi-RNN architecture from English Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) to independently identify arguments for each predicate. The second, and the paper's main proposal, is a "multi-sequence" model that employs a Grid-RNN to jointly process all predicates in a sentence simultaneously, aiming to capture and exploit interactions between them. Experiments on the standard NAIST Text Corpus show that both models outperform the previous state-of-the-art, with the multi-sequence model achieving the best results, particularly on the challenging task of identifying omitted (zero) arguments.

In my view, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new state-of-the-art system for Japanese PAS analysis that does not require syntactic features.** The paper successfully adapts a powerful sequence labeling architecture to the Japanese task and demonstrates that it can outperform previous, more complex systems that rely on potentially noisy syntactic parses. This is a strong empirical and engineering contribution.
2.  **The application of a Grid-RNN architecture to explicitly model multi-predicate interactions.** While the performance gain from this specific component over the strong single-predicate baseline is modest, it presents a novel method for jointly modeling arguments for all predicates in a sentence and shows a targeted improvement for the difficult zero-argument identification subtask.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The proposed models establish a new state-of-the-art on the standard NAIST Text Corpus benchmark. The best model achieves an F-measure of 81.42, a substantial improvement of over 2 points compared to the previous best system of Ouchi et al. (2015) at 79.23. Such a clear and significant gain on a standard task is a very strong argument for acceptance.
2.  **Effective Handling of Zero Arguments:** The identification of zero arguments is a notoriously difficult problem in Japanese PAS. The proposed models show a marked improvement on this subtask, with the Multi-Sequence model improving the F-score by over 3 points (from 44.09 to 47.12) compared to the prior state-of-the-art. This demonstrates that the models' ability to encode sentential context is particularly beneficial for resolving these long-distance dependencies.
3.  **Elimination of Reliance on Syntactic Parsers:** A major advantage of this work is achieving SOTA performance without using any syntactic features. This simplifies the overall system, makes it more self-contained, and crucially, avoids the problem of error propagation from upstream parsers. This is a valuable step forward for building more robust NLP pipelines.
4.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and easy to follow. The progression from the baseline single-sequence model to the more complex multi-sequence model is logical. The authors' plan to release their code is also a positive point that will facilitate reproducibility and future work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Missing Comparison to a Key Contemporary Baseline:** The paper's claim to be "state-of-the-art" is undermined by the lack of comparison with Shibata et al. (2016). This ACL 2016 paper is highly relevant, as it also uses a neural network to improve upon the Ouchi et al. (2015) model for the same task. The authors cite this work in their related work section but do not compare against it in their experiments. Without this comparison, it is difficult to accurately assess the proposed model's standing relative to other recent neural approaches.
2.  **Marginal Impact of the Main Technical Contribution:** The primary novelty of the paper lies in the multi-sequence Grid-RNN model designed to capture predicate interactions. However, the performance gain from this model over the simpler single-sequence baseline is quite small (+0.27 F1 overall, from 81.15 to 81.42). The vast majority of the improvement over prior work is achieved by the single-sequence model, which is a direct application of an existing architecture (Zhou & Xu, 2015). This raises questions about the practical significance of the more complex Grid-RNN architecture.
3.  **Understated Effect of Multi-Predicate Modeling:** The motivation for the multi-sequence model is based on the success of joint modeling in prior work (Ouchi et al., 2015). However, the performance gain attributed to modeling these interactions in this paper is much smaller than what Ouchi et al. reported for their joint features. The paper does not discuss this discrepancy. It is possible that the powerful contextual representations learned by the Bi-RNN in the single-sequence model already implicitly capture many of these interactions, leaving little room for improvement for the explicit Grid-RNN connections. A deeper analysis of this effect would have strengthened the paper's claims.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please address the omission of Shibata et al. (2016) from your experimental comparisons? Given its relevance as another recent neural approach to Japanese PAS, a comparison is necessary to fully situate your work and validate the state-of-the-art claims.
2.  The improvement from the multi-sequence model over the single-sequence baseline is statistically significant but small. Could you provide more insight into the trade-off between the additional complexity of the Grid-RNN and this modest performance gain? Is there any qualitative analysis (e.g., examining specific sentence structures or error types) that more clearly demonstrates where the explicit multi-predicate modeling is most beneficial?
