- Summary: Proposes purely neural sequence-based models for Japanese PAS zero-argument resolution, including a single-sequence BiRNN baseline and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates.  
- Summary: Experiments on the NAIST corpus show both models outperform prior syntax-dependent systems, with the Grid-RNN achieving state-of-the-art, especially for zero argument identification.

- Contribution: Introduces a novel application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions end-to-end without syntactic input.  
- Contribution: Empirically establishes a new state-of-the-art on a standard Japanese PAS benchmark, showing sequence-only models can beat syntax-based pipelines.

- Strength: Strong empirical gains across argument types, with substantial improvement on the hard zero-argument task.  
- Strength: Well-motivated and appropriate model design that captures predicate interactions within a unified architecture.  
- Strength: Achieves top performance without syntactic features, avoiding parser error propagation and simplifying the pipeline.  
- Strength: Paper is clear and accessible, with useful figures and a promise to release code for reproducibility.

- Weakness: Lacks qualitative analysis explaining why the multi-sequence model improves performance and which concrete cases benefit.  
- Weakness: Model does not enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., at most one argument per role), and the paper does not report how often invalid structures occur.  
- Weakness: Insufficient contextualization of the task scope—no statistics on intra- vs. inter-sentential zero anaphora or detailed discussion of Japanese-specific ambiguities.

- Question: Please provide NAIST statistics on the proportion of intra-sentential versus inter-sentential zero arguments.  
- Question: How are inter-sentential zero arguments handled in training and evaluation (e.g., mapped to null), and what are the implications?  
- Question: How often does the multi-sequence model violate linguistic constraints (e.g., multiple NOMs for one predicate) on the dev set?  
- Question: Why does the Multi-Seq model improve particularly on NOM zero arguments, and can you show concrete example(s) where it succeeds but Single-Seq fails?
