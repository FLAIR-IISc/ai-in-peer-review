**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a joint neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates. The primary contribution is the empirical demonstration that this syntax-free approach achieves new state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus. By modeling predicate interactions directly from word sequences, the work effectively bypasses the need for syntactic parsers and their associated error propagation.

**Strengths**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper presents a solidly executed study with convincing results. The proposed model significantly outperforms previous systems, especially on the challenging task of identifying zero-pronoun arguments, thereby establishing a new benchmark.
2.  **Effective Syntax-Free Modeling:** A key strength is achieving these results without syntactic parser output. This makes the model simpler and more robust by eliminating error propagation from an upstream component.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and accessible to non-specialists in Japanese NLP, with helpful figures illustrating the model architectures.

**Weaknesses**

The paper's main weakness is a lack of deeper analysis and contextualization.
1.  **Superficial Analysis of Gains:** The paper demonstrates *that* the joint model is better but provides little insight into *why*. A qualitative analysis comparing Single-Seq and Multi-Seq outputs is needed to illustrate where joint modeling helps. The observation that gains are concentrated on NOM arguments (Table 3) is not discussed.
2.  **Insufficient Task Context:** The contribution would be clearer with more detail on the task's difficulty, such as the ambiguity between NOM/ACC cases and the specific challenges of zero-pronoun resolution. Statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are resolvable intra-sententially are needed to properly frame the task.
3.  **Model Behavior and Constraints:** The model does not explicitly enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per role). An analysis of whether the model learns these constraints implicitly is missing.
4.  **Presentation:** The paper contains minor but frequent typographical and casing errors that should be addressed (e.g., inconsistent capitalization in references, "significent test").

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify how inter-sentential zero pronouns are represented in your data (e.g., no label, or an explicit empty argument marker)? What are the implications of this choice?
2.  Can you provide an analysis of how often your model violates linguistic constraints, for example, by assigning two different fillers to the same argument slot for a given predicate?
