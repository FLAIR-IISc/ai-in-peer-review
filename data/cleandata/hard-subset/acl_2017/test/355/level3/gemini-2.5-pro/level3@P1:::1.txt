**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis. The primary goal is to address the challenging problem of identifying predicate-argument relations, especially for zero-pronouns (omitted arguments), without relying on explicit syntactic information from parsers, which can introduce errors. The authors propose two models: a "single-sequence" model using stacked bidirectional RNNs that analyzes each predicate independently, and a more sophisticated "multi-sequence" model using Grid-RNNs that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture their interactions. The experiments, conducted on the NAIST Text Corpus, demonstrate that both models outperform previous state-of-the-art systems, with the Grid-RNN model achieving the best results, particularly on the difficult task of zero argument identification.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions for PAS.** This is a sophisticated use of a complex neural architecture that is well-motivated by the linguistic phenomenon of predicate-predicate dependencies in languages like Japanese. It provides a principled, end-to-end way to capture information that previously required joint inference over structured outputs.
2.  **Demonstrating state-of-the-art performance for Japanese PAS without syntactic features.** The paper shows convincingly that a purely sequence-based neural model can outperform previous systems that heavily rely on syntactic parse trees. This is a significant result, as it suggests a path toward more robust semantic analysis systems that are not bottlenecked by parsing performance.
3.  **A detailed and clearly presented empirical study.** The paper's description of the models, features, and experimental setup is thorough and transparent, making the work easy to understand and likely reproducible.

**Strengths**

1.  **Sophisticated and Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The core technical contribution, the multi-sequence Grid-RNN model, is a strong point. The architecture directly addresses the known issue that predicates within a sentence are often semantically related and can provide clues for each other's argument structures. Moving from a single-sequence model to a grid structure that allows information to flow between predicate-specific sequences is an elegant solution. The significant F-measure improvement for zero arguments (from 46.10 to 47.12) provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of this approach.

2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The paper achieves new state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark for Japanese PAS. Crucially, it does so without relying on syntactic features, which distinguishes it from prior work (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015) and removes a major source of error propagation. The gains are particularly notable for zero arguments (+3.03 F1 over Ouchi et al., 2015), which is arguably the most difficult aspect of this task. The detailed breakdown of results per case role (Table 3) further strengthens the claims, showing consistent improvements across the board.

3.  **Exceptional Clarity and Detail:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The models are explained clearly with helpful diagrams (Figures 3 and 6). The feature extraction process is described in sufficient detail (Section 3.1), and the experimental setup is transparent. This high level of clarity and detail makes the contributions easy to appreciate and builds confidence in the results.

**Weaknesses**

The paper is of high quality, and its weaknesses are minor, primarily relating to presentation. Most of these points are suggestions for the camera-ready version. The paper would benefit from one final proofreading pass to correct a number of small typographical and grammatical errors (a detailed list is provided below in "Additional Comments"). These minor issues do not detract from the paper's technical contributions but improving them would enhance the final publication.

**Questions to Authors**

My primary question during the review process concerned the use of boldface in Table 2, which was unclear. The authors clarified during the response period that bolding was used to indicate the best-performing model configuration for both the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models, respectively. I am satisfied with this clarification.

**Additional Comments (Suggestions for Camera-Ready)**

The paper is strong and I recommend acceptance. The following are minor suggestions for improvement, mostly regarding English fluency and typos.

*   **Suggestion for Clarity:** In Section 3.1, when describing the PRED feature, it would be helpful to walk through a concrete example using the sentence from Figure 1. For instance, for the predicate `逮捕した (arrested)`, clearly list what the five words in the PRED feature window would be. This would make the feature's function more intuitive.
