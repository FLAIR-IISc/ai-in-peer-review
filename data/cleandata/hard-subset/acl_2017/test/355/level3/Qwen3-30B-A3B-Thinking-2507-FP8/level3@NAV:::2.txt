

This paper proposes a neural modeling approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, extending Zhou & Xu's (2015) English SRL model to capture multi-predicate interactions through Grid-RNNs. The main contributions are: (1) extending single-sequential modeling to multi-sequential modeling to capture interactions between arguments of multiple predicates, (2) demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information, and (3) showing slight improvements in zero argument detection through Grid-RNN variants.

Strengths include strong empirical results on the standard NAIST Text Corpus benchmark, with the multi-sequence model achieving 81.42% F1 (significantly better than Ouchi et al.'s 79.23%). The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated by the challenging zero argument problem in Japanese PAS. The authors provide thorough ablation studies showing that Grid-RNNs specifically improve zero argument detection by approximately 1.0 point (46.10% vs. 47.12%).

The primary weakness is that the main contribution represents an extension rather than a fundamentally novel method. The impact of multi-predicate interactions appears smaller than reported in Ouchi et al. (2015), as the improvement from single to multi-sequential modeling is marginal (81.15% vs. 81.42%). Most significantly, the paper omits comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. with neural-network modeling, making the claimed "state-of-the-art" claim questionable. The paper also lacks sufficient discussion on why their approach yields different results than Ouchi et al. regarding the impact of predicate interactions.

The authors should address whether their Grid-RNN approach would be applicable to English SRL, and provide a more detailed comparison with Shibata et al. (2016) to clarify the novelty and significance of their contribution relative to existing neural approaches for Japanese PAS.
