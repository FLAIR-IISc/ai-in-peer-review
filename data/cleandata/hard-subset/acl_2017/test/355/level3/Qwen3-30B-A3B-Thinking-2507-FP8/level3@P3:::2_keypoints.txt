- Proposes neural models for Japanese PAS, extending Zhou & Xu (2015) and introducing single-sequential and multi-sequential (Grid-RNN) models to capture multi-predicate interactions.
- Evaluated on NAIST Text Corpus and achieves state-of-the-art performance without using syntactic parser information.
- Claims significant improvements in zero argument identification, a key challenge in Japanese PAS.

- Extends single-sequential to multi-sequential modeling using Grid-RNNs to model interactions among predicates.
- Demonstrates neural models can match or exceed prior state-of-the-art without relying on syntactic features.
- Reports notable gains for zero arguments, supporting the need to model cross-predicate interactions.

- Eliminates pipeline parser error propagation and achieves higher overall F1 (81.42% vs. 79.23%) without syntactic input.
- Grid-RNN multi-sequence model yields clear improvements on zero arguments (47.12% vs. 44.09%).
- Paper is well-structured, motivated, and provides reproducible experimental and ablation details.

- Main novelty is incremental (single- to multi-sequential) and adapts existing Grid-RNN architectures rather than proposing a fundamentally new method.
- Multi-sequence gives only a small F1 gain over single-sequence (0.27 points), much less than gains reported by Ouchi et al. (2015).
- Lacks comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), which also applied neural methods to Japanese PAS.
- Does not clearly explain which aspects of their neural/Grid-RNN approach yield improvements over prior neural approaches.

- How does the Grid-RNN approach differ from Shibata et al. (2016), and why does it outperform theirs without syntactic input?
- Why is the F1 improvement from single- to multi-sequence (0.27 points) much smaller than the 2–3 point gains reported by Ouchi et al.?
- Can you clarify specific advantages of your method over Shibata et al. (2016), who also achieved strong non-syntactic results?
- Please provide detailed error analysis showing how your model improves zero argument identification compared to prior approaches.
