

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper introduces a neural modeling approach for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis that uses Grid-RNNs to automatically induce features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information, without relying on syntactic information. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art systems that depend on syntactic parsers, particularly for the challenging task of zero pronoun resolution.

## Main Contributions
1. A Grid-RNN-based neural model that achieves state-of-the-art results for Japanese PAS analysis without requiring syntactic information, thus avoiding error propagation issues inherent in pipeline approaches that depend on syntactic parsers.
2. A significant improvement in zero pronoun resolution (a major challenge in Japanese PAS analysis), with the multi-sequence model achieving a 3-point F-measure improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (47.12% vs 44.09%).
3. Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing the importance of multi-predicate interactions, network depth, and residual connections for the task, with ablation studies demonstrating that the grid-type neural architecture effectively captures multi-predicate interactions.

## Strengths
1. The paper's primary contribution—developing a neural model that achieves state-of-the-art results without syntactic information—is highly significant. Previous state-of-the-art systems (Ouchi et al., 2015) rely on syntactic parsers, which introduce error propagation issues that degrade performance. The authors' model avoids this by directly learning from word sequences, representing a substantial methodological advance for Japanese PAS analysis.

2. The paper makes a strong contribution to the challenging zero pronoun resolution task. As noted in the paper, zero pronouns (omitted arguments) are a major issue in Japanese PAS analysis because they lack direct syntactic dependencies. The authors demonstrate that their multi-sequence model significantly improves zero argument identification (47.12% F-measure vs. 44.09% for Ouchi et al., 2015), showing that modeling multi-predicate interactions is particularly effective for this problem.

3. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured. The authors provide comprehensive ablation studies on network depth, residual connections, and multi-predicate interactions. They also evaluate performance by case role, showing particularly strong improvements for the dative case (which appears infrequently but is important for semantic analysis), with their model achieving a 30-point improvement over previous work.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper contains several minor grammatical errors and typos that need correction. For example, "The F-measure remains 10-20% (Taira et al., 2008; Imamura et al., 2009; Sasano and Kurohashi, 2011)." should likely be "The F-measure remains 10-20 percentage points..." (as noted in the key points).

2. The use of boldface in Table 2 is unclear and needs clarification. The reviewers require explanation of what the boldface indicates in the results.

3. The explanation of how the PRED-context feature works could be improved, particularly in relation to the example in Figure 6 where the accusative marker is included with the verb. The authors should clarify how this feature is extracted and used in the model.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please clarify the use of boldface in Table 2.
2. Please explain how the PRED-context feature works with the example in Figure 6, where the accusative marker is included with the verb.

## Additional Comments
The paper is well-written overall, with clear descriptions of the model architecture and experiments. The authors provide detailed implementation information that will facilitate reproducibility. The main issues are the minor grammatical errors and unclear elements noted above, which should be addressed before publication. The authors have already demonstrated a clear understanding of the task and the challenges in Japanese PAS analysis, and their approach represents a meaningful contribution to the field.
