

Review: The paper introduces a neural approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that eliminates reliance on syntactic parsers by modeling multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. The core contribution is the Grid-RNN architecture, which processes all predicates and their argument candidates in a sentence simultaneously to capture semantic dependencies without error-prone syntactic pipelines. This addresses a critical challenge in Japanese PAS: identifying zero pronouns (omitted arguments), where prior joint models (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015) suffered from syntactic error propagation. The authors demonstrate that their multi-sequence Grid-RNN model achieves state-of-the-art results (81.42% F1) on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, significantly outperforming the SOTA (81.23%) and notably improving zero-argument identification by 3.0 points (47.12% vs. 44.09%). The single-sequence RNN model also exceeds baselines (81.15% F1), proving contextual learning from word sequences alone suffices for high accuracy.  

Strengths:  
1. **Solves a fundamental flaw in PAS pipelines**: The paper compellingly demonstrates that Grid-RNNs bypass syntactic error propagation—a major limitation of prior joint models—by inducing multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. The 3.0-point gain on zero arguments (the most challenging case) validates this approach’s practical impact.  
2. **Rigorous ablation and analysis**: The authors systematically isolate contributions: Grid-RNNs outperform single-sequence RNNs (81.42% vs. 81.15% F1), residual connections boost deeper networks, and the model excels on low-frequency dative cases (32.35% F1 vs. 24.43% for SOTA). The case-role analysis (Table 3) conclusively shows Grid-RNNs’ superiority for zero arguments (e.g., +2.5 F1 on NOM-zero).  
3. **Clear, reproducible implementation**: The model details (Fig. 3–6, Sec. 3–4) and experimental setup (Table 1, Sec. 6.1–6.2) are exceptionally thorough. Public code availability and standard dataset splits further enhance credibility.  

Weaknesses:  
1. **Minor English fluency issues**: Several grammatical errors and awkward phrasings (e.g., "the interaction information can be a clue" → "the interaction information serves as a clue"; "we focus on intra-sentential argument analysis" → "we focus exclusively on intra-sentential argument analysis") reduce readability. A proofread is needed.  
2. **Ambiguous Table 2 notation**: The boldface in Table 2 (e.g., "88.17†") lacks explanation—readers must infer it denotes statistical significance. Clarify this in the revised manuscript.  
3. **Fig. 6 could better illustrate PRED-context**: The figure shows feature extraction but doesn’t explicitly demonstrate how the PRED feature (e.g., "食べた" as predicate with context "パンを食べた") integrates into the Grid-RNN input. A concrete example (e.g., highlighting "パン" as context for "食べた") would aid understanding.  

Questions to Authors:  
1. In Table 2, what statistical test (e.g., bootstrap resampling) confirms the significance level (p < 0.05) for bolded results?  
2. For Fig. 6, could you add a concrete example showing how the PRED feature (e.g., the window "パンを食べた") is mapped to the Grid-RNN input sequence?  
3. The paper states the model uses "no external resources," yet Table 3 references "external resources" for prior work. Clarify whether NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 was used *without* external resources in all experiments.  

The paper makes a strong contribution by proving that neural architectures can replace syntactic pipelines for Japanese PAS, especially for the critical zero-argument task. The Grid-RNN innovation is novel, well-validated, and directly addresses a long-standing limitation in the field. Minor language and presentation fixes are required, but the technical substance is robust and impactful. Accept.
