

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper introduces neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that aim to address the limitation of current state-of-the-art approaches which heavily rely on syntactic information from parsers. The authors propose extending a single-sequence model (based on Zhou & Xu, 2015) to a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The models are evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, demonstrating state-of-the-art results without using syntactic information.

## Main Contributions
1. A neural model for Japanese PAS analysis that achieves state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic information from parsers.
2. An extension from single-sequence to multi-sequence modeling using Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions, with particular benefits for zero argument identification.
3. Comprehensive evaluation showing that neural models can effectively learn contextual information from word sequences alone, without the need for manually engineered syntactic features.

## Strengths
1. The paper successfully addresses a significant limitation in current Japanese PAS analysis: the reliance on syntactic information from parsers, which suffers from error propagation. By demonstrating state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information, the authors present a more robust and potentially more scalable approach to PAS analysis.

2. The evaluation is thorough and well-structured, including detailed comparisons with multiple baselines, analysis of network depth effects, and per-case-role performance breakdowns. The results consistently show improvements over previous approaches, particularly for zero arguments (which are challenging due to their lack of direct syntactic dependencies).

3. The paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of zero argument identification, which is one of the most problematic issues in Japanese PAS analysis. The results show that their multi-sequence model improves zero argument identification by approximately 1.0 point in F-measure over the single-sequence model, and by 3.0 points over the previous state-of-the-art (Ouchi et al., 2015).

## Weaknesses
1. The main contribution appears to be an extension from single- to multi-sequential modeling rather than a fundamentally novel method. The authors build directly on Zhou & Xu (2015), which is an English SRL model, and adapt it to Japanese. The novelty is limited to the extension to multi-predicate interactions, and the Grid-RNN component is not substantially different from previous work.

2. The paper lacks comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. (2015) with neural-network modeling. Shibata et al. is a recent and highly relevant work in the same space, and its omission makes the evaluation incomplete and potentially misleading about the true state-of-the-art.

3. The impact of modeling predicate interactions is smaller than reported in Ouchi et al. (2015). While Ouchi et al. reported significant gains from joint modeling, this paper shows only marginal improvements (0.27 F1 points) from the single-sequence to multi-sequence model. The authors do not adequately address why their results differ from Ouchi et al. or whether the difference is due to corpus differences, experimental setup, or model limitations.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your multi-sequence model compare to Shibata et al. (2016), which also uses neural networks for joint modeling of predicates in Japanese PAS? Given that Shibata et al. is a recent and relevant work, its omission from the comparison seems significant.

2. The paper claims that "the neural model using Grid-RNNs achieves the best result," but the improvement over the single-sequence model is marginal (0.27 F1 points). Is this marginal improvement sufficient to justify the more complex Grid-RNN architecture, particularly given the computational cost?

3. Why does your model show a smaller improvement from multi-predicate interactions compared to Ouchi et al. (2015)? Could this be due to differences in the corpus (NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 vs. 1.4β), experimental setup, or the specific way multi-predicate interactions are modeled?
