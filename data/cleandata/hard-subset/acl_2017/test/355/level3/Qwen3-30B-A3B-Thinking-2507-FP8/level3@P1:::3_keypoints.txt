- Paper tackles Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis with neural sequence models that capture multi-predicate interactions without syntactic input.  
- Proposes two models: a single-sequence RNN and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN.  
- Main contributions: automatic induction of multi-predicate-sensitive features, state-of-the-art on NAIST Text Corpus v1.5, and large gains on zero-argument identification.  
- Multi-sequence model achieves 81.42% F (vs. prior SOTA 81.15%), and 47.12% F on zero arguments (+3.0 over best baseline).  
- Shows high PAS performance without syntactic parsers, avoiding parser error propagation that hampered prior joint approaches.  
- Clear technical explanation of Grid-RNNs, helpful figures, and sound experimental design with significance testing.  
- Fails to sufficiently explain task complexity and how Japanese case-role ambiguity (e.g., NOM vs ACC) differs from English SRL.  
- No analysis of what fraction of zero pronouns are intra-sentential vs inter-sentential, leaving task difficulty unclear.  
- Model does not enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per case role) and paper lacks analysis of whether/how such constraints are learned.  
- Paper reports modest NOM improvement for multi-sequence but lacks systematic analysis of when and why multi-sequence outperforms single-sequence.  
- Unclear how inter-sentential zero pronouns are represented/handled in the dataset and model outputs.  
- Questions for authors: proportion of intra-sentential zero pronouns; handling/representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns; case analyses where multi-sequence wins and distinguishing linguistic features; frequency and handling of constraint violations (multiple arguments per case).  
- Minor presentation issues: inconsistent "Grid-RNNs"/"Grid-RNN" naming, hyphenation of "multi-sequence," Figure 6 needs a caption explaining inter-sequence connections.  
- Clarify whether the output "null" label covers both non-arguments and inter-sentential zero pronouns.  
- Overall verdict: solid, valuable contribution demonstrating effective neural PAS without syntax; recommend acceptance pending requested clarifications and additional analyses.
