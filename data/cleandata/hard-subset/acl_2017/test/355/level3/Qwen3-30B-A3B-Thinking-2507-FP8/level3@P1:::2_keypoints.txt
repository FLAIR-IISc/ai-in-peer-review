- Proposes neural models for Japanese PAS, introducing a single-sequence model and a Grid-RNN multi-sequence model to handle zero pronoun resolution.  
- Key contribution: achieves state-of-the-art PAS performance without syntactic parser input, avoiding error propagation.  
- Grid-RNN extension models multi-predicate interactions and improves zero-argument identification.  
- Demonstrates that word sequence information alone suffices for high-performance PAS without hand-engineered features.  
- Empirical results on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5: multi-sequence F1 81.42 vs single 81.15 (≈+0.27), zero-argument 47.12% vs 46.10%, improvements significant (p<0.05).  
- Outperforms prior state-of-the-art (Ouchi et al., 2015) on zero arguments by 2.17 points (47.12% vs 44.09%).  
- Strong experimental setup with appropriate baselines, ablations, deeper networks with residuals, and case-role analysis showing consistent gains (notably dative).  
- Architecture is well-motivated and clearly presented (Figure 6 aids understanding).  
- Main weakness: contribution is incremental—extension of single- to multi-sequence modeling building on Zhou & Xu (2015), not a fundamentally new method.  
- Impact of modeling predicate interactions is smaller than in prior joint methods (only ~3.0-point improvement vs 11.3 points reported by Ouchi et al., 2015).  
- Missing comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), a closely related neural baseline without syntactic input.  
- Recommendations: clarify differences and superiority relative to Shibata et al. (2016) and explain why Grid-RNN yields only marginal gains over the single-sequence model.  
- Questions for authors: explain the smaller multi-predicate impact vs Ouchi et al. (2015); justify omission of Shibata et al. (2016); clarify how this Grid-RNN application differs from prior Grid-RNN uses (e.g., Graves & Schmidhuber, 2009).
