

**Reviewer Summary**

This paper introduces a neural modeling approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information. The main contributions are: (1) a novel neural architecture that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequences; (2) state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5; and (3) demonstration that joint modeling of predicates improves zero-pronoun resolution without error propagation from parsers.

**Strengths**  
The work is well-executed with solid empirical results, achieving SOTA performance without syntactic dependencies. The model's ability to handle zero-pronoun ambiguity—particularly for NOM arguments (improving by 2.5 points)—is significant for Japanese PAS. The presentation is accessible to non-Japanese specialists, with clear figures illustrating the model architecture. The comparison between single-sequence and multi-sequence models effectively demonstrates the value of capturing multi-predicate interactions.

**Weaknesses**  
The paper underemphasizes task complexity, particularly the ambiguity between NOM/ACC cases and how zero-pronoun resolution differs from standard SRL. Crucially, the authors omit statistics on the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns, which would clarify task difficulty. The model also fails to enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per case type per predicate), and the authors do not analyze how well these constraints are learned. While Multi-Seq improves NOM (Table 3), the paper lacks discussion of *why* this improvement occurs and in which contexts.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Provide statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable.  
2. Clarify how inter-sentential zero pronouns are represented (no marking vs. explicit empty argument) and discuss implications.  
3. Analyze whether the model enforces linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per case type) and how well it learns them.  
4. Compare outputs of Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models to identify systematic differences in error patterns.  
5. Discuss why Multi-Seq primarily improves NOM (Table 3) and the contexts where this occurs.  

*Minor note: Several typographical errors exist (e.g., "F-measure" vs. "F-measure," inconsistent casing in "Zero" vs. "zero").*
