

Summary: This paper addresses Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis by introducing neural sequence models that capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information. The authors propose two models: a single-sequence model using RNNs and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs. The main contributions are: (1) a neural approach that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequences; (2) state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without syntactic information; (3) significant improvements for zero argument identification, which is particularly challenging in Japanese PAS.

Strengths: The work makes a strong contribution by demonstrating that high-performance PAS analysis can be achieved without syntactic parsers, addressing a key limitation of previous joint modeling approaches that suffered from error propagation. The multi-sequence model achieves 81.42% F-measure, significantly outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (81.15% for single-sequence and 81.42% for multi-sequence). The most impressive results are for zero arguments (47.12% F-measure), where the multi-sequence model improves by 3.0 points over the best baseline (44.09%), demonstrating the effectiveness of modeling multi-predicate interactions for the most challenging aspect of Japanese PAS. The paper provides clear technical explanations of the Grid-RNN architecture with helpful figures, and the experimental design is sound with proper statistical significance testing.

Weaknesses: The paper fails to sufficiently explain the task complexity, particularly the ambiguity between case roles (e.g., NOM vs ACC) and how this differs from English semantic role labeling, which would help contextualize the results. There's no analysis of what proportion of zero pronouns in the dataset are intra-sententially resolvable, which is critical for understanding the task difficulty. The model appears not to enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per case role per predicate), and the paper lacks analysis of how well the model learns these constraints. The paper shows multi-sequence model improves NOM by 0.63 points (Table 3), but doesn't provide systematic analysis of when the model outperforms the single-sequence model or what linguistic features distinguish these cases. Additionally, the paper doesn't clarify how inter-sentential zero pronouns (which the authors acknowledge are not addressed) are handled in the dataset.

Questions to Authors: 1) What proportion of zero pronouns in the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 are intra-sententially resolvable, and how does this affect the practical difficulty of the task? 2) How does the model handle inter-sentential zero pronouns (e.g., are they marked as "null" or explicitly represented as empty arguments)? 3) Can you provide a case analysis showing specific examples where the multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence model, and what linguistic features distinguish these cases? 4) How does the model handle violations of linguistic constraints (e.g., multiple arguments for the same case role), and what is the frequency of such violations in the predictions?

Additional Comments: The paper is well-written overall, but contains minor language issues. For instance, "Grid-RNNs" is sometimes written as "Grid-RNN" in the text, and the term "multi-sequence" should be consistently hyphenated. The presentation of the Grid-RNN architecture in Figure 6 could be improved by adding a brief caption explaining the inter-sequence connections. The authors should clarify whether the "null" label in the output layer includes both non-arguments and inter-sentential zero pronouns.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to Japanese PAS analysis that demonstrates the effectiveness of neural approaches without syntactic information. The work addresses a meaningful limitation of previous approaches and achieves strong results, particularly for zero arguments. With the suggested clarifications and additional analysis, this paper would make a valuable contribution to ACL.
