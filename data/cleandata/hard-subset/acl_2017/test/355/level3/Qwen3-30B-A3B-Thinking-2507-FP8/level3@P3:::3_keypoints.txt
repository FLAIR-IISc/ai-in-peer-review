- Paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese PAS focusing on zero pronouns, including a Single-Seq and a Grid-RNN Multi-Seq model that jointly models predicate interactions without syntactic parsers.
- Contribution: Novel Grid-RNN architecture that models multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS without syntactic parsing.
- Contribution: State-of-the-art results on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, notably improving zero pronoun resolution (47.12% F1 vs. 44.09% prior).
- Contribution: Demonstrates neural models can learn contextual and multi-predicate features from raw word sequences, reducing feature engineering.
- Strength: Addresses error propagation from syntactic parsers with an elegant neural solution capturing multi-predicate interactions.
- Strength: Strong empirical results — Multi-Seq achieves 81.42% F1 and notable improvement on zero pronouns.
- Strength: Well-written and accessible with clear figures and useful comparison to English SRL.
- Weakness: Underemphasizes task complexity and ambiguity between NOM and ACC cases; needs clearer discussion of why Japanese PAS is harder than English SRL.
- Weakness: Lacks statistics on proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable, which affects task difficulty interpretation.
- Weakness: Model does not enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per type per predicate) and lacks analysis of whether it learns these constraints.
- Weakness: No comparative output analysis between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq to show systematic differences in outputs.
- Weakness: Notes Multi-Seq mainly improves NOM but doesn’t analyze why or in which contexts this occurs.
- Question: Provide statistics on what proportion of zero pronouns are intra-sententially resolvable.
- Question: How does the model handle inter-sentential zero pronouns — are they marked or represented as explicit empty arguments?
- Question: Provide analysis of whether the model learns linguistic constraints like one argument per type per predicate and effects on performance if not.
- Question: Provide comparative output analysis between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq to show systematic differences and contexts of improvement.
- Question: Explain contexts where Multi-Seq improves NOM, ACC, and DAT cases and why NOM sees the largest gain.
- Additional: Fix Table 1 "Zero" labeling inconsistency.
- Additional: Correct Figure 2 caption to "(ii) multi-sequence" for consistency.
- Additional: Edit Section 3.3 wording to remove redundancy ("case role").
- Additional: In Table 3, change "Imamura+ 09" to "Imamura et al. (2009)" for consistency.
