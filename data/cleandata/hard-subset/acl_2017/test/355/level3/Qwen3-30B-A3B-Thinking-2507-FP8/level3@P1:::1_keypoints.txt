- Introduces Grid-RNN, a neural model for Japanese PAS that models multi-predicate interactions from word sequences without relying on syntactic parsers.  
- Targets the zero-pronoun (omitted argument) problem and avoids syntactic error propagation of prior joint models.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art 81.42% F1 on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, improving zero-argument identification by 3.0 points (47.12% vs. 44.09%).  
- Single-sequence RNN baseline also strong (81.15% F1), indicating contextual word-sequence learning suffices for high accuracy.  
- Strength: Effectively fixes a fundamental PAS pipeline flaw by inducing predicate interactions directly, validated by gains on zero arguments.  
- Strength: Rigorous ablation and analysis show Grid-RNN > single-sequence RNN, benefits from residual connections, and improved performance on low-frequency dative cases.  
- Strength: Clear, reproducible implementation with detailed model description, standard splits, and public code.  
- Weakness: Minor English fluency and grammatical issues that require proofreading.  
- Weakness: Ambiguous notation in Table 2 (boldface/†) — unclear indication of statistical significance.  
- Weakness: Fig. 6 does not clearly show how the PRED-context feature is integrated; needs a concrete example.  
- Question: Which statistical test (e.g., bootstrap resampling) was used to confirm p < 0.05 for bolded results in Table 2?  
- Question: Can you provide a concrete example (e.g., "パンを食べた") showing how the PRED feature is mapped into the Grid-RNN input in Fig. 6?  
- Question: Clarify the claim of using "no external resources" — was the NAIST Text Corpus used without external resources in all experiments?  
- Overall conclusion: Novel, well-validated contribution demonstrating neural architectures can replace syntactic pipelines for Japanese PAS, especially for zero-argument analysis; accept with minor presentation fixes.
