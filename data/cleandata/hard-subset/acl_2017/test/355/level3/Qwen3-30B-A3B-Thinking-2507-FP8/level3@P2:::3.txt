

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that automatically induce features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information, eliminating reliance on syntactic parsers. The main contributions are:

1. A neural model using Grid-RNNs that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions without syntactic information, addressing the error propagation problem in previous joint modeling approaches.
2. State-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, with significant improvements for zero argument identification (up to 3.0 points F-measure over the previous best model).
3. Clear demonstration that multi-predicate interactions are particularly beneficial for zero argument resolution, a challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis.

## Strengths

1. The paper successfully addresses a critical limitation in existing Japanese PAS analysis approaches. Previous joint modeling techniques heavily rely on syntactic parsers, suffering from error propagation. The authors' neural approach that operates directly on word sequences without syntactic dependencies is both novel and practically valuable, as evidenced by their state-of-the-art results.

2. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed. The authors provide comprehensive comparisons across multiple metrics (Dep, Zero, and overall F-measure), with proper statistical significance testing. The results clearly demonstrate that their neural models, particularly the multi-sequence Grid-RNN model, significantly outperform previous approaches, especially for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 44.09% for the best prior work).

3. The paper makes a strong case for the importance of multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis, particularly for zero arguments. The results consistently show that incorporating interactions between predicates leads to meaningful improvements, which aligns with linguistic intuition about semantic relationships between predicates in a sentence.

4. The presentation is generally accessible to non-Japanese specialists, with helpful figures explaining the neural architectures (particularly Figure 2). The paper clearly distinguishes between Dep and Zero arguments and explains the challenges of zero pronoun resolution.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper does not sufficiently discuss how the model respects linguistic constraints of the task, such as the fact that an argument can only fill one slot per predicate. Without enforcing these constraints, the model might produce inconsistent outputs that would be problematic for downstream applications. The authors should analyze how well the model learns these constraints.

2. The paper lacks statistics on what proportion of zero pronouns are intrasententially resolvable in the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5. This would help clarify the actual difficulty of the task and whether the improvements are addressing a meaningful subset of the problem.

3. The paper does not adequately address how inter-sentential zero pronouns (Inter-Zero) are handled. The authors state they focus on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero), but it's unclear how the model deals with Inter-Zero cases (which are not included in their evaluation). This is a significant gap in understanding the model's capabilities and limitations.

4. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of where the multi-sequence model improves over the single-sequence model. The main improvement appears to be in the nominative case (NOM) as shown in Table 3, but the authors don't discuss why this case shows the most improvement or what specific contexts lead to better predictions.

5. The paper could better contextualize its contribution relative to English SRL work. While the authors mention similarities to Zhou and Xu (2015), they don't clearly explain how their work differs in terms of the dependency-based nature of Japanese PAS versus the constituent-based English SRL, which is a key distinction.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide statistics on what proportion of zero pronouns in the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 are intrasententially resolvable? This would help clarify the actual difficulty of the task.

2. How are inter-sentential zero pronouns represented in the dataset (no marking vs. explicit empty argument), and how does this impact the model's ability to handle them?

3. The paper doesn't enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per type per predicate). How well does the model learn these constraints, and what implications does this have for the reliability of the outputs?

4. Could you provide a comparative analysis of outputs between the single-sequence and multi-sequence models, showing where the improvements occur and in which specific contexts?

5. Why does the multi-sequence model mainly improve performance for the nominative case (NOM) as shown in Table 3, and what does this suggest about the nature of the improvements?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written overall, though it contains some minor language issues (e.g., "the analysis for such Zero arguments is much more difficult" should be "Zero arguments" as a noun phrase). The presentation quality is good, but the authors should address the points above to strengthen the paper's contribution and clarity.
