

This paper introduces a neural modeling approach for Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-type RNNs, which achieves state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic information. The main contributions are: (1) a neural model that outperforms existing systems by avoiding error propagation from syntactic parsers, (2) a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions for zero pronoun identification, and (3) a detailed demonstration of how RNN-based approaches can address the specific challenges of pro-drop languages like Japanese.

Strengths: The most significant contribution is the model's ability to achieve superior performance without syntactic information, which represents a major advancement in Japanese PAS analysis. The paper excels in addressing the particularly challenging zero pronoun problem through the multi-sequence model, which achieves a 3-point improvement over the state-of-the-art for zero arguments. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured, with clear comparisons across different model configurations and case roles.

Weaknesses: The paper contains several minor typos and English fluency issues that require correction. The boldface in Table 2 is not explained, creating confusion about its significance. The PRED-context point could be better illustrated using the Fig. 6 example as suggested. While the model's technical details are well-described, the paper could provide more explicit explanation of how Grid-RNNs specifically capture multi-predicate interactions.

Questions to Authors: Please clarify the purpose of boldface in Table 2. Could you elaborate on how the Grid-RNN architecture specifically models multi-predicate interactions beyond what's described in Section 4.2?

The paper is well-written overall and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Japanese PAS analysis. The authors have satisfactorily addressed all reviewer concerns in their response. The minor issues noted do not detract from the paper's significant technical contribution.
