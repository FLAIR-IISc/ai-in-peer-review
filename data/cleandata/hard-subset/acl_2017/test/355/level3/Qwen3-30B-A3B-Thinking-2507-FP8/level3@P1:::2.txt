

This paper proposes neural models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on identifying arguments for predicates, particularly addressing the challenging problem of zero pronoun resolution (omitted arguments). The authors introduce two neural sequence models: a single-sequence model that processes each predicate independently and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The key contributions are: (1) demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance without relying on syntactic information from parsers, overcoming error propagation issues; (2) extending the single-sequence model with Grid-RNNs to effectively model multi-predicate interactions, particularly improving zero argument identification; and (3) showing that word sequence information alone is sufficient for high-performance PAS analysis without manually engineered features or syntactic parsing.

The paper makes a strong contribution by showing that neural models can achieve superior results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 benchmark without syntactic information, which is a significant advantage over previous approaches. The multi-sequence model with Grid-RNNs outperforms the single-sequence model by approximately 0.27 points in overall F-measure (81.42% vs. 81.15%), with more substantial improvements for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 46.10%). These results are statistically significant (p<0.05), demonstrating that the Grid-RNN architecture effectively captures multi-predicate interactions. The authors also show that the neural models significantly outperform previous state-of-the-art approaches by 2.17 points for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 44.09% in Ouchi et al., 2015), which is particularly important given that zero argument identification is a major challenge in Japanese PAS analysis.

The experimental setup is well-designed with appropriate baselines (Imamura et al., 2009 and Ouchi et al., 2015) and thorough ablation studies. The paper effectively demonstrates that deeper networks with residual connections improve performance, and the analysis of different case roles (NOM, ACC, DAT) shows consistent improvements across the board, with particularly strong gains for the dative case (32.35% vs. 24.43% in Ouchi et al., 2015). The architecture is well-motivated and clearly explained, with Figure 6 providing an excellent overview of the multi-sequence model.

The main weaknesses of the paper are: (1) the primary contribution appears to be an extension from single- to multi-sequential modeling rather than a fundamentally novel method, as the approach builds heavily on Zhou & Xu (2015) for English SRL; (2) the impact of modeling predicate interactions is smaller than reported in Ouchi et al. (2015), where the joint approach improved zero argument F-measure by 11.3 points over the pointwise approach, while the current work shows only a 3.0-point improvement over the previous state-of-the-art; and (3) the paper omits comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. with neural networks and represents a more directly relevant baseline.

The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of how this work differs from Shibata et al. (2016) and why their approach is superior. The claim that "our model exceeds the accuracy of the state-of-the-art Japanese PAS analyzer without syntactic information" is accurate, but the comparison should explicitly address how this differs from Shibata et al. (2016), which also used neural networks without syntactic information.

The authors should also clarify why the Grid-RNN approach yields only marginal improvements over the single-sequence model (0.27 points) compared to the larger gains reported in Ouchi et al. (2015) for their joint approach. This is particularly important as the paper's main motivation is the linguistic intuition that multi-predicate interactions are beneficial for PAS analysis.

Questions to authors: 
1. How do you explain the smaller impact of multi-predicate interactions (0.27 point improvement) compared to Ouchi et al. (2015), who reported much larger gains (11.3 points) for their joint approach?
2. Why was Shibata et al. (2016) not included in the comparison, given that they also used neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic information?
3. The paper claims to be the first to use Grid-RNNs for Japanese PAS analysis, but Grid-RNNs were introduced in Graves and Schmidhuber (2009). How does your application differ from prior uses of Grid-RNNs in NLP tasks?
