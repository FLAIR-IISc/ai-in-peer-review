Summary and contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). The authors adapt a strong sequence labeling architecture (à la Zhou & Xu, 2015) to Japanese PAS and further propose a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly predicts arguments for all predicates in a sentence, thereby modeling multi-predicate interactions without relying on predicted syntax. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 (standard splits), the models outperform prior state of the art, with the Grid-RNN offering modest yet significant gains over the single-sequence baseline, especially for zero arguments.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first) are:
1) An effective syntax-free PAS model that sets a new state of the art on NAIST 1.5 using only word sequence information and a predicate marker, with substantial gains on zero arguments.
2) A practical adaptation of multi-dimensional/Grid RNNs to jointly encode multiple predicate-specific sequences, yielding small but statistically significant improvements and demonstrating that inter-predicate information helps.
3) Empirical analysis of depth (with residual connections) showing deeper stacks improve performance, supporting the design and training choices.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains without syntax and reduced error propagation: On NAIST 1.5, the single-sequence model achieves 81.15 F1 overall and 46.10 Zero F1; the multi-sequence model reaches 81.42 overall and 47.12 Zero F1, improving over Ouchi et al. (2015) (79.23 overall; 44.09 Zero) despite not using syntactic parses. This is impactful for robustness in real-world settings where parsing errors can hurt PAS.
- Multi-predicate modeling helps zero anaphora: The Grid-RNN yields significant improvements for Zero arguments (+1.0 F1 over single-seq; +3.0 over Ouchi+15). This aligns with the intuition that interactions among predicates provide useful signals for omitted arguments, a core challenge in Japanese PAS.
- Careful, well-structured experimental evaluation with ablations on depth/residual connections: The paper reports results across depths (2–8 layers) and shows residual connections are critical for training deeper models, improving F1 by about 1 point at 8 layers. This increases confidence in the model design.
- Competitive per-role performance, especially for rare DAT: The neural models markedly improve DAT (intra-sentential) over older baselines (e.g., vs Imamura+09), suggesting the approach learns robust representations even for infrequent roles, and highlights the advantage of sequence encoders over feature engineering.
- Clear writing and motivation; code release: The paper is easy to follow, the figures are helpful, and public code (as stated) boosts reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond an application-oriented adaptation: The single-sequence model closely follows prior sequence models (e.g., Zhou & Xu, 2015), and the Grid-RNN extension is a straightforward application of multi-dimensional RNNs to stacked predicate-specific sequences. The methodological contribution is incremental.
- Modest gains from multi-predicate interactions compared to prior joint models: While Ouchi et al. (2015) reported sizable gains from modeling inter-predicate interactions in a joint framework, the Grid-RNN’s incremental improvement over the single-sequence baseline is small (e.g., +0.27 overall F1). The paper would benefit from deeper analysis explaining when and why the grid helps.
- Missing or incomplete comparison with Shibata et al. (2016): Shibata also integrates neural modeling into the joint PAS framework on NAIST and is a closely related baseline. The paper argues settings differ, but the lack of a quantitative comparison (or a careful discussion aligning setups) weakens the empirical positioning.
- Decoding constraints and consistency not discussed: The per-word softmax predicts NOM/ACC/DAT/null independently across positions for a given predicate. PAS typically imposes “at most one” per case per predicate. Without explicit global constraints, the model could produce multiple NOMs or none; the paper does not describe how consistency is enforced at test time or how violations are handled in evaluation.
- Limited error analysis and diagnostic insights: Beyond aggregate F1s and a depth ablation, the paper provides little analysis of error types, predicate-density effects, or concrete examples where multi-predicate interactions help/hurt. Notably, per-role Zero results show trade-offs (e.g., DAT Zero drops from 9.83 to 7.52 from single- to multi-seq), which merit investigation.

Questions to authors
- How do you enforce or handle uniqueness constraints per predicate (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT)? Do you use any global decoding or post-processing, or is it pure per-token softmax? If multiple tokens are labeled NOM, how is evaluation computed?
- Can you provide a quantitative comparison or at least a careful normalization of settings with Shibata et al. (2016)? If exact replication is infeasible, can you re-run your model under their setup or vice versa to situate results?
- What is the computational overhead of the Grid-RNN versus the single-sequence model as a function of the number of predicates per sentence (training and inference time; memory)? Any batching strategies?
- Can you include an ablation on the input feature groups (ARG vs PRED vs MARK) to show their contributions, especially to Zero performance?
- The DAT per-role numbers in Table 3 suggest some degradation with the Grid-RNN compared to single-seq (e.g., DAT Zero 9.83 → 7.52). What causes this trade-off? Are there particular sentence configurations where inter-predicate signals mislead the model?
- Have you tried incorporating syntactic cues (e.g., dependency distance) or pretrained embeddings to see if the Grid-RNN and syntax are complementary?

Additional comments
- Please clarify whether the evaluation counts a prediction as correct if the correct argument is predicted in the correct case regardless of duplicates, and how conflicts are resolved.
- Some references and years appear incomplete or inconsistent; please clean up the bibliography.
- A qualitative section with examples where the grid helps resolve zero pronouns would strengthen the paper and help readers generalize the insight.
