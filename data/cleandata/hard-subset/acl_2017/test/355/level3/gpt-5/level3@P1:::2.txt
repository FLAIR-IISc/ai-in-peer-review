Summary
This paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis without relying on predicted syntax, addressing error-propagation issues in pipeline approaches. The authors first adapt a single-sequence BiRNN (BiGRU + softmax) model akin to Zhou & Xu (2015) to Japanese dependency-style PAS, then extend it to a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, the approach outperforms prior state-of-the-art systems that use syntactic features, and the Grid-RNN yields modest but statistically significant gains over the single-sequence baseline, especially for zero arguments.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by importance)
1) Strong empirical result for Japanese PAS without syntactic features: A simple, well-engineered BiRNN sequence model achieves new state-of-the-art on NAIST 1.5, improving overall F from 79.23 (Ouchi et al., 2015) to 81.15, and zero-argument F from 44.09 to 46.10.

2) Joint modeling of multi-predicate interactions via a Grid-RNN: Extending the single-sequence model to a grid over predicates yields further significant gains (overall F 81.42, zero-argument F 47.12), demonstrating that end-to-end neural modeling can capture cross-predicate signals without explicit syntax.

3) Architectural and training insights: Residual connections and increased depth materially help; the paper documents these choices and shows their impact.

Strengths
- Clear empirical superiority without syntax: The models surpass strong syntax-heavy baselines on NAIST 1.5. Compared to Ouchi et al. (2015), overall F improves from 79.23 to 81.15 (single) and 81.42 (grid). Zero arguments improve from 44.09 to 46.10 and 47.12, respectively. This is practically meaningful because zero anaphora is a key challenge for Japanese PAS.

- Multi-predicate interactions help where it matters: The Grid-RNN’s main benefit is on zero arguments (+1.0 F over the single-sequence model), consistent with the intuition that omitted arguments benefit from global predicate context. The improvement over the single model is statistically significant.

- Sensible, well-motivated adaptation of prior neural SRL: The paper thoughtfully adapts Zhou & Xu’s single-predicate sequence tagging framework to dependency-based PAS (softmax instead of CRF), then extends it to a grid across predicates. The design choices (predicate-centric features, shared BiGRUs, residual connections) are reasonable and well-justified.

- Thorough experimental setup on a standard benchmark: The work follows standard NAIST 1.5 splits, uses bootstrap significance testing, and reports detailed breakdowns (Dep vs Zero; case roles). The observation that DAT benefits substantially underscores the model’s robustness to infrequent roles.

- Clarity and structure: The paper is readable and well-organized, with clear diagrams and a stepwise exposition from single- to multi-sequence modeling. Implementation details (optimizer, hyperparameters, depth) are given, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core components (BiGRU sequence tagging, residual connections) are standard; the Grid-RNN application to multi-predicate modeling is a straightforward extension rather than a fundamentally new modeling idea. The paper’s main novelty is empirical (syntax-free SOTA) and the specific grid formulation.

- Modest gains from multi-predicate interactions: While significant, the Grid-RNN’s gains over the single model are small in aggregate (+0.27 overall F; +1.02 for Zero; +0.07 for Dep). This is notably less impactful than interaction modeling reported by Ouchi et al. (2015), suggesting that most of the improvement comes from stronger single-sequence context modeling rather than the grid architecture.

- Missing direct comparison to recent neural PAS/SRL baselines: Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016) also apply neural models to closely related tasks. Even if datasets/settings differ, a careful discussion or controlled reimplementation (or showing non-comparability clearly) would strengthen claims about the state of the art.

- Limited analysis of what the grid captures: The paper does not provide qualitative or quantitative analysis diagnosing which types of cross-predicate cues the Grid-RNN exploits (e.g., predicate distance, argument sharing patterns, coordination). An error analysis or probing study would clarify when multi-predicate modeling is most beneficial.

- Scope restricted to intra-sentential arguments: While consistent with prior work, excluding inter-sentential (Inter-Zero) limits the broader impact. It would be helpful to discuss how the architecture could be extended to cross-sentence contexts (e.g., document-level grids) and the computational implications.

Questions to Authors
- Can you clarify the (non-)comparability to Shibata et al. (2016)? If datasets/splits differ, can you provide any bridging experiments or a detailed argument why results are not directly comparable?

- How sensitive are the results to the predicate window size C, embedding dimensions, and GRU hidden size? A brief sensitivity analysis could help practitioners.

- What is the computational overhead of the Grid-RNN versus the single-sequence model (training time, memory) as a function of number of predicates and sentence length?

- Did you try adding simple syntax-aware signals (e.g., gold or predicted dependency heads/distances) to the single-sequence model? This could contextualize the benefits of syntax-free modeling versus lightweight syntactic cues.

- Can you provide qualitative examples where the Grid-RNN corrects an error made by the single-sequence model, illustrating the nature of cross-predicate information being captured?

- Do pretrained embeddings (e.g., from large unlabeled Japanese corpora) further improve zero-argument performance? If tried, please report; if not, please discuss expected impact.

Additional comments
- Releasing code and trained models (the URL in the draft seems placeholder) would materially aid reproducibility.
- Minor: consider reporting performance by sentence length and number of predicates to substantiate claims about long-distance/context modeling.
