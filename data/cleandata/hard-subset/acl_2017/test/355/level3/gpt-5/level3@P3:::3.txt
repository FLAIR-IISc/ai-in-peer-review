Summary
The paper proposes a neural approach to Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that captures interactions among multiple predicates within a sentence using Grid-RNNs (“Multi-Seq”), and compares it to a strong single-predicate baseline based on stacked BiRNNs (“Single-Seq”). Without using syntactic parses or selectional preferences, both models achieve new state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 for intra-sentential PAS; the Grid-RNN yields a small but statistically significant gain, particularly for zero-NOM arguments.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A grid-structured RNN architecture that jointly encodes multiple predicate-specific sequences to model inter-predicate interactions, enabling state-of-the-art Japanese PAS without syntax.
2) A strong, well-tuned single-sequence BiRNN baseline that already surpasses prior joint models using syntax, clarifying how much can be gained from sentence-level contextualization alone.
3) An empirical analysis (including depth and residual connections) demonstrating modest but significant gains from multi-predicate modeling, with the largest improvements on zero-NOM.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results without syntax: On NAIST 1.5, both Single-Seq and Multi-Seq outperform prior pointwise and joint baselines; the Multi-Seq model yields a significant overall F1 improvement (81.42 vs 81.15), and improves Zero by ~1 F1 compared to Single-Seq and by ~3 F1 compared to Ouchi et al. (2015), showing the value of sentence-level context and predicate interaction modeling.
- Careful comparison and solid engineering: Clear model definitions, consistent splits, and hyperparameter sweeps (depth, residual connections). The depth/residual study is useful to the community.
- Accessible presentation: The task and models are explained clearly, with helpful figures. The paper is approachable even for readers not specialized in Japanese PAS.
- Focus on zero pronouns: The emphasis on Zero (intra-sentential) evaluation targets the most challenging and under-resourced part of Japanese PAS, with meaningful improvements on zero-NOM.

Weaknesses
- Limited characterization of task difficulty: The paper does not quantify how often zero pronouns are intra-sententially resolvable in NAIST 1.5, nor how this breaks down by case. Without these statistics, it is hard to contextualize absolute F1 levels and compare difficulty against SRL settings.
- Output well-formedness not analyzed: The model uses token-level softmax per predicate with no global structural constraints (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate). The paper does not report rates of constraint violations (e.g., multiple NOMs for a predicate) or whether the model implicitly learns these constraints, especially in Multi-Seq vs Single-Seq.
- Mixed per-role effects of Multi-Seq: Table 3 suggests Multi-Seq mainly improves NOM (Dep +0.43, Zero +1.14) but decreases ACC/DAT for Zero (ACC −2.72, DAT −2.31). The paper does not discuss why multi-predicate interactions help NOM but hurt other roles, or in which contexts the gains/losses occur.
- Ambiguity and error analysis: There is little discussion of common confusions (e.g., NOM vs ACC when arguments are omitted, long-distance dependencies, control/coordination). A confusion analysis would clarify where the Grid-RNN helps.
- Scope/annotation clarity for Inter-Zero: The paper focuses on intra-sentential arguments, but it is unclear how inter-sentential zero arguments are represented in the data and labels (e.g., null vs explicit empty arguments), and what implications this has for negative examples and evaluation.

Questions to Authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- Zero-pronoun statistics: What proportion of gold zero pronouns in NAIST 1.5 are intra-sententially resolvable overall and per case (NOM/ACC/DAT)? What is the average predicate–argument distance for Zero vs Dep? How many predicates per sentence on average?
- Representation of Inter-Zero: For predicate–case pairs whose gold argument is inter-sentential, how are these annotated in NAIST 1.5 and handled in your training/evaluation? Are they excluded, or treated as null within the sentence? Please discuss implications for class balance and false negatives.
- Well-formedness: Do you post-process to enforce one argument per case per predicate? If not, how often do you predict multiple NOM/ACC/DAT for the same predicate in dev/test, and does Multi-Seq reduce such violations relative to Single-Seq?
- Where Multi-Seq helps/hurts: Provide a comparative output analysis showing in which contexts Multi-Seq changes Single-Seq predictions (e.g., number of predicates in the sentence, argument distance, control/raising, coordination). Why does it mainly improve NOM but degrade Zero ACC/DAT in Table 3?
- Ambiguity: Provide a confusion matrix (at least for Zero) to quantify NOM↔ACC confusions. How much of the remaining error is due to NOM/ACC ambiguity versus true long-distance linking?
- Reproducibility: Please provide the actual code URL (the paper lists “http:xxx”). Also report training time and memory for Multi-Seq vs Single-Seq.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Emphasize task complexity: Briefly contrast Japanese PAS with English SRL (span-based vs dependency-based, high rate of argument omission, zero-pronoun ambiguity), and motivate NOM vs ACC ambiguity in the absence of overt case markers.
- Optional extension: Including an SRL experiment (even small-scale) or a discussion on portability would strengthen claims of broader applicability, though I acknowledge this is additional work.

Overall, this is a solid and timely paper. Clarifying the task scope and difficulty, analyzing well-formedness and error types, and discussing the uneven per-role effects of Multi-Seq would significantly strengthen the work.
