Summary
The paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on intra-sentential arguments (both dependency-linked and zero arguments). It proposes two neural sequence models that avoid reliance on predicted syntax: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN model that encodes sentence-level context for one predicate at a time, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to model multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, the multi-sequence model achieves new state-of-the-art performance without syntactic features, with particularly notable gains on zero arguments. The paper includes ablations on network depth and residual connections, per-case-role results, and statistical significance testing.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A grid-style multi-sequence RNN architecture that explicitly captures multi-predicate interactions from word sequences, eliminating reliance on predicted syntax and mitigating error propagation.
2) Strong empirical result on NAIST 1.5 showing that syntax-free models can outperform prior syntax-dependent SOTA, especially for zero pronouns; thorough evaluation with significance tests and per-role analysis.
3) Practical modeling insights for PAS: leveraging predicate-local context features (ARG/PRED/MARK), residual connections for deeper grids, and demonstrating that depth helps when training end-to-end on sentence sequences.

Strengths
- Significance and impact: Demonstrating that a syntax-free, sequence-based approach surpasses strong syntax-based baselines is an important result for PAS (and broadly SRL) in pro-drop languages. The gains on Zero (≈ +3 F1 vs Ouchi et al., and +1 F1 vs the single-sequence model) directly address the most challenging aspect of Japanese PAS.
- Modeling of multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN inter-sequence connections are a clean, general mechanism to propagate information across predicates. The residual connections make deeper grids trainable and substantively improve performance, indicating the approach scales with depth.
- Solid experimental design and reporting: Clear dataset split, no external resources, hyperparameter ranges, optimization details, and bootstrap significance testing. The per-case-role breakdown is valuable; the large DAT gains highlight robustness despite class imbalance.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The architecture is well explained (input features, information flow in odd/even layers, output formulation). The comparison to related SRL work (Zhou & Xu, Collobert et al.) situates the contribution well.
- Focus on zero pronouns: The analysis correctly emphasizes long-distance context modeling and multi-predicate cues as key to zero-anaphora improvements.

Weaknesses
- Scope limited to intra-sentential arguments: While this is standard for NAIST 1.5 evaluations, it would help to more explicitly discuss how the approach might scale to inter-sentential zero anaphora (e.g., document-level grids, memory constraints).
- Missing ablations on input features: The contribution of ARG vs PRED vs MARK features, and the effect of the PRED window size C, are not disentangled. A short ablation (even on dev) would strengthen the understanding of where the gains come from.
- Constraints at inference: It is not fully clear whether any structural constraints (e.g., one argument per case per predicate) are enforced at decoding time. Clarifying whether decoding is purely independent per token (per predicate) or uses simple constraints would help interpret per-role gains.
- Minor clarity issues: The role of boldface in Table 2 is not explained; and a few figure captions could better highlight how predicate-local context (e.g., case markers around the predicate) is operationalized.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Table 2 boldface: What does bold denote (best per column, per block, or overall)? Please clarify in the caption.
- Decoding constraints: Are case assignments for a given predicate subject to any global constraint (e.g., unique NOM/ACC/DAT), or are labels predicted independently per token? If independent, did you try a simple post-hoc constraint and does it help?
- Computational complexity: What are the training/inference time and memory characteristics as functions of sentence length T and number of predicates M? Any practical limits observed on NAIST?
- Feature ablations: Can you report (even briefly) the effect of removing PRED or MARK features, or varying the PRED window size?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Visualization: To better illustrate the PRED-context feature, please augment the Figure 6 example to explicitly show how the predicate-centered window captures nearby case markers (e.g., the accusative を co-occurring with the verb) and how that propagates across predicates via the grid.
- Typos and style (examples; please proofread throughout):
  - Abstract: “show” → “shows”; “state-of-theart” → “state-of-the-art”; “suffers from the error propagation” → “suffers from error propagation”.
  - Sec. 1: “achieved the state-of-theart result” → “state-of-the-art”; “as an solution” → “as a solution”; “takes as input all predicates … at a time” → “takes all predicates … as input at once”.
  - Sec. 2: Be consistent with “intra-sentential” (avoid mixing “intrasentential”). Remove stray spaces in “男 i (man i)”.
  - Sec. 3: “evennumbered” → “even-numbered”; prefer “binary indicator” over “binary index”.
  - Sec. 4: “lowerright” → “lower-right”; ensure consistent capitalization of “Grid layer(s)”.
  - Sec. 6: “significent test” → “significance test”; “improved around X points” → “improved by around X points”; “compared the models” → “compared with the models”; Table 3 caption: “nominal” → “nominative”.
  - General hyphenation: “multi-predicate”, “case-role”, “state-of-the-art”.
- Minor clarity:
  - Specify whether F-measures are micro-averaged over instances.
  - Where possible, report confidence intervals alongside bootstrap significance markers.
  - If code is to be released post-review, add a reproducibility checklist in the camera-ready.

Overall assessment
This is a strong paper with a clear and practically impactful contribution: a syntax-free, grid-based neural architecture that advances the state of the art in Japanese PAS, notably for zero pronouns. The empirical gains are convincing, the method is well motivated and described, and the weaknesses are minor and largely addressable in the final version. I recommend acceptance. The authors’ response addressed my concerns satisfactorily.
