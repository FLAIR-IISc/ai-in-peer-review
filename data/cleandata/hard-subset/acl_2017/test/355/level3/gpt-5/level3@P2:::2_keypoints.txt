- Paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, adapting sequence labeling and proposing a multi-sequence Grid-RNN to jointly predict intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero).
- Models set a new state of the art on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 using only word sequences and a predicate marker, with notable gains on zero arguments.
- Main contributions: effective syntax-free PAS model (SOTA), practical multi-dimensional/Grid RNN for multi-predicate encoding, and empirical depth analysis showing benefits of residual connections.
- Strength: strong empirical gains without relying on syntactic parses, reducing error propagation from parsing.
- Strength: Grid-RNN improves Zero argument prediction, supporting the value of inter-predicate interactions for zero anaphora.
- Strength: careful experimental evaluation with ablations across depths and demonstration that residual connections enable deeper, better models.
- Strength: improved per-role performance (notably DAT) over older baselines, indicating robustness for rare roles.
- Strength: clear writing, helpful figures, and code release enhancing reproducibility.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—single-sequence follows prior work and Grid-RNN is a straightforward application of multi-dimensional RNNs.
- Weakness: modest overall gains from multi-predicate modeling (e.g., +0.27 overall F1), lacking deeper analysis of when the grid helps.
- Weakness: missing or incomplete quantitative comparison with closely related Shibata et al. (2016), weakening empirical positioning.
- Weakness: paper does not discuss decoding constraints or how per-predicate “at most one” case-role uniqueness is enforced at test time.
- Weakness: limited error analysis and diagnostics; lacks investigation of error types, predicate-density effects, and examples where multi-predicate signals help or hurt.
- Observation: per-role Zero results show trade-offs (e.g., DAT Zero drops from 9.83 to 7.52 with Grid-RNN), warranting deeper investigation.
- Question: How are uniqueness constraints per predicate handled—any global decoding or post-processing, and how are multiple NOMs treated in evaluation?
- Question: Can the authors provide a quantitative or normalized comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), or run models under matched setups?
- Question: What is the computational overhead (training/inference time, memory) of Grid-RNN vs single-sequence as predicate count increases, and what batching strategies are used?
- Question: Can the authors ablate input feature groups (ARG vs PRED vs MARK) to show their contributions, particularly for Zero performance?
- Question: What causes the DAT degradation with Grid-RNN—are there sentence configurations where inter-predicate signals mislead the model?
- Question: Have the authors tried adding syntactic cues or pretrained embeddings to test complementarity with Grid-RNN?
- Additional comment: Clarify whether evaluation counts a prediction correct regardless of duplicate predictions and how conflicts are resolved.
- Additional comment: Clean up inconsistent or incomplete bibliography entries.
- Additional comment: Include qualitative examples showing where the Grid-RNN resolves zero pronouns to strengthen interpretability.
