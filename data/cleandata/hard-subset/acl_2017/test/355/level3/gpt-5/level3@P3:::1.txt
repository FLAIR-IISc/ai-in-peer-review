Summary
This paper proposes a neural approach to Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that models interactions among all predicates in a sentence using a grid-type recurrent neural network (Grid-RNN). Unlike prior state-of-the-art systems that rely heavily on syntactic parses, the proposed models operate on word sequences without explicit syntactic inputs. A single-sequence BiRNN baseline and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN are introduced; the latter jointly predicts arguments for all predicates in a sentence and yields the best performance, especially on zero pronouns. Experiments on NAIST 1.5 show consistent gains over strong syntax-based baselines and solid ablations on network depth and residual connections.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture that models multi-predicate interactions without explicit syntax and achieves new state-of-the-art accuracy on Japanese PAS, with notable gains on zero pronouns.
2) Strong empirical evidence that syntax-free sequence models can surpass syntax-dependent systems for PAS on NAIST 1.5, with thorough ablations (depth, residuals) and per-case-role analyses.
3) Clear, replicable implementation and experimental setup, including hyperparameters and significance testing.

Strengths
- State-of-the-art results without syntax: Both single- and multi-sequence models outperform strong prior systems that rely on parsing (Imamura et al., Ouchi et al.). This is an impactful finding for robustness and portability, as it removes a major source of error propagation from pipeline parsing.
- Targeted improvements on zero pronouns: The multi-sequence model improves zero-case F by ~3 points over Ouchi et al., and by ~1 point over the single-sequence model. Given the centrality and difficulty of dropped arguments in Japanese, this is a particularly meaningful advance.
- Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN provides consistent, statistically significant improvements over the single-sequence baseline, supporting the linguistic intuition that predicates in a sentence constrain one another’s arguments.
- Thorough experimental detail and analysis: The paper provides implementation details, hyperparameters, training choices, significance testing, per-case-role breakdowns (NOM/ACC/DAT; Dep vs Zero), and ablations on depth and residual connections. This clarity greatly aids reproducibility and interpretability.
- Sound deep sequence modeling practice: The use of stacked BiRNNs/GRUs and residual connections is well-motivated and empirically validated; deeper models with residuals yield the best performance.

Weaknesses
- Presentation issues (minor): The paper contains a noticeable number of typos/grammar slips, occasional notational inconsistencies (e.g., evennumbered), and some unclear phrasing in tables/captions. These detract from an otherwise clear presentation and should be corrected.
- Clarification needed for Table 2 formatting: The meaning of boldface is not explained in the caption; it is unclear whether bold denotes best-in-column, best-per-model, or best overall. Please clarify in the caption.
- Minor exposition gap around PRED-context: The PRED windowing feature is important; an additional illustration would help. Specifically, using the Fig. 6 example, show how the PRED context window can include the accusative marker with or near the verb and how that signal is encoded.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Table 2: What exactly does boldface denote (best per column across all models, best per model, or best per residual setting)? Please specify in the caption.
- Significance testing: You mention bootstrap resampling; please state the number of samples and whether stratification was used.
- Complexity: What is the computational overhead of the multi-sequence Grid-RNN relative to the single-sequence model (training time and memory) as a function of number of predicates M?

This will concretize how the model leverages local functional morphology for PAS decisions without explicit syntax.

Overall recommendation
Accept. This is a solid, well-executed neural modeling paper with a clear contribution: jointly modeling all predicates via a Grid-RNN to outperform syntax-based SOTA, particularly on zero pronouns. The work is clearly written, carefully evaluated, and practically useful. Remaining issues are minor presentation edits.
