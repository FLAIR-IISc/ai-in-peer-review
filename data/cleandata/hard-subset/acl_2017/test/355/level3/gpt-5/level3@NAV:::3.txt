Summary: The paper proposes a joint neural model for Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that captures multi-predicate interactions with Grid-RNNs, contrasted against a single-sequence BiRNN baseline. On NAIST 1.5, the approach achieves state-of-the-art performance without syntactic features, with the largest gains on zero arguments.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) An effective syntax-free, joint Grid-RNN architecture for PAS that delivers SOTA on NAIST 1.5, especially improving zero-pronoun cases.
2) A solid empirical comparison to single-sequence RNNs, including depth/residual ablations.
3) Released code enabling reproducibility.

Strengths:
- Empirical strength: Clear, statistically significant improvements over strong baselines (Ouchi et al., 2015), with ~+3 F for Zero and overall +2+ over older systems.
- Practical impact: Removes parser dependency, reducing error propagation while still handling intra-sentential zero anaphora well.
- Careful experimentation: Sensible data splits, ablations on depth and residual connections, and per-case-role reporting (Table 3).
- Accessibility: The paper is readable for non-Japanese specialists, with helpful figures that clarify the grid architecture.

Weaknesses:
- Underspecified task complexity: The ambiguity between NOM and ACC and the contrasts with SRL (especially zero-pronoun ambiguity) are under-emphasized.
- Missing statistics: No breakdown of what proportion of zero pronouns are intra-sententially resolvable versus inter-sentential, which is key to contextualizing difficulty.
- Representation gap: It is unclear whether inter-sentential zero pronouns are explicitly marked as empty arguments or left unmarked, and how this choice affects learning.
- Structural constraints: The model seems not to enforce one-argument-per-case per predicate or unique-slot constraints; no analysis of whether these are learned implicitly.
- Limited qualitative analysis: Multi-Seq gains appear concentrated in NOM (Table 3); there is no systematic output comparison to Single-Seq to reveal when/why joint modeling helps.

Questions to Authors:
- Provide statistics: What fraction of zero pronouns in NAIST 1.5 are intra-sentential vs inter-sentential? Among intra-sentential, how often are they resolvable without discourse?
- Clarify annotation/modeling: How are inter-sentential zeros represented (no marking vs explicit empties)? Implications for negative examples and calibration?
- Constraint learning: Report violation rates of linguistic constraints and whether simple decoding constraints would help.
- Diagnostic analyses: Compare Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq outputs (agreement rates, contexts: subject-sharing, coordination, predicate distance, number of predicates per sentence). Break down improvements by distance and case role, especially why gains concentrate in NOM.
- Optional: Include preliminary SRL results or discuss portability.
