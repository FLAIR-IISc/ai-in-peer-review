- Joint Grid-RNN model for Japanese PAS capturing multi-predicate interactions achieves SOTA on NAIST 1.5, with largest gains on zero arguments.  
- Main contributions: a syntax-free joint Grid-RNN architecture, thorough comparison/ablations against single-sequence RNNs, and released code for reproducibility.  
- Empirical strength: statistically significant improvements over strong baselines (~+3 F for Zero, overall +2+).  
- Practical impact: removes parser dependency, reducing error propagation while still handling intra-sentential zero anaphora well.  
- Careful experimentation: sensible data splits, depth/residual ablations, and per-case-role reporting.  
- Accessibility: paper is readable for non-Japanese specialists with helpful figures clarifying the grid architecture.  
- Underspecified task complexity: ambiguity between NOM and ACC and contrasts with SRL (zero-pronoun ambiguity) are under-emphasized.  
- Missing statistics: no breakdown of what proportion of zero pronouns are intra-sentential versus inter-sentential.  
- Representation gap: unclear whether inter-sentential zeros are explicitly marked as empty arguments or left unmarked and how that affects learning.  
- Structural constraints: model appears not to enforce one-argument-per-case or unique-slot constraints; no analysis of whether these are learned implicitly.  
- Limited qualitative analysis: Multi-Seq gains seem concentrated in NOM but no systematic output comparison to Single-Seq to show when/why joint modeling helps.  
- Requested statistic: report fraction of zero pronouns that are intra- vs inter-sentential and how often intra-sentential ones are resolvable without discourse.  
- Clarification requested: how inter-sentential zeros are represented in annotation/modeling and implications for negative examples and calibration.  
- Constraint analysis requested: report violation rates of linguistic constraints and whether simple decoding constraints would improve results.  
- Diagnostic analyses requested: compare Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq outputs on agreement rates and contexts (subject-sharing, coordination, predicate distance, number of predicates) and break down improvements by distance and case role.  
- Optional suggestion: include preliminary SRL results or discuss portability of the approach to SRL.  
- Minor language/typographical corrections suggested (e.g., state-of-the-art, intra-sentential, lower-right, even-numbered, “a solution”, “significance test”, reword unclear sentences, “remains low (10–20%)”, “She ate bread.”).
