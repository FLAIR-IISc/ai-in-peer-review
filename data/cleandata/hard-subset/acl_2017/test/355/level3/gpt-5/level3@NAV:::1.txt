Summary: The paper proposes a syntax-free neural approach to Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that models interactions among multiple predicates using Grid-type RNNs. The authors present a single-sequence model (per-predicate) and a multi-sequence model (joint over all predicates in a sentence). On NAIST 1.5, the multi-sequence Grid-RNN surpasses state-of-the-art systems that rely on explicit syntax, with especially notable gains for zero pronouns.

Contributions (as I see them):
- Primary: A strong empirical demonstration that modeling multi-predicate interactions with a Grid-RNN, without explicit syntactic input, achieves state-of-the-art PAS performance and substantially improves zero-pronoun resolution.
- Secondary: A carefully described and reproducible architecture and training setup with ablations (network depth, residual connections) that clarify design choices.
- Tertiary: A joint multi-sequence formulation that operationalizes multi-predicate interaction for PAS and can transfer to related SRL tasks.

Strengths:
- Significant, consistent improvements over strong syntax-based baselines, particularly for Zero (≈+3 F1 vs Ouchi et al. 2015) and overall (+2+ F1 over Imamura et al. 2009), validating the value of context-only modeling.
- Clear methodological exposition and implementation details, including hyperparameters, optimization, depth/residual ablations, and significance testing.
- Focus on zero pronouns, a core challenge in Japanese, with the multi-sequence model yielding the best Zero results and a solid case-role breakdown.
- No reliance on external resources or syntactic parsers, mitigating error propagation and improving portability.

Weaknesses:
- Minor presentation issues: several typos/fluency problems; table/figure annotations could be clearer.
- The role of PRED-context could be better illustrated; Fig. 6 is a good opportunity to show how the accusative marker attaches with the verb in context.
- Table 2: the meaning of boldface is unclear (best within column? with/without residuals?). Please clarify in caption/footnote.
- Optional: brief reporting of training/inference time and memory would help practitioners assess practicality.
