- Paper addresses Japanese PAS (intra-sentential Dep and Zero) with two neural models: Single-Seq BiRNN and Grid-RNN multi-sequence, avoiding syntactic parsers.
- Both models achieve SOTA on NAIST 1.5 without syntax; Grid-RNN further improves Zero arguments; deeper residual stacks help.
- Contribution: empirical SOTA results on NAIST 1.5 without syntactic features, notably for Zero and dative roles.
- Contribution: modeling — a simple Grid-RNN capturing multi-predicate interactions and a strong Single-Seq baseline; residuals enable deeper networks.
- Contribution: experimental analysis with single vs multi-predicate comparison, depth/residual ablations, and per-case-role breakdown.
- Strength: strong results without syntax, outperforming Ouchi et al. (2015) and reducing pipeline/parser error dependence.
- Strength: demonstrates value of sentence-level context and predicate interactions; multi-predicate modeling aids pro-drop language PAS.
- Strength: thorough ablations showing deeper residual networks consistently improve performance.
- Strength: per-case-role analysis highlights large gains on dative and that Grid-RNN mainly helps Zero NOM.
- Strength: reproducible setup using standard splits, no external resources, and code release.
- Weakness: limited modeling novelty — straightforward RNN/Grid-RNN adaptations, novelty mainly empirical/application-focused.
- Weakness: no structural constraints enforced (e.g., one argument per case, unique token roles) and no analysis of invalid outputs.
- Weakness: insufficient error analysis of where multi-predicate modeling helps or hurts (e.g., harms Zero ACC/DAT).
- Weakness: unclear handling and proportion of inter-sentential vs intra-sentential zeros; no quantification of resolvable zeros.
- Weakness: limited scope — claims about broader SRL applicability lack experiments or discussion beyond Japanese PAS.
- Q: report proportions of intra- vs inter-sentential zero pronouns (train/dev/test) overall and by case.
- Q: clarify how predicates with only inter-sentential arguments are represented and how this affects training labels/class imbalance.
- Q: how often does model violate one-argument-per-case or assign same token multiple roles; rates for Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq and effect of constrained post-processing?
- Q: provide analysis where Grid-RNN helps most: by case, token-predicate distance, number of predicates per sentence, and shared-argument cases.
- Q: explain why Grid-RNN improves Zero NOM but degrades Zero ACC/DAT and whether reweighting/regularization helps.
- Q: report sensitivity to number of predicates per sentence (M) and whether Grid-RNN’s advantage grows with M.
- Presentation: fix phrasing (“state of the art” usage), typos (“significent”→“significance”), capitalization consistency, citation/spacing/hyphenation, footnote placement, and minor grammar (e.g., “we firstly”→“we first”).  
- Overall: strong empirical paper with clear takeaways; address constraint adherence and deeper comparative error analysis to strengthen contribution; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
