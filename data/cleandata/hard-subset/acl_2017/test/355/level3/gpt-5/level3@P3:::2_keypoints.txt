- Introduces syntax-free neural models for Japanese PAS: a strong single-sequence BiGRU and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models predicates.  
- Both models outperform prior state of the art on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5; Grid-RNN gives small but statistically significant gains, especially for zero arguments.  
- Main contribution: a strong, syntax-free BiRNN baseline that sets a new NAIST 1.5 state of the art, showing sentence-level context can replace syntax features.  
- Main contribution: a Grid-RNN extension that models multiple predicates jointly to capture inter-predicate interactions.  
- Main contribution: empirical study showing deeper networks with residual connections improve PAS performance (~1 F1 gain with 8 layers + residuals).  
- Strength: strong empirical gains without syntactic features (Single: 81.15 F1; Grid-RNN: 81.42 F1) and notable improvements on Zero and Dep cases.  
- Strength: rigorous experimental methodology—standard splits, bootstrap significance testing, detailed hyperparameters, and ablation studies.  
- Strength: simple and generalizable approach that avoids hand-engineered syntax and external resources, easing portability to other languages/tasks.  
- Strength: Grid-RNN’s main improvements are on Zero arguments, addressing one of the hardest PAS subproblems.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—single-sequence model is an adaptation of Zhou & Xu (2015) and Grid-RNN is an incremental architectural extension.  
- Weakness: multi-predicate interaction gains are modest (All +0.27 F1; Zero +1.02), smaller than some prior interaction benefits, questioning practical impact.  
- Weakness: missing direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), which also uses neural scoring and could affect state-of-the-art claims.  
- Weakness: limited qualitative/diagnostic analysis of when and why Grid-RNN helps, and no discussion of computational overhead or result variance.  
- Question: please compare to Shibata et al. (2016) under comparable settings or discuss expected differences.  
- Question: how stable are results across random seeds—report mean±std for Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq given small gains?  
- Question: what are the training/inference time and memory costs of Grid-RNN vs single model as predicate count grows?  
- Question: provide qualitative examples where Grid-RNN corrects single-model errors, especially for Zero cases and different roles.  
- Question: did you try alternative interaction mechanisms (cross-predicate attention, biaffine coupling, CRF-like coupling), and how do they compare?  
- Question: do you enforce global decoding constraints (one argument per case per predicate), and how often are such constraints violated without them?
