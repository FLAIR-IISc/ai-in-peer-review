Summary and contributions
This paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis with a focus on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It introduces two neural sequence models: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN/GRU model that predicts arguments for one predicate at a time from word sequences without syntactic features, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines, with the Grid-RNN achieving the best overall results, especially for zero arguments.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical: State-of-the-art performance on NAIST 1.5 without syntactic features, with notable gains on zero arguments (particularly NOM), surpassing prior systems that rely on syntax.
2) Architectural: A grid-type multi-sequence RNN that jointly encodes predicate-argument candidate sequences to capture multi-predicate interactions without explicit syntax; depth and residual connections are shown to help.
3) Analysis/ablations: Comparisons of single vs multi-sequence, depth, and residual connections; per-case-role breakdown.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results without syntax: Both single- and multi-sequence models outperform prior pointwise and joint baselines on NAIST 1.5, with larger improvements on Zero, where syntax is traditionally most helpful. The multi-sequence model yields significant overall gains over single-sequence, indicating that predicate interactions are captured effectively without parser input.
- Clear demonstration of benefits for zero arguments: Improvements of about +2–3 F on Zero, and case-role analysis shows especially strong gains on DAT and NOM (Zero), aligning with the claim that sentence-level context and predicate interactions help long-distance and omitted arguments.
- Solid modeling and training details: The paper presents well-specified architectures (input features, GRU details), optimization choices, and sensible ablations (varying layer depth, residual connections). Residual connections clearly stabilize deeper models, yielding consistent performance gains.
- Relevance and accessibility: The work addresses a core and difficult subproblem in Japanese NLP (zero pronouns). The presentation, figures, and explicit positioning relative to SRL make the paper accessible even to non-Japanese specialists.
- Practical significance: Removing dependency on syntactic parsers mitigates error propagation, simplifying deployment; outperforming syntax-reliant systems underscores the practical utility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the architecture: While the Grid-RNN application to PAS is interesting, grid/multidimensional RNNs and multi-predicate joint modeling are established. The novelty lies in their integration and empirical validation rather than theoretical or architectural innovation.
- Small net gains of multi-sequence over single-sequence: The overall improvement of the Grid-RNN over the single-sequence model is statistically significant but modest (~0.27 F overall; ~+1 F on Zero). The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of when and why multi-sequence helps, and where it does not.
- Constraints and decoding: The model predicts per-word per-predicate labels via softmax without explicit structural constraints (e.g., one argument per case per predicate; a token cannot fill multiple roles simultaneously). It is unclear how often illegal configurations occur and whether implicit learning suffices. Lack of constrained decoding or post-processing may cap performance or lead to inconsistent outputs.
- Scope of evaluation and fairness: The paper focuses on intra-sentential arguments (reasonable), but it is unclear how inter-sentential zeros are represented in training/evaluation (e.g., unmarked vs explicit null). Also, statistical significance is reported only for single vs multi; significance vs external baselines is not shown. More rigorous comparative analysis would strengthen claims.
- Limited error and qualitative analysis: The results would be more compelling with error typologies, distance-based breakdowns for Zero, and illustrative examples where multi-predicate interactions reverse single-predicate errors.

Questions to authors
- Task framing and statistics: What proportion of gold zero arguments in NAIST 1.5 are intra- vs inter-sentential? Among intra-sentential, what fraction is actually resolvable based on within-sentence cues (e.g., antecedent present)? Please provide these stats to contextualize difficulty.
- Representation of inter-sentential zeros: How are arguments that are truly inter-sentential treated in training labels—unlabeled (null), or explicitly marked as empty arguments? What are the implications for learning decision boundaries, especially for NOM?
- Constraints: Do you enforce any hard constraints at inference (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate)? If not, how often do you observe violations, and how do they affect F1? Could simple post-processing constraints improve performance?
- Analysis of multi-sequence gains: Where do multi-sequence predictions differ most from single-sequence? Please provide a comparative output analysis (e.g., by predicate distance, number of predicates, clause boundaries) and examples illustrating recovered errors.
- Case-role differences: The largest multi-sequence gains appear for NOM (Zero). Why less so for ACC/DAT? Are improvements tied to subject sharing or topicalization patterns? Any evidence that inter-predicate signals act as soft syntactic cues?
- Significance vs baselines: Are improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015) statistically significant (bootstrap)? Please report p-values.

Additional comments (minor)
- Clarify the claim: You sometimes say “exceeds state-of-the-art without syntactic information.” Since Ouchi et al. (2015) uses syntax, please distinguish “SOTA overall” vs “SOTA without syntax,” and state significance vs baselines clearly.
- Consider adding an SRL experiment (even preliminary) to support generality claims; otherwise, temper the generalization to SRL in the conclusion.
- Efficiency: Please report training/inference speed and memory, and sensitivity to number of predicates (M) and depth (L).
- Language/typo suggestions:
  - “state-of-theart” → “state-of-the-art”
  - “This ap- proach” → “This approach”
  - “intrasentential”/“intra-sentential”: use one consistently (prefer “intra-sentential”)
  - “The F-measure remains 10-20%” → “The F-measure remains 10–20%”
  - “significent test” → “significance test”
  - “The mark † denotes the significantly better results” → “denotes a significantly better result”
  - Use consistent casing for Dep/Zero (stick with Dep, Zero)
  - “ALLCases Joint Model” → “AllCases Joint Model”
  - Ensure equations have consistent spacing and vector/matrix dimensions; fix broken line hyphenations throughout.

Overall: Well-executed and practically impactful work with clear empirical gains and a clean modeling story. Addressing the analysis, constraints, and clarity points above would strengthen the contribution. I lean positive.
