Summary
This paper adapts sequence-based SRL modeling to Japanese PAS and introduces a Grid-RNN to jointly model interactions among multiple predicates without relying on syntactic parses. On NAIST 1.5, both the single-sequence and Grid-RNN models outperform prior systems, with the Grid-RNN yielding small but significant gains, particularly for zero arguments.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Strong empirical demonstration that syntax-free, sequence-based models can surpass the prior state-of-the-art for Japanese PAS on NAIST 1.5, with notable improvements on zero arguments.
- An extension from single-sequence modeling to a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that captures multi-predicate interactions and delivers consistent, if modest, additional gains.
- A careful empirical study of depth and residual connections for PAS modeling.

Strengths
- State-of-the-art accuracy on a standard benchmark, achieved without syntactic features, is a clear and practically important result for languages with weaker parsers or pipeline error propagation.
- Consistent improvements on zero arguments, where context and long-distance dependencies matter most; the reported ~1–3 F1 gains over strong baselines support the modeling choice.
- The Grid-RNN design is simple, computationally reasonable, and integrates seamlessly with the single-sequence baseline, making the approach reproducible and extensible.
- Solid experimental methodology: standard splits, multiple baselines, bootstrap significance testing, and depth/residual ablations provide credible evidence for the claims.
- The paper is well-structured and motivated; architectural choices are clearly explained.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: the core advances are an adaptation of Zhou & Xu (2015) to Japanese PAS and a straightforward application of Grid/MD-RNNs for multi-sequence interaction; the technique itself is not fundamentally new.
- The absolute gains from multi-predicate interaction are modest (e.g., ~0.3 overall F1), and smaller than the effect sizes reported by Ouchi et al. (2015), calling into question the practical impact of the interaction component alone.
- Missing comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), which also augments Ouchi et al. with neural modeling; this omission makes it hard to position the contribution within current neural PAS work.
- Limited analysis of when predicate interactions help (e.g., by sentence length, number of predicates, dependency distance), which would clarify the conditions under which Grid-RNN is beneficial.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report results and architectural differences relative to Shibata et al. (2016) under the same split?
- How do gains vary with number of predicates per sentence and sentence length?
- Did you try enriching the model with lightweight syntactic cues (e.g., dependency distance) to test complementarity?
- Are improvements concentrated in specific zero-argument cases (NOM vs ACC vs DAT) and particular distances? An error analysis would help.
