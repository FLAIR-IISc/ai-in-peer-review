- Paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) for intra-sentential Dep and Zero, proposing a single-sequence BiRNN/GRU and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN; Grid-RNN yields best results, especially on zero arguments.

- Main empirical contribution: state-of-the-art on NAIST 1.5 without syntactic features, with notable gains on Zero (particularly NOM).

- Main architectural contribution: a grid/multi-sequence RNN to jointly model all predicates and capture multi-predicate interactions without explicit syntax; depth and residuals help.

- Analysis contribution: ablations comparing single vs multi-sequence, layer depth, residual connections, and per-case-role breakdowns.

- Strength: Strong empirical results without syntax, outperforming prior pointwise and joint baselines, with the Grid-RNN improving over the single-sequence model.

- Strength: Clear demonstration of benefits for zero arguments (+2–3 F on Zero), with particularly strong gains on DAT and NOM.

- Strength: Well-specified modeling and training details; residual connections stabilize deeper models and improve performance.

- Strength: Relevant and accessible presentation addressing a core Japanese NLP problem and connecting to SRL literature.

- Strength: Practical significance—removing dependency on syntactic parsers reduces error propagation and simplifies deployment.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—grid/multidimensional RNNs and multi-predicate joint modeling are established; main novelty is integration and empirical validation.

- Weakness: Small net gains of Grid-RNN over single-sequence (~0.27 F overall; ~+1 F on Zero); needs deeper analysis of when multi-sequence helps.

- Weakness: No explicit structural constraints or constrained decoding; unclear frequency and impact of illegal label configurations.

- Weakness: Evaluation scope/fairness issues—focus on intra-sentential arguments leaves unclear treatment of inter-sentential zeros and significance vs external baselines.

- Weakness: Limited error analysis and qualitative examples; missing distance-based breakdowns and illustrative cases where multi-predicate modeling helps.

- Question: What proportion of gold zero arguments are intra- vs inter-sentential, and how many intra-sentential zeros are resolvable with within-sentence cues?

- Question: How are truly inter-sentential zeros represented in training labels (unlabeled vs explicitly marked), and what are the implications for learning?

- Question: Are any hard inference constraints enforced (e.g., one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate); if not, how often do violations occur and do simple post-processing rules help?

- Question: Where do multi-sequence predictions differ most from single-sequence (predicate distance, number of predicates, clause boundaries); provide comparative analyses and examples.

- Question: Why are multi-sequence gains largest for NOM (Zero) and smaller for ACC/DAT—are gains tied to subject sharing or topicalization patterns?

- Question: Are improvements over prior baselines (e.g., Ouchi et al. 2015) statistically significant—please report p-values.

- Minor/commentary: Clarify "SOTA without syntax" vs overall SOTA and report significance; consider adding an SRL experiment or temper generalization claims.

- Minor/commentary: Report training/inference speed, memory, and sensitivity to number of predicates and depth; fix typos and consistency (e.g., “state-of-the-art”, intra-sentential, Dep/Zero casing).
