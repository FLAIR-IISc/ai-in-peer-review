- Summary: Proposes Grid-RNN (Multi-Seq) to model inter-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS and compares it to a strong Single-Seq BiRNN; both set new state-of-the-art on NAIST 1.5, with Multi-Seq giving a small but significant gain, especially for zero-NOM.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a grid-structured RNN that jointly encodes multiple predicate-specific sequences to capture inter-predicate interactions without syntax.

- Contribution 2: Presents a well-tuned single-sequence BiRNN baseline that outperforms prior syntax-based joint models, clarifying gains from sentence-level contextualization.

- Contribution 3: Empirically analyzes depth and residual connections, showing modest but significant improvements from multi-predicate modeling, largest for zero-NOM.

- Strength: Strong empirical results without syntax—both models beat prior baselines and Multi-Seq improves overall F1 and Zero performance substantially.

- Strength: Careful experimental setup with clear model definitions, consistent data splits, and hyperparameter sweeps (including depth/residual studies).

- Strength: Clear, accessible presentation with helpful figures that make the work approachable beyond Japanese-specialists.

- Strength: Focus on zero pronouns addresses the hardest part of Japanese PAS and shows meaningful improvements for intra-sentential zero-NOM.

- Weakness: Lacks quantification of task difficulty—no stats on how often zero pronouns are intra-sententially resolvable or per-case breakdowns to contextualize F1 scores.

- Weakness: No analysis of output well-formedness—does not report violations of structural constraints (e.g., multiple NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate) or whether model learns them.

- Weakness: Mixed per-role effects—Multi-Seq mainly improves NOM but degrades Zero ACC/DAT, and the paper does not analyze why.

- Weakness: Limited error analysis—little discussion of common confusions (NOM vs ACC), long-distance dependencies, control/coordination, or where Grid-RNN helps.

- Weakness: Unclear scope/annotation for inter-sentential zeros—unclear how inter-zero cases are represented in NAIST 1.5 and handled in training/evaluation.

- Question: What proportion of gold zero pronouns in NAIST 1.5 are intra-sentential overall and per case, and what are average predicate–argument distances for Zero vs Dep?

- Question: How are inter-sentential (inter-zero) predicate–case pairs annotated and handled—excluded or treated as null—and what are implications for class balance and false negatives?

- Question: Do you post-process to enforce one argument per case per predicate, and how often does the model predict multiple NOM/ACC/DAT for the same predicate; does Multi-Seq reduce such violations?

- Question: Provide analysis of contexts where Multi-Seq changes Single-Seq predictions (e.g., sentence predicate count, argument distance, control/coordination) and explain why NOM improves while Zero ACC/DAT degrade.

- Question: Provide a confusion matrix (at least for Zero) to quantify NOM↔ACC confusions and how much error is due to ambiguity vs long-distance linking failures.

- Question: Provide the actual code URL (instead of “http:xxx”) and report training time and memory requirements for Multi-Seq vs Single-Seq.

- Suggestion: Emphasize task complexity by contrasting Japanese PAS with English SRL (dependency vs span, high argument omission, zero-pronoun ambiguity) and motivate NOM vs ACC ambiguity.

- Suggestion: Optionally include an SRL experiment or discussion on portability to strengthen broader applicability claims.

- Typographical/phrasing fixes: Numerous specific corrections suggested (e.g., state-of-the-art, even-numbered, “As a solution”, consistent article usage for models, spacing in “mark embedding matrix Emark”, clearer phrasing, provide code URL, consistency in “nominative” vs “nominal”, “improved by about X points”, remove hyphenation artifacts).
