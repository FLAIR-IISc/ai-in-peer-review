Summary
The paper introduces neural models for Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that avoid explicit syntactic features. A strong single-sequence BiGRU model adapts ideas from Zhou & Xu (2015) to the dependency-based Japanese setting, predicting arguments for one predicate at a time. The authors then extend this to a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits, both models outperform the previous state of the art, with the Grid-RNN yielding small but statistically significant gains over the single-sequence baseline, especially for zero arguments.

Main contributions (as I see them; ordered by importance)
1) A strong, syntax-free BiRNN baseline for Japanese PAS that sets a new state of the art on NAIST 1.5, demonstrating that sentence-level contextual modeling can rival and surpass feature-rich, syntax-dependent systems.
2) An extension from single-sequence prediction to a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models multiple predicates, delivering small but significant gains, particularly for zero arguments.
3) An empirical study on depth and residual connections for these architectures, showing that deeper networks with residual links improve performance.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results on a standard benchmark without syntax: On NAIST 1.5, the single-sequence model achieves 81.15 F1 and the Grid-RNN reaches 81.42 F1, surpassing Ouchi et al. (2015) at 79.23. Improvements are notable for Zero arguments (44.09 → 46.10/47.12) and Dep (86.07 → 88.10/88.17). The DAT case sees especially large gains in Dep (38.39 → 65.91 with the single model).
- Solid experimental methodology and clarity: The paper uses the widely adopted splits, reports statistical significance via bootstrap resampling, provides detailed hyperparameters, and includes ablations on network depth and residual connections (showing ~1 F1 gains with 8 layers + residuals). The writing and structure are clear and well-motivated.
- Simple, generalizable modeling: The approach avoids hand-engineered syntactic features and external resources, relying on word sequence information and learned representations. This simplifies deployment and suggests broader applicability (e.g., to languages with unreliable parsers or to SRL settings).
- Focus on zero arguments: The Grid-RNN’s main gains are on Zero arguments (+1.0 F1 over the single model), aligning with the task’s hardest cases where syntax is least helpful. This supports the value of modeling cross-predicate cues for zero pronoun resolution.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The core single-sequence model is a straightforward adaptation of Zhou & Xu (2015) to dependency PAS with softmax instead of CRF. The Grid-RNN extension is an incremental architectural change to enable multi-sequence interactions. The conceptual advance is mainly from single- to multi-sequence modeling rather than a fundamentally new method.
- Modest gains from multi-predicate interactions vs prior work: The Grid-RNN yields small improvements over the single model (All: +0.27 F1; Zero: +1.02). This is a smaller effect than the interaction benefits reported by Ouchi et al. (2015), raising questions about the practical impact of the multi-sequence architecture.
- Missing comparison to Shibata et al. (2016): Shibata also extends Ouchi et al. with a neural scoring component. A direct comparison (ideally under the same data and settings) would strengthen claims about state of the art and clarify where the proposed models stand among neural PAS approaches.
- Limited analysis of why/when Grid-RNN helps and at what cost: The paper shows statistical gains but offers little qualitative/error analysis of predicate-interaction effects beyond Zero improvements. There is also no discussion of computational overhead (training/inference time, memory) for Grid-RNN vs single-sequence, or variance across random seeds, which matters given the small deltas.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- Can you provide a comparison with Shibata et al. (2016) under as comparable a setting as possible (same NAIST 1.5 split, same constraints on features/resources)? If not feasible, please discuss expected differences and why.
- How stable are results across random seeds and runs? Given the small Grid-RNN gains, please report mean±std over multiple seeds for Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq.
- What is the training/inference cost and memory footprint of the Grid-RNN compared to the single model as a function of number of predicates per sentence? Is the approach practical for longer sentences with many predicates?
- Can you provide qualitative examples where the Grid-RNN corrects errors made by the single model, especially for Zero cases and for different case roles?
- Did you try alternative interaction mechanisms (e.g., cross-predicate attention, biaffine coupling, or CRF-like coupling across predicates)? If so, how do they compare; if not, why is Grid-RNN preferable?
- Do you enforce any global constraints (e.g., one argument per case per predicate) at decoding time? If not, how often are such constraints violated, and could lightweight constraints further improve results?
