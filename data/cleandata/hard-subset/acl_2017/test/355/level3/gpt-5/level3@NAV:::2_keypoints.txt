- Adapts sequence-based SRL to Japanese PAS and introduces a Grid-RNN to model multi-predicate interactions without syntactic parses.
- On NAIST 1.5, both single-sequence and Grid-RNN outperform prior systems, with Grid-RNN giving small but significant gains, especially for zero arguments.

- Empirically shows syntax-free, sequence-based models can surpass prior state-of-the-art for Japanese PAS, notably improving zero-argument prediction.
- Extends single-sequence modeling to a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that captures predicate interactions and yields consistent, modest additional gains.
- Provides a careful empirical study of model depth and residual connections for PAS modeling.

- Achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on a standard benchmark without syntactic features, reducing reliance on parsing pipelines.
- Delivers consistent improvements on zero arguments (~1–3 F1 over strong baselines), supporting the modeling choice for long-distance dependencies.
- Grid-RNN is simple, computationally reasonable, integrates with the baseline, and is reproducible/extensible.
- Uses solid experimental methodology: standard splits, multiple baselines, bootstrap significance testing, and ablation experiments.
- Paper is well-structured and clearly motivates architectural choices.

- Limited novelty: mainly an adaptation of prior sequence SRL and straightforward application of Grid/MD-RNNs rather than a fundamentally new technique.
- Multi-predicate interaction yields modest absolute gains (~0.3 overall F1) and less impact than reported in some prior work, limiting perceived practical benefit.
- Omits comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), making it hard to position against recent neural PAS approaches.
- Lacks detailed analysis of when predicate interactions help (e.g., by sentence length, predicate count, dependency distance).

- Provide results and architectural differences relative to Shibata et al. (2016) on the same data split.
- Report how gains vary with number of predicates per sentence and sentence length.
- Indicate whether lightweight syntactic cues (e.g., dependency distance) were tried to test complementarity.
- Provide error analysis showing whether improvements concentrate on specific zero-argument cases (NOM/ACC/DAT) and at particular distances.
