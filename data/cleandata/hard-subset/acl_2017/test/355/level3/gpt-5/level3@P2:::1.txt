Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that model interactions among multiple predicates without relying on explicit syntactic parses. The key idea is a grid-type RNN (Grid-RNN) “multi-sequence” architecture that processes all predicates in a sentence jointly, enabling information flow across predicate-specific sequences of candidate arguments. A “single-sequence” BiRNN baseline that ignores cross-predicate interactions is also presented. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, the models achieve state-of-the-art results, notably improving zero-pronoun (intra-sentential) resolution.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture that captures multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, dispensing with explicit syntactic features yet outperforming strong parser-based systems.
2) Strong empirical results and thorough evaluation on NAIST 1.5, including significant gains on zero arguments and an analysis of depth and residual connections.
3) A clear, reproducible setup (public code, detailed hyperparameters) and a careful comparison against prior pointwise and joint baselines.

Strengths
- State-of-the-art accuracy without syntactic information: On NAIST 1.5, the multi-sequence model achieves 81.42 F1 overall, surpassing Ouchi et al. (2015) (79.23). This is particularly notable because the proposed approach forgoes parser-derived features, avoiding error propagation while improving robustness.

- Substantial improvements on zero pronouns: The multi-sequence model yields 47.12 F1 on Zero vs 44.09 (Ouchi+15), with the single-sequence already at 46.10. This directly targets a central challenge in Japanese PAS and supports the claim that sentence-level context and predicate interactions help zero anaphora.

- Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions: The multi-sequence Grid-RNN significantly outperforms the single-sequence variant (81.42 vs 81.15 overall; 47.12 vs 46.10 for Zero), showing that cross-predicate signals are beneficial even without syntax. The effect persists for Dep cases (88.17 vs 88.10), indicating broad utility.

- Careful ablations on depth and residual connections: The paper explores 2–8 layers and shows residual connections ease optimization and improve accuracy (~+1 F1 at 8 layers), providing architectural guidance beyond the specific task.

- Clarity and reproducibility: The model components (input features, gating choice, dimensions), training details (optimizer, schedule, regularization), and evaluation protocol are described in detail. Code availability is a strong plus.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty on the modeling primitive: Grid-/multidimensional RNNs exist; the novelty lies in applying them to multi-predicate PAS. While the application is impactful, a deeper discussion contrasting this design with alternative interaction mechanisms (e.g., attention across predicates, shared contextual encoders with predicate-conditioning) would strengthen the methodological contribution.

- Scope limited to intra-sentential arguments: The work explicitly excludes inter-sentential zero anaphora. While consistent with prior work, the paper could better discuss how the approach might be extended to document context (e.g., memory or hierarchical grids), since zero anaphora frequently crosses sentence boundaries.

- Limited analysis/interpretability: The paper would benefit from more qualitative error analyses or attention/activation visualizations showing how information flows across predicates in the grid, especially for successful zero-pronoun cases and typical failure modes.

- Computational considerations are not discussed: The grid introduces an extra dimension over predicates; complexity and memory scale with sentence length, number of predicates, and depth. Reporting training/inference speed, memory use, and scalability with many predicates would help practitioners.

- Presentation issues (minor): A number of typos and phrasing problems reduce polish. Also, the use of boldface in Table 2 is unclear (see questions).

Questions to authors (optional)
- Table 2: What does boldface indicate? Best within each configuration (with/without residual), per depth, or something else? Please clarify in the caption.
- Complexity and runtime: How do training and inference time scale with number of predicates M and sentence length T? Any practical limits or batching strategies?
- Inputs and resources: Are target predicates gold or predicted? Are word boundaries and tokenization gold from NAIST annotations? Any differences from Ouchi+15 settings that could affect comparability?
- Predicate conditioning: Beyond the PRED window and MARK feature, did you try alternative predicate-conditioning (e.g., predicate embeddings injected at all positions, or attention from candidates to predicates)?
- Many-predicate sentences: Do performance or runtime degrade with high M? Any heuristics for extremely predicate-dense sentences?
