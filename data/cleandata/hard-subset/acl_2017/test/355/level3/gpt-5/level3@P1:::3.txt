Summary:
This paper targets Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes two neural sequence models that avoid reliance on syntactic parsers: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN model that predicts case labels for each predicate independently, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model that jointly models interactions among all predicates in a sentence. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong non-neural and joint baselines without using syntactic features, with the Grid-RNN further improving Zero arguments. Ablations on depth and residual connections show deeper networks with residuals perform best, and per-case-role analysis indicates large gains on dative compared to past baselines and that multi-predicate modeling primarily helps Zero NOM.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical advance: State-of-the-art PAS performance on NAIST 1.5 without syntactic information, with notable gains on Zero arguments and dative compared to prior baselines.
2) Modeling advance: A simple, effective Grid-RNN architecture that captures multi-predicate interactions from word sequences, complemented by a strong single-sequence baseline. Residual connections enable deeper, better-performing stacks.
3) Experimental analysis: Comparisons between single vs multi-predicate modeling and depth/residual ablations; a per-case-role breakdown that sheds light on where gains occur.

Strengths:
- Strong results without syntax: Both models outperform the widely used Ouchi et al. (2015) joint model, despite not using syntactic features. The Grid-RNN further improves Zero arguments (Table 1), demonstrating robustness to parser error propagation and simplifying the pipeline.
- Clear evidence for sentence-level context and multi-predicate interactions: The single-sequence model’s zero-argument gains demonstrate the value of global sentence context with BiRNNs. The multi-sequence model provides additional gains, validating the intuition that predicate interactions help PAS in pro-drop languages.
- Thorough ablations: The depth and residuals study is well motivated; deeper models with residual connections consistently help (Table 2), supporting the architecture choices.
- Per-case-role analysis: The large improvements on dative compared to prior work (Table 3) suggest the models learn rare roles more robustly than feature-engineered baselines. The breakdown also highlights where multi-predicate modeling helps (Zero NOM).
- Reproducibility: Standard splits on NAIST 1.5, no external resources, and code release make the work easy to verify and extend.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty in modeling: The single-sequence RNN and Grid-RNN extensions are technically straightforward adaptations. The core novelty lies mostly in application to PAS and the empirical findings, rather than introducing fundamentally new architectures.
- Lack of structural constraints: The model does not enforce common linguistic constraints (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate; a token does not fill multiple roles for the same predicate). There is no analysis of how often invalid configurations occur, or whether adding a constrained decoding layer (e.g., ILP/CRF/post-hoc matching) would help.
- Insufficient error analysis of multi-predicate benefits: While Table 1 shows overall Zero improvements, Table 3 suggests the Grid-RNN mainly helps Zero NOM and is negative for Zero ACC/DAT. The paper does not analyze why, nor in what contexts (e.g., distance, number of predicates, predicate types) multi-predicate interactions help or hurt.
- Task clarity around zero pronouns: The paper excludes inter-sentential (Inter-Zero) arguments but does not quantify how many zeros are intra-sententially resolvable, which is important for interpreting difficulty and headroom. It is also unclear how inter-sentential zeros are represented in training instances (explicit empty argument vs. no marking), and what this implies for label distributions and class imbalance.
- Scope: While the paper motivates broader SRL applicability, no evidence is provided beyond Japanese PAS. Even a small-scale SRL transfer experiment or discussion of expected failure modes would strengthen the generality claim.

Questions to Authors:
- What proportion of zero pronouns in NAIST 1.5 are intra-sentential vs inter-sentential (overall and by case)? Please report these rates on train/dev/test to contextualize task difficulty and the Zero results.
- How are predicates with only inter-sentential arguments handled? Are inter-sentential zeros marked as null for all tokens, or excluded? Please clarify how this representation affects training labels and class imbalance.
- Constraint adherence: How often does the model output violate one-argument-per-case or assign the same token to multiple roles for a predicate? What is the empirical rate of such violations for Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq, and does simple constrained post-processing improve F1?
- Where does Grid-RNN help most? Please provide a comparative analysis of Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq predictions: breakdown by (i) case role, (ii) argument-predicate token distance, (iii) number of predicates per sentence, and (iv) whether the improved predicate shares arguments with others.
- Table 3 shows Grid-RNN improves Zero NOM but degrades Zero ACC/DAT relative to Single-Seq. What hypotheses explain this pattern (e.g., class imbalance, interaction biases), and can reweighting/regularization mitigate it?
- Sensitivity to the number of predicates per sentence M: how does performance change as M increases? Does Grid-RNN’s advantage correlate with higher M?

Additional comments (presentation/typos):
- Use “state of the art” (not “state-of-the-art” as a noun phrase) consistently.
- “significent test” → “significance test”; “the mark † denotes significantly better results at p < 0.05 compared to Single-Seq.”
- Ensure consistent capitalization of “Grid-RNN” and “Bi-RNN”; avoid mixing “grid-type RNNs” and “Grid-RNNs” without definition.
- Prefer “alleviate” over “remedy” when describing error propagation mitigation.
- Fix spacing/punctuation around citations and hyphenation (e.g., “multi-predicate,” “intra-sentential,” “reimplemented”).
- Check footnote markers and placement; some notes interrupt sentences awkwardly.
- Minor grammar: “we firstly introduce” → “we first introduce”; “improved around 2.0 points” → “improved by about 2.0 points.”

Overall: Strong empirical paper with clear takeaways on modeling PAS without syntax and leveraging multi-predicate interactions. Addressing the constraint adherence and providing a deeper comparative error analysis would significantly strengthen the scientific contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
