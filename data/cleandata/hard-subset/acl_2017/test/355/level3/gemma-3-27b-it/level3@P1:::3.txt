This paper presents a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, addressing the challenge of argument omission common in the language. The core contribution lies in leveraging grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs) to automatically induce features capturing multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, removing reliance on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus, exceeding existing systems, including those utilizing syntactic information. A secondary, yet significant, contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation comparing single-sequence RNNs to the proposed Grid-RNN architecture, highlighting the benefits of modeling inter-predicate relationships. Finally, the clear presentation and publicly available code contribute to the reproducibility and potential impact of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The model demonstrably outperforms previous state-of-the-art systems on a standard benchmark (NAIST Text Corpus). This establishes the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach and its potential for real-world application. The consistent improvements across various metrics, particularly for zero arguments, are convincing.
2. **Effective Architecture for Multi-Predicate Interactions:** The introduction of Grid-RNNs is a well-motivated approach to capturing the crucial interactions between predicates in Japanese sentences. The ablation study, comparing the Grid-RNN model to a single-sequence RNN, clearly demonstrates the benefit of explicitly modeling these interactions for PAS analysis. The significant improvement on zero anaphora resolution particularly validates the approach.
3. **Clear and Accessible Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The inclusion of illustrative figures (e.g., Figure 2 showing the model overview) aids understanding, even for readers not intimately familiar with Japanese linguistics. The explanations of the different model components are detailed and easy to follow.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive experimental setup, including comparisons with established baselines and an analysis of the effect of network depth and residual connections. The use of statistical significance testing (bootstrap resampling) strengthens the validity of the reported results. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Task Complexity:** While the paper acknowledges the difficulty of PAS analysis in Japanese due to argument omission, it doesn't fully emphasize the inherent ambiguity. A more detailed discussion of the challenges, such as the potential for ambiguity between nominative (NOM) and accusative (ACC) cases, and a comparison to similar challenges in semantic role labeling (SRL) would provide valuable context and highlight the novelty of the approach.
2. **Task Scope and Inter-Sentential Pronouns:** The focus on *intra*-sentential arguments is justified, but the treatment of *inter*-sentential zero pronouns is unclear. The authors state they do not address inter-sentential analysis, but do not clearly state how inter-sentential zero pronouns are represented in the corpus (e.g., are they explicitly marked as empty arguments, or are they simply absent?). Clarifying this would provide a more complete understanding of the task setup.
3. **Lack of Linguistic Constraints:** The model, as described, appears to lack explicit enforcement of linguistic constraints common in PAS analysis (e.g., each predicate typically has only one argument of each type, and an argument can only fill one semantic role). An analysis of how well the model *learns* these constraints implicitly would be beneficial. Do the model's predictions often violate these rules, and if so, in what contexts?
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. A comparative analysis of the output from the single-sequence and multi-sequence models would provide insights into *how* the Grid-RNNs improve performance. Are the errors different in kind, or simply fewer in number? In what types of sentences does the multi-sequence model consistently outperform the single-sequence model?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What proportion of zero pronouns in the NAIST Text Corpus are resolvable *intra*-sententially? This would provide a clearer understanding of the difficulty of the chosen task.
2. How are inter-sentential zero pronouns represented in the corpus? Are they explicitly marked as empty arguments, or are they simply absent? Discuss the implications of this representation for potential future work addressing inter-sentential anaphora resolution.
3.  Could you provide some examples where the Multi-Sequence model’s predictions significantly differ from those of the Single-Sequence model? This would help illustrate the benefits of capturing inter-predicate interactions.
4. Table 3 shows that the Multi-Sequence model primarily improves NOM F-measure. Can you discuss the potential reasons for this relative difference and the specific contexts where the improvement is most pronounced?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper contains several minor language issues. For example:
*  "w1:T = (w1, · · · , wT)" – consider using "w1…T" or "w1 to wT" for brevity.
*  "Inter-Zero" should be consistently hyphenated.
*  "Taira+ 08" – suggest using "Taira et al. (2008)" throughout.
* "g(ℓ)" –  be consistent with notation and clearly define all functions.
