This paper proposes neural models for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, adapting a strong English SRL model (Zhou & Xu, 2015) and extending it with Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates. The models are evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus and achieve state-of-the-art performance.

**Contributions:**

1. **Strongest:** Establishing a strong neural baseline for Japanese PAS analysis. The authors successfully adapt a state-of-the-art English SRL model to Japanese, demonstrating its effectiveness and establishing a new benchmark without relying on syntactic information. This is particularly valuable given the challenges posed by argument omission in Japanese.
2. **Significant:** Demonstrating the benefit of multi-sequential modeling via Grid-RNNs for Japanese PAS. While the gain isn't substantial, the consistent improvement provided by the Grid-RNN variant, especially in zero argument detection, validates the intuition that modeling predicate interactions is beneficial.
3. **Valuable:** Providing a comprehensive empirical evaluation on a standard Japanese SRL benchmark. The thorough experimentation and clear presentation of results contribute to the community’s understanding of current SRL performance in Japanese and provides a solid foundation for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art Performance:** The proposed models achieve significantly better performance than the previous state-of-the-art system (Ouchi et al., 2015) on the NAIST Text Corpus, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach. This is a significant achievement, especially considering the challenge of zero anaphora resolution in Japanese.
2. **Well-Motivated Problem and Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of Japanese SRL, particularly argument omission, and convincingly argues for the benefits of a neural approach that avoids reliance on potentially noisy syntactic information. The adaptation of Zhou & Xu (2015) is well-justified.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The descriptions of the models and experiments are clear and concise. The inclusion of illustrative examples (Figures 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6) is helpful.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies (network depth, residual connections) and comparisons to existing baseline methods. This provides a strong empirical validation of the proposed approach.
5. **Publicly Available Code:** Making the source code publicly available promotes reproducibility and encourages further research in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The primary contribution lies in the successful adaptation of an existing English SRL model to Japanese, rather than the introduction of a fundamentally novel technique. The Grid-RNN extension, while valuable, represents an incremental improvement. The extension is relatively straightforward and builds directly upon established work.
2. **Modest Impact of Predicate Interactions:** The improvement gained from incorporating Grid-RNNs to model predicate interactions is relatively small compared to the gains reported in Ouchi et al. (2015), which utilized syntactic information. This raises questions about the effectiveness of the proposed method in capturing complex semantic relationships. The impact is most noticeable in zero argument detection, suggesting a targeted benefit rather than a broad improvement.
3. **Missing Comparison:** The paper lacks a direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. (2015) with neural network modeling. Including this comparison is crucial to properly position the work within the current landscape of Japanese SRL research. It's unclear whether the proposed model outperforms or falls behind this recent approach.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while standard, is relatively small and may not fully represent the complexities of real-world Japanese text. Results on larger, more diverse datasets would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
5. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter selection, more details about the search process and rationale behind the chosen values would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the performance of your model against Shibata et al. (2016), including specific metrics and statistical significance testing?
2. What are the potential reasons for the relatively modest improvement achieved by the Grid-RNN extension compared to Ouchi et al. (2015)? Could further experimentation with different Grid-RNN architectures or training strategies yield more substantial gains?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the window size (C) used in the PRED feature? Did you explore a wider range of window sizes during hyperparameter tuning?
4. Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of the NAIST Text Corpus and discuss plans to evaluate the model on larger and more diverse datasets in the future?

**Additional Comments:**

- It would be helpful to include an error analysis to identify common types of errors made by the model and suggest potential directions for improvement.
- The explanation of the residual connections could be slightly more detailed.
- While the paper acknowledges the limitations of focusing on intra-sentential arguments, briefly discussing potential strategies for handling inter-sentential arguments would be valuable.
