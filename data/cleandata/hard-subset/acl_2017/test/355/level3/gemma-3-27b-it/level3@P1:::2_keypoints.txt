- Proposes neural sequence models (single- and multi-sequence with Grid-RNNs) for Japanese PAS, addressing argument omission in pro-drop languages.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, outperforming prior systems without using syntactic information.  
- Main contribution: adapting and extending Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS.  
- Secondary contribution: empirical validation that modeling predicate interactions helps, especially for zero argument resolution.  
- Public release of source code enhances reproducibility and community value.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance, notably improved zero argument identification.  
- Strength: multi-sequence Grid-RNN consistently outperforms single-sequence, with ablation showing benefit of residual connections.  
- Strength: robust performance without relying on potentially noisy syntactic parses.  
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation with sufficient experimental detail.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—primarily an adaptation of existing techniques rather than a new architecture.  
- Weakness: the gain from predicate interactions is modest compared to syntactic joint models like Ouchi et al. (2015).  
- Weakness: missing direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), which also extended prior work with neural methods.  
- Weakness: limited error analysis; lacks insight into remaining error types (long-range dependencies, ambiguity, etc.).  
- Question: need deeper analysis of why Grid-RNNs improve zero anaphora resolution—what linguistic patterns are captured?  
- Question: why was Shibata et al. (2016) excluded from comparisons, and how would results likely compare?  
- Question: what is the computational cost of Grid-RNNs versus single-sequence models, and is the accuracy gain justified?  
- Additional comment: discuss limitations of the NAIST Text Corpus and generalization challenges to other datasets/domains.  
- Additional comment: code availability is excellent and facilitates reproducibility.
