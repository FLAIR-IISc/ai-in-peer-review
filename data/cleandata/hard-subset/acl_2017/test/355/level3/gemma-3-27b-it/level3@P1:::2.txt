This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, addressing the challenge of argument omission in pro-drop languages like Japanese. The authors introduce a multi-sequence model utilizing Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates, building upon a single-sequence model inspired by Zhou & Xu (2015). Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus demonstrate that their models achieve state-of-the-art performance, exceeding previous systems even without relying on syntactic information.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful adaptation and extension of sequence modeling techniques, specifically Grid-RNNs, to the Japanese PAS task, demonstrating their effectiveness in capturing multi-predicate interactions. While not a fundamentally novel architectural innovation, the application of this architecture to a challenging linguistic problem, and the resulting performance gains, represent a significant advance. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation that explicitly modeling interactions between predicates is beneficial for Japanese PAS analysis, particularly for resolving zero arguments. Finally, the public release of source code is a valuable addition to the research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed models achieve superior results on the NAIST Text Corpus compared to existing approaches (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015). This is a strong indicator of the model's effectiveness and practical utility. The improvement, particularly on zero argument identification, is a significant contribution to addressing a key challenge in Japanese PAS analysis.
2. **Effective Use of Grid-RNNs:** The multi-sequence model with Grid-RNNs consistently outperforms the single-sequence model, demonstrating the benefits of explicitly modeling interactions between predicates. This validates the authors' intuition that considering semantic relationships between predicates aids in accurate PAS analysis. The ablation study confirming the improvement with residual connections further strengthens this argument.
3. **Independence from Syntactic Information:** The models achieve strong performance *without* relying on potentially noisy syntactic parses, a common weakness of previous Japanese PAS systems. This makes the approach more robust and potentially applicable to scenarios where accurate parsing is unavailable. 
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The clear explanation of the model architecture and experimental setup is commendable. The inclusion of relevant related work demonstrates a good understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the application of Grid-RNNs to Japanese PAS is valuable, the core contribution is an adaptation of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new architectural innovation. The paper doesn’t strongly differentiate itself beyond showing the effectiveness of a known technique in a new domain. 
2. **Modest Impact of Predicate Interactions:** The performance gain from incorporating predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs, while statistically significant, appears relatively modest compared to the improvements reported by Ouchi et al. (2015) through their joint modeling approach leveraging syntactic information. The authors should discuss this discrepancy in more detail.
3. **Missing Comparison to Recent Work:** The evaluation lacks a direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), which also extended Ouchi et al. (2015) by incorporating neural network modeling.  Including this comparison would provide a more complete assessment of the proposed model's performance relative to the state-of-the-art. It would also clarify whether the improvements are attributable to the use of Grid-RNNs specifically, or simply to the use of neural networks in general.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  A more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding the types of errors the model still makes could provide valuable insights for future improvements. Specifically, are the remaining errors related to long-range dependencies, ambiguity, or other linguistic phenomena?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper states that the improvement from Grid-RNNs is particularly noticeable for zero arguments. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* the Grid-RNNs are more effective at resolving zero anaphora compared to the single-sequence model? Are there specific linguistic features or patterns that the Grid-RNNs are better at capturing?
2.  Why was Shibata et al. (2016) not included in the experimental comparison?  How do you hypothesize your results would compare to theirs?
3. Could you discuss the computational cost of the Grid-RNN model compared to the single-sequence model? Does the increased accuracy justify the potential increase in computational complexity?



**Additional Comments:**

- It would be helpful to briefly discuss the limitations of the NAIST Text Corpus and the potential challenges of generalizing the model to other datasets or domains.
- The source code availability is excellent and greatly facilitates reproducibility and further research.
