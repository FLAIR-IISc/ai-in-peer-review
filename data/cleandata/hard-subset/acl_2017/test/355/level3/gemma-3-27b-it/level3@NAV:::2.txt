## Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

This paper presents a neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, adapting the English state-of-the-art model by Zhou & Xu (2015) and extending it with Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates. The core contribution lies in extending a single-sequential model to a multi-sequential one, aiming to improve argument identification, particularly for zero arguments, without relying heavily on syntactic parsing. The authors demonstrate significantly improved performance on the NAIST Text Corpus, surpassing existing state-of-the-art results.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the successful adaptation and evaluation of a sequence-based neural approach—specifically, Grid-RNNs—to the task of Japanese SRL. While the core idea of leveraging multi-predicate interactions isn’t entirely novel (as acknowledged by the authors and evidenced by Ouchi et al., 2015), this work provides a strong demonstration of how such interactions can be effectively modeled *without* relying on potentially noisy syntactic information. A secondary contribution is the empirical evidence showing the benefits of the Grid-RNN architecture for improving the detection of zero arguments, a particularly challenging aspect of Japanese SRL. Finally, the paper provides a solid and reproducible experimental evaluation on a standard benchmark. 

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model demonstrably outperforms the current state-of-the-art on the NAIST Text Corpus, providing convincing evidence of its effectiveness. The improvements, particularly for zero arguments, are noteworthy.
* **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of Japanese SRL, specifically the issue of zero anaphora and the limitations of relying on parser output.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The explanation of the model and experiments is clear and concise.
* **Reproducibility:** Publicly available source code enhances the value of the work and allows for further research and comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While the adaptation to Japanese and the application of Grid-RNNs are valuable, the core methodology builds heavily on existing work (Zhou & Xu, 2015, Ouchi et al., 2015). The contribution feels incremental rather than fundamentally groundbreaking.
* **Missing Comparison:** A direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. (2015) with neural network modeling, is notably absent. Including this comparison would provide a more complete picture of the proposed method's position within the existing literature.
* **Modest Impact of Interaction Modeling:** The authors acknowledge that the impact of modeling predicate interactions is smaller than reported in Ouchi et al. (2015). Further investigation into the reasons behind this difference would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the reasons why the impact of modeling predicate interactions appears smaller in your work compared to Ouchi et al. (2015)? Were there differences in data preprocessing, hyperparameter tuning, or other aspects of the experimental setup that might explain this discrepancy?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to assess the individual contributions of the Grid-RNN component and the residual connections? Which of these contributes more significantly to the observed performance gains?
3.  While you mention the applicability to multilingual SRL, could you speculate on potential challenges that might arise when applying this model to languages with different syntactic structures or argument realization patterns?

**Additional Comments:**

The discussion of related work is comprehensive. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of error analysis, highlighting the types of errors the model still makes and potential directions for future improvement.
