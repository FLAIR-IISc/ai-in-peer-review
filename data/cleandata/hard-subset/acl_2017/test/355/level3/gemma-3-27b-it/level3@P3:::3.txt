This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, utilizing Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, surpassing previous methods that relied on syntactic information. The work is a solid contribution to the field, presenting a well-executed and thoroughly evaluated model.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Application of Grid-RNNs to Japanese PAS:** The successful application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS without explicit syntactic parsing is a significant contribution. This demonstrates the power of neural networks to learn such interactions directly from the data, alleviating error propagation issues inherent in pipeline approaches.
2. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on a well-established benchmark (NAIST Text Corpus) strengthens the validity and practical impact of the proposed method.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes a comprehensive experimental setup, ablation studies (network depth, residual connections), and comparisons with strong baselines, providing a clear understanding of the model’s capabilities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently superior performance of the proposed models, especially the multi-sequence model, across various metrics (overall F-measure, individual case roles) is compelling evidence of their effectiveness. The statistical significance tests further support these findings.
2. **Clear and Accessible Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and accessible, even to researchers not specializing in Japanese NLP. The inclusion of helpful figures illustrating the model architectures (Figures 2, 3, 4, 5, 6) significantly aids understanding.
3. **Thorough Baseline Comparison:**  The inclusion of relevant and competitive baselines (Imamura et al., Ouchi et al.) provides a strong context for evaluating the improvements achieved by the proposed models.
4. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The detailed description of experimental settings (dataset splits, learning parameters, implementation details) allows for reproducibility and facilitates future research.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The analysis comparing single-sequence and multi-sequence models, as well as the discussion of the impact of network depth and residual connections, provides valuable insights into the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Underestimation of Task Complexity:** While the paper acknowledges the difficulty of zero-pronoun resolution, it doesn't fully emphasize the complexities inherent in Japanese PAS. Specifically, the ambiguity between NOM and ACC roles is a significant challenge, and the contrast with Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) – where argument identification is often more constrained – could be drawn more explicitly. Zero-pronoun ambiguity is also not fully elaborated upon.
2. **Lack of Zero Pronoun Resolvability Statistics:** A key aspect of the difficulty of this task is how often zero pronouns are even *resolvable* within the sentence. Providing statistics on the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would greatly clarify the inherent challenges of the dataset.
3. **Inter-Sentential Zero Pronoun Representation:** The paper focuses on intra-sentential arguments. It would be beneficial to clarify how inter-sentential zero pronouns are handled (or not handled) in the corpus and how they might be represented differently (e.g., as explicit empty arguments). Discussing the implications of this design choice would be valuable.
4. **Absence of Linguistic Constraints:** The model doesn't appear to enforce any explicit linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per predicate per type, an argument filling only one slot). An analysis of how well the model implicitly learns these constraints, or a discussion of why they are not enforced, is missing.
5. **Limited Output Analysis:**  A comparative analysis of the outputs produced by the single-sequence and multi-sequence models (beyond aggregate statistics) would be insightful. Identifying specific contexts where the multi-sequence model consistently outperforms the single-sequence model and understanding *why* would enhance the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What proportion of the zero pronoun arguments in the NAIST Text Corpus are intra-sententially resolvable?
2.  How are inter-sentential zero pronouns represented in the NAIST Text Corpus (e.g., are they explicitly marked as empty arguments)? What are the implications of this representation for the model?
3.  Have you investigated whether the model implicitly learns any linguistic constraints related to argument structure (e.g., one argument per type per predicate)? If so, what are the findings?
4.  Can you provide examples of sentences where the multi-sequence model significantly improves performance over the single-sequence model, and analyze the underlying reasons for this improvement?
5.  Table 3 shows the Multi-Seq model achieves NOM improvement relative to Single-Seq. Can you discuss contexts/examples where these improvements are most pronounced, and hypothesize why?
