## Review for ACL 2017 - Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

This paper presents a novel approach to Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, leveraging grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs) to model interactions between multiple predicates without relying on explicit syntactic information. The authors demonstrate that their model surpasses the performance of state-of-the-art Japanese PAS analyzers that *do* utilize syntactic parsing, a significant accomplishment. The focus on handling argument omission (zero pronouns), a particularly challenging aspect of Japanese, further strengthens the contribution.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that effective Japanese PAS analysis can be achieved *without* relying on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. This is achieved through the novel application of Grid-RNNs, which effectively model multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. A secondary contribution lies in the detailed empirical evaluation, demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines on the NAIST Text Corpus, particularly in the challenging zero-pronoun identification task. Finally, the thorough description of the model architecture and experimental setup provides a valuable resource for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Significance of Approach:** The core idea of circumventing the need for syntactic parsing in Japanese PAS analysis is highly innovative. Japanese, with its free word order and frequent argument omission, often suffers from parser errors that propagate into downstream semantic analysis.  This work successfully addresses this problem with a purely neural approach. The fact that the proposed model outperforms syntactic-parser-dependent systems is a strong indicator of its effectiveness and potential impact.

2. **Effective Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions:** The use of Grid-RNNs appears to be a key factor in the success of the model. The ability to capture relationships between predicates without explicit syntactic links is crucial for handling argument omission and resolving ambiguities. The experimental results strongly support this claim, demonstrating the superior performance of the multi-sequence model compared to the single-sequence baseline.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a rigorous evaluation of their model, comparing it against established baselines on a standard dataset. The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., analyzing the impact of residual connections) and per-case-role analysis provides valuable insights into the model's strengths and weaknesses. The use of statistical significance testing (bootstrap resampling) further strengthens the validity of the results.

4. **Clear and Detailed Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the intricacies of the proposed model. The diagrams are helpful and well-integrated into the text. The authors provide sufficient details about the implementation and experimental setup, allowing for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **English Fluency and Typos:** While the paper is generally well-written, there are numerous instances of minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout the text. While not critical, these issues detract from the overall clarity and professionalism of the presentation. Specific corrections are noted throughout the document (pages 1-8).

2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results demonstrate overall performance improvements, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding the types of errors the model still makes could provide insights into potential areas for future improvement.  Specifically, analyzing cases where the model fails on zero anaphora resolution could be particularly informative.

3. **Clarity of Table 2:** The use of boldface in Table 2 is not explained and is therefore confusing. It is unclear what information the boldface is intended to convey. Clarification is needed regarding the purpose of this formatting choice.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you clarify the purpose of the boldface in Table 2?

2. Would it be possible to provide examples of cases where the model still struggles with zero anaphora resolution?  Illustrating this with examples would aid in understanding the remaining challenges.

3. Figure 6 shows the accusative marker included with the predicate in the input layer. Could you elaborate on why this is included specifically, and how this context influences the Grid-RNNs' ability to model predicate interactions? A short discussion connecting this to the PRED-context concept in Section 3.1 would be helpful.

**Additional Comments:**

- Numerous minor typographical errors have been noted throughout the paper, ranging from missing articles ("the") to incorrect verb tenses.
- Consider adding a discussion of computational complexity, especially concerning the Grid-RNNs, which may be more resource-intensive than simpler recurrent architectures.
