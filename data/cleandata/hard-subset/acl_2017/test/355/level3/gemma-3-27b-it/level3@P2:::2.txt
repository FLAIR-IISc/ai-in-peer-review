This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, extending existing work by incorporating Grid-RNNs to model interactions between predicates. The models are evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance, exceeding previous results including Ouchi et al. (2015).

The primary contribution of this work is the adaptation and extension of sequence modeling techniques, specifically Grid-RNNs, to the task of Japanese PAS analysis, demonstrating their effectiveness in capturing multi-predicate interactions. While the core idea of leveraging RNNs for this task isn’t entirely novel, the application to Japanese, a pro-drop language where argument identification is particularly challenging, and the successful integration of Grid-RNNs, represent a valuable contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over existing baselines, particularly in zero argument identification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The models achieve state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, demonstrably improving upon previous methods. The reported F-measures, particularly the gains in zero argument identification, are significant and validate the approach. The statistical significance testing using bootstrapping further strengthens these claims.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of Japanese PAS analysis, specifically focusing on argument omission and the need to model interactions between predicates. The motivation for using Grid-RNNs to address these challenges is well-explained.
3. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors conduct thorough experiments, varying network depth and including/excluding residual connections. The results provide insights into the effectiveness of different architectural choices and provide a good understanding of the models' behavior. The ablation study regarding residual connections is particularly valuable.
4. **Clear and Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The figures and tables are informative and effectively illustrate the proposed models and experimental results. The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches.
5. **Publicly Available Code:** Making the source code publicly available promotes reproducibility and encourages further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the reported results are strong, the core contribution is more of an adaptation and extension of existing sequence modeling techniques (Zhou & Xu, 2015) rather than a fundamentally novel approach. The Grid-RNN component, while effective, builds directly upon established architectures.
2. **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The paper lacks a direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. (2015) using neural network modeling. Including this baseline would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed models relative to the current state-of-the-art. It is unclear how much of the performance gain is attributable to the Grid-RNNs vs. simply adopting a neural network approach.
3. **Modest Impact of Predicate Interactions:** The reported impact of modeling predicate interactions with Grid-RNNs, while statistically significant, appears relatively modest. The paper acknowledges that the improvement is not as substantial as reported in Ouchi et al. (2015), raising questions about the extent to which the Grid-RNNs truly capture the benefits of multi-predicate modeling. A more detailed analysis of *which* predicate interactions are most effectively modeled would be beneficial.
4. **Dataset Limitation:** Evaluation is limited to a single dataset (NAIST Text Corpus 1.5). While this is a standard benchmark, it would be beneficial to explore the generalizability of the models to other Japanese corpora or different domains.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the specific predicate interactions that are most effectively captured by the Grid-RNNs? Are there specific linguistic patterns or semantic relationships that the model seems to learn?
2.  Why was Shibata et al. (2016) not included in the baseline comparison? How do you anticipate your models would perform relative to their results?
3.  Given the relatively modest impact of predicate interactions, do you have any hypotheses about why the gains from Grid-RNNs are not more substantial? Could factors like the limited dataset size or the specific architectural choices be contributing?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work could be slightly expanded to include more recent advances in neural SRL.
*   The authors mention applying the models to multilingual SRL as future work. Briefly outlining the challenges and potential approaches for adapting the models to other languages would strengthen this discussion.
