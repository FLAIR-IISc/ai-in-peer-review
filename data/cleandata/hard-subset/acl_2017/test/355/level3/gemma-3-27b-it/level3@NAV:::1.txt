## Review: Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

This paper presents a novel approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-RNNs, achieving state-of-the-art results without relying on explicit syntactic information. The core contribution lies in effectively modeling multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, which is particularly beneficial for handling the frequent phenomenon of dropped arguments (zero pronouns) in Japanese. A secondary contribution is the detailed implementation and experimental validation of both single-sequence and multi-sequence models, providing a clear baseline and demonstrating the improvement gained by incorporating grid-type RNNs. The focus on zero pronoun resolution, a challenging aspect of Japanese PAS, is also a significant strength.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a thorough explanation of the proposed models and experiments. The results consistently outperform previous state-of-the-art systems, showcasing the efficacy of the approach. The ablation studies, demonstrating the impact of network depth and residual connections, further strengthen the findings. The detailed error analysis per case role (Table 3) adds valuable insight into the model's strengths and weaknesses. 

However, the paper is not without minor flaws. Numerous typographical errors and instances of awkward phrasing exist throughout (detailed corrections provided separately). While not hindering comprehension, they detract from the overall polish. A more intuitive illustration of the “PRED-context” point (as suggested) using the example in Figure 6, where the accusative marker is included with the verb, would be helpful. Furthermore, the use of boldface in Table 2 is unclear and needs clarification. It’s currently difficult to discern the significance of the bolded values.

Despite these minor issues, the technical contribution of this work is substantial. The ability to achieve competitive results *without* syntactic parsing is particularly noteworthy and has implications for resource-poor languages or scenarios where parsing is unreliable. The clear writing and thorough experimentation make this a valuable addition to the field.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please clarify the purpose of using boldface in Table 2?
2.  Could you provide a more intuitive example demonstrating the “PRED-context” concept, potentially using the example provided in Figure 6 where the accusative marker is included?
