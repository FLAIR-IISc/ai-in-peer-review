This paper introduces a neural model using grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs) for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, aiming to overcome the limitations of traditional methods that heavily rely on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. The core contribution lies in the model’s ability to automatically induce features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, achieving state-of-the-art performance *without* relying on syntactic information. A secondary, yet significant, contribution is the detailed analysis and improved performance on zero pronoun resolution – a crucial and challenging aspect of Japanese PAS due to frequent argument omission. Finally, the comprehensive experimental evaluation on the NAIST Text Corpus, alongside thorough ablation studies examining the effects of network depth and residual connections, strengthens the validity and understanding of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Effectiveness of the Grid-RNN Architecture:** The use of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions is a compelling approach. The results clearly demonstrate that this architecture effectively captures relationships between predicates, enabling the model to outperform existing state-of-the-art systems that rely on syntactic information. This is particularly noteworthy given the error propagation issues often associated with pipeline approaches.
2. **Addressing a Core Challenge in Japanese NLP:** The paper directly tackles the problem of zero anaphora resolution, a key challenge in Japanese PAS. The significant performance gains observed on this task indicate that the model effectively leverages contextual information and inter-predicate relationships to identify omitted arguments. This focus on a specific linguistic challenge strengthens the paper’s impact.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model, including comparisons to strong baselines, ablation studies, and analysis of performance across different argument types (Dep, Zero, NOM, ACC, DAT). The inclusion of statistical significance testing (bootstrap resampling) adds to the rigor of the evaluation.  The detailed results in Table 1 and Table 3 provide a clear understanding of the model’s strengths and weaknesses.
4. **Detailed Implementation Description:** The paper offers a detailed description of the model architecture, training process, and hyperparameter settings. This level of detail facilitates reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work. The clear explanation of the single-sequence and multi-sequence models, alongside the Grid Layer architecture, is well presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **English Fluency and Minor Typos:** While the technical content is strong, the paper suffers from occasional awkward phrasing and minor typographical errors. Though these do not impede understanding, they detract from the overall polish and readability. (Specific examples are noted in "Additional Comments" below.)
2. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are compelling, a more detailed qualitative error analysis would be beneficial. Examining specific examples where the model fails could provide valuable insights into its limitations and suggest avenues for future improvement.  Understanding *why* the model makes certain errors is crucial for advancing the field.
3. **Illustrative Examples Could Be More Focused:** While the paper does utilize illustrative examples, the connection between these examples and the model’s mechanism isn’t always immediately clear. For instance, in Figure 6, demonstrating how the context around the predicate (including accusative markers) is captured would provide a more intuitive understanding of the model's input processing.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  In Table 2, the values in boldface are not explained. Could you clarify the meaning of the bolding in this table?
2. Could you provide examples of specific failure cases for the multi-sequence model? What types of zero anaphora resolutions are most challenging for the model?
3. The paper focuses on intra-sentential arguments. Have you considered approaches to handle inter-sentential dependencies, and if so, what challenges did you encounter?
