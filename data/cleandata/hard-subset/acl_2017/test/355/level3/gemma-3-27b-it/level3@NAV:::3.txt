## Review for Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

This paper presents a novel approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis utilizing Grid-RNNs to model interactions between multiple predicates. The authors address the issue of error propagation in pipeline systems by proposing a joint neural model that doesn't rely on syntactic information. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that Grid-RNNs can effectively capture these inter-predicate relationships directly from word sequences, achieving state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of this approach, particularly for resolving zero pronouns, a notoriously difficult aspect of Japanese PAS. Finally, the thorough experimental setup and comparison to existing methods provides a strong baseline for future work in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The model demonstrably outperforms previous state-of-the-art systems on a well-established benchmark (NAIST Text Corpus) without relying on potentially noisy syntactic parsing. This is a significant practical advancement.
2. **Effective Use of Grid-RNNs:**  The application of Grid-RNNs to model predicate interactions appears well-motivated and successful, effectively capturing dependencies that single-sequence models miss. The performance gains, particularly for zero pronoun resolution, support this claim.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a clear experimental setup, ablation studies (network depth, residual connections), and a detailed comparison with baselines, offering strong evidence for the proposed method’s efficacy.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and accessible, even to those not deeply familiar with Japanese linguistics. Figures are helpful in illustrating the model architecture and task description.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Task Complexity:** The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the challenges inherent in Japanese PAS, particularly the ambiguity between nominative and accusative cases, and how this contrasts with the challenges in Semantic Role Labeling (SRL).  The severity of zero-pronoun ambiguity is understated.
2. **Zero Pronoun Resolvability Statistics:**  The authors mention the difficulty of zero-pronoun resolution, but a crucial piece of information is missing: what proportion of zero pronouns in the dataset are *intra-sententially resolvable*? This would provide a clearer picture of the task difficulty and allow for a more informed assessment of the model’s performance. 
3. **Inter-Sentential Zero Pronoun Representation:** Clarification is needed on how inter-sentential zero pronouns are represented (are they simply unlabelled, or is there an explicit "empty argument" representation?) and the implications of this representation choice.
4. **Linguistic Constraints:** The model doesn't explicitly enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per predicate per type, an argument filling only one slot). An analysis of how well the model *learns* these constraints implicitly would be valuable.
5. **Output Analysis:** A comparative analysis of the outputs between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models – showing *where* they differ systematically – is missing.  Table 3 indicates NOM improvements, but a qualitative analysis would be insightful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What percentage of zero pronouns in the NAIST corpus are actually resolvable within the same sentence?
2. How are inter-sentential zero pronouns handled in your data representation, and what is the rationale for this choice?
3. Could you provide some example cases where the Multi-Seq model correctly identifies an argument that the Single-Seq model fails on, illustrating the benefits of modeling inter-predicate interactions?
4. Have you investigated methods to incorporate linguistic constraints into the model architecture (e.g., through regularization or constraints on the output layer)?
