**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a new approach to Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis using neural networks. The authors introduce a single-sequence model that uses bidirectional RNNs to induce context-sensitive representations from a sequence of argument candidates of a predicate. They also extend this model to a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that their models achieve state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Extension of single-sequence model to multi-sequence model**: The authors propose a multi-sequence model that captures interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence using Grid-RNNs.
2. **State-of-the-art performance without syntactic information**: The experiments show that their models achieve state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus without using syntactic information.
3. **Effective capture of long distance dependencies**: The results suggest that the neural models effectively capture long distance dependencies using RNNs that can encode the context in the entire sentence.

**Strengths**

1. **Significant improvement in zero argument identification**: The multi-sequence model achieves a considerable improvement in zero argument identification, which is one of the problematic issues in Japanese PAS analysis.
2. **Robust performance on different case roles**: The results show that the neural models achieve robust performance on different case roles, including nominative, accusative, and dative cases.
3. **Effective use of word sequence information**: The models demonstrate the effectiveness of using word sequence information to induce context-sensitive representations for PAS analysis.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty in the method**: The authors extend the single-sequence model to a multi-sequence model, but the underlying method is not fundamentally new.
2. **Limited comparison with other models**: The paper does not compare the results with other models that also extend Ouchi et al. (2015) with neural-network modeling.
3. **No analysis of error types**: The paper does not provide an analysis of error types, which could help to identify areas for further improvement.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the Grid-RNNs capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence?
2. How do you plan to extend this work to multilingual SRL tasks?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the error types and how to improve the performance further?
