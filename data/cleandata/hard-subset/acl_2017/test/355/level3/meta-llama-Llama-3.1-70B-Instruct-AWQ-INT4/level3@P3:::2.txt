This paper proposes new Japanese Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) prediction models by adapting the English state-of-the-art model (Zhou & Xu, 2015) and extending it to capture interactions between arguments of multiple predicates using Grid-RNNs. The authors evaluate their models on a standard Japanese SRL benchmark and achieve significantly better performance than the current state of the art.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. The authors propose a neural model that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs, which leads to improved performance on Japanese SRL tasks.
2. They extend the single-sequential model to a multi-sequential model, which allows the model to capture interactions between arguments of multiple predicates.
3. They demonstrate that their neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance on Japanese SRL tasks without relying on syntactic information.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their models and experiments, making it easy to follow their work.
2. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their models on a standard benchmark, which provides a strong evidence for their claims.
3. They provide a detailed comparison with previous work, which helps to understand the contributions of their work.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. The main contribution of this work appears to be an extension from single- to multi-sequential modeling rather than a fundamentally novel method.
2. The impact of modeling predicate interactions is smaller than reported in Ouchi et al. (2015), which may indicate that the improvement is not as significant as claimed.
3. The authors do not provide a comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. with neural-network modeling.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more details on how the Grid-RNNs capture the interactions between arguments of multiple predicates?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of error propagation in the pipeline processing of syntactic information?
3. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with Shibata et al. (2016) to clarify the contributions of your work?

Additional comments:

1. The authors may want to consider providing more visualizations of their models and results to help illustrate their points.
2. The related work section could be more comprehensive, including more recent work on Japanese SRL tasks.
3. The authors may want to consider providing more details on the implementation of their models, such as the hyperparameter settings and the optimization methods used.
