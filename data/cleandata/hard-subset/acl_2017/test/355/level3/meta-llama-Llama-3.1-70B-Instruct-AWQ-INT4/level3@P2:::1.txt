**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, which is a basic semantic analysis task. The model uses grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs) to automatically induce features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information of a sentence. The authors claim that their model outperforms the state-of-the-art Japanese PAS analyzer without using syntactic information.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Effective use of Grid-RNNs for PAS analysis**: The authors propose a novel neural architecture that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
2. **State-of-the-art performance without syntactic information**: The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS analysis without using explicit syntactic structure, which is a challenging task.
3. **Improved performance on zero pronouns**: The authors show that their multi-sequence model, which considers all predicates in a sentence, achieves the best performance on zero pronouns, which are frequent and challenging in Japanese.

**Strengths**

1. **Sophisticated neural application**: The authors propose a sophisticated neural architecture that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs.
2. **Clear and detailed description**: The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the implementation and experimental results, making it easy to follow and understand.
3. **Special focus on zero pronouns**: The authors focus on dropped arguments (zero pronouns), which are frequent and challenging in Japanese, and demonstrate improved performance on this task.
4. **Strong experimental results**: The authors provide strong experimental results, including a comparison with state-of-the-art systems, to support their claims.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Minor typos and English fluency issues**: The paper contains several minor typos and English fluency issues that need to be corrected.
2. **Lack of illustration**: The paper could benefit from additional illustrations, such as a figure to illustrate the PRED-context point.
