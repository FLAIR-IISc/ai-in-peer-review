This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, which extends the single-sequence model to capture interactions between arguments of multiple predicates using Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks (Grid-RNNs). The main contributions of this work are:

1. **Extension of the single-sequence model to capture multi-predicate interactions**: The authors propose a Grid-RNN-based model that takes into account the interactions between arguments of multiple predicates in a sentence, which is a key challenge in Japanese PAS analysis.
2. **State-of-the-art performance on a standard Japanese SRL benchmark**: The proposed model achieves significantly better performance than the current state-of-the-art system on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, a widely used benchmark for Japanese PAS analysis.
3. **Effective capture of long-distance dependencies**: The model's ability to capture long-distance dependencies is demonstrated through its improved performance on zero arguments, which is a challenging task in Japanese PAS analysis.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. **Well-structured and well-motivated**: The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear motivation for their work, including a thorough review of the related literature.
2. **Improved accuracy over the previous state-of-the-art system**: The proposed model achieves a significant improvement in accuracy over the previous state-of-the-art system, demonstrating its effectiveness.
3. **Grid-RNN variant yields additional gains**: The Grid-RNN variant of the model yields slight additional gains, mainly by improving the detection of zero arguments, which is a challenging task in Japanese PAS analysis.
4. **Effective use of residual connections**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of using residual connections in their model, which helps to improve the performance of deeper models.

The weaknesses of this paper are:

1. **Lack of comparison with Shibata et al. (2016)**: The authors do not compare their model with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. with neural-network modeling, making it difficult to assess the novelty of their approach.
2. **Impact of modeling predicate interactions is smaller than reported in Ouchi et al. (2015)**: The authors report a smaller impact of modeling predicate interactions compared to Ouchi et al. (2015), which may indicate that the contribution of this work is not as significant as claimed.
3. **Limited novelty of the proposed method**: The main contribution of this work appears to be an extension from single- to multi-sequential modeling rather than a fundamentally novel method, which may limit its impact.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with Shibata et al. (2016) and explain how your approach differs from theirs?
2. How do you plan to address the limited novelty of the proposed method, and what future directions do you envision for this research?
3. Can you provide more insights into the impact of modeling predicate interactions on the performance of the model, and how it compares to Ouchi et al. (2015)?

Additional Comments:

* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their model and its components.
* The use of Grid-RNNs is an interesting approach, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in capturing multi-predicate interactions.
* The paper could benefit from more visualizations, such as diagrams or plots, to help illustrate the model's architecture and its performance.
