This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks (Grid-RNNs) and compares it to a single-sequence RNN model. The authors demonstrate that their models achieve state-of-the-art results on a well-established Japanese PAS task without relying on syntactic information.

**Contributions:**

1. The authors propose a novel neural architecture for Japanese PAS analysis that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs.
2. They demonstrate that their models achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without relying on syntactic information.
3. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the performance of their models, including comparisons with baseline models and analysis of the effects of network depth and residual connections.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper presents a solidly executed work that achieves state-of-the-art results on a well-established Japanese PAS task.
2. The authors provide a clear and accessible presentation of their work, including helpful figures that illustrate the architecture of their models.
3. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed neural architecture in capturing multi-predicate interactions, which is a key challenge in Japanese PAS analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the task complexity, including the ambiguity between NOM and ACC and the contrast with semantic role labeling (SRL), especially zero-pronoun ambiguity.
2. The authors do not provide statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable, which would help to clarify the task difficulty.
3. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how inter-sentential zero pronouns are represented (e.g., no marking vs. explicit empty argument) and the implications of this representation.
4. The model appears not to enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per type per predicate; an argument filling only one slot), and the authors do not provide an analysis of how well the model learns these constraints.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable to clarify the task difficulty?
2. How do you represent inter-sentential zero pronouns (e.g., no marking vs. explicit empty argument), and what are the implications of this representation?
3. Can you provide an analysis of how well the model learns linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per type per predicate; an argument filling only one slot)?
4. Can you provide a comparative output analysis between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models to show whether the outputs are similar or differ systematically and in which contexts?

**Additional Comments:**

1. The presentation quality is good, but there are minor language issues throughout the paper. A list of specific typographical/casing corrections would be helpful.
2. Including SRL results would be valuable, but this would require additional work.
3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relative differences and contexts of improvement between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models, especially with regard to the NOM case.
