**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks (Grid-RNNs). The authors introduce a multi-sequence model that captures interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence, improving the performance of PAS analysis, particularly for zero arguments. The model is evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 and achieves state-of-the-art results without using syntactic information.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Joint Neural Modeling**: The authors propose a joint neural modeling approach that captures interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence, improving the performance of PAS analysis.
2. **Grid-RNNs**: The authors introduce a Grid-RNN architecture that effectively models multi-predicate interactions, achieving state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5.
3. **Improved Performance on Zero Arguments**: The authors demonstrate that their multi-sequence model significantly improves the performance on zero arguments, a challenging task in Japanese PAS analysis.

**Strengths**

1. **State-of-the-art Results**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without using syntactic information.
2. **Improved Performance on Zero Arguments**: The authors demonstrate that their multi-sequence model significantly improves the performance on zero arguments.
3. **Effective Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions**: The authors propose a Grid-RNN architecture that effectively models multi-predicate interactions, improving the performance of PAS analysis.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Linguistic Constraints**: The authors do not enforce linguistic constraints, such as one argument per type per predicate, which may lead to overfitting or incorrect predictions.
2. **Limited Analysis of Model Behavior**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of how the model learns linguistic constraints or how it handles different types of arguments.
3. **Minor Language Issues**: The authors note minor language issues, such as typographical errors, which may affect the clarity of the paper.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the lack of linguistic constraints in their model?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of how the model learns linguistic constraints or how it handles different types of arguments?
3. How do the authors plan to extend their model to handle inter-sentential zero pronouns?

**Additional Comments**

The authors may consider providing a more detailed analysis of the performance of their model on different types of arguments, such as NOM, ACC, and DAT. Additionally, the authors may consider comparing their model with other state-of-the-art models on different datasets to demonstrate its generalizability.
