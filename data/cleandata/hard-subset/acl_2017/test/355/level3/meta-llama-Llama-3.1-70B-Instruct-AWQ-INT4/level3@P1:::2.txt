**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, which is a fundamental task in natural language processing. The authors extend the single-sequential model by incorporating Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks (Grid-RNNs) to capture interactions between arguments of multiple predicates. The proposed model is evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus, a standard Japanese SRL benchmark, and achieves significantly better performance than the current state-of-the-art system.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. **Extension of the single-sequential model to multi-sequential modeling**: The authors adapt the English state-of-the-art model (Zhou & Xu, 2015) to Japanese PAS analysis and extend it to capture interactions between arguments of multiple predicates using Grid-RNNs.
2. **Improved performance on Japanese SRL benchmark**: The proposed model achieves significantly better performance than the current state-of-the-art system on the NAIST Text Corpus.
3. **Effective capture of predicate interactions**: The Grid-RNN variant yields slight additional gains, mainly by improving detection of zero arguments.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Well-structured and well-motivated paper**: The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, and the paper is well-organized and easy to follow.
2. **Improved performance over the previous state-of-the-art system**: The proposed model achieves significantly better performance than the current state-of-the-art system on the NAIST Text Corpus.
3. **Effective use of Grid-RNNs**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of Grid-RNNs in capturing interactions between arguments of multiple predicates.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Lack of comparison with Shibata et al. (2016)**: The authors do not compare their model with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. with neural-network modeling.
2. **Impact of modeling predicate interactions is smaller than reported in Ouchi et al. (2015)**: The authors report a smaller impact of modeling predicate interactions compared to Ouchi et al. (2015).
3. **Limited novelty of the method**: The main contribution of the paper appears to be an extension from single- to multi-sequential modeling rather than a fundamentally novel method.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with Shibata et al. (2016) and explain why your model outperforms theirs?
2. How do you plan to address the limited novelty of the method, and what future directions do you propose for improving the model?
3. Can you provide more insights into the impact of modeling predicate interactions and why it is smaller than reported in Ouchi et al. (2015)?
