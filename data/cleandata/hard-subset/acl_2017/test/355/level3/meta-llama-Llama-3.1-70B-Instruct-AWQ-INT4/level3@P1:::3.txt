**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural modeling approach for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, which is a fundamental task in natural language processing. The authors introduce a joint neural model using grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs) to capture multi-predicate interactions, and compare its performance with a single-sequence RNN model. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, a well-established Japanese PAS task.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. **Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions**: The paper proposes a novel neural architecture using Grid-RNNs to capture the interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence, which is a key challenge in Japanese PAS analysis.
2. **State-of-the-art results on Japanese PAS task**: The proposed model achieves the best results on the NAIST Text Corpus, outperforming previous state-of-the-art models, including those that rely on syntactic information.
3. **Robust learning of contextual information**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed model can effectively learn contextual information from word sequences, without relying on syntactic information, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Strengths of the Submission**

1. **Solid execution of the work**: The paper presents a well-executed study, with a clear description of the proposed model, experimental settings, and results.
2. **Accessible presentation**: The paper is mostly accessible to non-Japanese specialists, with helpful figures and explanations of the task and proposed model.
3. **State-of-the-art results**: The proposed model achieves the best results on the NAIST Text Corpus, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses of the Submission**

1. **Lack of emphasis on task complexity**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the complexity of the Japanese PAS task, including the ambiguity between NOM and ACC, and the contrast with semantic role labeling (SRL), especially zero-pronoun ambiguity.
2. **Limited discussion of inter-sentential zero pronouns**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns and the implications of this representation.
3. **No analysis of linguistic constraints**: The paper does not provide an analysis of how well the proposed model learns linguistic constraints, such as one argument per type per predicate, and an argument filling only one slot.

**Questions to Authors**

1. **Statistics on zero pronouns**: Could you provide statistics on what proportion of zero pronouns are intra-sententially resolvable to clarify the task difficulty?
2. **Representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns**: Could you clarify how inter-sentential zero pronouns are represented in the proposed model, and discuss the implications of this representation?
3. **Linguistic constraints**: Could you provide an analysis of how well the proposed model learns linguistic constraints, such as one argument per type per predicate, and an argument filling only one slot?
4. **Comparative output analysis**: Could you provide a comparative output analysis between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models to show whether the outputs are similar or differ systematically and in which contexts?

**Additional Comments**

1. **Minor language issues**: There are some minor language issues, such as typographical errors and casing inconsistencies, which should be corrected.
2. **Including SRL results**: Including SRL results would be valuable, but this is acknowledged as additional work.
3. **Discussion of relative differences**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relative differences between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models, and the contexts in which the Multi-Seq model improves performance.
