This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-RNNs. The authors compare the performance of their proposed model with a single-sequence RNN model, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach in capturing multi-predicate interactions. The paper presents solidly executed work, achieving state-of-the-art results on the well-established Japanese PAS task.

The presentation is mostly accessible to non-Japanese specialists, with helpful figures that facilitate understanding of the proposed models. However, the authors could further emphasize the complexity of the task, particularly the ambiguity between nominative (NOM) and accusative (ACC) cases and the contrast with Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), especially zero-pronoun ambiguity.

The authors report impressive results, with the multi-sequence model outperforming the single-sequence model and the baseline models. However, it would be beneficial to provide more insights into the performance of the models, such as the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable. This information would help clarify the task difficulty and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results.

The representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns is not explicitly discussed in the paper. The authors should provide clarification on whether the model uses explicit empty arguments or no marking for inter-sentential zero pronouns and discuss the implications of their approach.

The proposed model appears not to enforce linguistic constraints, such as one argument per type per predicate or an argument filling only one slot. The authors should analyze how well the model learns these constraints and discuss the potential benefits and limitations of their approach.

A comparative output analysis between the single-sequence and multi-sequence models would be valuable in understanding whether the outputs are similar or differ systematically and in which contexts. This analysis could provide insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each model and help identify areas for further improvement.

The results in Table 3 show that the multi-sequence model mainly improves the performance on the NOM case. The authors should discuss the relative differences and contexts of improvement to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results.

Including SRL results would be valuable, but it is acknowledged as additional work. The presentation quality is good, but there are minor language issues. A list of specific typographical/casing corrections is provided below:

* "In this paper, we firstly introduce a basic model using RNNs..." should be "In this paper, we first introduce a basic model using RNNs..."
* "The word boundaries annotated to the NAIST Text Corpus..." should be "The word boundaries annotated in the NAIST Text Corpus..."
* "The dimensions dword and dmark of the embeddings are set to 32." should be "The dimensions of the word embeddings (dword) and mark embeddings (dmark) are set to 32."

**Main Contributions:**

1. The paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach for Japanese PAS analysis using Grid-RNNs, achieving state-of-the-art results on the well-established Japanese PAS task.
2. The authors compare the performance of their proposed model with a single-sequence RNN model, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach in capturing multi-predicate interactions.
3. The paper presents a solidly executed work, with a clear presentation and helpful figures that facilitate understanding of the proposed models.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to Japanese PAS analysis, leveraging the strengths of Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their proposed model, comparing its performance with a single-sequence RNN model and baseline models.
3. The paper presents a clear and well-organized presentation, with helpful figures that facilitate understanding of the proposed models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the task complexity, particularly the ambiguity between NOM and ACC cases and the contrast with SRL.
2. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns, which could impact the model's performance.
3. The proposed model appears not to enforce linguistic constraints, which could lead to potential errors or inconsistencies in the output.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more insights into the performance of the models, such as the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable?
2. How do you represent inter-sentential zero pronouns in your model, and what are the implications of your approach?
3. Can you analyze how well the model learns linguistic constraints, such as one argument per type per predicate or an argument filling only one slot?
