## Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

This paper presents a neural approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, adapting a state-of-the-art English model (Zhou & Xu, 2015) and extending it with Grid-RNNs to better capture interactions between predicates. The core contribution lies in moving from single-sequential modeling to a multi-sequential approach for improved performance, particularly in identifying zero arguments. The work is well-motivated, addressing the challenge of argument omission in Japanese, and the paper is clearly structured and well-written. The experimental results on the NAIST Text Corpus demonstrate significant improvements over the previous state-of-the-art, validating the effectiveness of the proposed model.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the successful adaptation and extension of existing sequential modeling techniques to the specific challenges of Japanese SRL, specifically the issue of zero arguments. While the Grid-RNN extension isn't fundamentally novel, its application to this task and the demonstrated performance gains are valuable. A secondary contribution is the strong empirical validation on a standard benchmark, providing a solid foundation for future research. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves significantly better performance than the current state-of-the-art on the NAIST Text Corpus, demonstrating its practical effectiveness. The improvements are statistically significant, as indicated by the bootstrap resampling method.
2. **Addresses a Key Challenge:** The paper directly tackles the problem of zero argument identification, a significant hurdle in Japanese SRL, and shows notable improvements in this area.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is easy to follow, with a logical flow and clear explanations of the proposed model and experiments.
4. **Solid Baseline Comparison:** The comparison against established baseline models (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015) provides a strong context for evaluating the contribution of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** The core method builds heavily on existing work (Zhou & Xu, 2015) and the Grid-RNN component, while effective, isn’t a fundamentally new architectural innovation. The contribution is more in the adaptation and application than in groundbreaking novelty.
2. **Limited Comparison:** The paper lacks a comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. (2015) with neural network modeling. This omission makes it difficult to assess the relative benefits of the proposed approach compared to other recent advancements.
3. **Impact of Interactions:** The reported impact of modeling predicate interactions, while positive, appears smaller than reported in Ouchi et al. (2015). A more detailed analysis of *where* and *why* the Grid-RNNs are most effective would strengthen the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the cases where the Grid-RNNs contribute the most significant improvements? Are there specific types of predicate interactions that benefit most from this modeling approach?
2. Why was Shibata et al. (2016) not included in the comparison? How do you anticipate your results would compare to their work?
3.  The paper mentions exploring large-scale unlabeled data in future work. Could you elaborate on the potential strategies for leveraging such data within your proposed framework?
