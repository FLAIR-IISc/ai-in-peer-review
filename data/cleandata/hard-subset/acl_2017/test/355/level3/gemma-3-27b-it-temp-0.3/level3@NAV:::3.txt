## Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

This paper presents a neural approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenge of zero pronoun resolution. The authors propose a multi-sequence model utilizing Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates, aiming to improve upon existing state-of-the-art methods that rely heavily on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing. The work is solidly executed, achieving new state-of-the-art results on the established NAIST Text Corpus.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the successful application of Grid-RNNs to the Japanese PAS task, demonstrating their effectiveness in modeling multi-predicate interactions *without* relying on explicit syntactic information. A secondary contribution is the strong empirical performance, establishing a new baseline for this task. Finally, the thorough experimental setup and clear presentation of results are valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art Performance:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing methods on a well-known benchmark, providing strong evidence of its effectiveness. The reported improvements, particularly for zero arguments, are significant.
2. **Effective Architecture:** The use of Grid-RNNs appears well-motivated and effectively captures the necessary inter-predicate relationships for improved PAS analysis. The ablation studies (Table 2) further support the benefit of deeper networks and residual connections.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and accessible, with helpful figures illustrating the model architectures. The experimental setup is clearly described, allowing for reproducibility.
4. **Focus on a Challenging Problem:** The paper directly addresses the core difficulty of Japanese PAS – zero pronoun resolution – and demonstrates improvements in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Task Complexity Understated:** While the paper acknowledges the difficulty of zero pronoun resolution, it doesn’t fully emphasize the ambiguity inherent in Japanese PAS. The ambiguity between nominative (NOM) and accusative (ACC) roles, and how this differs from the challenges in Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), should be discussed more thoroughly.
2. **Zero Pronoun Resolvability:** The authors should provide statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable in the NAIST corpus. This would provide a clearer understanding of the inherent difficulty of the task and contextualize the reported results.
3. **Inter-Sentential Zero Pronoun Representation:** The paper briefly mentions inter-sentential zero pronouns but doesn’t clarify how these are represented (e.g., as no marking or an explicit empty argument). A discussion of the implications of this representation choice would be beneficial.
4. **Linguistic Constraints:** The model doesn’t appear to enforce linguistic constraints (e.g., one argument per type per predicate, an argument filling only one slot). An analysis of how well the model *learns* these constraints implicitly would be valuable.
5. **Comparative Output Analysis:** A comparative analysis of the outputs of the single-sequence and multi-sequence models, beyond the aggregate F-measure, would be insightful. Identifying *where* the multi-sequence model improves and *how* its outputs differ systematically would strengthen the findings. The discussion of Table 3 could be expanded to provide such context.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What proportion of zero pronouns in the NAIST corpus are intra-sententially resolvable?
2. How are inter-sentential zero pronouns represented in the data and by the model? What are the implications of this representation?
3. Could you provide examples of cases where the multi-sequence model correctly identifies an argument that the single-sequence model fails on, and vice versa?
4. Have you investigated whether the model implicitly learns linguistic constraints such as one argument per type per predicate?
