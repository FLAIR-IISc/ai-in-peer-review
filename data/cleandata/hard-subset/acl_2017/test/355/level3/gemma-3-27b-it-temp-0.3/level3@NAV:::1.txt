## Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

This paper presents a novel approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-RNNs, achieving state-of-the-art results without relying on explicit syntactic information. The core contribution lies in the successful application of grid-type recurrent neural networks to capture multi-predicate interactions, a crucial aspect of PAS analysis, particularly in a pro-drop language like Japanese where arguments are frequently omitted. A secondary, but significant contribution, is the detailed implementation and experimental validation of this approach on the NAIST Text Corpus, with a specific focus on the challenging task of identifying zero pronouns. The multi-sequence model, which considers all predicates in a sentence, demonstrably improves performance on zero pronoun resolution.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. The detailed description of the model architecture and experimental setup is commendable, allowing for reproducibility. The clear presentation of results, including ablation studies (network depth, residual connections) and per-case role analysis, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The focus on zero pronoun resolution is particularly valuable, addressing a key challenge in Japanese NLP. The fact that the model outperforms systems relying on syntactic parsing is a significant achievement, highlighting the power of learning interactions directly from word sequences.

However, the paper is not without minor weaknesses. The writing quality occasionally suffers from fluency issues and contains several typographical errors throughout (as noted in the author response). While these do not impede understanding, they detract from the overall polish of the submission. The use of boldface in Table 2 is unclear and requires clarification. Additionally, while the paper mentions the benefit of residual connections, a more intuitive explanation of *why* they improve performance would be beneficial. The illustration of the PRED-context point could be strengthened by explicitly referencing the accusative marker inclusion in Fig. 6, as suggested.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you clarify the purpose of the boldface formatting in Table 2?
2.  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of why residual connections improve performance, beyond simply stating that they do?
