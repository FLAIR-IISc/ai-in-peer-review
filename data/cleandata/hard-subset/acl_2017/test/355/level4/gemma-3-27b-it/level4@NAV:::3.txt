This paper introduces a joint neural network approach, utilizing Grid-RNNs, for Phrase Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese. The method is evaluated against a conventional single-sequence RNN, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on this established task. The paper is generally well-written and makes the task accessible even to those unfamiliar with Japanese linguistics. However, the inherent complexities of the PAS task could be more thoroughly discussed. For example, the ambiguity present in examples like Figure 1 – specifically the potential for the nominal (NOM) argument of the first predicate to also function as the nominal argument of the second – merits further explanation. A clearer articulation of how this task differs from Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly concerning the challenges posed by zero pronouns, would also be beneficial. Further, reporting statistics on the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would help quantify the task’s difficulty, as successful PAS requires distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential anaphora. A clarification regarding the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns is needed: are they represented as absent arguments, or explicitly marked as empty arguments? The current approach appears to omit any representation, which potentially limits the model's ability to effectively handle zero anaphora, and warrants discussion.

The model does not appear to explicitly capture constraints commonly leveraged in other SRL methods, such as the typical one-to-one relationship between predicates and argument types (e.g., a predicate generally having only one NOM or ACC argument) and between arguments and predicate slots. Analyzing the model’s output to assess its implicit learning of these constraints would be valuable. Moreover, the mixed results in Table 3, showing improvement for Multi-Seq only on NOM arguments, suggest a need for further investigation into the differences between the two models. Specifically, a comparison of their outputs – whether largely identical or divergent yet comparable in aggregate performance – and an identification of contexts where they diverge, would provide insight into the models’ inner workings. Such analysis would elevate this from a solid contribution to a strong one; beyond including results for SRL (a substantial undertaking), this is the primary area for improvement. 

The paper's presentation is clear, and the figures are helpful. There are minor linguistic issues: line 19 should read “error propagation”; line 190, “a solution”; line 264 (and Figure 2), “bread”; line 351, “independence”; line 512, “good”; line 531, “of their model”; line 637, “significance”; line 638, “both”; and the casing of terms in the references (e.g., "japanese", "lstm", "conll", "ilp") should be consistent.
