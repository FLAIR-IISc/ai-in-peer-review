This paper proposes a joint neural modelling approach to PAS analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, which it compares with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach.

This is a solidly-executed paper, targeting a well-established task in Japanese but achieving state-of-the-art results, and presenting the task in a mostly accessible manner for those not versed in Japanese. While the complexity of the task is generally well-motivated, expanding on the inherent ambiguity—such as the example in Figure 1 involving potential interpretations of nominative and accusative arguments—would strengthen the presentation. Further contextualizing the task’s challenges compared to semantic role labeling (SRL), particularly concerning the ambiguity introduced by zero pronouns, would also be beneficial. Including statistics regarding the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would clarify the task’s scope and inherent difficulty. A point of clarification is needed regarding inter-sentential zero pronouns: is their absence represented, or are they explicitly marked as empty arguments? The current approach, omitting such marking, raises concerns about a potentially incomplete representation, and a discussion of this design choice would be appreciated.

Certain constraints common in SRL, yet seemingly unaddressed by the model, could be explored. These include the typical one-to-one mapping between predicates and argument types (particularly nominative and accusative), and the expectation that arguments generally occupy a single slot per predicate. Analysis of model outputs to assess its implicit learning of these constraints would be valuable. Furthermore, given the nuanced performance differences between the single-sequence and multi-sequence models in Table 3, particularly the limited improvements for nominative arguments, a deeper investigation into their differing behaviors is warranted. Examining specific contexts where the models diverge could illuminate the benefits of multi-predicate interaction modelling. Such analysis would elevate the paper from solid to strong, though incorporating SRL results would represent a more substantial undertaking.

The paper’s presentation is strong, with figures effectively illustrating the model architecture.
