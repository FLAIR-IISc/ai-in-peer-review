This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, and compares it to a conventional single-sequence RNN approach.

This is a solidly-executed paper that tackles a well-established task in Japanese, achieving state-of-the-art results and presenting the task in a largely accessible manner, even for those unfamiliar with Japanese linguistics. That said, I believe the paper could benefit from further discussion of the task’s complexity. For instance, with respect to the example in Figure 1, elaborating on the inherent ambiguity between the nominative (NOM) and accusative (ACC) arguments of the first predicate, and how the NOM argument could also be the argument of the second predicate, would be helpful. A clearer description of how this task differs from semantic role labeling (SRL), particularly regarding the challenges posed by zero pronouns, would also strengthen the introduction. I would also appreciate statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are actually resolvable within the sentence. This would further illustrate the complexity of the defined task – namely, the need to implicitly differentiate between intra-sentential and inter-sentential zero anaphora. A related question is: in cases of inter-sentential zero anaphora for an argument of a given predicate, what representation is used? Is the argument simply unmarked, or is it marked as empty? My understanding is the former, meaning there’s no explicit representation of the zero pronoun itself. This seems like a potentially limiting design choice, as it might impact the model’s ability to effectively capture zero pronoun information, and a discussion of this would be valuable. 

The model also doesn’t appear to explicitly capture certain constraints that are commonly leveraged in other approaches (e.g., ILP-based SRL methods): (1) a predicate generally has only one argument of each type (particularly NOM and ACC), and (2) an argument generally fills only one slot per predicate. I’d like to have seen some analysis of the model’s output to assess how well it learns these constraints. More broadly, given the mixed results in Table 3 (with improvement for the Multi-Seq model largely limited to NOM), I would have liked to see a discussion of the differences in the outputs of the two models. Are they largely identical, or significantly different in their predictions, despite similar aggregate performance? In what specific contexts do differences arise? Analysis of this kind, shedding light on the models’ internal workings, would elevate the paper from solid to strong. This is the main area where I believe the paper could be improved, although including results for SRL would be a significant addition, albeit a substantial undertaking.

The presentation of the paper is generally good, with the figures effectively illustrating the model. There are a few minor language issues:
