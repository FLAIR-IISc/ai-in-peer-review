This paper presents a sophisticated application of Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks to the task of predicate-argument structure (PAS) identification in Japanese. Notably, the approach achieves strong performance *without* relying on explicit syntactic structure, even outperforming current state-of-the-art (SOTA) systems that *do* incorporate syntactic information. The authors provide a clear and detailed description of both the implementation and experimental results. Their attention to the challenging issue of dropped arguments and zero pronouns – phenomena prevalent in Japanese and particularly difficult for PAS systems – is commendable. Specifically, their multi-sequence model, which considers all predicates within a sentence, achieves the best performance on instances involving these types of arguments. Overall, this is a well-written and detailed paper.

The weaknesses of the paper are minor. While generally fluent, the English could be further improved by addressing several typos (detailed below). To better illustrate the benefit of including contextual information around the predicate within the PRED string, it would be helpful to explicitly reference and discuss the example provided in Figure 6, where the accusative marker is included with the verb. Furthermore, the use of boldface in Table 2 on page 7 is unclear and should be clarified.

**General Discussion:**

This work represents a significant contribution to the field of PAS identification, particularly for morphologically rich and relatively free-word-order languages like Japanese. The success of a grid-RNN architecture *without* explicit syntactic parsing is a compelling result and suggests that rich contextual representations can effectively capture predicate-argument relationships. The focus on handling implicit arguments is especially valuable, and the multi-sequence modeling approach appears to be a key factor in achieving strong performance on this challenging aspect of the task.
