This paper presents a joint neural modeling approach to predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, utilizing Grid-RNNs. The authors compare their model to a conventional single-sequence RNN, achieving state-of-the-art results on this established task. The paper is generally well-written and accessible, even for those unfamiliar with Japanese linguistics. However, a more thorough discussion of the task's inherent complexities would strengthen the contribution.

Specifically, the example provided in Figure 1 illustrates a significant ambiguity: the nominative (NOM) and accusative (ACC) arguments of the first predicate can also be interpreted as the nominative argument of the second. Elaborating on this type of ambiguity, and contrasting PAS analysis with semantic role labeling (SRL) – particularly highlighting the challenges posed by zero pronouns – would be beneficial. Providing statistics regarding the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would further illuminate the task’s difficulty, as correctly identifying these requires distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential anaphora. 

A key question arises concerning the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns: does the model simply omit any marking for these arguments, or are they explicitly represented as empty arguments? The paper implies the former, which potentially limits the model’s ability to effectively capture zero pronoun information. A discussion of this design choice, and its potential impact, would be valuable.

Furthermore, the model does not appear to explicitly capture constraints commonly leveraged in rule-based or ILP-based SRL systems, such as (1) the expectation that a predicate generally has only one argument of a given type (e.g., only one NOM or ACC argument) and (2) the expectation that an argument generally fills only one slot per predicate. An analysis of the model's output, demonstrating its ability to implicitly learn these constraints, would be insightful. 

The results in Table 3 show improvements for the multi-sequence model primarily for NOM arguments. A deeper analysis of the differences between the outputs of the single-sequence and multi-sequence models is warranted. Are their predictions largely identical, or are they substantially different despite similar aggregate performance? Identifying the specific contexts where the models diverge would offer valuable insight into the benefits of the joint modeling approach. Such analysis would elevate the paper from solid to strong; it represents the primary area for improvement. While including results for standard SRL would represent a substantial extension, it would further contextualize the contribution.

The paper’s presentation is generally clear, and the figures effectively illustrate the model. Minor language issues were noted:
