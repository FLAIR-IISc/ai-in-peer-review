This paper proposes a joint neural modelling approach to PAS analysis in
Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, which it compares with a conventional
single-sequence RNN approach.

This is a solidly-executed paper, targeting a well-established task in
Japanese but achieving state-of-the-art results and presenting the task in a
mostly accessible manner for those not versed in Japanese linguistics.
However, I felt the paper could have highlighted the complexity of the task
further, for example, by discussing the inherent ambiguity in the example in
Figure 1. Specifically, elaborating on the potential for misinterpreting the
NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate as the NOM argument of the
second predicate, and better describing how the task differs from SRL (largely
through the ambiguity of zero pronouns), would strengthen the introduction. I
would also have appreciated statistics regarding the proportion of zero
pronouns that are actually resolvable within the same sentence. This would
further illustrate the challenges of the task as defined – specifically, the
need to implicitly distinguish between intra- and inter-sentential zero
anaphors. I was unclear on one point: in the case of an inter-sentential zero
pronoun for the argument of a given predicate, what representation is used? Is
the argument simply unmarked, or is it marked as empty? My understanding is
that it is the former, meaning there’s no explicit representation of the zero
pronoun. This seems like a potential limitation in the representation, and a
discussion of this would have been valuable.

There are some constraints that don’t appear to be captured by the model
(which some ILP-based methods for SRL explicitly model): (1) a given
predicate typically has only one argument of a given type (especially NOM
and ACC); and (2) a given argument generally fills only one argument slot
per predicate. I would have liked to see some analysis of the model’s output
to assess how well it learns these types of constraints. More generally,
given the mixed results in Table 3, with improvement primarily seen for NOM
arguments with the Multi-Seq model, I would have appreciated a discussion of
the differences in output between the two models. Are their predictions largely
identical, or significantly different overall? In what contexts do the models
diverge? Such an analysis to illuminate the internal workings of the models
would have elevated the paper from solid to strong, and represents the primary
area for improvement (though including results for SRL would require
considerable additional work).

The presentation of the paper is good, with figures aiding understanding of
the model. There were some minor language issues, but nothing substantial:
