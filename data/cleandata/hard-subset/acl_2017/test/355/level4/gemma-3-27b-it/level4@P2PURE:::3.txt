This paper introduces a joint neural modeling approach to predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, utilizing Grid-RNNs, and compares its performance to a conventional single-sequence RNN. 
This is a well-executed paper that addresses a well-established task in Japanese, achieving state-of-the-art results and presenting the task in a largely accessible manner, even for those unfamiliar with the language. However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the task’s complexity. For example, the example in Figure 1 illustrates an inherent ambiguity – the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate can also be interpreted as the NOM argument of the second predicate – and a more detailed explanation of this ambiguity would be helpful. Furthermore, a clearer comparison to semantic role labeling (SRL), specifically highlighting the challenges posed by zero pronouns, would strengthen the presentation. I would also appreciate statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are actually resolvable within the sentence, as this further defines the task’s difficulty and necessitates distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential anaphora. A clarification regarding the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns would be valuable: are these arguments simply unmarked, or are they explicitly represented as empty arguments? My understanding is the former, meaning the model lacks an explicit representation of these pronouns, which could potentially limit its ability to accurately capture them; a discussion of this design choice would be welcome.

The model appears to lack certain constraints that are commonly incorporated into rule-based or ILP-based SRL systems: (1) a predicate generally has only one argument of a given type (particularly NOM and ACC), and (2) an argument typically occupies only one slot per predicate. Analysis of the model's output to assess its ability to learn these constraints would be insightful. More broadly, given the mixed results in Table 3 – with NOM being the only category showing significant improvement for the multi-sequence model – a discussion of the differences between the outputs of the two models would be beneficial. Are the outputs largely identical, or are they substantially different despite similar aggregate performance? In what contexts are discrepancies observed? Such an analysis would provide a deeper understanding of the model’s internals and elevate the paper from solid to strong. While including results for SRL would be a substantial undertaking, it represents a potential avenue for future work.

The paper’s presentation is generally good, with helpful figures that aid in understanding the model. There are a few minor language issues: l19: “the error propagation” -> “error propagation”; l190: “an solution” -> “a solution”; l264 (and Figure 2): “a bread” -> “bread”; l351: “the independence” -> “independence”; l512: “the good” -> “good”; l531: “from their model” -> “of their model”; l637: “significent” -> “significance”; l638: “both of” -> “both”. Please also ensure consistent casing in the references (e.g., “japanese,” “lstm,” “conll,” “ilp”).
