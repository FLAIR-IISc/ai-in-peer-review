This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to PAS analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, which it compares with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. The work achieves state-of-the-art results on the task and presents it in a largely accessible manner.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that a grid-RNN architecture can effectively model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis, surpassing previous state-of-the-art performance without relying on syntactic information. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough empirical evaluation on the NAIST text corpus, which provides a strong validation of the proposed approach. Finally, the paper offers a well-structured presentation of a complex task, making it understandable even for those unfamiliar with Japanese linguistics.

**Strengths:**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing methods on the NAIST Text Corpus, a standard benchmark for Japanese PAS analysis. This is a significant accomplishment, particularly given the avoidance of syntactic parsing, which is often a source of error propagation. The reported improvements, especially for zero anaphora resolution, are compelling.
2.  **Effective Use of Grid-RNNs:** The adoption of Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates is a key strength. The experimental results support the claim that this architecture is well-suited to modeling the complex relationships between predicates, a crucial aspect of Japanese PAS analysis. The comparison to the single-sequence model clearly illustrates the benefits of incorporating inter-predicate information.
3.  **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The figures effectively illustrate the model architecture and the flow of information. The inclusion of detailed experimental settings and comparisons to baselines enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.
4.  **Addresses a Challenging Problem:** Japanese PAS analysis, particularly with zero anaphora, is a notoriously difficult task. The authors explicitly acknowledge this difficulty and propose a solution that directly addresses the challenges posed by argument omission.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Analysis of Model Internals:** While the paper demonstrates strong performance gains, it lacks a deeper analysis of *why* the multi-sequence model performs better. A closer examination of the differences in model outputs, particularly in cases where the two models diverge, would provide valuable insights into the mechanisms by which Grid-RNNs capture multi-predicate interactions. Specifically, understanding *where* the model is leveraging inter-predicate information would strengthen the claims.
2.  **Representation of Inter-Sentential Zero Anaphora:** The paper doesn’t explicitly address how inter-sentential zero anaphora are handled. The implicit assumption that they are simply absent from the input representation seems potentially limiting, as it doesn't allow the model to learn any patterns related to these references. A discussion of this design choice and its potential implications would be beneficial.
3.  **Oversimplification of Argument Constraints:** The current model doesn't explicitly enforce common linguistic constraints, such as the one-to-one relationship between predicates and argument types (e.g., a predicate generally has only one nominative argument) or the fact that an argument typically fills only one role for a given predicate. Incorporating these constraints, potentially through a decoding step or specialized loss function, could further improve performance and robustness.
4.  **Limited Contextualization of the Task:** Although the task is introduced, a more thorough discussion of the challenges specific to Japanese PAS, beyond the mention of zero anaphora, would strengthen the paper. For example, elaborating on the ambiguity between arguments and adjuncts, or the high degree of word sense disambiguation needed, would provide a richer understanding of the task's complexity.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide examples of instances where the multi-sequence model and the single-sequence model make different predictions, and explain what linguistic features or interactions appear to drive these differences?
2.  What is the rationale behind not explicitly representing inter-sentential zero anaphora? Have you considered alternative approaches, and if so, what were the trade-offs?
3.  Have you investigated methods for incorporating linguistic constraints (e.g., one-to-one argument mapping) into the model, and if so, what were the results?
4.  What percentage of zero pronouns in the NAIST corpus are actually intra-sententially resolvable?
