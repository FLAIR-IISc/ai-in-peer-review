This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, adapting techniques from successful English semantic role labeling approaches, notably (Zhou and Xu, 2015). The core contribution lies in extending a single-sequence model to a multi-sequence model leveraging Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between arguments of multiple predicates. Evaluation on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 demonstrates performance improvements over current state-of-the-art systems.

**Strengths:**

The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and provides strong motivation for the research. 
The proposed models achieve improved accuracy compared to existing state-of-the-art Japanese PAS analyzers.
The use of Grid-RNNs demonstrably improves performance, particularly in identifying zero arguments – a key focus of the work – over the single-sequence baseline. This suggests the grid architecture effectively models multi-predicate interactions.
The extensive experimental setup, including ablation studies examining network depth and residual connections, provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach.
The results demonstrate the effectiveness of learning contextual information from word sequences without relying on potentially error-prone syntactic information.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary contribution of this work appears to be the adaptation and extension of existing sequence modeling techniques to incorporate multi-predicate interactions via Grid-RNNs, rather than a fundamentally novel architectural innovation. The performance gains attributed to these interactions, while statistically significant, are relatively modest compared to improvements reported in prior work (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015).
The paper lacks a direct comparison with related neural network-based extensions of previous work (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016), which makes it difficult to fully assess the novelty and impact of the proposed models. 
The analysis of case role-specific performance (Table 3) reveals substantial improvements for the dative case, which may warrant further investigation to understand the underlying reasons for this disproportionate gain.

**Questions to Authors:**

Could the authors provide a detailed comparison of their multi-sequence model with the neural network extension of Ouchi et al. (2015) presented in Shibata et al. (2016), including a discussion of the differences in methodologies and performance?
The results in Table 3 show a particularly strong improvement in dative case identification. Can the authors elaborate on why their model is particularly effective for this case role?
