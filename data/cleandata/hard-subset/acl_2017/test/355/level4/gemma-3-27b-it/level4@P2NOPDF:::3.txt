This paper introduces a novel joint neural modeling approach to predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, utilizing Grid-RNNs. The method is evaluated comparatively against a conventional single-sequence RNN baseline, achieving state-of-the-art results. The paper effectively presents a challenging task to a broader audience, even those unfamiliar with Japanese linguistics.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the task’s inherent complexities. For instance, elaborating on the ambiguity illustrated in Figure 1 – specifically, the potential for the nominative (NOM) and accusative (ACC) arguments of the first predicate to be re-interpreted as the nominative argument of the second – would strengthen the motivation. A clearer contrast between this task and traditional Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), focusing specifically on the challenges posed by zero pronouns, would also be valuable. Providing statistics on the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would further illuminate the task's difficulty, as currently defined, and highlight the need to differentiate between intra- and inter-sentential anaphora. 

A key question arises regarding the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns: are they explicitly marked as empty arguments, or are they entirely absent from the input representation? The paper implies the latter, which may represent a suboptimal design choice, potentially hindering the model’s ability to effectively capture zero pronoun information. A discussion of this design decision and its potential implications is warranted.

Furthermore, the model doesn’t appear to explicitly enforce constraints commonly leveraged in traditional SRL approaches, such as the one-to-one relationship between predicate-argument types (e.g., a predicate typically has only one NOM and one ACC argument) and the one-to-one assignment of arguments to predicate slots. Analyzing the model’s output to assess its implicit learning of these constraints would be insightful. 

The results presented in Table 3 reveal improvements for the multi-sequence model primarily with respect to NOM argument identification. A more detailed comparison of the outputs of the single-sequence and multi-sequence models would be beneficial. Investigating the degree of similarity or divergence between their predictions – whether they produce largely identical outputs or differ significantly while maintaining similar aggregate performance – and identifying the contexts where these differences occur could provide valuable insights into the internal workings of each model. Such an analysis would elevate this paper from solid to strong, and constitutes the primary area for improvement. Inclusion of results on a standard SRL dataset would provide additional context, although this is acknowledged as a more substantial undertaking.

Overall, the paper is well-presented, with informative figures that aid comprehension of the model.
