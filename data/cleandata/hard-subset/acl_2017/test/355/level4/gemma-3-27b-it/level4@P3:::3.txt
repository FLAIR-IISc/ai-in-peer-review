This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to PAS analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, which it compares with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. The paper addresses a challenging task – Japanese PAS analysis – and achieves state-of-the-art results. The presentation is generally accessible, even for those unfamiliar with Japanese NLP.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **State-of-the-art results on Japanese PAS analysis:** The proposed Grid-RNN model surpasses previous state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach. This is the most significant contribution.
2. **Effective application of Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions:** The paper successfully applies Grid-RNNs to model relationships between predicates without relying on syntactic information, a common bottleneck in previous approaches. This is particularly valuable for a language like Japanese with flexible word order.
3. **Demonstration of a robust neural model without syntactic parsing:** The model’s ability to achieve strong performance without syntactic features is noteworthy, highlighting the power of neural networks to learn relevant features directly from the data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The consistent improvement over baseline models, particularly for zero anaphora resolution, provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach. The statistical significance tests further support these findings.
2. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. Figures are effectively used to illustrate the model architecture.
3. **Relevant task and problem setting:** Japanese PAS analysis, especially with the challenges posed by pro-drop phenomena, is an important and complex NLP task. The paper effectively frames the problem and highlights the limitations of prior work.
4. **Thorough experimentation:** The authors conduct thorough experiments, including ablation studies (single vs. multi-sequence models, with/without residual connections) and analysis of performance across different case roles.
5. **Publicly available source code:**  The availability of the source code promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited analysis of model internals:** While the paper demonstrates performance gains, it lacks in-depth analysis of *why* the Grid-RNN model performs better. Specifically, a comparison of the types of errors made by the single-sequence and multi-sequence models, or examples illustrating where the multi-sequence model successfully leverages predicate interactions, would be insightful.
2. **Simplification of zero anaphora representation:** The paper’s treatment of inter-sentential zero anaphora (implicitly treating them as non-arguments) is a potential limitation. A discussion of how this representation might impact the model’s performance, and consideration of alternative representations, would strengthen the work.
3. **Lack of explicit modeling of argument constraints:** The model does not explicitly enforce constraints such as a predicate generally having only one argument of a given type. Analyzing the model's ability to implicitly learn these constraints, or exploring ways to explicitly incorporate them, could improve performance and robustness. 
4. **Limited discussion of task complexity:**  While the paper touches upon the difficulty of the task, a more detailed explanation of the ambiguity inherent in Japanese PAS analysis, particularly concerning zero anaphora and argument disambiguation, would better contextualize the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide examples of sentences where the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model, and explain how the Grid-RNN appears to be leveraging multi-predicate interactions in those cases?
2. What is the proportion of intra-sentential resolvable zero pronouns in the NAIST Text Corpus? How does your model specifically address the challenge of distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors?
3. Have you explored any methods to explicitly incorporate constraints on argument roles (e.g., one argument per role per predicate) into the model architecture or loss function?
4.  Regarding the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns, have you considered alternative approaches (e.g., a special "null" argument marker) and what impact would those have on performance?
