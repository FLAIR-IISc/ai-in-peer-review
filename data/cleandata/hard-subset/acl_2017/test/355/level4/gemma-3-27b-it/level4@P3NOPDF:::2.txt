This paper presents novel prediction models for the Japanese Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) task, building upon the strong performance of the English SRL model proposed by Zhou and Xu (2015). The authors extend this foundation by integrating a Grid-RNN framework to explicitly model interactions between arguments of multiple predicates – a key challenge in SRL. Evaluation on a standard Japanese SRL benchmark demonstrates that the proposed models achieve significantly improved performance over existing state-of-the-art systems.

**Strengths:**

The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and provides strong motivation for the work. The reported improvements in accuracy compared to current state-of-the-art Japanese SRL systems are substantial and represent a valuable contribution. The investigation of Grid-RNNs to capture predicate-argument interactions is particularly noteworthy. The authors correctly identify and focus on the challenging problem of zero argument detection, and demonstrate that the Grid-RNN model achieves a slight, but meaningful, performance gain over the single-sequential model largely due to improvements in this area. This focus on a specific challenge within SRL strengthens the contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

While the results are impressive, the core contribution of this work appears to be primarily an effective adaptation and extension of existing models – specifically, evolving a single-sequential model into a multi-sequential one using Grid-RNNs – for the Japanese language. The impact of explicitly modeling predicate interactions, while positive, seems somewhat limited when compared to the findings of Ouchi et al. (2015). Furthermore, the related work section does not adequately address the contributions of Shibata et al. (2016), which also builds upon the work of Ouchi et al. (2015) through neural network modeling. A direct comparison with Shibata et al. (2016) would be highly beneficial, particularly detailing the differences in methodology and resulting performance.  It is unclear whether the proposed model incorporates any aspects of their approach, or why a comparison was not included. 

**Questions/Suggestions for the Authors:**

*   Could the authors elaborate on the specific architectural choices made when adapting the Zhou and Xu (2015) model for Japanese? What modifications were necessary to accommodate the differences between the two languages?
*   A more in-depth error analysis would be valuable. What types of errors does the proposed model still make? Are there specific argument types or predicate-argument combinations that remain particularly challenging?
*   The paper mentions an improvement in zero argument detection. Could the authors provide more detail on how the Grid-RNN architecture specifically contributes to this improvement? Perhaps provide examples demonstrating where the Grid-RNN correctly identifies a zero argument when the sequential model fails.
*   Given the work of Shibata et al. (2016), a direct comparison, including reporting their performance on the same dataset, is strongly encouraged. This will help clarify the novelty and specific advantages of the presented approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and well-executed contribution to Japanese SRL. The reported performance gains are significant, and the use of Grid-RNNs is a reasonable approach to modeling predicate-argument interactions. However, the novelty of the contribution could be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of related work (specifically Shibata et al., 2016) and a more detailed analysis of the impact of the Grid-RNN architecture. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these points.
