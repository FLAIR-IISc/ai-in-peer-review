## Review: Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis

This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, extending the work of Zhou and Xu (2015) to this specific linguistic setting. The core idea is to leverage Grid-RNNs to model interactions between arguments of multiple predicates, addressing a key challenge in Japanese – argument omission. The models are evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 and achieve state-of-the-art performance, exceeding existing systems, including Ouchi et al. (2015).

**Contributions:**

1. **Strong Performance on Japanese PAS:** The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on a standard benchmark dataset, establishing a new state-of-the-art for Japanese PAS analysis. This is a significant contribution as accurate PAS analysis is a crucial step in many NLP pipelines for Japanese.
2. **Effective Application of Grid-RNNs:** The successful application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis represents a valuable contribution. The results highlight the effectiveness of this architecture in capturing relationships between predicates and improving argument identification, particularly for zero arguments. 
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation, including ablation studies analyzing the impact of network depth and residual connections. The detailed results and comparisons with existing models provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed multi-sequence model consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, achieving significant improvements, especially in zero argument identification. This demonstrable improvement is a strong indicator of the approach’s effectiveness.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of Japanese PAS analysis, specifically the issue of argument omission and the need to model multi-predicate interactions. The proposed approach directly addresses these challenges with a logically sound architecture.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is well-defined, with clear descriptions of the dataset, evaluation metrics, and implementation details. The inclusion of a significance test strengthens the validity of the results.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The ablation studies examining network depth and residual connections provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and highlight the importance of these design choices. The per-case role analysis further demonstrates the model’s strengths and weaknesses.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Contribution:** While achieving state-of-the-art results is valuable, the novelty of extending a successful model (Zhou & Xu, 2015) to a new language and task, while incorporating Grid-RNNs, is somewhat incremental. The performance gain over previous Japanese PAS systems, while statistically significant, isn't a dramatic leap forward.
2. **Limited Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches:** The comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), which also builds upon Ouchi et al. (2015) with neural network modeling, is mentioned briefly but lacks detailed analysis. A more comprehensive comparison of the proposed model with this work would provide a clearer understanding of its relative strengths and weaknesses.  It would be helpful to understand *how* the proposed architecture differs from and improves upon Shibata et al.’s approach.
3. **Focus on Intra-Sentential Analysis:** The paper explicitly focuses on intra-sentential argument analysis, following previous work. While this is a reasonable scope, acknowledging the limitations and discussing potential extensions to handle inter-sentential arguments would strengthen the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your model with Shibata et al. (2016), specifically outlining the architectural differences and their impact on performance?
2. How much computational resources (e.g., training time, memory usage) are required to train and run your models, especially the multi-sequence model with deeper layers?
3.  Given that the improvements are most significant for zero arguments, what specific characteristics of the Grid-RNN architecture contribute to its effectiveness in resolving zero anaphora?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
* The availability of source code is a positive aspect of this submission.
* Consider clarifying the role of the “residual connections” for readers less familiar with deep learning techniques.
