- Primary contribution: Demonstrates Grid-RNNs effectively model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS, achieving state-of-the-art results without syntactic features.  
- Secondary contribution: Provides thorough empirical validation on the NAIST Text Corpus.  
- Presentation: Clear, well-structured exposition that makes the complex task accessible.

Strengths:
- State-of-the-art performance on NAIST, notably improving zero anaphora resolution without relying on syntactic parsing.  
- Effective use of Grid-RNNs to capture inter-predicate interactions, outperforming single-sequence baselines.  
- Clear and comprehensive presentation with helpful figures and detailed experimental settings.  
- Directly addresses the challenging problem of Japanese PAS and argument omission.

Weaknesses:
- Limited analysis of model internals; lacks investigation into why and where Grid-RNNs outperform single-sequence models.  
- Does not explicitly handle or discuss inter-sentential zero anaphora, potentially limiting learning of cross-sentence patterns.  
- Oversimplifies argument constraints; no mechanism to enforce one-to-one or role-uniqueness constraints.  
- Limited contextualization of task-specific challenges (e.g., argument vs. adjunct ambiguity, word-sense issues).

Questions for authors:
- Can you show examples where multi-sequence and single-sequence models differ and explain the driving linguistic interactions?  
- Why were inter-sentential zero anaphora not explicitly represented, and were alternatives considered?  
- Have you tried incorporating linguistic constraints (e.g., one-to-one argument mapping) and what were the outcomes?  
- What fraction of zero pronouns in NAIST are resolvable within the same sentence?

Additional comments:
- Minor language edits needed and ensure consistent capitalization in references (e.g., LSTM, CONLL, ILP).  
- Consider discussing potential applications beyond PAS (e.g., information extraction, machine translation).
