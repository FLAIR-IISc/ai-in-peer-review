This paper introduces novel neural sequence models for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, adapting principles from successful English semantic role labeling approaches like (Zhou and Xu, 2015). The core contribution lies in extending a single-sequence model with a grid-RNN architecture to better capture interactions between arguments of multiple predicates, a key challenge in Japanese due to frequent argument omission. Evaluation on the NAIST Text Corpus demonstrates performance exceeding the current state-of-the-art.

**Contributions:**

1. **Strongly:** The paper demonstrates that a grid-RNN architecture effectively models multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis, achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark. This is evidenced by significant improvements, particularly in zero-argument identification, a known difficulty in this task.
2. **Moderately:** The paper successfully adapts and applies recurrent neural network techniques, specifically grid-RNNs, to the Japanese PAS task, which traditionally relies heavily on syntactic parsing. This represents a valuable step towards more robust and parser-independent PAS systems.
3. **Weakly:** The experiments provide a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed models, including an ablation study on network depth and the use of residual connections, offering useful insights into model design.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art Performance:** The proposed multi-sequence model achieves demonstrably superior performance to existing methods on the NAIST Text Corpus. The reported improvements, particularly for zero arguments, are significant and support the effectiveness of the approach. The use of bootstrap resampling for significance testing further strengthens this claim.
2. **Effective Multi-Predicate Interaction Modeling:** The results clearly show that the grid-RNN architecture effectively captures the relationships between predicates in a sentence, leading to improved performance, especially when dealing with omitted arguments. This is a critical contribution for a language like Japanese where argument omission is prevalent.
3. **Robustness and Generalizability:** The model's performance across different case roles, as shown in Table 3, suggests a degree of robustness and ability to generalize beyond simple syntactic dependencies. The improvement on the challenging dative case is particularly noteworthy.
4. **Well-Structured and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the proposed models and experimental setup. The diagrams and tables effectively communicate the key concepts and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the results are strong, the core innovation—extending a sequential model to a multi-sequential one using grid-RNNs—builds directly on established techniques. The impact of modeling predicate interactions, while positive, appears modest in comparison to prior work that focused on improved feature engineering or joint modeling (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015).
2. **Missing Comparison to Related Neural Work:** The paper does not directly compare against recent neural network-based approaches to Japanese PAS analysis, such as the work of Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends previous models with neural networks. A direct comparison would clarify the specific advantages of the proposed grid-RNN approach over these alternatives.
3. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper demonstrates improved performance, a more detailed analysis of the remaining error cases could provide further insights into the limitations of the model and suggest directions for future research. For instance, understanding *which* zero anaphora resolutions still pose challenges would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your approach with the neural model proposed by Shibata et al. (2016), including a direct performance comparison on the same dataset and experimental settings?
2. What types of errors does the multi-sequence model still make, particularly regarding zero anaphora resolution? Providing examples of challenging cases would be helpful.
3. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the size of the training dataset? Would performance continue to improve with access to larger corpora?
