This paper presents a sophisticated application of Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks to the task of determining predicate-argument structures (PAS) in Japanese. The approach does not rely on any explicit syntactic structure and outperforms current state-of-the-art (SOA) systems that do utilize syntactic information. The authors provide a clear and detailed description of the implementation and results. They particularly focus on performance with dropped arguments, or zero pronouns, which are prevalent and challenging in Japanese PAS. Their multi-sequence model, considering all predicates in a sentence, achieves the best performance on these examples. The paper is detailed and clearly written.

**Main Contributions:**

1.  **Novel Application of Grid-RNNs to Japanese PAS:** The paper introduces a compelling use of Grid-RNNs for capturing multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis, a task typically reliant on syntactic parsing. This is the primary contribution, demonstrating a viable alternative to pipeline-based approaches.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance Without Syntactic Information:**  The proposed models achieve superior accuracy compared to existing methods *without* leveraging syntactic information. This is a significant accomplishment, as syntactic parsers are often a source of error propagation.
3.  **Improved Handling of Zero Pronouns:** The work specifically addresses the challenging issue of zero pronoun resolution in Japanese, demonstrating performance gains particularly for identifying arguments that are omitted from the sentence.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus consistently demonstrate that the proposed models outperform existing state-of-the-art methods, validating the effectiveness of the approach. The inclusion of statistical significance testing (bootstrap resampling) strengthens this claim.
2.  **Clear and Thorough Description:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of the model architecture, implementation details, and experimental setup, allowing for reproducibility and facilitating understanding. 
3.  **Focus on a Core Linguistic Challenge:** The paper directly tackles a key challenge in Japanese PAS (zero anaphora resolution) and provides evidence that the proposed model effectively addresses it.
4.  **Effective Use of Grid-RNNs:** The architecture effectively models the interactions between multiple predicates, a feature that demonstrably improves performance, particularly in resolving zero pronouns. The analysis of the impact of network depth and residual connections adds to the thoroughness.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents overall performance metrics, a more detailed analysis of specific error cases would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the model still struggles could provide valuable insights for future work.
2.  **Presentation of Ablation Studies:** While the effect of network depth is discussed, it could be beneficial to present results that show the contribution of each major component of the model (e.g., input layer configurations, specific RNN types) in a more systematic way.
3.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** There are some minor stylistic issues and typos throughout the paper (detailed below), which, while not critical, detract slightly from readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a few specific examples of error cases where the model performs poorly, and discuss potential reasons for these errors?
2.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the proposed models compared to the baseline systems?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as embedding dimensions and learning rate?
