This paper presents a sophisticated application of Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks to the task of determining predicate-argument structures (PAS) in Japanese. The approach does not rely on any explicit syntactic structure, and outperforms current state-of-the-art systems that do include such structure. The authors provide a clear and detailed description of the implementation and results. In particular, they pay close attention to performance on dropped arguments, or zero pronouns, which are prevalent in Japanese and especially challenging for PAS. Their multi-sequence model, which considers all predicates in the sentence, achieves the best performance for these examples. The paper is detailed and clearly written.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that a grid-RNN architecture, without relying on syntactic parsing, can achieve state-of-the-art performance on Japanese predicate argument structure analysis, specifically excelling in handling zero anaphora. This is significant as it addresses a key limitation of previous approaches that were susceptible to error propagation from potentially inaccurate syntactic parsers. A secondary contribution is the NAIST Text Corpus results, which offer a strong empirical validation of the proposed method. Finally, the detailed architecture and implementation presented in the paper facilitate reproducibility and future research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus without utilizing syntactic information, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed grid-RNN architecture for capturing multi-predicate interactions. This is particularly noteworthy given the challenges posed by zero anaphora in Japanese.
2. **Effective Handling of Zero Anaphora:** The paper explicitly addresses the issue of zero anaphora, a significant challenge in Japanese PAS analysis. The multi-sequence model’s improved performance on zero arguments confirms its ability to effectively model relationships between predicates and their unexpressed arguments.
3. **Clear and Detailed Presentation:** The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive explanation of the model architecture, implementation details, and experimental setup. This facilitates understanding and reproducibility. 
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors provide comprehensive results including ablation studies (network depth, residual connections) and comparison to baseline models. This demonstrates a careful evaluation of the proposed approach and its components.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** While the results on the NAIST Text Corpus are impressive, the generalizability of the approach to other datasets or languages remains unclear. The corpus is specific to newspaper text, and the performance may vary on different domains.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The numerous hyperparameter choices (RNN layers, learning rate, embedding dimensions, etc.) suggest a potential sensitivity to these settings. The paper does mention a selection process, but a more detailed analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters would be beneficial.
3. **Presentation of Results:** The use of boldface in Table 2 is unclear and doesn't add to the readability of the results. A brief explanation would improve clarity. Additionally, a visual illustration, perhaps utilizing the example from Figure 6, could better explain how the predicate (PRED) feature includes contextual information surrounding the verb, including accusative markers.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you comment on the computational cost of the proposed multi-sequence model compared to simpler approaches?
2. Have you explored the use of pre-trained word embeddings, and if so, how did they impact performance?
3. Do you foresee any challenges in adapting this model to other languages with similar grammatical structures (e.g., Korean, Chinese)?
