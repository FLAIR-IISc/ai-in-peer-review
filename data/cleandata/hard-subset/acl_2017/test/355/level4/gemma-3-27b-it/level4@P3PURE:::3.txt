This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, and compares its performance to a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. This is a solidly-executed paper, addressing a well-established task in Japanese natural language processing while achieving state-of-the-art results, and presenting the task in a largely accessible manner even for those not versed in Japanese linguistics. However, I felt the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the task’s complexity. For example, regarding the illustration in Figure 1, elaborating on the inherent ambiguity between the nominative (NOM) and accusative (ACC) arguments of the first predicate, and their potential interpretation as the nominative argument of the second predicate, would be valuable. A clearer description of how this task contrasts with semantic role labeling (SRL) – particularly concerning the ambiguity introduced by zero pronouns – would also be helpful. Furthermore, providing statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are actually intra-sententially resolvable would further highlight the complexity inherent in the task definition, as it necessitates implicitly distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphora. A point of clarification is needed regarding inter-sentential zero pronouns: what representation is used when a predicate’s argument is a zero pronoun referring outside the current sentence? Is the argument simply unmarked, or is it explicitly marked as empty? My understanding is that the former is true, resulting in no explicit representation of the zero pronoun itself. This seems like a potentially limiting design choice, which could impact the model’s ability to effectively capture zero pronoun information; some discussion of this would be appreciated.

The model also appears to lack explicit constraints commonly found in other PAS systems (e.g., ILP-based SRL methods): (1) predicates generally have only one argument of each type (particularly NOM and ACC), and (2) arguments typically fill only one slot per predicate. I would have liked to see an analysis of the model’s output to assess how well it learns these constraints. More generally, given the mixed results in Table 3 – where NOM is the only argument type showing consistent improvement for the multi-sequence model – a discussion of the differences between the two models’ outputs would be beneficial. Are their predictions largely identical, or divergent yet comparable in aggregate performance? In what contexts do the models disagree? Such an analysis, shedding light on the internal workings of the models, would elevate this paper from solid to strong. This is the primary area where I believe the paper could be improved. While including results for SRL would be valuable, it would require substantial additional work.

The paper’s presentation is good, with the figures effectively illustrating the model. There are some minor linguistic issues: l19: “the error propagation” -> “error propagation”; l190: “an solution” -> “a solution”; l264 (and Figure 2): “a bread” -> “bread”; l351: “the independence” -> “independence”; l512: “the good” -> “good”; l531: “from their model” -> “of their model”; l637: “significent” -> “significance”; l638: “both of” -> “both”. Additionally, please ensure consistent casing in your references (e.g., “japanese”, “lstm”, “conll”, “ilp”).
