This paper proposes novel prediction models for Japanese predicate argument structure analysis, adapting ideas from the state-of-the-art English model by Zhou and Xu (2015). The authors extend this model using a Grid-RNN framework to better handle interactions between arguments of multiple predicates. Evaluation on a standard Japanese SRL benchmark demonstrates significantly improved performance compared to existing state-of-the-art systems.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application and adaptation of Grid-RNNs to Japanese predicate argument structure analysis, demonstrating their effectiveness in modeling multi-predicate interactions. While previous work has explored neural network extensions to existing pipeline approaches (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016), this work presents a novel end-to-end neural architecture that surpasses current performance without relying on syntactic information. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of improved performance specifically on the challenging task of zero anaphora resolution, a key focus of Japanese PAS analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Performance:** The proposed models achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, exceeding the performance of established methods like Ouchi et al. (2015). This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Effective Multi-Predicate Modeling:** The Grid-RNN architecture demonstrably improves performance over the single-sequence model, particularly for zero anaphora resolution. This supports the authors' hypothesis that modeling interactions between predicates is crucial for accurate PAS analysis in Japanese. The significant improvement on zero arguments highlights the model's ability to capture contextual information necessary for resolving omitted arguments.
*   **Well-Structured and Motivated:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, providing a strong motivation for the work and a clear explanation of the proposed methods.
*   **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors provide a comprehensive experimental setup, including comparisons to strong baselines and ablation studies exploring the effects of network depth and residual connections.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the results are strong, the core contribution – extending a sequential model to a multi-sequential one – feels somewhat incremental. The performance gain from explicitly modeling predicate interactions, while statistically significant, appears relatively modest compared to the gains reported in Ouchi et al. (2015).
*   **Missing Comparison:** The paper does not compare directly with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends the Ouchi et al. (2015) model with neural network components. A direct comparison would clarify the unique benefits of the proposed Grid-RNN approach relative to other recent advancements in the field.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates improvements in overall F-measure, a more detailed error analysis would be valuable. Understanding *where* the model still struggles could provide insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Could you provide a detailed comparison of your model’s performance against that of Shibata et al. (2016)?
*   Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifying the types of errors the model makes and providing examples?
*   The paper mentions exploring large-scale unlabeled data for future work. Can you elaborate on your planned approach and the potential benefits you expect to see?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is quite extensive.
*   The notation used throughout the paper is consistent and clear.
