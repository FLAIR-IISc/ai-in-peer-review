This paper presents a sophisticated application of Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks to the task of identifying predicate-argument structures (PAS) in Japanese. The proposed approach is notable for not relying on any explicit syntactic structure, yet it outperforms current state-of-the-art systems that do. The paper is clearly written and provides a detailed description of the implementation and a thorough analysis of the results.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this work is its novel and effective model. By leveraging Grid-type RNNs, the authors have developed a system that achieves state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS analysis without the need for syntactic features from a parser. This is a significant contribution, as it demonstrates a path toward high-performing models that are not dependent on potentially noisy, upstream parsing pipelines and are thus less susceptible to error propagation.

A particularly impressive aspect of this work is its careful attention to dropped arguments, or zero pronouns, which are a prevalent and challenging phenomenon in Japanese. The authors rightly highlight this issue and show that their multi-sequence model, which considers all predicates in a sentence simultaneously, achieves the best performance on these difficult cases. This result effectively demonstrates the model's capacity to capture important multi-predicate interactions within the sentence.

Finally, the paper itself is well-organized, detailed, and clearly written, making the authors' methodology and findings easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

My comments are minor and are primarily suggestions for improving the clarity of the presentation.

1.  The discussion of the PRED representation, which includes contextual information around the predicate, would benefit from a more explicit illustration. For example, using the sentence from Figure 6 to point out in the text how the accusative marker is included with the verb in the PRED string would provide a concrete and helpful example for the reader.

2.  In Table 2 on page 7, the meaning of the boldface font is not explained. Adding a brief note to the caption to clarify its purpose would improve the table's readability.
