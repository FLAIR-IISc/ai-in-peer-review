This paper proposes a joint neural model for Predicate-Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, using Grid-RNNs. The proposed approach is compared against a conventional single-sequence RNN baseline.

This is a solidly-executed paper on a well-established task in Japanese NLP. The authors achieve state-of-the-art results and present the task in a manner that is largely accessible to a non-Japanese-speaking audience. However, the paper could be strengthened by further emphasizing the complexity of the task. For instance, using the example in Figure 1, the authors could walk the reader through the inherent ambiguity of identifying the NOM argument of the second predicate, which could be either the NOM or ACC argument of the first predicate. A more detailed description of how Japanese PAS analysis contrasts with standard Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly concerning the challenges posed by zero pronouns, would also be beneficial. To further motivate the problem, it would be helpful to include statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are resolvable within a sentence. This would clarify the model's challenge in implicitly distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphora.

A key point that remains unclear is the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns. When an argument of a predicate is an inter-sentential zero pronoun, is it explicitly marked as an empty argument, or is there simply no argument labeled for that slot? My interpretation is the latter, which suggests there is no explicit signal that a zero pronoun exists. This seems like a potential representational weakness that could hinder the model's ability to learn about zero pronouns, and a discussion of this design choice would have been welcome.

The proposed model does not appear to explicitly enforce common linguistic constraints, which are often handled by ILP-based approaches to SRL. These include: (1) a predicate typically takes only one argument of a specific core type (e.g., one NOM, one ACC); and (2) a single word phrase generally fills only one argument role for a given predicate. It would be valuable to see an analysis of the model's output to determine whether it learns these constraints implicitly.

Furthermore, the analysis of the results could be deepened. In Table 3, the Multi-Seq model's improvement over the Single-Seq baseline is concentrated almost entirely in the NOM case. A qualitative comparison of the outputs from the two models would be highly informative. For example, are their predictions largely identical, or do they make different types of errors that result in similar aggregate scores? In what specific linguistic contexts do their predictions diverge? This kind of error analysis, which would shed light on the models' respective strengths and weaknesses, would significantly strengthen the paper and elevate it from a solid contribution to a truly strong one. This is the primary area for improvement.

The paper is well-presented, and the figures are helpful for understanding the model architecture. There are a few minor grammatical and stylistic issues that should be corrected:
