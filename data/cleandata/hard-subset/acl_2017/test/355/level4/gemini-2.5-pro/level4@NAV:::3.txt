This paper introduces a joint neural modeling approach for Predicate-Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, utilizing Grid-RNNs, and evaluates it against a more conventional single-sequence RNN method.

This is a well-executed paper that tackles an established Japanese NLP task and successfully advances the state-of-the-art. The authors also do a commendable job of presenting the task in a manner that is generally accessible to readers unfamiliar with Japanese linguistics. That said, the paper could be strengthened by further emphasizing the task's complexity. For example, a more detailed walkthrough of the example in Figure 1 could illuminate the inherent ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate and the NOM argument of the second. Similarly, a clearer explanation of how this task differs from standard Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly regarding the ambiguity introduced by zero pronouns, would be beneficial. I would also have found it useful to see statistics regarding the percentage of zero pronouns that are resolvable intra-sententially, as this highlights a key challenge of the task (i.e., the need to implicitly differentiate between intra- and inter-sentential anaphors).

One point of uncertainty was the model's handling of inter-sentential zero pronouns. For an argument of a predicate realized as such a pronoun, what is the target representation? Is the argument simply omitted from the output, or is it explicitly marked as an empty argument? My interpretation is that the former is the case, which seems like a potential representational weakness, as it fails to signal the existence of a zero pronoun. This could, in turn, affect the model's ability to learn about them. A discussion of this design choice would have been welcome.

The proposed model does not appear to explicitly enforce certain linguistic constraints that are often handled by methods like ILP in SRL. These include: (1) a predicate generally has at most one argument of a given semantic role (especially core roles like NOM and ACC); and (2) an argument phrase typically fills only one semantic role for a given predicate. An analysis of the model's output to determine if it learns to respect these constraints implicitly would have strengthened the paper. More broadly, the mixed results in Table 3—where the Multi-Seq model's improvement is concentrated on the NOM case—call for a deeper discussion. It would be valuable to understand the qualitative differences between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models. Are their outputs mostly identical, or are they significantly different but result in similar aggregate scores? In which specific contexts do their predictions diverge? Including this type of analysis to illuminate the models' behavior would elevate this from a solid to a strong paper and is the most significant area for potential improvement (short of adding a full SRL comparison, which would be a much larger undertaking).

The paper's presentation is clear, with helpful figures that effectively illustrate the model architecture. There are some minor language errors and inconsistencies to be corrected:

l190: an solution -> a solution
l264 (and Figure 2): a bread -> bread
l351: the independence -> independence
l512: the good -> good
l531: from their model -> of their model
l637: significent -> significance
