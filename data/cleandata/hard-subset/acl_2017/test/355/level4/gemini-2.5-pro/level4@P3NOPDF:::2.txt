This paper addresses the task of Japanese Semantic Role Labeling (SRL). The authors adapt the state-of-the-art English SRL model of Zhou and Xu (2015) to Japanese and propose two new prediction models. The primary contribution is an extension of this single-predicate model to a multi-predicate framework using Grid-RNNs, which is designed to capture the interactions between the arguments of multiple predicates within the same sentence. The models are evaluated on a standard Japanese SRL benchmark, where they achieve a new state-of-the-art, significantly outperforming previous systems.

**Strengths**

*   The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, and the proposed solution is logically presented.
*   The proposed models set a new state-of-the-art on a standard Japanese SRL benchmark, demonstrating a significant performance improvement over existing methods. This is a strong and valuable result for the field.
*   The multi-sequential model, which leverages Grid-RNNs, shows a further, albeit small, improvement over the strong single-sequential baseline. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that this gain is concentrated in the detection of zero arguments, which is a challenging aspect of Japanese SRL and a key focus of this work. This targeted improvement provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of modeling inter-predicate dependencies.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

*   The primary novelty lies in extending the single-predicate sequence-to-sequence model to a multi-predicate version using Grid-RNNs. While effective, the performance gain from this extension is modest. The paper also notes that the impact of modeling predicate interactions is somewhat smaller than that reported in Ouchi et al. (2015), which raises questions about the relative benefits of the different modeling approaches.
*   A more significant concern is the omission of a discussion and comparison with Shibata et al. (2016). This prior work also extended the model of Ouchi et al. (2015) using neural networks to jointly model multiple predicates. Given the strong similarities in motivation and approach, a direct comparison or at least a thorough discussion of the differences between the proposed Grid-RNN model and the model in Shibata et al. (2016) is essential for contextualizing this paper's contribution. Could the authors comment on the relationship between these two models and speculate on their relative advantages and disadvantages? A clarification on this point would greatly strengthen the paper.
