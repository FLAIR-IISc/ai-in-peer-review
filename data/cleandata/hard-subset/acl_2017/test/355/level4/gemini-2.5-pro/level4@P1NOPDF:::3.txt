This paper proposes a joint neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis based on Grid-RNNs. The proposed model is compared against a conventional single-sequence RNN approach.

This is a solidly executed paper on a well-established task in Japanese NLP. The authors achieve state-of-the-art results and present the task in a manner that is largely accessible to readers unfamiliar with Japanese.

That said, the paper could have better highlighted the task's complexity. For instance, the authors could have used the example in Figure 1 to walk the reader through the inherent ambiguity where the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate are also potential NOM arguments for the second predicate. Additionally, a clearer description of how Japanese PAS analysis contrasts with standard Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly concerning the challenge of zero pronouns, would have been beneficial. I would also have liked to see statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are resolvable within a sentence. This would help quantify a key difficulty of the task: the need to implicitly distinguish between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors.

One point was unclear: in the case of an inter-sentential zero pronoun, what representation is used for that predicate's argument? Is the argument slot left unmarked, or is it explicitly marked as empty? I inferred from the paper that the former is the case, meaning there is no explicit representation of the zero pronoun itself. This seems like a potential weakness in the representation, as it might hinder the model's ability to learn about zero pronouns. Some discussion of this design choice would have been welcome.

The model does not appear to explicitly enforce common linguistic constraints (often handled by ILP-based SRL methods), such as: (1) a given predicate generally takes only one argument of a given type (especially NOM and ACC); and (2) a given phrase generally fills only one argument slot for a given predicate. An analysis of the model's output would be valuable to determine if it learns these constraints implicitly.

More generally, given the mixed results in Table 3 between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models (where the Multi-Seq model only shows a clear improvement for the NOM case), a discussion of the qualitative differences in their outputs would have strengthened the paper. For example, are their outputs largely identical, or are they different while achieving similar aggregate scores? In what contexts do the models' predictions diverge? This kind of analysis, which would offer deeper insight into the models' behavior, would elevate the paper from solid to strong. It represents the main area for potential improvement, aside from a more direct comparison with SRL models (which would admittedly require substantial additional work).

The paper is well-presented, and the figures are particularly helpful for understanding the proposed model. There are a few minor English errors and typos, but they do not impede comprehension:
