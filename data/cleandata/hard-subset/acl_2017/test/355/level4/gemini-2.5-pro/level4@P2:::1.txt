### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural network approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that does not rely on syntactic features. The core problem addressed is that previous state-of-the-art systems heavily depend on syntactic parsers, making them vulnerable to error propagation. To overcome this, the authors introduce two models: a "single-sequence" model using stacked Bidirectional RNNs to analyze each predicate independently, and a more advanced "multi-sequence" model using Grid-RNNs to jointly model interactions between all predicates in a sentence. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that both models, particularly the multi-sequence model, outperform previous syntax-based systems, with significant improvements in the challenging task of identifying zero arguments (dropped pronouns).

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Syntax-Free Architecture for Joint PAS Analysis:** The primary contribution is the multi-sequence Grid-RNN model, which effectively captures interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence from the raw word sequence. This demonstrates that complex semantic relationships can be learned without explicit syntactic information, providing a robust end-to-end alternative to traditional pipeline-based approaches.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance on a Challenging Task:** The paper presents strong empirical results, establishing a new state-of-the-art on the standard Japanese PAS benchmark (NAIST Text Corpus). The model's success is particularly notable on the difficult sub-task of zero argument identification, a long-standing challenge in pro-drop languages.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Performance without Syntactic Features:** The paper's most significant strength is its ability to achieve state-of-the-art results while deliberately avoiding the use of syntactic features from parsers. The multi-sequence model outperforms the previous best model (Ouchi et al., 2015), which relies on syntactic dependencies. This is a compelling result, as it shows a path to building more robust semantic analysis systems that are not bottlenecked by the performance of an upstream syntactic parser, thereby avoiding the classic problem of error propagation.
2.  **Effective Solution for Zero Argument Identification:** The work makes a substantial contribution to the difficult problem of identifying zero arguments (dropped pronouns). As shown in Table 1, the proposed models yield a significant improvement of 2-3 F-measure points over the previous state-of-the-art for the "Zero" argument category. The superior performance of the multi-sequence model over the single-sequence model on this specific task strongly supports the authors' hypothesis that modeling multi-predicate interactions is crucial for resolving these ambiguous cases.
3.  **Clarity of Exposition and Model Design:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors first introduce a simpler baseline RNN model (single-sequence) and then clearly motivate and explain its extension to the more complex Grid-RNN architecture (multi-sequence). The architectural diagrams, feature descriptions, and experimental setup are detailed and transparent, which enhances the reproducibility and credibility of the work. The analysis of network depth and residual connections (Table 2) further adds to the thoroughness of the empirical investigation.

### Weaknesses

My critiques are minor and are mainly suggestions for improving the paper's presentation and analysis.

1.  **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth qualitative analysis or error analysis. For example, presenting a specific sentence where the multi-sequence model succeeds in identifying a zero argument while the single-sequence model fails would provide a concrete illustration of the Grid-RNN's advantages. Such an example would offer valuable insight into *how* the learned multi-predicate interactions help resolve ambiguities in practice, moving beyond the aggregate F-scores.
2.  **Minor Lack of Clarity in Presentation:** A few details in the presentation could be clarified.
    *   In Table 2, the meaning of the boldfaced numbers is not explicitly stated. While one can infer they represent the best performance in their respective settings, this should be mentioned in the caption for clarity.
    *   The description of the PRED feature (Section 3.1) could be strengthened with a better illustration. For instance, using the example sentence from Figure 4, it would be helpful to explicitly show the window of words around the predicate "食べた (ate)" that constitute the PRED feature for an argument candidate like "彼女 (she)".
