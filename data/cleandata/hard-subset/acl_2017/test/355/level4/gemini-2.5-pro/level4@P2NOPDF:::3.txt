This paper presents a joint neural model using Grid-RNNs for Predicate-Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese. The proposed model is compared against a conventional single-sequence RNN approach and achieves state-of-the-art results on this established task. The work is solidly executed, and the authors do a commendable job of presenting the task in a manner that is accessible to a non-Japanese-speaking audience.

Despite these strengths, the paper could be significantly improved with a more detailed discussion of the task's nuances and a deeper analysis of the model's behavior.

**Task Complexity and Zero Anaphora**

While the paper provides a good introduction to Japanese PAS analysis, its contribution could be strengthened by further emphasizing the task's complexity, particularly for readers unfamiliar with Japanese linguistics.
*   The example in Figure 1 presents an excellent opportunity to walk the reader through the inherent ambiguity in argument identification—for instance, the ambiguity between the NOM/ACC arguments of the first predicate and the NOM argument of the second.
*   The paper should more clearly contrast Japanese PAS analysis with standard Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), especially regarding the central role of zero pronouns (unexpressed arguments).
*   To this end, providing statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns in the dataset that are resolvable intra-sententially would be highly informative. This would highlight the challenge the model faces in implicitly distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential anaphors.

Furthermore, the paper's handling of inter-sentential zero pronouns is unclear. How does the model represent an argument slot filled by a pronoun whose antecedent is outside the sentence? Is the argument slot simply left unmarked, or is an explicit "empty" marker used? It appears to be the former, which could be a representational weakness, as the model is not explicitly informed that a zero pronoun exists. A discussion of this design choice and its potential impact on the model's ability to capture zero anaphora would be welcome.

**Model Analysis and Constraints**

The current analysis lacks a deep dive into the model's output, which would provide valuable insights into what it has learned.
*   The proposed model does not appear to explicitly enforce common linguistic constraints that are often modeled in ILP-based approaches. These include: (1) a predicate generally takes only one argument of a given core type (e.g., one NOM, one ACC), and (2) a single word/phrase generally fills only one argument slot for a given predicate. An error analysis examining how often the model violates these constraints would be very useful for understanding its limitations and whether it learns these properties implicitly.
*   The quantitative comparison in Table 3 reveals mixed results, with the Multi-Seq model's improvement over the Single-Seq baseline being concentrated almost entirely on NOM arguments. This warrants a more detailed qualitative analysis. Are the predictions from the two models largely similar, or are they qualitatively different while achieving comparable aggregate scores? Identifying the specific linguistic contexts where one model outperforms the other would provide much deeper insight into their respective behaviors.

Incorporating this kind of analysis would shed light on the model's internal workings and elevate the paper from a solid contribution to a strong one. In my view, this is the primary area for improvement.
