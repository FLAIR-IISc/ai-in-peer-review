This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-RNNs, comparing its performance against a conventional single-sequence RNN baseline. This is a well-executed paper that achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging and well-established task. The authors do a commendable job of presenting the task in a manner that is largely accessible to an audience not familiar with Japanese linguistics.

That said, the paper could be strengthened by providing more context on the task's inherent complexities. For instance, when discussing the example in Figure 1, a more explicit walkthrough of the ambiguity—where arguments of the first predicate could also serve as the NOM argument of the second—would be beneficial. Similarly, a clearer articulation of how Japanese PAS analysis differs from standard Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly concerning the prevalence and ambiguity of zero pronouns, would strengthen the paper's motivation. To this end, providing statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are resolvable intra-sententially would be valuable. This would underscore the difficulty of the task, which requires the model to implicitly distinguish between intra- and inter-sentential anaphora.

A crucial point that requires clarification is the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns. In cases where the argument of a predicate is a zero pronoun resolved outside the sentence, does the model simply omit the argument slot, or is there a specific marker for an empty argument? The current text suggests the former. If so, there is no explicit representation that a zero pronoun exists, which seems like a potentially limiting representational choice that could impact the model's ability to learn about these phenomena. Some discussion of this design choice and its implications would be appreciated.

The paper would be substantially improved by a deeper analysis of the model's behavior and its handling of linguistic constraints. Many traditional methods for SRL and related tasks (e.g., those using ILP) explicitly model constraints such as:
1.  A predicate typically accepts only one argument of a given core grammatical function (e.g., one NOM, one ACC).
2.  A single argument phrase generally fills only one semantic role for a given predicate.

While the proposed neural model does not explicitly enforce these constraints, an analysis of the system's output could reveal whether it learns them implicitly. Such an analysis would provide valuable insight into the model's capabilities beyond aggregate performance metrics.

Furthermore, the mixed results in Table 3—where the Multi-Seq model shows a clear advantage primarily for NOM arguments—warrant more detailed discussion. A qualitative comparison of the outputs from the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models would be highly informative. Are their predictions largely identical, with minor differences accounting for the score variation? Or do they produce qualitatively different analyses that result in comparable aggregate scores? Identifying the specific linguistic contexts where one model outperforms the other would illuminate the practical differences between the architectures and the true impact of the joint modeling approach. This level of analysis is the main area for improvement and could elevate the paper from a solid contribution to a truly strong and insightful one.

The paper is well-presented, and the figures effectively illustrate the proposed model. There are a few minor grammatical and typographical errors that should be corrected for the final version:
