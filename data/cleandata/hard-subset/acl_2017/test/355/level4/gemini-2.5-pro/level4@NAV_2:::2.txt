This paper presents a neural approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis. The authors propose two models that operate directly on word sequences, aiming to circumvent error propagation from external syntactic parsers. The first is a single-sequence model using stacked Bi-RNNs to predict arguments for each predicate independently. The primary proposal is a multi-sequence model employing Grid-RNNs to capture and leverage interactions between all predicates within a sentence. The evaluation, conducted on the standard NAIST Text Corpus, demonstrates that the proposed models achieve a new state-of-the-art result, outperforming previous syntax-reliant methods, particularly on the challenging task of zero-argument identification.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel multi-sequence model for Japanese PAS analysis that uses a Grid-RNN architecture to explicitly model interactions between multiple predicates. This syntax-free approach proves particularly effective for resolving zero arguments, a key challenge in the field.
2.  The demonstration that a purely sequence-based model can achieve state-of-the-art performance, surpassing prior joint models that depend heavily on syntactic features. This suggests a valuable direction for developing simpler and more robust end-to-end systems for semantic analysis.

### Strengths
1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance without Syntactic Features:** A key strength of this work is its ability to achieve state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark without relying on any syntactic information. The proposed multi-sequence model surpasses the previous best system (Ouchi et al., 2015) by over 2 points in F-measure (81.42 vs. 79.23). This is a compelling result, as it effectively mitigates the problem of error propagation from upstream parsing components, which has been a major bottleneck for previous systems.
2.  **Effective Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions:** The central technical contribution—the Grid-RNN model for capturing inter-predicate dependencies—is well-motivated and empirically validated. The experiments clearly show that the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms its strong single-sequence counterpart (Table 1). Crucially, the improvement is most pronounced for identifying zero arguments (+1.02 F1), which directly supports the authors' hypothesis that modeling these interactions is beneficial for this notoriously difficult subtask.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis and Clear Presentation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed. The authors compare against strong and appropriate baselines, report significance tests, and provide insightful analysis with performance broken down by argument type and case role (Table 3). Additionally, the study on the effects of network depth and residual connections (Table 2) offers practical guidance for building deep models for this task. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the methodology and results easy to comprehend.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Incremental Novelty of the Single-Sequence Model:** The single-sequence model serves as a strong baseline but is largely an adaptation of the Bi-RNN architecture proposed by Zhou and Xu (2015) for English Semantic Role Labeling. While its successful application to Japanese PAS is a contribution, the paper's core novelty rests almost entirely on the multi-sequence extension.
2.  **Modest Performance Gain from the Multi-Sequence Architecture:** Although the improvement of the multi-sequence model over the single-sequence model is statistically significant, the absolute gain in overall F-measure is relatively small (+0.27%). This raises the question of whether the added complexity of the Grid-RNN architecture provides a sufficient performance benefit. The paper would be strengthened by a more qualitative analysis illustrating specific cases where the multi-predicate modeling was essential for correct predictions.
3.  **Omission of a Key Neural Baseline Comparison:** The paper does not compare against or discuss the work of Shibata et al. (2016), who also proposed a neural network-based model for Japanese PAS that builds on the joint modeling ideas of Ouchi et al. (2015). Although their model uses syntactic features, it is a highly relevant neural baseline. A discussion of the architectural differences and potential performance trade-offs is necessary to fully contextualize the contribution of the proposed syntax-free model.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The work of Shibata et al. (2016) also presents a neural model for Japanese PAS that incorporates joint modeling of predicates. Could you elaborate on the key differences between your syntax-free approach and their syntax-based neural model? A discussion on why a direct comparison was not included would be helpful to better situate your results.
2.  The overall F1-score improvement of the Multi-Sequence model over the strong Single-Sequence baseline is 0.27%. Could you provide a qualitative analysis or some examples that illustrate cases where modeling multi-predicate interactions was crucial for a correct prediction, especially for zero arguments? This would help clarify the practical impact of the Grid-RNN architecture.
