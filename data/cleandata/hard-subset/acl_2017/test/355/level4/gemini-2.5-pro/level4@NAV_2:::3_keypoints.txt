- Paper proposes a Grid-RNN multi-sequence neural model for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that captures interactions between all predicates from the word sequence without syntactic parsers.
- Model achieves new state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, notably improving zero-argument identification.

- Contribution: a multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture enabling joint modeling of multi-predicate interactions without relying on upstream syntactic parsers.
- Contribution: empirical demonstration of significant performance gains, especially on zero argument identification, outperforming prior syntax-aware joint models.

- Strength: strong empirical performance with state-of-the-art results and substantial gains over strong baselines, particularly for zero arguments.
- Strength: well-motivated, elegant architecture that shares information across predicate-centric views and addresses pipeline error propagation.
- Strength: solid experimental setup with a strong single-sequence baseline, analysis of depth and residual connections, and clear writing/figures.

- Weakness: lacks qualitative/error analysis showing why the multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence model and which cases benefit from predicate interactions.
- Weakness: model predicts independently per word-predicate pair via softmax, not explicitly enforcing linguistic constraints (e.g., single core argument per predicate) and lacks analysis of constraint violations.
- Weakness: unclear treatment of inter-sentential zero pronouns—paper doesn't specify whether outside-sentence arguments are labeled as null or ignored, nor gives statistics on intra- vs. inter-sentential zeros.

- Question: How are inter-sentential zero arguments handled—labeled as `null` during training or excluded from loss?
- Question: Have you measured how often the model violates linguistic constraints (e.g., predicts multiple core-role arguments for one predicate)?
- Question: Can you provide qualitative examples where the multi-sequence model correctly identifies (especially zero) arguments that the single-sequence model misses?
