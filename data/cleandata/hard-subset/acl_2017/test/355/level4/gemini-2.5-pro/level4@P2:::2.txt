### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a particular focus on the challenging problem of identifying omitted arguments (zero arguments). The authors propose a neural sequence modeling approach that, unlike previous state-of-the-art systems, does not rely on syntactic features from external parsers, thereby avoiding potential error propagation. The work introduces two models: a "single-sequence" model using stacked Bidirectional RNNs to analyze each predicate independently, and a more novel "multi-sequence" model that employs a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly process all predicates in a sentence and explicitly model their interactions. Experiments conducted on the standard NAIST Text Corpus benchmark show that both proposed models outperform the previous state-of-the-art, with the multi-sequence Grid-RNN model achieving the best performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A State-of-the-Art Syntax-Free Model:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that a purely sequence-based neural model can achieve state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS analysis, surpassing previous methods that depend heavily on syntactic information. This is significant because it shows that a sufficiently powerful neural architecture can implicitly learn the complex relationships required for this task directly from text, mitigating the pipeline dependency on syntactic parsers.

2.  **A Novel Application of Grid-RNNs for Multi-Predicate Interaction:** The paper introduces a novel and effective method for modeling the interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence. The proposed multi-sequence model, based on Grid-RNNs, provides a principled, end-to-end architecture for jointly analyzing all predicates. The empirical results validate that this joint modeling is particularly beneficial for the difficult task of zero argument identification.

### Strengths

1.  **New State-of-the-Art without Syntactic Features:** The paper's strongest argument for acceptance is its impressive empirical results. The proposed models outperform the previous state-of-the-art system (Ouchi et al., 2015) on a standard benchmark dataset (Table 1). The fact that this is achieved without any syntactic features is a compelling result, as it removes a major source of potential errors from upstream tools and simplifies the overall system architecture. The single-sequence model alone improves the F1-score from 79.23 to 81.15, which is a substantial gain.

2.  **Effective Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions:** The multi-sequence model provides a clear and well-motivated approach to capturing inter-predicate dependencies. The use of Grid-RNNs is an elegant way to allow information to flow between the processing streams for each predicate. The results confirm the value of this approach, showing a statistically significant improvement over the very strong single-sequence baseline. Crucially, the gains are most pronounced for zero arguments (F1-score of 47.12 vs. 46.10), which directly supports the authors' central hypothesis that predicate interactions are key to resolving these non-local dependencies.

3.  **Clarity and Thorough Analysis:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The architectural progression from the single-sequence to the multi-sequence model is logical and well-explained. The experimental analysis is thorough, including not only comparisons with baselines but also an ablation study on the effect of network depth and residual connections (Table 2) and a detailed per-case-role performance breakdown (Table 3). The public release of the source code further enhances the value and reproducibility of this work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Performance Gain of the Main Contribution:** While the multi-sequence Grid-RNN model is the paper's core technical novelty, its performance improvement over the simpler single-sequence model is relatively small (an absolute F1 improvement of 0.27 points). The single-sequence model, which is a straightforward application of an existing architecture (Zhou and Xu, 2015) to Japanese PAS, is responsible for the vast majority of the gains over prior work. This makes the impact of the more complex Grid-RNN architecture appear somewhat limited, suggesting that the main discovery is the general effectiveness of deep Bi-RNNs for this task, rather than the specific benefits of the grid structure.

2.  **Missing Comparison with a Key Neural Baseline:** The paper positions its contribution against the syntax-based joint model of Ouchi et al. (2015). However, it fails to discuss or compare its work with Shibata et al. (2016), who also proposed a neural network-based model for Japanese PAS that builds upon Ouchi et al. (2015). Although Shibata et al.'s model still uses syntactic features, it represents a more direct and recent neural competitor. A discussion of the architectural differences and relative merits would be necessary to properly situate this work within the literature and to fully appreciate the contribution of the proposed syntax-free approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The performance gain from the multi-sequence Grid-RNN model over the strong single-sequence baseline is statistically significant but modest in absolute terms. Could you provide a more detailed analysis or intuition regarding this? For example, are there specific types of sentences or predicate interaction phenomena where the Grid-RNN provides a substantial benefit, and others where it offers little advantage over the single-sequence model?

2.  Could you please elaborate on the relationship between your work and that of Shibata et al. (2016)? Since both papers employ neural networks to model multi-predicate relationships in Japanese PAS, it would be very helpful to understand the key advantages of your syntax-free, Grid-RNN architecture compared to their approach of integrating a feed-forward network into a syntax-dependent joint model.
