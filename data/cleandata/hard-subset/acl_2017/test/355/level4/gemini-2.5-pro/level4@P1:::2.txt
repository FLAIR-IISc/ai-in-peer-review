### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on identifying arguments without relying on syntactic features. The authors first propose a "Single-Sequence" model, adapting the bidirectional RNN architecture from Zhou and Xu (2015) to the Japanese PAS task. They then extend this with a novel "Multi-Sequence" model using Grid-RNNs, which is designed to jointly model all predicates in a sentence and capture their interactions. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that both models outperform the previous state-of-the-art, with the multi-sequence model achieving the best results, particularly on the difficult task of identifying zero arguments.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel neural architecture (the Multi-Sequence model) that uses Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence for Japanese PAS analysis. This allows for joint modeling without explicit syntactic dependency information.
2.  An empirical demonstration that sequence-only neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results on this task, outperforming previous syntax-reliant systems. This is a valuable finding, as it offers a way to avoid the problem of error propagation from upstream syntactic parsers.

### Strengths

1.  **New State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed models achieve a significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art system (Ouchi et al., 2015) on the standard NAIST Text Corpus benchmark. As shown in Table 1, the Multi-Sequence model improves the overall F-measure from 79.23% to 81.42%. Crucially, this is achieved without any syntactic information, which simplifies the processing pipeline and avoids potential error propagation from parsers, a key limitation of prior work.

2.  **Effective Identification of Zero Arguments:** The paper's focus is on improving the analysis of omitted arguments (zero arguments), a key challenge in Japanese. The proposed models show substantial gains on this subtask. The Multi-Sequence model improves the F-measure for zero arguments by over 3 points compared to Ouchi et al. (2015) (47.12% vs. 44.09%). This suggests the RNN-based architectures are particularly effective at capturing the sentence-level context required to resolve these long-distance dependencies.

3.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Presented Work:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear justification for moving away from syntax-based models and systematically build their argument, starting with a strong baseline (Single-Sequence model) and extending it to the more complex Multi-Sequence model to test their hypothesis about multi-predicate interactions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Marginal Improvement from the Multi-Sequence Model:** While the paper's primary novel contribution is the Multi-Sequence model designed to capture predicate interactions, its performance improvement over the simpler Single-Sequence baseline is quite small. The overall F-measure increases from 81.15% to 81.42% (a 0.27 point gain). While this gain is reported as statistically significant, it raises the question of whether the added complexity of the Grid-RNN architecture provides a substantial practical benefit over a strong, direct application of existing RNN sequence labeling models. The impact of explicitly modeling predicate interactions appears less pronounced here than in prior work like Ouchi et al. (2015) which introduced the joint modeling concept.

2.  **Missing Comparison to Relevant Neural Baseline:** The paper compares its models against Ouchi et al. (2015), a non-neural joint model. However, it omits a comparison or substantive discussion regarding the work of Shibata et al. (2016), who also applied a neural network to this task by integrating it into the joint model of Ouchi et al. (2015). Although Shibata et al. (2016) used syntactic features, a comparison would be crucial for contextualizing the current work's contribution. It is currently unclear whether the reported gains are primarily due to the novel Grid-RNN architecture or due to the general advantages of end-to-end RNNs over feature-engineered models, and how this syntax-free approach compares to a syntax-aware neural model.
