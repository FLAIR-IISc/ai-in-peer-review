- Paper proposes two neural sequence models for Japanese PAS: a stacked Bi-RNN single-sequence baseline and a Grid-RNN multi-sequence model that jointly models all predicates.  
- Models operate directly on word sequences, avoiding reliance on syntactic parsers and their error propagation.  
- Grid-RNN is designed to capture interactions across predicates to help resolve omitted (zero) arguments.  
- Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show both models outperform prior state-of-the-art, with Grid-RNN achieving the best results.  
- Main contribution: novel application of a Grid-RNN architecture for joint PAS analysis across predicates in a sentence.  
- Main contribution: demonstration that sequence-based neural models can surpass syntax-dependent systems, removing parser error bottlenecks.  
- Main contribution: new state-of-the-art empirical results on a standard benchmark, especially improving zero-argument identification.  
- Strength: strong empirical improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015), notably +3.03 F1 on zero arguments for the multi-sequence model.  
- Strength: well-motivated, novel architecture that naturally models both word-wise and cross-predicate information flow.  
- Strength: clear presentation and effective figures that distinguish the single- and multi-sequence models.  
- Weakness: lack of qualitative/error analysis comparing single- and multi-sequence outputs to explain where grid-RNN helps.  
- Weakness: model makes independent softmax decisions per word-predicate pair and does not enforce structural constraints (e.g., one core role per predicate), with no analysis of invalid outputs.  
- Weakness: unclear handling of inter-sentential zero arguments (Inter-Zero) during training/evaluation and potential ambiguity in labels.  
- Question: how are inter-sentential zero arguments annotated and labeled in training/evaluation—treated as null or otherwise?  
- Question: please provide qualitative/error examples where Multi-Sequence corrects Single-Sequence errors due to predicate interactions.  
- Question: does the model produce structurally invalid outputs (e.g., multiple NOMs per predicate) and how often does this occur?  
- Minor comments: several small typos and wording fixes suggested and a request for consistent capitalization in references.
