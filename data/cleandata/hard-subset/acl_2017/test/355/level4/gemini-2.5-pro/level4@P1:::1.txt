### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a neural network approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis. The primary contribution is a model that uses a Grid-type Recurrent Neural Network (Grid-RNN) to jointly model interactions between all predicates within a sentence. A significant aspect of this work is that it achieves state-of-the-art performance without relying on any explicit syntactic information from parsers, thus avoiding the common problem of error propagation. The authors compare a simpler single-sequence RNN model, which analyzes each predicate independently, with their proposed multi-sequence Grid-RNN model. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that their syntax-free neural models outperform previous systems that use syntactic features, with the Grid-RNN model showing particular strength in the challenging task of identifying zero arguments.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance Without Syntactic Features:** The paper's most significant strength is its demonstration that a purely sequence-based neural model can outperform previous state-of-the-art systems that rely heavily on syntactic parse information. This is an important result, as it offers a way to bypass the problem of error propagation from upstream parsers, which is a known bottleneck in pipelined NLP systems. The strong results validate the authors' hypothesis that RNNs can effectively induce the necessary contextual features directly from the word sequence.

2.  **Novel Application of Grid-RNNs for Multi-Predicate Modeling:** The application of Grid-RNNs to jointly model interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence is novel and well-motivated. The architecture provides an elegant way to pass information between the parallel analyses of different predicates. The experiments convincingly show that this joint modeling approach (the "multi-sequence model") yields statistically significant improvements over an already strong single-predicate baseline (the "single-sequence model").

3.  **Thorough Analysis and Strong Empirical Results:** The paper is detailed, clearly written, and presents a thorough empirical evaluation. The authors provide a clear description of their models and experimental setup. The results are broken down by argument type (Dep vs. Zero) and case role, providing valuable insights. In particular, the significant gains observed for zero argument identification (+3.0 F1 points over the previous state-of-the-art) highlight the effectiveness of the proposed model for one of the most challenging aspects of Japanese PAS analysis.

### Weaknesses

My criticisms of this work are minor and mostly concern points of clarification that would strengthen the paper.

1.  **Unclear Formatting in Tables:** In Table 2, the use of boldface is not explained. While one can infer that it highlights the best-performing model in a given setting, this should be explicitly stated in the caption for clarity. This would help the reader to more easily interpret the results concerning the effects of network depth and residual connections.

2.  **Motivation for PRED Feature Could Be Clearer:** While the feature design is straightforward and effective, the motivation for the `PRED` feature (a window of words around the predicate, described in Section 3.1) could be more explicitly connected to linguistic phenomena. For instance, the authors could mention that this window is useful for capturing crucial local cues like case-marking particles, which are often adjacent to the predicate in Japanese. Illustrating this point with the example from Figure 4 would make the feature's design choice more intuitive.
