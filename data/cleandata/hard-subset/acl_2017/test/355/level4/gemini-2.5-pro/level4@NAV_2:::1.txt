**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper presents a neural network approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis. The core of the proposed method is a multi-sequence model based on a Grid-type Recurrent Neural Network (Grid-RNN) that jointly analyzes all predicates in a sentence. Notably, this model operates directly on word sequences and does not require any syntactic information from external parsers. The experiments, conducted on the NAIST Text Corpus, show that the proposed model surpasses the performance of previous state-of-the-art systems.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel neural model that achieves state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS analysis without relying on syntactic features. This is a significant contribution as it avoids the common problem of error propagation from upstream parsing components, which prior work depended on.
2.  An effective application of a Grid-RNN architecture to explicitly model interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence. This approach is shown to be particularly beneficial for the difficult task of zero argument identification, a key challenge in Japanese.
3.  A thorough and clear empirical evaluation demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed architecture. The authors provide detailed results, comparing against strong baselines and analyzing performance on different argument types (Dep vs. Zero) and case roles.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive performance without syntactic dependency:** The strongest argument for this paper is that its syntax-free approach outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods that explicitly use syntactic parse information. By learning directly from word sequences, the model circumvents the potential for error propagation from parsers, offering a more robust and self-contained solution for PAS analysis. The results in Table 1 clearly demonstrate this advantage over the Ouchi et al. (2015) model.
2.  **Effective handling of zero arguments:** The paper effectively addresses the challenging problem of zero-pronoun resolution in Japanese PAS. The multi-sequence model, which captures inter-predicate dependencies, shows a significant F-measure improvement of approximately 3 points for "Zero" arguments over the previous state-of-the-art (Table 1). This result provides strong evidence that the proposed grid-based architecture is well-suited to capturing the sentence-wide context needed to resolve these ambiguous cases.
3.  **Clarity and detailed methodology:** The paper is well-written and provides a clear, detailed description of the model architectures, from the feature representations in the input layer to the single-sequence and multi-sequence RNN models. This level of detail, combined with a well-documented experimental setup, enhances the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Minor clarity issues in presentation:** While the paper is generally clear, there are minor areas where the presentation could be improved. For example, the meaning of the boldface formatting used in Table 2 is not explained, leaving its interpretation to the reader. Similarly, the explanation of the "PRED" feature could be enhanced with a more explicit example to illustrate how context words around the predicate are incorporated.
2.  **Minor grammatical errors:** The manuscript contains several minor typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "As an solution," inconsistent use of articles before model names). These errors do not obscure the paper's meaning but detract from its overall quality. A careful proofreading pass would be beneficial for the final version.
