This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach using Grid-RNNs for Predicate-Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, comparing it against a conventional single-sequence RNN baseline.

This is a well-executed paper on the established task of Japanese PAS analysis. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results, and the authors present the task in a manner that is largely accessible to readers unfamiliar with Japanese. However, the paper would be strengthened by further emphasizing the complexity of the task. For instance, using the example in Figure 1, the authors could walk through the inherent ambiguity where the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate are also potential NOM arguments for the second predicate. A more explicit comparison with standard Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly highlighting the challenges posed by zero-pronoun ambiguity, would also be beneficial.

Additionally, providing statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are resolvable intra-sententially would help contextualize the task's difficulty, as the model must implicitly distinguish between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors. A key point also requires clarification: how are inter-sentential zero pronouns represented for a given predicate's argument? Is the argument slot left unmarked, or is it explicitly marked as empty? The paper seems to imply the former. If so, the lack of an explicit representation for a zero pronoun could be a limitation, potentially hindering the model's ability to learn about them. A discussion of this design choice and its implications would be valuable.

The proposed model does not appear to explicitly enforce common linguistic constraints, which are often modeled in ILP-based SRL systems. These include constraints such as: (1) a predicate typically takes only one argument of a given type (e.g., one NOM, one ACC), and (2) an argument phrase typically fills only one semantic role for a given predicate. An analysis of the model's output to determine whether it learns these constraints implicitly would have been insightful.

Furthermore, the results in Table 3 show a mixed performance difference between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models, with the latter showing clear improvement only for the NOM case. This calls for a more qualitative analysis. Are the outputs of the two models largely similar, or are they qualitatively different while achieving similar aggregate scores? In what specific contexts do their predictions diverge? Such an analysis would provide valuable insight into the models' behaviors and elevate the paper from solid to strong. This is the primary area for improvement.

The overall presentation is clear, with helpful figures. However, there are several minor typos and formatting issues that should be corrected:
