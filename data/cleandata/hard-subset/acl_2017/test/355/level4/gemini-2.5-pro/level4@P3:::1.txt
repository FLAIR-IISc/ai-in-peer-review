### Review

**Summary**
This paper addresses the task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenge of error propagation from syntactic parsers in existing systems. The authors propose two neural sequence models: a baseline single-sequence Bi-RNN model that handles predicates independently, and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to jointly model interactions between all predicates in a sentence. The key idea is to learn effective features directly from the word sequence, avoiding any reliance on explicit syntactic information. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that the proposed models, particularly the multi-sequence Grid-RNN, outperform the previous state-of-the-art.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is a novel application of Grid-RNNs to jointly model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis directly from word sequences. This end-to-end approach successfully bypasses the need for syntactic parsers, thereby avoiding the common issue of error propagation.
2.  The paper provides a strong empirical result, establishing a new state-of-the-art on the standard NAIST Text Corpus benchmark. The proposed model demonstrates significant improvements over prior syntax-based methods, especially on the difficult sub-task of identifying zero arguments (dropped pronouns).

**Strengths**
1.  **Effective Syntax-Free Modeling:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that a sophisticated neural architecture can outperform systems that rely heavily on explicit syntactic features. By learning directly from word sequences, the model avoids the pipeline problem of error propagation from parsers, a known limitation in previous work. This is a significant finding for PAS analysis, particularly in languages like Japanese.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance on a Challenging Task:** The proposed multi-sequence model achieves a new state-of-the-art F-measure on the NAIST Text Corpus (Table 1). The gains are particularly impressive for identifying 'Zero' arguments (an improvement of over 3 F-points compared to Ouchi et al., 2015), a notoriously difficult aspect of Japanese PAS. This highlights the effectiveness of the Grid-RNN architecture in capturing the sentence-wide context needed to resolve these ambiguities.
3.  **Clarity and Thoroughness:** The paper is well-written, clear, and provides a detailed description of the model architectures, experimental setup, and results. The analysis of network depth and the inclusion of residual connections (Table 2), as well as the detailed breakdown of performance by case role (Table 3), provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and contribute to the paper's high quality.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Minor Lack of Clarity in Presentation:** While the paper is generally very clear, a few details could be better explained. For instance, the motivation for including a context window for the PRED feature (Section 3.1) could be more explicitly illustrated with an example. Additionally, the meaning of the boldface formatting in Table 2 is not explained in the text, leaving the reader to guess its significance. These are minor points but addressing them would improve the paper's overall clarity.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  In Section 3.1, you describe the PRED feature as including a window of words around the target predicate. Could you provide a brief example or further justification for this design choice? For instance, does this help capture case markers or auxiliary verbs that are adjacent to the predicate and crucial for its interpretation?
2.  In Table 2, what is the meaning of the boldfaced numbers? Please clarify this in the final version of the paper.
