- Addresses Japanese PAS with frequent zero pronouns using a Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates from the word sequence to avoid syntactic parser reliance.  
- Shows superior performance on the NAIST corpus versus single-sequence RNN and prior syntax-based joint models, especially for zero arguments.

- Novel application of Grid-RNNs to jointly model multi-predicate interactions end-to-end without explicit syntactic features.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST benchmark, outperforming previous syntax-based joint models.  
- Empirically demonstrates multi-predicate modeling (Multi-Sequence) yields significant gains over a strong single-predicate baseline, notably for zero arguments.

- Strong empirical results with a substantial (≈3-point F) improvement for zero-argument identification.  
- Well-motivated, elegant model that captures inter-sequence predicate-argument connections and avoids parser error propagation.  
- Paper is clear, well-structured, and authors plan to release code, aiding reproducibility.

- Lacks qualitative analysis explaining why the Multi-Sequence model outperforms the Single-Sequence model and what linguistic phenomena it captures.  
- Does not analyze whether the model implicitly learns linguistic constraints (e.g., one NOM per predicate) or how often outputs violate such constraints.  
- Ambiguous treatment of inter-sentential zero pronouns and no corpus statistics on intra- vs. inter-sentential zero argument prevalence, leaving task scope unclear.

- Request a qualitative error analysis showing examples where Multi-Sequence succeeds and Single-Sequence fails, explaining Grid-RNN contributions.  
- Ask for analysis/reporting of how often the model violates basic predicate-argument constraints (e.g., multiple NOMs).  
- Clarify how inter-sentential zero arguments are represented (treated as `null`?) and the impact on learning.  
- Provide proportions of zero arguments in NAIST that are intra-sentential versus inter-sentential.
