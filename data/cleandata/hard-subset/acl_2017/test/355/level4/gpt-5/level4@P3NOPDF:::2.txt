Summary
This paper adapts the state-of-the-art English SRL model of Zhou and Xu (2015) to Japanese and further extends it with a Grid-RNN architecture to model interactions among arguments of multiple predicates. Experiments on a standard Japanese SRL benchmark show significant gains over the current best reported system. The Grid-RNN variant yields additional improvements, particularly for zero anaphora, which is the paper’s main focus.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and organization. The paper articulates the challenges of Japanese SRL and zero anaphora well and positions the approach relative to prior work.
- Strong empirical results. The adapted single-sequence model already surpasses the previous state of the art, indicating that modern neural SRL architectures transfer effectively to Japanese.
- Added value of predicate interaction modeling. The Grid-RNN further improves performance, mainly by better detecting zero arguments—an important and difficult aspect of Japanese SRL.

Weaknesses and Concerns
- Incremental contribution. The primary novelty is extending a single-sequence model to a multi-sequence/Grid-RNN framework. While useful, this feels incremental compared to earlier efforts that model multi-predicate interactions more aggressively (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015).
- Limited comparison to closely related neural work. Shibata et al. (2016) extends Ouchi et al. (2015) with neural modeling. A direct empirical comparison is crucial for establishing the contribution of the proposed Grid-RNN over existing neural approaches to multi-predicate interaction.
- Magnitude of interaction effect. The performance gains attributable to predicate interactions appear smaller than those reported by Ouchi et al. (2015). It would help to clarify whether this is due to differences in datasets, features, supervision, or optimization, or whether the single-sequence neural baseline already captures most of the useful contextual cues.
- Analysis depth. The paper highlights improvements on zero anaphora, but a deeper error analysis would strengthen the claims—e.g., breakdowns by argument type, distance to predicate, case marker presence/absence, and discourse distance.

Questions for the Authors
- How does the proposed Grid-RNN compare directly with Shibata et al. (2016) under identical data and evaluation protocols? If a direct comparison is not possible, can you provide a reasoned estimate or partial replication?
- What is the exact contribution of predicate interaction modeling versus other components (e.g., embeddings, character/subword features, dropout, CRF/decoding choices)? An ablation isolating the Grid-RNN’s effect would clarify this.
- Are the reported improvements statistically significant across multiple random seeds? Please state the test used and p-values.
- What is the computational overhead of the Grid-RNN relative to the single-sequence model (training time, memory, inference latency)?
- Does the Grid-RNN improve recall primarily for zero arguments, or does it also help precision? A precision/recall breakdown by role type and predicate class would be informative.

Suggestions
- Include a direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016) or, at minimum, replicate their setup to the extent possible for a fair head-to-head evaluation.
- Provide detailed ablations (with/without Grid-RNN; with/without predicate-level features; with/without cross-predicate connections) and report per-category metrics for zero arguments.
- Add qualitative examples showing how cross-predicate signals resolve difficult zero-anaphora cases, and contrast with failure modes of the single-sequence baseline.
- Clarify implementation details (initialization, pretrained embeddings, hyperparameters, decoding) to ensure reproducibility and to rule out confounds driving the gains.
- Discuss generalization: could the Grid-RNN approach benefit other pro-drop or non-configurational languages, or even English SRL with long-distance dependencies?

Presentation and Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. A schematic comparison between the single-sequence model and the Grid-RNN would help readers grasp where cross-predicate information flows. Ensure all datasets, splits, and evaluation scripts adhere to community standards and are clearly documented.

Overall Assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution: adapting a strong neural SRL model to Japanese yields new state-of-the-art results, and modeling predicate interactions with a Grid-RNN provides further gains on zero anaphora. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental, and the absence of a direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016) limits the strength of the empirical case. I lean toward acceptance if the camera-ready can include a thorough comparison and ablations clarifying the impact of predicate interactions, along with a deeper analysis of zero-anaphora improvements.
