Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis without relying on predicted syntax. It first builds a single-sequence BiRNN model that predicts case roles for each predicate using only sentence-level word sequence features. It then introduces a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture inter-predicate interactions.
- On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines, and the Grid-RNN further improves performance, especially on zero arguments. The work demonstrates that competitive, sometimes state-of-the-art, accuracy is achievable without explicit syntactic features, thereby reducing error propagation from parsing.

I see the primary contributions as:
1) A grid-type multi-sequence architecture that jointly models multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS without syntax.
2) A strong syntax-free single-sequence baseline adapted to dependency-based PAS that already surpasses prior systems.
3) An empirical analysis showing gains on zero arguments and the benefits of model depth and residual connections.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains without syntax: Both the single-sequence and Grid-RNN models outperform widely used baselines (e.g., Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015) on NAIST 1.5. This is notable given they avoid syntactic features, mitigating parser error propagation.
- Improvements on zero arguments: The Grid-RNN provides additional, statistically significant gains on zero arguments, which are traditionally the hardest cases in Japanese due to argument omission and long-distance dependencies. This directly targets a central challenge of PAS.
- Joint modeling of predicate interactions: The grid architecture explicitly connects representations across predicates, operationalizing the intuition in prior work that inter-predicate information aids PAS, while avoiding a pipeline dependency on parse trees.
- Sensible ablations: The paper investigates network depth and residual connections, showing deeper models with residuals yield better F-measures, which supports the modeling choices and improves reproducibility.
- Presentation and scope: The paper is well organized, with a clear task setup, model descriptions (single vs multi-sequence), and evaluation on a standard benchmark.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural SRL/PAS: The single-sequence model closely follows established sequence models (e.g., Zhou and Xu, 2015), and Grid-RNNs are not new. The main novelty lies in applying a grid-style architecture to Japanese PAS without syntax. While useful, the conceptual leap may feel incremental.
- Incomplete comparisons to contemporary neural baselines: Shibata et al. (2016) also incorporate neural modeling within a strong joint framework. A direct empirical comparison or a careful discussion of the trade-offs (with/without syntax, model capacity) would clarify the contribution’s significance within the neural PAS landscape.
- Effect attribution: It is difficult to disentangle how much of the improvement comes from modeling inter-predicate interactions versus increased capacity or implicit context aggregation. Additional controls (e.g., matched-parameter single vs multi-sequence, shuffled predicate order, or disabled inter-sequence connections) would strengthen the causal claim.
- Narrow evaluation setting: Results are limited to NAIST 1.5 and intra-sentential arguments. While standard, the generality of the approach (e.g., cross-domain, other pro-drop languages, or inter-sentential cases) remains untested.
- Practical considerations underexplored: The paper does not quantify training/inference cost or memory overhead for Grid-RNNs vs single-sequence models, which is relevant for real-world deployment and for justifying the added complexity.

Questions for the authors
- How does your Grid-RNN compare directly with Shibata et al. (2016) when both are trained and evaluated under as similar settings as possible? If a direct comparison is not feasible, can you provide a discussion of expected differences (e.g., reliance on syntax, error propagation, computational cost)?
- Can you isolate the contribution of inter-predicate connections by matching parameter counts across single and multi-sequence models, or by ablating inter-sequence edges while keeping the grid depth?
- What is the computational overhead (training time, GPU memory, inference latency) of the Grid-RNN relative to the single-sequence model, as a function of the number of predicates per sentence?
- How sensitive are results to the predicate window size C in the input features and to embedding dimensions? Did you try pre-trained embeddings or character/subword features?
- The abstract mentions public code, but the URL is a placeholder. Will the code and scripts to reproduce the reported numbers be released, including preprocessing for NAIST 1.5 and the exact hyperparameters?
