Summary and contributions:
This paper proposes neural models for Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis by adapting the English state-of-the-art sequence model of Zhou and Xu (2015) and extending it to capture interactions among multiple predicates using Grid-RNNs. The models are evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus, showing statistically significant improvements over the current state-of-the-art system.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A neural single-sequence model adapted from Zhou and Xu (2015) to the Japanese PAS setting, achieving improved accuracy over prior systems.
2) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN model that explicitly models interactions across predicates and yields further gains, mainly via better detection of zero arguments.
3) An empirical evaluation on a standard benchmark demonstrating significant improvements over the previous state of the art.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation and organization: The paper is well-structured and frames the problem convincingly. The extension from a single-predicate sequence model to a multi-predicate framework is natural and well explained.
- Strong empirical results on a standard benchmark: The proposed models achieve higher accuracy than the current state-of-the-art system on the NAIST Text Corpus, with significance testing reported. This strengthens the empirical claim and facilitates comparison.
- Benefit of modeling multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN variant delivers slightly better performance than the single-sequence baseline. The gains are attributable primarily to improvements in zero argument detection, which is the central challenge the paper targets. This aligns the modeling choice with the empirical improvements and the paper’s stated focus.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty in the core architecture: The primary technical contribution appears to be extending a single-sequence model to a multi-sequence Grid-RNN to handle predicate interactions. While useful, this is a relatively incremental architectural extension rather than a fundamentally new modeling approach.
- Smaller impact of predicate interactions than prior work: The reported effect of multi-predicate interactions is modest, and appears smaller than the gains reported in Ouchi et al. (2015). It would be helpful to better contextualize why the interaction modeling helps less here than in that prior work.
- Missing comparison to closely related neural work: Shibata et al. (2016) extend Ouchi et al. (2015) with neural components. A direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016) is important to position the contribution relative to contemporaneous neural approaches; the absence of such a comparison makes it harder to assess the significance of the advances reported here.

Questions to authors (optional):
- Can you provide a direct comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), or explain why such a comparison is infeasible (e.g., differences in data splits, resources, or target subtasks)? Even a discussion-based comparison would help readers situate your results.
- The gains from modeling multi-predicate interactions are relatively small compared to Ouchi et al. (2015). Do you have an analysis (e.g., by case, distance, or sentence length) that explains when the Grid-RNN helps and when it does not?
- How sensitive are the improvements to architectural depth and hyperparameters in the Grid-RNN vs. the single-sequence model? For example, is the multi-predicate gain robust across depths, or concentrated at specific settings?
