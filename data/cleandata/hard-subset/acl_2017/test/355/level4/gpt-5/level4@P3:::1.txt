Summary and Contributions
- The paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that do not rely on predicted syntactic parses. The key contribution is a multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions.
- A strong single-sequence BiRNN baseline is introduced and carefully compared against the multi-sequence model, highlighting the added value of modeling predicate interactions.
- Comprehensive experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 show new state-of-the-art results without syntactic features, including analyses by argument type (Dep vs. Zero), case role (NOM/ACC/DAT), and network depth with residual connections.

Strengths
- State-of-the-art accuracy without syntax and reduced error propagation: Both the single- and multi-sequence models outperform strong syntax-based systems (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015), demonstrating that sentence-level context learned from word sequences is highly effective for Japanese PAS. On the test set, the multi-sequence model achieves 81.42 F vs. 79.23 for Ouchi et al. (2015), with significance tested via bootstrap resampling.
- Clear gains on zero pronouns: The models substantially improve Zero argument identification, which is the most challenging setting due to argument omission and lack of direct syntactic cues. Compared to Ouchi et al. (2015), the single-sequence model improves Zero by ~2.0 F and the multi-sequence model by ~3.0 F (46.10 → 47.12), indicating that RNN-based sentence-level context and multi-predicate information are both beneficial.
- Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions: The multi-sequence Grid-RNN significantly improves over the single-sequence baseline overall (81.42 vs. 81.15 F) and for Zero arguments (+~1 F), supporting the intuition that predicate interactions provide disambiguating signals, particularly for omitted arguments. This is a clean and well-motivated extension that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence.
- Strong case-role results, especially for DAT: The models deliver large gains on less frequent roles. For example, for Dep-DAT, the single-sequence model improves from 38.39 (Ouchi et al., 2015) to 65.91 F (+27.5), and the multi-sequence model reaches 64.38. These improvements suggest that the neural models learn robust representations even for sparse phenomena.
- Thorough experimental analysis and reproducibility: The paper reports implementation details, hyperparameters, and training settings; conducts depth and residual-connection ablations (showing deeper models with residuals yield ~+1 F); and evaluates per case role and argument type. Significance testing and code release further strengthen the empirical contribution.

Weaknesses
- Novelty of modeling approach is moderate: Grid-RNNs and multi-dimensional recurrent architectures are known; the main technical contribution is their application to multi-predicate PAS without syntax. The single-sequence baseline is already very strong, and the multi-sequence gains, while consistent, are modest overall (+0.27 F). The paper’s advances are primarily empirical and task-driven rather than architectural.
- Limited ablations on design choices: While depth/residual analyses are included, there is no ablation of key inputs (e.g., removing MARK or PRED-window features), no comparison against alternative mechanisms to capture cross-predicate dependencies (e.g., cross-sequence attention, shared self-attention over predicates, or joint CRF/ILP decoding), and no study of pretraining (word embeddings or language models) that could be relevant for zero anaphora.
- Scope and generalization: The work focuses on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero) and evaluates only on NAIST 1.5. How the approach scales to inter-sentential zero anaphora (where the search space and context needs are larger), and to other languages or SRL formalisms, remains open. Similarly, practical aspects (speed/memory) for sentences with many predicates are not discussed.
- Clarity issues: A few presentation points could be clearer. For example, the PRED feature includes a window around the predicate; an explicit example (e.g., Fig. 6) clarifying when the accusative marker appears with the predicate tokenization would help. The meaning of boldface in Table 2 is unclear. There are several minor English errors (listed below) that slightly hinder fluency.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- PRED feature: Could you clarify with a concrete example how the PRED window is constructed and when case markers (e.g., accusative) are included with the predicate? How sensitive are results to the window size C?
- Scaling and complexity: What are the training and inference time/memory costs as a function of the number of predicates M and sentence length T? Have you profiled performance on sentences with many predicates?
- Alternative interaction mechanisms: Did you try cross-sequence attention or a shared self-attention layer across predicates as a lighter-weight interaction than Grid-RNN? Any preliminary comparisons?
- M=1 case and robustness: When the sentence has only one predicate, does the multi-sequence model reduce to the single-sequence model behaviorally, or is there a noticeable difference?
- Incorporating syntax: Although your main goal is to avoid parser error propagation, do you anticipate gains from adding lightweight syntactic signals (e.g., dependency heads or distances) as features, and have you tried this?

Overall Assessment
This is a strong, well-executed paper that advances Japanese PAS by demonstrating that carefully designed sequence models can surpass syntax-based systems, with particularly meaningful gains on zero pronouns and DAT. The technical novelty is incremental, but the empirical contribution and clarity of the approach merit publication. I have read the author response and am satisfied with it.
