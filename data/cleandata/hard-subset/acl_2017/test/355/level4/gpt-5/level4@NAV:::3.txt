This paper presents a joint neural modeling approach to Japanese PAS built on Grid-RNNs, and compares it against a conventional single-sequence RNN setup.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper addressing a well-known Japanese task, achieving state-of-the-art performance and explaining the task in a way that is mostly accessible to readers without Japanese expertise. That said, I think you could better emphasize the task’s complexity—for example, in the Figure 1 example, by walking through the ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate versus the NOM argument of the second predicate—and more clearly delineate how the task differs from SRL (largely due to ambiguity from zero pronouns). I would also have liked to see statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are actually resolvable within the sentence, since this further complicates the task as framed (i.e., implicitly separating intra- vs. inter-sentential zero anaphora). One point that wasn’t clear to me: for an inter-sentential zero pronoun filling an argument of a given predicate, what representation do you adopt? Is the argument simply left unmarked, or is it explicitly marked as empty? My reading is the former, which would mean there is no explicit indication that a zero pronoun exists; this feels like a slightly flawed representation (potentially limiting the model’s ability to capture zero pronouns). Some discussion here would be useful.

There appear to be constraints that the model doesn’t explicitly encode (which some ILP-based SRL methods do), namely: (1) a predicate typically has only one argument per role type (especially NOM and ACC); and (2) a given argument generally fills only one role for a predicate. I would have appreciated an analysis of model outputs to see whether the model implicitly learns such constraints. More broadly, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq (with gains mainly for NOM in Multi-Seq), I would have liked discussion of the relative output differences: are the outputs mostly the same, or different in ways that average out to similar overall performance? In what contexts do the two models diverge? Such analysis would illuminate the models’ behavior and would have elevated the paper from solid to strong; this is the primary area for improvement (aside from adding SRL results, which would require substantially more effort).

The paper is clearly presented, and the figures help in understanding the model. There are a few minor language issues, but nothing serious:
