This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis based on Grid-RNNs, and compares it to a conventional single-sequence RNN. It is a solidly executed paper on a well-established task in Japanese, achieves state-of-the-art results, and presents the task in a largely accessible way for readers unfamiliar with Japanese.

That said, I think you could better emphasize the task’s complexity. For example, with respect to Figure 1, it would help to walk through the inherent ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate versus the NOM argument of the second predicate, and to clarify how the task contrasts with SRL, particularly due to ambiguity from zero pronouns. I would also like to see statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable, since this complicates the task as you define it (i.e., requiring an implicit distinction between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors). One point is unclear: when an argument is an inter-sentential zero pronoun, what representation do you use? Is the argument simply left unmarked, or is it explicitly marked as empty? My reading is the former, which means there is no explicit representation that a zero pronoun exists. That seems like a slightly defective representation that could hinder the model’s ability to capture zero pronouns; some discussion would be appreciated.

There are also constraints that do not seem to be modeled (which some ILP-based SRL methods encode explicitly), such as: (1) a given predicate typically has only one argument of a given type (especially NOM and ACC); and (2) a given argument typically fills only one role for a given predicate. I would have liked to see an analysis of outputs to assess how well the model learns such constraints implicitly.

More generally, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq (with meaningful improvements mainly for NOM), some discussion of the relative differences in their outputs would strengthen the paper. Are the predictions largely identical, or quite different but similar in aggregate performance? In what contexts do you observe divergences between the two models? Qualitative and quantitative analysis along these lines would provide insight into the models’ internals and would elevate the paper from solid to strong. This is the main area for improvement (aside from adding SRL results, which I recognize would require substantially more work).
