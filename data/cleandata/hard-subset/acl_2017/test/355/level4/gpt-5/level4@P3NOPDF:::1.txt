Summary
This paper presents a sophisticated application of grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid RNNs) to Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis. The approach deliberately avoids explicit syntactic parsing yet outperforms strong syntax-aware baselines, including state-of-the-art systems. The authors provide a clear and detailed description of their models and results. Notably, the paper carefully analyzes performance on dropped arguments (zero pronouns), which are prevalent and particularly challenging in Japanese. The multi-sequence model, which jointly considers all predicates in a sentence, yields the best results on these difficult cases. Overall, the paper is well written and thorough.

Strengths
- Novelty and significance: Demonstrates that a syntax-agnostic Grid RNN can surpass syntax-based PAS systems for Japanese, an important and nontrivial result.
- Empirical strength: Strong overall performance with particular gains on zero pronouns and long-distance dependencies, where PAS systems often struggle.
- Modeling insight: The multi-sequence architecture that models multi-predicate interactions is well motivated and empirically effective.
- Clarity: The paper offers a careful exposition of the architectures, training setup, and evaluation, with useful qualitative analyses.

Weaknesses
- Only minor presentation issues. Some English typos and article usage detract slightly from fluency.
- The definition of PRED would benefit from a concrete illustration clarifying that it can include contextual tokens around the predicate (e.g., the accusative marker in Fig. 6).
- The meaning of boldface in Table 2 (p. 7) is not explained; it would help to specify whether bold indicates the best result in each column, statistical significance, or another criterion.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the PRED span with a brief example drawn from Fig. 6 to make explicit that the predicate representation may include surrounding morphemes/markers when beneficial.
- Add a note in the caption or text explaining the boldface convention in Table 2 (e.g., “bold indicates the best score in each column” or “bold indicates results that are statistically significant at p < …”).
- Consider a short remark on why the multi-sequence model helps zero pronoun resolution (e.g., disambiguating shared arguments across predicates), tying the empirical gains to the modeling intuition.

Minor issues and typos
- p. 1: Remove “the” in “error propagation” and “multi-predicate interactions.”
- p. 2: “As an solution” → “As a solution”; “single-sequence model” → “a single-sequence model”; “multi-sequence model” → “a multi-sequence model.”
- p. 3 (Fig. 4 example): “She ate a bread” → “She ate bread.”
- p. 4: “assumes the independence” → “assumed independence” (or “assumes independence,” depending on tense consistency); “the multi-predicate interactions” → “multi-predicate interactions”; “the multi-sequence model” → “a multi-sequence model.”
- p. 7: “the residual connections” → “residual connections”; “the multi-predicate interactions” → “multi-predicate interactions” (twice).
- p. 8: “NAIST Text Corpus” → “the NAIST Text Corpus”; “the state-of-the-art result” → “state-of-the-art results.”

Overall assessment
This is a strong paper with clear contributions to PAS for Japanese, both empirically and methodologically. The analysis of zero pronouns and the demonstrated benefits of modeling multiple predicates jointly are particularly compelling. With minor clarifications and copyedits as noted above, the paper will be even stronger.
