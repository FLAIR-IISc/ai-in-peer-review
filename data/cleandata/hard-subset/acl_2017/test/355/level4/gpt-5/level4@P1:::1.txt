Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes neural models for Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that do not rely on explicit syntactic information. A single-sequence BiRNN model encodes sentence-level context for each predicate, and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions.
- Empirically, the models outperform strong prior systems on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, including methods that use syntactic features. The largest gains are on zero anaphora (dropped arguments), a known challenge in Japanese PAS.
- The work provides a clear architectural description, examines the effect of network depth and residual connections, and demonstrates that modeling interactions among predicates yields additional improvements, especially for zero arguments.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results without syntax: Both the single-sequence and multi-sequence models surpass competitive baselines that use syntactic information. This is notable given the importance of syntax in PAS and suggests the models effectively learn contextual cues from raw word sequences.
- Effective handling of zero pronouns: The largest improvements are on zero arguments, where the lack of direct dependencies makes the task difficult. The multi-sequence Grid-RNN further improves over the single-sequence model for zero arguments, indicating that multi-predicate interactions are informative in resolving omissions.
- Clear, thorough methodology: The paper carefully explains the input representations, RNN/Grid-RNN architectures, training setup, and hyperparameters. The separation between the single-sequence and multi-sequence settings makes the contribution of multi-predicate interactions easy to assess.
- Insightful analysis of depth and residual connections: The experiments show that deeper models with residual connections yield consistent gains, supporting the design choice and giving practical guidance for model configuration.
- Focus on an important, under-resourced aspect: Addressing Japanese PAS without external resources or syntactic parsers increases robustness and reduces error propagation from pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Mostly minor presentation issues:
  - Several typos and phrasing issues reduce fluency (listed below).
  - The paper could better illustrate how PRED features include local context around the predicate. The example in Figure 6 (where an accusative marker appears with the verb in the PRED string) would help clarify this.
  - The meaning of boldface in Table 2 (p. 7) is unclear; please explain what is being emphasized.
