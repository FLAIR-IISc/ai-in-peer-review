Summary
The paper proposes a joint neural approach to Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-RNNs, compared against a conventional single-sequence RNN baseline. The study targets a well-established task in Japanese and reports state-of-the-art performance. The paper is generally clear and accessible to readers not specialized in Japanese, aided by effective figures.

Strengths
- Solid technical execution and careful comparison with a reasonable baseline.
- Clear motivation for joint modeling across positions in the sentence.
- Strong empirical results, with improvements particularly on nominative (NOM) arguments.
- Good use of figures to illustrate the model and data layout.

Main weaknesses and suggestions
- Better motivation of task complexity: The paper would benefit from a more explicit walkthrough of the ambiguity in Figure 1, especially the competition between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate versus the NOM of the second predicate. This would foreground how Japanese zero pronouns and case ellipsis complicate PAS, and clarify how the task differs from standard SRL (primarily through pervasive zero anaphora and case marker omission).
- Zero pronouns: Please report corpus statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable versus those requiring inter-sentential resolution. This is central to the learning and evaluation setup, since the model must implicitly separate “no argument,” “intra-sentential zero anaphor,” and “inter-sentential zero anaphor.”
- Representation of inter-sentential zero anaphors: It appears that when an argument is realized outside the sentence, the model simply leaves the slot unfilled (i.e., no explicit empty-argument or NULL label). If so, the representation conflates “no argument” with “unresolvable zero pronoun,” which may hinder the model’s ability to learn cues for zero pronouns. Please clarify and, if applicable, discuss alternatives (e.g., explicit NULL/EXO tags) and their potential impact.
- Structural constraints: Many SRL/PAS systems benefit from enforcing simple global constraints (often via ILP), such as:
  1) Per predicate, at most one argument of a given role (especially NOM and ACC).
  2) Per predicate, an argument instance should not fill multiple roles.
  While your model may learn these regularities implicitly, it would be valuable to quantify how often its outputs violate them. A post-hoc analysis (e.g., violation rates, or gains from applying a light inference layer to enforce constraints) would strengthen the case for joint modeling and illuminate remaining error modes.
- Analysis of Single-Seq vs. Multi-Seq outputs: Table 3 shows gains mainly for NOM. Please analyze where the models diverge. For example:
  - Does Multi-Seq help more with long-distance dependencies or across-clause arguments?
  - Are gains concentrated when case markers are omitted vs. present?
  - Are improvements associated with specific predicate types, voice/aspect, or topicalization?
  - Breakdowns by dependency distance, presence/absence of overt case particles, clause boundaries, and argument type would provide insight into what the grid structure captures that the single sequence does not.
  Such analysis would elevate the paper from solid to strong by clarifying what the model actually learns.
- Relation to SRL: While full SRL experiments may be beyond scope, a more explicit contrast in task formulation and modeling implications (e.g., richer label sets vs. greater ambiguity from zero anaphora; head-finding vs. latent anaphor detection) would improve positioning.

Questions for clarification
- Data and labels: For arguments realized extra-sententially, do you use an explicit label (e.g., EXO/NULL) or leave the slot empty? How is this evaluated?
- Decoding: Is inference fully local per grid cell, or is there any global decoding that enforces role uniqueness or argument exclusivity?
- Training setup: Are predicates given or predicted? Are all tokens considered candidate arguments, or do you pre-filter based on dependency structure or POS/case cues?

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed paper that meaningfully advances Japanese PAS with a principled joint neural architecture and strong empirical results. The main area for improvement is analysis: make the zero-pronoun setup and representation explicit; quantify structural constraint adherence; and provide a targeted error analysis contrasting Single-Seq and Multi-Seq. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution and clarify the source of gains.
