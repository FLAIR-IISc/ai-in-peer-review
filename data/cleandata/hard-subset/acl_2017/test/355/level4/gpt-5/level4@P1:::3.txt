Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neural approach to Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis that models interactions among multiple predicates using Grid-RNNs. It compares this joint, multi-sequence architecture against a conventional single-sequence RNN baseline. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 show state-of-the-art results without using syntactic information, with particularly strong gains for zero arguments.

Contributions as I see them, in order of strength:
1) A strong empirical result: achieving state-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS without syntactic features, outperforming prior pointwise and joint baselines (Imamura+09; Ouchi+15), especially for zero arguments.
2) A modeling framework that captures multi-predicate interactions via a grid-style RNN over predicate-specific sequences, yielding small but significant improvements over a single-sequence model.
3) An empirical exploration of network depth and residual connections, showing that deeper models with residuals yield further gains.

Strengths
- Strong performance without syntax: The models outperform both a competitive pointwise system and a joint ILP-based system on NAIST 1.5 (Table 1). This is notable given the usual dependence on syntactic parsers and associated error propagation.
- Substantial gains on zero arguments: The multi-sequence model improves Zero F1 by roughly 3 points over Ouchi+15 and ~1 point over the single-sequence model, indicating that sentence-wide context and cross-predicate signals are helpful for zero pronoun resolution.
- Robust improvements for the dative case: Table 3 shows the neural models greatly outperform baselines on DAT (by ~30 points), despite sparsity, suggesting the networks learn more resilient case-specific cues from sequence context.
- Clear model presentation: Figures effectively explain the architectures; training details are provided; results include statistical significance testing via bootstrap resampling.
- Depth and residuals matter: The depth/residual ablations (Table 2) show consistent gains, especially with 8 layers and residual connections, supporting the architectural choices.

Weaknesses
1) Limited analysis of multi-predicate effects: While the multi-sequence model yields overall gains, per-role improvements are mixed (notably concentrated in NOM in Table 3). The paper lacks a qualitative/quantitative analysis of when interactions help, how predictions differ from the single-sequence model, and whether learned interactions reflect plausible linguistic dependencies.
2) Missing structural constraints: The model does not enforce simple decoding constraints commonly used in SRL/PAS (e.g., typically one NOM/ACC per predicate; one role per argument for a given predicate). It would help to report how often the model violates these and whether constrained decoding (e.g., ILP) would further improve results.
3) Unclear handling of inter-sentential zeros: It appears inter-sentential zero pronouns are effectively unmarked rather than explicitly labeled as omitted but unresolved within the sentence. This deprives the model of an explicit signal that a zero pronoun exists and may hinder learning. Clarification and discussion of alternatives would strengthen the paper.
4) Underplayed task complexity and dataset characterization: The paper could better motivate the inherent ambiguity (e.g., NOM/ACC interactions across predicates in Fig. 1) and contrast to SRL, where zero-pronoun ambiguity is less central. Also missing are corpus statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable under your setup, which directly affects task difficulty and evaluation.
5) Minor presentation issues: Typos, inconsistent reference casing, and a placeholder code URL (“http:xxx”). Including SRL results would be informative but likely requires substantial additional work.

Questions to authors
- How do you represent inter-sentential zero pronouns during training and evaluation? Is there an explicit marker indicating an omitted argument that is not resolvable within the sentence?
- Do your outputs ever assign multiple arguments of the same type (e.g., multiple NOMs) to a predicate, or assign the same argument to multiple roles for that predicate? How frequent are such violations? Did you try constrained decoding or post-processing (e.g., ILP), and with what effect?
- Can you provide an analysis comparing Single-Seq and Multi-Seq predictions? In what contexts does the Grid-RNN change decisions, and which predicate interactions are most helpful?
- What proportion of zero pronouns in the test set are intra-sententially resolvable? How does performance vary with predicate–antecedent distance?
