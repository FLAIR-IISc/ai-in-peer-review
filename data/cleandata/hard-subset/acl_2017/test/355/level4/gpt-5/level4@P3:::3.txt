Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a neural approach to Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that models interactions among multiple predicates without relying on predicted syntactic parses. The authors introduce (i) a single-sequence BiRNN baseline that predicts arguments for each predicate independently from word sequences, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence by propagating information across sequences. On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines, with the Grid-RNN achieving the best overall performance, particularly on zero arguments.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A grid-type neural architecture that captures multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, removing reliance on syntactic input and associated error propagation.
2) Strong empirical results on NAIST 1.5 without syntactic features, with notable gains for zero arguments and statistically significant improvements over the single-sequence model.
3) An investigation of network depth and residual connections for PAS, showing that deeper models with residual connections are beneficial.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains without syntax. The models outperform Imamura et al. (2009) and Ouchi et al. (2015) across Dep, Zero, and overall F1 (Table 1). The zero argument improvements are substantive (+2–3 F1 over Ouchi et al. for Zero), supporting the claim that sequence context alone is highly informative.
- Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions. The Grid-RNN yields a significant overall improvement over the single-sequence model (81.42 vs. 81.15 F1), and a larger gain specifically for Zero (47.12 vs. 46.10). This aligns with prior observations that multi-predicate information is valuable for Japanese PAS and demonstrates that a neural grid can capture such interactions without syntax.
- Sensible architectural design and analysis. The paper motivates both the single- and multi-sequence models, enabling a clean comparison of predicate-independent vs. joint modeling. The depth study with residual connections is useful: deeper stacks with residual links notably improve performance (~+1 F1), which is informative for future work and replication.
- Solid experimental setup. The dataset split follows prior work; training details, hyperparameters, and significance testing are clearly reported. The case-wise analysis (Table 3) is helpful, and the especially large gains on DAT suggest the model can learn robustly even for rarer case roles.

Weaknesses
- Limited task framing and dataset statistics. The paper could more clearly articulate the unique challenges of Japanese PAS compared to SRL (especially ambiguity from zero pronouns) and quantify the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra- vs inter-sententially resolvable in NAIST 1.5. This matters because the task definition implicitly requires distinguishing intra- from inter-sentential zeros.
- Ambiguity about inter-sentential zeros in the modeling. It appears that inter-sentential zero arguments are effectively treated as “no argument” within the sentence rather than being explicitly marked as “missing/zero here but resolved elsewhere.” If so, the model receives no explicit supervision that a zero pronoun exists for that role, which could limit its ability to learn zero-pronoun cues. This design choice deserves discussion and, ideally, an empirical check.
- No explicit structural constraints. Common constraints used in SRL/PAS (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC per predicate; an argument generally fills a single role per predicate) are not enforced. The paper does not analyze whether the model learns these constraints implicitly, nor how often outputs violate them. A brief violation analysis or a constrained decoding variant would strengthen the claims.
- Limited analysis of where Grid-RNN helps. Beyond aggregate gains, there is little exploration of differences between single- and multi-sequence outputs. For example, Table 3 suggests mixed case-wise patterns (multi-sequence improves Zero-NOM, but not consistently across roles). A qualitative/error analysis (e.g., by distance, predicate density, argument type) would clarify when multi-predicate information is most beneficial.
- Scope and generalization. While the models are applicable to SRL in principle, the paper does not test on SRL or explore combining sequence-based cues with syntax. A discussion of complementarity with syntactic signals, or a small experiment combining them, would improve the paper’s practical relevance.

Questions to authors
- How are inter-sentential zero arguments represented during training? If a predicate’s argument is inter-sentential, is the corresponding role simply “null” for all tokens in the sentence, or is there an explicit marker indicating “zero pronoun present but outside the sentence”?
- What proportion of zero pronouns in NAIST 1.5 are intra-sentential vs inter-sentential? Do your gains hold uniformly across sentences with many vs few inter-sentential zeros?
- Do you observe violations of basic PAS constraints (e.g., multiple NOMs for a predicate, or the same token assigned to multiple roles for the same predicate)? If so, how frequent are they, and do they differ between single- and multi-sequence models?
- Where does the Grid-RNN help most? For example, is the gain larger in sentences with more predicates, longer distances between predicates and arguments, or particular predicate types/case markers?
- Did you try combining your sequence-based model with syntactic or selectional-preference features? If yes, how did they interact; if not, what are your expectations?
- What is the computational overhead of the Grid-RNN vs. single-sequence, and how does runtime scale with the number of predicates M?
