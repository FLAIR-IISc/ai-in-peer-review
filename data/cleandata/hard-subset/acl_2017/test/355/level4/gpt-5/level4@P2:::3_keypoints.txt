- Paper summary: Proposes a Grid-RNN that models interactions among multiple predicates for Japanese PAS without using syntactic features, achieving state-of-the-art non-syntax results on NAIST 1.5, especially for zero arguments.

- Contribution: Introduces a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates and candidate arguments from word sequences.

- Contribution: Provides empirical evidence that modeling predicate–predicate interactions improves zero argument identification vs. predicate-independent models.

- Contribution: Shows deeper networks with residual connections improve performance and analyzes depth/residual effects.

- Strength: Strong empirical results without syntactic inputs; both single-sequence and Grid-RNN outperform competitive baselines.

- Strength: Clear architectural comparison isolating the effect of multi-predicate modeling, with Multi-Seq notably improving zero argument recall.

- Strength: Solid experimental setup using standard NAIST 1.5 splits, no external resources, significance testing, and informative ablations.

- Strength: Accessible presentation with helpful figures and clear model flow.

- Weakness: Insufficient discussion of Japanese PAS complexity and zero-pronoun ambiguity; example analyses could be clearer about NOM/ACC ambiguities.

- Weakness: Unclear treatment of inter-sentential zero pronouns—may be unlabeled, potentially limiting learning of zero-pronoun behavior.

- Weakness: No explicit structural constraints (e.g., role uniqueness) enforced or analyzed to see if the model implicitly learns them.

- Weakness: Limited comparative analysis of Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq outputs; improvements seem concentrated (largely NOM) and need finer-grained breakdowns.

- Weakness: Limited discussion/evaluation on generalization beyond Japanese PAS or connection to broader SRL tasks or other languages.

- Question: How are inter-sentential zero arguments represented during training—unlabeled or explicitly marked; any experiments with explicit signals?

- Question: What proportion of zero pronouns in NAIST 1.5 are intra-sententially resolvable, and do results vary with predicate count or predicate-argument distance?

- Question: Do predictions respect role-uniqueness constraints in practice; how often are such constraints violated and with what impact?

- Question: Can the authors provide error analysis comparing Single-Seq and Multi-Seq on zero arguments (e.g., long-distance antecedents, coordination, case-marker patterns)?

- Minor comments: Several language/formatting typos and reference-casing inconsistencies noted (examples: “the error propagation” → “error propagation”, “an solution” → “a solution”, “a bread” → “bread”, and other line-specific typos).
