Summary:
The paper presents a neural approach to Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis that models interactions among multiple predicates without relying on predicted syntax. The core idea is a Grid-RNN architecture that jointly processes all predicates and their candidate arguments within a sentence, contrasted with a single-sequence RNN baseline that treats predicates independently. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 show state-of-the-art performance among systems that do not use syntactic features, with notable gains on zero arguments.

Main contributions (as I see them):
- A multi-sequence Grid-RNN model that captures multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, achieving strong results without syntactic inputs.
- Empirical evidence that modeling predicate–predicate interactions improves zero argument identification compared to single-predicate modeling.
- An analysis of depth and residual connections showing deeper networks with residual links yield better performance.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical results without syntactic features: Both the single-sequence and multi-sequence models outperform competitive baselines, including a prior state-of-the-art joint model. The gains on zero arguments are particularly compelling and align with the model’s motivation to capture sentence-level context and cross-predicate cues.
- Clear architectural comparison: The paper cleanly contrasts a predicate-independent baseline with the proposed Grid-RNN, isolating the effect of multi-predicate modeling. The improvement of the multi-sequence model over the single-sequence model, especially for zero arguments, supports the linguistic intuition that predicates in a sentence provide mutual cues.
- Solid experimental setup and analysis: The paper follows standard splits on NAIST 1.5, avoids external resources, and reports significance testing. The ablation on depth and residual connections is informative and shows practical training considerations for deeper sequence models.
- Accessible presentation: Figures are helpful and the model flow is easy to follow, making the work approachable even for readers less familiar with Japanese PAS.

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of task complexity and ambiguity: The paper could better motivate the inherent difficulties of Japanese PAS, especially the ambiguity caused by zero pronouns and how this differs from SRL. For example, in the Figure 1 example, it would be helpful to walk through the ambiguity between NOM/ACC arguments of the first predicate and the NOM of the second predicate, and to more explicitly explain the role of zero pronoun ambiguity.
- Treatment of inter-sentential zero pronouns is unclear: It appears that inter-sentential zero arguments are simply unmarked (i.e., not explicitly represented as empty or “missing”). If so, the model may lack a direct signal that a zero pronoun exists, which could limit its ability to learn zero pronoun behavior. Some justification or empirical analysis here would be valuable, as would statistics on what proportion of zero pronouns are resolvable intra-sententially in the corpus.
- Missing structural constraints: The model does not enforce basic constraints often encoded in ILP-based SRL/PAS systems, such as (a) each predicate typically has at most one argument per core role (especially NOM/ACC), and (b) a given argument span typically fills at most one role for a predicate. An error analysis or constraint satisfaction study would clarify whether the model implicitly learns these properties.
- Limited comparative analysis of Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq outputs: Table 3 suggests that improvements from Multi-Seq are concentrated (largely NOM), with mixed or small differences elsewhere. A qualitative/quantitative breakdown of where the two models diverge (e.g., distances, clause boundaries, argument types) would strengthen the claim that cross-predicate interactions are generally beneficial, beyond aggregate metrics.
- Generalization beyond Japanese PAS: While the paper mentions relevance to SRL, no cross-task or cross-lingual evaluation is provided. Even a small-scale SRL experiment or discussion of what would be required (e.g., span labeling vs dependency labeling) would broaden the impact.

Questions to authors:
- How are inter-sentential zero arguments represented during training? Are they simply unlabeled (no explicit “missing argument” indicator), or do you mark them in some way? If unlabeled, have you considered adding an explicit “zero-present-but-out-of-sentence” signal?
- What proportion of zero pronouns in NAIST 1.5 are intra-sententially resolvable? Do results vary substantially across sentences with different numbers of predicates or with different distances between predicates and arguments?
- Do your predictions obey role uniqueness constraints in practice (e.g., at most one NOM per predicate)? If not enforced, how often are they violated, and what is the impact on precision/recall?
- Can you provide a brief error analysis comparing Single-Seq and Multi-Seq on zero arguments (e.g., do gains come from resolving long-distance antecedents, coordinating structures, or specific case markers)?
