- Proposes neural sequence models for Japanese PAS that avoid predicted syntactic parses, with a multi-sequence Grid-RNN to jointly model all predicates.
- Adds a strong single-sequence BiRNN baseline and compares it to the multi-sequence model to quantify predicate-interaction benefits.
- Reports new state-of-the-art results on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without syntactic features, with analyses by argument type, case role, and network depth.
- Achieves SOTA accuracy without syntax and reduces error propagation compared to prior syntax-based systems (81.42 F vs. 79.23).
- Substantial improvements on Zero (omitted) arguments, with single-sequence and multi-sequence models improving Zero F by ~2.0 and ~3.0 respectively.
- Multi-sequence Grid-RNN effectively captures multi-predicate interactions, yielding modest but consistent overall gains (81.42 vs. 81.15) and ~+1 F for Zero.
- Strong gains on sparse case roles, notably large improvements for DAT (e.g., Dep-DAT from 38.39 → 65.91 F).
- Thorough experimental analysis and reproducibility: implementation details, hyperparameters, depth/residual ablations, significance testing, and code release.
- Technical novelty is moderate since Grid-RNNs/multi-dimensional RNNs are known; contributions are primarily empirical/task-driven.
- Limited ablations on key inputs and design choices (e.g., MARK/PRED-window), no comparisons to alternative cross-predicate mechanisms or pretraining studies.
- Scope/generalization concerns: evaluation restricted to intra-sentential arguments on NAIST 1.5; unclear scaling to inter-sentential zero anaphora, other languages/formalisms, and sentences with many predicates.
- Practical efficiency (training/inference time and memory as function of number of predicates and sentence length) is not reported.
- Presentation/clarity issues: PRED window construction and examples, unclear boldface in Table 2, and several minor English errors.
- Questions for authors: clarify PRED window construction and sensitivity to window size C.
- Questions for authors: report training/inference time and memory scaling with number of predicates M and sentence length T, and profiling on predicate-rich sentences.
- Questions for authors: did you try lighter-weight interaction mechanisms (cross-sequence attention or shared self-attention) and any preliminary comparisons?
- Questions for authors: behavior when M=1 — does multi-sequence reduce to the single-sequence model or differ?
- Questions for authors: would adding lightweight syntactic signals help, and were such experiments attempted?
- Minor comments: list of typos and wording fixes provided; request clarification of boldface use in Table 2.
- Overall assessment: strong, well-executed empirical paper with meaningful gains (especially on zero pronouns and DAT); novelty is incremental but the work merits publication and the reviewer is satisfied after the author response.
