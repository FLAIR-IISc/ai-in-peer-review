This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to Japanese PAS analysis based on Grid-RNNs, compared against a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. It is a solidly executed study on a well-established task in Japanese, achieves state-of-the-art results, and presents the task in a largely accessible way for readers unfamiliar with Japanese.

That said, the paper could do more to articulate the task’s complexity. For example, in the Figure 1 example, it would help to walk through the inherent ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate versus the NOM argument of the second predicate, and to clarify how PAS contrasts with SRL, particularly due to ambiguity around zero pronouns. I would also like to see statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable, since the task as defined implicitly requires distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors. One point I am unsure about: for an inter-sentential zero pronoun filling a given predicate’s argument, what representation do you use? Is the argument simply unmarked, or is it explicitly marked as empty? My reading is that it is the former; if so, there is no explicit representation of the presence of a zero pronoun. This seems like a slightly defective representation that could hinder the model’s ability to capture zero pronouns, and some discussion of this choice would be appreciated.

There also appear to be constraints that are not explicitly captured in the model but are often enforced in ILP-based SRL methods, such as:
- A given predicate will generally have only one argument of a given type (especially NOM and ACC).
- A given argument generally fills only one argument slot for a given predicate.
I would have liked to see an analysis of the model’s output to assess how well it implicitly learns these constraints.

More generally, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq (with improvements for Multi-Seq largely limited to NOM), I would have appreciated discussion of how the outputs differ between the two models. Are the outputs largely identical, or do they differ substantially while yielding similar aggregate scores? In what contexts do you observe differences between the models? Such analysis, shedding light on the models’ internals and error profiles, would elevate the paper from solid to strong. Including SRL results for fuller comparison would also be valuable, although I appreciate that this would require substantially more work.

The paper’s presentation is good overall, and the figures aid understanding of the model. There are some minor language issues that can be easily fixed:

- l190: an solution -> a solution
- l264 (and Figure 2): a bread -> bread
- l351: the independence -> independence
- l512: the good -> good
- l531: from their model -> of their model
- l637: significent -> significance
- l638: both of -> both
