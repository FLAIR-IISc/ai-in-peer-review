- Tackles Japanese PAS without predicted syntax using a single-sequence BiRNN and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN to model inter-predicate interactions.
- Both models outperform strong baselines on NAIST 1.5; Grid-RNN yields additional gains on zero arguments and competitive accuracy without syntactic features.

Contributions:
- Proposes a grid-style multi-sequence architecture that jointly models multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS without syntax.
- Provides a strong syntax-free single-sequence baseline adapted to dependency-based PAS that surpasses prior systems.
- Empirically analyzes gains on zero arguments and shows benefits of model depth and residual connections.

Strengths:
- Demonstrates clear empirical gains without relying on syntactic features, reducing parser error propagation.
- Shows statistically significant improvements on zero (omitted) arguments, a key challenge in Japanese PAS.
- Jointly models predicate interactions explicitly, operationalizing prior intuitions about inter-predicate benefits.
- Includes sensible ablations: deeper models and residual connections improve F-measure and reproducibility.
- Well organized presentation and evaluation on a standard benchmark.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: single-sequence follows established sequence models and grid-RNNs are not new; contribution may be incremental.
- Incomplete comparison to contemporary neural baselines (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016) and lack of discussion on trade-offs.
- Unclear effect attribution: improvements may stem from increased capacity or context aggregation rather than inter-predicate edges.
- Narrow evaluation: results confined to NAIST 1.5 and intra-sentential cases; generality to other domains/languages or inter-sentential cases untested.
- Practical costs underexplored: no quantification of training/inference time or memory overhead for Grid-RNNs.

Questions for authors:
- Can you provide a direct comparison or detailed discussion versus Shibata et al. (2016) under similar settings (syntax reliance, error propagation, compute)?
- Can you isolate inter-predicate connection effects by matching parameter counts or ablating inter-sequence edges while keeping grid depth?
- What is the computational overhead (training time, GPU memory, inference latency) of Grid-RNN vs single-sequence as predicate count grows?
- How sensitive are results to predicate window size C, embedding dimensions, and use of pre-trained or character/subword embeddings?
- Will the code, preprocessing scripts for NAIST 1.5, and exact hyperparameters be publicly released to reproduce reported numbers?
