Summary and contributions
- Primary: The paper proposes a grid-type recurrent neural network (Grid-RNN) that jointly models interactions among all predicates in a sentence for Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis without relying on predicted syntax. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without syntactic features, with clear gains on zero pronoun (intra-sentential) arguments.
- Secondary: A strong syntax-free single-sequence baseline (stacked Bi-GRU with softmax) is presented; ablations show deeper models with residual connections improve performance.
- Tertiary: The work provides a careful empirical breakdown by argument type and case role, highlighting when multi-predicate modeling helps (notably Zero), along with significance testing.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results against competitive baselines. Both single- and multi-sequence models outperform Imamura et al. (2009) and Ouchi et al. (2015) on NAIST 1.5. The multi-sequence model improves overall F1 (81.42 vs. 79.23) and Zero F1 (47.12 vs. 44.09), with bootstrap significance (p < 0.05). This demonstrates that sequence models can surpass syntax-dependent pipelines while avoiding parser error propagation.
- Clear benefit on zero-pronoun arguments. Modeling context across the sentence and interactions among predicates yields clear gains on Zero arguments, the hardest part of Japanese PAS. The joint, predicate-aware Grid-RNN improves Zero F1 over the independent single-sequence baseline (47.12 vs. 46.10), supporting the hypothesis that cross-predicate signals are useful.
- Careful ablations and analyses. The paper systematically examines network depth and residual connections, showing deeper models with residuals yield ~+1 F1 at 8 layers. It also reports per-role breakdowns (NOM/ACC/DAT for Dep and Zero), with notable improvements for DAT compared to prior work despite data sparsity.
- Well-presented and reproducible. The architecture, training setup, and hyperparameters are described in detail, with public code. Figures make the model design and feature construction easy to follow.
- Conceptual contribution: joint modeling of multiple predicates via Grid-RNNs. Applying grid-style recurrence across predicate-specific sequences is a neat, general idea for capturing multi-predicate interactions without explicit syntax.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope. Experiments are restricted to intra-sentential arguments on a single domain (newspaper) and a single dataset (NAIST 1.5). No out-of-domain tests or inter-sentential (Inter-Zero) results are provided, limiting claims about generality.
- Non-uniform gains across roles. While overall Zero improves, the multi-sequence model underperforms the single-sequence baseline on some fine-grained categories (e.g., Zero ACC and Zero DAT; Dep DAT). An analysis of when interactions help or hurt would strengthen the case for the grid architecture.
- Baseline coverage could be broader. The choice to replace CRF with softmax for dependency-based PAS is reasonable, but an empirical comparison to strong syntax-free alternatives (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF, attention-based models) would clarify whether Grid-RNN is essential to the observed gains.
- Computational considerations are underreported. The complexity and runtime/memory impact of Grid-RNNs as the number of predicates increases are not discussed. Practical deployment considerations (max predicates per sentence, training/inference time) would be useful.
- Minor presentation issues. The role of the PRED feature window (e.g., inclusion of case particles with the predicate in Japanese) could be illustrated more explicitly. The meaning of boldface in Table 2 is unclear.

Questions to authors (optional)
- What are the training and inference runtimes and memory footprints for the single- vs multi-sequence models, and how do they scale with the number of predicates per sentence?
- Did you try pre-trained word embeddings or external resources, and if so, how did they affect results?
- Can syntactic cues (when available) be added as optional features on top of the Grid-RNN; if tried, do they bring further gains?
- Could you provide qualitative examples where multi-predicate interactions changed a decision compared to the single-sequence model?
- Please clarify the boldface in Table 2 and add a concrete example showing how the PRED window captures particles attached to predicates in Japanese.
