- Summary: Proposes Grid-RNNs to model interactions among multiple predicates for Japanese PAS, achieving state-of-the-art on NAIST 1.5 without syntax and strong gains on zero arguments.

- Contribution 1: Strong empirical result — SOTA performance without syntactic features, outperforming prior pointwise and joint baselines, especially for zero arguments.

- Contribution 2: Modeling framework — a grid-style RNN over predicate-specific sequences that captures multi-predicate interactions and improves over a single-sequence RNN.

- Contribution 3: Architectural exploration — depth and residual connections matter; deeper models with residuals yield further gains.

- Strength: Strong performance without syntax, avoiding parser error propagation.

- Strength: Substantial improvements on zero-argument resolution (~+3 F1 vs prior work, ~+1 vs single-seq).

- Strength: Large gains on the dative case (DAT), indicating robustness to sparse case types.

- Strength: Clear model presentation, provided training details, and statistical significance testing.

- Strength: Ablation shows depth and residuals consistently improve results (best at 8 layers + residuals).

- Weakness: Limited analysis of how multi-predicate interactions help — mixed per-role improvements and little qualitative/quantitative diagnosis.

- Weakness: No enforcement of common structural decoding constraints (e.g., one NOM/ACC per predicate); violations and impact of constrained decoding are not reported.

- Weakness: Unclear handling of inter-sentential zero pronouns — lack of explicit markers may hinder learning and should be clarified.

- Weakness: Underplayed task complexity and dataset characterization — missing motivation, contrasts with SRL, and corpus stats on intra-sententially resolvable zeros.

- Weakness: Minor presentation issues (typos, inconsistent reference casing, placeholder code URL).

- Question: How are inter-sentential zero pronouns represented during training and evaluation — is there an explicit marker for omitted but unresolved arguments?

- Question: Do outputs violate structural constraints (multiple NOMs/ACC per predicate or same argument assigned multiple roles)? How frequent, and did constrained decoding/ILP help?

- Question: Can the authors provide an analysis comparing Single-Seq and Multi-Seq predictions — contexts where Grid-RNN changes decisions and which predicate interactions help most?

- Question: What proportion of zero pronouns in the test set are intra-sententially resolvable, and how does performance vary with predicate–antecedent distance?

- Minor language issues: list of specific typos and capitalization inconsistencies to correct (several line-level fixes and reference capitalization).
