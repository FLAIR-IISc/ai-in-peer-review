This paper presents a joint neural approach to Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-RNNs, compared against a conventional single-sequence RNN. The work is solidly executed, tackles a well-established task, achieves state-of-the-art performance, and is mostly accessible to readers unfamiliar with Japanese.

That said, the paper could better convey the task’s complexity. For example, in Figure 1, please walk the reader through the ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate versus the NOM of the second predicate. Also clarify how PAS in Japanese contrasts with SRL, especially due to the ambiguity and frequency of zero pronouns. I would appreciate statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable, since this implicitly requires distinguishing intra- from inter-sentential zero anaphora and directly affects the problem setting and evaluation.

Relatedly, I am unsure how you represent inter-sentential zero pronouns. For an argument of a predicate that is realized outside the sentence, do you leave the argument unmarked or do you use an explicit empty placeholder? My reading is that it is left unmarked. If so, there is no explicit signal that a zero pronoun exists, which seems like a suboptimal representation and could limit the model’s ability to learn to detect zero pronouns. Some discussion of this design choice and its implications would strengthen the paper.

The model appears not to enforce certain structural constraints that ILP-based SRL methods often model explicitly: (1) a predicate typically has only one argument per role (especially for NOM and ACC); and (2) a given argument usually fills only a single role for a predicate. It would be helpful to analyze whether the model learns these constraints implicitly. For instance, how often does the system assign multiple NOMs to the same predicate, or assign the same argument to multiple roles? An error-type breakdown along these lines would be informative.

More broadly, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq (with clear gains primarily for NOM in the Multi-Seq model), I would like to see a deeper comparison of their outputs. Are the predictions largely overlapping, or do the models make different errors that average out to similar aggregate scores? In which contexts does Multi-Seq help or hurt—e.g., for long-distance dependencies, multiple predicates in a sentence, or cases involving zero pronouns? Such analysis would provide insight into what the joint modeling is capturing and would elevate the paper from solid to strong. Including direct comparisons to SRL would also be interesting, though I recognize that would require more extensive additional work.

The paper is clearly presented overall, and the figures support understanding of the architecture. A few minor language and style issues to address:
- l19: “the error propagation” → “error propagation”
- l190: “an solution” → “a solution”
- l264 (and Figure 2): “a bread” → “bread”
- l351: “the independence” → “independence”
- l512: “the good” → “good”
- l531: “from their model” → “of their model”
- l637: “significent” → “significance”
- l638: “both of” → “both”
- Please also standardize casing in references (e.g., “Japanese,” “LSTM,” “CoNLL,” “ILP”).

In summary, this is a well-executed paper with strong results and clear presentation. The main opportunities for improvement are: (i) clarifying the representational treatment of zero pronouns, especially inter-sentential ones; (ii) analyzing whether role-uniqueness and argument-uniqueness constraints are learned; and (iii) providing a comparative error/ablation analysis to better illuminate the differences between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models. These additions would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
