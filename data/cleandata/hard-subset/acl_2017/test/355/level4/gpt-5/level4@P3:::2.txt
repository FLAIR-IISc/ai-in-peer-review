Summary and Contributions
- Primary: An end-to-end neural approach to Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis that dispenses with predicted syntactic information and still surpasses prior state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5. The core technical contribution is a grid-style recurrent architecture (Grid-RNN) that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions.
- Secondary: A strong single-sequence BiRNN/GRU baseline that models sentence-wide context for each predicate independently using simple token/predicate/marker features, along with a systematic study of depth and residual connections.
- Tertiary: Empirical evidence that multi-predicate modeling provides statistically significant, if modest, gains overall and clearer benefits for zero-argument identification; detailed per-case-role analysis (NOM/ACC/DAT) highlights substantial improvements, especially for DAT.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results without syntax: Both the single-sequence and Grid-RNN models outperform prior baselines on NAIST 1.5, including Ouchi et al. (2015). Overall F1 improves from 79.23 (Ouchi+15) to 81.15 (single) and 81.42 (grid). This is notable given that the proposed models use only word-sequence features, avoiding error-prone dependency parses.
- Benefits for zero anaphora: Zero arguments, historically difficult due to the lack of direct dependencies, see meaningful gains. The single model improves Zero F1 by ~2 points over Ouchi+15, and the Grid-RNN further adds ~1 point (46.10 → 47.12). These results support the claim that sequence context and cross-predicate signals help with omitted arguments.
- Multi-predicate modeling is effective and statistically significant: The Grid-RNN yields consistent, significant gains over the already-strong single-sequence baseline in overall F1 (81.15 → 81.42) and especially in Zero (46.10 → 47.12), demonstrating the utility of explicitly modeling predicate–predicate interactions without relying on syntactic structures.
- Depth and residuals: The paper provides a useful analysis showing that deeper stacks (up to 8 layers) benefit performance, and residual connections yield roughly +1 point F1, indicating attention to stability and optimization of deep RNNs.
- Per-role breakdown reveals robust DAT gains: The proposed models substantially improve DAT across Dep and Zero settings (e.g., Dep-DAT 38.39 → 65.91 with the single model), suggesting the architectures can learn reliable cues for rarer argument types, where feature engineering and syntax have historically struggled.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural SRL: The single-sequence model closely follows established BiRNN-based SRL architectures (e.g., Zhou and Xu, 2015). The Grid-RNN extension for multi-predicate modeling is reasonable but incremental; the reported overall gains over the single model are modest.
- Comparison to closely related neural PAS/SRL systems is incomplete: Shibata et al. (2016) integrate neural scoring into a strong joint framework (Ouchi et al., 2015). A direct, controlled comparison is missing, making it hard to situate the proposed method against the most relevant neural baselines in Japanese PAS.
- Limited ablation and analysis of design choices: Beyond depth/residuals, the paper does not isolate the contribution of key components (e.g., predicate-window features, marker embeddings, GRU vs. LSTM, alternatives to Grid-RNN such as attention across predicates). Qualitative error analysis for zero arguments is also limited.
- Scope restricted to intra-sentential arguments: While consistent with prior work, excluding inter-sentential zero anaphora limits the broader impact on end-to-end PAS/zero anaphora resolution. Some discussion of how the Grid-RNN might extend across sentences would strengthen the paper.
- Efficiency and scalability: The Grid-RNN introduces inter-sequence recurrences over M predicates and T tokens. The paper does not report training/inference cost, memory usage, or speed comparisons to the single-sequence model and to joint syntactic baselines, which is relevant for practical adoption.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Comparison to Shibata et al. (2016): Can you provide a direct comparison on NAIST 1.5 under matched resources and splits? If not possible, please discuss methodological differences that could explain performance differences.
- Statistical significance vs. prior SOTA: You report significance between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq. Are the improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015) also statistically significant under your bootstrap test?
- Model variants and ablations: 
  - What is the impact of the predicate window size C and the MARK feature? 
  - Have you tried LSTM or highway/skip connections other than residuals? 
  - Can Grid-RNN be replaced with attention across predicates, and if so, how do results compare?
- Efficiency: Please report training/inference time and memory for single vs. grid models, as a function of number of predicates per sentence. How does performance degrade or improve with more predicates?
- Generalization and resources: Have you tried pretrained embeddings or semi-supervised objectives? Would incorporating syntactic signals on top of your models further improve results, and have you experimented with this?

Overall Evaluation
This is a clear and well-executed paper that demonstrates a strong syntax-free neural baseline and a principled Grid-RNN extension to model multi-predicate interactions, yielding new SOTA on NAIST 1.5. The main concerns are limited novelty beyond established neural SRL designs and incomplete comparisons to the most relevant neural PAS work. With strengthened baselines/ablations and clearer efficiency reporting, this would be a solid contribution. I lean toward acceptance given the empirical strength and clarity, especially the improvements on zero arguments and DAT, which are of practical interest in Japanese PAS.
