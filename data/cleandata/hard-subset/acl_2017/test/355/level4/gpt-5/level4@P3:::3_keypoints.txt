- Proposes neural PAS models: a single-sequence BiRNN baseline and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that models multi-predicate interactions without syntactic input.  
- Grid-RNN yields best performance on NAIST 1.5, especially improving zero-argument prediction.  
- Contribution: a grid-style architecture capturing multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, avoiding syntax and error propagation.  
- Contribution: strong empirical results on NAIST 1.5 without syntactic features, with notable zero-argument gains and statistical improvements over the single-sequence model.  
- Contribution: analysis showing deeper models with residual connections improve PAS performance.  
- Strength: Clear empirical gains over prior work (Imamura 2009; Ouchi 2015), including substantive +2–3 F1 on Zero.  
- Strength: Grid-RNN effectively models multi-predicate interactions, improving overall F1 and Zero scores versus predicate-independent modeling.  
- Strength: Sensible, well-motivated architectural comparison between predicate-independent and joint modeling.  
- Strength: Deeper stacks with residual links provide ~+1 F1 improvement, informative for replication.  
- Strength: Solid experimental setup—standard dataset split, clear training/hyperparameter details, significance testing, and useful case-wise analysis (large DAT gains).  
- Weakness: Task framing and dataset statistics are limited; needs clearer distinction from SRL and quantification of intra- vs inter-sentential zeros.  
- Weakness: Unclear treatment of inter-sentential zeros—model may treat them as “no argument,” possibly depriving supervision for zero-pronoun existence.  
- Weakness: No explicit structural constraints (e.g., one NOM/ACC per predicate); paper lacks analysis of constraint violations or constrained decoding.  
- Weakness: Limited analysis of where Grid-RNN helps—insufficient qualitative/error analysis by distance, predicate density, or argument type.  
- Weakness: Scope and generalization are limited; no SRL experiments or exploration of combining sequence cues with syntax.  
- Question: How are inter-sentential zero arguments represented during training—null or explicit marker?  
- Question: What proportion of zero pronouns are intra- vs inter-sentential in NAIST 1.5, and do gains hold across sentences with varying inter-sentential zeros?  
- Question: Do model outputs violate basic PAS constraints, how frequently, and do violation rates differ between models?  
- Question: Under which conditions does Grid-RNN provide the largest gains (more predicates, longer distances, specific cases)?  
- Question: Were sequence models combined with syntactic/selectional-preference features, and if not, what are expectations?  
- Question: What is the computational overhead and runtime scaling of Grid-RNN vs the single-sequence model?  
- Additional: Minor language and reference-casing corrections suggested throughout the paper.  
- Additional: Clarify the Figure 1 example to better motivate NOM/ACC ambiguity and multi-predicate modeling.  
- Additional: Recommend adding a short qualitative/error analysis showing cases where multi-sequence changes predictions.
