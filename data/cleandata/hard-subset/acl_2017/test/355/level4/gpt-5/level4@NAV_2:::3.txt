This paper presents a joint neural approach to Japanese PAS that leverages Grid-RNNs, and contrasts it against a standard single-sequence RNN baseline. The work is carefully executed, addresses a mature task, achieves state-of-the-art performance for the setting without syntax, and is largely accessible even to readers without deep familiarity with Japanese. That said, the paper could do more to foreground the task’s difficulty. For example, in relation to Figure 1, it would help to walk through the inherent ambiguity where a candidate can function as the ACC or NOM of the first predicate and as the NOM of the second predicate, and to more clearly articulate how the task differs from SRL (notably due to zero-pronoun ambiguity). It would also be useful to report statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intrasententially resolvable, since the task implicitly requires distinguishing intra- from inter-sentential zero anaphors. One point that was unclear: for an inter-sentential zero pronoun serving as an argument to a given predicate, how is it represented? Is the position simply left unmarked, or do you mark an empty argument? My reading is the former, implying no explicit representation of the presence of a zero pronoun; this seems somewhat suboptimal and could affect the model’s ability to capture zero pronouns. Some discussion here would strengthen the paper.

There are also constraints that the model appears not to encode (which some ILP-based SRL methods explicitly enforce), namely: (1) a predicate typically has at most one argument per role (especially NOM and ACC); and (2) an argument generally fills only one role for a given predicate. An analysis of outputs to assess how well the model internalizes these constraints would be valuable. More broadly, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq (with notable gains primarily for NOM in Multi-Seq), a discussion comparing the two models’ outputs would be informative: are the predictions largely overlapping, or different but with similar aggregate scores? In what contexts do you observe divergences? Such qualitative/diagnostic analysis would elevate the work from solid to strong (separately from adding SRL results, which would be more involved).

The exposition is generally clear, and the figures help elucidate the model. There are a few minor language issues, none of which are critical:
