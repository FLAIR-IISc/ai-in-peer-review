This paper presents a joint neural modeling approach to Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-RNNs, contrasted with a conventional single-sequence RNN baseline.

Overall, the work is solid and well executed. It targets a well-established task in Japanese, achieves state-of-the-art results, and explains the task in a way that is mostly accessible to readers unfamiliar with Japanese. That said, the paper could better emphasize the task’s complexity. For example, with respect to Figure 1, it would help to walk through the inherent ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate and the NOM argument of the second predicate, and to more clearly articulate how the task differs from SRL (largely due to the ambiguity introduced by zero pronouns). I would also like to see statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are actually resolvable intra-sententially, since this further complicates the task as defined (i.e., the need to implicitly distinguish intra- vs. inter-sentential zero anaphors). One point that was unclear: for an inter-sentential zero pronoun filling an argument of a given predicate, what representation do you use? Is the argument simply left unmarked, or is there an explicit empty-argument marker? My reading is the former, which means there is no explicit representation of the existence of a zero pronoun. This seems like a somewhat deficient representation that could hinder the model’s ability to capture zero pronouns; some discussion of this choice would be appreciated.

There appear to be constraints that the model does not explicitly encode (which some ILP-based SRL methods do), such as: (1) a predicate typically has only one argument of a given type (especially NOM and ACC); and (2) a given argument typically fills only one role for a given predicate. I would have liked to see an analysis of model outputs to assess how well these constraints are learned implicitly. More generally, given the mixed numbers in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq—where the main improvement for Multi-Seq is on NOM—I would appreciate discussion of the relative differences in their outputs. Are they largely identical, or do they produce different predictions that happen to balance out in aggregate? In what contexts do you observe divergences between the two models? Such analysis to illuminate model internals would elevate the paper from solid to strong. Including SRL results would also be informative, though I recognize that would require substantially more work.

The paper is well presented overall, and the figures help clarify the model. There are a few minor language issues:
