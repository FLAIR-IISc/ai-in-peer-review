1. Neural models achieve state-of-the-art PAS performance using only word sequences, avoiding parser reliance.  
2. Multi-sequence Grid-RNN captures multi-predicate interactions and improves zero-argument identification.  
3. Thorough evaluation on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with case-wise analyses shows consistent gains, especially for dative arguments.  
4. Key strength: avoids parser error propagation, demonstrating strong empirical results (81.42% F-measure).  
5. Key strength: notable improvement on zero arguments (47.12% vs prior 44.09%).  
6. Key strength: comprehensive comparisons and detailed metric breakdowns bolster empirical claims.  
7. Weakness: contribution may be incremental—single- to multi-sequence gain is modest (81.15% → 81.42%) with limited justification of linguistic significance.  
8. Weakness: missing comparison to Shibata et al. (2016), weakening novelty and context of improvements.  
9. Weakness: insufficient theoretical justification for choosing Grid-RNNs over other sequence/multi-sequence architectures.  
10. Question: Why is the ~1.0 point zero-argument F-measure gain linguistically significant, and what specific phenomena does the model capture that prior work missed?  
11. Question: How exactly does the Grid-RNN approach differ from Shibata et al. (2016)’s neural extension of Ouchi et al. (2015), and why does it outperform them?  
12. Question: Do the feature choices (word indices, position info) truly avoid encoding syntactic information, or do they implicitly carry syntactic cues?
