

This paper introduces a neural approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that avoids reliance on syntactic parsers by modeling multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences using Grid-RNNs. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, achieving significant improvements—particularly for zero-argument identification—without syntactic dependencies. The core contribution is the Grid-RNN architecture that captures inter-predicate relationships across all argument candidates in a sentence, addressing error propagation in prior joint modeling approaches.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Strong empirical results without syntactic dependencies**: The model outperforms prior work (Ouchi et al., 2015) by 1.0–3.0 F1 points on zero arguments (47.12% vs. 44.09%) and achieves the best results on the standard NAIST benchmark. Crucially, this is achieved *without* syntactic features, proving that word-sequence information alone suffices for high-quality PAS analysis—a notable contribution to the field.  
2. **Effective handling of zero pronouns**: The multi-sequence model’s 1.0-point improvement on zero arguments (vs. single-sequence) directly addresses the paper’s core challenge (Section 2.2). This validates the authors’ hypothesis that multi-predicate interactions are especially valuable for resolving omitted arguments, which lack syntactic cues.  
3. **Novel architecture with clear justification**: The Grid-RNN extension to standard RNNs (Section 4) is well-motivated by linguistic intuition and effectively captures cross-predicate dependencies. The residual connections (Section 4.2) also demonstrate a thoughtful approach to training deeper networks, with 8-layer models outperforming shallower ones by ~1.0 F1 point.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient discussion of zero-argument resolution complexity**: The paper defines intra-sentential zero arguments (Zero) but omits critical context about how often these are resolvable *within* the sentence. For instance, what proportion of Zero arguments in the NAIST corpus are truly intra-sentential (vs. inter-sentential)? Without this, it’s unclear whether the model’s success stems from solving a well-defined subtask or accidentally conflating intra- and inter-sentential cases. The authors should clarify: *How does the model represent inter-sentential zero pronouns (e.g., as empty arguments or unmarked)?* If unmarked, this risks masking a key challenge for the task.  
2. **Lack of constraint analysis**: Japanese PAS enforces strict linguistic constraints (e.g., one NOM argument per predicate, one slot per argument). The paper does not analyze whether the model respects these (e.g., by checking output consistency in Table 3). The marginal gains for Multi-Seq over Single-Seq on NOM (88.17% vs. 88.10%) suggest minimal model divergence—yet the authors do not discuss *why* the improvements are concentrated on NOM (Table 3) or how outputs differ in practice. A brief case study (e.g., "In 15% of cases where Multi-Seq corrects Single-Seq, how does the constraint violation manifest?") would strengthen the contribution.  
3. **Misalignment with SRL literature**: The paper claims its model extends Zhou and Xu (2015) for English SRL (Section 5.3), but the task is fundamentally different: Japanese PAS uses dependency-based argument identification (not constituent spans), and the model *jointly* predicts all predicates (unlike Zhou and Xu’s per-predicate approach). This comparison is misleading; the authors should clarify the *differences* (e.g., "Our model handles multi-predicate interactions; Zhou and Xu focus on single-predicate context") rather than imply a direct extension.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. In the NAIST corpus, what percentage of Zero arguments are intra-sententially resolvable (vs. inter-sentential)? How does this impact the model’s ability to distinguish these cases?  
2. For cases where Multi-Seq corrects Single-Seq (e.g., NOM arguments), does the model violate linguistic constraints (e.g., multiple NOMs per predicate), and if so, how frequently?  
3. How are inter-sentential zero pronouns represented in the training data? Are they labeled as "null" (unmarked) or explicitly marked as inter-sentential? If unmarked, could this confuse the model?  

**Additional Comments**  
- The presentation is clear, but minor language errors persist (e.g., "an solution" → "a solution" in l.190; "significent" → "significance" in l.637; "a bread" → "bread" in l.264).  
- The reference list requires consistent casing (e.g., "Japanese," "ILP," "CONLL").  
- The paper’s focus on intra-sentential arguments (Section 2.1) is justified, but the lack of discussion on inter-sentential challenges (e.g., "Inter-Zero" in Figure 1) weakens the task framing.  

This is a well-executed paper with strong results. Addressing the weaknesses—particularly clarifying zero-argument resolution scope and model constraints—would elevate it from "solid" to "strong." The core contribution (Grid-RNN for multi-predicate PAS) is novel and impactful for Japanese NLP.
