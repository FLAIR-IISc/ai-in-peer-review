- Paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis and proposes neural models that learn multi-predicate interactions from word sequences without syntactic parsers, claiming SOTA on the NAIST Text Corpus and strong zero-pronoun performance.

- Contribution: A Grid-RNN-based multi-sequence model that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions.

- Contribution: Empirical demonstration of state-of-the-art performance on NAIST without syntactic information, especially improving zero pronoun identification.

- Strength: Avoids error propagation from pipeline systems using parsers and outperforms parser-based prior work by ~2–3 F1 on zero pronouns.

- Strength: Strong experimental validation with systematic comparisons, depth/residual ablations, and case-role analysis; multi-sequence > single-sequence (81.42% vs. 81.15% F1), zero-arg gain (47.12% vs. 46.10%).

- Strength: Clear technical exposition and illustrative figures; ablation studies support benefits of residual connections and deeper networks.

- Weakness: Several grammatical inconsistencies and awkward phrases reduce readability and need proofreading.

- Weakness: PRED feature description is ambiguous and lacks a concrete example linking text and figures to clarify what context is included.

- Weakness: Table 2 uses unexplained/inconsistent boldface formatting that should be clarified or removed.

- Question: Why does the multi-sequence model only marginally improve Dep arguments (88.17% vs. 88.10%)—is this due to Dep relying more on syntactic dependencies?

- Question: Dative case gains may be affected by NAIST corpus revision (1.5); did the authors evaluate on 1.4β or 1.5, and how much improvement comes from annotation changes versus the model?
