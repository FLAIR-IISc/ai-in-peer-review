

This paper proposes Japanese SRL models based on Zhou and Xu's (2015) English SRL framework, extending it with Grid-RNNs to model interactions between arguments of multiple predicates. The approach is evaluated on the standard Japanese SRL benchmark (JNLP-2015), achieving statistically significant improvements over the current state of the art.  

**Strengths:**  
The paper is clearly structured and well-motivated, effectively positioning its contribution within the Japanese SRL literature. The proposed model demonstrates a meaningful accuracy gain over prior work, with the Grid-RNN variant offering a modest yet notable improvement over the single-sequential baseline. Crucially, this enhancement primarily stems from better detection of zero-valued arguments—addressing a key challenge highlighted in the paper. The experimental design is sound, and results are presented with appropriate statistical validation.  

**Weaknesses:**  
The core contribution—extending single-sequential modeling to a multi-sequential framework via Grid-RNNs—appears incremental relative to existing work. Specifically, the reported gains from modeling predicate interactions are smaller than those achieved by Ouchi et al. (2015), who first introduced multi-predicate argument interactions in Japanese SRL. More critically, Shibata et al. (2016) already extended Ouchi et al.'s framework using neural networks, directly addressing the same challenge of predicate argument interactions. The authors should explicitly clarify how their Grid-RNN approach differs from Shibata et al. (2016) and why their results represent a meaningful advancement. Without this comparison, the novelty of the method is unclear, and the claimed "significant improvement" over prior SOTA may stem more from dataset-specific optimizations than from a substantial conceptual advance.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper presents a technically competent adaptation of existing methods with demonstrable gains on a standard benchmark. However, the lack of comparative analysis with Shibata et al. (2016)—a direct predecessor in neural modeling of predicate interactions—undermines the perceived novelty. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should:  
1) Provide a detailed comparison between their Grid-RNN model and Shibata et al.'s neural extension of Ouchi et al. (2015), focusing on architectural differences, implementation specifics, and performance disparities.  
2) Clarify whether their zero-argument improvements are uniquely attributable to Grid-RNNs or could be replicated with simpler interaction mechanisms.  
Without these clarifications, the work risks being viewed as a minor adaptation rather than a substantive advance. The results are promising but require stronger contextualization within the existing neural SRL literature.  

**Overall:**  
The paper makes a valid technical contribution to Japanese SRL with a well-executed evaluation. However, the novelty claim is weakened by insufficient differentiation from Shibata et al. (2016). With revisions addressing the comparative analysis and conceptual novelty, this could be a strong submission. As-is, the incremental nature of the extension limits its impact. I recommend *conditional acceptance* pending clarification of the relationship to Shibata et al. (2016) and a more precise articulation of the method's unique advantages.
