

This paper introduces a neural modeling approach for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis that eliminates reliance on syntactic parsers. The primary contribution is a Grid-RNN architecture that captures multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, achieving state-of-the-art results without syntactic features. The secondary contribution is a single-sequence RNN baseline that also outperforms prior syntactic-dependent systems. The authors demonstrate that modeling predicate interactions—particularly for zero arguments (omitted arguments)—is critical for Japanese PAS, where syntactic dependencies are often absent.

Strengths:  
1. **Novel architecture for syntax-free PAS**: The Grid-RNN model (Multi-Seq) is the first to achieve SOTA results without syntactic information, addressing a key limitation of prior work (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015). This is significant because error propagation from parsers has long hindered Japanese PAS accuracy.  
2. **Substantial improvement on zero arguments**: The model achieves 47.12% F1 for zero arguments (vs. 46.10% for the single-sequence baseline and 44.09% for Ouchi et al., 2015), a 3.03-point gain over the prior SOTA. This directly addresses the paper’s core motivation—zero argument identification is notoriously difficult in Japanese due to missing syntactic cues.  
3. **Clear evidence of multi-predicate utility**: The Grid-RNN’s 0.27-point absolute F1 improvement over the single-sequence model (81.42% vs. 81.15%) is statistically significant (p<0.05). Crucially, this gain is most pronounced for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 46.10%), confirming that multi-predicate interactions are especially valuable for resolving omitted arguments.  

Weaknesses:  
1. **Incremental contribution**: The primary advancement (Grid-RNN) extends the single-sequence model in a relatively straightforward manner. While the results are compelling, the novelty is limited to architectural adaptation rather than a fundamentally new approach to multi-predicate modeling. The paper overstates this as a "key contribution" compared to Ouchi et al. (2015), whose linguistic intuition about predicate interactions the authors build upon.  
2. **Insufficient comparison with Shibata et al. (2016)**: Shibata et al. (2016) also used neural networks to improve joint PAS modeling but *relied on syntactic features* (unlike this work). The authors should explicitly contrast their syntax-free approach with Shibata’s neural extension of Ouchi’s framework. Without this, it’s unclear whether their key advance is the neural architecture or the elimination of syntactic dependency.  
3. **Overstated novelty of "no syntactic information"**: While the model avoids parsers, it still uses word indices and contextual features (e.g., windowed predicates), which implicitly encode syntactic-like patterns. The paper should clarify whether these features constitute "syntactic information" in the traditional sense (e.g., dependency trees) or merely lexical context.  

Questions to Authors:  
1. How do the Grid-RNN’s inter-sequence connections specifically model the *semantic* interactions between predicates (e.g., causality or temporal relations), beyond mere proximity?  
2. Given that Shibata et al. (2016) used neural networks with syntactic features, why is their model not a direct baseline for your syntax-free approach?  
3. The paper claims "without syntactic information," but the PRED feature (windowed predicate context) resembles syntactic dependency modeling. Please clarify whether this feature is considered "syntactic" per the field’s standard definition.
