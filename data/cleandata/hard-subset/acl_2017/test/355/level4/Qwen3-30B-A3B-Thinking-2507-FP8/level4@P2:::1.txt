

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces a neural approach to Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis that eliminates reliance on syntactic parsers, addressing a key limitation in prior work. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a neural model that achieves state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information, thereby avoiding error propagation from pipeline processing; (2) a Grid-RNN-based architecture that effectively models multi-predicate interactions for identifying zero arguments (a particularly challenging aspect of Japanese PAS); and (3) comprehensive empirical validation demonstrating significant improvements on zero arguments, which are often omitted in Japanese sentences.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Significant performance gain without syntactic information**: The paper demonstrates that neural models can outperform all prior state-of-the-art systems that rely on syntactic parsing. The multi-sequence model achieves 81.42% F-measure (vs. 79.23% for the best prior system), with a particularly notable 3.0-point improvement on zero arguments (47.12% vs. 44.09%). This is a major contribution because it shows that error-prone syntactic pipelines are unnecessary for high-quality PAS analysis, which is especially valuable for low-resource languages where parsers may be unreliable.  

2. **Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions**: The Grid-RNN architecture successfully captures interactions between predicates across the entire sentence, which is crucial for identifying zero arguments. The multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence variant by 0.27 points in overall F-measure (81.42% vs. 81.15%), with a 1.0-point improvement on zero arguments. This validates the authors' core insight that multi-predicate interactions provide critical information for PAS, especially in cases where syntactic dependencies are absent.  

3. **Thorough evaluation and analysis**: The paper provides detailed results across different case roles (NOM, ACC, DAT) and argument types (Dep vs. Zero), demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines. The analysis of dative case performance (which saw a 30-point improvement) is particularly insightful, showing the model's robustness despite infrequent occurrence of dative arguments. The ablation study on network depth and residual connections further strengthens the claims about model design choices.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Ambiguous explanation of PRED feature**: Section 3.1's description of the PRED feature lacks clarity, particularly regarding how context around the predicate is incorporated. For example, Figure 4 shows "食べた (ate)" as the predicate, but the PRED feature includes the accusative marker "を" (as in "パンを食べた"), which should be explicitly explained. The current description does not clarify whether the PRED window includes grammatical markers or just content words.  

2. **Unclear formatting in Table 2**: The use of boldface in Table 2 (p. 7) is inconsistent and confusing, as it appears to highlight certain results without clear rationale. This detracts from the paper's readability and could mislead readers about the significance of specific values.  

3. **Underdeveloped comparison to English SRL**: Section 5.3 contrasts the work with English SRL approaches (Zhou and Xu, 2015) but does not sufficiently explain why the authors chose to replace CRF with softmax (due to Japanese dependency structure) or how their multi-predicate approach differs from Yang and Zong (2014) in English. A clearer articulation of these distinctions would strengthen the contribution claim.  

4. **Minor grammatical issues**: The paper contains several grammatical errors that affect readability, such as "As an solution" (p. 2), "the multi-predicate interactions" (p. 4), and "NAIST Text Corpus" (p. 8). These should be corrected to maintain professionalism.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. In Figure 4, the PRED feature for "食べた" includes the accusative marker "を" in the window. Could the authors clarify whether this is intentional (as part of the linguistic context) or an artifact of the feature extraction?  
2. The paper states that the multi-sequence model "jointly predicts arguments for all predicates in a sentence." How does this differ from the joint approach of Ouchi et al. (2015), which also considers all predicate-argument combinations?  
3. The authors note that their model outperforms prior work without external resources. Given the success of neural approaches in English SRL using unlabeled data (e.g., Collobert et al., 2011), would incorporating unlabeled Japanese data further improve performance?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper is well-written overall and makes a strong contribution to Japanese PAS analysis. The minor grammatical issues noted above should be addressed in the final version. The authors' response to the typo corrections was satisfactory.
