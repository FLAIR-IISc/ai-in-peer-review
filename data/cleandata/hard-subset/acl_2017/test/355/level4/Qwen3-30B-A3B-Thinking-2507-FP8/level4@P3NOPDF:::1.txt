

The paper presents a compelling application of Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks for predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, achieving notable results without relying on explicit syntactic structure. This is particularly significant as the proposed approach outperforms state-of-the-art systems that incorporate syntactic features, demonstrating the effectiveness of the neural architecture alone. The authors provide a clear, detailed technical description of their multi-sequence model—which processes all predicates in a sentence simultaneously—and its application to challenging Japanese phenomena like dropped arguments and zero pronouns. Their rigorous evaluation on these specific cases, where the model achieves the best performance, is a major strength, as these are pervasive yet underexplored challenges in Japanese PAS. The paper is well-structured, thoroughly documented, and makes a substantive contribution to the field.

The primary weakness is minor and largely confined to presentation. The rationale for the boldface formatting in Table 2 (p. 7) is unclear; clarifying this would enhance readability. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a concrete illustration of the PRED tokenization strategy described in Section 3.2 (p. 5), particularly regarding how contextual elements like the accusative marker are included in the PRED string. The example in Figure 6, where the verb "tabemashita" appears with the accusative marker "o", would effectively demonstrate this point. Including this would resolve potential ambiguity about the model's input representation.

The following typos and grammatical corrections are recommended to improve fluency and precision:
*   p. 1: "error propagation does not need 'the'"; "multi-predicate interactions" (no article).
*   p. 2: "As a solution" (not "an solution"); "a single-sequence model" (added article); "a multi-sequence model" (added article).
*   p. 3 (Fig. 4 example): "She ate bread" (not "a bread").
*   p. 4: "assumed independence" (not "assumes"); "multi-predicate interactions" (not "the multi-predicate interactions"); "a multi-sequence model" (added article).
*   p. 7: "residual connections" (not "the residual connections"); "multi-predicate interactions" (corrected twice).
*   p. 8: "the NAIST Text Corpus" (added article); "state-of-the-art results" (plural, as multiple results are compared).

The authors’ response to these points is adequate, and the suggested corrections are straightforward. The paper’s core contribution—demonstrating that a syntactically-agnostic neural model can surpass syntactically-informed baselines on Japanese PAS, especially for zero pronouns—is robust and well-supported. The multi-sequence approach effectively captures predicate interactions, which is a key insight. The writing is generally clear, but the minor grammatical issues and presentation points noted above should be addressed for final publication.

This work offers a valuable methodological advance for Japanese NLP, particularly in handling its unique morphosyntactic features. The results are convincing, the model is well-motivated, and the focus on challenging zero-pronoun cases is especially relevant. With the minor editorial revisions proposed, the paper would be suitable for publication at ACL 2017.
