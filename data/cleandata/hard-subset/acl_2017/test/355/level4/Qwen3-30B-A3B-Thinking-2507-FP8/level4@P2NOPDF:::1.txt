

Strengths:  
This paper presents a sophisticated application of Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks to predicate-argument structure (PAS) prediction in Japanese, achieving state-of-the-art results without relying on explicit syntactic structures—a notable advancement over prior work. The authors provide a clear, detailed description of their implementation and results, with particular emphasis on handling challenging Japanese phenomena: dropped arguments and zero pronouns. Their multi-sequence model, which jointly processes all predicates in a sentence, delivers the strongest performance on these cases. The paper is well-structured, technically thorough, and clearly written, making its contributions accessible and compelling.  

Weaknesses:  
The weaknesses are minor. First, the paper could clarify the composition of the "PRED" token in Figure 6 (e.g., by explicitly noting that the accusative marker is included within the PRED string). Second, the boldface formatting in Table 2 (p. 7) is unclear and should be explained. These are easily addressed and do not detract from the paper’s core contribution.

- p. 8: "NAIST Text Corpus" → "the NAIST Text Corpus"; "the state-of-the-art result" → "state-of-the-art results."  

The authors’ response adequately addresses these points. The paper makes a strong contribution to Japanese PAS, particularly through its effective handling of zero pronouns and dropped arguments without syntactic dependencies. The multi-sequence approach is well-motivated and empirically validated. Given the minor nature of the requested revisions, this paper is suitable for acceptance.
