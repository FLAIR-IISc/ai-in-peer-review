

This paper presents a joint neural modeling approach for Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese using Grid-RNNs, comparing it against a conventional single-sequence RNN baseline. The work is well-executed, addresses a well-established task in Japanese NLP, achieves state-of-the-art results, and explains the task accessibly for non-Japanese speakers. However, the paper could strengthen its presentation of the task’s inherent complexity. Specifically, the ambiguity in Figure 1’s example—where the first predicate’s NOM argument overlaps with the second predicate’s NOM argument and the ACC argument—warrants deeper discussion. Clarifying how this ambiguity differs from Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly regarding zero pronouns, would better contextualize the challenge. Additionally, including statistics on the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would highlight a key complication: the need to implicitly distinguish between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors. 

A critical question remains about the model’s representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns. The paper appears to omit explicit marking for such arguments (i.e., no representation of the zero pronoun itself), which seems suboptimal. If zero pronouns are simply unmarked, the model lacks explicit signals to capture them, potentially limiting its performance. A brief discussion of this design choice and its implications would significantly strengthen the work. 

The paper also overlooks two constraints commonly modeled in ILP-based SRL approaches: (1) predicates typically have only one argument per type (e.g., NOM/ACC), and (2) a single argument usually occupies only one slot per predicate. An analysis of whether the model learns these constraints—e.g., via error patterns or output statistics—would provide valuable insight. Furthermore, Table 3 shows minimal improvements for Multi-Seq (beyond NOM), yet the paper does not explore *why*. A comparative analysis of the two models’ outputs—e.g., whether their predictions are largely identical, differ significantly but yield similar aggregate scores, or exhibit distinct patterns in specific contexts—would illuminate the model’s internal behavior and elevate the paper from "solid" to "strong." Such analysis would also clarify whether the Multi-Seq approach’s gains are meaningful or superficial. 

The presentation is generally clear, with figures effectively illustrating the model. Minor language issues are present but easily correctable:

- Line 637: "significance" (not "significent")

- Ensure consistent capitalization in references (e.g., "Japanese," "LSTM," "CoNLL," "ILP").  

In summary, the paper makes a valuable contribution to Japanese PAS analysis with strong empirical results. Addressing the task complexity, zero pronoun representation, constraint modeling, and model comparison would substantially enhance its impact. These refinements would transform a solid paper into a compelling one, particularly for readers outside Japanese NLP.
