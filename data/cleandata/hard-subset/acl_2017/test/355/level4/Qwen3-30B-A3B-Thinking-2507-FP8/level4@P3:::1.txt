

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces a neural modeling approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that avoids reliance on syntactic parsers, addressing the error propagation problem inherent in current state-of-the-art (SOA) systems. The authors propose two models: a single-sequence model that processes each predicate independently and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions across all predicates in a sentence. Their main contributions are: (1) a novel neural architecture that eliminates syntactic dependencies while achieving SOA performance; (2) empirical demonstration that multi-predicate interactions significantly improve zero argument identification—a critical challenge in Japanese PAS; (3) validation that word-sequence-based neural models can outperform syntactic-dependent approaches without external resources.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel error-avoidance approach**: The paper effectively tackles the error propagation issue plaguing current PAS systems by eliminating syntactic dependencies. By processing all predicates simultaneously through Grid-RNNs, the model avoids the pipeline errors of previous work (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015), achieving 81.42% F1 on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5—surpassing all prior syntactic-dependent methods. This represents a fundamental methodological advance in the field.

2. **Significant improvement on zero arguments**: The multi-sequence model achieves a 3.0-point F1 improvement (47.12% vs. 44.09%) over Ouchi et al.'s SOA system for zero arguments (the most challenging case type). This is particularly noteworthy as zero arguments lack direct syntactic dependencies, making them notoriously difficult to resolve. The authors convincingly demonstrate that multi-predicate interactions provide crucial context for identifying these omitted arguments.

3. **Strong empirical validation**: The experiments are thorough and well-designed, with clear ablation studies showing the benefits of Grid-RNNs (81.42% vs. 81.15% for single-sequence) and residual connections (1.0-point improvement for deeper models). The results are statistically significant (p < 0.05), and the model's performance on dative arguments (64.38% F1) is especially impressive given their low frequency.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient comparison with neural baselines**: The paper compares only with syntactic-dependent systems but fails to benchmark against neural approaches that also operate without syntactic information (e.g., Shibata et al., 2016; Iida et al., 2016). The claim that "even without syntactic information, our neural models realize SOA performance" lacks context—how do they compare to other neural models using similar input features? This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Ambiguous feature description**: The PRED feature (Section 3.1) is poorly explained. The paper states it "extracts words around the predicate" but doesn't clarify how this is implemented in the Grid-RNN context (e.g., Figure 6's PRED string includes "を" (ACC marker) with the verb). A concrete example would clarify whether the PRED feature captures syntactic context or merely word sequences.

3. **Unclear Table 2 formatting**: The boldface in Table 2 (p. 7) is confusing and unexplained. Without knowing which values are emphasized, readers cannot assess the significance of the performance differences between models with/without residual connections.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How do your results compare to Shibata et al. (2016), who also used neural networks for PAS analysis but incorporated syntactic features? Could their model achieve similar results without syntactic information if adapted to your architecture?

2. In Figure 6, the PRED feature for "食べた" (ate) includes "を" (ACC marker). Does this imply your model implicitly captures syntactic information through word sequence patterns, or is this an artifact of the input representation?

3. The paper states "our neural models realize SOA performance without syntactic information." Given that previous neural approaches (e.g., Iida et al., 2016) used selectional preferences as features, how do you justify that your model's input (pure word sequences) is more effective than these features?

**Additional Comments**  
- The paper contains minor grammatical errors (e.g., "As an solution" → "As a solution," "the multi-predicate interactions" → "multi-predicate interactions" without "the"). These should be corrected for clarity.  
- The claim that "the multi-sequence model significantly outperformed the single-sequence model" (Table 1) should specify whether this holds across all case roles (e.g., dative shows a smaller gain).  
- The discussion of "zero pronouns" (Section 2.2) would benefit from a clearer distinction between Dep and Zero arguments in the example sentence.
