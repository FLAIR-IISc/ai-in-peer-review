

Summary: This paper introduces neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that operate without syntactic information. The main contributions are: (1) a single-sequence model using RNNs to capture context-sensitive representations for argument identification; (2) a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates; (3) demonstration that these models achieve state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus without relying on syntactic information.

Strengths: The paper effectively addresses the challenging problem of zero argument identification in Japanese PAS analysis by demonstrating that neural models can capture long-distance dependencies without syntactic information. The multi-sequence model achieves a significant improvement (47.12% F1 vs. 46.10% for single-sequence) specifically for zero arguments, directly addressing the core challenge in Japanese PAS analysis. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with proper comparison against relevant baselines including the state-of-the-art model by Ouchi et al. (2015), and the results demonstrate clear improvements across multiple metrics.

Weaknesses: The primary contribution appears to be extending the single-sequence model to a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach, with only modest performance gains (0.27 F1 points) over the single-sequence model. The paper does not adequately compare with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extended Ouchi et al. (2015)'s model with neural networks, making it difficult to assess the novelty and relative contribution of the current work. The impact of capturing multi-predicate interactions appears smaller than claimed in the literature (Ouchi et al., 2015), as the improvement for zero arguments (1.0 F1 point) is comparable to the improvement over the baseline model rather than representing a substantial advancement.

Questions to Authors: Could you provide a detailed comparison between your multi-sequence model and the neural model proposed by Shibata et al. (2016), particularly regarding their approach to handling multi-predicate interactions? The paper claims that the Grid-RNN model "effectively captures multi-predicate interactions," but the performance improvement over the single-sequence model is modest. Could you elaborate on why the improvement is not larger, and what specific interactions are most beneficial for zero argument identification?
