

This paper introduces a joint neural approach for Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese using Grid-RNNs, comparing it against a conventional single-sequence RNN baseline. The work is well-executed, achieves state-of-the-art results on a well-established task, and presents the problem accessibly for non-Japanese specialists. However, several improvements would strengthen the contribution.

The paper could better articulate the task's inherent complexity. For instance, Figure 1’s example demonstrates ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate and the NOM argument of the second predicate. Clarifying how this ambiguity—particularly the challenge of resolving zero pronouns—distinguishes PAS from Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) would be valuable. Additionally, including statistics on the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would highlight a key difficulty: the task requires implicitly distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors. A critical question remains: for inter-sentential zero pronouns (where the argument is not explicitly stated in the sentence), how does the model represent this? The paper suggests arguments are omitted entirely rather than marked as empty. This lack of explicit representation for zero pronouns may hinder the model’s ability to capture them effectively, and the paper would benefit from addressing this design choice.

The model also appears to overlook two well-known constraints in PAS that ILP-based SRL methods explicitly enforce: (1) a predicate typically has only one argument per type (e.g., NOM or ACC), and (2) a single argument generally fills only one slot per predicate. Analysis of the model’s outputs to assess how well these constraints are learned would provide deeper insight. Furthermore, Table 3 shows minimal gains for Multi-Seq (Grid-RNN) over Single-Seq, with improvements primarily limited to NOM arguments. A more detailed comparison of the models’ outputs—such as whether differences are context-specific (e.g., clustered in particular syntactic constructions), statistically significant, or merely additive in aggregate—would clarify the added value of the joint modeling approach. This analysis would transform a solid contribution into a stronger one by revealing the model’s internal behavior.

The presentation is generally clear, with figures effectively illustrating the model. Minor language issues require correction:  
- Line 19: "the error propagation" → "error propagation"  
- Line 190: "an solution" → "a solution"  
- Line 264 (Figure 2): "a bread" → "bread"  
- Line 351: "the independence" → "independence"  
- Line 512: "the good" → "good"  
- Line 531: "from their model" → "of their model"  
- Line 637: "significent" → "significant"  
- Line 638: "both of" → "both"  
Also, ensure consistent casing in references (e.g., "Japanese," "LSTM," "CoNLL," "ILP").  

While including SRL results would be valuable, this would require substantial additional work beyond the current scope. The core improvements should focus on clarifying the task’s complexity, resolving the zero pronoun representation question, validating constraint adherence, and providing nuanced model comparison. Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and clarity.
