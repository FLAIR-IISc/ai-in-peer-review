

Summary and Contributions:
This paper addresses Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on the challenging problem of zero argument identification in pro-drop languages. The authors propose neural sequence models that process word sequences directly without relying on syntactic information from parsers, which addresses the error propagation issue in previous joint modeling approaches. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) a neural model for PAS analysis that eliminates reliance on syntactic parsers, achieving state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information; (2) a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions, particularly improving zero argument identification; and (3) empirical demonstration that word sequence information alone can yield competitive results, challenging the assumption that syntactic features are essential for this task.

Strengths:
1. The paper successfully addresses a fundamental limitation of current Japanese PAS analysis: the heavy dependence on syntactic parsers that introduces error propagation. By developing a neural model that processes word sequences directly, the authors eliminate this critical weakness, demonstrating that high accuracy can be achieved without syntactic information. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it simplifies the pipeline and avoids cascading errors.

2. The multi-sequence model with Grid-RNNs achieves a notable 3-point improvement in F-measure for zero arguments over the previous state-of-the-art, which is particularly important given that zero arguments represent a major challenge in Japanese PAS analysis. The paper provides convincing evidence that capturing multi-predicate interactions significantly benefits zero argument identification, with the multi-sequence model outperforming the single-sequence model by 1.0 point for zero arguments specifically.

3. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed, with comprehensive comparisons against multiple baselines on the standard NAIST Text Corpus benchmark. The results are statistically significant, and the paper provides detailed analysis of performance across different case roles, demonstrating consistent improvements across the board, especially for the challenging dative case.

4. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution and evaluation. The technical descriptions of the single-sequence and multi-sequence models are sufficiently detailed, making the contributions accessible to readers.

Weaknesses:
1. The main contribution of extending the single-sequence model to a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs appears somewhat incremental. While the authors demonstrate improved performance, the value of multi-predicate interactions was already established by Ouchi et al. (2015), who showed that joint modeling of multiple predicates improves PAS analysis. The current work builds upon this insight but doesn't substantially advance the linguistic understanding of why these interactions matter.

2. The paper lacks a sufficient comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), which also introduced neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis by extending Ouchi et al. (2015)'s model. Shibata et al. used a feed-forward neural network for the score calculation part of the joint model. The current paper's comparison with this work is cursory and doesn't explain why the authors' approach is superior or how it differs conceptually from Shibata et al.'s neural extension.

3. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why Grid-RNNs were chosen as the architecture to capture multi-predicate interactions, compared to other neural network approaches that could potentially handle sequence-to-sequence relationships. The authors mention that Grid-RNNs were used for their ability to propagate information across sequences, but a more detailed justification would strengthen the contribution.

4. The significance of the 1-3 point F-measure improvement in the context of the field isn't fully contextualized. While the improvements are statistically significant, the paper doesn't discuss whether these gains are substantial enough to make a meaningful difference in practical applications or whether the marginal gains are typical for the state of the art in this specific task.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do your Grid-RNN-based multi-sequence model and Shibata et al. (2016)'s neural extension of Ouchi et al. (2015) differ conceptually in terms of how they model predicate interactions? Could Shibata et al.'s approach be adapted to use a similar multi-sequence architecture?

2. The paper demonstrates that your model achieves state-of-the-art results without syntactic information, but what specific features of the word sequence information are most important for capturing multi-predicate interactions? Could you provide more insight into what the model has learned about these interactions?

3. Given that the multi-sequence model's main improvement is for zero arguments, which is the focus of the paper, how would you expect your model to perform on tasks where zero arguments are less frequent or absent, such as English Semantic Role Labeling?
