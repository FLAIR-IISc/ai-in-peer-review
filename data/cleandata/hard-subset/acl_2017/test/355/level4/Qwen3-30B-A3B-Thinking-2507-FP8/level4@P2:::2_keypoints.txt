- Proposes a neural PAS model that removes reliance on syntactic parsers and achieves state-of-the-art results without syntactic input.  
- Introduces a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model to capture multi-predicate interactions, improving zero-argument identification (≈3-point F gain).  
- Shows empirically that raw word sequence information alone can be competitive with syntax-based approaches.  

- Eliminates parser-induced error propagation, simplifying the pipeline while maintaining high accuracy.  
- Multi-sequence/Grid-RNN yields notable gains for zero arguments, outperforming single-sequence by ~1.0 point for that case.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough on the NAIST Text Corpus with statistically significant improvements and detailed per-case analysis (notably dative).  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written with adequate technical detail on the models.  

- Extension to multi-sequence/Grid-RNN is somewhat incremental given prior work (Ouchi et al. 2015) showing benefits of multi-predicate modeling.  
- Comparison with Shibata et al. (2016) is insufficient; lacks clear conceptual or empirical differentiation.  
- Choice of Grid-RNNs is under-justified versus other architectures that could model sequence interactions.  
- The practical significance of the 1–3 point F-measure improvements is not fully contextualized for real-world impact.  

- How do the Grid-RNN multi-sequence approach and Shibata et al. (2016)’s neural extension differ conceptually, and can Shibata’s method adopt a multi-sequence setup?  
- Which specific aspects of the word-sequence signal are most important for modeling multi-predicate interactions, and what has the model learned about these interactions?  
- How would the model perform on tasks or languages with few/no zero arguments (e.g., English SRL)?
