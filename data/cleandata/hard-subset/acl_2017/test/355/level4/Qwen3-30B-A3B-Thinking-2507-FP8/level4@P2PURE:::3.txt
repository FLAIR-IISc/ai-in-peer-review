

This paper presents a joint neural modeling approach for Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese using Grid-RNNs, comparing it against conventional single-sequence RNN methods. It is a well-executed study targeting a well-established Japanese NLP task, achieving state-of-the-art results while presenting the task accessibly to readers unfamiliar with Japanese. However, the paper could better highlight the task's complexity: for instance, Figure 1's example demonstrates inherent ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate (which also serves as the NOM argument of the second predicate), and a clearer contrast with Semantic Role Labeling (SRL)—particularly regarding ambiguity in zero pronouns—would strengthen the motivation. Additionally, statistics on the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would further clarify the task's difficulty (i.e., the need to implicitly distinguish intra- vs. inter-sentential zero anaphors). A critical question remains: for inter-sentential zero pronouns (as arguments), how is the argument represented? The paper appears to omit explicit marking (i.e., no representation for the zero pronoun), which constitutes a slightly suboptimal representation (potentially hindering the model's ability to capture zero pronouns); a discussion of this design choice is warranted.

The model also fails to capture constraints explicitly addressed in some ILP-based SRL methods: (1) a predicate typically has only one argument per type (especially NOM/ACC), and (2) an argument generally occupies only one slot per predicate. Analysis of the model's output regarding these constraints would be valuable. Furthermore, the mixed results in Table 3 (where Multi-Seq shows improvement primarily for NOM) warrant deeper exploration: Are the models' outputs largely identical, or do differences exist yet yield similar aggregate scores? Identifying specific contexts where the models diverge would illuminate their internal behavior and would be the paper's most significant improvement opportunity (beyond adding SRL results, which would require substantial additional work).

The paper's presentation is generally strong, with figures effectively clarifying the model. Minor language issues require correction:
