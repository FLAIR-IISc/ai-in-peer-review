

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on identifying semantic arguments for predicates in sentences. The authors propose neural models that automatically learn features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequences alone, eliminating reliance on syntactic parsers and avoiding error propagation. The primary contributions are: (1) a Grid-RNN-based multi-sequence model that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions, and (2) empirical demonstration that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus without syntactic information, particularly excelling at identifying zero pronoun arguments (a key challenge in Japanese PAS).

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel approach to error propagation**: The work effectively sidesteps the error propagation problem inherent in pipeline-based PAS systems by using neural networks to directly model multi-predicate interactions from word sequences. This is a significant methodological contribution, as prior state-of-the-art systems (Ouchi et al., 2015) rely on syntactic parsers that introduce cumulative errors. The authors demonstrate that their model outperforms these systems by 2-3 F1 points on zero pronoun arguments alone—a critical improvement for Japanese PAS.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The experiments are thorough and well-structured. The authors systematically compare single-sequence and multi-sequence models, ablate network depth and residual connections, and provide detailed case-role analysis. Crucially, they show that the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model (81.42% vs. 81.15% F1), with particularly notable gains for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 46.10%). This directly validates their core hypothesis about the value of multi-predicate interactions.

3. **Clear technical exposition**: The paper excels in explaining the model architecture (e.g., Grid-RNN layer details in Section 4.2) and implementation choices (e.g., feature engineering in Section 3.1). The figures (Figures 2-6) effectively illustrate the model's design, and the ablation studies (Table 2) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of residual connections and deeper networks.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Minor grammatical inconsistencies**: The paper contains several grammatical issues that hinder readability, such as "As an solution" (p. 2), "the multi-predicate interactions" (p. 4), and inconsistent use of "the" (e.g., "the NAIST Text Corpus" vs. "NAIST Text Corpus" on p. 8). These should be corrected for professional presentation.

2. **Ambiguous description of PRED features**: The PRED feature (Section 3.1) is described as "words around the predicate," but the explanation lacks a concrete example from the paper's figures. For instance, Figure 6 shows the PRED feature including the accusative marker with the verb, but the text doesn't explicitly connect this to the feature definition. Adding a brief example (e.g., "For the predicate '食べた' in '彼女はパンを食べた,' the PRED feature includes the context 'パンを食べた'") would clarify this critical design choice.

3. **Confusing formatting in Table 2**: The boldface in Table 2 (p. 7) is unexplained and appears inconsistent. The authors should either clarify the meaning of boldface (e.g., "bold indicates significant improvement") or remove it to avoid confusion.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper states that the multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence model for Dep arguments (88.17% vs. 88.10%), but the improvement is small. Could the authors elaborate on why this minimal gain occurs despite the model's design to capture multi-predicate interactions? Is this due to the inherent syntactic dependencies of Dep arguments?

2. The authors note that dative case results improved dramatically (Table 3), but the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 revision (p. 10) changes the annotation criterion for DAT. How much of this improvement is attributable to the corpus update versus the model? Could the authors clarify whether their model was evaluated on the original 1.4β or 1.5 version for the dative results?
