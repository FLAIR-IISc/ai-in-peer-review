

This paper proposes a neural modeling approach for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis using grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs), comparing it with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus without relying on syntactic information, addressing the error propagation problem inherent in previous joint modeling approaches.

Main contributions as I see them:
1. The paper introduces a neural model that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information, eliminating the need for syntactic parsing and avoiding error propagation.
2. The model significantly improves zero argument identification, which is one of the most challenging aspects of Japanese PAS analysis.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a well-executed solution to a well-established problem in Japanese natural language processing, achieving state-of-the-art results without syntactic information.
2. The model design is clear and well-motivated, with a solid experimental evaluation on a standard dataset that demonstrates the effectiveness of Grid-RNNs for capturing multi-predicate interactions.
3. The presentation is accessible to readers not familiar with Japanese linguistic nuances, with helpful figures illustrating the model architecture and key concepts.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could better elaborate on the inherent complexity of the task, particularly the ambiguity in zero pronoun resolution (e.g., distinguishing between NOM and ACC arguments for the first predicate versus the NOM argument for the second predicate).
2. There is insufficient discussion about how the model handles inter-sentential zero pronouns (whether they are marked as empty arguments or simply not represented), which affects the model's ability to capture zero pronouns.
3. The paper does not analyze how well the model learns important linguistic constraints (e.g., one predicate having only one argument per case role), which would provide deeper insights into the model's behavior.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the model represent inter-sentential zero pronouns (e.g., are they marked as empty arguments or not represented at all)?
2. Could the authors provide an analysis of the model's output to assess how well it enforces linguistic constraints like one argument per predicate type?
3. What specific contexts or sentence structures lead to differences between the Single-Seq and Multi-Seq model predictions?
