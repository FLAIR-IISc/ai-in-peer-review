

This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach for Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese using Grid-RNNs, comparing it against a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. The work is well-executed, targeting a well-established Japanese NLP task, achieving state-of-the-art results, and presenting the task accessibly for non-Japanese specialists.  

However, the paper could strengthen its exposition of task complexity. For instance, Figure 1’s example involving ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate (and their relationship to the NOM argument of the second predicate) warrants deeper discussion. Explicitly contrasting PAS with Semantic Role Labeling (SRL)—particularly regarding the challenge of zero pronouns—would clarify the task’s distinctiveness. Additionally, including statistics on the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would further highlight the task’s complexity (i.e., the need to implicitly distinguish intra- vs. inter-sentential zero anaphors). A critical question remains: for inter-sentential zero pronouns, how is the argument represented? The paper implies no marking (i.e., no explicit representation of the zero pronoun), which risks being a defective representation for the model’s ability to capture zero anaphora. A discussion of this design choice would be valuable.  

The model also appears to omit certain constraints implicitly handled by ILP-based SRL methods: (1) a predicate typically has only one argument per type (e.g., NOM/ACC), and (2) a single argument generally fills only one slot per predicate. Analysis of the model’s output to assess how well these constraints are learned would strengthen the work. Furthermore, Table 3 reveals that Multi-Seq improvements are largely confined to NOM, with minimal gains elsewhere. A deeper comparison of the two models’ outputs—e.g., whether differences are context-specific, how frequently outputs diverge, or why NOM benefits most—would illuminate the models’ internal behavior. Such analysis would elevate the paper from solid to strong, addressing the core limitation in the current presentation (beyond the scope of adding SRL results, which would require significant additional work).
