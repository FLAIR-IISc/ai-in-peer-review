

This paper presents a joint neural approach for Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese using Grid-RNNs, achieving state-of-the-art results on a well-established task. The work is solidly executed and accessible to readers unfamiliar with Japanese linguistics, making a valuable contribution to the field. However, several improvements would strengthen the paper’s impact and clarity.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the task’s inherent complexity, particularly regarding the ambiguity in argument identification. For instance, Figure 1’s example involves ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate (which also serves as the NOM argument of the second predicate). Clarifying this ambiguity—especially how it differs from Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), where zero pronouns are less prevalent—would better contextualize the challenge. Additionally, including statistics on the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would highlight the task’s difficulty: resolving such pronouns requires distinguishing between intra-sentential and inter-sentential anaphora, a nuance not fully addressed. A critical question arises here: how are inter-sentential zero pronouns represented? The paper implies they are omitted entirely (e.g., no marker for the argument), but this risks creating an incomplete representation. If the model cannot explicitly recognize the presence of a zero pronoun (rather than treating it as absent), its ability to resolve such cases may be compromised. A brief discussion of this representation choice would clarify the model’s limitations.

The paper also overlooks key linguistic constraints that other methods (e.g., ILP-based SRL systems) explicitly model. Specifically:  
(1) Predicates typically have at most one argument per role (e.g., only one NOM argument), and  
(2) Arguments generally occupy only one role per predicate.  
The authors should analyze whether their model implicitly learns these constraints. For example, Table 3 shows marginal improvements for Multi-Seq (Grid-RNN) over Single-Seq, primarily for NOM arguments. A deeper analysis of *where* the models diverge—such as examining specific error cases or comparing output structures—would reveal whether the improvements stem from better constraint adherence or other factors. Without this, it remains unclear if the model’s success arises from capturing linguistic structure or incidental patterns. Such analysis would transform this from a "solid" to a "strong" paper.

Regarding presentation, the paper is well-structured, with figures effectively illustrating the model. However, minor language issues require correction:  
- Line 19: "the error propagation" (not "error propagation")  
- Line 190: "a solution" (not "an solution")  
- Line 264 & Figure 2: "bread" (not "a bread")  
- Line 351: "independence" (not "the independence")  
- Line 512: "good" (not "the good")  
- Line 531: "of their model" (not "from their model")  
- Line 637: "significance" (not "significent")  
- Line 638: "both" (not "both of")  
- Ensure consistent casing in references (e.g., "Japanese," "LSTM," "CoNLL," "ILP," not "japanese," "lstm," "conll," "ilp").

While the paper’s core contribution is valuable, the absence of deeper analysis into the model’s internal behavior and task-specific challenges slightly limits its impact. Addressing the representation of zero anaphors, the modeling of linguistic constraints, and the contextual differences between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq outputs would significantly enhance the paper’s rigor. Including SRL comparisons (as noted in the draft) would be beneficial but is secondary to the above points, as it would require substantial additional work beyond the current scope.

Overall, this is a strong paper with clear empirical results and a well-motivated approach. With the suggested revisions—particularly clarifying task complexity, zero pronoun representation, and constraint learning—it would make an excellent contribution to ACL. The authors should prioritize these additions to maximize the paper’s clarity and scholarly value.
