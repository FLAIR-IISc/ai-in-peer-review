- Primary contribution: Grid-RNN architecture that models multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, achieving SOTA PAS without syntactic parsers.  
- Secondary contribution: Single-sequence RNN baseline that outperforms prior syntactic-dependent systems.  
- Demonstrated that modeling predicate interactions is critical for Japanese PAS, especially for zero (omitted) arguments.  
- Strength: Novel syntax-free architecture addressing parser error propagation and achieving SOTA compared to prior work (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015).  
- Strength: Substantial improvement on zero-argument F1 (47.12% vs. 46.10% baseline and 44.09% prior SOTA), a +3.03 point gain.  
- Strength: Clear, statistically significant (p<0.05) benefit of multi-predicate modeling—Grid-RNN beats single-sequence model (81.42% vs. 81.15%), largest gain on zero arguments.  
- Weakness: Incremental novelty—Grid-RNN is a straightforward extension of the single-sequence model and builds on Ouchi et al.’s linguistic intuition.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), which used neural nets with syntactic features; unclear whether gains come from architecture or removing syntactic features.  
- Weakness: Claim of “no syntactic information” is overstated because PRED/windowed predicate features may implicitly encode syntactic-like information and need clarification.  
- Question: How do Grid-RNN inter-sequence connections model semantic predicate interactions (e.g., causality/temporal) beyond proximity?  
- Question: Why isn’t Shibata et al. (2016) included as a direct baseline for the syntax-free approach?  
- Question: Is the PRED feature (windowed predicate context) considered “syntactic information” by field standards, and please clarify its role.
