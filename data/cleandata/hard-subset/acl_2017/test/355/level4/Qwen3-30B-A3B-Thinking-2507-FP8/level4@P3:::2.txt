

# Review for "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, a semantic task that identifies arguments for each predicate in a sentence. The authors propose neural sequence models that eliminate reliance on syntactic parsers, thereby avoiding error propagation issues inherent in previous joint modeling approaches. Their main contributions, ranked by strength, are:

1. Demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art PAS performance without syntactic information, using only word sequence data.
2. Introducing a multi-sequence model with Grid-RNNs that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions, particularly improving zero argument identification (a challenging problem in Japanese PAS due to omitted arguments).
3. Providing a thorough evaluation that shows significant improvements over previous approaches, especially for zero arguments where the multi-sequence model achieves 47.12% F-measure compared to 44.09% for the previous state-of-the-art.

## Strengths
1. **Novel approach to eliminating syntactic dependency**: The paper makes a strong contribution by showing that neural models can bypass the need for syntactic parsers, which has been a major limitation in previous PAS systems. By using only word sequences, the authors avoid error propagation from parser errors, a significant practical advantage. The results (81.42% F-measure) clearly demonstrate that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information.

2. **Effective handling of zero arguments**: The multi-sequence model shows particular improvement on zero arguments (47.12% vs 44.09% for the previous state-of-the-art), which is the paper's main focus. This is significant because zero arguments are challenging to identify due to the lack of direct syntactic dependencies. The authors convincingly demonstrate that capturing multi-predicate interactions through Grid-RNNs helps address this specific challenge.

3. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a comprehensive comparison against multiple baselines on the standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5. The evaluation includes detailed analysis by case roles (NOM, ACC, DAT), showing consistent improvements across all metrics. The results for dative arguments are especially noteworthy, with the neural models achieving around 30-point improvements over previous approaches.

## Weaknesses
1. **Incremental contribution**: While the paper presents a solid technical contribution, the main advancement (extending single-sequence to multi-sequence model) is somewhat incremental. The improvement in F-measure from the single-sequence to multi-sequence model is modest (81.15% to 81.42%), and the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this small improvement is significant compared to the claimed linguistic importance of multi-predicate interactions.

2. **Missing comparison with relevant prior work**: The paper fails to compare with Shibata et al. (2016), which also extends Ouchi et al. (2015) using neural networks. Shibata et al. (2016) uses a feed-forward neural network for the score calculation part of Ouchi et al.'s joint model, while this paper uses Grid-RNNs for multi-predicate interactions. The absence of this comparison weakens the paper's claim of novelty.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why Grid-RNNs are particularly suited for this task compared to other sequence-to-sequence or multi-sequence architectures. While the authors reference Grid-RNNs from Graves and Schmidhuber (2009), they don't clearly articulate how their implementation differs from other approaches or why it's better for capturing multi-predicate interactions.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that multi-predicate interactions are particularly important for zero arguments, but the improvement in F-measure for zero arguments (46.10% vs 47.12%) is only about 1.0 point. Could you elaborate on why this modest improvement is significant for a task where the previous state-of-the-art was already 44.09%? What specific linguistic phenomena does your model capture that previous approaches missed?

2. Shibata et al. (2016) also used neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis, extending Ouchi et al. (2015) with a feed-forward neural network. How does your Grid-RNN approach differ from theirs, and why does your method achieve better results? Specifically, how does your multi-sequence modeling of predicate interactions differ from their neural extension of the joint model?

3. The paper mentions that their models "realize the state-of-the-art performance by using word sequence information of a sentence" without syntactic information. However, the feature extraction (Section 3.1) appears to use word indices and position information that could be considered as implicit syntactic features. Could you clarify whether these features are truly syntactically neutral or if they encode some syntactic information?
