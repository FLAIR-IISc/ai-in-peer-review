

Summary: The paper introduces a neural modeling approach for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis that eliminates reliance on syntactic parsers by automatically capturing multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information. The primary contributions are: (1) a neural architecture using Grid-RNNs that achieves state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information; (2) significant improvements in zero pronoun resolution, a particularly challenging aspect of Japanese PAS; and (3) thorough experimental validation demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach across multiple metrics and case roles.

Strengths: The work demonstrates that neural models can overcome the error propagation problem inherent in current approaches by eliminating dependency on syntactic parsers. The multi-sequence Grid-RNN model achieves superior results compared to previous state-of-the-art systems, particularly for zero arguments (47.12% F1 vs. 44.09% for the best prior system), which is a critical contribution given the prevalence of argument omission in Japanese. The detailed experimental analysis, including ablation studies on network depth and residual connections, provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed architecture. The paper offers a clear and well-structured explanation of the model components and their relationship to linguistic phenomena, particularly regarding the handling of zero pronouns. The evaluation on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits provides a solid foundation for comparison with prior work.

Weaknesses: The manuscript contains several minor grammatical inconsistencies that affect readability, including "an solution" instead of "a solution" and inconsistent use of "the" before technical terms. The explanation of the PRED feature in Figure 6 could be clarified, particularly regarding how the accusative marker is included with the verb in the PRED string example. The use of boldface in Table 2 is unexplained and potentially confusing for readers. The paper could benefit from a more explicit comparison with similar neural approaches in English semantic role labeling, especially regarding the differences between constituent-based and dependency-based argument identification.

Questions to Authors: Please clarify how the Grid-RNN architecture differs from previous neural approaches to multi-predicate interactions in semantic role labeling, particularly in terms of architectural choices and their impact on performance. Additionally, could you elaborate on why the dative case (DAT) showed such a dramatic improvement compared to other case roles in your neural models?
