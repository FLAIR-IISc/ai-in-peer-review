

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper addresses Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on identifying arguments for predicates in pro-drop languages where arguments are often omitted (zero pronouns). The authors propose a neural approach that avoids reliance on syntactic parsers by using Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. The main contributions are: (1) a novel neural architecture (Grid-RNN) that captures multi-predicate interactions without syntactic information, achieving state-of-the-art results; (2) empirical validation demonstrating significant improvements (up to 3 points F1) for zero argument identification—a key challenge in Japanese PAS; and (3) a thorough ablation showing that multi-predicate interactions are particularly beneficial for resolving zero pronouns.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel approach to a well-established problem**: The paper effectively addresses the error propagation issue in existing joint PAS models that rely on syntactic parsers. By using Grid-RNNs to directly model interactions from word sequences, the authors eliminate a major bottleneck in the pipeline, demonstrating that high accuracy is achievable without syntactic dependencies. The results (81.42% F1) significantly outperform previous state-of-the-art (79.23%) on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, validating the core insight.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The ablation studies provide clear evidence for the value of multi-predicate interactions. The multi-sequence model (81.42% F1) outperforms the single-sequence model (81.15%) by a statistically significant margin, with the largest gains (1.03 points) for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 46.10%). This aligns with linguistic intuition that zero pronouns require contextual information beyond direct syntactic dependencies.

3. **Clear task definition and evaluation**: The paper properly distinguishes intra-sentential (Dep/Zero) from inter-sentential (Inter-Zero) arguments, justifying their focus on the former. The evaluation on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits and careful handling of zero pronouns (e.g., 47.12% F1 for Zero) provides a solid foundation for comparing with prior work.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient task complexity explanation**: The paper fails to adequately explain the challenges of zero pronoun resolution. For example, Figure 1 shows ambiguity between NOM/ACC arguments of the first predicate and the NOM argument of the second predicate, but the paper doesn't clarify how this affects modeling. A detailed analysis of intra-sentential zero pronoun resolution rates (e.g., what percentage of zeros are resolvable within a sentence) would strengthen the task justification.

2. **Unclear representation of inter-sentential zeros**: The paper states that inter-sentential arguments (Inter-Zero) are excluded from analysis, but it's unclear how these are handled in the training data. Are they marked as empty arguments, or simply omitted? If marked as empty, this could introduce bias in the model's ability to distinguish intra- vs. inter-sentential zeros. The authors should clarify this and discuss potential impacts.

3. **Lack of constraint analysis**: The model doesn't explicitly enforce linguistic constraints like "one argument per case role" or "one role per argument." The paper shows marginal improvements for Multi-Seq over Single-Seq (Table 3), but doesn't analyze *where* these improvements occur. For instance, are the differences concentrated in specific contexts (e.g., long-distance dependencies)? A qualitative analysis of model outputs would reveal whether the improvements are meaningful or superficial.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does your model handle inter-sentential zero pronouns (e.g., where the antecedent appears in a previous sentence)? Are they marked as empty arguments in the training data, and if so, how does this affect the model's ability to distinguish intra-sentential zeros?  

2. Given that Multi-Seq only shows marginal improvements over Single-Seq for most case roles (e.g., ACC: 49.51% vs. 50.65%), could you provide examples where the Multi-Seq model makes qualitatively different predictions that lead to the observed F1 gains?  

3. The paper states that "arguments can be divided into Dep, Zero, and Inter-Zero" but excludes Inter-Zero from analysis. What percentage of zero pronouns in the NAIST corpus are intra-sentential (i.e., resolvable within the sentence)? This would help contextualize the task's difficulty.  

**Additional Comments**  
- The paper uses "significent" (l.637) instead of "significant" and has minor grammatical issues (e.g., "an solution" l.190).  
- The comparison to English SRL (Section 5.3) could be strengthened by explicitly addressing how the dependency-based PAS task differs from constituent-based SRL in terms of modeling requirements.  
- The reference list has inconsistent casing (e.g., "japanese," "lstm").
