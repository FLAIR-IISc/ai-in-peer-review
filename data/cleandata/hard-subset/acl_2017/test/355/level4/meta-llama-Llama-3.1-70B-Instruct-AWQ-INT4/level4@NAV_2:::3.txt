This paper proposes a joint neural modelling approach to PAS analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, which it compares variously with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach.

Summary of the paper:
The paper presents a neural model for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information of a sentence. The model uses grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNN) to capture the interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The authors evaluate their model on the NAIST Text Corpus and demonstrate that it achieves state-of-the-art results without syntactic information.

Main contributions:
1. A neural model for Japanese PAS analysis that captures multi-predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs.
2. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus without syntactic information.
3. The authors demonstrate that the model is effective for zero argument identification, one of the problematic issues in Japanese PAS analysis.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a novel approach to Japanese PAS analysis that captures multi-predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs.
2. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus without syntactic information.
3. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the model's performance and compare it with baseline models.
4. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the model and its components.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the model's output and how it captures constraints such as the uniqueness of arguments for a given predicate.
2. The authors could provide more discussion on the relative differences in the outputs of the single-sequence and multi-sequence models.
3. The paper could include results for semantic role labeling (SRL) to demonstrate the model's applicability to other tasks.
4. There are some minor language issues throughout the paper that could be improved.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide more analysis on the model's output and how it captures constraints such as the uniqueness of arguments for a given predicate?
2. How do the single-sequence and multi-sequence models differ in their outputs, and what contexts do you observe differences between the two models?
3. Have you considered applying the model to other tasks such as semantic role labeling (SRL), and if so, what results have you obtained?
4. Can you provide more discussion on the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns in the model, and how it impacts the model's ability to capture zero pronouns?
