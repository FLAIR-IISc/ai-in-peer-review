This paper proposes new prediction models for the Japanese Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) task by adapting the English state-of-the-art model of Zhou and Xu (2015). The authors also extend the model by applying the framework of Grid Recurrent Neural Networks (Grid-RNNs) to handle the interactions between the arguments of multiple predicates. The evaluation is performed on a well-known benchmark dataset in Japanese SRL, and the proposed model achieves significantly better performance than the current state-of-the-art system.

The paper is well-structured and well-motivated, providing a clear introduction to the Japanese SRL task and the limitations of existing models. The authors effectively explain the need for a new approach and how their proposed model addresses these limitations.

The proposed model demonstrates an improvement in accuracy compared to the current state-of-the-art system. Furthermore, the model using Grid-RNNs achieves slightly better performance than the proposed single-sequential model, mainly due to the improvement in detecting zero arguments, which is a primary focus of this paper. This is a notable contribution, as zero arguments are a challenging aspect of Japanese SRL.

However, to the best of my understanding, the main contribution of this paper is an extension of the single-sequential model to a multi-sequential model. While this extension is valuable, the impact of predicate interactions is somewhat smaller compared to Ouchi et al. (2015). Additionally, there is previous work by Shibata et al. (2016) that extends Ouchi et al.'s (2015) model using neural network modeling. I would be interested in seeing a comparison between these approaches to better understand the novelty and significance of the proposed model.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the Japanese SRL task, and the proposed model demonstrates promising results. With some further clarification on the comparison with existing approaches, this paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
