This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach for predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis in Japanese, utilizing Grid-RNNs and comparing its performance with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. The paper is solidly executed, targeting a well-established task in Japanese and achieving state-of-the-art results while presenting the task in a mostly accessible manner for non-experts in Japanese.

However, I felt that the authors could have further emphasized the complexity of the task, such as explaining the inherent ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate, as the NOM argument of the second predicate, as shown in Figure 1. Additionally, a more detailed description of how the task contrasts with semantic role labeling (SRL), particularly in terms of the ambiguity in zero pronouns, would be beneficial. I would have also appreciated some statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable, as this adds complexity to the task by requiring implicit distinction between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors.

One aspect that was unclear to me is the representation used for inter-sentential zero pronouns as arguments of a given predicate. Does the model mark such arguments as empty or simply omit them? My interpretation of the paper suggests the latter, which may lead to a defective representation, potentially impacting the model's ability to capture zero pronouns. A discussion on this point would be appreciated.

There are some constraints that appear to be unaccounted for in the model, which some ILP-based methods for SRL explicitly capture, such as: (1) a given predicate typically having only one argument of a specific type (especially NOM and ACC), and (2) a given argument generally filling only one argument slot for a given predicate. I would have liked to see an analysis of the model's output to determine how well it learns these constraints. Furthermore, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq models (with improvements for Multi-Seq primarily observed for NOM), a discussion on the relative differences in the outputs of the two models would be valuable. For instance, are the outputs largely identical or significantly different but similar in aggregate? In what contexts do the authors observe differences between the two models? Such analysis would provide insight into the models' internals and elevate the paper from solid to strong.

The presentation of the paper is good, with the figures effectively aiding understanding of the model. However, there are some minor language issues: l19: "the error propagation" should be "error propagation"; l190: "an solution" should be "a solution"; l264 (and Figure 2): "a bread" should be "bread"; l351: "the independence" should be "independence"; l512: "the good" should be "good"; l531: "from their model" should be "of their model"; l637: "significent" should be "significance"; l638: "both of" should be "both". Additionally, the authors should ensure consistent casing in their references (e.g., "Japanese", "LSTM", "CoNLL", "ILP").
