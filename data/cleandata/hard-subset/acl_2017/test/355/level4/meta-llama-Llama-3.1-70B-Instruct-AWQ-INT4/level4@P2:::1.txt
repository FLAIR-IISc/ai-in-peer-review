**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information of a sentence. The model uses grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNN) to capture interactions between multiple predicates without relying on syntactic information predicted by parsers. The authors evaluate their model on the NAIST Text Corpus and demonstrate that it exceeds the accuracy of the state-of-the-art Japanese PAS analyzer.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Effective modeling of multi-predicate interactions**: The authors propose a neural model that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs, which leads to improved performance in Japanese PAS analysis.
2. **State-of-the-art results without syntactic information**: The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus without relying on syntactic information predicted by parsers.
3. **Improved performance on dropped arguments**: The authors show that their multi-sequence model, which takes all predicates in the sentence into account, achieves the best performance for dropped arguments (zero pronouns), which are prevalent in Japanese and especially challenging with respect to PAS.

**Strengths**

* The paper presents a sophisticated application of Grid-type Recurrent Neural Nets to the task of determining predicate-argument structures (PAS) in Japanese.
* The approach does not use any explicit syntactic structure and outperforms the current SOA systems that do include syntactic structure.
* The authors give a clear and detailed description of the implementation and of the results.
* The paper pays close attention to the performance on dropped arguments, zero pronouns, which are prevalent in Japanese and especially challenging with respect to PAS.

**Weaknesses**

* Minor typos and formatting issues (e.g., boldface in Table 2).
* Some sentences could be rephrased for improved clarity and grammar.

**Questions to Authors**

* How do you handle out-of-vocabulary words in the input sentence?
