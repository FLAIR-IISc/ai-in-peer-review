This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis using Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks (Grid-RNNs), comparing it with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, achieving significant improvements over baseline models, particularly in the identification of zero arguments.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper addresses the task of Japanese PAS analysis, which involves identifying semantic units of a sentence, such as who did what to whom. The authors propose a neural modeling approach using Grid-RNNs, which captures multi-predicate interactions and improves performance on zero argument identification. The approach is compared with a single-sequence RNN model, and the results show that the Grid-RNN model achieves better performance, especially on zero arguments.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a neural modeling approach using Grid-RNNs for Japanese PAS analysis, which captures multi-predicate interactions and improves performance on zero argument identification.
2. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, achieving significant improvements over baseline models, particularly in the identification of zero arguments.
3. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the performance of the Grid-RNN model and the single-sequence RNN model, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to Japanese PAS analysis using Grid-RNNs, which captures multi-predicate interactions and improves performance on zero argument identification.
2. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the performance of the Grid-RNN model and the single-sequence RNN model, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
3. The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, achieving significant improvements over baseline models, particularly in the identification of zero arguments.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the complexity of the task, including the inherent ambiguity between NOM and ACC arguments and the contrast with SRL.
2. The authors could provide more analysis of the output of the model to see how well it learns constraints such as the uniqueness of arguments and argument slots.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison of the relative differences in the outputs of the two models, including an analysis of the contexts in which differences are observed.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns in the model? Is there an explicit representation of the fact that there is a zero pronoun, or is it simply not marked?
2. How do the authors plan to address the issue of inter-sentential zero anaphora resolution in future work?
3. Can you provide more analysis of the output of the model to see how well it learns constraints such as the uniqueness of arguments and argument slots?
