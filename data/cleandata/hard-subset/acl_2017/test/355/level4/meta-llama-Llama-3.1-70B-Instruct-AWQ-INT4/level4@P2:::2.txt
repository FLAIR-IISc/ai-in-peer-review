This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, extending the state-of-the-art English semantic role labeling (SRL) model of (Zhou and Xu, 2015) to handle multi-predicate interactions. The authors introduce a grid-type recurrent neural network (Grid-RNN) to capture these interactions, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper presents a neural model for Japanese PAS analysis, which automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information of a sentence. The model uses a Grid-RNN to capture the interactions between all predicates in a sentence, without relying on syntactic information predicted by parsers. The authors evaluate their model on the NAIST Text Corpus and demonstrate that it outperforms the state-of-the-art Japanese PAS analyzer.

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. A neural model that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis without relying on syntactic information.
2. The introduction of a Grid-RNN to handle the interactions between all predicates in a sentence.
3. State-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, outperforming the current state-of-the-art Japanese PAS analyzer.

**Strengths**

1. The paper is well-structured and well-motivated, providing a clear overview of the problem and the proposed solution.
2. The proposed model obtains an improvement in accuracy compared to the current state-of-the-art system.
3. The model using Grid-RNNs achieves a slightly better performance than the proposed single-sequential model, mainly due to the improvement on the detection of zero arguments.

**Weaknesses**

1. The impact of predicate interactions, while significant, may not be as substantial as that of (Ouchi et al., 2015).
2. There is a previous work (Shibata et al., 2016) that extends the (Ouchi et al., 2015)'s model with neural network modeling, and it would be beneficial to compare the proposed model with this work.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison between your model and the previous work of (Shibata et al., 2016), which also extends the (Ouchi et al., 2015)'s model with neural network modeling?
2. How do you plan to exploit large-scale unlabeled data to learn the neural models, as mentioned in the future work section?
