This paper proposes new prediction models for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis by adopting the English state-of-the-art model of (Zhou and Xu, 2015). The authors also extend the model by applying the framework of Grid-RNNs to handle the interactions between the arguments of multiple predicates. The evaluation is performed on the well-known benchmark dataset in Japanese PAS analysis, and the proposed model obtains a significantly better performance than the current state-of-the-art system.

Summary of the paper:
The paper presents a neural sequence model that automatically induces effective feature representations from word sequence information of a sentence for Japanese PAS analysis. The proposed model consists of two components: a single-sequence model and a multi-sequence model. The single-sequence model exploits stacked bidirectional RNNs to capture context-sensitive representations from a sequence of argument candidates of a predicate. The multi-sequence model extends the single-sequence model by applying the framework of Grid-RNNs to capture predicate-sensitive representations from multiple sequences of argument candidates of all predicates in a sentence.

Main contributions:
The main contributions of this work are: (1) the proposal of a neural sequence model that automatically induces effective feature representations from word sequence information of a sentence for Japanese PAS analysis, and (2) the extension of the single-sequence model to the multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to handle the interactions between the arguments of multiple predicates.

Strengths:
The paper is well-structured and well-motivated. The proposed model obtains an improvement in accuracy compared with the current state-of-the-art system. Also, the model using Grid-RNNs achieves a slightly better performance than that of the proposed single-sequential model, mainly due to the improvement on the detection of zero arguments, which is the focus of this paper.

Weaknesses:
To the best of my understanding, the main contribution of this paper is an extension of the single-sequential model to the multi-sequential model. The impact of predicate interactions is a bit smaller than that of (Ouchi et al., 2015). There is a previous work (Shibata et al., 2016) that extends the (Ouchi et al., 2015)'s model with neural network modeling. I am curious about the comparison between them.

Questions to authors:
1. Can you provide more details on the comparison between the proposed model and the previous work (Shibata et al., 2016) that extends the (Ouchi et al., 2015)'s model with neural network modeling?
2. How do you plan to explore effective methods for exploiting large-scale unlabeled data to learn the neural models in future work?
3. Can you provide more insights on the impact of predicate interactions on the performance of the proposed model, and how it compares to the impact of predicate interactions in the previous work (Ouchi et al., 2015)?
