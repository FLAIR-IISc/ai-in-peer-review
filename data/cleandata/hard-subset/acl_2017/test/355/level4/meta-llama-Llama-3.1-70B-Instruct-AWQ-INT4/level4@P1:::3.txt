This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to PAS analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, which it compares with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. 

The paper's main contributions are:
1. A neural model using Grid-RNNs for Japanese PAS analysis that achieves state-of-the-art results without syntactic information.
2. A comparison between the proposed multi-sequence model and a single-sequence model, demonstrating the effectiveness of modeling multi-predicate interactions.
3. An evaluation of the proposed models on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, showing their ability to capture long-distance dependencies and improve performance on zero argument identification.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a well-executed approach to a well-established task in Japanese, achieving state-of-the-art results and demonstrating the effectiveness of neural models for PAS analysis.
2. The authors provide a clear comparison between the proposed multi-sequence model and a single-sequence model, highlighting the benefits of modeling multi-predicate interactions.
3. The evaluation on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 is thorough, and the authors provide detailed results and analysis, including a comparison with baseline models.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the complexity of the task, including the inherent ambiguity in Japanese PAS analysis and the challenges of zero pronoun resolution.
2. The authors do not provide explicit representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns, which may impact the model's ability to capture zero pronouns.
3. The paper lacks analysis of the output of the model, including how well it learns constraints such as the uniqueness of arguments and argument slots.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide more details on how you represent inter-sentential zero pronouns in your model?
2. How do you plan to address the lack of explicit representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns in future work?
3. Can you provide more analysis of the output of the model, including how well it learns constraints such as the uniqueness of arguments and argument slots?
