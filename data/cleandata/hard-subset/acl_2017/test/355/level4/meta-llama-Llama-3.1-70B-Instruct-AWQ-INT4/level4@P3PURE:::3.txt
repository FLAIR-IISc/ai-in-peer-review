This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to PAS (Predicate Argument Structure) analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, which it compares with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. 

The paper is solidly executed, targeting a well-established task in Japanese and achieving state-of-the-art results. The authors present the task in a mostly accessible manner for non-experts in Japanese. However, I felt that the complexity of the task could have been emphasized further. For instance, the example in Figure 1 could have been used to discuss the inherent ambiguity between the nominative (NOM) and accusative (ACC) arguments of the first predicate, as well as the NOM argument of the second predicate. A clearer explanation of how the task contrasts with Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly with regards to the ambiguity in zero pronouns, would have been beneficial.

Additionally, I would have liked to see some statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable, as this further complicates the task by requiring the implicit distinction between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors. One aspect that was unclear to me was the representation used for inter-sentential zero pronouns as arguments of a given predicate. Does the model mark such arguments as empty or does it simply not mark them at all? My interpretation of the paper suggests the latter, which seems to be a slightly defective representation, potentially impacting the model's ability to capture zero pronouns. Some discussion on this point would have been appreciated.

There are certain constraints that do not appear to be captured by the model, which some ILP-based methods for SRL explicitly model. For example, a given predicate typically has only one argument of a given type (especially NOM and ACC), and a given argument usually fills only one argument slot for a given predicate. I would have liked to see an analysis of the model's output to determine how well it learned these constraints. Furthermore, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq (where the only significant improvement for Multi-Seq is for NOM), I would have appreciated a discussion on the relative differences in the outputs of the two models. Are they largely identical or very different but similar in aggregate? In what contexts do the authors observe differences between the two models? Such analysis would have provided insight into the models' internals and elevated the paper from solid to strong. This is the main area where I believe the paper could be improved, aside from including results for SRL, which would require significant additional work.

The presentation of the paper is good, with the figures aiding in understanding the model. There are some minor language issues: "the error propagation" should be "error propagation" (l19), "an solution" should be "a solution" (l190), "a bread" should be "bread" (l264 and Figure 2), "the independence" should be "independence" (l351), "the good" should be "good" (l512), "from their model" should be "of their model" (l531), "significent" should be "significance" (l637), and "both of" should be "both" (l638). Additionally, the authors should ensure consistent casing in their references (e.g., "Japanese", "LSTM", "CoNLL", "ILP").
