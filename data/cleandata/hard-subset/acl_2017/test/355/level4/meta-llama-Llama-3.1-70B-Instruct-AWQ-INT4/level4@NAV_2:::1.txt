**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a neural modeling approach for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, specifically targeting multi-predicate interactions. The authors introduce a Grid-type Recurrent Neural Network (Grid-RNN) model that automatically induces features sensitive to these interactions from word sequence information of a sentence. The model is evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus and achieves state-of-the-art results without relying on syntactic information.

**Main Contributions**
1. The authors propose a novel neural architecture using Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis.
2. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus without relying on syntactic information.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in identifying zero arguments, a challenging task in Japanese PAS analysis.

**Strengths**
1. The paper presents a sophisticated application of Grid-type Recurrent Neural Nets to the task of determining predicate-argument structures (PAS) in Japanese.
2. The approach does not use any explicit syntactic structure and outperforms current state-of-the-art systems that include syntactic structure.
3. The authors give a clear and detailed description of the implementation and results.
4. The paper pays close attention to the performance on dropped arguments, zero pronouns, which are prevalent in Japanese and especially challenging with respect to PAS.

**Weaknesses**
1. Minor typos and grammatical errors are present throughout the paper, which can be corrected to improve English fluency.
2. The use of boldface in Table 2 is unclear and may benefit from additional explanation.
3. The paper could benefit from additional illustrations or examples to support the explanation of certain concepts, such as the PRED feature.

**Questions to Authors (Optional)**
1. Can you provide additional information on the hyperparameter tuning process for the Grid-RNN model?
2. How do the authors plan to extend this work to other languages or tasks that involve multi-predicate interactions?
3. Are there any plans to release the source code for the Grid-RNN model to facilitate reproducibility and further research?
