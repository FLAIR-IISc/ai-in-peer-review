This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, extending the state-of-the-art English semantic role labeling (SRL) model of Zhou and Xu (2015) to handle multi-predicate interactions using Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks (Grid-RNNs). The authors evaluate their model on the NAIST Text Corpus, achieving a significantly better performance than the current state-of-the-art system.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes two neural models for Japanese PAS analysis: a single-sequence model and a multi-sequence model. The single-sequence model uses stacked bidirectional RNNs to estimate arguments for each predicate independently, while the multi-sequence model uses Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence. The authors claim that their models achieve state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus without using syntactic information.

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. The proposal of a neural model for Japanese PAS analysis that can capture multi-predicate interactions using Grid-RNNs.
2. The achievement of state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus without using syntactic information.
3. The demonstration of the effectiveness of the Grid-RNN architecture in capturing long-distance dependencies and improving the accuracy of zero argument identification.

**Strengths**

1. The paper is well-structured and well-motivated, providing a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed solution.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their models on the NAIST Text Corpus, achieving a significantly better performance than the current state-of-the-art system.
3. The use of Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions is a novel contribution that improves the accuracy of zero argument identification.

**Weaknesses**

1. The impact of predicate interactions in the proposed model is not significantly different from that of Ouchi et al. (2015), which also captures multi-predicate interactions.
2. There is a previous work (Shibata et al., 2016) that extends the Ouchi et al. (2015) model with neural network modeling, and a comparison between the two models is not provided.
3. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by their models, which could provide insights into the limitations of the approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison between your model and Shibata et al. (2016), including an evaluation of the two models on the same dataset?
2. How do you plan to extend your model to handle inter-sentential arguments, which are not considered in this paper?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by your models, including an evaluation of the types of errors that are most common and the factors that contribute to them?
