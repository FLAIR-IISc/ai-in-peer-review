This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, leveraging Grid-type Recurrent Neural Networks (Grid-RNNs) to capture multi-predicate interactions. The authors compare their approach to a conventional single-sequence RNN method and achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus.

The paper is well-executed, targeting a well-established task in Japanese and presenting the task in a mostly accessible manner for non-experts. However, the authors could have further emphasized the complexity of the task, such as the inherent ambiguity between NOM and ACC arguments in Figure 1, and better described how PAS analysis contrasts with Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly regarding zero pronouns.

The authors should provide statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable, as this further complicates the task. Additionally, it is unclear how the authors represent inter-sentential zero pronouns for a given predicate's argument. Is there simply no marking of that argument, or is it marked as an empty argument? This representation may impact the model's ability to capture zero pronouns.

The model does not seem to capture certain constraints, such as a given predicate typically having only one argument of a given type (especially NOM and ACC), and a given argument generally filling only one argument slot for a given predicate. The authors should analyze the model's output to assess its ability to learn these constraints.

The results in Table 3 show a mix of numbers between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq, with significant improvement for Multi-Seq only in NOM. The authors should discuss the relative differences in the outputs of the two models, including whether they are largely identical or very different but similar in aggregate. Analyzing the contexts where differences between the two models are observed would provide valuable insights into the models' internals.

The presentation of the paper is good, with helpful figures that aid understanding of the model. However, there are some minor language issues, such as "the error propagation" instead of "error propagation" (line 19), and inconsistent casing in the references.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors propose a joint neural modeling approach to Japanese PAS analysis using Grid-RNNs, which captures multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information.
2. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, outperforming baseline models in various metrics.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model in capturing long-distance dependencies and zero pronouns, which are challenging aspects of Japanese PAS analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper presents a well-executed and innovative approach to Japanese PAS analysis, leveraging Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model, comparing it to baseline models and analyzing its performance on various metrics.
3. The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with helpful figures that aid understanding of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The authors could have further emphasized the complexity of the task and provided more context for non-experts.
2. The model does not seem to capture certain constraints, such as argument uniqueness and slot filling, which are important aspects of PAS analysis.
3. The authors should provide more analysis of the model's output, including its ability to learn constraints and capture zero pronouns.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more context on the complexity of Japanese PAS analysis and how it contrasts with SRL?
2. How do you represent inter-sentential zero pronouns for a given predicate's argument, and what impact does this have on the model's ability to capture zero pronouns?
3. Can you analyze the model's output to assess its ability to learn constraints, such as argument uniqueness and slot filling?
