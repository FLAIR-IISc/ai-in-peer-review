This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach for PAS (Predicate-Argument Structure) analysis in Japanese, leveraging Grid-RNNs, and compares it with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. The paper is well-executed, tackling a well-established task in Japanese and achieving state-of-the-art results while presenting the task in an accessible manner for non-Japanese speakers.

However, I felt that the authors could have further highlighted the complexity of the task, such as explaining the inherent ambiguity between the NOM and ACC arguments of the first predicate and the NOM argument of the second predicate, as shown in Figure 1. A more detailed comparison with Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) would also be beneficial, particularly in terms of the ambiguity in zero pronouns. Additionally, providing statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable would be helpful, as this adds complexity to the task.

One aspect that was unclear to me was the representation used for inter-sentential zero pronouns as arguments of a given predicate. Does the model mark such arguments as empty or not at all? My interpretation is that the model does not explicitly represent the presence of a zero pronoun, which may impact its ability to capture zero pronouns. A discussion on this representation choice would be appreciated.

There are certain constraints that are not captured by the model, such as the fact that a given predicate typically has only one argument of a given type (especially NOM and ACC), and a given argument generally fills only one argument slot for a given predicate. These constraints are explicitly modeled by some ILP-based methods for SRL. I would have liked to see an analysis of the model's output to determine how well it learns these constraints.

Furthermore, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq (with improvements for Multi-Seq mainly observed for NOM), a discussion on the relative differences in the outputs of the two models would be beneficial. Are the outputs largely identical, or are they different but similar in aggregate? In what contexts do the authors observe differences between the two models? Such analysis would provide insight into the models' internals and strengthen the paper.

The presentation of the paper is clear, with the figures effectively aiding understanding of the model. However, there are some minor language issues: "the error propagation" (l19) should be "error propagation", "an solution" (l190) should be "a solution", "a bread" (l264 and Figure 2) should be "bread", "the independence" (l351) should be "independence", "the good" (l512) should be "good", "from their model" (l531) should be "of their model", "significent" (l637) should be "significance", and "both of" (l638) should be "both". Additionally, the authors should ensure consistent casing in their references (e.g., "Japanese", "LSTM", "CoNLL", "ILP").
