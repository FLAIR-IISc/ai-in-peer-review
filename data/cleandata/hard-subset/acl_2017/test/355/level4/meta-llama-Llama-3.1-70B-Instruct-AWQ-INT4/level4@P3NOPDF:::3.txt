This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to PAS (Predicate Argument Structure) analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, which it compares with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. The authors present a solidly-executed paper that targets a well-established task in Japanese and achieves state-of-the-art results. The presentation of the task is mostly accessible to readers not familiar with Japanese.

However, to further enhance the paper, the authors could have emphasized the complexity of the task. For instance, the example in Figure 1 highlights the inherent ambiguity between the NOM (nominative) and ACC (accusative) arguments of the first predicate, as well as the NOM argument of the second predicate. A more detailed explanation of how the task contrasts with Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) would also be beneficial, particularly in terms of the ambiguity in zero pronouns. Additionally, providing statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable would further complicate the task, as it requires implicitly distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors.

One aspect that requires clarification is the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns for the argument of a given predicate. The paper seems to suggest that there is no explicit marking of that argument, which could potentially impact the model's ability to capture zero pronouns. A discussion on this representation would be appreciated.

The authors could also explore constraints that are not captured in the model, such as the fact that a given predicate typically has only one argument of a given type (e.g., NOM and ACC), and a given argument generally only fills one argument slot for a given predicate. An analysis of the model's output to examine how well it learns these constraints would be valuable. Furthermore, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq, a discussion on the relative differences in the outputs of the two models would provide insight into the models' internals. Specifically, the authors could investigate whether the models produce largely identical outputs or significantly different outputs with similar aggregate performance. Identifying the contexts in which differences between the two models are observed would strengthen the paper.

The presentation of the paper is good, with the figures effectively aiding understanding of the model. However, there are some minor language issues, including: "the error propagation" (line 19) should be "error propagation"; "an solution" (line 190) should be "a solution"; "a bread" (line 264 and Figure 2) should be "bread"; "the independence" (line 351) should be "independence"; "the good" (line 512) should be "good"; "from their model" (line 531) should be "of their model"; "significent" (line 637) should be "significance"; and "both of" (line 638) should be "both". Additionally, the authors should ensure consistent casing in the references (e.g., "Japanese", "LSTM", "CoNLL", "ILP").
