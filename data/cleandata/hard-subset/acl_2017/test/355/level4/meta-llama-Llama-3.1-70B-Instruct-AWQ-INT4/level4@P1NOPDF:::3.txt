This paper proposes a joint neural modeling approach to PAS (Predicate-Argument Structure) analysis in Japanese, based on Grid-RNNs, which it compares with a conventional single-sequence RNN approach. The paper is well-executed, targeting a well-established task in Japanese and achieving state-of-the-art results while presenting the task in a mostly accessible manner for non-experts in Japanese.

However, I felt that the authors could have further highlighted the complexity of the task. For instance, discussing the inherent ambiguity between the nominative (NOM) and accusative (ACC) arguments of the first predicate, as well as the NOM argument of the second predicate, using the example in Figure 1, would have been beneficial. Additionally, a clearer description of how the task differs from Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), particularly in regards to the ambiguity in zero pronouns, would have been appreciated.

I would also have liked to see some statistics on the proportion of zero pronouns that are intra-sententially resolvable, as this further complicates the task by requiring implicit distinction between intra- and inter-sentential zero anaphors. One aspect that was unclear to me was the representation used for inter-sentential zero pronouns as arguments of a given predicate. Does the model use no marking for that argument or mark it as an empty argument? My interpretation of the paper suggests that it is the former, which means there is no explicit representation of the zero pronoun. This seems like a slightly defective representation, potentially impacting the model's ability to capture zero pronouns. Some discussion on this point would have been appreciated.

There are some constraints that do not appear to be captured in the model, which some ILP-based methods for SRL explicitly model. For example, a given predicate typically has only one argument of a given type (especially NOM and ACC), and a given argument usually fills only one argument slot for a given predicate. I would have liked to see an analysis of the model's output to determine how well it learned these constraints. Furthermore, given the mixed results in Table 3 between Single-Seq and Multi-Seq (where Multi-Seq only shows improvement for NOM), I would have liked to see a discussion on the relative differences in the outputs of the two models. Are they largely identical or very different but similar in aggregate? In what contexts do the authors observe differences between the two models? Some analysis to shed light on the models' internals would have elevated the paper from solid to strong and is the main area where I believe the paper could be improved (aside from including results for SRL, which would require significant additional work).

The presentation of the paper is good, with the figures aiding in understanding the model. There were some minor language issues, but nothing major.
