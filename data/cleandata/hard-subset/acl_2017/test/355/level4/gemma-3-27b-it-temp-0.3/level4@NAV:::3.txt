This paper introduces a joint neural network approach, utilizing Grid-RNNs, for PAS (Predicate-Argument Structure) analysis in Japanese. The method is rigorously evaluated and compared to a conventional single-sequence RNN, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on this established task. The paper is generally well-written and presents the task in a manner accessible to researchers unfamiliar with Japanese linguistics.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the inherent complexities of the PAS task. For instance, elaborating on the ambiguity present in examples like the one in Figure 1 – specifically, the potential for the nominal argument of the first predicate to also function as the nominal argument of the second – would be valuable. A clearer contrast between this task and Semantic Role Labeling (SRL), focusing on the challenges posed by zero pronouns, would also strengthen the introduction. Providing statistics regarding the proportion of intra-sententially resolvable zero pronouns would further highlight the task’s difficulty, as it necessitates distinguishing between intra- and inter-sentential anaphora.  Furthermore, clarification is needed regarding the representation of inter-sentential zero pronouns: are they explicitly marked as empty arguments, or simply omitted? The current approach appears to omit them, which may be a limiting factor in the model’s ability to effectively capture zero pronoun information, and a discussion of this design choice would be appreciated.

The model does not appear to explicitly capture certain constraints commonly leveraged in SRL, such as the typical one-to-one relationship between predicates and arguments of a given type (e.g., nominative and accusative) and the expectation that an argument generally occupies only one slot per predicate. Analyzing the model’s output to assess its learning of these constraints would be insightful.  The results in Table 3 reveal improvements primarily for nominative argument identification with the multi-sequence model. A more detailed comparison of the outputs of the single-sequence and multi-sequence models – examining their similarities, differences, and the contexts in which discrepancies arise – would provide valuable insight into the internal workings of each model. Such analysis would elevate the paper from solid to strong and represents the primary area for improvement. While including results for SRL would be a significant addition, it would require substantial further work.

The paper’s presentation is generally strong, with helpful figures that clarify the model. There are a few minor linguistic issues: line 19 should read “error propagation,” line 190 should read “a solution,” line 264 (and Figure 2) should read “bread,” line 351 should read “independence,” line 512 should read “good,” line 531 should read “of their model,” line 637 should read “significance,” and line 638 should read “both.”  Additionally, please ensure consistent capitalization in the references (e.g., “Japanese,” “LSTM,” “CONLL,” “ILP”).
