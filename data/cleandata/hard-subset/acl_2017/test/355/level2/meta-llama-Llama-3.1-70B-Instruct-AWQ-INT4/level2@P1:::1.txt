This paper presents a neural modeling approach for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, which is a basic semantic analysis task. The authors propose two models: a single-sequence model and a multi-sequence model. The single-sequence model uses stacked bidirectional RNNs to induce context-sensitive representations from a sequence of argument candidates of a predicate. The multi-sequence model extends the single-sequence model by using Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions between all predicates in a sentence.

The authors claim the following contributions:
1. The single-sequence model, which can induce context-sensitive representations from a sequence of argument candidates of a predicate without syntactic information.
2. The multi-sequence model, which can capture multi-predicate interactions between all predicates in a sentence using Grid-RNNs.
3. The state-of-the-art result on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without syntactic information.

Strengths:
1. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the task and the proposed models, including a detailed description of the single-sequence and multi-sequence models.
2. The experimental results show that the proposed models outperform the baseline models in all metrics, and the multi-sequence model achieves the state-of-the-art result on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5.
3. The authors provide a detailed comparison of the results per case role, which shows that the proposed models can robustly learn the dative case, which appears infrequently compared to the other two case arguments.

Weaknesses:
1. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of why they chose to use Grid-RNNs for the multi-sequence model. While they mention that Grid-RNNs can capture multi-predicate interactions, they do not provide a detailed analysis of how this is achieved.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed models. While they mention that the models can learn contextual information effective for PAS analysis from a word sequence of the sentence, they do not discuss the potential limitations of this approach.
3. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed models can be applied to other tasks, such as semantic role labeling (SRL). While they mention that the models can be regarded as an extension from the model of Zhou and Xu (2015), they do not provide a detailed analysis of how this can be achieved.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why you chose to use Grid-RNNs for the multi-sequence model? How do Grid-RNNs capture multi-predicate interactions, and what are the advantages of using this approach?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations of the proposed models? What are the potential limitations of learning contextual information effective for PAS analysis from a word sequence of the sentence, and how can these limitations be addressed?
3. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the proposed models can be applied to other tasks, such as SRL? How can the models be modified to capture the nuances of SRL, and what are the potential advantages of using this approach?
