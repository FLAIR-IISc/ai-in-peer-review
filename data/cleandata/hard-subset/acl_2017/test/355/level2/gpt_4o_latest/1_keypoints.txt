- Contribution: Proposes a Grid-RNN-based approach for Japanese PAS analysis that captures multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences without syntactic input.  
- Contribution: Introduces a single-sequence Bi-RNN model for context-sensitive representations from word sequences.  
- Contribution: Presents a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model that improves zero-argument identification by modeling multi-predicate interactions.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus, especially for zero arguments.  
- Strength: Novelty — Grid-RNNs offer a significant innovation over parser-dependent methods and are effective for zero argument identification.  
- Strength: Empirical validation — consistent, statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including Ouchi et al. (2015).  
- Strength: Practical impact — high performance without syntactic parsers reduces error propagation and increases robustness/applicability.  
- Strength: Reproducibility — implementation details and source code are provided.  
- Weakness: Limited evaluation — focuses only on intra-sentential (Dep and Zero) arguments and omits inter-sentential (Inter-Zero) cases.  
- Weakness: Missing comparisons — no benchmarks against models that use external resources, limiting competitiveness assessment.  
- Weakness: Interpretability — lacks detailed analysis, visualizations, or qualitative examples showing how Grid-RNN captures interactions.  
- Weakness: Generalization untested — claims about multilingual SRL applicability are not empirically validated.  
- Question: How does performance change when incorporating external resources like pre-trained embeddings?  
- Question: Can the authors provide qualitative examples or visualizations to illustrate Grid-RNN multi-predicate modeling?  
- Question: Have the authors considered extending to inter-sentential arguments (Inter-Zero), and what challenges are expected?  
- Conclusion: Solid, syntactic-independent advancement with state-of-the-art results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address scope, interpretability, and generalization.
