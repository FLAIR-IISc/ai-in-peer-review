This paper proposes a neural model using Grid-RNNs to improve Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, aiming to overcome the error propagation issues inherent in traditional pipeline approaches reliant on syntactic parsing. The core idea is to directly model multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information, bypassing the need for accurate syntactic predictions. The authors claim to achieve state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus without using syntactic information.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that Grid-RNNs can effectively model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS analysis, leading to improved performance, particularly in identifying zero arguments (arguments omitted from the sentence). A secondary contribution is the publicly available source code, enabling reproducibility and further research. A third, more minor contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation on the NAIST corpus, providing a solid benchmark for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that the proposed Grid-RNN model outperforms both baseline models (Imamura et al., 2009 and Ouchi et al., 2015) and the single-sequence RNN baseline. The reported improvements in F-measure, particularly for zero arguments, are significant. This is a strong indication that the proposed approach is effective and addresses a key challenge in Japanese PAS analysis.
2. **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial issue in Japanese PAS – handling argument omission. The reliance on syntactic information in previous approaches makes them vulnerable to parsing errors. The proposed method directly addresses this limitation by learning interactions from the word sequence itself. The focus on zero argument identification is particularly well-motivated.
3. **Clear and Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental setup is well-defined, including detailed explanations of the dataset, learning parameters, and baseline models. The ablation study examining the effect of network depth and residual connections provides valuable insights into the model's behavior. The inclusion of results broken down by case role (Table 3) is also helpful.
4. **Well-Motivated Architecture:** The use of Grid-RNNs is appropriately justified as a mechanism for capturing multi-predicate interactions. The explanations of how the Grid-RNNs operate are clear and easy to follow. The inclusion of diagrams (Figures 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6) greatly enhances understanding of the proposed architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates improved performance, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still struggles and *why* would provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, are there specific types of zero anaphora that remain challenging?
2. **Lack of Comparison with More Recent Approaches:** Although the paper compares against strong baselines, the field of neural PAS has advanced since the publication of Ouchi et al. (2015). Including comparisons to more recent state-of-the-art models would strengthen the claim of novelty.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but lacks details on the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings. A more in-depth discussion of hyperparameter optimization and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Generalizability Discussion:** The paper focuses exclusively on Japanese. While justified by the task, a brief discussion of the potential for adapting the approach to other pro-drop languages or even different semantic analysis tasks would broaden the impact of the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common types of errors made by the model and discussing potential reasons for these errors?
2. Have you experimented with more recent architectures or pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) for this task? If so, how do they compare to the proposed Grid-RNN model?
3. What was the process of hyperparameter optimization? Were any automated hyperparameter tuning techniques employed, and if so, which ones?
4. Could you discuss the potential challenges and adaptations required to apply this approach to other pro-drop languages?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and appears to cover relevant prior work.
*   The code availability is a positive aspect of this submission.
*   The notation used throughout the paper is clear and consistent.
