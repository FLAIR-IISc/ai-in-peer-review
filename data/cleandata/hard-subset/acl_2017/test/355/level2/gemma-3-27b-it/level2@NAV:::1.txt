## Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically focusing on mitigating error propagation caused by reliance on syntactic parsers. The authors propose a novel model utilizing Grid-RNNs to automatically induce features capturing multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, bypassing the need for explicit syntactic information. The core idea is sound and tackles a key limitation of previous approaches.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that a Grid-RNN based model can achieve state-of-the-art accuracy on the NAIST Text Corpus for Japanese PAS analysis *without* relying on syntactic information. A secondary contribution is the empirical evidence supporting the benefit of explicitly modeling multi-predicate interactions using the proposed grid architecture, particularly for zero-argument resolution. A third, more minor contribution, is a thorough experimental evaluation and comparison with existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The model demonstrably outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods on the NAIST corpus, a significant achievement. The reported improvements, especially for zero arguments, are noteworthy.
* **Novelty in Architecture:** The application of Grid-RNNs to PAS analysis, specifically for capturing multi-predicate interactions, is a novel approach. While Grid-RNNs themselves are not new, their adaptation to this task is creative and effective.
* **Addressing a Key Problem:** The paper directly addresses a core issue in Japanese PAS – the dependence on potentially noisy syntactic parsing – and provides a viable alternative.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a detailed experimental setup, including ablation studies (network depth, residual connections) and comparison to relevant baselines.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed models and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The evaluation is solely focused on the NAIST Text Corpus. While a standard benchmark, it would be beneficial to see performance on other datasets to assess the generalizability of the proposed approach.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but lacks details regarding the search space and the rationale behind specific choices. This makes reproducibility more challenging.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of *where* the model succeeds and fails. Identifying specific error patterns could provide valuable insights for future work.
* **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** The computational complexity of Grid-RNNs, compared to simpler RNN architectures, is not discussed. This is an important consideration for practical applications.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the criteria used for selection?
2.  Have you explored the effect of different embedding sizes or pre-trained word embeddings on performance?
3.  Can you provide a qualitative error analysis, highlighting common types of errors made by the model?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The weaknesses are relatively minor and addressable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
