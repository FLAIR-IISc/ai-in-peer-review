This paper introduces a neural model using grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs) for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis. The motivation is to address the error propagation inherent in traditional pipeline approaches that rely on syntactic parsing. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that a model relying solely on word sequence information, leveraging Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions, achieves state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus. A secondary contribution is the empirical analysis showing the benefit of grid-type architectures for modeling interactions between predicates, particularly for resolving zero anaphora. Finally, the ablation studies showcasing the impact of network depth and residual connections provide insights into model design choices.

Strengths:

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper convincingly demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark (NAIST Text Corpus) without relying on syntactic information.  This is a significant achievement, as previous methods heavily depended on parser output, making them susceptible to cascading errors. The reported improvements, particularly for zero anaphora resolution, are noteworthy.
2. **Effective Use of Grid-RNNs:**  The adoption of Grid-RNNs appears to be a crucial element of the success. The results clearly indicate that modeling multi-predicate interactions is beneficial, and the grid architecture provides a suitable mechanism for capturing these interactions. The ablation study specifically highlights this advantage.
3. **Well-Designed Experiments and Ablation Studies:**  The experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons to strong baselines. The ablation studies exploring network depth and residual connections are particularly valuable, offering insights into the model's behavior and informing best practices for similar architectures.
4. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the challenges of Japanese PAS analysis, particularly the issue of zero anaphora, and motivates the proposed approach as a solution to the limitations of existing methods. 

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall performance, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *where* the model still struggles, beyond simply noting improvement in zero anaphora, could suggest avenues for further research. Are there specific types of predicate-argument combinations where performance remains low?
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST Text Corpus, while standard, is relatively small.  The generalizability of the findings to other datasets or domains is not fully established. Mentioning this limitation and considering experiments on larger datasets in future work would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters would be helpful. Understanding the impact of choices like embedding dimensions, learning rate, and batch size could provide valuable practical guidance.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Transformer-Based Models:** The work predates the widespread adoption of Transformers. Including a comparison, even if briefly, to a representative Transformer-based model for sequence tagging or SRL would help situate the contribution within the current landscape of NLP research.



Questions to Authors:

1. Could the authors provide examples of cases where the model still fails, particularly in zero anaphora resolution?  This would help understand the limitations of the current approach.
2. How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of window size (C) in the input layer?
3. Was any attention mechanism considered, either within the RNNs or between the Grid-RNN layers, and if so, why was it ultimately not adopted?

Additional Comments:

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The code availability is a positive aspect. The references are comprehensive. The discussion of related work is adequate.
