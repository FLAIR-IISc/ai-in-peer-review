**Summary of the paper and its contributions:**

This paper tackles the task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, a challenge complicated by the frequent omission of arguments (zero pronouns). The authors propose to improve performance by modeling the interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence, crucially doing so without relying on syntactic parsers to avoid error propagation. They introduce a single-sequence Bi-RNN model as a strong baseline and a novel multi-sequence model using a Grid-RNN architecture that jointly processes all predicates to explicitly capture their interdependencies.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  The novel and effective application of a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly model all predicate-argument structures in a sentence. This allows the model to learn complex interactions between predicates directly from the word sequence, leading to significant improvements in identifying difficult zero-arguments.
2.  A clear demonstration that a purely sequence-based neural model, without any syntactic features, can achieve state-of-the-art results on a standard Japanese PAS benchmark. This underscores the power of end-to-end learned representations over methods reliant on external parsing tools.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thorough Analysis:** The paper presents compelling results on the NAIST Text Corpus, significantly outperforming the previous state-of-the-art. The authors provide a robust evaluation, including significance tests and detailed performance breakdowns by argument type (Dep vs. Zero) and case role. This thorough analysis provides strong evidence for the model's effectiveness.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Architecture:** The motivation to remove dependency on syntactic parsers is well-founded, and the proposed Grid-RNN architecture is an elegant solution for learning multi-predicate interactions. The design directly addresses the core problem of capturing sentence-level context for all predicates simultaneously.
3.  **Clarity and High-Quality Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The model architectures are explained effectively with helpful diagrams, and the work is well-situated within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Discussion on Computational Cost:** The multi-sequence Grid-RNN model seems computationally more intensive than the single-sequence baseline, as it processes M sequences for M predicates. A brief discussion on the trade-offs in terms of computational complexity and training/inference time would be beneficial for assessing the model's practical applicability.
2.  **Lack of Feature Ablation:** The input features are relatively simple, inspired by prior work. While effective, the paper could be strengthened by an ablation study analyzing the contribution of each feature type (ARG, PRED, MARK). This would offer deeper insight into what information the model is leveraging most effectively.
