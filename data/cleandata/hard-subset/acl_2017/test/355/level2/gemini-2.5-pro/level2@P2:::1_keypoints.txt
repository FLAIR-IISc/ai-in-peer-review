- Paper proposes a sequence-based neural model for Japanese PAS that operates on words (stacked Bi-RNN baseline) and introduces a Multi-Sequence Grid-RNN to jointly model all predicates.  
- Main contribution: Grid-RNN jointly models predicate interactions, improving especially zero-argument identification.  
- Contribution: achieves new SOTA on NAIST without syntactic features, simplifying the pipeline and avoiding parser error propagation.  
- Contribution: principled and effective application of Grid-RNN for multi-predicate interactions with empirical gains over a strong single-predicate baseline.  
- Strength: strong empirical results — overall F1 improved from 79.23 to 81.42, with +3.03 F1 on zero arguments.  
- Strength: principled model design; direct Single-Sequence vs Multi-Sequence comparison shows significant improvement (p < 0.05).  
- Strength: thorough experimental evaluation including strong baselines, depth/residual analyses, and breakdowns by argument type and case role.  
- Weakness: unclear description of input features, specifically how the x_pred_t vector incorporates C-word predicate context into x_t.  
- Weakness: limited qualitative analysis — no case studies showing what multi-predicate interactions the Grid-RNN learns.  
- Weakness: technical novelty is incremental — uses existing components (Bi-GRUs, Grid-RNNs, residuals); novelty is mainly in application and system design.  
- Question: please clarify how x_pred_t is constructed and how the C context word embeddings are combined into x_t for each token.  
- Question: is the predicate order along the grid’s m-dimension linear by sentence position, and is performance sensitive to that ordering?  
- Question: why does modeling predicate interactions also improve Dep arguments that already have direct syntactic links to their predicate?
