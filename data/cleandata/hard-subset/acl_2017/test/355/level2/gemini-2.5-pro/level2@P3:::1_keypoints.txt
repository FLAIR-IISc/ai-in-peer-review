- Task: Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis focused on resolving difficult zero arguments using neural sequence models without syntactic features.  
- Models: Proposes a single-sequence model (stacked BiRNN per predicate) and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that models interactions between predicates.  
- Main contribution: Grid-RNN enables inter-predicate information flow and is the paper’s principal architectural novelty.  
- Empirical result: Both models outperform prior syntax-based methods on the NAIST Text Corpus; Grid-RNN achieves new state-of-the-art, especially on zero argument identification.  
- Contribution claim: Shows high PAS performance without syntactic parses, questioning the necessity of syntax for semantic analysis and simplifying the pipeline.  
- Strength: Strong empirical gains over a strong syntax-based joint model, with pronounced improvements on zero arguments and reduced parser error propagation.  
- Strength: Well-motivated, novel architecture that directly models multi-predicate dependencies rather than relying on reranking or engineered joint features.  
- Strength: Thorough experiments and analyses, including an informative single-sequence ablation, depth/residual studies, and breakdowns by argument type and case role.  
- Strength: Clear exposition, helpful diagrams, and commitment to releasing code for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: No qualitative analysis or example sentences illustrating specifically what interactions the Grid-RNN captures compared to the single-sequence model.  
- Weakness: Implicit assumption of linear predicate ordering in Grid-RNN, which may not align with true semantic/syntactic relations in complex sentences.  
- Weakness: Limited input feature set (word, predicate context, binary marker); missing discussion of other non-syntactic features (POS, character embeddings) that might help.  
- Question: Were alternative predicate orderings considered (e.g., syntactic dependency-based ordering), and how would ordering affect performance?  
- Question: Can the authors provide a concrete test-set example where multi-sequence connections enabled correct argument identification that single-sequence missed?
