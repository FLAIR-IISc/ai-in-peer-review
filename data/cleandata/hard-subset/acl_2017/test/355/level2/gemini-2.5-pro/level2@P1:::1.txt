### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, with a focus on resolving zero arguments, a particularly challenging problem in pro-drop languages. The authors propose a neural sequence modeling approach that avoids reliance on syntactic parsers, thereby circumventing the issue of error propagation from upstream tasks. The core idea is to model the interactions between multiple predicates within a sentence to better identify arguments. Two models are presented: a baseline "Single-Sequence" model using stacked bidirectional RNNs to analyze each predicate independently, and the main "Multi-Sequence" model which uses Grid-RNNs to jointly model all predicates in a sentence, allowing information to flow between their respective argument candidate sequences. Experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus show that both proposed models outperform the previous state-of-the-art, with the Multi-Sequence model achieving the best results, especially on the difficult task of identifying zero arguments.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel application of Grid-RNNs to create an end-to-end model for Japanese PAS that effectively captures interactions between multiple predicates without requiring any syntactic features. This provides a compelling alternative to traditional pipeline or joint models that rely on syntactic parsers.
2.  Achieving new state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark for Japanese PAS. The significant improvements, particularly for zero-argument identification, demonstrate the effectiveness of modeling sentence-level context and predicate interactions through deep recurrent architectures.

### Strengths

1.  **Effective and Well-Motivated Method:** The core idea of using a grid architecture to model interactions between parallel sequences (one for each predicate) is an elegant way to translate the linguistic intuition of joint modeling into a neural framework. It directly addresses the shortcomings of prior work that relied on brittle syntactic features. The empirical results strongly validate this design choice, as the Multi-Sequence model significantly outperforms the Single-Sequence baseline, demonstrating the value of modeling these interactions.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper presents a clear and convincing set of experiments. The proposed models outperform a strong state-of-the-art baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015) across all metrics, despite using much simpler input features (i.e., no syntactic information). This is a significant result. The analysis is thorough, breaking down performance by argument type (Dep vs. Zero) and by case role. This detailed breakdown highlights that the largest gains are made on the most difficult sub-tasks, such as identifying zero arguments and dative case arguments, which strengthens the paper's claims.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The model architectures are explained clearly, and the accompanying diagrams (especially Figure 6) are highly effective at illustrating the information flow in the Grid-RNN. The authors' decision to make their source code available is commendable and greatly benefits the community by ensuring the work is reproducible.

4.  **Demonstration of Deeper Architectures:** The paper successfully employs deep networks (up to 8 layers) and shows the benefit of using residual connections for this task. This provides useful insights into building effective deep models for structured prediction problems in NLP.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper would be more impactful with a qualitative analysis or a case study. The central claim is that the Grid-RNN "effectively captures the multi-predicate interactions," but this is only supported by aggregate F-scores. An example of a sentence where the Multi-Sequence model correctly identifies a zero argument that the Single-Sequence model misses would provide valuable insight into *what kind* of interactions the model is learning. This would make the contribution more concrete and intuitive.

2.  **Limited Discussion on Feature Space:** The paper's strength is achieving SOTA results without syntax. However, there is no discussion about the potential impact of other, simpler linguistic features (e.g., POS tags, which are generally more robust than full dependency parses). While not a requirement, a brief discussion or a simple experiment could help delineate the trade-offs more clearly and explore the ceiling of this architecture.

3.  **Potential Sensitivity to Predicate Ordering:** The Grid-RNN processes the M predicate sequences in a fixed order (m=1 to M), which appears to be based on their sequential position in the sentence. It is unclear if this ordering affects performance. Is the model robust to permutations of the predicate order? While processing in sentence order is a natural choice, a brief comment on this design decision and its potential implications would be welcome.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide an example from your test set that illustrates the benefit of the Multi-Sequence model? Specifically, a case where it correctly identifies a difficult (e.g., Zero) argument that the Single-Sequence model fails on, and if possible, offer an intuition as to what predicate interaction might have helped the model make the correct decision.

2.  The performance improvement on dative (DAT) arguments is particularly striking (e.g., from 38.39 F1 to ~65 F1 for Dep arguments). This is a much larger gain than for NOM or ACC. Do you have any insight or hypothesis as to why your RNN-based models are so much more effective for this specific case role compared to previous methods?

3.  Regarding the grid architecture, have you experimented with or considered the effect of the ordering of the predicate sequences (the m-dimension in the grid)? Does the model implicitly learn to be robust to this ordering, or is the natural sentence order of predicates crucial for its success?
