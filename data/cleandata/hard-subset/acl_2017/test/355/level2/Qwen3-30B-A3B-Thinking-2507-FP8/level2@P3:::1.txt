

This paper introduces a neural modeling approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that eliminates reliance on syntactic parsers by leveraging grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs) to capture multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. The primary contribution is a neural architecture that processes all predicates and their argument candidates in a sentence simultaneously, avoiding error propagation from syntactic dependencies. A secondary contribution is demonstrating that this approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 without any syntactic information, particularly improving zero-argument identification.

Strengths:  
1. The paper successfully addresses a critical limitation in existing Japanese PAS systems—dependency on error-prone parsers—by showing that neural models using raw word sequences can match or exceed the performance of pipeline-based approaches (Table 1). The 3.0-point F-measure improvement on zero arguments (47.12% vs. 44.09%) over Ouchi et al. (2015) is significant, as zero-arguments are the most challenging aspect of PAS due to absent syntactic dependencies.  
2. The ablation study (Table 2) provides strong evidence that the multi-sequence model’s grid-RNN architecture (not just deeper networks) is key to capturing multi-predicate interactions. The 0.27-point gain in overall F-measure (81.42% vs. 81.15%) for the multi-sequence model over the single-sequence variant, with statistically significant gains on zero arguments (p < 0.05), directly supports the core claim about interaction modeling.  
3. The experimental rigor is commendable: standard dataset splits, bootstrap significance testing, thorough hyperparameter tuning (e.g., residual connections, layer depth), and comparison against both pointwise and joint baselines. The performance on dative arguments (Table 3), which previously suffered due to annotation ambiguities, shows robust learning (64.38% vs. 38.39% for Ouchi et al.), indicating the model handles low-frequency cases effectively.

Weaknesses:  
1. The paper does not sufficiently justify the choice of Grid-RNNs over other sequence modeling approaches (e.g., attention mechanisms or standard RNNs with cross-predicate connections). While Grid-RNNs are cited from Kalchbrenner et al. (2016), the work does not empirically compare them to alternatives like Transformer-based models or simpler cross-sequence RNNs, making it unclear whether the grid structure is uniquely beneficial.  
2. The comparison to English SRL work (Zhou & Xu 2015) is superficial. The paper notes differences in dependency-based vs. constituent-based argument identification and multi-predicate handling but does not evaluate whether the proposed architecture would transfer to English SRL or explain why the Japanese-specific challenges (e.g., zero anaphora) necessitate the grid design. This weakens the broader applicability claim.  
3. The analysis of failure cases is limited. The paper reports strong overall results but does not investigate *why* certain zero-arguments remain misidentified (e.g., long-distance dependencies beyond the model’s capacity), which is critical for understanding the approach’s limitations and guiding future work.

Questions to Authors:  
1. How would the model’s performance change if the grid-RNN were replaced with a standard attention mechanism or cross-predicate RNN (e.g., feeding the output of one predicate’s RNN into another’s input)?  
2. The paper states the model achieves SOTA without external resources. Could the performance be further improved by incorporating unlabeled data (e.g., via semi-supervised learning), as suggested in the conclusion? If so, what would be the most promising approach for Japanese PAS?
