

This paper proposes a neural approach to Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that models multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information from parsers, addressing the error propagation issue in previous joint modeling approaches. The authors introduce two neural models: a single-sequence model that captures context within argument candidates for individual predicates, and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs to capture interactions across all predicates in a sentence.

The main contributions are: (1) a neural framework that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information alone, eliminating the need for syntactic parsers; (2) significant improvements in zero argument identification (a core challenge in Japanese PAS), with the multi-sequence model achieving 47.12% F1 (vs. 44.09% for the state-of-the-art Ouchi et al., 2015); and (3) empirical demonstration that word sequence information alone can achieve state-of-the-art performance without syntactic features.

Strengths include the clear identification of a key limitation in existing approaches (error propagation from parsers), the substantial improvement in zero argument identification (a particularly challenging aspect), and thorough experimental validation including ablation studies on network depth and residual connections. The results are statistically significant, with the multi-sequence model outperforming baselines in all metrics (81.42% vs 79.23% F1).

Weaknesses include insufficient comparison with Shibata et al. (2016), who also used neural networks for Japanese PAS but with syntactic features—this limits the understanding of whether their approach's advantage comes from the Grid-RNN architecture or the absence of syntactic features. The paper also lacks analysis of computational complexity and training time, which is important for practical adoption. Additionally, the explanation of Grid-RNN's novelty is somewhat superficial despite citing Graves and Schmidhuber (2009), making it difficult to assess the architectural contribution.

The paper is well-written and addresses a meaningful problem in Japanese NLP. To strengthen it, the authors should clarify the Grid-RNN's specific advantages over standard RNNs, provide a more comprehensive comparison with neural approaches that use syntactic features, and discuss computational efficiency. The work has clear potential for broader application in multilingual semantic analysis.
