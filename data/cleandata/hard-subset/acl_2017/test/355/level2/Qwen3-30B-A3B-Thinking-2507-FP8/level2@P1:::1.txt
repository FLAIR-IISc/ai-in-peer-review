

This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that do not rely on syntactic information from parsers, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches. The main contributions are: 1) a neural model that achieves state-of-the-art performance for Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic information, avoiding error propagation issues; 2) a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions, particularly beneficial for zero argument identification; and 3) demonstration that RNN-based models can learn contextual information from word sequences to handle the challenging zero argument problem.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a significant problem in Japanese PAS analysis: the reliance on syntactic information from parsers that causes error propagation. By showing that neural models can achieve SOTA results without this dependency, the authors make a meaningful contribution to the field. The multi-sequence model's 3.0-point improvement over the previous state-of-the-art for zero arguments is particularly compelling.

2. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured. The comparison with baseline models (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015) demonstrates clear improvements across all metrics, especially for zero arguments. The detailed analysis by case role (Table 3) provides valuable insights into the model's strengths.

3. The architecture is well-motivated and clearly explained. The Grid-RNN approach for modeling multi-predicate interactions is a novel application of this architecture to PAS analysis, and the residual connections effectively address training difficulties in deeper networks.

4. The paper makes a strong case for the value of neural approaches in Japanese PAS analysis. The results show that neural models can learn effective representations from word sequences alone, eliminating the need for syntactic features that previous approaches heavily relied upon.

5. The work is well-positioned within the related literature, with clear comparisons to previous approaches for Japanese PAS analysis and English SRL.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't adequately explain why Grid-RNNs are specifically advantageous for this task compared to other neural architectures that could model multi-predicate interactions (e.g., attention mechanisms or transformers). This is particularly important given the recent advances in attention-based models.

2. The comparison with other neural approaches for Japanese PAS is limited. The related work section mentions Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016), but doesn't provide a clear comparison of their neural architectures with the authors' approach. A more detailed discussion of how this work advances beyond previous neural approaches would strengthen the contribution.

3. The paper claims their approach is the first to achieve SOTA without syntactic information, but this isn't fully justified. The related work section notes that Shibata et al. (2016) used neural networks for score calculation but still relied on syntactic features. However, the paper doesn't clearly establish that no previous neural approach has completely eliminated syntactic dependency.

4. The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity or training time compared to previous approaches, which would be valuable for practitioners considering adoption of this method.

5. The paper mentions that their models are applicable to SRL, but doesn't provide evidence or discussion of how this might work for English or other languages with similar zero pronoun phenomena.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would you compare your Grid-RNN approach to using attention mechanisms or transformers for capturing multi-predicate interactions in this context? What specific advantages does Grid-RNN offer for this particular task?

2. Have you considered training your models on larger datasets or incorporating pre-trained language models, which might further improve performance, particularly for zero arguments?

3. The paper states that their model achieves SOTA without syntactic information, but the related work section mentions Shibata et al. (2016) used neural networks with syntactic features. Can you clarify whether any previous work has attempted to eliminate syntactic dependency entirely?

4. How does the computational complexity of your Grid-RNN model compare to previous approaches? Would this be a barrier to practical adoption in resource-constrained settings?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to Japanese PAS analysis by demonstrating that neural models can achieve SOTA results without relying on syntactic information. The results are compelling, particularly for zero argument identification, and the approach addresses a real problem in the field. With some clarification on the comparison to other neural architectures and a more detailed discussion of the novelty claims, this work would be even stronger. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
