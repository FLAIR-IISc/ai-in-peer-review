- Proposes neural sequence models for Japanese PAS that avoid parser-based syntactic input, achieving SOTA without syntactic features.  
- Introduces a multi-sequence Grid-RNN to model multi-predicate interactions, improving zero-argument identification by ~3.0 points.  
- Shows RNNs can learn contextual representations from raw word sequences to handle zero argument phenomena.

- Addresses the key problem of parser-error propagation by removing syntactic dependencies while improving performance.  
- Thorough, well-structured experimental evaluation with clear improvements over baselines, especially for zero arguments and case-role analyses.  
- Well-motivated architecture; Grid-RNN application is novel for PAS and residual connections aid deeper network training.  
- Strong case that neural models can replace syntactic features for Japanese PAS.  
- Good positioning within related work and comparisons to prior Japanese PAS and English SRL studies.

- Fails to justify why Grid-RNNs are superior to attention-based models or transformers for multi-predicate interactions.  
- Limited comparison to prior neural approaches (e.g., Shibata et al., Iida et al.); lacks clear differentiation from those architectures.  
- Claim of first SOTA without syntactic information is not fully substantiated against prior work.  
- Omits discussion of computational complexity and training time versus previous methods.  
- Asserts applicability to SRL/other languages but provides no empirical evidence or discussion for cross-lingual transfer.

- How does Grid-RNN compare empirically and theoretically to attention mechanisms or transformers for this task?  
- Have you tried larger datasets or pre-trained language models to further boost zero-argument performance?  
- Can you clarify whether any prior work fully eliminated syntactic dependency for Japanese PAS?  
- What is the computational complexity and training time of the Grid-RNN relative to previous approaches, and is it practical for resource-constrained settings?

- Overall recommendation: strong, well-written paper with meaningful contributions and a recommendation for acceptance, pending clarifications on novelty, architectural comparison, and complexity.
