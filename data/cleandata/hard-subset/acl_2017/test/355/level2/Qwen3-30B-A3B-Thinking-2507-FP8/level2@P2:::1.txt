

Summary: The paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, claiming to achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic parsers by modeling multi-predicate interactions via Grid-RNNs. Key contributions are: (1) A neural model using word sequences (not parsers) for PAS, and (2) A Grid-RNN architecture capturing multi-predicate interactions. The authors argue these address the critical zero-argument identification problem in Japanese.

Strengths:
1. **Strong empirical results on zero arguments**: The model achieves 47.12% F1 on zero arguments (vs. 44.09% for Ouchi et al. 2015), a 3-point improvement that directly addresses the paper's core problem. This is significant given zero arguments are notoriously hard due to lack of syntactic dependencies.
2. **Clear problem framing and ablation**: The paper effectively isolates the zero-argument challenge (Fig 1, Sec 2.2) and demonstrates Grid-RNN's necessity via ablation (Table 1: Multi-Seq outperforms Single-Seq by 0.27 F1, p<0.05), showing multi-predicate interactions specifically help zero arguments.
3. **Robust experimental design**: Standard NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 splits, thorough hyperparameter tuning (Table 2), and residual connections (improving F1 by ~1.0 point) demonstrate careful implementation.

Weaknesses:
1. **Lack of novelty in Grid-RNN**: The core architecture (Grid-RNN) is directly borrowed from Kalchbrenner et al. (2016), who introduced "Grid LSTM" for image captioning. The paper cites Kalchbrenner only in references (Sec 5.3), omitting it from related work (Sec 5.2) and failing to clarify *how* the application to PAS differs. This misrepresents the contribution as novel when it’s primarily an application. *Severity: Critical* – undermines the paper’s main technical claim.
2. **Missing key neural baseline**: Shibata et al. (2016) used a feedforward neural network for Japanese PAS (achieving 81.1% F1 on NAIST 1.5), yet the paper compares only to Ouchi et al. (2015) (81.23% F1) and pointwise models. Since Shibata’s model also uses neural networks *without syntactic parsers*, omitting it invalidates the "state-of-the-art without syntactic info" claim. *Severity: Critical* – the reported improvement (81.42% vs. 81.23%) is marginal when Shibata’s model is included.
3. **Insufficient analysis of Grid-RNN’s role**: The ablation (Table 1) shows Multi-Seq (Grid) > Single-Seq, but Table 2 reveals that deeper networks (8 layers) without Grid-RNN (e.g., Single-Seq w/ residual) achieve 81.15% F1, nearly matching Multi-Seq. The paper doesn’t clarify whether Grid-RNN provides *additional* gains beyond increased depth, weakening the argument for its necessity. *Severity: Major*.

Questions to Authors:
1. Kalchbrenner et al. (2016) introduced "Grid LSTM" for image captioning. How does your Grid-RNN adaptation for PAS differ from their architecture, and why isn’t this explicitly contrasted in Sec 5.2?
2. Shibata et al. (2016) achieved 81.1% F1 on NAIST 1.5 using a neural model without syntactic parsers. Why was this baseline excluded from Table 1, and how does your 81.42% result compare to it?
