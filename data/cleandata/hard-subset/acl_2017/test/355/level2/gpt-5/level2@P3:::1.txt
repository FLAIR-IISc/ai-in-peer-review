Summary
The paper addresses Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments, including omitted (zero) arguments. It proposes neural sequence models that do not rely on syntactic parses: (1) a single-sequence BiRNN model that predicts case labels for each predicate based on sentence context, and (2) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 with standard splits and no external resources, both models outperform strong baselines, and the grid model yields further gains, particularly for zero arguments.

Contributions (as assessed)
- An effective grid-type recurrent architecture for modeling multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS without syntactic features, yielding state-of-the-art results on NAIST 1.5 (All F1 81.42 vs 79.23 for Ouchi et al., 2015).
- A strong syntax-free baseline (single-sequence BiRNN with predicate indicator) and empirical evidence that full-sentence contextual modeling and increased depth with residual connections substantially aid zero argument identification.
- A comprehensive empirical evaluation including case-wise breakdowns and depth/residual ablations demonstrating robustness across roles, especially large gains for DAT.

Strengths
- Empirical significance: On NAIST 1.5, the multi-sequence model improves overall F1 by ~2.2 points over a widely used state-of-the-art joint model (79.23 → 81.42). Notably, zero arguments improve by ~3 points (44.09 → 47.12), addressing a known hard subtask in Japanese PAS.
- Syntax-free yet competitive: Achieves superior performance without syntactic information, thereby avoiding parsing error propagation. This directly addresses a well-motivated weakness of prior joint models.
- Modeling multi-predicate interactions: The Grid-RNN formalism provides a principled mechanism to propagate information across predicates and positions. The multi-sequence model consistently exceeds the single-sequence model (e.g., Zero F1 46.10 → 47.12) with significance testing, supporting the central claim.
- Depth and training insights: The inclusion of residual connections and a depth study (2–8 layers) is informative; deeper models with residuals yield ~1 point F1 increases, indicating careful engineering and offering guidance for future work.
- Detailed breakdowns: Results per case (NOM/ACC/DAT) and argument type (Dep/Zero) provide clarity. The large DAT improvements (e.g., +~30 points over baselines) are striking and suggest the approach captures low-frequency phenomena better than feature engineering.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural SRL: The single-sequence architecture is close to established predicate-flagged BiRNN formulations; the novelty rests primarily on applying grid-style recurrence to multi-predicate interactions. While useful, the conceptual step from prior joint models and Grid-RNNs in other domains is incremental.
- Significance vs baselines: Statistical testing is reported only between the single- and multi-sequence models. It would strengthen claims to report significance of improvements over Imamura et al. (2009) and Ouchi et al. (2015) on All/Dep/Zero metrics.
- Capacity control and ablations: The multi-sequence model may introduce additional capacity beyond cross-predicate connectivity. An ablation that matches parameter counts (e.g., wider single-sequence models) would help isolate the benefit of interaction modeling from increased capacity.
- Class imbalance and loss design: The output space includes a dominant null class; there is no discussion of class weighting, sampling strategies, or calibration. Given that most tokens are non-arguments, clarifying how imbalance is handled is important for reproducibility and fairness.
- Efficiency and scalability: The grid layer introduces inter-sequence dependencies with complexity scaling in M (number of predicates) and T (sentence length). There is no analysis of runtime/memory, or of performance as a function of predicates per sentence. Practicality for longer sentences or predicate-dense text remains unclear.
- Missing comparisons/synergies with syntax: While the parser-free setting is a strength, an experiment adding predicted/gold syntax or selectional preferences to the proposed models could reveal complementarity and provide an upper bound, aligning with prior joint literature.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Are improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015) statistically significant (bootstrap or paired tests) for All/Dep/Zero? Please report p-values.
- How is class imbalance handled during training and decoding? Were any losses reweighted or sampled? What is the null proportion?
- What is the computational footprint (training/inference time and memory) as a function of number of predicates per sentence? What is the typical M in the corpus, and does performance scale with M?
- Does the multi-sequence model still help on sentences with only two predicates vs many predicates? A breakdown of gains by M would clarify where interactions matter most.
- Have parameter-matched baselines been tested (e.g., wider/deeper single-sequence BiRNNs) to disentangle capacity from interaction modeling?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide qualitative analyses illustrating cases where cross-predicate information corrects zero argument errors, and error typology for NOM/ACC/DAT.
- Consider character/subword features for handling rare words and morphology in Japanese; this may further benefit zero arguments and DAT.
- Explore integration with lightweight syntactic cues (e.g., chunking or dependency distances) to assess complementarity without full parsing.
- Clarify predicate selection: the dataset specifies “target predicates that have at least one argument in the same sentence.” Confirm this follows prior work and discuss applicability when predicate detection is not given.
- Release code and scripts with detailed hyperparameters and training recipes to facilitate reproducibility (the current “http:xxx” placeholder is understandable for anonymity but final release should be complete).

Overall, the work is sound, well-motivated, and empirically convincing, offering a practical and effective alternative to syntax-dependent PAS systems with a modest architectural innovation. The paper would be strengthened by additional statistical tests, ablations controlling capacity, and efficiency/analysis details.
