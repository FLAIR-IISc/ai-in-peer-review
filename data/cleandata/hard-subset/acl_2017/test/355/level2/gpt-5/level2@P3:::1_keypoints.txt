- Paper tackles Japanese PAS (including zero arguments) with syntax-free neural models: a predicate-flagged single-sequence BiRNN and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN for joint multi-predicate modeling.  
- Evaluation on NAIST 1.5 (no external resources) shows both models beat strong baselines; Grid-RNN further improves especially on zero arguments.  

Contributions:
- Proposes a grid-type recurrent architecture to model multi-predicate interactions without syntactic features, achieving state-of-the-art on NAIST 1.5 (All F1 81.42 vs 79.23).  
- Presents a strong syntax-free baseline (single-sequence BiRNN with predicate indicator) and shows full-sentence context and deeper residual networks aid zero argument identification.  
- Provides comprehensive empirical analyses (case-wise breakdowns, depth/residual ablations) demonstrating robustness and large DAT gains.

Strengths:
- Empirically significant improvements: overall +~2.2 F1 and zero-argument +~3 F1 over prior joint model.  
- Competitive without syntax, avoiding parser error propagation and matching or exceeding syntax-dependent systems.  
- Grid-RNN effectively propagates cross-predicate information and outperforms the single-sequence model with statistical testing.  
- Depth and residual connections yield consistent gains (~1 F1), showing careful engineering.  
- Detailed per-case and per-type breakdowns, with particularly large DAT improvements implying capture of low-frequency phenomena.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: single-sequence model is similar to established predicate-flagged BiRNNs; grid application is incremental relative to prior work.  
- Statistical testing only compares single- vs multi-sequence models; significance vs prior baselines is not reported.  
- Potential confound with model capacity: no parameter-matched ablations to separate capacity from interaction modeling.  
- Class imbalance handling is unspecified despite dominant null class; no discussion of weighting, sampling, or null proportion.  
- Efficiency/scalability concerns: grid layer complexity scales with number of predicates and sentence length, with no runtime/memory analysis.  
- Missing experiments showing complementarity with syntax (predicted/gold) to establish upper bounds or hybrid benefits.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal):
- Are improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015) statistically significant for All/Dep/Zero—please report p-values?  
- How was class imbalance handled during training/decoding; were losses reweighted or sampled and what is the null proportion?  
- What is the computational footprint (time/memory) as a function of number of predicates per sentence, and typical M in the corpus?  
- Does the multi-sequence model help equally on low-M (e.g., two predicates) versus high-M sentences; please provide a breakdown by M.  
- Were parameter-matched baselines (wider/deeper single-sequence BiRNNs) tested to disentangle capacity from interaction benefits?

Additional suggestions:
- Include qualitative analyses showing examples where cross-predicate info corrects zero-argument errors and an error typology by case.  
- Consider character/subword features to better handle rare words and morphology, which may improve zero/DAT detection.  
- Explore lightweight syntactic cues (chunking or dependency distances) to test complementarity without full parsing.  
- Clarify predicate selection protocol (target predicates with at least one in-sentence argument) and applicability when predicate detection is not given.  
- Release code, hyperparameters, and training scripts for reproducibility (placeholder URL acceptable for anonymity now).
