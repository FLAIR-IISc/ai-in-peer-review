Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis that do not rely on syntactic parses. The key ideas are: (i) a single-sequence BiRNN model that induces context-sensitive representations over word sequences for each predicate, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. Evaluated on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform strong baselines; the Grid-RNN further improves zero argument identification. Residual connections enable deeper (8-layer) networks to train effectively.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A grid-type recurrent architecture that explicitly models interactions among multiple predicates in a sentence without syntactic features, yielding significant gains over a strong single-sequence RNN.
2) A strong syntax-free PAS system that achieves state-of-the-art results on NAIST 1.5, notably improving zero anaphora and especially the dative case.

Strengths
- Clear modeling of multi-predicate interactions: Extending the single-sequence BiRNN to a Grid-RNN across predicates is well-motivated by prior joint models in Japanese PAS. The design of inter-sequence connections (odd/even layers propagating across predicate dimension) is technically sound and a principled way to capture predicate–predicate dependencies from token sequences alone.

- Competitive accuracy without syntax: On NAIST 1.5, both Single-Seq and Multi-Seq surpass prior work (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015) that relied on syntactic information. This is practically valuable because it removes a dependency on external parsers and avoids error propagation.

- Strong gains on challenging cases: The largest improvements appear for Zero arguments and for DAT (Table 3), where data are sparse and long-distance cues are important. The reported +~3 F1 for Zero overall (vs Ouchi+15) and very large gains for DAT (e.g., Dep DAT 65.91 vs 30.97) suggest the models capture broader context and predicate co-occurrence patterns that syntactic features (particularly noisy predicted ones) may miss.

- Depth and residual connections: The paper demonstrates that deeper networks with residual connections yield consistent gains (Table 2). This supports the architectural decisions and offers guidance for practitioners.

- Experimental rigor: Standard data splits, bootstrap significance testing, and comparisons to both pointwise and joint baselines strengthen the empirical claims. The setup uses no external resources, isolating the effect of the architecture.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations on what drives the multi-sequence gain: While the Multi-Seq model significantly outperforms Single-Seq, the paper does not disentangle which aspects of the inter-sequence design matter (e.g., directionality across predicate dimension, number of grid layers, alternative ways to share information). A simpler alternative such as cross-predicate self-attention or sharing a pooled predicate context could be competitive; such comparisons would help establish the specific value of the Grid-RNN.

- Inference constraints unclear: The output layer uses a per-word softmax over {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null} for each predicate. It is unclear how the model enforces standard PAS constraints at inference (e.g., at most one argument per case per predicate). If no global constraint is applied, multiple tokens could be assigned the same case for a predicate. Clarifying whether there is any post-processing or decoding to enforce uniqueness would be important for reproducibility and for understanding remaining error types.

- Missing analysis and qualitative evidence: The paper reports macro improvements but provides little analysis of when multi-predicate interactions help (e.g., as a function of number of predicates per sentence, predicate distance, voice/tense, or argument distance). Qualitative examples would strengthen the claim that the grid captures meaningful predicate–predicate cues, beyond general sequence context.

- Surprising magnitude of DAT improvements: The Dep-DAT jump (e.g., ~+35 F1 in Table 3 vs Ouchi+15) is unusually large. While the paper notes annotation differences between NAIST 1.4β and 1.5, both compared systems in the 1.5 block should be directly comparable. Additional discussion (e.g., role of case-marker tokens, frequency, or data preprocessing) would help rule out confounds.

- Computational considerations and scalability: The Grid-RNN scales with O(M×T×L), where M is number of predicates, T tokens, and L layers. The paper does not report training/inference speed, memory usage, or how performance varies with sentence length and predicate count. Practitioners would benefit from this.

Questions to Authors
- How do you enforce uniqueness of arguments per case per predicate at test time? Is there any global decoding or constraint (e.g., selecting the top token per case), or is the output purely per-token softmax? If constraints are applied, please specify.

- What is the impact of the number of predicates M on accuracy and runtime? Could you include a breakdown by M and by sentence length?

- Did you try simpler cross-predicate information sharing (e.g., adding a pooled predicate-context vector to each time step, or attention across predicates) and how did these compare to the Grid-RNN?

- Can you provide error analysis or examples illustrating cases where Multi-Seq corrects Single-Seq, especially for Zero NOM and DAT?

- The DAT gains are very large. Are there any preprocessing choices (e.g., tokenization of case markers, handling of adjunct vs argument “に”) that might especially benefit your models? Could you provide per-label frequency stats to contextualize the gains?

- Do you use gold predicates, and do you include nominal predicates? If predicates are restricted to verbal predicates with at least one intra-sentential argument, how might performance change with predicted predicates?

Additional Comments
- Please clarify whether you considered bunsetsu/unit-level candidates vs word-level candidates; many Japanese PAS systems operate over bunsetsu heads. A note on how this choice affects comparability would help.

- Reporting dev/test variance over multiple random seeds would strengthen claims given small differences (e.g., +0.27 overall F1 Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq).

- Consider exploring synergy with lightweight syntax (e.g., predicted dependencies as an auxiliary input) to test whether your approach complements syntactic signals.
