Summary and claimed contributions
This paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). It proposes:
1) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic parses.
2) A strong syntax-free single-sequence BiRNN baseline that learns context-sensitive features from word sequences with minimal hand-crafted features.
3) Empirical results on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 showing state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information, surpassing a syntax-based joint model (Ouchi et al., 2015), with especially notable gains on Zero arguments; analysis of depth and the impact of residual connections.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated modeling of multi-predicate interactions without syntax: The Grid-RNN design is a natural extension of stacked BiRNNs to a 2D lattice over predicates × tokens, providing a plausible mechanism to propagate information across predicates. This directly targets a known source of error propagation in pipeline approaches that rely on syntactic parsers.
- Strong empirical performance and rigor: On NAIST 1.5, both the single-sequence and multi-sequence models outperform strong baselines, including a syntax-based joint model. The improvement on Zero arguments (roughly +2–3 F1 over Ouchi et al., 2015; +1 over the single-sequence baseline) is meaningful given the difficulty of zero anaphora. The use of standard splits and bootstrap significance testing strengthens the empirical claims.
- Minimal supervision and simplicity of features: The input uses only word embeddings, a predicate indicator, and a local predicate-centered window, with no external resources or syntactic features. Despite this, the models achieve competitive results, suggesting the architectures successfully learn useful representations from sequence information.
- Depth and training insights: The paper examines the effect of increasing depth and residual connections; deeper models with residual connections yield improvements, indicating careful consideration of optimization issues in stacked recurrent networks.
- Per-role breakdown and analysis: Reporting per-case performance (NOM/ACC/DAT; Dep vs Zero) is informative. The large gains on DAT and Zero-NOM suggest the model is capturing longer-distance dependencies that syntax-free models historically struggle with.

Weaknesses
- Modest incremental gain from multi-predicate modeling: The overall gain of the multi-sequence model over the single-sequence baseline is small (~0.3 F1 overall, ~1 F1 on Zero). While statistically significant, the practical impact versus added architectural complexity and computational cost is unclear. A breakdown by the number of predicates per sentence would help show when Grid-RNNs matter most.
- Missing efficiency and complexity discussion: The grid connection introduces O(M×T) hidden states per layer (M predicates, T tokens). The paper does not report training/inference time, memory usage, or parameter counts, nor scalability to sentences with many predicates, which is important for applicability.
- Limited ablations and analysis of what drives gains: Beyond depth and residuals, there is no ablation of input features (e.g., removing MARK or the predicate window), update directions, or inter-sequence connections (e.g., replacing Grid-RNN with simple predicate pooling/attention). Without this, it is hard to isolate how much of the improvement comes from predicate interactions versus stronger single-sentence contextualization.
- Incomplete or unclear experimental details: The code link is a placeholder (“http:xxx”), hindering reproducibility. Training details are missing for regularization (dropout, gradient clipping), and the grid layer computation order/boundary handling could be specified more precisely. It is also not stated whether predicate detection is gold or predicted (the text implies gold predicates with at least one intra-sentential argument), which could inflate results relative to end-to-end PAS systems.
- Limited qualitative/error analysis: The paper asserts improvements due to better modeling of long-distance dependencies and interactions, but provides no qualitative examples or error typology (e.g., topic/subject zeros, distance bins, ambiguity with “ni” (DAT), etc.). This would make the contributions more convincing and instructive.

Questions to authors
- How does the multi-sequence model perform on sentences with a single predicate versus multiple predicates? Please report a breakdown to substantiate the contribution of multi-predicate interactions.
- What is the computational overhead (training time, inference time, peak memory) of the multi-sequence model relative to the single-sequence baseline, as a function of M and T?
- Are the predicates provided as gold during evaluation? If so, how sensitive are the results to predicate detection errors? Would integrating a predicate detector change conclusions?
- Please provide ablations: removing the MARK feature, removing the predicate-centered window, and replacing Grid-RNN inter-sequence connections with simpler mechanisms (e.g., shared BiRNN with predicate-specific attention).
- Could you clarify the exact update schedule and boundary conditions in the grid (odd/even layers, handling m=1/M, t=1/T)? Did you try bidirectional flow in both axes within a layer?
- The code link appears incomplete. Will you release code and configuration to ensure reproducibility?

Additional comments
- Consider adding qualitative examples illustrating successful zero anaphora resolutions enabled by cross-predicate interactions, and an error analysis highlighting remaining challenges.
- Reporting precision/recall (not only F1) per case role and for Dep/Zero would shed light on error trade-offs.
- A brief discussion on combining your syntax-free representations with (robust) syntactic cues could be valuable, given that syntax and learned context may be complementary.
- Several references contain placeholder years or formatting issues; please fix for camera-ready.
