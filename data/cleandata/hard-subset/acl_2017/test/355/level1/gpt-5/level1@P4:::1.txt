Summary and claimed contributions (as assessed)
The paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis, with emphasis on zero arguments, by learning from word sequences without syntactic parses. It introduces: (i) a single-sequence stacked BiRNN (GRU) model that predicts case labels for each token with respect to a predicate using minimal word/predicate features; and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions via inter-sequence recurrent connections and residual links. Experiments on NAIST Text Corpus 1.5 show both models outperform strong baselines, with the Grid-RNN yielding the best overall F1 and larger gains on zero arguments.

Contributions (ordered by strength)
1) A grid-style recurrent architecture that jointly processes all predicates and their token sequences to capture multi-predicate interactions without syntactic input, improving overall and zero-argument performance over a strong single-sequence baseline.
2) An empirical demonstration that syntax-free sequence models can surpass previous state-of-the-art systems (which use syntactic information) on NAIST 1.5, including significance testing, with detailed breakdowns (Dep vs Zero; case roles) and depth/residual analyses.
3) A well-specified, reproducible baseline (single-sequence BiRNN with minimal features) that is itself competitive and clarifies the incremental value of multi-predicate modeling.

Strengths
1) Clear modeling of multi-predicate interactions without syntax and measurable gains where it matters most. The Grid-RNN explicitly shares information across predicates (inter-sequence connections), yielding significant improvements over the single-sequence model and prior work, especially on Zero arguments (Table 1). This directly targets the known challenge in Japanese PAS—omitted arguments—and reduces reliance on parsed syntax (and potential error propagation).
2) Solid experimental protocol and reporting. The work uses the standard NAIST 1.5 splits, avoids external resources, and reports bootstrap significance for key comparisons. It further analyzes depth and residual connections (Table 2) and per-role results (Table 3), providing a nuanced view beyond aggregate F1.
3) Competitive performance without syntactic features. Despite using only word indices, a predicate-centered window, and a predicate marker, the models outperform syntax-dependent baselines (Imamura et al., 2009; Ouchi et al., 2015). The dative case gains are substantial, suggesting robustness even for infrequent roles.
4) Architecture and training details are reasonably complete. Equations, layer definitions, hyperparameter ranges, and implementation choices (GRUs, Adam, initialization, early stopping, residual connections) are given, facilitating replication. The multi-sequence Grid-RNN is described with update equations and directional layering.
5) Focused scope and controlled comparisons. By isolating intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero), the evaluation matches prior work and allows clean comparison to established baselines on the same corpus/version and splits.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior neural architectures. The single-sequence model is a straightforward adaptation of BiRNN sequence labeling to dependency-based PAS, and the multi-sequence model is a direct application of grid/multidimensional RNNs to align predicate and token axes. While the application to PAS and modeling of multi-predicate interactions are useful, the methodological novelty is moderate.
2) Small effect size of the Grid-RNN over the BiRNN baseline. The reported overall improvement (81.42 vs. 81.15 F1) is statistically significant but marginal in absolute terms. The added architectural complexity and computational cost are not quantified, making the trade-off unclear. An ablation against simpler cross-predicate sharing (e.g., shared encoder with predicate-specific attention or feature pooling) is missing.
3) Decoding/constraint handling is under-specified. PAS typically involves structural constraints (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate). The model performs token-wise softmax per predicate, which can yield multiple tokens labeled with the same case. The paper does not detail how consistency is enforced at test time (e.g., argmax-per-role selection, tie-breaking, or ILP/greedy constraints). This omission impedes assessment of the system’s correctness and comparability to joint ILP-style baselines.
4) Limited analysis of when multi-predicate modeling helps. The paper attributes gains to predicate interactions but does not analyze performance as a function of the number of predicates per sentence, predicate distances, or shared arguments. Such analysis would substantiate the claimed mechanism and explain the modest overall gain.
5) Comparisons to alternative syntax-free baselines are incomplete. While the single-sequence baseline is strong, there is no comparison to other plausible syntax-free designs (e.g., BiRNN with global role-wise decoding, attention across predicates without grid recurrence, or inclusion of simple linguistic features like POS/morphology). This makes it harder to isolate the specific advantage of grid-style inter-sequence recurrence.

Questions to Authors (for response)
- How is structural consistency enforced at inference time (e.g., ensuring at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate)? If multiple tokens receive the same case label, what selection/post-processing is applied?
- What is the computational cost of the Grid-RNN relative to the single-sequence model (training/inference time, memory) as a function of sentence length T and number of predicates M?
- Can you report performance stratified by the number of predicates per sentence and by predicate distance (e.g., nearest neighbor predicates vs. distant), to quantify when interaction modeling helps most?
- The code link in the abstract appears as a placeholder (“http:xxx”). Is the code publicly available, and does it exactly reproduce the reported results?
- Did you experiment with simple alternatives to the grid connections (e.g., shared encoder plus cross-predicate attention or feature averaging) and, if so, how do they compare?

Additional comments
- An error analysis (especially for Zero cases) would strengthen the claim that the models capture long-distance dependencies.
- Clarifying whether predicates are given (gold or automatically identified) and whether the evaluation filters to those with intra-sentential arguments only at test time would aid comparability.
