Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper tackles Japanese predicate–argument structure (PAS) analysis without relying on predicted syntax, arguing that pipeline parsing causes error propagation. It proposes two neural sequence models: (i) a single-sequence stacked BiRNN that predicts arguments for one predicate at a time, and (ii) a multi-sequence Grid-RNN that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence, allowing information flow across predicate-specific sequences to model multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections enable deeper networks.
- Main contributions:
  1) A syntax-free, grid-type recurrent architecture that jointly models multi-predicate interactions and achieves new state-of-the-art on NAIST 1.5, surpassing strong syntax-based baselines (e.g., Ouchi et al., 2015) in overall F1 and especially for zero arguments.
  2) Empirical evidence, with significance tests, that multi-predicate interactions provide measurable gains over an already strong single-predicate sequence model, particularly for zero anaphora.
  3) An analysis of depth/residual connections demonstrating that deeper RNN/Grid-RNNs with residuals improve PAS performance, suggesting capacity to encode long-distance sentence context.

Strengths
- Strong results without syntax and clear gains on zero arguments:
  • On NAIST 1.5, Multi-Seq achieves 81.42 F vs 79.23 (Ouchi+15) and 78.15 (Imamura+09). For Zero, improvements are substantial: 47.12 vs 44.09 (Ouchi+15) (+3.0 F) and vs 41.65 (Imamura+09) (+5.5 F). Since zero arguments lack direct syntactic links, this is precisely where syntax-free sequence modeling should shine; the evidence supports the claim.
- Demonstrated benefit of multi-predicate interactions:
  • Multi-Seq outperforms Single-Seq overall (81.42 vs 81.15; p<0.05) and for Zero (+1.0 F). The improvement is moderate but statistically significant, indicating that predicate–predicate information flow adds value beyond a strong contextual encoder.
- Substantial improvements on dative (DAT) roles:
  • Compared to Ouchi+15, Dep-DAT rises from 38.39 to 65.91 (Single-Seq) / 64.38 (Multi-Seq). Zero-DAT rises from 4.80 to 9.83 (Single-Seq). Given DAT’s sparsity and difficulty, these numbers suggest the models learn robust case-role patterns from sequence context alone.
- Depth/residual analysis with clear takeaways:
  • Performance improves as layers deepen, and residual connections add ~1 F at 8 layers. This supports the technical soundness of the training setup and the design choice to use residuals in deep recurrent stacks.
- Sound evaluation practice:
  • Standard NAIST 1.5 splits, no external resources, bootstrap significance testing, and comparisons to strong, widely-cited baselines make the empirical claims credible.

Weaknesses
- Limited structural constraints and decoding discussion:
  • The model labels every token with {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null} per predicate via per-token softmax, but Japanese PAS typically requires at most one filler per case per predicate. The paper does not describe any decoding constraints (e.g., global inference or ILP) to enforce uniqueness, nor analyze potential duplicates. This is a methodological gap: even if duplicates are rare in practice, the system should explain how well-formedness is ensured or quantify violation rates.
- Ablations and simpler alternatives:
  • The paper shows Single-Seq vs Multi-Seq and depth/residual effects, but lacks ablations on input features (ARG/PRED/MARK), class weighting, or simpler multi-predicate coupling strategies (e.g., a single shared contextual BiRNN over words reused for all predicates, with predicate-specific MLPs; or attention-based predicate–predicate communication). Without these, it is hard to isolate how much of the gain comes from Grid-RNN versus parameter sharing or feature design.
- Modest magnitude of multi-predicate gains:
  • The improvement from Single-Seq to Multi-Seq is statistically significant but small overall (+0.27 F) and mixed across roles (e.g., Dep-DAT slightly decreases; Zero-ACC and Zero-DAT decrease for Multi-Seq vs Single-Seq in Table 3). This indicates the proposed interaction mechanism helps mainly in specific cases (notably Zero-NOM), and calls for deeper analysis of when and why it helps.
- Claims about avoiding parser error propagation are not fully quantified:
  • While the model outperforms a syntax-based baseline with predicted parses, the paper does not include comparisons to: (i) gold syntax to bound the benefit lost to parsing errors, or (ii) injecting predicted/gold syntactic features into the neural models. Such comparisons would directly support the central claim regarding parser error propagation.
- Analysis depth is limited:
  • No breakdown by sentence length or predicate–argument distance to substantiate the “long-distance dependency” claim. No error analysis by construction (e.g., pronoun type, argument reentrancy, case marker ambiguity). No runtime/memory analysis for Grid-RNN given it scales in M×T, which matters for deployability.

Questions to authors
- How do you enforce well-formedness at decoding time (e.g., at most one argument per case per predicate)? If not enforced, how frequent are violations, and how are they scored in evaluation?
- Did you try a shared sentence-level BiRNN encoder reused across predicates (single pass over words), followed by a predicate-conditioned classifier, and/or a simple cross-predicate attention/message-passing layer? How do these compare to Grid-RNN in accuracy and efficiency?
- Are your baselines trained/evaluated with identical predicate sets and data filtering (e.g., “predicates that have at least one in-sentence argument”)? Please clarify any differences from Ouchi+15 that could affect comparability.
- What is the training/inference complexity of Multi-Seq versus Single-Seq (time per sentence, GPU memory)? How does performance scale with number of predicates M?
- Did you explore class imbalance handling (e.g., loss weighting) given the large null class? If so, what impact did it have?
- Can you provide a breakdown by argument distance and sentence length to substantiate the long-distance modeling claim?
- The paper mentions code availability; can you confirm a working URL and any steps needed to reproduce the reported numbers?

Overall evaluation
- Novelty: Moderate. Grid/MD RNNs exist, but their application to multi-predicate PAS with syntax-free SOTA is new and useful.
- Soundness: Generally solid, but decoding constraints and ablation breadth should be strengthened.
- Significance: Good. Clear improvements over strong baselines, especially for zero anaphora and DAT, and a compelling demonstration that syntax-free models can match/exceed syntax-based systems on NAIST 1.5.
- Clarity: Good; architecture and training details are mostly clear, though more decoding and analysis details would help.

I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying decoding constraints and strengthening analysis/ablations.
