Summary
The paper addresses Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis without reliance on syntactic parsers by introducing sequence models that operate over word sequences. A single-sequence BiRNN baseline predicts arguments for each predicate independently, while a multi-sequence Grid-RNN model jointly models all predicates in a sentence to capture multi-predicate interactions. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform prior work, with the Grid-RNN providing small but significant gains over the single-sequence model, especially for zero arguments. Residual connections enable deeper networks and contribute to performance.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical result: Demonstration that a syntax-free, sequence-based approach can match or exceed prior state of the art on Japanese PAS, with notable improvements on zero arguments.
2) Modeling contribution: An adaptation of Grid-RNNs to jointly encode multiple predicate–candidate sequences in a sentence, yielding statistically significant gains over a strong single-sequence baseline.
3) Experimental analysis: Depth and residual-connection study and per-case-role breakdown that illuminate where the gains arise.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance without syntactic features: Overall F1 81.42 vs 79.23 (Ouchi et al. 2015), and larger gains on Zero (47.12 vs 44.09), showing robustness to parser error and simplifying the pipeline.
- Clear evidence that modeling multi-predicate interactions helps: Multi-sequence outperforms single-sequence (81.42 vs 81.15 overall; +1.0 for Zero), with significance testing, supporting the central hypothesis.
- Methodological simplicity and reproducibility: Minimal feature engineering (word and predicate markers), publicly released code, and standard optimization make the work accessible.
- Depth matters: Residual connections enable deeper RNN/Grid layers and yield ~1 point F1 gains, a useful practical insight.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Grid/MD-RNNs and multi-predicate joint modeling are known; the contribution is mostly an application/engineering adaptation rather than a fundamentally new architecture.
- Modest absolute gains of the multi-sequence model over the single-sequence baseline (0.27 overall F1), raising questions about the cost–benefit tradeoff.
- Missing decoding constraints and analysis: With per-token softmax, role uniqueness constraints are not enforced; the paper does not quantify violations or discuss remedies.
- Generality and scope: Results are limited to a single corpus and language; no comparison to strong syntax-augmented neural baselines or hybrids to isolate where syntax still helps.
- Limited ablations on the source of gains: No tests removing predicate-window features, shuffling predicate order, or parameter-matched controls to disentangle interaction modeling from capacity.

Questions to Authors
- Do you observe multiple NOM/ACC/DAT predictions per predicate, and if so, how frequent and how harmful? Could simple global constraints further improve results?
- What is the computational overhead of the Grid-RNN versus single-sequence (time/memory as a function of predicates per sentence)?
- Are gains still present when parameter counts are matched between single- and multi-sequence models?
- Have you tried incorporating light syntactic cues (e.g., predicted heads) to test complementarity?
