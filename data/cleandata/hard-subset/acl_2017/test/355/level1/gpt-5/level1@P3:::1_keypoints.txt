- Paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) without syntactic parses using two neural sequence models (single-predicate BiRNN and multi-predicate Grid-RNN) and reports state-of-the-art results on NAIST 1.5, especially for zero arguments.

- Contribution: shows a syntax-free sequence approach outperforms a strong syntax-dependent joint model, with notable gains on zero anaphora and dative arguments.

- Contribution: proposes a practical Grid-RNN that jointly models all predicates in a sentence, improving over a single-sequence baseline.

- Contribution: analyzes model depth and residual connections and provides per-case/per-category breakdowns of gains.

- Strength: strong empirical results without syntax, with meaningful zero-argument improvements.

- Strength: clear, scalable architectural idea for multi-predicate interactions that is simple and end-to-end trainable.

- Strength: sensible, reproducible experimental setup using standard splits, significance testing, and public code; residuals provide consistent gains.

- Strength: comprehensive per-case analysis highlighting improvements on rare DAT and zero NOM cases.

- Strength: minimal hand-engineering; lightweight feature set suffices to capture traditional parse features.

- Weakness: limited novelty and small absolute gains of Grid-RNN over the single-seq baseline; lacks ablations against simpler multi-predicate sharing mechanisms.

- Weakness: unclear inference/decoding constraints—paper does not specify enforcement of “one argument per case per predicate” or post-processing.

- Weakness: missing comparisons to other strong syntax-free baselines (CRF, BiRNN+attention, pointer/biaffine) and missing ablations on key inputs.

- Weakness: scalability and efficiency concerns unaddressed—Grid-RNN scales with number of predicates and limits parallelization; no runtime/memory analysis.

- Weakness: limited qualitative/error analysis showing what multi-predicate interactions are actually captured.

- Question: how is the “one argument per case per predicate” constraint enforced at inference, and how often are multiple arguments predicted?

- Question: what is the distribution/average number of predicates per sentence (M), and how do training/inference time and memory compare between models as M grows?

- Question: can you provide variance across random seeds (mean±std) for reported improvements?

- Question: what ablations were tried on input features (predicate mark, predicate-window, particle embeddings, morphology)?

- Question: have you compared the Grid-RNN to simpler multi-predicate mechanisms like shared encoder + predicate-conditioned attention or aggregation?

- Question: do gains persist when adding predicted syntactic features to your model, and how does Ouchi et al. (2015) perform with gold vs predicted parses?

- Question: please provide qualitative examples where the multi-sequence model corrects single-sequence errors, especially for zero arguments.

- Overall: solid and timely paper showing syntax-free sequence models can compete on Japanese PAS and multi-predicate modeling helps modestly; with clarifications and additional ablations/efficiency/qualitative analysis it deserves acceptance.
