- Tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) with zero arguments using sequence models without syntactic parses; proposes a single-sequence BiRNN and a multi-sequence Grid-RNN, with Grid-RNN giving best F1 and larger zero-argument gains on NAIST 1.5.
- Contribution: Grid-style recurrent architecture jointly models all predicates and tokens to capture multi-predicate interactions without syntax, improving overall and zero-argument performance.
- Contribution: Empirical demonstration that syntax-free sequence models can outperform prior syntax-dependent state-of-the-art on NAIST 1.5, with significance testing and detailed breakdowns.
- Contribution: Provides a well-specified, reproducible single-sequence BiRNN baseline that clarifies the incremental value of multi-predicate modeling.
- Strength: Explicit multi-predicate interaction modeling without syntax yields measurable and significant improvements, especially for Zero arguments.
- Strength: Solid experimental protocol (standard splits, no external resources) and thorough reporting (bootstrap significance, per-role and ablation analyses).
- Strength: Competitive results using minimal features (word indices, predicate marker), with notable gains on rarer roles like dative.
- Strength: Architecture and training details are sufficiently complete (equations, hyperparameters, GRUs/Adam/residuals) to facilitate replication.
- Strength: Focused evaluation on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero) enabling clean comparison to prior work.
- Weakness: Moderate methodological novelty; models are straightforward applications/adaptations of existing BiRNN and grid/multidimensional RNN ideas.
- Weakness: Absolute effect size of Grid-RNN over BiRNN is small (81.42 vs. 81.15 F1); added complexity and cost are not quantified.
- Weakness: Decoding/constraint handling is under-specified—paper omits how structural constraints (e.g., at most one NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate) are enforced at inference.
- Weakness: Limited analysis of when multi-predicate modeling helps (no stratification by number of predicates, distance, or shared arguments).
- Weakness: Incomplete comparisons to alternative syntax-free baselines (e.g., shared encoder + attention, simple linguistic features), leaving specific advantage of grid recurrence unclear.
- Question: How is structural consistency enforced at inference (e.g., handling multiple tokens assigned same case per predicate)?
- Question: What is the computational cost (training/inference time, memory) of Grid-RNN vs. single-sequence model as functions of sentence length T and predicate count M?
- Question: Can you report performance stratified by number of predicates per sentence and by predicate distance to show when interactions help?
- Question: Is the code publicly available (placeholder link in abstract) and does it reproduce reported results exactly?
- Question: Did you compare to simpler alternatives to grid connections (e.g., shared encoder + cross-predicate attention or feature averaging), and what were the results?
- Additional comment: An error analysis for Zero cases would strengthen claims about modeling long-distance dependencies.
- Additional comment: Clarify whether predicates are given (gold or auto) and whether evaluation filters to predicates with intra-sentential arguments for comparability.
