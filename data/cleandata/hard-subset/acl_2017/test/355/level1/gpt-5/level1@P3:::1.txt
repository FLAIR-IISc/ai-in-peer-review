Summary and claimed contributions
The paper addresses Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis without relying on syntactic parses. It proposes two neural sequence models: (i) a single-predicate BiRNN classifier that labels each token with NOM/ACC/DAT/PRED/null for a given predicate; and (ii) a multi-predicate Grid-RNN that processes M predicate-specific sequences jointly and propagates information across predicates to capture multi-predicate interactions. Residual connections enable deeper models. On NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, both models outperform prior work; the Grid-RNN yields the best results, with the largest gains on zero arguments.

Contributions as I see them (in order of significance):
1) Empirical finding: A syntax-free neural sequence approach surpasses a strong syntax-dependent, joint model (Ouchi et al., 2015) on NAIST 1.5, with especially large improvements on zero anaphora and dative arguments.
2) Modeling contribution: A practical Grid-RNN architecture that jointly models all predicates in a sentence, yielding statistically significant gains over a strong single-sequence baseline.
3) Analysis: An exploration of depth and residual connections for PAS, and per-case/per-category breakdowns demonstrating where the gains arise.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical results without syntax. Demonstrating that a purely sequence-based model can match or surpass syntactic joint models on Japanese PAS (including zero arguments) is impactful for languages with noisy parses or low-resource settings. The zero-argument gains (+2–3 F1 over Ouchi et al., +1 F1 for Grid vs single-seq) are particularly meaningful for a known hard subproblem.

2) Clear, scalable architectural idea for multi-predicate interactions. The Grid-RNN formalizes predicate-wise sequence processing with inter-sequence recurrence. It is simple, end-to-end trainable, and avoids pipeline error propagation inherent to syntax-driven joint models.

3) Sensible training setup and fair comparison on a standard split. Use of NAIST 1.5 with standard train/dev/test, bootstrap significance testing, and public code support reproducibility. The report of depth and residuals (up to 8 layers) is useful and shows consistent trends (notably ~+1 F1 from residuals).

4) Comprehensive per-case analysis. The improvements on rare DAT and zero NOM cases are notable and suggest the model captures long-range contextual cues better than feature-engineered baselines.

5) Minimal hand-engineering. The feature set is intentionally lightweight (word embeddings, predicate window, predicate mark), underscoring that learned context representations suffice to capture many cues traditionally injected via parse features.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty and small absolute gains from the Grid over a strong single-seq baseline. While the syntax-free result is compelling, the Grid-RNN is a relatively straightforward extension of stacked BiRNNs along an extra dimension (cf. Graves & Schmidhuber; Kalchbrenner et al.). The net gain over the single-seq model is modest overall (+0.27 F1) and about +1 F1 for Zero. A stronger case for the architectural contribution would require ablations against simpler multi-predicate sharing (e.g., shared encoder with predicate-conditioned attention, pooling or message passing) and cost/benefit analysis.

2) Inference constraints and well-formedness are unclear. The token-wise softmax per predicate does not appear to enforce structural constraints such as at most one argument per case per predicate. If no constraints or post-processing are applied, the model can predict multiple NOM/ACC/DAT for a predicate or miss all. The paper should clarify whether any decoding constraint, tie-breaking, or global normalization is used, and how this affects evaluation.

3) Missing comparisons and ablations. The paper does not compare to strong syntax-free baselines beyond the single-seq BiRNN, such as CRF over token labels, BiRNN with attention, or pointer/biaffine argument selection. Nor does it present ablations on key inputs (e.g., removing the predicate mark or predicate-window features) to substantiate where the signal comes from. A syntax-aware variant (with predicted parses) would also quantify the true impact of removing syntax.

4) Scalability and efficiency are not discussed. The Grid-RNN increases time/memory roughly linearly with the number of predicates M (and the inter-sequence recurrence prevents easy parallelization). There is no runtime/memory analysis or a study of performance as M grows, leaving open whether gains persist on sentences with many predicates and whether the approach is practical in production.

5) Limited analysis of what interactions are captured. The paper lacks qualitative/error analysis showing concrete cases where multi-predicate context helps (e.g., aligning shared subjects, case alternations, predicate selectional coherence). Without this, it is hard to judge whether the model learns genuine cross-predicate semantics vs. superficial correlations.

Questions to Authors
- How do you enforce or encourage the “one argument per case per predicate” constraint at inference? If unconstrained, how often do you predict multiple NOM/ACC/DAT for a predicate? Is any post-processing applied?

- What is the average number of predicates per sentence (M) and the distribution? Please report training/inference time and peak memory for single-seq vs Grid-RNN, and performance as a function of M (including the subset with M=1 where no interaction should help).

- Can you provide variance over multiple random seeds? The improvements of the Grid model are relatively small; reporting mean±std would strengthen the significance claim.

- What ablations did you try on input features? For Japanese, case-marking particles are crucial. Do explicit particle embeddings or morphological features help beyond raw word embeddings?

- Have you compared against simpler multi-predicate mechanisms (e.g., shared encoder with predicate-conditioned attention to other predicates, or aggregating hidden states across predicate sequences) to isolate the value of Grid recurrence?

- Do the gains persist if you add predicted syntactic features to your model? Conversely, how does Ouchi et al. (2015) perform with gold vs predicted parses to quantify error propagation?

- Please include qualitative examples where the multi-sequence model corrects errors made by the single-sequence model, especially for zero arguments.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid and timely paper with convincing evidence that syntax-free neural sequence models can compete in Japanese PAS and that modeling multi-predicate interactions helps, albeit modestly. With clarifications on decoding constraints, added ablations/baselines, and some efficiency/qualitative analysis, it would be a strong accept. As is, I lean toward acceptance based on empirical impact and simplicity.
