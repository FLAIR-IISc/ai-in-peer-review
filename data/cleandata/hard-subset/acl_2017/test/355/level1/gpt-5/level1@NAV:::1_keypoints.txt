- Summary: Proposes syntax-free sequence models (single-sequence BiRNN and multi-sequence Grid-RNN) for Japanese PAS, outperforming prior work with Grid-RNN yielding small but significant gains, especially on zero arguments; residual connections enable deeper, better networks.

- Contribution 1: Shows a syntax-free, sequence-based approach can match/exceed prior state of the art on Japanese PAS, with notable improvements on zero arguments.

- Contribution 2: Adapts Grid-RNNs to jointly model multiple predicate–candidate sequences in a sentence, producing statistically significant gains over a strong single-sequence baseline.

- Contribution 3: Provides experimental analysis on network depth, residual connections, and per-case-role performance to explain where gains arise.

- Strength: Strong empirical performance without syntactic features (overall F1 81.42 vs 79.23; Zero 47.12 vs 44.09), simplifying the pipeline and improving robustness to parser error.

- Strength: Clear evidence that modeling multi-predicate interactions helps (multi-sequence > single-sequence; +1.0 on Zero) with significance testing.

- Strength: Methodologically simple and reproducible — minimal feature engineering, public code, and standard optimization.

- Strength: Demonstrates that depth and residual connections matter, yielding ~1-point F1 improvements.

- Weakness: Limited novelty — Grid/MD-RNNs and multi-predicate joint modeling are known; contribution is primarily an application/adaptation.

- Weakness: Modest absolute gains of Grid-RNN over the strong single-sequence baseline (0.27 overall F1), questioning cost–benefit.

- Weakness: Lacks decoding constraints and analysis — per-token softmax may allow multiple NOM/ACC/DAT per predicate, but violations are not quantified or addressed.

- Weakness: Limited generality — experiments only on one corpus/language and no comparison to strong syntax-augmented neural baselines or hybrids.

- Weakness: Insufficient ablations to pinpoint sources of gains (no tests removing predicate-window features, shuffling predicate order, or parameter-matched controls).

- Question: How frequent and harmful are multiple NOM/ACC/DAT predictions per predicate, and would simple global constraints improve results?

- Question: What is the computational overhead (time/memory) of Grid-RNN versus the single-sequence model as predicates per sentence scale?

- Question: Do gains persist when parameter counts are matched between single- and multi-sequence models?

- Question: Have you tried adding light syntactic cues (e.g., predicted heads) to test complementarity with the syntax-free approach?
