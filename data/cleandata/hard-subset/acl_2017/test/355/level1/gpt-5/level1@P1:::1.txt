Summary and main contributions
This paper tackles Japanese predicate-argument structure (PAS) analysis without relying on predicted syntax, focusing on intra-sentential arguments (Dep and Zero). The authors propose:
1) A strong BiRNN-based single-sequence model that labels each token for a given predicate and achieves state-of-the-art results on NAIST 1.5 without any syntactic features or external resources.
2) A multi-sequence Grid-RNN architecture that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence, explicitly modeling multi-predicate interactions via inter-sequence recurrences, yielding further gains—especially for Zero arguments.
3) An empirical study showing that deeper networks with residual connections improve PAS performance.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over strong baselines without syntax. On NAIST 1.5 (standard splits), the single-sequence model achieves 81.15 F1 and the multi-sequence model 81.42 F1 vs 79.23 for Ouchi et al. (2015) and 78.15 for Imamura et al. (2009). For Zero arguments, gains are larger: 46.10 (single) and 47.12 (multi) vs 44.09, confirming that sentence-level contextualization helps argument omission resolution. The multi vs single improvement is statistically significant (p < 0.05).
- Modeling multi-predicate interactions without syntax is novel and effective for Japanese PAS. Prior “joint” methods relied on syntactic parses and suffered error propagation; the Grid-RNN connects predicate-specific sequences at each position and improves both Dep (88.10→88.17) and Zero (46.10→47.12), with notably stronger impact on Zero, aligning with linguistic intuition and prior findings (Ouchi et al., 2015).
- Strong performance on infrequent cases. The model substantially boosts dative Dep F1 (65.91 single, 64.38 multi) compared to Ouchi et al. (38.39), indicating robustness when supervised examples are sparse and suggesting the model leverages sentence-level cues beyond local syntax.
- Methodologically simple and resource-light. Only word indices, a predicate-centered window, and a predicate marker are used; no POS, lemmas, parses, selectional preferences, or external embeddings. This simplicity reduces feature engineering and dependency on external tools, and demonstrates the competitiveness of sequence models for PAS.
- Useful architectural insights. The depth/residuals study shows consistent improvements as layers increase, and that residual connections enable training deeper RNN/Grid-RNN stacks (+~1 F1 at 8 layers), which is valuable guidance for future PAS/SRL sequence models.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural SRL: architecture is a direct adaptation of multi-dimensional RNNs/Grid-RNN to multi-predicate sequences. While applying grid connections across predicate sequences is a nice idea in this setting, the components (BiRNN/GRU, softmax tagging, residuals, grid connections) are known; the paper lacks deeper theoretical or algorithmic innovation beyond this composition.
- Inference formulation and constraints are under-specified. The model assigns per-token labels via softmax among {NOM, ACC, DAT, PRED, null}. PAS typically enforces at most one argument per case per predicate; it is unclear whether any global constraint or postprocessing ensures uniqueness (e.g., argmax per case across tokens), or whether multiple tokens can be assigned the same case. This affects both interpretability and comparability to prior work, and could partially explain dative improvements.
- Comparison set and ablations are incomplete. There is no comparison to:
  • A strong single-predicate BiRNN with CRF or max-margin inference (even if IOB isn’t needed, CRF could model label competition across tokens).
  • Variants including simple predicate interaction mechanisms (e.g., attention across predicate representations) to isolate the specific benefit of grid connections.
  • Syntax-aware versions of the proposed model to quantify the value of syntax when combined with grids.  
  Feature ablations (e.g., removing MARK or the predicate-centered window) are also missing.
- Evaluation details that may affect fairness/replicability are unclear. The paper states “target predicates that have at least one argument in the same sentence” are used—does the test set use gold predicate lists, and does excluding predicates without intra-sentential arguments change the label distribution vs prior work? The use of gold word boundaries is noted; whether tokenization errors (as in realistic pipelines) would reduce gains is not assessed. Statistical significance is only reported for Multi-Seq vs Single-Seq, not against baselines.
- Scalability and efficiency concerns are unaddressed. Grid-RNN complexity grows with M×T; sentences with many predicates may increase compute/memory substantially. No training speed, memory, or convergence discussion is provided, nor handling of variable predicate counts (padding/masking strategies, batching).

Questions to authors
- How do you enforce PAS constraints at inference? Specifically, do you restrict to one argument per case per predicate? If so, how is this implemented with per-token softmax outputs? If not, how does evaluation handle multiple tokens predicted with the same case?
- What is the precise test-time predicate set? Are predicates provided as gold, and are predicates without intra-sentential arguments excluded at test time? How does performance change if all predicates are included?
- Did you evaluate significance of improvements over Ouchi et al. (2015) and Imamura et al. (2009)? If so, please report.
- Can you provide ablations: removing MARK, varying the predicate window C, and replacing grid connections with a simpler interaction (e.g., shared BiRNN with predicate indicators, or cross-predicate attention)?
- What are typical M (number of predicates per sentence) and runtime/memory statistics for training/inference? Any issues with longer sentences?
- Error analysis: where does the grid help most? Qualitative examples for Zero-NOM gains would strengthen the case that long-distance and cross-predicate cues are being captured.

Additional comments
- Reporting results with and without gold tokenization and exploring larger embedding/hidden sizes or pretrained embeddings would clarify how much of the gains are due to architecture vs optimization choices.
