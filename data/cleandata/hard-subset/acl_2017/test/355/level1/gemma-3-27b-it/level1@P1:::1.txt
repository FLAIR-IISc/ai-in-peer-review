This paper proposes a neural model using Grid-RNNs for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, aiming to overcome the error propagation issues inherent in traditional pipeline approaches that rely on syntactic information. The core idea is to automatically induce features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions directly from the word sequence. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus, exceeding existing systems *without* using syntactic information.

**Contributions:**

1. **Effective Grid-RNN Architecture for Multi-Predicate Interaction:** The primary contribution lies in the successful application of Grid-RNNs to capture interactions between predicates in Japanese PAS. This allows the model to leverage semantic relationships between predicates without relying on potentially noisy syntactic parses. The architecture demonstrably improves performance, particularly for zero pronoun resolution.
2. **State-of-the-Art Performance without Syntactic Information:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the NAIST corpus *without* syntactic parsing is a significant accomplishment. This demonstrates the potential of sequence-based neural models to overcome the limitations of pipeline approaches in languages like Japanese with flexible word order and frequent argument omission.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies (network depth, residual connections) and comparisons to existing baseline systems. The analysis of performance per case role offers valuable insights into the model’s strengths and weaknesses.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Grid-RNNs:** The adaptation of Grid-RNNs to the task of Japanese PAS is a clever and effective approach.  The architecture is well-suited for capturing the relationships between multiple predicates in a sentence, addressing a critical challenge in this task. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the benefits of the grid structure.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods on the NAIST corpus are substantial and convincing. The statistical significance testing further strengthens the validity of these results.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the proposed solution. The model architecture is explained with helpful figures, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.
4. **Focus on Zero Pronoun Resolution:**  Addressing the zero pronoun resolution problem is crucial for Japanese PAS, and the model's performance improvements in this area are particularly noteworthy. The authors correctly identify this as a major challenge and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.
5. **Publicly Available Code:** The availability of source code is a significant benefit, allowing for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents quantitative results, a more detailed qualitative error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding the types of errors the model still makes could provide valuable insights for future improvements.  Specifically, are there systematic errors related to specific types of predicates or argument structures?
2. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Models:** The comparison to baseline models is somewhat limited. While Ouchi et al. (2015) is a strong baseline, more recent neural approaches to Japanese PAS (if any exist) should be included in the evaluation. It would be helpful to understand how the proposed model compares to other end-to-end neural models for this task.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but the details are relatively brief.  A discussion of the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings would be useful.  Are there specific hyperparameters that have a significant impact on the results?
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST corpus is a standard benchmark, but it is relatively small. Evaluating the model on a larger and more diverse dataset would provide a more robust assessment of its generalizability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting the types of errors the model makes and the potential reasons for these errors?
2. Are there any recent neural models for Japanese PAS that you considered for comparison? If so, why were they not included in the evaluation?
3.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, embedding dimension, number of RNN/Grid layers)?
4.  Have you considered using pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) to initialize the embedding layer? Would this improve performance?

**Additional Comments:**

* The citation style is inconsistent in places.
* Consider mentioning the computational cost of the Grid-RNN architecture compared to simpler RNN models.
* It would be interesting to investigate the interpretability of the learned Grid-RNN representations – can we visualize or understand the interactions being captured by the model?
