## Review of "Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure Analysis"

This paper presents a neural model leveraging Grid-RNNs to improve Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically addressing the challenges of argument omission common in the language. The key idea is to move away from reliance on potentially error-prone syntactic parsing by directly modeling interactions between predicates from the word sequence itself. The authors demonstrate that their model exceeds the performance of a state-of-the-art PAS analyzer on the NAIST Text Corpus, without utilizing syntactic information.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the successful application and demonstration of Grid-RNNs for capturing multi-predicate interactions in the context of Japanese PAS. While RNNs have been used in this area before, the use of a grid architecture to directly model these interactions without relying on syntactic parsing is a significant step.  A secondary, but valuable contribution, is the empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of this approach on a standard benchmark dataset, showing tangible improvements, particularly for zero-pronoun resolution. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model demonstrably outperforms the state-of-the-art baseline (Ouchi et al., 2015) on the NAIST corpus, particularly in identifying zero arguments, which is a key challenge in Japanese PAS. This provides concrete evidence of the model's effectiveness.
2. **Novel Application of Grid-RNNs:** The use of Grid-RNNs to directly model interactions between predicates is a novel and promising approach. The architectural choice seems well-motivated by the problem and the experiments support its effectiveness.
3. **Reduced Reliance on Syntactic Information:** By removing the dependency on parser output, the proposed method mitigates the problem of error propagation, which is a known weakness of pipeline approaches. This is a valuable contribution to the field.
4. **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written and the experimental setup is clearly described, including implementation details and hyperparameter settings. The availability of source code further enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Grid-RNN Behavior:** While the authors demonstrate *that* the Grid-RNN improves performance, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *how* it achieves this.  Visualizing the learned interactions within the grid or conducting ablation studies could provide more insight.
2. **Comparative Scope:** The comparison is primarily against one recent baseline.  Comparing against a wider range of models, including recent transformer-based approaches, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's strengths and weaknesses.
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The NAIST corpus, while standard, is relatively small. Evaluating on larger, more diverse datasets would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide examples of specific interactions captured by the Grid-RNN that are beneficial for zero-pronoun resolution? 
2. Have you experimented with different ways of incorporating word embeddings (e.g., pre-trained embeddings like Word2Vec or fastText)? If so, what impact did it have on performance?
3.  How does the performance scale with sentence length? Are there limitations in the grid architecture that become apparent with longer sentences?

**Additional Comments:**

* The connection to related work on semantic role labeling (SRL) in English is well-established.
* The inclusion of residual connections appears beneficial, but a more detailed ablation study investigating their impact would be useful.
