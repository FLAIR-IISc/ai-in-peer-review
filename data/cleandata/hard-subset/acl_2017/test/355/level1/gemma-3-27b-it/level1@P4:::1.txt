This paper introduces a neural model using grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNNs) for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, aiming to overcome the error propagation issues of traditional methods reliant on syntactic parsing. The core contribution is the development and demonstration of a model that achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on the NAIST Text Corpus *without* syntactic information, relying instead on word sequence information and multi-predicate interactions. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that modeling these multi-predicate interactions, via the Grid-RNN architecture, yields significant improvements, particularly for zero anaphora resolution. A further, albeit smaller, contribution is the exploration of network depth and residual connections to improve performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that their Grid-RNN model surpasses existing state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus, crucially doing so *without* relying on potentially noisy syntactic parses. This is a significant achievement, as the authors correctly identify syntactic error propagation as a major limitation of previous approaches. The reported improvements, particularly on zero anaphora, are substantial and noteworthy.
2. **Addressing a Key Challenge in Japanese PAS:** The focus on zero anaphora is well-motivated. The paper acknowledges the difficulties associated with resolving omitted arguments in Japanese, and the proposed model demonstrably improves performance on this challenging aspect of the task. This targeted approach makes the work particularly valuable.
3. **Sound Architectural Choices:** The use of Grid-RNNs is a reasonable and well-justified choice for capturing multi-predicate interactions. The explanation of how the grid structure allows information flow between different predicate sequences is clear. The inclusion of residual connections, and the exploration of network depth, are also indicative of a careful and systematic approach.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments, including comparisons with strong baselines, analysis of network depth, and ablation studies regarding residual connections. The use of bootstrap resampling for significance testing is commendable. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of Grid-RNNs and the specific application to Japanese PAS *without* syntactic parsing is novel, the individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs, residual connections) are well-established in the literature. The novelty primarily lies in the application and the demonstrated effectiveness within this specific problem domain.  The claim of "introducing" Grid-RNNs feels slightly overstated as the technology is fairly established.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still struggles would provide valuable insights for future work. Are there specific types of zero anaphora that remain challenging? Are there particular syntactic constructions that pose difficulties?
3. **Dataset Specificity:** The evaluation is limited to the NAIST Text Corpus. While this is a standard dataset for Japanese PAS, it would be beneficial to explore the generalizability of the model to other datasets or domains. The potential impact of dataset-specific characteristics on the observed performance is not addressed.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but does not detail the process extensively.  A more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the rationale behind the selected values would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, outlining the types of errors the model still makes? Specifically, what kinds of zero anaphora are most difficult to resolve?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of embedding dimensionality (dword)? Did you explore other values, and if so, what were the results?
3.  The paper states the model is applicable to SRL in other languages. Could you elaborate on the potential challenges and adaptations required to apply the model to languages with different syntactic structures (e.g., English)?
4.  You mention exploring large-scale unlabeled data. What specific approaches are you considering for leveraging such data (e.g., pre-training, semi-supervised learning)?
