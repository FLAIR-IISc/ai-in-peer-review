- Proposes Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, avoiding syntactic parsing.
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on the NAIST Text Corpus without using syntactic features.
- Demonstrates consistent improvements for zero-anaphora resolution through multi-predicate modeling.
- Provides ablation studies on network depth and residual connections that inform model design.
- Strong empirical performance with statistically significant gains over prior work.
- Grid-RNN architecture is well-motivated and empirically more effective than single-sequence RNNs for predicate interactions.
- Clear focus on the challenging problem of Japanese PAS and intra-sentential zero-anaphora.
- Paper is well-written with clear model descriptions, diagrams, and experimental setup.
- Lacks detailed error analysis identifying where the model still fails and which zero-anaphora types are problematic.
- Comparisons rely on older baselines; missing comparisons to recent neural models or models using pretrained/contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT).
- No comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., embedding dimension, learning rate).
- Does not discuss computational cost or scalability of Grid-RNNs for training and inference.
- Requests a detailed error breakdown categorizing common model errors and zero-anaphora failure modes.
- Asks whether pretrained embeddings or contextual models were tried and their impact on performance.
- Seeks clarification on training/inference computational cost and scaling with sentence length and predicate count.
- Questions whether other connection types (e.g., dense connections) or architectural variants were explored beyond residual links.
- Notation is consistent and clear throughout the paper.
- Publicly available code is a valuable contribution.
- References are comprehensive and relevant.
- Suggests discussing limitations such as reliance on the relatively small NAIST corpus and generalization to other domains or dialects.
