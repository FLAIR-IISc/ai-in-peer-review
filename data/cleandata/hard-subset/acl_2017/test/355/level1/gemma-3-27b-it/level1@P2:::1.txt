This paper proposes a neural model using Grid-RNNs for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, aiming to overcome the error propagation issues inherent in traditional pipeline approaches that rely on syntactic information. The core idea is to directly model multi-predicate interactions from word sequences, bypassing the need for a parser.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Grid-RNN architecture for multi-predicate interaction modeling:** The authors successfully adapt Grid-RNNs to capture relationships between predicates within a sentence, a novel application for this task. This is the strongest contribution, as it directly addresses the stated problem of error propagation from syntactic parsers.
2. **State-of-the-art performance on NAIST Text Corpus:** The proposed model achieves competitive and, in some cases, superior results compared to previous state-of-the-art methods on a standard benchmark dataset, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Demonstration of parser-free PAS analysis:** The work shows that high accuracy can be achieved without relying on potentially noisy syntactic information, making the system more robust and applicable in scenarios where parsing is unreliable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of Japanese PAS analysis, particularly the issue of argument omission and the limitations of parser-dependent approaches. This provides strong motivation for the proposed work.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed, utilizing a standard dataset (NAIST Text Corpus) and comparing against established baseline models. The ablation study with and without residual connections further strengthens the analysis.
3. **Detailed Model Description:** The paper provides a comprehensive explanation of the proposed models (single-sequence and multi-sequence) including the architecture, input features, and training process. The figures are helpful in understanding the network structure.
4. **Significant Results for Zero Arguments:** The authors specifically highlight the improvements achieved for zero argument identification, which is a particularly challenging aspect of Japanese PAS, demonstrating the practical benefits of their approach.
5. **Publicly Available Code:** Releasing the source code promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of Grid-RNNs with PAS analysis is novel, the individual components (RNNs, Grid-RNNs) are not new. The paper doesn’t sufficiently emphasize *how* the Grid-RNN is specifically tailored and optimized for this task beyond the general concept of capturing interactions.
2. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on quantitative results. Including some examples of cases where the model succeeds or fails would provide valuable insights into its strengths and weaknesses. A qualitative error analysis would strengthen the understanding of the model’s behavior.
3. **Limited Comparison to Recent Advances:** The comparison to related work is somewhat dated. The field of neural PAS analysis is rapidly evolving, and including comparisons to more recent, state-of-the-art models (even if not directly comparable due to different datasets/settings) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of different hyperparameters (e.g., RNN layer size, learning rate) is not thoroughly explored. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide examples of sentences where the multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence model and explain what specific predicate interactions contribute to the performance gain?
2. What is the computational cost of the Grid-RNN model compared to the single-sequence model? Is there a significant increase in training or inference time?
3. Have you investigated the use of attention mechanisms within the Grid-RNNs to further focus on relevant predicate interactions?
4. Are there specific types of predicates or argument structures where the model consistently struggles?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper could benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential future research directions.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the proposed model and the baseline models.
* The reference list is extensive, which is good, but could be formatted more consistently.



Overall, this is a solid and well-written paper that presents a promising approach to Japanese PAS analysis. While the individual components may not be entirely novel, the effective application of Grid-RNNs to model multi-predicate interactions and the demonstrated state-of-the-art performance make it a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
