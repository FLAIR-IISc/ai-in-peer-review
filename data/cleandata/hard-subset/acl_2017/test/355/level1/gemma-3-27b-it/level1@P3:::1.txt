This paper addresses the challenging task of Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, specifically focusing on mitigating the error propagation inherent in traditional pipeline approaches reliant on syntactic parsing. The authors propose a neural model leveraging Grid-RNNs to directly capture multi-predicate interactions from word sequences, bypassing the need for explicit syntactic information. The key contribution is demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus *without* syntactic features, showcasing the effectiveness of the grid-RNN architecture for modeling predicate interactions. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that modeling multi-predicate interactions (using the grid-RNN) consistently improves performance, particularly for zero-anaphora resolution. Finally, the ablation studies regarding network depth and residual connections provide valuable insights into model design choices.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this work is the demonstrated performance. Achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark (NAIST) *without* relying on syntactic parsing is a significant achievement. The improvements, especially concerning zero-anaphora resolution (a known bottleneck in Japanese PAS), are convincing. The statistical significance testing supports the claim of improvement over existing methods.
2. **Effective Architecture for Multi-Predicate Interaction:** The use of Grid-RNNs appears to be a well-motivated and effective architectural choice. The paper clearly explains the rationale behind using Grid-RNNs – to directly model interactions between predicates – and provides empirical evidence supporting its effectiveness compared to a single-sequence RNN. The ablation studies around residual connections further strengthen the design choice.
3. **Focus on a Challenging Problem:** The paper explicitly addresses the difficulties of zero-anaphora resolution in Japanese, which is a core challenge for PAS analysis. By focusing on this issue and showing improvements in this specific area, the work demonstrates a strong understanding of the problem domain. The justification for focusing on intra-sentential arguments is also reasonable given the complexity of inter-sentential analysis.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The descriptions of the models (single-sequence and multi-sequence) are clear, and the diagrams effectively illustrate the architecture. The experimental setup is clearly defined, and the results are presented in a concise and easy-to-understand manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper presents strong overall performance numbers, it lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still fails (types of zero anaphora, specific predicates causing issues, etc.) would provide valuable insights for future work and a more nuanced understanding of the model’s limitations. What types of sentences are still problematic for the Grid-RNN model?
2. **Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison with Ouchi et al. (2015) is relevant, that work is now several years old. Are there more recent or more sophisticated neural approaches to Japanese PAS analysis that could serve as stronger baselines? A comparison with models incorporating pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText, or even more recent contextualized embeddings like BERT) would be informative.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning (learning rate, batch size, L2 regularization), but does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the sensitivity of the models to these parameters. Demonstrating robustness to hyperparameter choices would strengthen the findings. How sensitive are the results to the embedding dimension?
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** Grid-RNNs, by their nature, are computationally more expensive than standard RNNs. The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training and inference, which is an important consideration for practical applications.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common errors made by the model and potential reasons for these errors? Specifically, can you categorize the types of zero-anaphora resolution errors?
2.  Have you experimented with incorporating pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText, or BERT) into your models? If so, what was the impact on performance? If not, why not?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training and inference with the Grid-RNN model compared to the single-sequence model? How does the computational cost scale with sentence length and the number of predicates?
4.  The paper mentions residual connections improving performance. Was any attempt made to explore different types of connections (e.g., dense connections) or other architectural variations to further enhance performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used throughout the paper is consistent and clear.
*   The publicly available code is a valuable contribution to the research community.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the potential limitations of the model, such as its reliance on a relatively small dataset (NAIST Text Corpus) and its potential difficulty generalizing to other domains or dialects.
