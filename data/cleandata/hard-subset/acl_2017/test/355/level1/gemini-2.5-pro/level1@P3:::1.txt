### Summary of the paper
This paper proposes two neural models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis, a task complicated by frequent argument omission (zero anaphora). The key innovation is to move away from traditional models that rely heavily on syntactic parser output, thereby avoiding error propagation. The first model, a "Single-Sequence" model, is a relatively standard stacked Bi-RNN that processes a sentence for each predicate independently. The main contribution is the second model, a "Multi-Sequence" model, which uses a Grid-RNN architecture to jointly analyze all predicates in a sentence simultaneously. This grid structure allows information to flow not only along the time axis (word sequence) but also along the predicate axis, explicitly modeling interactions between multiple predicates without needing syntactic information. Experiments on the standard NAIST Text Corpus show that both models outperform the previous state-of-the-art syntax-based system, with the Multi-Sequence model achieving the best results, particularly on the challenging task of identifying zero arguments.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A novel and effective application of Grid-RNNs for modeling inter-task dependencies.** The paper frames the PAS analysis for M predicates in a sentence as M parallel sequence labeling tasks. The Grid-RNN provides an elegant and principled architecture to model the interactions between these parallel tasks directly from the word sequence, which is a significant conceptual contribution beyond the specific application to Japanese PAS.
2.  **State-of-the-art performance on Japanese PAS without syntactic features.** The paper convincingly demonstrates that a purely sequence-based neural model can surpass complex, feature-engineered models that rely on syntactic parsers. This is a strong empirical result that underscores the power of end-to-end learning for structured prediction tasks in NLP and contributes to the broader trend of moving away from pipeline-based systems.

### Strengths
1.  **Elegant and Novel Methodology:** The core idea of using a Grid-RNN to model multi-predicate interactions is compelling. It extends the standard 1D sequence modeling of RNNs to a 2D grid (time × predicates), providing a natural mechanism for information to flow between the analyses of different predicates. This avoids the need for explicit, feature-engineered representations of predicate interactions (e.g., based on syntactic distance or dependency paths) and allows the model to learn these dependencies automatically. This is a sophisticated and well-motivated architectural choice.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The paper achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark dataset. The improvements over the previous best system (Ouchi et al., 2015) are substantial, particularly for identifying zero arguments where the F-measure improves by over 3 points (44.09 to 47.12). This is a difficult subtask, and such a large improvement clearly validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results convincingly show that removing the dependency on syntactic parsers does not hurt, but in fact helps performance when coupled with an appropriate neural architecture.
3.  **Excellent Ablation and Analysis:** The paper is structured around a clear and effective ablation study. The Single-Sequence model serves as a strong baseline that isolates the effect of using an RNN for contextual encoding. The performance lift gained by moving to the Multi-Sequence model (from 81.15 to 81.42 F1) directly quantifies the benefit of modeling multi-predicate interactions. The authors further break down the results by argument type (`Dep` vs. `Zero`) and case role, showing that the gains are most pronounced for the more difficult `Zero` arguments, which strongly supports their central hypothesis. The analysis of network depth and the positive impact of residual connections further strengthens the paper's experimental rigor.
4.  **Simplicity and Reduced System Complexity:** A major advantage of this work is its departure from complex pipelines. By operating directly on word sequences, the model eliminates the need for a separate syntactic parsing step. This not only simplifies the overall system but also makes it more robust by avoiding the propagation of parsing errors, which can be a significant problem, especially on out-of-domain text.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper is missing a qualitative analysis that would provide insight into *what* the Multi-Sequence model is actually learning. The claim is that it captures "multi-predicate interactions," but this remains an abstract concept. Including an example sentence where the Multi-Sequence model succeeds and the Single-Sequence model fails (e.g., in resolving a difficult zero anaphora) would make the contribution much more concrete and interpretable. What specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., argument sharing, coreference constraints) is this architecture capturing? Without this, the Grid-RNN remains something of a black box.
2.  **Unclear Predicate Ordering in the Grid:** The Grid-RNN architecture as described in Eq. (4) is directional along the predicate dimension (`m`), processing information from `m-1` to `m` in odd layers and from `m+1` to `m` in even layers. This implies a fixed ordering of predicates. The paper does not specify how this ordering is determined (e.g., by linear position in the sentence). This design choice could have a significant impact on what is learned, as information flow is asymmetric. For instance, the analysis of the last predicate can benefit from all previous ones, but not vice-versa in an odd-numbered layer. A discussion of this choice, its potential impact, and alternatives (e.g., a fully bidirectional grid along the predicate dimension) is needed.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Alternative Neural Architectures:** The paper presents the Grid-RNN as the solution for modeling interactions. However, other neural architectures could plausibly achieve a similar goal. For instance, one could employ attention mechanisms, where the representation of a word for a given predicate attends to the representations of the same word for other predicates. Another simpler approach might involve creating a global sentence/predicate summary vector that is then used as additional input for each predicate-specific analysis. While the Grid-RNN is a fine choice, the paper would be stronger if it briefly discussed alternative designs and justified why the grid-based approach was chosen.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify how the predicates are ordered along the 'm' dimension of the Grid-RNN? Is this based on their linear order of appearance in the sentence? Have you investigated whether this ordering affects the model's performance?
2.  To better understand the mechanism, could you provide a concrete example from your test set that highlights the difference between the Single-Sequence and Multi-Sequence models? Specifically, an example of a zero argument that is correctly identified by the Multi-Sequence model but missed by the Single-Sequence model would be very insightful. What information from another predicate's analysis appears to help in the correct identification?
