- Proposes two neural models for Japanese PAS: Single-Sequence (per-predicate Bi-RNN) and Multi-Sequence (Grid-RNN jointly modeling time × predicates) to avoid reliance on syntactic parsers.  
- Grid-RNN jointly models interactions among multiple predicates, allowing information flow along time and predicate axes.  
- Both models outperform prior syntax-based state-of-the-art on the NAIST Text Corpus, with the Multi-Sequence model best overall and especially on zero arguments.  
- Main contribution: novel application of Grid-RNN to frame M predicates as M parallel sequence-labeling tasks and model their interactions.  
- Main contribution: demonstrates state-of-the-art Japanese PAS performance without syntactic features, supporting end-to-end sequence-based approaches.  
- Strength: elegant and well-motivated architecture that learns predicate interactions without hand-engineered syntactic features.  
- Strength: strong empirical gains, notably a >3-point F-measure improvement on zero-argument identification (44.09 → 47.12).  
- Strength: thorough ablation and analysis isolating Single- vs Multi-Sequence effects, breakdown by Dep/Zero and case role, and exploration of depth/residual connections.  
- Strength: simpler, more robust system by removing dependency on external syntactic parsers and pipelines.  
- Weakness: lacks qualitative examples showing what linguistic phenomena the Multi-Sequence model captures, leaving Grid-RNN behavior opaque.  
- Weakness: unclear how predicates are ordered along the predicate dimension and how the directional processing affects learning; no discussion of alternatives (e.g., bidirectional predicate axis).  
- Weakness: limited discussion/comparison of alternative neural approaches (attention, global sentence/predicate summaries) that could model predicate interactions.  
- Question: clarify how predicate ordering along the m dimension is determined and whether ordering impacts performance.  
- Question: provide concrete test-set examples where Multi-Sequence succeeds but Single-Sequence fails, explaining what cross-predicate information enabled success.
