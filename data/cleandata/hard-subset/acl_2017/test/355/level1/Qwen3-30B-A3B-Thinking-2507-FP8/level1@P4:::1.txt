

Summary and Main Contributions:
This paper proposes a neural network approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that eliminates reliance on syntactic parsers, addressing the error propagation problem in previous joint modeling approaches. The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model using bidirectional RNNs that captures context from the entire sentence for individual predicates, and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that explicitly models interactions between all predicates in a sentence. The main contributions are: (1) a neural approach that achieves state-of-the-art PAS analysis without syntactic information, (2) a novel application of Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences, and (3) significant improvements on zero argument identification, a particularly challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a genuine problem in Japanese PAS analysis: error propagation from syntactic parsers used in previous joint modeling approaches. By eliminating the need for syntactic information, the authors provide a clean solution to this well-documented problem in the literature (Iida and Poesio, 2011; Sasano and Kurohashi, 2011). The results demonstrate that neural models can achieve high accuracy without relying on potentially erroneous syntactic parses.

2. The experimental results are strong and well-documented. The multi-sequence model achieves 81.42% F-measure, significantly outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (81.15% for single-sequence, 79.23% for Ouchi et al., 2015) with p < 0.05 significance. The improvements are particularly notable for zero arguments (47.12% vs 44.09%), which is a key challenge in Japanese PAS due to the lack of direct syntactic dependencies. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach for the most difficult aspect of the task.

3. The paper provides thorough ablation studies showing the effectiveness of key components. The multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model (81.42% vs 81.15%), demonstrating the value of modeling multi-predicate interactions. The study also shows that deeper networks with residual connections (8 layers) achieve better results (81.42% vs 80.42%) compared to shallower networks, supporting the design choices.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the Grid-RNN application is limited. While Grid-RNNs were introduced by Kalchbrenner et al. (2016), the paper doesn't clearly articulate how their specific application to PAS analysis differs from or improves upon the original Grid-RNN architecture. The Grid-RNN structure is applied in a straightforward manner to model multi-predicate interactions, which doesn't represent a significant architectural innovation beyond the original work.

2. The comparison with previous neural approaches is inadequate. Shibata et al. (2016) and Iida et al. (2016) also used neural networks for PAS analysis, but they incorporated syntactic features. The paper claims to outperform them without syntactic information, but doesn't provide a fair comparison since the feature sets differ. A more appropriate comparison would re-implement these models without syntactic features to isolate the effect of the neural architecture.

3. The paper lacks qualitative analysis to explain why the Grid-RNN approach works better for multi-predicate interactions. While the results show that the multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence model, particularly for zero arguments, the paper doesn't provide examples or analysis to illustrate how the model captures these interactions. This limits the understanding of the model's behavior and the value of the proposed architecture.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does your Grid-RNN architecture specifically differ from Kalchbrenner et al. (2016) in terms of connection patterns or training procedure? What specific aspects of multi-predicate interactions does your architecture capture that the original Grid-RNN might not?

2. The paper states that neural models achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information, but previous neural approaches (Shibata et al., 2016; Iida et al., 2016) used syntactic features. Could you provide results for re-implementing these models without syntactic features to enable a fair comparison?

3. The paper demonstrates that the multi-sequence model significantly improves zero argument identification. Could you provide specific examples showing how the model identifies zero arguments using multi-predicate interactions, compared to the single-sequence model?
