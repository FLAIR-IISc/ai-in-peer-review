Proposes a Grid-RNN neural model to capture multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS without syntactic information, claiming state-of-the-art on NAIST Text Corpus.

Main contribution: Grid-RNN processes multiple predicates' argument candidates simultaneously to model inter-predicate interactions.

Secondary contribution: Significant improvement in zero argument identification (47.12% vs. 44.09% F1).

Addresses error propagation from dependency on syntactic parsers by removing syntactic input.

Grid-RNN architecture is well-motivated and effectively models predicate sequence connections, outperforming single-sequence models.

Strong empirical results overall, marginally beating prior SOTA by 0.17% F1 and showing a ~3.0 point gain for zero arguments.

Comprehensive evaluation includes per-case-role analysis, with large gains for the dative case (~30 points).

Novelty concern: Grid-RNN itself is not new (Graves & Schmidhuber 2009; Kalchbrenner et al. 2016) and the paper fails to clearly differentiate its use.

Inadequate comparisons to prior neural approaches (Shibata et al. 2016; Iida et al. 2016) and unclear whether those used syntactic information.

Modest absolute performance gains (0.17% F1) raise questions about practical significance given implementation effort.

No discussion of computational cost or efficiency trade-offs of the Grid-RNN versus simpler models.

Question: How would a Transformer/attention-based model compare for capturing multi-predicate interactions and why wasn't it explored?

Question: Did Shibata et al. (2016) use syntactic information, and how does this model compare under identical conditions (with/without syntax)?

Question: Are the zero-argument improvements due to Grid-RNN specifically, or to other factors like network depth or residual connections?

Overall: Valuable and conceptually sound contribution demonstrating syntax-free SOTA and strong zero-argument gains, but novelty, comparative baselines, and efficiency analyses need strengthening for a stronger acceptance case.
