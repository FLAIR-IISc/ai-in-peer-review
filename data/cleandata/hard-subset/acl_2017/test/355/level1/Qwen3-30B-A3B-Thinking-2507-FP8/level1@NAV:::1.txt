

This paper proposes a neural approach for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that avoids reliance on syntactic information from parsers by modeling multi-predicate interactions directly from word sequences. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that state-of-the-art PAS analysis can be achieved without syntactic information, challenging previous assumptions; (2) introducing a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that effectively captures interactions between all predicates in a sentence; and (3) significantly improving zero argument identification, the most challenging aspect of Japanese PAS analysis.

Strengths: The work successfully addresses a key limitation in previous approaches (error propagation from parsers) and achieves strong results on the standard NAIST Text Corpus. The multi-sequence model shows a significant 3.0-point improvement in zero argument F-measure (47.12% vs. 44.09%) over the previous state-of-the-art, demonstrating the value of modeling multi-predicate interactions directly from text. The approach is simpler and avoids pipeline errors inherent in prior work. The results are particularly impressive for the dative case, where performance improved by approximately 30 points.

Weaknesses: The claimed novelty is questionable as the Grid-RNN architecture is similar to Kalchbrenner et al. (2016), with insufficient explanation of how this work advances the state of the art. The performance gains are modest (only 0.27% absolute improvement in overall F-measure), making it difficult to justify the added model complexity. The paper fails to adequately explain why previous neural approaches for Japanese PAS (e.g., Shibata et al. 2016) did not achieve similar results, weakening the novelty claim. The comparison with English SRL work (Zhou and Xu 2015) is superficial and doesn't clearly establish why their approach would generalize.

Questions to Authors: How does your Grid-RNN architecture specifically differ from Kalchbrenner et al.'s (2016) Grid LSTM, and why does it yield better results for Japanese PAS? Can you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component (residual connections, multi-sequence vs. single-sequence) to the final performance? Why did previous neural approaches for Japanese PAS not achieve comparable results without syntactic information?
