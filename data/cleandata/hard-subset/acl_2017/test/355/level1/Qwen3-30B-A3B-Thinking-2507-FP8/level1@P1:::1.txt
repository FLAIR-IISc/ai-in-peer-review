

# Peer Review

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes neural sequence models for Japanese Predicate Argument Structure (PAS) analysis that automatically capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information from parsers. The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model that processes each predicate independently, and a multi-sequence model using Grid-RNNs that jointly processes all predicates in a sentence. The main contributions I see are:

1. Demonstration that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance for Japanese PAS analysis without syntactic information, addressing the error propagation problem in pipeline approaches.
2. Novel application of Grid-RNNs to capture multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis, with empirical evidence showing significant improvements for zero argument identification.
3. Comprehensive experimental validation showing that the multi-sequence model outperforms previous approaches, particularly for zero arguments (which are challenging due to lack of direct syntactic dependencies).

## Strengths

1. **Significant Performance Gains Without Syntactic Information**: The paper demonstrates that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art results (81.42% F1) without relying on syntactic parsers, which is a major advancement in Japanese PAS analysis. The most compelling evidence is the substantial improvement for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 44.09% for the previous state-of-the-art), which are particularly challenging due to the lack of direct syntactic dependencies. This addresses a critical limitation in current approaches and shows that contextual information from the entire sentence is sufficient for high-quality PAS analysis.

2. **Novel Grid-RNN Architecture for Multi-Predicate Interactions**: The Grid-RNN architecture is a well-motivated and effective solution for capturing multi-predicate interactions. The paper clearly demonstrates that the multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model (81.42% vs. 81.15% F1), with the most significant improvements for zero arguments (47.12% vs. 46.10%). This shows that the Grid-RNN architecture successfully captures the semantic relationships between predicates, which is crucial for Japanese PAS analysis.

3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments including ablation studies on network depth, residual connections, and detailed per-case role analysis. The comparison across different case roles (NOM, ACC, DAT) reveals that the neural models particularly excel at identifying dative cases (32.35% vs. 24.43% for the previous state-of-the-art), which is significant given that dative arguments appear infrequently. The results consistently show that the proposed models outperform previous approaches across all metrics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Previous Neural Approaches**: The paper claims to achieve state-of-the-art results without syntactic information but fails to compare directly with Shibata et al. (2016), who also used neural networks for Japanese PAS analysis. The paper mentions Shibata et al. as using syntactic information, but doesn't clarify why their approach is different or why it's more effective without syntactic information. This weakens the novelty claim and makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method.

2. **Lack of Analysis on What the Model Learned**: While the paper demonstrates that the Grid-RNN model improves performance, it doesn't provide any analysis of what the model actually learned about multi-predicate interactions. The paper would be significantly strengthened by visualizations of attention or feature representations showing how the model captures interactions between predicates, particularly for zero arguments.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the model would scale to longer sentences or whether the performance gains would hold for other languages with similar pro-drop characteristics. The paper also doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches to Japanese PAS analysis that may have emerged after the cited works.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that previous neural approaches (like Shibata et al., 2016) relied on syntactic information, but Shibata et al. (2016) actually used a neural network for the score calculation part of a joint model that still required syntactic information. Can you clarify exactly what syntactic information was used in Shibata et al. (2016) and why your approach is different in terms of syntactic dependency?

2. The paper demonstrates that the multi-sequence model outperforms the single-sequence model for zero arguments. Could you provide specific examples from the test set where the multi-sequence model correctly identifies zero arguments that the single-sequence model failed to identify, to better understand how the Grid-RNN captures multi-predicate interactions?

3. The paper mentions that the Grid-RNN architecture is inspired by Graves and Schmidhuber (2009) and Kalchbrenner et al. (2016), but doesn't explain why Grid-RNNs are particularly suited for this task compared to other sequence models. Could you elaborate on the specific advantages of Grid-RNNs for capturing multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis?

4. The paper states that "our neural models realize the state-of-the-art performance by using word sequence information of a sentence." However, the comparison with previous neural approaches is limited. Could you provide a more comprehensive comparison with other neural models for Japanese PAS analysis, including their use of syntactic information and performance metrics?

This paper makes a solid contribution to Japanese PAS analysis by demonstrating that neural models can achieve state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information, with particular improvements for the challenging zero argument identification task. The Grid-RNN architecture is a well-motivated approach to capturing multi-predicate interactions. With some clarification on the comparison with previous neural approaches and additional analysis of what the model learned, this paper could make an even stronger contribution to the field.
