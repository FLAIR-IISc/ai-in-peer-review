- Proposes a novel Grid-RNN architecture to model multi-predicate interactions for Japanese PAS without relying on syntactic parsers, avoiding pipeline error propagation.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance without syntactic information, with a notable 3.0-point F-measure gain for zero argument identification (47.12% vs. 44.09%).  
- Provides thorough experimental validation on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, comparing to strong baselines and using bootstrap resampling for significance testing; consistent gains across NOM/ACC/DAT, especially DAT.  
- Major strength: substantial improvement on the hard problem of zero pronoun resolution, a long-standing challenge in Japanese PAS.  
- Major strength: eliminates error propagation from syntactic parsers, addressing a key limitation of prior pipeline approaches.  
- Major strength: clear, well-designed experiments and comparisons to prior state-of-the-art systems.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis explaining why Grid-RNN outperforms simpler neural models—no qualitative examples or ablation showing specific multi-predicate interaction effects.  
- Weakness: lacks direct comparison with other neural approaches (e.g., Shibata et al.) under the same input feature settings (word sequences only).  
- Weakness: no discussion of computational efficiency or trade-offs between neural model complexity and parser-based pipelines.  
- Question: provide example sentences where Grid-RNN correctly resolves zero arguments that single-sequence models miss, with explanations.  
- Question: how does Grid-RNN perform when given syntactic information—does it still outperform baseline neural models?  
- Question: what specific features or representations does the model learn that capture multi-predicate interactions for zero arguments?  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel contribution with convincing empirical gains and recommended for acceptance, but would benefit from deeper analysis and comparisons.
