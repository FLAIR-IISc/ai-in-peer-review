**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis, which focuses on identifying semantic units of a sentence, such as who did what to whom. The model uses grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNN) to capture multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information predicted by parsers. The authors introduce two models: a single-sequence model that estimates arguments of each predicate independently and a multi-sequence model that jointly predicts arguments for all predicates in a sentence at a time. The experiments on the NAIST Text Corpus demonstrate that the proposed models achieve the state-of-the-art result without syntactic information, particularly improving the performance for zero argument identification.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural Model for Japanese PAS Analysis**: The authors propose a neural model that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions without relying on syntactic information predicted by parsers.
2. **Grid-RNN Architecture**: The paper introduces a grid-type recurrent neural network architecture that can capture long-distance dependencies and multi-predicate interactions.
3. **State-of-the-Art Result**: The experiments demonstrate that the proposed models achieve the state-of-the-art result without syntactic information, particularly improving the performance for zero argument identification.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective Capture of Multi-Predicate Interactions**: The proposed Grid-RNN architecture effectively captures multi-predicate interactions, which is beneficial for Japanese PAS analysis.
2. **Robust Performance**: The models achieve robust performance without relying on syntactic information predicted by parsers, which reduces the error propagation.
3. **Improved Performance for Zero Argument Identification**: The multi-sequence model significantly outperforms the single-sequence model in F-measure for zero arguments, which is one of the problematic issues in Japanese PAS analysis.
4. **Comparative Analysis**: The paper provides a comprehensive comparative analysis with previous studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed models.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Analysis of Model Parameters**: The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the model parameters, such as the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis**: The paper does not provide a detailed error analysis, which could help to identify the limitations of the proposed models.
3. **Dependence on Pre-Training**: The models rely on pre-trained word embeddings, which may not be optimal for Japanese PAS analysis.
4. **Limited Generalizability**: The paper only evaluates the proposed models on a single dataset (NAIST Text Corpus), which may not be representative of other datasets or languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the model parameters (e.g., number of layers, window size) affect the performance of the proposed models?
2. Can you provide a detailed error analysis to identify the limitations of the proposed models?
3. How do the pre-trained word embeddings affect the performance of the proposed models?
4. Can you evaluate the proposed models on other datasets or languages to demonstrate their generalizability?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed models and experiments. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed models for Japanese PAS analysis, particularly improving the performance for zero argument identification. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the model parameters and error analysis to identify the limitations of the proposed models.
