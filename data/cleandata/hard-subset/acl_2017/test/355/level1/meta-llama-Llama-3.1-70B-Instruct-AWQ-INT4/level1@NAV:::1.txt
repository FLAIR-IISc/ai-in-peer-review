**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural model for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis that automatically induces features sensitive to multi-predicate interactions from word sequence information of a sentence. The model uses grid-type recurrent neural networks (Grid-RNN) to capture the interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence, without relying on syntactic information predicted by parsers. The authors evaluate their model on the NAIST Text Corpus and demonstrate that it achieves the state-of-the-art result, outperforming previous models that use syntactic information.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural Modeling of Multi-Predicate Interactions**: The authors propose a novel neural architecture that effectively captures multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis, without relying on syntactic information.
2. **Grid-RNN for PAS Analysis**: The authors introduce the use of Grid-RNN for PAS analysis, which allows the model to capture long-distance dependencies and interactions between multiple predicates in a sentence.
3. **State-of-the-Art Result on NAIST Text Corpus**: The authors demonstrate that their model achieves the state-of-the-art result on the NAIST Text Corpus, outperforming previous models that use syntactic information.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective Capture of Multi-Predicate Interactions**: The authors' model effectively captures multi-predicate interactions in Japanese PAS analysis, which is a key challenge in this task.
2. **State-of-the-Art Result**: The authors' model achieves the state-of-the-art result on the NAIST Text Corpus, demonstrating its effectiveness in Japanese PAS analysis.
3. **No Need for Syntactic Information**: The authors' model does not rely on syntactic information predicted by parsers, which can be prone to errors and limit the performance of the model.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their model on the NAIST Text Corpus, which may not be representative of other datasets or languages.
2. **No Comparison with Other Neural Models**: The authors do not compare their model with other neural models that may also be effective for Japanese PAS analysis.
3. **No Analysis of Model Parameters**: The authors do not provide an analysis of the model parameters, such as the number of layers, hidden units, and learning rate, which may affect the performance of the model.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their model to other languages or datasets?
2. Can the authors provide a comparison with other neural models that may also be effective for Japanese PAS analysis?
3. How do the authors plan to analyze the model parameters to improve the performance of the model?
