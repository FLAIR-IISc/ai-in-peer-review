The paper proposes a convolutional neural network approach to model text coherence, building upon the well-known entity grid representation. The authors provide a clear motivation for their approach and offer insightful discussions on the intuitions behind certain design decisions, such as the choice of CNN and the explanation in the section 'Why it works'.

The extensive evaluation across several tasks demonstrates the proposed approach's superiority over previous methods. However, the inability to reproduce one previous result, specifically the Li/Hovy (2014) results, raises concerns about potential implementation or modeling errors that warrant attention.

Although the model is a relatively simple 'neuralization' of the entity grid model, it achieves promising results. Nevertheless, I found it unclear why 100-dimensional vectors are necessary to represent a four-dimensional grid entry (or a few more in the case of the extended grid). It would be beneficial to elaborate on how this representation helps. While optimizing directly for coherence ranking can lead to a better model, the difference in transition chains for up to k=3 sentences versus k=6 sentences may not be substantial, especially considering the brevity of many WSJ articles.

The writing could be improved, as certain parts are repeated throughout the paper. For instance, the introduction to entity grids is repeated, and section 2 also presents related work, making the first two-thirds of section 6 redundant and in need of deletion (or integration into section 2 where necessary). The remainder of section 6 could be incorporated into section 2 under a subsection, and section 2 could be renamed 'Related Work'.

Overall, this paper presents a solid implementation of applying a neural network model to entity-grid-based coherence. However, considering the proposed consolidation of previous work, I would expect more innovations in representations (e.g., additional features) or tasks from a full paper.

Minor points:

* The paper may benefit from proof-reading by a native speaker, as there are several instances of missing articles (e.g., 'the WSJ corpus' and 'the Brown toolkit').
* On page 1, bottom left column, 'Figure 2' should be corrected to 'Figure 1'.
* On page 1, 'Firstly' and 'Secondly' could be rephrased as 'First' and 'Second'.
* On page 1, 'limits the model to' could be rephrased as 'prevents the model from considering ...'.
* Consider removing the 'standard' final paragraph in section 1, as it is not necessary in a relatively short paper.
