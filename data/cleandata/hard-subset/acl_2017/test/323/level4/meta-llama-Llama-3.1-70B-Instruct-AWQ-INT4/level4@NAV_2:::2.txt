The paper proposes a convolutional neural network approach to model the coherence of texts, leveraging the well-known entity grid representation for coherence. The authors provide a clear discussion of the intuitions behind certain design decisions, such as the use of a CNN and the explanation in the section titled 'Why it works'.

The approach is well-motivated, and the extensive evaluation on several tasks demonstrates that the proposed approach outperforms previous methods. However, it is surprising that the results of Li and Hovy (2014) could not be reproduced, suggesting a potential implementation or modeling error that should be addressed.

The model is a relatively simple 'neuralization' of the entity grid model, and it is unclear why 100-dimensional vectors are necessary to represent a four-dimensional grid entry (or a few more in the case of the extended grid). The authors should provide more insight into how this representation helps, especially considering that optimizing directly for coherence ranking would likely result in a better model. The difference in transition chains for up to k=3 sentences versus k=6 might not be substantial, particularly since many WSJ articles may be very short.

The writing is lengthy, and certain parts are repeated throughout the paper. For example, the introduction to entity grids is repeated, and section 2 also presents related work, which is repeated again in section 6. The authors should consider consolidating the related work into section 2, renaming it as necessary, and removing the redundant parts.

Overall, this paper presents a solid implementation of applying a neural network model to entity-grid-based coherence. However, considering the proposed consolidation of previous work, more innovations in representations (e.g., other features) or tasks would be expected from a full paper.

Minor points:

* The paper may benefit from proof-reading by a native speaker, as there are missing articles in many places (e.g., 'the WSJ corpus' and 'the Brown toolkit').
* On page 1, the reference to 'Figure 2' should be 'Figure 1'.
* The use of 'Firstly' and 'Secondly' could be replaced with 'First' and 'Second'.
* The phrase 'limits the model to' could be rephrased as 'prevents the model from considering ...'.
* Consider removing the 'standard' final paragraph in section 1, as it is not necessary to follow such a short paper.
