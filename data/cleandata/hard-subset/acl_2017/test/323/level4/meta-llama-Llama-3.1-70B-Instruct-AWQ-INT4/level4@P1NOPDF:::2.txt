The paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to model text coherence, building upon the well-known entity grid representation. The authors' decision to combine these two concepts is well-motivated and clearly explained, particularly in the discussion of their design choices (e.g., the use of CNNs and the section titled 'Why it works').

The evaluation is extensive, covering several tasks and demonstrating the proposed approach's superiority over previous methods. However, it is puzzling that the authors were unable to replicate one previous result, as their findings on Li/Hovy (2014) suggest a possible implementation or modeling error that warrants attention.

While the model is a notable 'neuralization' of the entity grid model, it is relatively straightforward. I found it unclear why 100-dimensional vectors are necessary to represent a four-dimensional grid entry (or a few more in the case of the extended grid). It is apparent that optimizing directly for coherence ranking would help learn a better model, but the difference in transition chains for up to k=3 sentences versus k=6 might not be substantial, especially considering that many WSJ articles may be quite short.

The writing could be more concise, as certain sections are repetitive. For instance, the introduction to entity grids is repeated in multiple places. Specifically, section 2 also covers related work, making the first two-thirds of section 6 redundant and potentially removable (or integratable into section 2 as needed). The remainder of section 6 might be better suited as a subsection within section 2, which could then be renamed 'Related Work'.

Overall, this paper presents a solid implementation of applying a neural network model to entity-grid-based coherence. However, considering the proposed consolidation of previous work, I would expect more innovation in representations (e.g., additional features) or tasks from a full paper.

Minor points:

* The paper would benefit from proofreading by a native speaker, as there are several instances of missing articles (e.g., 'the WSJ corpus' and 'the Brown toolkit').
* On page 1, bottom left column, 'Figure 2' should be corrected to 'Figure 1'.
* On page 1, 'Firstly' and 'Secondly' could be rephrased as 'First' and 'Second'.
* On page 1, 'limits the model to' might be rephrased as 'prevents the model from considering'.
* Consider removing the 'standard' final paragraph in section 1, as it is not necessary in a relatively short paper.
