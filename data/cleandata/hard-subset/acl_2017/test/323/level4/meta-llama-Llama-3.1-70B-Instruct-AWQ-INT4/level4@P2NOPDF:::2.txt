The paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to model the coherence of texts, building upon the well-known entity grid representation. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated explanation of their approach, including insightful discussions on the intuitions behind certain design decisions, such as the choice of CNN and the section titled 'Why it works'.

The evaluation of the proposed approach is extensive and demonstrates its superiority over previous methods on several tasks. However, the failure to reproduce one previous result, specifically the results on Li/Hovy (2014), raises concerns about potential implementation or modeling errors that should be addressed.

While the model is a notable 'neuralization' of the entity grid model, it is relatively simple and relies on 100-dimensional vectors to represent a four-dimensional grid entry (or a few more in the case of the extended grid). The necessity and benefits of this design choice are not entirely clear. It is understandable that optimizing directly for coherence ranking would lead to a better model, but the difference in transition chains for up to k=3 sentences versus k=6 might not be substantial, particularly considering the brevity of many WSJ articles.

The writing is somewhat lengthy, with certain parts repeated throughout the paper, such as the introduction to entity grids. Section 2 also presents related work, making the first two-thirds of section 6 redundant and in need of deletion (or integration into section 2 where necessary). The remainder of section 6 could be added to section 2 under a subsection, and section 2 should be renamed as 'Related Work'.

Overall, this paper presents a solid implementation of a neural network model applied to entity-grid-based coherence. However, considering the consolidation of previous work, a full paper would be expected to provide more innovative representations, features, or tasks.

Minor points to consider:

- The paper would benefit from proof-reading by a native speaker to address missing articles and minor grammatical errors.
- On page 1, the bottom left column should be corrected to read 'Figure 1' instead of 'Figure 2'.
- The use of 'Firstly' and 'Secondly' on page 1 could be replaced with 'First' and 'Second'.
- The phrase 'limits the model to' on page 1 could be rephrased as 'prevents the model from considering ...'.
- The 'standard' final paragraph in section 1 is not necessary and could be removed.
