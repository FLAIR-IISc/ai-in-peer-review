The paper introduces an extension of the entity grid model. A convolutional neural network is used to learn sequences of entity transitions indicating coherence, permitting better generalisation over longer sequences of entities than the direct estimates of transition probabilities in the original model.

**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

The paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on a convolutional neural network that operates over the entity grid representation of a text. The model captures long-range entity transitions along with entity-specific features without losing generalization, thanks to the power of distributed representation. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A neural architecture for coherence assessment that can capture long-range entity transitions along with arbitrary entity-specific features.
2. An end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high-level features automatically in the model.
3. State-of-the-art results on three different coherence assessment tasks, outperforming existing models by a significant margin.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to modeling local coherence using a convolutional neural network, which allows for better generalization over longer sequences of entities.
2. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on three different coherence assessment tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
3. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the model, including a comparison with existing models and an analysis of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with existing neural coherence models, particularly the model proposed by Li and Hovy.
2. The experimental setup for evaluating the Li and Hovy model is not thoroughly explained, which raises questions about the validity of the comparison.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the results, including an investigation of the factors that contribute to the model's performance.

**Questions to authors:**

1. Can you provide more details about the experimental setup for evaluating the Li and Hovy model, including the corpus and task used?
2. Have you attempted to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy on their original data sets as a sanity check?
3. Can you provide more insight into the factors that contribute to the model's performance, including the impact of entity-specific features and the choice of convolutional neural network architecture?
