The paper proposes a convolutional neural network approach to model the coherence of texts, based on the well-known entity grid representation. The approach is well motivated and described, with clear discussions of the intuitions behind certain design decisions.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. A neural architecture for coherence assessment that can capture long-range entity transitions along with arbitrary entity-specific features.
2. An end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high-level features automatically in the proposed model.
3. State-of-the-art results on three different coherence assessment tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.

The strengths of the paper include:
1. A thorough evaluation on several tasks, showing that the proposed approach beats previous methods.
2. A clear and well-motivated discussion of the design decisions behind the model.
3. The use of a pairwise ranking approach to train the model, which allows for end-to-end learning of task-specific features.

However, there are some weaknesses:
1. The model is a relatively simple 'neuralization' of the entity grid model, and it is unclear whether the use of 100-dimensional vectors to represent a four-dimensional grid entry is necessary.
2. The results on Li/Hovy (2014) suggest an implementation or modelling error that should be addressed.
3. The writing is a bit lengthy, with some repetition of certain parts in several places.

Some questions to the authors:
1. Can you provide more insight into why 100-dimensional vectors are necessary to represent a four-dimensional grid entry?
2. How do you plan to address the issue with the Li/Hovy (2014) results, and what steps will you take to ensure that the implementation is correct?
3. Are there any plans to explore other features or tasks that could be used to further improve the model?

Minor points:
- The paper may benefit from proof-reading by a native speaker to address issues with missing articles and other grammatical errors.
- There are some minor formatting issues, such as incorrect figure references and inconsistent use of transition words.
