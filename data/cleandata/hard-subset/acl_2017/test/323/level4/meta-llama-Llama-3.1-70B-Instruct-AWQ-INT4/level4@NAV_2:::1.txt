The paper introduces an extension of the entity grid model. A convolutional neural network is used to learn sequences of entity transitions indicating coherence, permitting better generalisation over longer sequences of entities than the direct estimates of transition probabilities in the original model.

Summary of the paper: The authors propose a neural local coherence model based on a convolutional neural network that operates over the entity grid representation of a text. The model captures long range entity transitions along with entity-specific features without losing generalization, thanks to the power of distributed representation. The authors present a pairwise ranking method to train the model in an end-to-end fashion on a task and learn task-specific high level features.

Main contributions: 
1. The authors propose a neural architecture for coherence assessment that can capture long range entity transitions along with arbitrary entity-specific features. 
2. The authors present an end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high level features automatically in the proposed model. 
3. The authors evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed coherence model on three different evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

Strengths: 
1. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the existing entity grid models and their limitations, and propose a neural architecture to overcome these limitations. 
2. The authors evaluate the proposed model on three different evaluation tasks and demonstrate its effectiveness in achieving state-of-the-art results. 
3. The authors provide a detailed description of the proposed model, including the neural architecture, the training method, and the evaluation tasks.
4. The authors release their code with the submission, which is a significant contribution to the research community.

Weaknesses: 
1. The authors do not provide a comprehensive comparison with the existing neural coherence model of Li and Hovy. 
2. The authors do not investigate the performance of the proposed model on different corpora or tasks where the Li and Hovy model is more successful. 
3. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the results, including the error analysis and the discussion of the limitations of the proposed model.

Questions to Authors: 
1. Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup for the Li and Hovy model, including the hyperparameter settings and the training data? 
2. Can the authors investigate the performance of the proposed model on different corpora or tasks where the Li and Hovy model is more successful? 
3. Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, including the error analysis and the discussion of the limitations of the proposed model?
