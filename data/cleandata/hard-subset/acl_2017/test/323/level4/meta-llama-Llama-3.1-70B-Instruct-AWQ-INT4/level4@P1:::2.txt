The paper proposes a convolutional neural network approach to model the coherence of texts, building on the well-known entity grid representation for coherence. The authors provide a clear discussion of the intuitions behind certain design decisions, such as the use of a convolutional neural network and the section titled 'Why it works'. 

The main contributions of this paper are: (1) a neural architecture for coherence assessment that can capture long-range entity transitions along with arbitrary entity-specific features, (2) an end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high-level features automatically, and (3) state-of-the-art results on three coherence assessment tasks.

The strengths of this paper include: (1) a well-motivated approach that leverages the strengths of both entity grid models and neural networks, (2) an extensive evaluation on several tasks that demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, and (3) a clear and well-organized presentation of the model and its results.

The weaknesses of this paper include: (1) the model is a relatively simple 'neuralization' of the entity grid model, and it is unclear whether the use of 100-dimensional vectors to represent a four-dimensional grid entry is necessary or effective, (2) the paper repeats certain parts in several places, such as the introduction to entity grids, and (3) the writing could be more concise and polished.

To address the weaknesses, the authors could consider providing more justification for the use of 100-dimensional vectors and exploring other features or representations that could further improve the model's performance. Additionally, the authors could revise the paper to eliminate repetition and improve the clarity and concision of the writing.

Questions to authors:

* Can you provide more justification for the use of 100-dimensional vectors to represent a four-dimensional grid entry? How does this help to improve the model's performance?
