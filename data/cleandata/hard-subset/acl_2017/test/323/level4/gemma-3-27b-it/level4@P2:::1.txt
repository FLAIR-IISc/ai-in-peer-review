This paper presents a neural extension to the entity grid model for assessing text coherence. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid approaches—specifically, the use of discrete representations and task-agnostic feature computation—by leveraging a convolutional neural network (CNN) to capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion. The model represents text using an entity grid and employs distributed representations and convolutional filters to model coherence. Evaluation on three tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, outperforming existing models.

The primary contribution of this work is a novel neural architecture for modeling local coherence that effectively combines the strengths of entity grid representations with the representational power of CNNs. By using distributed representations and convolutional layers, the model overcomes the limitations of discrete representations and can capture longer-range dependencies than previous entity grid models. A second contribution lies in the end-to-end training approach, which allows the model to learn task-specific features directly from the data, rather than relying on hand-engineered features. Finally, the strong empirical results on three different coherence assessment tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing methods across three distinct evaluation tasks, achieving statistically significant improvements on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported gains (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary coherence rating) are substantial and indicate a meaningful advancement in the field.
2. **Effective Integration of CNNs and Entity Grids:** The paper successfully integrates the benefits of CNNs – particularly their ability to learn hierarchical representations and capture long-range dependencies – with the well-established entity grid framework. This combination addresses key limitations of traditional entity grid models.
3. **End-to-End Training:** The end-to-end training approach is a significant advantage, allowing the model to learn task-specific features automatically and reducing the reliance on manual feature engineering. This simplifies the development process and potentially leads to more robust and adaptable models.
4. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the motivation, methodology, and experimental results. The inclusion of a diagram illustrating the neural architecture is helpful.
5. **Code Release:** Releasing the code associated with the submission promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Recent Neural Models:** The comparison to related work, specifically the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014), is insufficient. The authors acknowledge unexpectedly low performance for Li and Hovy’s model but don't sufficiently investigate potential causes (e.g., implementation issues, hyperparameter sensitivity, dataset compatibility). A more thorough investigation and discussion would strengthen the paper.
2. **Minor Linguistic Issues:** The paper contains several instances of missing definite articles and awkward phrasing ("…limits the model to do X…"). While these issues do not significantly impact understanding, they detract from the overall polish of the manuscript. Proofreading to address these minor linguistic errors is recommended.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors report tuning hyperparameters using a development set, the extent of sensitivity to these hyperparameters is not discussed. Providing some analysis of how performance varies with different hyperparameter settings would be valuable.
4. **Limited Exploration of Entity-Specific Features:** While the incorporation of entity-specific features is presented, the experiment uses only a small subset of available features. Investigating the impact of a wider range of entity-specific features could further improve performance and provide insights into their relative importance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the challenges encountered when attempting to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014)?  Specifically, what modifications were attempted, and what led you to believe that the issue was not with your implementation?
2.  Did you perform any ablation studies to assess the contribution of the convolutional layers versus the entity grid representation? This would help to understand the relative importance of each component of the proposed architecture.
3.  What is the computational cost of training and running the proposed model compared to the traditional entity grid models?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a sanity check by attempting to reproduce the Li and Hovy results on their original datasets.
*   Document the challenges faced in running the Li and Hovy system in the final paper.
*   Address the minor linguistic issues (missing articles, awkward phrasing) through careful proofreading.
