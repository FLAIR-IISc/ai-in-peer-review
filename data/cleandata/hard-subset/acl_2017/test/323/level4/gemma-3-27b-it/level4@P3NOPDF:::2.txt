This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to model text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation. The motivation for this work is clear, and the paper is generally well-described. I particularly appreciated the authors’ discussion of the design choices, including the rationale for employing a CNN and the insightful section, “Why it Works.” 

The evaluation is extensive, spanning several coherence ranking tasks and demonstrating performance gains over existing methods. However, the inability to reproduce a prior result—specifically, the reported performance on Li and Hovy (2014)—is concerning and suggests a potential implementation or modeling error that requires investigation.

While the model represents a sensible “neuralization” of the entity grid framework, I found myself questioning a key design element: the use of 100-dimensional vectors to represent each grid entry (or slightly more in the extended grid configuration). The benefit of this high dimensionality isn’t immediately apparent; the paper would benefit from a justification for why this representation is superior to, for example, lower-dimensional embeddings or a different representation altogether. Furthermore, while directly optimizing for coherence ranking is a logical approach, the practical significance of varying the transition chain length from *k*=3 to *k*=6 sentences seems limited, especially given the brevity of many articles in the Wall Street Journal (WSJ) corpus.

The paper’s presentation could be streamlined. The writing is occasionally verbose, and certain concepts – particularly the introduction to entity grids – are repeated unnecessarily. Specifically, Section 6 currently duplicates material already presented in Section 2 related to prior work. I recommend consolidating the first two-thirds of Section 6 into Section 2 as a more comprehensive “Related Work” section. The remaining portion of Section 6 could then be incorporated into Section 2 as a dedicated subsection. This restructuring would improve the paper’s flow and readability.

Overall, this work presents a solid implementation of a neural network model applied to an entity-grid based coherence framework. However, given the comprehensive consolidation of previous work presented, I anticipated a more substantial contribution from a full paper. Potential avenues for further exploration include innovations in the feature representations (e.g., incorporating additional features beyond entity grids) or application to a broader range of coherence-related tasks.
