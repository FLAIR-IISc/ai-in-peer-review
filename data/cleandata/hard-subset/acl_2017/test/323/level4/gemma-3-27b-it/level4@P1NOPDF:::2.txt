The paper proposes a convolutional neural network approach to model text coherence. The model builds upon the well-established entity grid representation, augmenting it with a CNN. 

The approach is well motivated and clearly described. I particularly appreciated the detailed discussion of the design choices and the intuitions behind them, especially in the section titled 'Why it Works.’

The paper presents an extensive evaluation across several tasks, demonstrating that the proposed approach outperforms existing methods. However, the inability to reproduce a previous result is concerning; the results reported on Li and Hovy (2014) suggest a potential implementation or modeling error that should be addressed.

Despite this, the model represents a relatively straightforward ‘neuralization’ of the entity grid model. I am unclear about the necessity of using 100-dimensional vectors to represent each entry in the four-dimensional grid (or the slightly expanded grid). What benefit does this higher dimensionality provide? While I agree that directly optimizing for coherence ranking would likely improve the model, the impact of considering transition chains for up to *k*=3 sentences versus *k*=6 seems potentially marginal, particularly given that many articles in the WSJ corpus are relatively short.

The writing is somewhat verbose, and the paper repeats information in several places. For example, the introduction to entity grids is unnecessarily reiterated. Specifically, section 2 already covers related work, making the first two-thirds of section 6 redundant and suitable for deletion (or integration into section 2 where appropriate). The remainder of section 6 could be incorporated into section 2 as a subsection, prompting a renaming of section 2 to ‘Related Work.’

Overall, this appears to be a solid implementation of a neural network model applied to entity-grid-based coherence modeling. Given the consolidation of prior work presented, I would have expected a more substantial contribution from a full paper, such as innovations in the representations used (e.g., the inclusion of additional features) or exploration of new tasks.

Minor points:

- This paper would benefit from proofreading by a native English speaker. Numerous articles are missing, for instance, ‘the WSJ corpus’ (twice) and ‘the Brown… toolkit’ (twice).
- p.1, bottom left column: ‘Figure 2’ should be ‘Figure 1.’
- p.1: Replace ‘Firstly/Secondly’ with ‘First, Second.’
- p.1: Consider rephrasing ‘limits the model to’ as ‘prevents the model from considering…’
- Consider removing the standard concluding paragraph in section 1, as it feels unnecessary for a paper of this length.
