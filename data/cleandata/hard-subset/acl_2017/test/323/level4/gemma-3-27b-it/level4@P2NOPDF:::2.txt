This paper presents a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to modeling text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation. The motivation for this work is clear and well-articulated, and I particularly appreciated the authors’ insightful discussion of the design choices, notably the rationale for employing a CNN and the explanatory section “Why it Works.”

The evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating performance gains over previous state-of-the-art methods across several tasks. However, the inability to reproduce a prior result – specifically, the Li and Hovy (2014) findings – is concerning and suggests a potential implementation or modeling issue that requires investigation. 

While the model represents a straightforward application of neural networks to the entity grid framework, certain aspects warrant further clarification. The use of 100-dimensional vectors to represent each entry in the four-dimensional (or slightly expanded) grid is not intuitively justified. The authors should elaborate on how this higher dimensionality contributes to improved performance. Although directly optimizing for coherence ranking is a beneficial approach, the impact of varying the transition chain length from *k*=3 to *k*=6 sentences seems potentially limited, particularly given the frequent brevity of articles in the Wall Street Journal corpus.

The paper's writing would benefit from concision. There is noticeable repetition, particularly in the introduction to entity grids. Specifically, section 6 largely duplicates content already presented in section 2’s related work discussion; the first two-thirds of section 6 should be either removed or integrated into section 2, which could then be renamed “Related Work.” The remaining portion of section 6 would be more appropriately placed within section 2 as a dedicated subsection.

Overall, this is a solid implementation demonstrating the successful application of a neural network model to an entity-grid-based coherence system. Given the extensive consolidation of prior work presented, I anticipated a more substantial contribution from a full paper – perhaps through innovations in representation learning (e.g., incorporating additional features) or exploration of novel tasks.

**Minor Points:**

*   The paper would benefit from a review by a native English speaker. Numerous instances of missing articles were noted (e.g., “the WSJ corpus” (twice), “the Brown… toolkit” (twice)).
*   Page 1, bottom left column: “Figure 2” should be “Figure 1.”
*   Page 1: Replace “Firstly/Secondly” with “First, Second.”
*   Page 1: Consider rephrasing “limits the model to” as “prevents the model from considering…” for improved clarity.
*   The standard concluding paragraph in section 1 appears unnecessary given the paper's length and could be removed.
