The paper introduces a convolutional neural network to model text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation. This approach is well-justified and clearly articulated, with insightful explanations for key design choices, notably the rationale behind employing a CNN, as detailed in the ‘Why it works’ section.

The extensive evaluation across multiple tasks demonstrates performance improvements over existing methods. However, the inability to replicate a prior result raises a concern; the findings regarding Li and Hovy (2014) suggest a potential implementation or modeling issue that requires investigation. 

While the model represents a straightforward application of neural networks to the entity grid framework, the necessity of using 100-dimensional vectors to represent each grid entry (even with extensions) isn’t fully clear. The potential benefits of this dimensionality are not immediately apparent.  Directly optimizing for coherence ranking is a promising direction, though the practical impact of varying the transition chain length (k=3 vs. k=6) may be limited, particularly given the frequent brevity of articles in the WSJ corpus.

The paper’s length could be reduced through more concise writing. Certain sections contain repetitive information; specifically, the introduction to entity grids appears multiple times, and the first two-thirds of section 6 largely duplicates content already presented in section 2 and should be either removed or integrated into section 2, which could then be expanded into a comprehensive ‘Related Work’ section. The remaining portion of section 6 would be better suited as a subsection within this expanded section.

Overall, this work presents a sound implementation of a neural network applied to entity-grid coherence modeling.  Given the paper’s consolidation of previous work, a full paper submission could benefit from further innovation – perhaps exploring alternative representations or evaluating performance on additional tasks.
