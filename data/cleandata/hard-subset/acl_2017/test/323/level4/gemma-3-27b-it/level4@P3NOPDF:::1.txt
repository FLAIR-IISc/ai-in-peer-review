This paper presents an extension to the entity grid model, leveraging a convolutional neural network to learn sequences of entity transitions as indicators of coherence. This allows for better generalization to longer entity sequences than direct estimation of transition probabilities, as utilized in the original entity grid model. Overall, this is a well-written and clearly presented paper. The authors wisely choose to build upon existing work, employing a neural network to address specific limitations within a defined step of the process, rather than proposing a fully neural alternative. This is a reasonable and effective approach.

However, a more comprehensive comparison to existing neural coherence modeling approaches, specifically the model proposed by Li and Hovy, would strengthen the contribution. The authors acknowledge their surprise at the comparatively low performance of the Li and Hovy model in their experiments. This raises questions regarding potential discrepancies in experimental setup or corpus dependence. It would be beneficial to investigate whether the Li and Hovy model’s poor performance is specific to this corpus and task, and, if so, to report the performance of the presented model on datasets or tasks where the Li and Hovy model demonstrates stronger results. A deeper exploration of these factors would significantly bolster the paper’s argument.

The paper is generally fluent and highly readable. However, a careful proofread focusing on article usage is recommended. Definite articles appear to be frequently omitted (e.g., lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764, and likely in other instances). Furthermore, the recurring phrase “…limits the model to do X…” is somewhat awkward. Alternatives such as “limits the model’s ability to do X” or “prevents the model from doing X” may improve clarity.

**Final Recommendation (adjusted to 4 after considering author response):**

I agree with the authors that objective difficulties in reproducing results from external software should not be held against their work. However, the efforts made to implement and test the Li and Hovy system, along with the encountered challenges, should be explicitly documented within the paper. To further validate the results and address concerns regarding reproducibility, I recommend the authors attempt to reproduce the results reported by Li and Hovy on their original datasets as a sanity check – assuming this has not already been performed. This would provide valuable insight into the generalizability of both models and strengthen the overall validity of the findings presented in this paper.
