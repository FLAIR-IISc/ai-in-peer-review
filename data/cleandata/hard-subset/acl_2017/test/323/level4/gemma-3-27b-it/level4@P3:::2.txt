This paper proposes a convolutional neural network approach to model text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation. The motivation is clear, and the description of the approach is well-structured, with insightful explanations of design choices, particularly in the “Why it works” section. The extensive evaluation across multiple tasks demonstrates performance gains over previous methods. However, the inability to reproduce results from Li & Hovy (2014) raises a concern that warrants investigation.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of a convolutional neural network to learn coherence representations directly from entity grids, achieving state-of-the-art results on standard coherence evaluation tasks. This demonstrates the benefit of end-to-end training and distributed representations for capturing long-range entity transitions. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive empirical evaluation, which provides a strong baseline for future work in neural coherence modeling.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing methods on three different coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), achieving significant gains, especially on the insertion and summary coherence rating tasks. This empirical validation strongly supports the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Clear and Motivated Approach:** The paper presents a clear and well-motivated approach. The authors effectively articulate the limitations of traditional entity grid models and convincingly argue for the benefits of a neural network-based solution. The explanation of why a CNN is suitable for this task is particularly insightful.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The evaluation is thorough, utilizing established datasets and tasks.  The comparison against a range of baselines, including both traditional and more recent methods, provides a strong context for assessing the contribution of the proposed model.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors release their code, enhancing the reproducibility of their results and facilitating further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of a CNN to the entity grid framework yields strong results, the architecture itself is relatively straightforward. The paper doesn’t explore more complex neural architectures or novel ways to integrate entity-specific features beyond simple concatenation.  The advantage of using 100-dimensional embeddings for relatively small input spaces (e.g., four-dimensional grid entries) isn’t fully justified.
2. **Unreproduced Result & Potential Implementation Error:** The inability to reproduce the results of Li & Hovy (2014) is concerning and suggests a possible implementation issue or misunderstanding of their approach. This should be carefully addressed.
3. **Redundancy and Organization:** The paper suffers from some redundancy, particularly regarding the introduction to entity grids and related work.  Section 6 largely duplicates material presented in Section 2. A more streamlined organization, consolidating related work into Section 2, would improve clarity and conciseness.
4. **Writing Quality:** The writing could be improved with careful proofreading. Frequent missing articles (“the WSJ corpus,” “the Brown toolkit”) detract from the professional polish of the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the choice of 100-dimensional embeddings for representing grid entries, given the relatively small dimensionality of the input space?  Did you experiment with lower-dimensional embeddings, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Please address the discrepancy with the results reported by Li & Hovy (2014). Could you provide more details about your implementation and the settings used when attempting to reproduce their results?
3.  Could you comment on the computational cost of your model compared to traditional entity grid models?
