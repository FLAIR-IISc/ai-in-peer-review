- Proposes a neural local coherence model using CNNs over entity grids to capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features.
- Uses end-to-end pairwise ranking training to learn task-specific coherence features rather than hand-engineered ones.
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating tasks and releases code.
- Novel application of CNNs to entity grids enables distributed representations that capture nuanced and longer-range dependencies than discrete models.
- End-to-end training with pairwise ranking allows learning features directly optimized for coherence assessment, improving over previous feature-engineering approaches.
- Empirical gains are substantial, especially on summary coherence rating, showing consistent improvement across tasks.
- Comparison to other neural coherence models (e.g., Li and Hovy 2014) is limited and reproducing prior results proved difficult, leaving open why that model underperformed.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity and the impact of choices (filter sizes, embedding dimensions) are underreported; more ablation is needed.
- Paper contains several grammatical imprecisions and awkward phrasing that reduce clarity and require proofreading.
- Question: Please detail the difficulties and troubleshooting steps encountered when reproducing Li and Hovy (2014) and hypothesized reasons for the discrepancy.
- Question: Provide an ablation or sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with key hyperparameters like embedding dimension and filter size.
- Question: Have other features (e.g., syntactic features, discourse relations) been tried for integration, and what were the outcomes?
- Clear motivation and discussion of limitations of existing entity grid models strengthen the paper’s rationale.
- Figures effectively illustrate the model architecture and pairwise training; the code release enhances reproducibility.
- Recommend specifying statistical significance more precisely (report p-values) rather than using vague terms like “significantly superior.”
