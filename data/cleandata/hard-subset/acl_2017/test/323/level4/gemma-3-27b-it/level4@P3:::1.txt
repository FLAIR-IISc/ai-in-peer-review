This paper introduces a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on entity grids. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely, their use of discrete representations limiting the capture of long-range dependencies and the decoupling of feature computation from the target task – by leveraging distributed representations and end-to-end training. The primary contributions of this work are: (1) a neural architecture for modeling local coherence that captures long-range entity transitions and incorporates entity-specific features, and (2) a pairwise ranking training method for learning task-specific features. A third, supporting contribution is the strong empirical performance on three coherence assessment tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of CNNs to Entity Grid Modeling:** The use of convolutional neural networks to process entity grids is a sensible and effective approach. By moving beyond discrete representations and utilizing distributed embeddings, the model can potentially capture more nuanced and longer-range dependencies between entities than previous entity grid models. This provides a compelling way to overcome a key limitation of existing methods.
2. **End-to-End Training for Task-Specificity:** The end-to-end training approach, coupled with pairwise ranking, allows the model to learn features directly optimized for the coherence assessment task. This is a significant improvement over feature engineering methods used in earlier entity grid models where features were often defined independently of the task at hand. The results demonstrate that the model can effectively learn task-specific representations.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing entity grid models across three different evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), achieving state-of-the-art performance. The gains, particularly on the summary coherence rating task, are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The release of the code further supports the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Other Neural Coherence Models:** While the authors acknowledge and attempt to evaluate the Li and Hovy (2014) model, the difficulties encountered in reproducing their results raise concerns. The significantly lower performance of the Li and Hovy model on the presented datasets warrants further investigation. A more thorough comparison, even if it involves replicating the results on the original datasets, would strengthen the paper. The current discussion feels incomplete without a clearer understanding of *why* that model performed poorly.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Reporting:** While the authors detail their hyperparameter tuning process, the specific values chosen (Table 2) and their impact on performance aren’t deeply discussed. More insight into the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters would be helpful. For instance, a brief analysis of how performance changes with varying filter sizes or embedding dimensions would provide a better understanding of the model's behavior.
3. **Grammatical Imprecision:** The paper contains several instances of awkward phrasing and missing articles (as noted in the original review), which detract from the overall clarity. While not a major issue, a thorough proofread focusing on article usage and sentence structure would improve readability. Phrases like “limits the model to do X” are somewhat clunky and could be rephrased for better clarity.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific difficulties encountered when attempting to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014)? What steps were taken to troubleshoot the issue, and what potential reasons do you hypothesize for the discrepancy?
2.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of hyperparameter settings (e.g., embedding dimension, filter size)? Could you provide a brief ablation study demonstrating the impact of these parameters on the evaluation metrics?
3.  The paper focuses on incorporating entity-specific features. Have you explored other types of features (e.g., syntactic features, discourse relations) that could be integrated into the model, and what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the limitations of existing entity grid models is well-articulated and provides a clear motivation for the proposed work.
*   The figures are helpful in visualizing the model architecture and the pairwise training process.
*   The code release is a valuable contribution that will facilitate further research in this area.
*   Consider using more precise language regarding statistical significance. Instead of "significantly superior", specify the p-value threshold used.
