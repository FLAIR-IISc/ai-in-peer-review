- Proposes a CNN applied to the entity grid to learn representations, capture longer-range entity transitions, and incorporate entity-specific features.  
- Main contribution: neuralized entity grid achieving state-of-the-art on coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating).  
- Secondary contribution: end-to-end training that learns task-specific features and public code release for reproducibility.  
- Strong empirical results: consistent improvements (up to ~4% discrimination, 2.5% insertion, >4% summary) across multiple tasks.  
- Clear and intuitive explanations, including a useful “why it works” section explaining model mechanisms.  
- Thorough comparison against well-established baselines, including traditional grids and Li & Hovy (2014).  
- Practical contribution: code release facilitates reproducibility and follow-up research.  
- Weakness: limited core novelty—primarily a straightforward application of CNNs to an existing entity grid framework.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification for using 100-dimensional embeddings and lack of ablation on embedding size.  
- Weakness: redundant presentation of related work across sections, weakening the paper’s conciseness.  
- Weakness: inability to reproduce Li & Hovy (2014) results suggests a possible implementation error and raises experimental reliability concerns.  
- Question: provide ablation/analysis showing impact of embedding dimensionality on performance.  
- Question: report investigations into the discrepancy with Li & Hovy (2014) and any findings.  
- Question: give concrete examples where long-range context (k=6) improves performance versus smaller k.  
- Additional: paper needs careful proofreading for grammar and articles (e.g., “the WSJ corpus”).  
- Additional: streamline Section 1’s introductory paragraph to reduce redundancy.  
- Additional: consider rephrasing “limits the model to” to “prevents the model from considering.”  
- Additional: clarify motivation for specific hyperparameters (embedding size, filter size).  
- Additional: make future work discussion more specific and suggest potential applications beyond evaluated tasks.
