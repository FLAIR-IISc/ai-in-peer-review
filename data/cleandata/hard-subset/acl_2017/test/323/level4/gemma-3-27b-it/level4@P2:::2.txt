This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to model text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation. The core idea is to replace traditional feature engineering with a CNN that learns representations directly from the entity grid, enabling the model to capture longer-range entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features. The approach is well-motivated and clearly described, with thoughtful explanations of design choices. The extensive evaluation across several tasks demonstrates improvements over existing methods.

The primary contribution of this work is a successful neural network-based extension of the entity grid model that achieves state-of-the-art results on standard coherence evaluation tasks. This is demonstrated through consistent performance gains on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating. A secondary contribution lies in the end-to-end training methodology, which allows the model to learn task-specific features directly from the data. Finally, the release of the code facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods across three different evaluation tasks. The reported gains (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary coherence rating) are substantial and suggest a meaningful improvement in coherence modeling. The comprehensive evaluation strengthens the validity of the findings.
2. **Clear and Intuitive Explanation:** The paper provides a clear explanation of the motivation behind the proposed approach and the rationale for key design decisions, such as the use of a CNN. The “Why it works” section is particularly insightful, offering a good understanding of the model's internal mechanisms.
3. **Well-Established Baseline & Thorough Comparison:** The authors compare their approach against a variety of relevant baselines, including both traditional entity grid models and a more recent neural model (Li & Hovy, 2014). This thorough comparison positions the work effectively within the existing literature.
4. **Practical Contribution: Code Release:** The release of the code is a valuable contribution, promoting reproducibility and enabling other researchers to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Architecture:** While the results are strong, the proposed model can be viewed as a relatively straightforward application of CNNs to an existing framework (the entity grid). The core architectural innovation appears modest; it’s primarily a “neuralization” of the established approach.
2. **Justification for Dimensionality:** The paper does not fully justify the use of 100-dimensional embedding vectors for representing grid entries, particularly given the relatively small dimensionality of the underlying grid features. The benefit of this increased dimensionality in capturing subtle coherence patterns is not convincingly demonstrated.
3. **Redundant Presentation of Related Work:** Section 2 and the initial portions of Section 6 overlap significantly, presenting the same information in different places. This redundancy weakens the overall presentation and makes the paper feel longer than necessary. The repetitive introduction to entity grids should be consolidated.
4. **Unreplicated Result & Potential Implementation Error:** The authors acknowledge their inability to reproduce the results of Li & Hovy (2014), suggesting a potential implementation error. This raises concerns about the reliability of the experimental setup and should be investigated further.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dimensionality Analysis:** Could you provide some ablation studies or analysis showing the impact of the embedding dimensionality on performance? Specifically, how do results change as the embedding size is reduced?
2. **Li & Hovy (2014) Reproduction:** Have you investigated the cause of the discrepancy with the results reported by Li & Hovy (2014)? If so, could you elaborate on your findings?
3. **Long-Range Dependency Capture:** While the CNN allows for capturing longer-range transitions (k=6), can you provide examples of specific cases where this capability demonstrably improves performance compared to models with a smaller k?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a careful proofreading to address minor grammatical errors and missing articles (e.g., "the WSJ corpus").
*   Consider streamlining the introductory paragraph in Section 1.
*   The phrase “limits the model to” could be replaced with “prevents the model from considering”.
*   Please clarify what motivated the choice of specific hyperparameters (embedding size, filter size, etc.).
*   The discussion of future work could be more specific; for example, mentioning potential applications beyond the evaluated tasks.
