This paper introduces a convolutional neural network approach to model text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation. The motivation for this work is clear, and the paper is well-described; I particularly appreciated the insightful discussion of design choices, such as the rationale for employing a CNN and the explanatory section titled 'Why it works'. 

The model is extensively evaluated across multiple tasks, demonstrating performance gains over existing methods. However, the inability to reproduce a previous result is concerning; the findings on Li/Hovy (2014) suggest a potential implementation or modeling flaw that requires investigation.

Despite these strengths, the model represents a relatively straightforward application of neural networks to the entity grid framework. The use of 100-dimensional vectors to represent each entry in the four-dimensional grid (or the slightly expanded grid) is unclear. The benefit of this dimensionality is not readily apparent. While optimizing directly for coherence ranking is a sensible approach to model learning, the impact of increasing the transition chain length from *k*=3 to *k*=6 sentences seems potentially limited, particularly given the often-short length of articles in the WSJ corpus.

The paper’s length could be reduced through more concise writing. There is noticeable repetition, especially in the introduction to entity grids. Specifically, section 6 largely duplicates material already presented in section 2 and related work; the first two-thirds of section 6 should be either removed or integrated into section 2, which could then be expanded and renamed “Related Work.” The remaining portion of section 6 could be incorporated into section 2 as a subsection.

Overall, this work presents a solid implementation of a neural network model applied to entity-grid-based coherence modeling. However, given the consolidation of prior work, a full paper would ideally offer more substantial contributions, such as innovations in representation learning (e.g., incorporating additional features) or exploration of new tasks.
