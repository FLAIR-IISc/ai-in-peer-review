1. Paper proposes a convolutional neural coherence model over the entity grid with three contributions: neural architecture for long-range entity transitions, end-to-end pairwise ranking training, and state-of-the-art results on three tasks.

2. Clear and well-motivated argument that distributed representations overcome the curse of dimensionality in prior discrete entity-grid models.

3. Comprehensive and convincing experimental evaluation showing consistent improvements: +4% discrimination, +2.5% insertion, >4% summary coherence rating versus non-neural baselines.

4. Thoughtful explanation of design choices, explaining how convolutional filters detect transition patterns and max-pooling captures salient features.

5. Significant redundancy in describing the entity grid across multiple sections, weakening conciseness and impact.

6. Model appears as a straightforward neuralization of prior entity-grid approaches with limited innovation in representation or learning.

7. Unclear justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent grammatical roles that have only 4–5 discrete values.

8. Claimed benefit of capturing longer transitions (k=6 vs. k=3) is questionable given average WSJ document length (~22 sentences) and potential lack of sufficient transitions.

9. Failure to reproduce Li and Hovy (2014) results raises concerns about implementation, dataset differences, or applicability and needs clarification.

10. Question: Why are 100-dimensional grammatical-role vectors necessary and how do they improve over simpler representations?

11. Question: Was the inability to reproduce Li & Hovy (2014) due to implementation, dataset, or model limitations?

12. Question: How does the model handle shorter documents where long transition patterns (k=6) may not occur, and does this affect performance?

13. Minor issues: missing articles in text, prefer "First/Second" to "Firstly/Secondly," and incorrect figure reference ("Figure 2" should be "Figure 1").
