

Summary: The paper introduces a convolutional neural network (CNN) framework for modeling text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The primary contribution is a neural architecture that replaces discrete transition probability computation with distributed representations, enabling modeling of longer entity transitions (k=6 vs. traditional k≤3) while incorporating entity-specific features. The secondary contribution is an end-to-end pairwise ranking training method that learns task-specific features without decoupling feature extraction from downstream tasks. The tertiary contribution is state-of-the-art results across three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating).

Strengths:  
1. The technical justification for using CNNs to model entity transitions is exceptionally clear and well-motivated. The "Why it works" section (Section 3.1) effectively explains how convolutional filters detect transition patterns (e.g., 'SS-O-X') and how max-pooling captures salient features, addressing a key limitation of prior grid models.  
2. The evaluation is comprehensive and well-structured, demonstrating consistent improvements over non-neural baselines across all three tasks: +4.0% discrimination, +2.5% insertion, and +4.0% summary coherence rating. The use of multiple datasets (WSJ, AIRPLANES, EARTHQUAKES) and rigorous statistical testing (p<0.01) strengthens the claims.  
3. The end-to-end pairwise training approach (Section 3.2) effectively addresses the task-agnostic feature extraction problem in prior work. By sharing parameters across document pairs during training, the model learns task-specific features without manual feature engineering, as evidenced by the Extended Grid-CNN outperforming its non-neural counterpart by 2.5% on insertion.

Weaknesses:  
1. The failure to reproduce Li & Hovy (2014) results (19.34% discrimination accuracy) is unexplained and undermines confidence in the experimental setup. The authors must clarify whether this stems from dataset differences, implementation errors, or model limitations.  
2. The necessity of 100-dimensional embeddings for discrete grammatical roles (S/O/X/-) lacks justification. Given that the entity grid uses only 4–7 symbols (including entity features), the high dimensionality appears excessive and requires clarification on how it prevents overfitting.  
3. The claim of modeling "long-range" transitions (k=6) is questionable for the WSJ corpus (average 21.5 sentences/article), where k=6 covers only 28% of the document. The paper should address whether this length is sufficient for typical texts or if longer transitions are feasible.  
4. The paper exhibits significant repetition (e.g., entity grid description in Sections 1, 2, and 6), with Section 6 largely duplicating Section 2. This undermines conciseness and should be streamlined.

Questions to Authors:  
1. Please clarify the discrepancy in reproducing Li & Hovy (2014) results. Was the implementation identical to their public code, and if not, what modifications were made?  
2. Justify the choice of 100-dimensional embeddings for discrete grammatical roles. How does this prevent overfitting compared to lower-dimensional representations (e.g., 50D)?  
3. Given that WSJ articles average 21.5 sentences, why is k=6 sufficient for "long-range" modeling? What evidence supports that longer transitions (k>8) would not yield further gains?
