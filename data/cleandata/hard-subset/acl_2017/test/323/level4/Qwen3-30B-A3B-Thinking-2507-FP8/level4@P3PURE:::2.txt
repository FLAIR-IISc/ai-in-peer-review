

Review:  
The paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to model text coherence, building upon the well-established entity grid representation. The model is well motivated and clearly described, with particular appreciation for the intuitive explanations of key design choices (e.g., the rationale for using CNNs and the section titled "Why it works").  

The evaluation is extensive and demonstrates that the proposed method outperforms prior approaches across multiple tasks. However, it is noteworthy that one prior result (Li & Hovy, 2014) could not be reproduced, suggesting a potential implementation or modeling error that requires resolution.  

The approach constitutes a relatively straightforward "neuralization" of the entity grid model. The necessity of 100-dimensional vectors for representing four-dimensional grid entries (or slightly more in the extended grid) is unclear, and the rationale for this choice is not sufficiently justified. While direct optimization for coherence ranking may yield benefits, the difference between transition chains for up to k=3 versus k=6 sentences appears limited, especially given that many WSJ documents are short.  

The writing is somewhat verbose, with redundant content across sections. Specifically, the introduction to entity grids is repeated in both the introduction and Section 2 (which already covers related work), rendering the first two-thirds of Section 6 redundant. These sections should be removed and integrated into Section 2 (renaming Section 2 as "Related Work" to accommodate the content). The remaining material in Section 6 should be incorporated into Section 2 under a new subsection.  

Overall, the paper presents a solid application of neural networks to entity-grid-based coherence modeling. However, given the proposed consolidation of prior work, a full paper should ideally include more substantial innovations—such as novel representations (e.g., additional features) or expanded task coverage—to justify its contribution.  

Minor points:  
- The paper would benefit from proofreading by a native speaker; articles are missing in multiple instances (e.g., "the WSJ corpus" [2x], "the Brown toolkit" [2x]).  
- Page 1, bottom left column: "Figure 2" → "Figure 1".  
- Page 1: "Firstly/Secondly" → "First, Second".  
- Page 1: "limits the model to" → "prevents the model from considering ...".  
- Consider omitting the "standard" concluding paragraph in Section 1, as it is unnecessary for a concise paper.
