

The paper proposes a convolutional neural network approach to model text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation with a CNN. The motivation and description are clear, particularly the intuitive explanations for key design choices (e.g., the use of CNN and the section titled 'Why it works').  

The evaluation is extensive and demonstrates superior performance over prior methods. However, it is concerning that the results for Li/Hovy (2014) could not be reproduced, suggesting a potential implementation or modeling error that requires resolution.  

The model represents a relatively simple 'neuralization' of the entity grid approach. The necessity of 100-dimensional vectors for representing four-dimensional grid entries (or slightly more in the extended grid) is unclear and requires justification. While optimizing directly for coherence ranking is a valid improvement, the difference between transition chains for up to k=3 versus k=6 sentences may not be substantial, especially given the brevity of many WSJ articles.  

The writing is somewhat lengthy, with redundant content across sections. Specifically, the introduction to entity grids is repeated, and Section 6’s first two-thirds largely reiterate content that should be integrated into Section 2 (which covers related work). The remainder of Section 6 should be incorporated into Section 2 under a subsection, and Section 2 should be renamed to 'Related Work'.  

Overall, this presents a robust application of neural networks to entity-grid-based coherence modeling. However, given the consolidation of prior work, a full paper would benefit from deeper innovations, such as novel representations (e.g., additional features) or expanded task coverage.
