

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for local coherence assessment, addressing key limitations of prior approaches. The primary contribution is a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that operates over distributed entity representations to model long-range entity transitions, overcoming the "curse of dimensionality" in traditional entity grid models. A secondary contribution is a pairwise ranking training method that enables end-to-end learning of task-specific features. The third contribution is comprehensive evaluation across three coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), demonstrating state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths**  
1. **Effective Integration of Neural Methods with Existing Framework**: The authors successfully build upon the well-established entity grid model rather than proposing a completely new approach. By using a CNN to learn entity transitions, they address the key limitation of discrete representations in prior work, enabling modeling of longer transitions (up to 8 entities) without overfitting. This approach strikes a good balance between innovation and practicality.

2. **Significant Performance Gains**: The model achieves substantial improvements over non-neural baselines across all evaluation tasks (4% absolute gain on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating). These gains are statistically significant (p<0.01), demonstrating the effectiveness of their neural approach.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors evaluate on three established coherence tasks with appropriate datasets (WSJ for sentence ordering, DUC'03 for summary coherence), providing a thorough assessment of their model's capabilities. Their choice of WSJ corpus (larger and more diverse than AIRPLANES/ EARTHQUAKES) is well-justified and strengthens the validity of their results.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Insufficient Comparison with Li and Hovy (2014)**: The authors acknowledge their surprise at Li and Hovy's poor performance on their dataset (19.34% accuracy in discrimination), but fail to investigate why. This omission is significant since Li and Hovy's model is a direct neural approach to coherence assessment. The authors should clarify whether the poor performance stems from dataset differences, implementation errors, or model limitations.

2. **Lack of Analysis on Li and Hovy's Failure**: The paper does not explore whether the Li and Hovy model's poor performance is corpus-dependent or task-specific. The authors should address whether their approach would outperform Li and Hovy on datasets where it performs better (e.g., the original datasets used by Li and Hovy), or whether their neural architecture fundamentally addresses limitations in that model.

3. **Grammatical Issues and Unusual Phrasing**: The paper contains multiple grammatical errors, particularly missing articles (e.g., "limits the model to do X" instead of "limits the model's capacity to do X"). These issues reduce readability and professionalism. A thorough proofread focusing on article usage would significantly improve the paper.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Could you provide more details on why Li and Hovy's model performed so poorly on your WSJ dataset (19.34% accuracy)? Was this due to dataset characteristics, implementation issues, or model limitations?

2. Have you attempted to reproduce Li and Hovy's results on their original datasets (e.g., the AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES corpora)? If so, what results did you obtain, and how do they compare to your model?

3. Given that the Li and Hovy model uses a window-based approach (L=3 sentences) while your model handles longer transitions, could you elaborate on whether your approach would be more effective for tasks requiring understanding of longer discourse structures?

**Additional Comments**  
The paper is well-structured and clearly written overall. The choice to neuralize the entity grid model rather than proposing a fully neural approach is a strength, as it leverages established insights while addressing specific limitations. However, the grammatical issues noted above should be addressed before publication. The authors' efforts to test Li and Hovy's system should be documented in the paper to address concerns about objective difficulties in reproducing results.

**Final Recommendation**  
I recommend acceptance (3.5/5). The paper makes a solid contribution to the coherence modeling field with a well-executed neural extension of the entity grid model. While the comparison with Li and Hovy (2014) is insufficiently explored, this is a minor weakness given the significant performance gains demonstrated. The grammatical issues are easily fixable, and the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses.
