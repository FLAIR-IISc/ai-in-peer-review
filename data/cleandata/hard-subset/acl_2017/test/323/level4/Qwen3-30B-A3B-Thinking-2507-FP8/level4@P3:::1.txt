

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces a neural extension of the entity grid model for local coherence assessment. The primary contribution is a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that operates over the entity grid representation, capturing long-range entity transitions through distributed representations. This overcomes the "curse of dimensionality" in traditional models that rely on discrete transition probabilities. The second contribution is an end-to-end pairwise ranking training method that enables the model to learn task-specific features automatically. The third contribution is the demonstration of state-of-the-art performance across three coherence assessment tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—with consistent improvements over prior entity grid models (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence).

**Strengths**  
1. **Effective solution to a well-known limitation**: The paper successfully addresses the key limitation of traditional entity grid models—their inability to model long-range transitions due to discrete representation and sparse probability estimation. By using distributed representations and convolutional layers, the model handles transitions of length 5-8 (vs. k ≤ 3 in prior work) without overfitting. This is a significant technical contribution that directly enables the model's improved performance.

2. **Practical end-to-end training approach**: The pairwise ranking method allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering. This is a meaningful improvement over prior work where feature extraction was decoupled from the target task. The authors demonstrate this through consistent gains across all three evaluation tasks, showing the method's robustness.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on all three tasks, with statistically significant improvements over existing entity grid models. The gains are not marginal (e.g., 4% absolute improvement on discrimination), and the authors provide careful analysis of why insertion gains are smaller than discrimination gains (due to the task's inherent difficulty).

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Incomplete comparison with Li and Hovy (2014)**: The authors note that the Li and Hovy neural coherence model performed poorly on their dataset (19.34% accuracy in discrimination), but they do not investigate why. This is a major weakness because: (a) it leaves readers wondering whether the issue is with the Li and Hovy model, the authors' implementation, or corpus-specific factors; and (b) it weakens the claim that their model is a "neural" approach when a prior neural model was not properly evaluated. The authors should either reproduce Li and Hovy's results on their own data or provide a thorough analysis of the discrepancy.

2. **Insufficient explanation of model differences**: The paper contrasts their approach with Li and Hovy (2014) but does not clearly articulate how their convolutional grid-based architecture differs from Li and Hovy's window-based sentence representation approach. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

3. **Minor grammatical issues**: The paper contains several grammatical errors, particularly missing definite articles (e.g., "limits the model to do X" instead of "limits the model's capacity to do X") and awkward phrasing. While these are not major, they detract from the paper's professionalism and readability.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Could you provide more details about your implementation of the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Specifically, did you use the same hyperparameters as the original authors, or did you tune them for your dataset? If you tuned them, how did that affect the results?

2. The authors mention that the Li and Hovy model performed poorly on their dataset but note they "were not sure what could go wrong." Given that the Li and Hovy model was designed for coherence assessment, could you speculate on why it might be less effective on the WSJ corpus compared to other datasets (e.g., AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES)?

3. The paper states that the insertion task is "much more difficult" than discrimination. Could you elaborate on how the model's performance on insertion might be improved through task-specific training, given that the current model was trained for discrimination?
