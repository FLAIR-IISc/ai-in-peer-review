

The paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) to model text coherence, building on the established entity grid representation. The approach is well-motivated and clearly explained, with particularly valuable discussion of design intuitions (e.g., the rationale for using a CNN and the section titled "Why it works").  

The evaluation across multiple tasks demonstrates strong performance, outperforming prior methods. However, it is concerning that the authors could not reproduce a key result from Li and Hovy (2014), suggesting a potential implementation or modeling error that requires clarification.  

The model itself represents a relatively straightforward neural adaptation of the entity grid framework. The necessity of 100-dimensional vectors for representing four-dimensional grid entries (or slightly more in the extended grid) is unclear—how do these high-dimensional vectors contribute meaningfully? While optimizing directly for coherence ranking is a sensible design choice, the practical impact of extending transition chains from k=3 to k=6 sentences appears limited, especially given that many WSJ articles are short.  

The paper suffers from redundancy, particularly in Sections 2 and 6. Section 2 already covers related work, making the first two-thirds of Section 6 repetitive. These sections should be consolidated: the redundant material in Section 6 should be integrated into Section 2 (which should then be renamed "Related Work"), and the remaining content of Section 6 should be added as a subsection under "Related Work." The current structure unnecessarily repeats foundational concepts, such as the entity grid introduction.  

Overall, this is a solid implementation of a neural approach to entity-grid-based coherence modeling. However, given the proposed consolidation of prior work, a full paper should offer more substantial innovations—such as novel representations (e.g., additional features) or expanded task coverage—to justify the scope.  

**Minor points:**

- Page 1, bottom left: "Figure 2" → "Figure 1".
