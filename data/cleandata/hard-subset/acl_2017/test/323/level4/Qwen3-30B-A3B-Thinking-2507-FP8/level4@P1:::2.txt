

**Summary of Contributions**

The paper introduces a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to model text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The primary contribution is a neural architecture that captures long-range entity transitions while incorporating entity-specific features through distributed representations. A secondary contribution is an end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking to learn task-specific coherence features.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a well-motivated solution to key limitations of existing entity grid models. The authors clearly articulate how their CNN-based approach overcomes the curse of dimensionality problem, enabling modeling of longer entity transitions (k=5-8) without overfitting. This is a significant technical improvement over previous models that are limited to k≤3.

2. The evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates substantial improvements across three distinct coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results with absolute gains of approximately 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating compared to previous approaches.

3. The paper offers clear and insightful explanations for key design decisions, particularly regarding the use of convolutional networks. The "Why it works" section effectively demonstrates how convolutional filters detect specific transition patterns (e.g., 'SS-O-X' for coherent text), enhancing the paper's technical contribution and readability.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper reports an accuracy of only 19.34% for the Li and Hovy (2014) model on the WSJ dataset, which is substantially lower than expected and contradicts their original results. This discrepancy requires clarification, as it suggests either an implementation error or a fundamental issue with adapting their approach to this dataset.

2. The justification for using 100-dimensional vector representations for grid entries (which have a vocabulary size of 4-6) is insufficient. Given that the basic entity grid uses discrete representations with a small number of possible values (S, O, X, -), it's unclear how high-dimensional distributed representations provide meaningful advantages, especially considering the relatively short average document length (22.3 sentences) in the WSJ corpus.

3. The paper contains significant redundancy, particularly between Section 2 (Entity Grid and Its Extensions) and Section 6 (Related Work). Approximately two-thirds of Section 6 merely repeats information already presented in Section 2. This redundancy should be eliminated, and relevant related work should be integrated into Section 2.

**Questions to Authors**

1. The paper reports an accuracy of 19.34% for the Li and Hovy (2014) model on your WSJ dataset, which is substantially lower than their reported results. Could you clarify why your implementation performs so poorly on this dataset?

2. Why are 100-dimensional embedding vectors necessary for grid entries with a small vocabulary size (4-6)? What specific advantages do these high-dimensional representations provide that justify the increased model complexity?

3. Given that the WSJ corpus has an average of 22.3 sentences per document, how does the model ensure sufficient context for longer transitions (k=5-8) in many documents, particularly considering that the longest transitions may exceed the document length?

**Additional Comments**

- The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to address missing articles (e.g., "the WSJ corpus" instead of "WSJ corpus" and "the Brown coherence toolkit" instead of "Brown coherence toolkit").

- The reference to "Figure 2" on page 1 should be corrected to "Figure 1" as noted in the draft.

- Section 2.1's limitations should use consistent formatting (e.g., "First," "Second," instead of "Firstly," "Secondly").

- The concluding paragraph in Section 1 is unnecessary and should be removed to improve conciseness.

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution that effectively applies neural network techniques to coherence modeling. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the reproducibility concerns, better justifying key design choices, and eliminating redundant content.
