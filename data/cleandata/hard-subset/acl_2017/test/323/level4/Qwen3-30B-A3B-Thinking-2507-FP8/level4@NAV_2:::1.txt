

Summary: The paper introduces a neural architecture extending the entity grid model for coherence assessment, addressing two key limitations: discrete representation constraints on transition length and task-agnostic feature extraction. The primary contribution is a convolutional neural network that processes entity grids to model long-range transitions via distributed representations, enabling end-to-end learning of task-specific features. Secondary contributions include state-of-the-art results across three coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) and a clear empirical validation demonstrating consistent improvements over prior entity grid models.

Strengths:
1. The neural architecture effectively overcomes the discrete representation limitation of existing entity grid models, enabling modeling of longer entity transitions (up to k=8) without the curse of dimensionality, as evidenced by 4% absolute gains in discrimination and 2.5% in insertion tasks.
2. The end-to-end pairwise training approach successfully learns task-specific features, as shown by the Extended Grid-CNN model outperforming its non-neural counterpart by 2.5% on insertion and 3.7% on summary coherence rating, even when incorporating only a subset of entity-specific features.
3. The thorough experimental evaluation across three distinct tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) with proper baselines and statistical validation provides robust evidence of the model's superiority, establishing a new state of the art.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison with the recent neural coherence model by Li and Hovy (2014) is incomplete and lacks critical analysis. The authors report unexpectedly poor results (19.34% accuracy) for this model on their WSJ dataset but fail to investigate whether this reflects corpus-specific issues, implementation errors, or fundamental model limitations, weakening the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.
2. The paper contains multiple minor language issues, including missing definite articles (e.g., "the", "a") in key technical descriptions (lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674) and inconsistent phrasing like "limits the model to do X" instead of "limits the model's capacity to do X", which reduces readability.
3. The rationale for excluding the Li and Hovy model from results is insufficient; the authors should either reproduce their results on the original datasets to verify performance or provide a detailed analysis of why their implementation failed, as this directly impacts the novelty claim.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify whether the poor performance of the Li and Hovy (2014) model on your WSJ dataset is due to corpus-specific issues, implementation errors, or inherent limitations of their approach? Please attempt to reproduce their results on their original datasets as a sanity check.
2. Given that your Extended Grid-CNN achieves 25.95% accuracy on insertion (Table 3), how would you expect this model to perform on the AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES datasets used in prior work (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008; Li and Hovy, 2014), where the Li and Hovy model reportedly performed better?
3. The paper states that "the model that is trained to distinguish an original document from its random permutation may learn features that are not specific enough to distinguish documents when only one sentence differs." Could you elaborate on the specific feature limitations that cause this insertion performance gap?
