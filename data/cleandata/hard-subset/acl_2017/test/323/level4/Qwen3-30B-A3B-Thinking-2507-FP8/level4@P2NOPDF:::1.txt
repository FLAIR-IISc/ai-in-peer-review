

The paper introduces a well-motivated extension to the entity grid model, employing a convolutional neural network to learn sequences of entity transitions that capture coherence. This approach enables better generalization over longer entity sequences compared to the original model's direct transition probability estimates. The work is clearly written and demonstrates a sound methodological choice: rather than proposing a fully neural solution, the authors strategically integrate a neural component to address specific limitations in an existing framework. This incremental approach is valid and practical.

However, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with the neural coherence model by Li and Hovy (2014). The authors note their surprise at the Li and Hovy model's unexpectedly low performance on their task, which raises critical questions. Was the low score due to an experimental error, corpus-specific limitations, or a fundamental mismatch with the task? Crucially, the paper does not explore whether the proposed model would achieve stronger results on corpora where Li and Hovy's model performs better. A deeper analysis of these factors—such as testing the Li and Hovy model on the authors' data or vice versa—would significantly strengthen the argument for the proposed approach and its relative advantages.

The paper is generally fluent and readable, but requires attention to grammatical details. Multiple instances of missing definite articles appear (e.g., lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764), which should be corrected during proofreading. Additionally, the phrase "...limits the model to do X..." is used repeatedly and sounds unnatural. Replacing this with standard phrasing such as "limits the model's ability to do X" or "prevents the model from doing X" would improve clarity and academic tone.

Regarding the author response: I adjust my recommendation to a 4 (weak acceptance) based on their explanation. I acknowledge that practical challenges in reproducing prior work (e.g., software dependencies) are not grounds for criticism, as the authors documented their efforts and encountered genuine difficulties. To further solidify their claims, I strongly recommend that the authors attempt to reproduce Li and Hovy’s results on their original datasets as a sanity check—unless this has already been completed. Including this verification in the paper would address the lingering questions about the baseline model's performance and enhance the study's rigor. The current comparison lacks this critical validation step, which is essential for a fair evaluation of the proposed method against the state of the art.
