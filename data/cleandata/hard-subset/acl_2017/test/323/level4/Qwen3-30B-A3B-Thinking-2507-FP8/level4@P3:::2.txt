

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that operates over the entity grid representation of text using a convolutional neural network (CNN). The authors identify two key limitations in existing entity grid models: (1) their discrete representation of grammatical roles and features leads to the curse of dimensionality, limiting transition modeling to short ranges (k ≤ 3), and (2) their task-agnostic feature extraction prevents learning of task-specific features. The primary contributions are: (1) a neural architecture that captures long-range entity transitions through distributed representations (enabling k=5–8 transitions), (2) a pairwise ranking training method that learns task-specific features end-to-end, and (3) state-of-the-art results across three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating).

**Strengths**  
1. **Clear problem identification and motivation**: The paper effectively articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models, particularly the curse of dimensionality and task-agnostic feature extraction, providing a compelling rationale for their neural approach. The discussion of how distributed representations overcome these limitations is particularly strong.

2. **Thorough evaluation with consistent improvements**: The model achieves significant gains over prior work across all three tasks (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating). The ablation studies (e.g., Grid-CNN vs. Extended Grid-CNN) convincingly demonstrate the value of both distributed representations and entity-specific features. The use of multiple evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence) strengthens the validity of the results.

3. **Well-justified design choices**: The authors provide clear explanations for key design decisions, such as why CNNs are appropriate for modeling entity transitions (addressing locality and position-invariance) and why pairwise ranking is preferable to classification for coherence assessment. The "Why it works" section (Section 3.2) effectively explains the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Failure to reproduce prior work**: The paper claims to outperform Li and Hovy (2014) but reports "very disappointing" results (19.34% accuracy in discrimination) for this model on their dataset. The authors exclude this model from results without clarifying why it failed to reproduce, which undermines the paper's claim of comprehensive comparison. This requires clarification from the authors.

2. **Unjustified high-dimensional embeddings**: The use of 100-dimensional vectors for grid entries (e.g., S, O, X, -) seems unnecessarily high-dimensional given the limited number of discrete categories. The paper fails to justify why such high dimensionality is required, especially when the authors acknowledge the model can work with fewer entity-specific features. This appears to be an implementation detail without clear theoretical justification.

3. **Marginal gains from extended transition lengths**: While the paper claims k=5–8 transitions are possible, the average document length in the WSJ corpus is 21.5 sentences (Table 1), meaning k=6 transitions cover only ~30% of the document. The marginal gains in insertion (only 1% improvement over Grid-CNN) suggest the extended transitions may not be as impactful as claimed, particularly for shorter documents.

4. **Significant repetition**: Sections 2 and 6 redundantly introduce entity grid models and related work. Section 2 describes the entity grid and its extensions, while Section 6 repeats this content (with minor additions) as "Related Work." This repetition weakens the paper's flow and should be consolidated.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper states that Li and Hovy (2014) "produced very disappointing results" on the WSJ dataset. Could the authors clarify why this model failed to reproduce their reported results (19.34% accuracy in discrimination) and whether this was due to implementation differences or dataset-specific issues?

2. The authors use 100-dimensional embeddings for grid entries. Given that the entity grid has only 4–7 discrete categories (S, O, X, -, plus entity-specific features), why is such high dimensionality necessary? Could the authors provide evidence that lower-dimensional embeddings would not suffice?

3. The improvement from k=3 to k=6 transitions is marginal in the insertion task (1% vs. 2.5% gains). Given that many WSJ articles are relatively short (average 21.5 sentences), how meaningful are transitions of length 6 in practice? Could the authors clarify the practical significance of these longer transitions?

**Additional Comments**  
- The paper would benefit from tighter editing to eliminate redundancy (e.g., removing the repeated entity grid introduction in Section 6).
- The writing contains several grammatical issues (e.g., missing articles: "the WSJ corpus," "the Brown toolkit"), which should be addressed in the final version.
- The claim that "our model achieves state of the art results" is supported by strong results, but the exclusion of Li and Hovy (2014) without explanation weakens this claim.
