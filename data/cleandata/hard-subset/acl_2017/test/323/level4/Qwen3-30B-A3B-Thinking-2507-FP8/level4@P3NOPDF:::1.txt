

The paper proposes a meaningful extension to the entity grid model by incorporating a convolutional neural network (CNN) to learn entity transition sequences, thereby improving coherence modeling for longer entity sequences compared to the original model's direct transition probability estimates. The approach is well-motivated and elegantly bridges traditional modeling with neural enhancements, avoiding a full neural overhaul while addressing specific limitations. The writing is generally clear and fluent, though minor grammatical issues require attention.

The primary strength of the work lies in its practicality: the authors identify a concrete limitation (inability to generalize transitions beyond short sequences) and deploy a targeted neural component to resolve it. However, the evaluation would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison with Li and Hovy's neural coherence model (2016), which the authors note achieved unexpectedly low performance on their task. While the authors acknowledge their surprise at this result, the paper does not adequately address whether this reflects a methodological issue, corpus-specific sensitivity, or model limitations. A deeper investigation—such as analyzing why Li and Hovy's model underperforms on this dataset, testing it on corpora where it typically succeeds, or reproducing their results on their original datasets—would clarify the relative merits of the proposed approach and solidify the contribution. The current comparison risks appearing superficial, as the reader is left wondering whether the low baseline score is due to an experimental error, task mismatch, or inherent model constraints.

The paper also exhibits several minor grammatical and stylistic issues that undermine precision. Multiple instances of missing definite articles (e.g., "limits the model to do X" instead of "limits the model’s ability to do X") appear throughout, particularly in technical descriptions. Phrases like "...limits the model to do X..." (repeated on lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764) are grammatically awkward; alternatives such as "limits the model’s capacity to" or "prevents the model from" would enhance clarity. A focused proofread for article usage and phrasing is recommended.

Regarding the final recommendation: after reviewing the author response, I adjust my score to a weak accept (4). The authors persuasively explain that difficulties in reproducing Li and Hovy’s system—stemming from unavailability of code and dependencies—were objective hurdles beyond their control. However, the paper should explicitly document these challenges (e.g., in the experimental section or appendix) to preempt criticism about incomplete comparisons. Additionally, I strongly recommend the authors attempt to reproduce Li and Hovy’s results on their original datasets as a sanity check; if feasible, this would provide crucial context for interpreting the performance gap and further validate their evaluation. If they have already done so, this should be stated in the paper.

In summary, this is a technically sound contribution that advances entity coherence modeling through a thoughtful neural augmentation. Addressing the comparison with Li and Hovy more thoroughly, refining grammatical precision, and documenting experimental challenges would elevate the paper to a stronger accept. As currently written, it is a solid but not outstanding submission. The proposed improvements are achievable and would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and rigor.
