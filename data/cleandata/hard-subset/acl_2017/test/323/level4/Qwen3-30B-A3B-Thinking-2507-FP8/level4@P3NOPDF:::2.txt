

The paper introduces a convolutional neural network (CNN) model for text coherence assessment, building upon the entity grid representation. The approach is well-motivated, with a particularly strong discussion of design intuitions (e.g., "Why it works"). The extensive evaluation demonstrates competitive results across multiple tasks, though a critical issue arises: the authors cannot reproduce the Li & Hovy (2014) baseline, suggesting a potential implementation or modeling error that must be resolved before acceptance.

The core contribution—applying CNNs to entity grids—represents a relatively straightforward neural adaptation of prior work. While the rationale for optimizing directly for coherence ranking is compelling, the necessity of 100-dimensional vectors for four-dimensional grid entries (or slightly more for the extended grid) remains unclear. The authors do not sufficiently justify this dimensionality choice or explain how it enhances coherence modeling beyond the grid structure itself. Similarly, the marginal benefit of extending transition chains from *k*=3 to *k*=6 sentences appears questionable, especially given that many WSJ articles are short, potentially limiting the practical impact of this extension.

The paper’s structure contains significant redundancies. Section 2 introduces related work, yet the first two-thirds of Section 6 repeat this content verbatim. This repetition should be eliminated by integrating Section 6’s relevant discussion into Section 2 (renaming the latter to "Related Work"). The remaining content of Section 6 could be incorporated into Section 2 as a subsection on "Consolidation of Prior Work," streamlining the narrative. Additionally, the writing is occasionally verbose; for instance, the entity grid introduction is unnecessarily repeated across multiple sections.

The paper would benefit from a thorough language edit. Several missing articles ("the WSJ corpus" appears twice without "the," "the Brown toolkit" similarly) and minor grammatical issues (e.g., "Firstly/Secondly" instead of "First, Second," inconsistent phrasing like "limits the model to" vs. "prevents the model from considering") suggest the need for native-speaker proofreading. Specific corrections include: 
- Page 1, bottom-left column: "Figure 2" → "Figure 1"
- Page 1: "Firstly/Secondly" → "First, Second"
- Page 1: "limits the model to" → "prevents the model from considering"
- The concluding "standard" paragraph in Section 1 is redundant and should be removed.

While the model is a solid application of neural techniques to entity-grid coherence, the paper’s incremental nature is at odds with the stated consolidation of prior work. The authors promise a "consolidation" but deliver a minor adaptation rather than substantial innovation—e.g., in representations (e.g., incorporating additional features beyond entity grids) or tasks. Given the field’s momentum, a full paper should demonstrate clearer advancement beyond existing neural coherence models.

In summary, the work presents a plausible neural extension of entity grids with strong evaluation but suffers from critical reproducibility concerns, unclear design choices, structural redundancy, and language issues. Addressing the Li & Hovy (2014) discrepancy, justifying the vector dimensionality, and streamlining the paper’s structure would significantly strengthen it. As submitted, the contribution is incremental, and the paper does not fully justify its position relative to the broader literature. The authors should revise to clarify the innovation and resolve the reproducibility gap.
