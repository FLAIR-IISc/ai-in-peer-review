

The paper presents a neural extension of the entity grid model for local coherence assessment, using a convolutional neural network to learn sequences of entity transitions. This approach enables better generalization over longer entity sequences compared to the original model's direct estimation of transition probabilities.

This is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a reasonable contribution to the field. The authors' pragmatic approach of building on established entity grid methods rather than proposing a fully neural approach is well-justified. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison with the neural coherence model proposed by Li and Hovy (2014). The authors note they were surprised by the very low performance of the Li and Hovy model on their task, but they don't investigate why this might be the case. This omission leaves readers wondering whether the Li and Hovy model's poor performance stems from an experimental error, corpus-specific limitations, or whether it might perform better on different datasets. A deeper analysis of these factors would substantially strengthen the paper's argument.

The paper is generally well-written and readable, but I noticed several instances where articles are missing (e.g., lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764). I recommend proofreading the manuscript with a focus on article usage. Additionally, the phrase "limits the model to do X" appears repeatedly and sounds unnatural. I suggest revising these to "limits the model's ability to do X" or "prevents the model from doing X" for better clarity.

The paper's main contribution is a neural architecture that models long-range entity transitions while incorporating entity-specific features without losing generalization. The authors demonstrate this through a pairwise ranking training method that learns task-specific features end-to-end. Their evaluation on three coherence assessment tasks shows consistent improvements over non-neural counterparts, with absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating.

The paper's strengths include:
1. A clear and well-motivated approach that builds effectively on established work in coherence modeling
2. Comprehensive evaluation across multiple coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating)
3. Demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on all three tasks

The main weaknesses are:
1. Insufficient comparison with the Li and Hovy (2014) neural coherence model, which is the only other neural approach in the field
2. Lack of explanation for the poor performance of the Li and Hovy model on their dataset
3. Missing articles in several places throughout the paper, affecting readability

I would also recommend that the authors investigate whether the Li and Hovy model performs better on their original datasets as a sanity check. If they can reproduce Li and Hovy's results on their own data, this would help validate their experimental setup and provide a more complete comparison.

Final recommendation: I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (4). I've adjusted my score to 4 after considering the author response, which addressed my concerns about the Li and Hovy comparison. I agree that objective difficulties running other people's software shouldn't be held against the authors, but I do think the authors should document their efforts to test the Li and Hovy system, and I encourage them to attempt reproducing Li and Hovy's results on their original datasets as a sanity check.
