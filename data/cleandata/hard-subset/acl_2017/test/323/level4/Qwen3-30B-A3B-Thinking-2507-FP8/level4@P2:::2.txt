

Summary: The paper proposes a neural coherence model based on a convolutional neural network operating over the entity grid representation of text. The main contributions are: (1) a neural architecture that captures long-range entity transitions without losing generalization through distributed representations, (2) an end-to-end training method for learning task-specific features via pairwise ranking, and (3) state-of-the-art results on three coherence assessment tasks.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a clear and well-motivated explanation of why a neural approach addresses limitations in existing entity grid models. The authors effectively highlight how discrete representations in prior work lead to the curse of dimensionality and how their distributed representation approach overcomes this limitation.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating consistent improvements across three distinct coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The model achieves absolute gains of 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary coherence rating compared to non-neural counterparts.

3. The paper offers a thoughtful explanation of the model's design choices, particularly the use of convolutional layers and the intuitive "Why it works" section. The authors clearly articulate how convolutional filters detect specific transition patterns and how max-pooling captures salient features.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper suffers from significant redundancy, particularly in the description of entity grid concepts. The introduction of the entity grid is repeated in multiple sections (Introduction, Section 2, and Section 6), with Section 6 largely repeating content from Section 2. This repetition weakens the paper's impact and should be removed to improve conciseness.

2. The model appears to be a relatively straightforward neuralization of existing entity grid approaches without sufficient innovation in representation or learning. The justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent grammatical roles (which take only 4-5 discrete values) is unclear and needs stronger justification. The authors should explain why this distributed representation is necessary when the input space is small.

3. The authors claim the model can capture longer transitions (k=6 vs. k=3 in prior work), but this advantage is questionable given the average WSJ document length of 22 sentences. Many documents may not contain sufficient transitions to benefit from longer k values, and this limitation should be addressed in the paper.

4. The paper reports an inability to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014), which raises concerns about the model's implementation or applicability. The authors should clarify this discrepancy, as it undermines the claimed state-of-the-art results and should be addressed in the revision.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you explain why 100-dimensional vectors are necessary for representing grammatical roles that only take 4-5 discrete values? How does this representation improve performance beyond what could be achieved with a simpler approach?

2. The authors note that they could not reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014), which showed high performance on similar tasks. Could you clarify whether this was due to implementation differences, dataset variations, or model limitations?

3. Given the average WSJ document length of 22 sentences, how does the model handle shorter documents where longer transition patterns (k=6) may not be feasible? Does this affect performance on shorter texts?

Minor issues:
- The paper contains numerous missing articles (e.g., "the WSJ corpus" should be "the WSJ corpus" on two occasions).
- Section 1 should use "First" and "Second" instead of "Firstly" and "Secondly" for proper academic style.
- The reference to "Figure 2" on page 1 should be "Figure 1" based on the content.

Overall, this is a solid implementation of a neural approach to coherence modeling that demonstrates clear improvements over previous methods. However, the paper would benefit from more substantial innovation in the representation or learning method, as the current approach appears to be a relatively simple neuralization of existing work. The redundancy in the paper also reduces its impact, and the inability to reproduce prior results needs clarification. With these issues addressed, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field.
