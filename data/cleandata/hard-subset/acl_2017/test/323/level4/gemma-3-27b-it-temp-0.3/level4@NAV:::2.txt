This paper introduces a convolutional neural network to model text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation. The motivation for this approach is clear, and the paper is well-written, with a particularly insightful discussion of the design choices – notably, the rationale for employing a CNN and the explanation of the model’s functionality in the section “Why it works.”

The evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating performance gains over existing methods across multiple tasks. However, the inability to replicate a prior result raises a concern; the findings on Li/Hovy (2014) suggest a potential implementation or modeling issue that requires investigation. 

While the model effectively integrates a neural network with the entity grid framework, the use of 100-dimensional vectors to represent grid entries (even with extensions) isn’t fully justified. The benefit of this dimensionality isn’t immediately apparent. Although direct optimization for coherence ranking is a positive step, the practical impact of varying the transition chain length (k=3 vs. k=6) seems potentially limited, particularly given the often-short length of articles in the WSJ corpus.

The paper would benefit from concision. There is some redundancy, specifically in the repeated introduction of entity grids. Sections 2 and the first two-thirds of Section 6 overlap considerably; the latter could be streamlined by either removing the repetition or integrating the content into Section 2, which could then be expanded and renamed “Related Work.” The remaining portion of Section 6 could then be incorporated into this expanded Section 2.

Overall, this is a sound implementation of a neural network applied to entity-grid coherence modeling. Given the consolidation of prior work presented, a full paper might be expected to include further innovations, such as exploring alternative representations or tackling additional tasks.

Minor points:

- The paper would benefit from review by a native English speaker to address missing articles (e.g., “the WSJ corpus,” “the Brown… toolkit”).
- On p.1, “Figure 2” should be corrected to “Figure 1.”
- On p.1, “Firstly/Secondly” should be revised to “First, Second.”
- On p.1, consider rephrasing “limits the model to” as “prevents the model from considering…”
- The standard concluding paragraph in Section 1 appears unnecessary given the paper’s length and could be removed.
