Summary and contributions
- The paper introduces a neural local coherence model that applies a CNN over the entity grid representation. Grammatical-role symbols (and optionally entity features) are embedded, local transition patterns are learned via convolution and max pooling, and the model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) A neuralized entity-grid framework that leverages distributed representations and convolution to capture longer-range entity transitions without combinatorial sparsity.
  2) End-to-end, task-specific training via pairwise ranking, yielding consistent state-of-the-art results on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.
  3) A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features (e.g., NE type, salience, properness) without external resources, while still improving over prior grid variants.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains across tasks with significance testing: The model improves over both the basic and extended entity grid, with absolute gains of ~4% on discrimination and up to 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating. Statistical testing and multiple random seeds increase confidence in the reported improvements.
- Clear architectural motivation and explanation: The rationale for CNNs (location-invariant detection of local transition patterns) and the “Why it works” discussion help readers understand what the filters capture and how pooling aggregates salient patterns.
- End-to-end ranking objective: Training directly for pairwise coherence ranking is appropriate for the evaluation tasks and likely contributes to the observed gains compared to task-agnostic feature extraction.
- Practical feature design: The method for adding entity-specific features by composing them with grammatical roles is simple and effective, and avoids dependence on external corpora for most features.
- Reproducibility support: Use of public toolkits for baselines, significance testing, and code release are positives for replicability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond “neuralization”: Conceptually, the paper largely replaces discrete transition features with embeddings and CNNs over the grid. While the performance gains are valuable, the representational innovation is incremental relative to prior entity-grid work.
- Insufficient justification for representation size and transition length: Using 100-dimensional embeddings for a small symbol inventory (S/O/X/– and a few feature values) and large convolution windows (e.g., k up to 6–8) is not well motivated. It is unclear how much these choices, versus the ranking objective, drive the gains. Ablations on embedding size, window length k, and number of filters would clarify.
- Baseline reproducibility issue: The poor reproduction of Li & Hovy (2014) (19.34% in discrimination) suggests a possible implementation or setup mismatch. Without a careful analysis (data preprocessing, segmentation, hyperparameters, training objective), it is hard to interpret the comparative results.
- Task mismatch for insertion: The model is trained to prefer originals over random permutations, yet insertion is much harder and structurally different. This likely limits gains on insertion. A variant trained directly for insertion (or with harder negative samples) would strengthen the case.
- Exposition and organization: The paper repeats the entity-grid introduction and re-covers related work in both Section 2 and Section 6. Consolidating related work into Section 2 (with a focused Section 6 for only non-grid alternatives) would improve clarity. There are also minor language issues and a figure reference error (see Minor comments).

Questions to authors
- Representation ablations: How do performance and generalization change with embedding sizes (e.g., 20/50/100/200), number of filters, and convolution window length k? Is there a clear saturation point?
- Length confounds: Discrimination can be easier for longer articles. Do results hold when controlling for document length (e.g., binning by sentence count) or on shorter documents?
- Insertion-specific training: Have you tried training with negatives tailored to insertion (e.g., near-miss sentence positions), curriculum learning, or triplet losses? Any preliminary results?
- Coreference sensitivity: How robust is the model to coreference errors? Have you evaluated with different coreference systems or with gold coreference (if available) to bound the effect?
- Summary task pretraining: For the DUC task, what exactly is transferred from WSJ (embeddings, filters, full network), and how many layers are fine-tuned? Provide learning rate schedules and early stopping criteria for the fine-tuning step.
- Li & Hovy baseline: Could you detail the preprocessing (tokenization, sentence splitting, vocabulary), training data, and hyperparameters used, and whether the original evaluation protocol (window size L, negative sampling, objective) was matched?

Minor comments
- Language: Several missing articles and minor grammatical issues (e.g., “the WSJ corpus,” “the Brown coherence toolkit”). A careful proofread by a native speaker would help.
- Figure reference: p.1 bottom-left: “Figure 2” should be “Figure 1.”
- Style: Prefer “First, Second” to “Firstly, Secondly.” Consider “prevents the model from considering …” instead of “limits the model to …”
- Structure: Remove or shorten the standard “paper organization” paragraph in Section 1. Consolidate overlapping related work: merge Section 6’s first two-thirds into Section 2, and keep only non-grid neural and discourse-relations-based approaches in a concise Related Work section.
- References: Ensure consistent citation formatting (years, venues) and fix placeholders (e.g., “Barzilay and Lapata.,? 2005”).
