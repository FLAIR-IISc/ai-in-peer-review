Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) to the entity grid representation. The model embeds grid symbols, applies convolutions over entity transition sequences, and trains end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective. It is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and summary coherence rating, achieving state-of-the-art results.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) A CNN-based neuralization of the entity grid that enables learning distributed representations of entity roles and longer-range transition patterns, trained end-to-end via pairwise ranking.
2) A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features into the grid alphabet, showing further gains with a reduced feature set compared to prior extended grids.
3) An empirical study across three coherence tasks on WSJ and DUC summaries, with consistent improvements over strong non-neural baselines.

Strengths
- Clear modeling intuition and design: The paper motivates why discrete transition counts struggle with sparsity for longer k and why convolution over embedded transitions can capture position-invariant patterns. The “Why it works” section is especially helpful in making the filters’ role concrete.
- Strong empirical results across tasks: On WSJ, the neuralized basic grid (Grid-CNN) and the extended version (Extended Grid-CNN) improve over their non-neural counterparts, with absolute gains of roughly 4% on discrimination and up to 2.5% on insertion. On DUC summary rating, the pre-trained Extended Grid-CNN further improves performance by around 3–4%. Reported significance (p < 0.01) and multiple runs mitigate variance concerns.
- Sound training and evaluation protocol: The pairwise ranking objective matches the downstream scoring use case. Hyperparameter tuning on a held-out dev set, early stopping, dropout, and repeated trials with different seeds are appropriate. The choice of WSJ, with larger training data than AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES, is well argued for neural models.
- Practicality and accessibility: The model architecture is straightforward, relies on standard components (embeddings, CNN, max-pooling), and is likely easy to reproduce and extend. The claim that code is released is welcome.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond neuralization: Conceptually, this is a direct CNN layering over the entity grid with a standard ranking loss. The representational innovation is modest; there is no new discourse representation or learning objective beyond standard choices. For a full paper, deeper representational advances (e.g., modeling cross-entity interactions, discourse relations, or hierarchical structure) would strengthen the contribution.
- Missing analysis to justify design choices: The paper does not quantify why 100-dimensional embeddings for a small symbol set are needed, or how much the gains owe to embedding size versus longer transition windows (k). Similarly, there is no ablation on filter width, pooling size, or the effect of pretraining. These analyses are important to establish that performance is not simply due to increased capacity.
- Training-task mismatch for insertion: The insertion task differs from discrimination in difficulty and signal (one sentence moved vs. full permutation). The model is trained only on discrimination-style pairs. The paper notes smaller gains on insertion but does not test training specifically on insertion (e.g., pairwise comparisons formed from sentences inserted at different positions). This weakens the claim of task-specific feature learning.
- Scope and assumptions: The model treats entity columns independently and assumes location-invariant transition patterns, potentially ignoring interactions across entities and sentence-specific phenomena. There is no discussion of how coreference errors impact performance, or whether the model is robust across domains and styles beyond WSJ and DUC.
- Reproducibility concern with prior work: The failure to reproduce Li & Hovy (2014) (19.34% on discrimination) is noted but not investigated. Given that paper’s prominence, a careful analysis (data splits, preprocessing, window size, optimization, or implementation mismatches) is needed; otherwise it casts doubt on the comparative picture.

Questions to authors
- How do embedding dimensionality, transition window size k, number of filters, and pooling size affect performance? Please provide an ablation isolating each, and a learning curve showing gains as k increases (k ≤ 3 vs. k = 4–8).
- What fraction of the gains comes from end-to-end pairwise training versus switching from discrete counts to embeddings? For example, how does a non-neural linear model over learned embeddings perform?
- Can you train the model directly on insertion-style pairs and report whether insertion scores improve? If not, can you analyze which error types dominate on insertion?
- How sensitive are results to coreference quality? If you vary or ablate the resolver, how does performance change?
- For summary rating, how much does pretraining contribute relative to training from scratch? Please include variance across runs given the small dataset.

Additional comments and editorial suggestions
- Organization: Section 2 mixes the entity grid description with related work; Section 6 repeats much of this. Consider consolidating all prior-work discussions (including graph-based and discourse-relation approaches) into a unified Related Work section to avoid redundancy. The remainder of Section 6 that is not duplicative could be folded into Section 2 under a labeled subsection.
- Writing and style: A careful proofreading pass by a native speaker would help. Examples: add articles (“the WSJ corpus,” “the Brown coherence toolkit”), avoid “Firstly/Secondly” in favor of “First/Second,” and prefer “prevents the model from considering …” over “limits the model to …” where appropriate.
- Figures and references: On p.1, “Figure 2” should be “Figure 1.” Ensure consistent figure numbering and cross-references.
- Fairness of feature comparisons: The Extended Grid-CNN uses only three entity features, while the non-neural Extended Grid uses nine (some from external corpora). It would be useful to include a controlled comparison where both use the same feature subset to isolate the benefit of neuralization versus feature availability.
- Reproducibility: Since you state that code is released, please ensure that scripts for grid construction, coreference settings, data splits, and hyperparameters are provided. A small qualitative analysis (e.g., top activated filters with example transition patterns) would also make the learned patterns more interpretable.

Overall recommendation
This is a solid and well-executed application of CNNs to entity-grid-based coherence modeling with clear gains and clear writing of the core ideas. However, given the limited novelty, missing ablations, and organizational issues, I lean toward borderline for a full paper. Strengthening the analysis, training on insertion, and consolidating related work would significantly improve the paper.
