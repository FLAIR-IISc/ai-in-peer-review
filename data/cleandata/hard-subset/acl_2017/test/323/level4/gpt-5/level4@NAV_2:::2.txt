The paper introduces a convolutional neural network for modeling text coherence, built on top of the established entity grid representation. The approach is well motivated and clearly presented; I particularly appreciate the transparent rationale for architectural choices (e.g., the justification for CNNs and the explanatory “Why it works” section).

The evaluation spans multiple coherence assessment tasks and shows consistent improvements over prior work. However, it is puzzling that the Li & Hovy (2014) result could not be replicated; this suggests a potential implementation or setup discrepancy that should be investigated and explained.

That said, the method is essentially a straightforward neural reimplementation of the entity grid. I am not persuaded that 100-dimensional embeddings are necessary for encoding a four-value role inventory (or slightly larger in the extended setting). How does this dimensionality materially help? While directly optimizing a ranking objective is sensible, I remain unconvinced that extending transition windows from k ≤ 3 to around k = 6 yields substantial gains, particularly since many WSJ articles are relatively short.

The manuscript is somewhat verbose and repeats material; for instance, the entity grid introduction appears in multiple places. Because Section 2 already covers related work, much of the first two-thirds of Section 6 is redundant and should either be removed or folded into Section 2 (and Section 2 could be retitled to reflect its broader scope). The remainder of Section 6 could be integrated as a subsection there.

Overall, this is a solid application of neural methods to entity-grid-based coherence. Given the consolidation of prior ideas in a full paper, I would have expected more novelty, such as richer representational features or additional tasks.
