Summary
The paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) over the entity grid representation to model textual coherence. The approach is motivated by clear intuitions (e.g., why a CNN is suitable and the “Why it works” section), and is evaluated across several tasks, where it outperforms prior methods.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and articulation of design choices; the rationale for using CNNs on entity grids is well presented.
- Broad evaluation demonstrating consistent gains over previous approaches.
- A useful consolidation of the entity-grid paradigm with modern neural modeling.

Main Concerns
- Limited novelty beyond a straightforward “neuralization” of the entity grid. The paper largely preserves the traditional grid features and adds a CNN architecture. For a full paper, I would expect either richer representational innovations (e.g., additional linguistic features, discourse cues, coreference quality estimates) or new tasks demonstrating broader utility.
- Embedding dimensionality: The use of 100-dimensional vectors to represent a small, discrete set of grid entries (four roles or a few more in the extended grid) is not well justified. Please explain the modeling intuition and provide ablation results varying the embedding size (e.g., 4–8–16–32–64–100). Without this, the choice appears arbitrary and overparameterized relative to the input alphabet.
- Transition window size (k): The paper suggests extending transition chains up to k=6, but it is unclear how much this helps compared to k=3, especially given that many WSJ articles are quite short. A sensitivity analysis over k, broken down by document length, would clarify the benefit and guard against overfitting to longer texts.
- Reproducibility issue: The inability to reproduce one prior result (Li & Hovy, 2014) is concerning. This could indicate an implementation discrepancy, preprocessing mismatch, or a modeling error. Please:
  - Precisely document your preprocessing (tokenization, sentence segmentation, coreference resolution, grid construction).
  - Verify dataset splits and any filtering.
  - Provide a side-by-side replication attempt with diagnostics (e.g., ablations to isolate where performance diverges).
  - If your reimplementation is faithful yet yields different numbers, discuss likely causes and their implications.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Provide ablations and diagnostics:
  - Embedding size, filter widths/heights, number of filters, depth (if applicable), pooling strategy.
  - Impact of the ranking objective versus alternative losses.
  - Effect of extended grids versus basic grids.
  - Parameter counts and training time to contextualize complexity vs. gains.
- Clarify model advantages: Beyond capacity, what patterns does the CNN capture that transition counts cannot? For instance, do learned filters correspond to interpretable coherence motifs (e.g., subject continuity, reactivation patterns)? A qualitative analysis of top-activating n-gram transitions could strengthen the “Why it works” claim.
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Include stronger non-neural baselines if feasible (e.g., regularized linear or MLP models over transition features) to isolate the contribution of convolution and embeddings.
  - Ensure hyperparameter tuning parity across systems.
- Scope and contribution:
  - If possible, add a representation-oriented contribution (e.g., lexicalized roles, semantic roles, coreference confidence, discourse markers) or a new evaluation setting to better justify the full-paper scope.

Clarity and Organization
- The paper is somewhat repetitive. Related work appears in both Section 2 and the first two-thirds of Section 6.
  - Consolidate related work into Section 2 and remove redundant material in Section 6.
  - If the remaining content in Section 6 fits better as literature context, move it under a subsection in Section 2. Consider renaming Section 2 to “Related Work.”
- Streamline introductions to entity grids; the current exposition appears multiple times.
- Consider pruning generic concluding remarks that do not add insights (e.g., the “standard” final paragraph of Section 1).

Minor Comments and Style
- The paper would benefit from careful proofreading by a native speaker. Articles are often missing (e.g., “the WSJ corpus,” “the Brown … toolkit”). Fixing these will improve readability.
- p.1, bottom left column: “Figure 2” should be “Figure 1.”
- p.1: Replace “Firstly/Secondly” with “First, Second.”
- p.1: Consider revising “limits the model to” to “prevents the model from considering …” (if that is the intended meaning).
- Consider removing the standard final paragraph in Section 1 if it is formulaic and redundant.

Questions for the Authors
- Why 100 dimensions for the grid-entry embeddings? What performance–capacity trade-offs did you observe across different sizes?
- How sensitive are results to k (transition window size), and how does document length distribution affect the optimal k?
- Can you provide interpretability analyses of learned filters or embedding spaces to substantiate the “Why it works” section?
- What exactly differs in your setup versus Li & Hovy (2014)? Can you release code, preprocessing scripts, and detailed settings to facilitate verification?

Overall Assessment
This is a solid and well-executed application of CNNs to the entity grid framework, with clear motivation and strong empirical results. However, the technical novelty is modest, and key design choices (embedding size, window k) require justification through ablations. The reproducibility discrepancy with Li & Hovy (2014) should be investigated and documented more thoroughly. With tighter writing, stronger analysis, and additional evidence clarifying the model’s unique advantages, the paper would make a more compelling full-paper contribution.
