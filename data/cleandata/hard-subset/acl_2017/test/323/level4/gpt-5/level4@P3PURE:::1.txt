The paper introduces an extension of the entity grid model in which a convolutional neural network learns sequences of entity transitions that indicate coherence. This approach permits better generalization over longer sequences of entities than the direct estimates of transition probabilities used in the original model. The paper is well motivated, clearly written, and takes a sensible approach: rather than proposing a fully neural model, the authors build on established work and use a neural component to address a specific limitation in one step. This is a valid and pragmatic choice. However, it would be useful to expand the comparison with the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy. The authors themselves express surprise at the very low score that the Li and Hovy model achieves on their task. This raises questions about whether there may have been an error in the experimental setup, whether the other model’s low performance is corpus-dependent, and, if so, how the proposed model would perform on a corpus or task where the Li and Hovy model is more successful. A deeper investigation of these factors would strengthen the argument considerably.

In general, the paper is fluent and readable, but many definite articles are missing (e.g., on lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764, and likely elsewhere). I recommend a careful proofreading pass focused specifically on article usage. In addition, the expression “…limits the model to do X…,” which appears repeatedly, is unusual; “limits the model’s capacity to do X” or “prevents the model from doing X” would be clearer.

Final recommendation adjusted to 4 after considering the author response. I agree that objective difficulties in running other researchers’ software should not be held against the present authors. The efforts made to test the Li and Hovy system, and the problems encountered in doing so, should be documented in the paper. I would also suggest attempting to reproduce the Li and Hovy results on their original datasets as a sanity check (unless this has already been done), to see whether that works as expected.
