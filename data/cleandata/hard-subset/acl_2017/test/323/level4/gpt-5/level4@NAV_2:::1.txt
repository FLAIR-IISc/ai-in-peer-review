The paper presents an enhancement of the entity grid framework. A convolutional neural network is leveraged to learn patterns of entity transitions that signal coherence, yielding better generalization over longer transition sequences than the original model’s direct transition-probability estimates. The manuscript is clear and well organized. Rather than proposing a wholly neural solution, the authors augment the established grid approach with a neural component to address specific bottlenecks—an entirely reasonable strategy. However, the comparison against the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy could be expanded. The authors note being surprised by that model’s very low performance on their setup, which raises questions about potential experimental misconfiguration, corpus sensitivity of the competing model, and how the proposed method would fare on data or tasks where Li and Hovy performs well. A more thorough investigation of these factors would substantially strengthen the claims. Stylistically, the paper is generally fluent, but definite articles are often missing (e.g., lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764, and likely others). A careful pass focused on article usage is recommended. Also, the repeated phrase “…limits the model to do X…” reads oddly; alternatives such as “limits the model’s capacity to do X” or “prevents the model from doing X” are clearer.

Final recommendation updated to 4 after considering the author response. I agree that practical difficulties in running third-party code should not count against the authors. That said, the attempts to evaluate the Li and Hovy system—and the issues encountered—should be described in the paper. I also suggest reproducing Li and Hovy’s results on their original datasets as a sanity check (if not already done) to confirm the implementation behaves as expected in its native setting.
