Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for local coherence. The core idea is to apply a CNN over distributed representations of entity-role sequences extracted from an entity grid, enabling the model to learn longer-range transition patterns and entity-specific cues without suffering sparsity. Training uses a pairwise ranking objective for end-to-end learning tied to the target task. The model is evaluated on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, achieving state-of-the-art results.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
- A CNN-based architecture over entity grids that learns distributed representations of entity-role transitions and can model longer transitions than traditional grid approaches, while remaining data-efficient.
- End-to-end pairwise ranking for task-specific feature learning, yielding consistent gains across three coherence evaluation tasks.
- A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features (NE type, salience, proper mention) by augmenting grid symbols, attaining better results than non-neural extended grids even with a reduced feature set.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance across tasks and domains. On WSJ, the neural grid improves discrimination by roughly 4 points over the grid-all-nouns baseline and 3–4 points over the extended grid; on insertion, it gains up to 2.5 points; on DUC’03 summary rating, pretraining plus fine-tuning yields 3–4 point gains. These are solid, consistent improvements over competitive baselines.
- Principled neuralization of a well-established representation. Operating on entity grids keeps the model focused on coherence-relevant structure (entity transitions), while CNNs effectively capture longer patterns (e.g., k=5–8) without the combinatorial explosion that plagues discrete transition counts.
- Sound training and evaluation protocol. The use of pairwise ranking aligns with the ranking nature of coherence assessment. The authors report tuning on a dev set, average over multiple random seeds, and conduct significance testing, which increases confidence in the reported gains.
- Reduced feature engineering with better results. The Extended Grid-CNN uses only three easy-to-compute entity features yet outperforms the non-neural extended grid model that relies on nine features, several from external resources. This suggests the learned representations are capturing useful abstractions.
- Reproducibility support. The code release and use of standard datasets and toolkits (e.g., Brown coherence toolkit for baselines) improve transparency and reusability.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparison to prior neural coherence work. The attempt to run Li and Hovy (2014) yielded very low accuracy on WSJ discrimination, and the model was omitted from the results table. Without a deeper analysis, it is hard to attribute the discrepancy to implementation, data, or task differences. This weakens the empirical positioning of the paper among neural coherence models.
- Dependence on coreference with little error analysis. The approach inherits the entity grid’s reliance on coreference resolution. There is no sensitivity analysis to coreference errors, nor clarity on the resolver and settings used. This matters for robustness and portability to noisier domains.
- Training–evaluation mismatch for insertion. The model is trained on discrimination pairs but evaluated on insertion without targeted training. While results are still improved, the paper acknowledges smaller gains; a direct insertion-trained variant and analysis would strengthen the claim of generality.
- Limited interpretability and ablation analysis. While the paper provides architectural intuition, there is little qualitative analysis of learned filters/patterns or ablations isolating the contributions of transition length, pooling size, and individual entity-specific features. Such analysis would clarify what the model actually learns.
- Minor writing and reference issues. There are numerous missing articles and repeated use of “loosing” instead of “losing.” The phrase “limits the model to do X” is non-idiomatic. The references contain formatting glitches (placeholders like “?”, escaped characters).

Questions to authors
- Li and Hovy comparison: What exact data splits, preprocessing, and hyperparameters were used? Did you try reproducing their reported results on their original datasets as a sanity check? Can you share logs or diagnostic metrics that might indicate where performance diverged?
- Insertion-specific training: Have you trained the pairwise model directly on insertion comparisons (gold vs. candidate insertion positions)? If so, how does performance change?
- Coreference sensitivity: Which coreference resolver and configuration did you use? How sensitive is performance to coreference quality (e.g., using a different resolver or ablating coreference by using only sentence-local mentions)?
- Model analysis: Can you provide ablations for transition window size, pooling length, number of filters, and the three entity-specific features? Any qualitative examples of high-activation transition patterns?
- Domain transfer: For the summary rating task, how much does WSJ pretraining help per-cluster? Any evidence of domain mismatch effects?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Please document your efforts to run the Li and Hovy system, including settings tried and any troubleshooting, and consider reporting results on their original datasets to establish a fair comparison baseline.
- Improve language quality: add missing articles, replace “loosing” with “losing,” and rephrase “limits the model to do X” to “limits the model’s capacity to do X” or “prevents the model from doing X.”
- Clean the references (years, diacritics, stray punctuation/escapes) to meet ACL formatting standards.

Final recommendation
4 (after author response). The paper offers a clear, effective neuralization of the entity grid with solid empirical gains and good practice in training and evaluation. The main reservation is the shallow treatment of prior neural baselines and lack of analysis of coreference sensitivity and learned patterns. Addressing these points (especially documenting the Li and Hovy reproduction attempts and, if possible, reproducing their results on their original data) would further strengthen the paper.
