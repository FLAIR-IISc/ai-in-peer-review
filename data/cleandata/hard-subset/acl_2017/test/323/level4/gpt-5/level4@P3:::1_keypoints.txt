- Paper proposes a CNN-based neuralization of the entity grid that applies convolutions over distributed entity-role sequences and is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective; evaluated on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence with state-of-the-art results.

- Contribution: CNN architecture models longer-range entity-role transitions in a data-efficient way compared to discrete grids.

- Contribution: End-to-end pairwise ranking yields consistent gains across three coherence evaluation tasks.

- Contribution: Simple entity-specific feature augmentation (NE type, salience, proper mention) outperforms non-neural extended grids despite using fewer features.

- Strength: Strong empirical performance — consistent improvements on WSJ discrimination (~3–4 pts), insertion (up to 2.5 pts), and DUC’03 summary rating (3–4 pts with pretrain+finetune).

- Strength: Principled approach that preserves entity-grid structure while CNNs capture longer transition patterns without combinatorial sparsity.

- Strength: Sound training and evaluation protocol (pairwise ranking, dev tuning, multiple seeds, significance testing).

- Strength: Reduced feature engineering; the neural model outperforms baselines using only three easy-to-compute entity features.

- Strength: Reproducibility supported by code release and use of standard datasets/toolkits.

- Weakness: Limited comparison to prior neural coherence work (Li & Hovy 2014) — reproduction failed and was omitted without deeper analysis.

- Weakness: Heavy dependence on coreference with no sensitivity analysis or clear details on the resolver/settings used.

- Weakness: Training–evaluation mismatch for insertion (trained on discrimination, not insertion-specific comparisons); no insertion-trained variant reported.

- Weakness: Limited interpretability and ablation analysis — few qualitative examples of learned filters or ablations for transition length, pooling, filters, and entity features.

- Weakness: Minor writing and reference issues (grammar, repeated “loosing” vs “losing,” non-idiomatic phrases, formatting glitches in references).

- Question: What exact data splits, preprocessing, and hyperparameters were used for the Li & Hovy reproduction attempts, and were their original datasets used as a sanity check?

- Question: Have you trained the pairwise model directly on insertion comparisons, and how does that affect insertion performance?

- Question: Which coreference resolver and configuration were used, and how sensitive is performance to coreference quality or ablation?

- Question: Can you provide ablations for transition window size, pooling length, number of filters, and the three entity-specific features, plus qualitative high-activation examples?

- Question: For summary rating, how much does WSJ pretraining help per-cluster and is there evidence of domain mismatch effects?

- Suggestion: Document Li & Hovy reproduction efforts, improve language (fix articles, “losing,” rephrase awkward phrases), and clean reference formatting.

- Final recommendation: Score 4 (after author response) — promising, well-executed neuralization with solid gains but needs deeper baseline comparisons, coreference sensitivity analysis, and interpretability/ablation work.
