Summary
This paper extends the entity grid model of discourse coherence by introducing a convolutional neural network that learns patterns over sequences of entity transitions. By replacing direct estimation of transition probabilities with learned convolutional features, the approach captures longer-range dependencies and generalizes better than the original model. The paper is clearly motivated and, overall, well written.

Strengths
- Sensible and focused extension of a strong baseline: rather than adopting a fully neural pipeline, the paper targets a known limitation of the entity grid with a tailored neural component.
- Clear exposition of the model and its intended benefits for modeling longer transition sequences.
- Empirically convincing on the tasks considered, with improvements over the original entity grid.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Comparison to Li and Hovy’s neural coherence model is underdeveloped. The reported poor performance of that model on your setup raises questions about comparability and experimental correctness. Without a deeper investigation, it is difficult to interpret the relative gains of the proposed method.
  - Please document, in the camera-ready version, the exact efforts made to run Li and Hovy’s system (versions, hyperparameters, preprocessing, software issues) and the obstacles encountered. Objective difficulties with third-party code should not be held against you, but the record should be transparent.
  - As a sanity check, attempt to reproduce Li and Hovy’s published results on their original datasets and settings. Even partial replication would help rule out implementation or configuration issues on your side.
  - Discuss corpus and task dependence explicitly. Is the Li and Hovy model known to perform poorly on your corpus, and if so, why? What aspects of your data or task (e.g., entity annotation quality, domain, document length) might disadvantage that model but favor yours?
  - If feasible, evaluate your model on at least one dataset where Li and Hovy reported strong results, or provide a reasoned analysis of how your method would be expected to behave there.
  - Consider a small ablation or analysis to isolate what the CNN contributes beyond longer n-gram–like transition patterns (e.g., varying convolution widths, comparing to feature hashing or higher-order transition baselines).

Writing and presentation
- The paper is generally fluent and readable, but there are frequent omissions of definite articles (e.g., “the”) throughout the text. A careful proofreading pass focused on article usage would improve clarity and polish.
- The repeated phrase “limits the model to do X” is non-idiomatic. Prefer “limits the model’s capacity to do X,” “prevents the model from doing X,” or “constrains the model to X,” depending on the intended meaning.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and thoughtfully scoped contribution that improves a classic coherence model with a targeted neural component. The main limitation is the insufficient and somewhat puzzling comparison to a closely related neural baseline (Li and Hovy). Addressing this through documentation, replication on the baseline’s original data, and a discussion of corpus effects would substantially strengthen the empirical claims and help readers interpret where and why the proposed method excels.

Final recommendation
Score: 4 (after considering the author response)

Rationale: I accept the authors’ point that practical difficulties in running third-party systems should not be penalized. However, for the sake of completeness and interpretability, the paper should include a transparent account of the attempts to run Li and Hovy’s model and, if possible, a replication of their reported results on their datasets. With this additional documentation and analysis, I believe the paper will make a clear and useful contribution to research on discourse coherence.
