Summary and contributions
- The paper extends the entity grid model by applying a convolutional neural network (CNN) over entity-grid representations to learn local coherence patterns. Using distributed representations enables modeling longer entity-transition sequences than counting-based transition probabilities.
- The model is trained end to end with a pairwise ranking objective, allowing task-specific feature learning and straightforward integration of entity-specific features via symbol augmentation (e.g., PERSON-S).
- Empirically, the approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating).

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated incremental contribution: the paper strengthens a widely used, interpretable framework (entity grid) rather than replacing it wholesale, and it targets specific limitations (sparsity and task-agnostic features).
- Solid empirical results: consistent gains over strong non-neural baselines on multiple tasks, with additional improvements from entity-specific features and pretraining on WSJ for summary rating.
- Methodological clarity: the architecture, training objective, hyperparameter search, and significance testing are described in sufficient detail to enable replication. Code availability is a plus.
- Good use of distributed representations and convolution to capture longer transitions while maintaining generalization, addressing the curse of dimensionality in transition modeling.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of the comparison to Li and Hovy (2014). The reported very low performance of their neural coherence model on your setup is acknowledged as surprising but not investigated in depth. This raises concerns about experimental alignment (preprocessing, window size, negative sampling, optimization, early stopping, embeddings, domain mismatch). Without a deeper diagnostic, it is hard to conclude whether the performance gap reflects methodological differences or setup/implementation issues.
- Domain sensitivity not explored. If Li and Hovy’s model is corpus- or task-dependent, it would be helpful to test your model on datasets/settings where their approach performs well, and conversely to run their model under tightly matched preprocessing and evaluation on your WSJ tasks.
- Writing issues: frequent missing definite articles (e.g., around lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764 and elsewhere) hinder readability. The repeated phrase “limits the model to do X” is awkward; clearer alternatives include “limits the model’s capacity to X” or “prevents the model from X.”

Questions to authors
- Li and Hovy comparison:
  - What exact code/version, preprocessing pipeline (tokenization, sentence segmentation, coreference, POS/parse), embeddings (source, dimensionality), sentence-window size L, negative sampling strategy, optimizer, learning rates, early stopping, and evaluation protocol did you use?
  - Were you able to reproduce their reported results on their original datasets? If not, what failed and what diagnostics did you run?
  - Can you provide a controlled comparison where both models use matched preprocessing and training regimes on WSJ?
- Generalization and ablations:
  - Could you report ablations on transition window size k, pooling size, number of filters, and the impact of each entity-specific feature? How sensitive are results to these choices?
  - How do you handle vocabulary growth when concatenating entity features with roles (e.g., PERSON-S)? What is the OOV strategy, and how does this affect embedding size and data sparsity?
  - Have you evaluated your model on AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES or Li and Hovy’s datasets to assess domain robustness? Conversely, can you report Li and Hovy’s model on your WSJ setting after aligning preprocessing?
