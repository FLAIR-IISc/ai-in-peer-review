The paper introduces a convolutional neural network approach for modeling text coherence. It builds on the established entity grid representation for coherence, placing a CNN on top of it.

The method is well motivated and clearly explained; I particularly appreciate the explicit discussion of the intuitions behind key design choices (e.g., the rationale for using a CNN and the section titled “Why it works”).

The evaluation is extensive across multiple tasks and shows that the proposed method surpasses prior approaches. However, it is odd that one previously reported result could not be replicated: the numbers for Li/Hovy (2014) point to a potential implementation or modeling mistake that should be investigated.

That said, the model is essentially a fairly straightforward “neuralization” of the entity grid. I do not see why 100-dimensional embeddings are needed to encode a grid cell with only four states (or slightly more in the extended grid). What advantage does this bring? While optimizing directly for coherence ranking should help, the difference between using transition chains up to k=3 sentences versus k=6 may be minor, particularly because many WSJ articles are quite short.

The writing feels somewhat verbose, with repeated material—for example, the introduction to entity grids appears multiple times. In particular, Section 2 already presents related work, so the first two-thirds of Section 6 repeat earlier content and should be removed (or integrated into Section 2 where appropriate). The remainder of Section 6 likely belongs in Section 2 as a subsection (and Section 2 should be retitled as Related Work).

Overall, this is a solid application of a neural model to entity-grid-based coherence. Given the consolidation of prior work, though, I would expect a bit more for a full paper, such as novel representational ideas (additional features?) or new tasks.
