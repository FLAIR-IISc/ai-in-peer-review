The paper extends the entity grid model by using a convolutional neural network to learn patterns of entity transitions that signal discourse coherence. By capturing longer transition sequences, the approach generalizes beyond the direct transition probabilities used in the original model.

The paper is well organized and clearly written. Rather than proposing a fully neural solution, the authors judiciously integrate a neural component to address a specific limitation, which is a sensible and well-motivated design choice. However, the evaluation would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy. The authors note being surprised by Li and Hovy’s very low score on their task, which raises several questions: (i) could there be an issue with the experimental setup? (ii) is the poor performance due to corpus or task differences? and (iii) how would the proposed model perform on datasets where Li and Hovy’s approach is known to work well? A deeper investigation of these factors would substantially strengthen the paper’s claims.

The writing is generally fluent, but definite articles are missing in multiple places (e.g., lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764, and likely elsewhere). I recommend a careful proofreading pass focused on article usage. In addition, the repeated phrase “…limits the model to do X…” is non-idiomatic; clearer alternatives include “…limits the model’s capacity to do X” or “…prevents the model from doing X.”

Final recommendation: 4 (after author response). I agree that objective difficulties in running third-party software should not count against the authors. That said, the paper should document the concrete steps taken to evaluate the Li and Hovy system and the specific problems encountered. If feasible, I also encourage reproducing Li and Hovy’s published results on their original datasets as a sanity check (if this has not already been done), to clarify whether the discrepancy stems from implementation issues or dataset/task differences.
