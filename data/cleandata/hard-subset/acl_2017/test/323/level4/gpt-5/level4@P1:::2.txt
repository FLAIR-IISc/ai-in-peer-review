Summary and contributions
This paper introduces a local coherence model that applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) to the entity grid representation. The model uses distributed representations of grammatical roles (and optional entity-specific features) and is trained end-to-end via pairwise ranking. It is evaluated on three standard tasks—sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and summary coherence rating—and reports consistent gains over prior work.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An effective neuralization of the entity grid that yields consistent, statistically significant improvements on multiple coherence tasks, including stronger performance on sentence ordering and summary rating.
2) An end-to-end training framework (pairwise ranking) that learns task-specific features directly from data and enables the use of longer-range entity transitions.
3) A clear and well-motivated architectural design, with intuitive explanations of why the CNN over grids should capture meaningful transition patterns.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and model intuition: The paper provides a solid rationale for using CNNs (location-invariant pattern detection over entity transitions) and a helpful “Why it works” discussion that makes the design choices easy to follow.

- Strong empirical results across tasks: The model achieves absolute improvements of roughly 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary coherence rating, with significance tests indicating that the neural models outperform their non-neural counterparts. The evaluation covers multiple datasets/settings and compares against reasonable baselines.

- End-to-end, task-specific learning: Moving from hand-crafted transition probabilities to learned embeddings and filters addresses sparsity and allows the model to capture longer transition patterns (k ≈ 5–8), while training directly for ranking coherence.

- Breadth of evaluation and implementation details: The paper evaluates on three tasks, explores pretraining for the scarce-data summary rating setup, and provides sufficient training details and hyperparameters to support reproducibility. The use of a development set and multiple random seeds is good practice.

Weaknesses
- Modest novelty beyond neuralization: Conceptually, the approach is a relatively straightforward application of CNNs to the entity grid, rather than introducing new representations or features. For a full paper, I would expect either a more substantial advance in representation (e.g., richer linguistic features) or a broader set of tasks/analyses.

- Justification of embedding dimensionality and model capacity: It is not clear why 100-dimensional embeddings are necessary to represent a small set of grammatical roles (and a few entity features in the extended grid). A discussion or ablation on embedding size and filter count would help justify the capacity choices and clarify what the model is learning.

- Benefit of longer transitions not fully evidenced: While the model can handle longer transition lengths (e.g., k up to 6 or more), it remains unclear how much this contributes versus shorter chains (k ≤ 3). Given that many WSJ articles are relatively short, the incremental benefit of longer k may be limited; an ablation over k would strengthen the argument.

- Reproducibility gap for Li and Hovy (2014): The inability to reproduce Li and Hovy’s results (19.34% discrimination accuracy) suggests a potential implementation or configuration issue. This should be investigated and reported more thoroughly (e.g., dataset splits, preprocessing, window size, training setup) to rule out experimental errors.

- Writing and structure redundancies: The exposition is somewhat lengthy and repeats material. Section 2 mixes background and related work; then most of Section 6 repeats that content. The paper would be stronger if the related work were consolidated into a single place (e.g., fold the non-redundant parts of Section 6 into Section 2, and retitle Section 2 as “Related Work”).

Questions to the authors
- Embedding size: Why choose 100 dimensions for grammatical roles (and a small set of entity features)? Do smaller embeddings perform similarly? Please include an ablation.

- Transition length (k): How sensitive is performance to k? Do gains persist beyond k = 3? Please provide results that isolate the effect of increasing k.

- Insertion training: Since the insertion task differs from discrimination, have you trained the model directly on insertion pairs? If so, how does that compare? If not, could you comment on expected gains?

- Reproducing Li and Hovy (2014): What exact settings, preprocessing, and data splits were used? Did you try both recurrent and recursive variants, different sentence window sizes, or their reported hyperparameters? Any diagnostic analysis would be helpful.

- Model capacity and data size: Do larger models overfit? How do performance and variance change with filter count, pooling size, and dropout?
