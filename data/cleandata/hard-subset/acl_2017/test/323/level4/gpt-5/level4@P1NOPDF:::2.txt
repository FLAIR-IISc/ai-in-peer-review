This paper proposes a convolutional neural network model for text coherence built on the entity grid representation. The approach is well motivated and clearly described; I particularly appreciated the explicit rationale for design choices (e.g., why a CNN) and the “Why it works” section.

The evaluation spans several tasks and shows consistent gains over prior methods. However, one previously reported result could not be reproduced: your Li and Hovy (2014) numbers differ, suggesting a possible implementation or modeling error that should be diagnosed and resolved.

While effective, the model reads as a fairly straightforward “neuralization” of the entity grid. I do not see why 100-dimensional vectors are necessary to represent a grid entry with only four possible states (slightly more in the extended grid). Please clarify how such high-dimensional embeddings help beyond introducing unnecessary capacity. I agree that optimizing directly for a coherence-ranking objective is beneficial, but I am less convinced that moving from transition chains of up to k=3 sentences to k=6 materially affects performance, especially given that many WSJ articles in your data are quite short.

Writing and organization: The paper is longer than needed and repeats material—most notably the introduction to entity grids. Section 2 already surveys related work; as a result, the first two-thirds of Section 6 substantially repeats content and should be removed or merged into Section 2. The remainder of Section 6 would also fit better as a subsection of Section 2; if you do this, please retitle Section 2 as “Related Work.”

Overall assessment: This is a solid application of a neural network to an entity-grid-based coherence model, with clear exposition and strong empirical results. Given the stated goal of consolidating prior work, I would expect more novelty for a full paper—either in the representation (e.g., additional features) or in the tasks used to probe coherence.

Minor comments:

- Page 1, bottom left column: “Figure 2” should be “Figure 1.”
- Page 1: Replace “Firstly/Secondly” with “First/Second.”
- Page 1: Consider revising “limits the model to” to “prevents the model from considering ...”
- Consider removing the boilerplate final paragraph of Section 1; it is unnecessary in a short paper.
