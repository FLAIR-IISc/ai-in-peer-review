This paper introduces a convolutional neural network for modeling text coherence built on top of the well-established entity grid representation. The approach is well motivated and clearly presented, and I particularly value the explicit discussion of the design rationale (e.g., the case for using a CNN and the “Why it works” section).

The evaluation is thorough across multiple tasks and shows consistent improvements over prior methods. However, the inability to reproduce a previously reported result (Li and Hovy, 2014) is concerning. This discrepancy suggests a potential implementation or modeling issue that should be investigated and explained.

That said, the contribution feels largely like a straightforward “neuralization” of the entity grid. I am not convinced by the use of 100-dimensional embeddings to encode a grid entry with only four possible roles (slightly more in the extended grid). Why is such a high dimensionality needed, and how does it help beyond a simpler representation? While optimizing directly for coherence ranking is a sensible choice, I am unsure that extending transition chains from up to k=3 sentences to k=6 yields substantial gains, especially given the short length of many WSJ articles.

The writing is generally clear but somewhat verbose and repetitive. The introduction to entity grids appears multiple times. Section 2 already includes related work; as a result, the first two-thirds of Section 6 repeat earlier content and should be removed or merged into Section 2 as appropriate. The remaining material in Section 6 would fit better as a subsection within Section 2, which could then be retitled “Related Work.”

Overall, this is a solid application of a neural model to an entity-grid-based coherence framework, with good empirical results. Still, for a full-length paper that aims to consolidate prior work, I would expect stronger innovation either in the representation (e.g., additional or alternative features) or in the range of tasks explored.

Minor points:
- The paper would benefit from careful proofreading by a native speaker. Articles are often missing, e.g., “the WSJ corpus” (twice), “the Brown … toolkit” (twice), etc.
- Page 1, bottom of left column: “Figure 2” should be “Figure 1.”
- Page 1: Replace “Firstly/Secondly” with “First, Second.”
- Page 1: Consider changing “limits the model to” to “prevents the model from considering …”
- Consider removing the standard concluding paragraph in Section 1; it seems unnecessary for a short paper.
