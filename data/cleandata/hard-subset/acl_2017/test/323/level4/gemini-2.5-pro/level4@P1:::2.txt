### **Summary**

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) for modeling text coherence. The model builds upon the well-established entity grid representation, applying a CNN to learn features directly from the grid. This neural approach is designed to capture longer-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion, overcoming limitations of previous methods that rely on discrete, hand-crafted features.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel neural architecture that applies a convolutional network directly to the entity grid representation to score text coherence.
2.  An extensive empirical evaluation on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), which demonstrates that the proposed model achieves new state-of-the-art results, outperforming previous entity-grid-based and other coherence models.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and the approach is clearly motivated. The authors provide excellent intuition behind their design choices, particularly for using a CNN architecture. The section titled "Why it works" is especially helpful for understanding how the model components contribute to the overall goal.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The evaluation is thorough, covering three different coherence assessment tasks. The results are strong and consistently show that the proposed neural model significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods, including its non-neural counterparts. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

3.  **Solid Methodology:** The experimental setup is sound, and the comparison against several relevant baselines, including various extensions of the entity grid model, is comprehensive. The end-to-end training procedure via a pairwise ranking objective is well-suited for coherence assessment tasks.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Failure to Reproduce a Key Baseline:** The inability to reproduce the results of a recent neural baseline from Li and Hovy (2014) is a significant concern. The reported accuracy of 19.34% on the discrimination task is extremely low and suggests a potential issue with the implementation or experimental setup for that baseline. This omission weakens the comparison to other contemporary neural approaches and should be addressed.

2.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty:** While effective, the core contribution is essentially a "neuralization" of the classic entity grid model. The work is a solid application of a standard neural architecture (CNN) to a well-known representation. For a full paper, one might expect more significant innovations in the underlying coherence representation (e.g., incorporating new types of features) or the tasks themselves.

3.  **Lack of Justification for Hyperparameters:** The justification for certain hyperparameters is not entirely clear. For instance, it is not obvious why a high-dimensional (d=100) embedding is necessary to represent grid entries from a very small vocabulary (e.g., {S, O, X, -}). Furthermore, the practical benefit of modeling longer transition chains (up to k=6) compared to previous work (k<=3) is not fully analyzed, especially given that many articles in the WSJ dataset are relatively short.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more intuition or an analysis (e.g., an ablation study) on the choice of a 100-dimensional embedding space for the small vocabulary of grammatical roles? Is such a high dimensionality crucial for performance?

2.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model, have you investigated potential reasons for the very low performance you observed? The result seems surprisingly poor and might point to a simple configuration issue that, if fixed, could provide a more meaningful comparison.

### **Additional Comments**

*   **Paper Structure and Redundancy:** The paper's structure could be streamlined. There is significant overlap between the background on entity grids in Section 1, the detailed description in Section 2, and the Related Work in Section 6. Much of the first part of Section 6 repeats information already presented. I suggest merging the relevant related work into Section 2 to create a single, consolidated background section.

*   **Minor Writing and Formatting Issues:**
    *   The paper would benefit from a final proofread by a native English speaker to correct minor grammatical errors, such as missing articles (e.g., "the WSJ corpus," "the Brown coherence toolkit").
    *   The final "roadmap" paragraph of the introduction is standard but likely unnecessary for a paper of this length and could be removed to save space.
    *   On page 1, the text refers to Figure 2 when introducing the entity grid. This reference might be confusing, as Figure 2 (on page 3) illustrates the entire neural architecture. A simpler, dedicated figure for the grid itself might have been clearer.
