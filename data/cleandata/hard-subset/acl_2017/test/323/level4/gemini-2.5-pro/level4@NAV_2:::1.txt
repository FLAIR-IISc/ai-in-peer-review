This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, building upon the well-established entity grid representation. The core idea is to use a convolutional neural network (CNN) to learn features from the grid, replacing the explicit probability calculations of entity transitions used in prior work. By operating on distributed representations of grammatical roles, the model aims to capture longer-range entity transition patterns and learn task-specific features through end-to-end training. The authors evaluate their model on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and summary coherence rating tasks, reporting state-of-the-art results that significantly outperform previous entity grid models.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  The novel application of a convolutional neural network directly to the entity grid representation. This effectively addresses a known limitation of traditional grid-based models—the difficulty of modeling long-range dependencies due to data sparsity—by leveraging the generalization power of distributed representations.
2.  A strong empirical demonstration that this neural extension of a classic, linguistically-motivated representation achieves state-of-the-art performance on three standard coherence evaluation benchmarks. The consistent and significant improvements over strong non-neural counterparts validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

### Strengths

1.  **A Principled and Well-Motivated Approach.** Rather than proposing an entirely new, end-to-end neural model from raw text, the authors intelligently combine a classic, symbolic representation (the entity grid) with a targeted neural component. The CNN is used specifically to overcome a well-understood weakness of prior entity grid models (the curse of dimensionality in modeling long transition sequences). This hybrid approach is elegant, clearly motivated, and effectively leverages the strengths of both paradigms.

2.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results.** The paper presents compelling evidence of the model's effectiveness across three different coherence tasks. The proposed Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN models consistently outperform their respective non-neural counterparts (Grid-all nouns and Extended Grid) by significant margins (e.g., absolute gains of ~4% on discrimination and >4% on summary coherence rating). Achieving state-of-the-art results on these established benchmarks strongly supports the value of the contribution.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation and Reproducibility.** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the model is described in sufficient detail, and the experiments are well-designed. The authors are also to be commended for releasing their code, which strengthens the paper by enabling verification and facilitating future research that can build upon this work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with a Key Neural Baseline.** The primary weakness is the handling of the comparison with the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014). The authors report exceptionally poor performance for this baseline (19.34% accuracy) and consequently exclude it from the main results table. This is concerning, as such a low score could indicate an issue with the experimental setup or a strong corpus-dependency that warrants further investigation. Without a successful run or a more detailed analysis of this failure, the paper's claims of state-of-the-art performance over other *neural* approaches are not fully substantiated. It leaves the reader wondering how the proposed model would fare on a task or corpus where the Li and Hovy model is known to perform well.

2.  **Limited Architectural Novelty.** While the application of a CNN to the entity grid is novel, the neural architecture itself—an embedding layer followed by convolution, max-pooling, and a final scoring layer—is a standard design widely used in NLP for sentence and document classification. The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly situated its architectural choices within this context, focusing its claims of novelty more on the unique combination of this architecture with the entity grid representation rather than on the architecture itself.

3.  **Minor Issues in Writing.** The paper is generally very readable, but it would benefit from a final round of proofreading. There are several instances of missing definite articles and some recurring awkward phrases (e.g., "limits the model to consider..."). Polishing these minor grammatical issues would improve the overall quality of the manuscript.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on your experiments with the Li and Hovy (2014) model? The reported 19.34% accuracy is surprisingly low. Did you attempt to replicate their results on their original dataset as a sanity check for your implementation? Is it possible that their model is ill-suited to the properties of the WSJ corpus (e.g., document length, stylistic variation), and if so, could you speculate on the reasons? A more thorough discussion of this result is crucial for positioning your work against other neural coherence models.
