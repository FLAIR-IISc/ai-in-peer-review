This paper presents a well-conceived extension to the entity grid model for coherence modeling. The core innovation is the use of a convolutional neural network (CNN) to learn coherent sequences of entity transitions. This approach allows the model to generalize more effectively over longer entity sequences compared to the original model, which relies on direct estimates of transition probabilities.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow. A significant strength lies in its methodological approach. Rather than proposing an entirely new, end-to-end neural architecture, the authors judiciously build upon established work and use a neural component to address a specific, well-defined limitation. This targeted application of a CNN to learn transition patterns is an elegant solution that improves upon a known weakness of the original model. The overall approach is sound and contributes a valuable perspective to the literature on coherence.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the proposed model is promising, the paper's primary weakness lies in its experimental comparison to the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014). The authors report surprisingly low performance for this baseline, which they themselves acknowledge. This result raises several critical questions for the reader:

1.  Could there have been an issue in the experimental setup or implementation of the baseline model?
2.  Is the baseline's poor performance specific to the evaluation corpus? If so, what are the corpus characteristics that cause this discrepancy?
3.  Conversely, how would the proposed model perform on a corpus or task where the Li and Hovy model is known to be effective?

A deeper investigation of these factors would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and provide the community with a clearer understanding of the relative merits of each approach.

**Post-Rebuttal Considerations**

I acknowledge the authors' response regarding the significant objective difficulties encountered when trying to run the Li and Hovy model. Such challenges with reproducing prior work are unfortunately common, and I agree that these implementation issues should not penalize the current submission. However, for the benefit of the reader and to provide important context, I strongly recommend that these efforts and the specific problems encountered be documented in the final version of the paper. As a further sanity check, it would be valuable if the authors could attempt to reproduce the original results of Li and Hovy on their original datasets, if this has not already been done.

**Minor Comments on Clarity and Language**

The paper is generally fluent and readable, but it would benefit from a careful proofreading pass, particularly concerning the use of definite articles. Several instances of missing articles were noted (e.g., on lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764).

Additionally, the phrasing "...limits the model to do X...", which appears multiple times, is slightly awkward. I suggest rephrasing for clarity, for example, to "limits the model's ability to do X," "prevents the model from doing X," or "constrains the model to X."
