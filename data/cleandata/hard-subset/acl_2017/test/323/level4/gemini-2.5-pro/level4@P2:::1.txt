### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural extension to the classic entity grid model for assessing local text coherence. The core idea is to replace the explicit counting of entity transition probabilities with a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that operates on a distributed representation of the entity grid. This approach allows the model to learn features from longer-range entity transitions while mitigating the data sparsity issues that affect traditional grid models. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for Coherence Modeling:** The primary contribution is the novel application of a CNN to the entity grid representation. This is an effective fusion of a well-established, linguistically-motivated structure (the entity grid) with a powerful neural learning mechanism (the CNN), directly addressing known limitations of previous grid-based models, such as their inability to generalize well over long entity transition sequences.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper presents a strong and thorough evaluation on three standard coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The proposed model significantly outperforms previous entity grid models and other baselines, establishing new state-of-the-art results across all tasks and demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed architecture.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Approach:** The paper presents a clear and methodologically sound approach. Rather than proposing an entirely new framework from scratch, the authors build upon the successful and intuitive entity grid model, using a neural network specifically to overcome its key weaknesses. This targeted improvement makes the contribution easy to understand and appreciate. The end-to-end training procedure further allows the model to learn task-specific features, which is a notable advantage over prior methods where feature extraction is decoupled from the downstream task.

2.  **Comprehensive and Strong Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is robust. The authors test their model on three distinct tasks with varying levels of difficulty, using a standard and appropriately large dataset (WSJ). The gains over strong non-neural counterparts are consistent and significant (e.g., an absolute improvement of ~4% on the discrimination task and >4% on summary coherence rating for the extended model). This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the neural approach.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, fluent, and easy to follow. The motivation, model architecture, and experimental setup are all described with great clarity. The authors' decision to release their code is commendable and a significant asset to the community, promoting reproducibility and facilitating future work in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with a Key Neural Baseline:** The primary weakness lies in the comparison to the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014). The authors report that this model achieved a surprisingly low accuracy (19.34%) on their dataset and consequently excluded it from the main results. This is concerning and leaves a significant gap in the experimental comparison. It raises questions about whether there was an issue in the experimental setup for this baseline, or if the model's performance is highly corpus-dependent. Without a more thorough analysis or a successful comparison, the claim of superiority over other *neural* coherence models (as opposed to just neural versions of the entity grid) is not fully substantiated. A deeper investigation into why this baseline failed—for example, by attempting to reproduce the original paper's results on its own data as a sanity check, and then analyzing the differences in the datasets—would considerably strengthen the paper's claims.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the very poor performance of the Li and Hovy (2014) model, could you provide more details about your experimental setup? Were you able to replicate their results on their original dataset to validate your implementation?

2.  Could you offer a more detailed hypothesis for why a sentence-representation-based model like Li and Hovy's might fail so dramatically on the WSJ dataset, while your entity-grid-based model succeeds? Does this point to a fundamental advantage of entity-centric models for the types of texts found in the WSJ corpus? A brief discussion of this in the paper would be very valuable for positioning your work.
