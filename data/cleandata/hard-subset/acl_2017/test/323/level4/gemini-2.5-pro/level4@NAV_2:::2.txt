**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a neural network model for assessing local text coherence. The proposed approach applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) directly to the entity grid representation of a text, a classic structure used in coherence modeling. The model learns distributed representations for grammatical roles and uses convolutional filters to identify salient entity transition patterns. It is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective to score the coherence of a given document.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary rating). The proposed model achieves new state-of-the-art results on all tasks, outperforming previous entity-grid models and other baselines by a significant margin.
2.  **An Effective Neural Architecture for the Entity Grid:** The work successfully "neuralizes" the traditional entity grid model. By using a CNN over distributed representations, the model overcomes the curse of dimensionality that limited prior methods, allowing it to capture longer-range entity transitions (e.g., up to k=8) and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion.

**Strengths**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Results:** The strongest argument for this paper is its empirical success. The proposed model consistently and significantly outperforms a range of competing methods, including the highly-cited extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011), on three different and well-established evaluation benchmarks. The gains of ~4% on the discrimination task and ~2.5% on the insertion task are substantial.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Explained Model:** The paper provides a clear motivation for moving beyond discrete, feature-engineered entity grid models. The authors do an excellent job of describing their neural architecture and offer compelling intuitions for their design choices, particularly in the "Why it works" section. This clarity makes the model easy to understand and the results convincing.
3.  **Elegant Solution to Limitations of Prior Work:** The proposed method directly addresses two key weaknesses of previous entity grid models: the inability to model long transition sequences due to data sparsity and the decoupling of feature extraction from the downstream task. The use of distributed representations and a trainable convolutional architecture provides an elegant and effective solution to both problems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Methodological Novelty:** While the application is successful, the core methodology is a straightforward combination of two well-known components: the entity grid and a standard CNN architecture. The contribution is more an effective application of existing neural techniques to a classic problem than a fundamental advance in neural network architecture or coherence modeling itself.
2.  **Unexplained Model Complexity:** The paper uses 100-dimensional embeddings to represent a very small vocabulary of grammatical roles (S, O, X, -) and a few entity-specific features. There is no justification or analysis provided for why such a high-dimensional representation is necessary. This raises questions about whether the model is unnecessarily complex and whether similar performance could be achieved with a much smaller architecture.
3.  **Unresolved Discrepancy with Prior Work:** The authors report that they were unable to reproduce the results of a recent and highly relevant neural coherence model (Li and Hovy, 2014), achieving only 19.34% accuracy on the discrimination task. Simply excluding this result is unsatisfying, as it leaves an important comparison unresolved and raises potential concerns about the experimental setup or the robustness of the competing model on this dataset. A deeper investigation is warranted.
4.  **Structural Redundancy in Writing:** The paper's organization could be improved. Section 2 (Entity Grid and Its Extensions) and much of Section 6 (Related Work) cover very similar ground, leading to repetition. Consolidating the background and related work into a single, cohesive section would make the paper more concise and easier to read.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model, could you provide more detail on your attempts to reproduce their work? What factors might explain the significant performance drop on the WSJ dataset compared to the results reported in their paper? Addressing this would help situate your model's performance more clearly against other neural approaches.
2.  Could you provide some intuition or analysis regarding the necessity of 100-dimensional embeddings for the small vocabulary of grid symbols? For instance, an ablation study showing the effect of embedding size on performance would significantly strengthen the claim that this level of complexity is required.
3.  A key motivation for your model is its ability to capture longer-range dependencies (k > 3). Could you provide an analysis of the model's sensitivity to the filter/window size? It would be valuable to see how performance changes with different values of k, which would help quantify the practical benefit of modeling these longer transitions.
