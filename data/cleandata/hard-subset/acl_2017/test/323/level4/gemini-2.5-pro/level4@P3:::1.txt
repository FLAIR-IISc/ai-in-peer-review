### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, building upon the well-established entity grid representation. The core idea is to apply a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) directly to the entity grid. This approach allows the model to learn distributed representations of entity transitions and thereby capture longer-range dependencies than traditional entity grid models, which are limited by the curse of dimensionality when using discrete features. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective, enabling it to learn task-specific features. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and demonstrate that it achieves state-of-the-art results, significantly outperforming its non-neural counterparts.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for the Entity Grid:** The primary contribution is the application of a Convolutional Neural Network to the entity grid representation. This is a well-motivated approach that directly addresses a known limitation of prior grid-based models: their inability to effectively model entity transition patterns longer than 2-3 steps due to data sparsity. By using distributed embeddings for grid roles and convolutional filters to detect patterns, the model can learn robust features over longer sequences.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results on Standard Coherence Tasks:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on three different tasks, showing consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including the extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011). The gains of up to 4% absolute on the discrimination and summary rating tasks are substantial and establish a new state of the art for these benchmarks.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Design:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating its approach. Instead of proposing a completely new paradigm, it identifies specific, well-understood weaknesses in a successful existing model (the entity grid) and applies a targeted neural solution (a CNN) to resolve them. This combination of a classic, interpretable NLP representation with a modern neural architecture is a strong and compelling research strategy.

2.  **Convincing and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on three distinct and standard tasks, using a large and well-established dataset (WSJ). The results are consistently strong across all tasks, and the improvements over the non-neural versions of the entity grid model are statistically significant. This provides robust evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed neural architecture.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained logically, and the experimental setup is detailed. The authors' decision to release their code is commendable and greatly benefits the community by ensuring the work is reproducible.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Inadequate Comparison to Other Neural Coherence Models:** The most significant weakness is the handling of the comparison to the neural model of Li and Hovy (2014). The authors state that this model achieved "very disappointing" results on their dataset (19.34% accuracy) and consequently excluded it from the main comparison tables. This is problematic because Li and Hovy's work is the most closely related neural approach to coherence modeling. Its poor performance raises critical questions that are left unanswered:
    *   Was there a potential issue in the implementation or experimental setup for this baseline?
    *   Is the Li and Hovy model's performance highly corpus-dependent, failing on WSJ data while succeeding on others?
    *   Conversely, how would the model proposed in this paper perform on the datasets where the Li and Hovy model was originally shown to be successful?

    Without a more thorough investigation and discussion of these points, it is difficult to properly situate the paper's contribution within the broader landscape of *neural* coherence models. The current comparison primarily establishes superiority over *non-neural* entity grid models, which, while valuable, is an incomplete picture.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide more detail on your experiments with the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Given the surprisingly low accuracy you reported, it suggests a potential mismatch between the model and the task setup. Did you attempt to reproduce their original results on their datasets as a sanity check to ensure your implementation was working correctly? A more detailed analysis of why that model failed so dramatically would significantly strengthen your paper's claims.

### **Additional Comments**

The paper is very fluent and readable. However, there are numerous instances of missing definite articles ("the"). I would recommend a careful proofread of the final version with article usage specifically in mind (e.g., line 92 "output of L(G) is a tensor", line 132 "output layer of the network", line 488 "gain in insertion is less verbose").

Additionally, the expression "...limits the model to do X...", which appears several times, is slightly unnatural. Clearer alternatives would be "...limits the model's ability to do X..." or "...prevents the model from doing X...".
