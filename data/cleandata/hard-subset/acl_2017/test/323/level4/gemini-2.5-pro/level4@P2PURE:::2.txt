This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) for modeling text coherence. The model applies a CNN to the entity grid, a well-established representation for this task.

The approach is well-motivated and clearly described. The authors should be commended for the insightful discussion of the intuitions behind their design choices, particularly the rationale for using a CNN and the section titled 'Why it works'. The evaluation is extensive, covering several tasks, and demonstrates that the proposed model outperforms previous methods.

However, there are a few points of concern. The failure to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014) is worrying and suggests a potential implementation or modeling error that should be addressed. From a novelty standpoint, the proposed model is a relatively straightforward application of a neural network to the existing entity grid framework. In this vein, the justification for using high-dimensional (100-d) vectors to represent low-dimensional grid entries (e.g., a 4-dimensional feature vector) is unclear. The paper would be strengthened by an explanation of how this high-dimensional projection benefits the model. Furthermore, while optimizing directly for coherence ranking is a sound approach, the performance difference between models using transition chains of length k=3 versus k=6 may be marginal, especially given that many articles in the WSJ corpus are relatively short.

The writing could also be more concise. There are several instances of repetition; for example, the introduction to entity grids is presented multiple times. Section 6, in particular, largely repeats material from Section 2. I suggest merging the novel content from Section 6 into Section 2 and renaming that section to 'Related Work' to improve the paper's structure and eliminate redundancy.

In summary, the paper presents a solid implementation of a neural network for entity-grid-based coherence modeling. However, for a full paper, the contribution feels incremental, essentially being a neural adaptation of an existing framework. The work would be more impactful with greater innovation in the underlying representation (e.g., incorporating new features) or its application to new tasks.

Minor Points:
*   The paper would benefit from proofreading by a native English speaker. For instance, articles (e.g., 'the') are often missing before 'WSJ corpus' and 'Brown... toolkit'.
*   On page 1, bottom left column: 'Figure 2' should be 'Figure 1'.
*   On page 1: 'Firstly/Secondly' should be 'First, Second'.
*   On page 1: Consider rephrasing 'limits the model to' as 'prevents the model from considering...' for clarity.
*   The standard concluding paragraph in the introduction, which outlines the paper's structure, could be removed for conciseness, as it is not essential for a paper of this length.
