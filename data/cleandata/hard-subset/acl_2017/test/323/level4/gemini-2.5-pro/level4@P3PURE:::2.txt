This paper presents a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach for modeling text coherence. The proposed model is based on the well-established entity grid representation, applying a CNN to learn coherence patterns directly from the grid.

The approach is well-motivated and the model is described in detail. I particularly appreciated the clear discussion of the intuitions behind specific design choices, such as the use of a CNN and the dedicated "Why it works" section. The evaluation is extensive, covering several standard coherence tasks, and the results demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms previous methods.

However, there are several points that should be addressed:

*   A significant concern is the failure to reproduce a key result from previous work (Li & Hovy, 2014). This discrepancy is concerning and may suggest an implementation or modeling error that the authors should address.

*   While the model is effective, its contribution feels somewhat incremental, essentially being a "neuralization" of the classic entity grid model. In this context, certain design choices require further justification. For example, it is unclear why 100-dimensional vectors are necessary to represent a grid entry that is fundamentally four-dimensional (or slightly larger for the extended grid). The paper would be strengthened by an explanation of how this high-dimensional embedding benefits the model.

*   The paper highlights the model's ability to learn longer-range transition chains (up to k=6) compared to previous work (k=3). However, the practical impact of this is not fully clear, especially given that many articles in the WSJ corpus can be quite short. It would be helpful to understand if this expanded window size provides a significant advantage in practice.

*   The writing is generally clear but could be more concise. There is significant repetition, particularly in the introduction to entity grids and the discussion of related work. For instance, much of Section 6 repeats material already presented in Section 2. I recommend merging the related work portions of Section 6 into Section 2 and renaming Section 2 to "Related Work" to improve the paper's structure and flow.

In summary, this paper presents a solid application of a neural network to the well-established entity-grid representation for coherence. However, the overall contribution feels more like a consolidation and "neuralization" of previous work rather than a significant leap forward. For a full paper, I would have expected more innovation, perhaps in the form of novel features for the grid representation or the application to new tasks.

Minor Points:
*   The paper would benefit from proofreading by a native English speaker, as there are many missing articles (e.g., "the WSJ corpus", "the Brown... toolkit").
*   Page 1, bottom of the left column: "Figure 2" should be "Figure 1".
*   Page 1: "Firstly" and "Secondly" should be changed to "First" and "Second".
*   Page 1: Consider rephrasing "limits the model to" as "prevents the model from considering...".
*   The standard concluding paragraph in Section 1, which outlines the paper's structure, could be removed for conciseness in such a short paper.
