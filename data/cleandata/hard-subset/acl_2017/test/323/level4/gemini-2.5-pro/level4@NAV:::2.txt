This paper presents a convolutional neural network (CNN) designed to model textual coherence. The model builds upon the established entity grid representation, applying a CNN architecture to it.

The approach is well-motivated and clearly articulated. A notable strength is the transparent discussion of the intuitions behind design choices, particularly the rationale for using a CNN and the section titled 'Why it works'.

The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across several tasks, demonstrating that the proposed model outperforms previous methods. However, it is concerning that a result from prior work (Li/Hovy, 2014) could not be reproduced. This discrepancy points to a potential implementation or modeling issue that requires clarification.

From a conceptual standpoint, the model is a relatively straightforward neural adaptation of the entity grid. The justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent a four-dimensional grid entry (or slightly more for the extended grid) is not sufficiently explained. While optimizing directly for coherence ranking is a clear benefit, the performance difference between transition chains of k=3 versus k=6 might be marginal, especially considering the short length of many articles in the source corpus.

The writing is somewhat verbose, with noticeable repetition in several sections, such as the introduction to entity grids. Much of Section 6 is redundant, as related work is also covered in Section 2. It is recommended that the first two-thirds of Section 6 be removed and any unique content be integrated into Section 2, which could then be appropriately retitled.

Overall, this work is a solid implementation of a neural network applied to entity-grid-based coherence modeling. However, for a full paper that aims to consolidate previous work, the contribution could be stronger, perhaps through more significant innovations in the feature representations or explored tasks.

Minor suggestions:
- The manuscript would benefit from proofreading by a native English speaker due to numerous missing articles (e.g., 'the WSJ corpus,' 'the Brown ... toolkit').
- p.1, bottom left column: 'Figure 2' should be corrected to 'Figure 1'.
- p.1: 'Firstly/Secondly' should be revised to 'First, Second'.
- p.1: Consider rephrasing 'limits the model to' with a clearer alternative like 'prevents the model from considering...'.
- The standard final paragraph in Section 1 outlining the paper's structure could be omitted, as it is not essential for a paper of this length.
