This paper presents a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for modeling text coherence. The model is based on the well-established entity grid representation, applying a CNN architecture to learn coherence patterns directly from the grid.

The approach is well-motivated and clearly described. I particularly commend the authors for the transparent discussion of the intuitions behind their design decisions, such as the choice of a CNN and the insightful section titled "Why it works." The paper also includes an extensive evaluation across several tasks, demonstrating that the proposed model outperforms previous methods.

However, there are several points that weaken the paper in its current form.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Reproducibility and Experimental Setup:** A significant concern is the failure to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014). This discrepancy suggests a potential error in the implementation or modeling of the baseline, which casts some doubt on the validity of the comparisons. The authors should investigate this issue thoroughly and provide a clear explanation for the difference in results.

2.  **Model Contribution and Novelty:** While effective, the proposed model can be viewed as a relatively direct neural adaptation of the entity grid framework. The paper presents a solid implementation of this idea, but for a full conference paper, the contribution feels somewhat incremental. The work consolidates previous research by applying a standard neural architecture, but it lacks significant innovation in the underlying coherence representation (e.g., incorporating new features) or in the tasks it addresses.

3.  **Model Design Justification:** The motivation behind using high-dimensional vectors (100-d) to represent a grid entry that is fundamentally low-dimensional (four features, or slightly more for the extended grid) is not entirely clear. The paper would be strengthened by a justification for this design choice and an analysis of how this high-dimensional embedding contributes to the model's performance. Furthermore, it is questionable whether the distinction between transition chains of up to k=3 versus k=6 sentences makes a substantial practical difference, especially given that many articles in the WSJ corpus can be quite short.

**Writing and Structure:**

The paper could be made more concise, as there is some repetition. For instance, the introduction to entity grids is explained in multiple places. Most notably, the first two-thirds of Section 6 (Related Work) largely repeats information that belongs in Section 2. I recommend the following restructuring:
*   Delete the redundant parts of Section 6.
*   Merge the remaining, non-redundant content of Section 6 into Section 2.
*   Rename Section 2 to "Related Work" and structure it with appropriate subsections.

**Minor Points:**

*   The paper would benefit from proofreading by a native English speaker. There are several instances of missing articles (e.g., "the WSJ corpus," "the Brown... toolkit").
*   On page 1, bottom of the left column, the reference to "Figure 2" should be "Figure 1."
*   On page 1, "Firstly" and "Secondly" should be changed to "First" and "Second" for standard academic style.
*   On page 1, the phrase "limits the model to" could be rephrased for clarity, for example, to "prevents the model from considering..."
*   The final paragraph of the introduction, which outlines the paper's structure, is standard but unnecessary for a paper of this length and could be removed to improve conciseness.

Overall, this paper presents a solid and well-executed application of a CNN to an established coherence representation. The results are strong and the model is clearly explained. However, the concerns about the failure to reproduce a key baseline, the incremental nature of the contribution, and issues with writing and structure prevent me from giving a stronger recommendation at this time. Addressing these points would significantly improve the quality of the paper.
