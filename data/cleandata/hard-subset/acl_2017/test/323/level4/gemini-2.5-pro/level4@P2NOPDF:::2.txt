### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) for modeling text coherence. The model is built upon the well-established entity grid representation, applying a CNN to learn patterns from the grid structure. The approach is evaluated on several standard coherence tasks, and the results show an improvement over previous methods.

**Strengths**

*   The approach is well-motivated and clearly described. The authors are to be commended for the clear discussion of the intuitions behind their design choices, particularly in the section titled "Why it works."
*   A key strength of the paper is its extensive evaluation across several coherence-related tasks. The experiments are thorough and demonstrate that the proposed model generally outperforms previous methods.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

*   A significant concern is the failure to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014). This discrepancy is worrisome as it may indicate an issue with the experimental setup, the reimplementation of the baseline, or the underlying model itself. The authors should investigate this thoroughly and provide a detailed explanation in the paper.
*   While the results are strong, the proposed model can be seen as a relatively straightforward neural adaptation of the classic entity grid framework. For a full conference paper, a more substantial conceptual contribution might be expected, such as innovations in the underlying representation (e.g., incorporating additional features) or the application of the model to novel tasks.
*   The motivation for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent grid entries, which are inherently low-dimensional (e.g., four syntactic roles, plus a few extensions), is not sufficiently justified. The paper would be strengthened by an explanation of how this high-dimensional embedding contributes to the model's performance. An ablation study or further analysis on this design choice would be welcome.
*   Similarly, the paper could clarify the practical impact of extending transition chains from k=3 sentences to k=6. Given that many articles in the WSJ corpus can be quite short, the benefit of this larger window size is not immediately obvious and warrants further discussion or analysis.

**Clarity and Presentation**

The paper is generally well-written but could be made more concise.
*   There is significant repetition, particularly in the introduction of the entity grid model.
*   Specifically, the first two-thirds of Section 6 (Related Work) largely repeats content that is, or should be, in Section 2. It is recommended to merge the relevant, non-redundant parts of Section 6 into a unified "Related Work" section (currently Section 2) and remove the repetition entirely. The remainder of Section 6 could then be integrated into this new, consolidated section.

**Minor Points**

*   The paper would benefit from proofreading by a native English speaker, as there are several instances of missing articles (e.g., "the WSJ corpus," "the Brown... toolkit").
*   On page 1, bottom left column, "Figure 2" should be "Figure 1."
*   On page 1, "Firstly/Secondly" should be changed to "First, Second" for standard academic style.
*   On page 1, consider rephrasing "limits the model to" as something like "prevents the model from considering..." for improved clarity.
*   The standard final paragraph in the introduction, which outlines the paper's structure, could be removed for conciseness, as it is not essential for a paper of this length.
