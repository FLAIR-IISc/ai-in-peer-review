### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a neural network model for assessing local text coherence. The model applies a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) directly to the entity grid, a well-established representation for coherence modeling. The grid's discrete symbols (representing grammatical roles) are first mapped to dense embeddings. A convolutional layer with wide convolutions then extracts features from entity transition patterns, which are subsequently pooled and fed into a final layer to produce a coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for Entity-Grid Coherence:** The core contribution is the application of a CNN to the entity grid representation. This "neuralization" of a classic method allows the model to learn distributed representations for grammatical roles and capture longer-range entity transitions (e.g., window sizes of 5-6) in a way that is less susceptible to the data sparsity issues that limit traditional, feature-based grid models.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper provides a strong and comprehensive evaluation on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The proposed model consistently outperforms previous non-neural and neural baselines, establishing a new state of the art on these benchmarks.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The most compelling argument for this paper is its strong empirical results. The proposed model achieves significant absolute gains over strong baselines, including the extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011). For example, it improves discrimination accuracy by nearly 4% and summary coherence rating by over 4%. Achieving state-of-the-art results on multiple, well-established tasks demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Explained Model:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating its design choices. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional entity grid models (e.g., curse of dimensionality, decoupled feature extraction) and explain how their neural architecture addresses them. The intuition behind using a CNN to capture location-invariant transition patterns is well-justified, and the section titled "Why it works" provides a clear, intuitive explanation of the model's mechanism, making the work highly accessible.

3.  **Principled End-to-End Learning Framework:** By framing the problem as a pairwise ranking task and optimizing the model parameters directly for this objective, the approach moves beyond the decoupled feature engineering of previous grid-based models. This allows the network to learn task-specific features and representations that are optimized for distinguishing between more and less coherent texts, which is a more principled approach than relying on pre-defined, hand-engineered features like transition probabilities.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Modest Conceptual Novelty:** While effective, the core idea of the paper is an application of a standard neural architecture (a CNN) to a pre-existing, classic NLP representation (the entity grid). The work is more of an effective "neuralization" of a known model rather than the proposal of a fundamentally new method for modeling coherence. Given the trend of applying neural networks to existing NLP tasks, the conceptual leap is somewhat limited.

2.  **Insufficient Justification for Model Complexity and Parameters:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of its own model. For instance, using 100-dimensional embeddings to represent a tiny vocabulary of grammatical roles (e.g., {S, O, X, -}) seems excessive. It is unclear if this high dimensionality is necessary or what it learns. Furthermore, while the ability to model longer transitions (k=5 or 6) is claimed as a key advantage, there is no ablation study to show how much this contributes to the performance gains compared to the effect of distributed representations or end-to-end training alone. Comparing against a version of the same model with a smaller transition window (e.g., k=3) would strengthen this claim.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The authors report being unable to reproduce the results of a prominent and closely related neural coherence model (Li and Hovy, 2014), obtaining "very disappointing" results. While the transparency is appreciated, the inability to get a competitive baseline from this work leaves a significant gap in the comparison to recent neural approaches. This should be investigated further, as it may suggest a problem with the experimental setup or a lack of robustness in the baseline model that is worth noting.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more intuition or analysis on what is being learned in the 100-dimensional role embeddings? Given the small vocabulary size, this seems like a very high-dimensional space. Have you experimented with lower-dimensional embeddings, and how did that affect performance?

2.  The performance gains are attributed partly to modeling longer transitions. Could you provide an ablation study that isolates this contribution? Specifically, how does a `Grid-CNN` with a filter window size of k=3 compare to the reported model with k=6 and the non-neural `Grid-all nouns` model? This would help disentangle the benefits of the longer context window from the benefits of the neural architecture itself.

### Additional Comments (Minor Issues)

*   The paper's writing could be more concise. Section 2 and the first part of Section 6 (Related Work) are highly repetitive, as they both introduce the entity grid and its extensions. These sections should be consolidated.
*   The paper would benefit from proofreading by a native English speaker. There are several instances of missing articles (e.g., "WSJ corpus" instead of "the WSJ corpus", "Brown coherence toolkit" instead of "the Brown coherence toolkit").
*   p. 1, bottom left column: "Figure 2" should be "Figure 1".
*   p. 1: "Firstly, Secondly" is less common in formal writing than "First, Second".
*   p. 1: Consider rephrasing "limits the model to consider" to something like "prevents the model from considering".
*   The final paragraph of the introduction, which outlines the paper structure, is standard but arguably unnecessary for a short paper and could be removed to save space.
