This paper presents a well-motivated extension of the entity grid model for assessing text coherence. The authors employ a convolutional neural network (CNN) to learn coherent sequences of entity transitions, which allows for better generalization over longer entity sequences compared to the direct probability estimates used in the original model.

The paper is well-written and the proposed approach is methodologically sound. I appreciate that instead of introducing a completely novel, end-to-end neural architecture, the authors build upon established work, using a neural component to address a specific, well-defined limitation of the original model.

My primary suggestion for improvement concerns the experimental comparison to the existing neural coherence model by Li and Hovy (2014). The authors note their surprise at the very low performance of this baseline on their task. This result raises several questions: Was there a potential error in the experimental setup for the baseline model? Is the model's poor performance highly dependent on the corpus? If so, how would the model proposed in this paper perform on a task or dataset where the Li and Hovy model is known to be more successful? A deeper investigation into these factors would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and provide a more conclusive comparison.

On a minor note, while the paper is generally fluent and readable, it would benefit from careful proofreading. In several places, definite articles ("the") are missing (e.g., on lines 92, 132, 174, 488, 490, 547, 674, 764, and likely others). I recommend a pass specifically focused on article usage. Additionally, the recurring phrase "...limits the model to do X..." is stylistically unusual. Alternatives such as "limits the model's ability to do X" or "prevents the model from doing X" would enhance clarity.

**Post-Rebuttal Update:**

My final recommendation has been adjusted to 4 (Accept) after considering the author response. I agree that the authors should not be penalized for objective difficulties encountered when attempting to run another research group's software.

For the camera-ready version, I strongly suggest that the paper documents the efforts made to test the Li and Hovy system and the specific problems encountered. This transparency is valuable for the community. Furthermore, as a sanity check, I would also recommend that the authors attempt to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy on their original datasets, if they have not done so already, to confirm the functionality of their implementation.
