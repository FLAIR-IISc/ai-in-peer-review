### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network approach for modeling local text coherence. The proposed model applies a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to the entity grid, a well-established representation for text coherence. The core idea is to learn distributed representations (embeddings) for the grammatical roles in the grid and use a CNN to capture local transition patterns over these embeddings. The entire model is trained end-to-end for a specific task using a pairwise ranking objective.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on three standard coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), outperforming previous entity grid models by a significant margin.
2.  **An End-to-End Framework for the Entity Grid:** The work successfully reframes the entity grid model within a neural, end-to-end learning framework. This allows features to be learned in a task-specific manner, addressing a key limitation of prior work where feature extraction was decoupled from the downstream task.
3.  **A Novel Application of CNNs for Coherence Modeling:** The paper introduces a novel method for applying CNNs to the entity grid representation to capture longer-range entity transitions than was feasible with previous discrete models.

### **Strengths**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The strongest argument for acceptance is the model's excellent performance. The experimental results, presented in Tables 3 and 4, show consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines across multiple datasets and tasks. For instance, the Extended Grid-CNN achieves an absolute gain of nearly 4% on the discrimination task and over 2.5% on the more challenging insertion task compared to the previous state-of-the-art non-neural model.
2.  **Clear Motivation and Exposition:** The paper is well-written and the proposed approach is clearly motivated. The authors effectively explain the limitations of traditional entity grid models (e.g., the curse of dimensionality for long transitions) and provide clear intuitions for their design choices, such as the use of a CNN and the brief but helpful "Why it works" section.
3.  **Effective End-to-End Learning:** The pairwise ranking approach allows the model to learn task-specific features directly, which is a conceptual improvement over prior methods. The strong results validate this design choice, suggesting that learning the representation jointly with the ranking objective is more effective than using pre-defined, task-agnostic features.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty:** While the results are strong, the core idea is a relatively straightforward "neuralization" of the classic entity grid model. The approach applies a standard CNN architecture to a pre-existing, well-known text representation. The paper does not propose innovations in the underlying representation of coherence itself (e.g., by incorporating new features or information sources). As such, the contribution feels more incremental and application-focused rather than a fundamental advance in coherence theory or modeling.
2.  **Insufficient Justification for Model Complexity:** The paper uses 100-dimensional embeddings to represent a very small vocabulary of grammatical roles (S, O, X, -), even in the basic model. There is no justification for why such a high-dimensional space is necessary for this task. It is unclear if this complexity is crucial for performance or simply a default choice. Similarly, while the model is motivated by its ability to capture longer transition chains (e.g., k=6), the paper does not analyze the impact of the filter window size or demonstrate that this capability is critical to its success, especially on the WSJ dataset where many articles can be short.
3.  **Issues with Experimental Comparison and Reproducibility:** The authors report that they were unable to reproduce the results of a key related work (Li and Hovy, 2014), obtaining a surprisingly low accuracy of 19.34%. While excluding a non-working baseline is understandable, this failure raises questions about the experimental setup and potentially weakens the claim of a comprehensive comparison to recent neural coherence models. This should be investigated and clarified.
4.  **Paper Organization and Redundancy:** The paper's structure could be improved. Section 2 (Entity Grid) and Section 6 (Related Work) are highly redundant. Much of the first part of Section 6 simply repeats the background already provided in Section 2. These sections should be consolidated to create a single, non-repetitive "Background and Related Work" section, which would make the paper more concise.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the failure to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014)? This is a highly relevant neural baseline, and the reported 19.34% accuracy on the WSJ dataset seems exceptionally low, suggesting a potential issue in the implementation or experimental setup. Clarification on this point is important for contextualizing your results.
2.  What is the justification for using 100-dimensional embeddings for a small vocabulary of grammatical roles? Have you performed any ablation studies with lower-dimensional embeddings, and if so, what was the impact on performance?
3.  One of your model's key advantages is its ability to capture longer-range dependencies (e.g., filter window sizes of 5-6). Could you provide an analysis showing the performance impact of varying the filter window size? Specifically, how much performance is gained by moving from k=3 (a common choice in prior work) to the optimal k found in your experiments? This would help quantify the practical benefit of this modeling choice.
