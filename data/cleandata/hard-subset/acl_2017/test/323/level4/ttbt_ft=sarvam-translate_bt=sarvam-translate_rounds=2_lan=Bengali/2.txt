This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for text coherence modeling. This model is based on the widely known entity grid representation for coherence, but a CNN is superimposed on top. The method is provided with sufficient reasoning and details, especially a clear discussion of the concepts behind some design decisions (such as the "Why CNN" and "Why it Works" sections), which I particularly appreciate.

A comprehensive evaluation has been conducted on several works, which shows that the proposed method has surpassed previous methods. However, the previous results could not be reproduced, which is strange: the results of Li/Hovey (2014) indicate an implementation or modeling error that should be addressed.

Still, the model is a relatively simple 'neuralization' of the entity grid model. I don't understand why a 100-dimensional vector is needed to represent a four-dimensional grid entry (or a few more in the case of an extended grid). How does this help? I understand that learning a good model optimized for direct coherence ranking will help, but the difference between the k=3 and k=6 transition chains may not make a big difference, especially since many WSJ articles can be very short.

The text seems a bit long, the paper repeats some parts in a few places, for example, the introduction to the entity grid. In particular, the work related to section 2 is also presented, so the first 2/3 of section 6 should be repeated and deleted (or added to section 2 if necessary). The rest of section 6 should probably be added under a sub-paragraph under section 2 (then the name of section 2 should be changed and the related work should be done).

Overall, it seems to be a difficult implementation of a neural network model for entity-grid-based coherence. However, considering the proposed integration of previous work, I would expect more from a complete paper, such as innovations in presentation (other features?) or work.

A few minor points:
- This paper could benefit from proof-reading by a native speaker: the article is missing in many places, such as 'The WSJ Corpus' (2x), 'The Brown ... Toolkit' (2x), etc.
- P.1 bottom left column: 'Figure 2' -> 'Figure 1'
- P.1 firstly/secondly -> first, second
- P.1 'limits the model' -> 'model prevents considering ...'?
- Consider removing the 'standard' last paragraph of section 1, since it is not necessary to follow such a short paper.
