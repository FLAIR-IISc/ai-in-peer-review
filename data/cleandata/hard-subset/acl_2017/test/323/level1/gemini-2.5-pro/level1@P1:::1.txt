**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel neural architecture for modeling local text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The core idea is to apply a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) directly to the entity grid. Grammatical roles (and optional entity-specific features) in the grid are first mapped to dense embeddings. A 1D convolutional layer then slides over each entity's transition column to learn abstract, location-invariant transition patterns. Max-pooling selects the most salient features, which are fed to an output layer to produce a final coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating—and show that it achieves new state-of-the-art results, significantly outperforming previous entity grid models.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and effective neural architecture for the entity grid.** The paper presents a well-motivated "neuralization" of the classic entity grid model. By using embeddings and convolutions, the model elegantly overcomes the "curse of dimensionality" that limited previous discrete models to short transition patterns. This allows it to learn distributed representations of transitions and capture longer-range dependencies.
2.  **Strong empirical evidence demonstrating the superiority of the neural approach.** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across three distinct and challenging tasks. The proposed models consistently and significantly outperform strong, directly comparable non-neural baselines (Elsner & Charniak, 2011), establishing a new state of the art and clearly validating the effectiveness of the architecture.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model:** The application of a CNN to the entity grid is a clever and natural architectural choice. Treating each entity's transition sequence as a 1D signal for a convolutional filter is an intuitive way to model transition patterns. The use of embeddings to represent grid symbols (e.g., 'S', 'O', 'PERSON-S') is an effective solution to the data sparsity and feature explosion problems that plagued prior work. The entire pipeline, from the grid input to the end-to-end ranking-based training, is sound and well-designed.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper presents convincing and strong results. The proposed models achieve significant absolute gains over powerful baselines on all three evaluation tasks: ~4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary rating. These are not minor improvements, and they clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed architecture.
3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong. The authors use standard, well-understood datasets (WSJ, DUC'03) and evaluation tasks. Crucially, they compare against the most relevant and competitive baselines, namely the extended entity grid models of Elsner & Charniak (2011), using the publicly available Brown coherence toolkit for a fair comparison. The inclusion of significance testing and reporting averages over multiple runs adds to the reliability of the conclusions.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model is described clearly, and Figure 2 provides an excellent visualization of the architecture and training process. The authors' decision to release their code is highly commendable and a significant asset to the community, enabling verification of their results and facilitating future work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Analysis and Insight:** The paper strongly motivates the model by its ability to learn "task-specific high level features" and capture "long range entity transitions," but provides no analysis to substantiate these claims. The "Why it works" section is purely speculative. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an analysis of the learned filters. What kinds of transition patterns does the model learn to associate with coherence? Is it possible to visualize or interpret them? Furthermore, an ablation study on the filter window size (`k`) is needed to empirically show the benefit of using larger windows (e.g., 5-8) compared to the smaller ones (k<=3) used in traditional models. Without this analysis, the performance gains feel like a black box.
2.  **Incomplete Comparison to Other Neural Models:** The authors' decision to exclude the neural model of Li and Hovy (2014) from the main results table is a weakness. Simply stating that the results were "very disappointing" is insufficient. A proper scientific comparison requires either successfully running the baseline or providing a detailed analysis of why it failed (e.g., dataset mismatch, inherent architectural limitations for this task). As it stands, the paper demonstrates superiority over non-neural models but leaves its standing relative to other contemporary neural approaches unclear.
3.  **Uneven Feature Comparison in the Extended Model:** The proposed Extended Grid-CNN uses only three entity-specific features, whereas its non-neural counterpart, the Extended Grid (E&C), uses nine. While it is impressive that the neural model still wins, this is not an apples-to-apples comparison of the models' ability to leverage rich features. This methodological choice weakens the claim about the neural model's superiority in incorporating entity-specific information, as it's unclear what the performance would be if all nine features were incorporated.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide some qualitative analysis of the learned convolutional filters? For instance, can you show examples of transition patterns that receive high activation from specific filters, and do these patterns intuitively correspond to coherent or incoherent discourse phenomena?
2.  The key advantage claimed over previous grid models is the ability to model longer transitions. Could you please provide an ablation study on the filter window size (`k`)? How does performance change as you vary `k` from 2 up to the optimal value found (5 or 6)? This would provide direct evidence for the importance of modeling longer-range transitions.
3.  Can you please elaborate on the potential reasons for the poor performance of the Li and Hovy (2014) model on your dataset? Do you believe their sentence-compositional approach is fundamentally less suited to the sentence ordering tasks than your entity-focused approach, and if so, why?
