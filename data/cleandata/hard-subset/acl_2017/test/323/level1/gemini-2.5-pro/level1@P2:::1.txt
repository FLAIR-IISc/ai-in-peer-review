**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper proposes a neural model for assessing local text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The core idea is to replace the traditional, discrete feature engineering approach of entity grid models with a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates on learned, distributed representations of grammatical roles. The model first transforms the symbolic entity grid into a tensor of dense vectors via an embedding lookup layer. It then applies 1D convolutions along each entity column to learn representations of local entity transitions. A max-pooling layer identifies the most salient transition patterns, which are then used to compute a final coherence score for the text. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective neural architecture for the entity grid.** The paper presents a well-motivated "neuralization" of the classic entity grid model. By applying a CNN to learned embeddings, the model elegantly overcomes the primary limitations of its predecessors, namely the curse of dimensionality that prevented them from modeling long-range transitions and the task-agnostic nature of their feature extraction.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on standard coherence evaluation tasks.** The paper demonstrates significant and consistent improvements over strong baselines, including the widely-used extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011). The gains are substantial across three different tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary rating), providing strong empirical validation for the proposed approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the long-standing limitations of traditional entity grid models, specifically their reliance on sparse, discrete features which restricts the length of transitions that can be modeled. The proposed neural architecture directly and effectively addresses these limitations. The end-to-end training approach also solves the issue of task-agnostic feature engineering. This strong motivation makes the contribution clear and impactful.
2.  **Sound and Well-Suited Model Architecture:** The choice of a 1D convolutional network is a natural fit for processing entity grids. A CNN is adept at learning position-invariant local patterns, which maps perfectly to the task of identifying characteristic entity transition patterns (e.g., a subject-to-subject transition) regardless of where they occur in the text. The architecture is simple, clearly explained, and demonstrates a thoughtful application of neural methods to a structured linguistic representation, rather than treating the text as an unstructured sequence of words.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and methodologically sound. The authors test their model on three standard tasks and use the large, general-domain WSJ corpus, justifying this choice well. Crucially, they compare their neural models directly against their non-neural counterparts (Grid-CNN vs. Grid-all nouns; Extended Grid-CNN vs. Extended Grid), which provides a very clear measure of the performance gain attributable to the neural architecture. The reported improvements are statistically significant and large enough to be practically meaningful (e.g., ~4% absolute gain in discrimination accuracy).
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model, training procedure, and experiments are described in sufficient detail to understand the work. The authors' statement that they have released their code is a major strength, enhancing the paper's value to the community by facilitating replication and future extensions.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Simplistic Integration of Entity-Specific Features:** The proposed method for incorporating entity features (e.g., creating atomic symbols like 'PERSON-S') is a simple first step but lacks sophistication. This approach causes the vocabulary of roles/features to grow combinatorially and prevents the model from learning shared representations. For example, the model cannot easily learn that 'PERSON-S' and 'ORG-S' share the property of being a subject, or that 'PERSON-S' and 'PERSON-O' share the property of referring to a person. A more principled approach, such as using separate embedding channels for roles and features and concatenating them, could lead to better generalization and a more scalable model.
2.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows that the model works well, but it offers little insight into *why* it works or *what* it has learned. The convolutional filters are meant to capture salient transition patterns, but no analysis is provided to inspect what these patterns are. Are they linguistically plausible? Does the model learn to recognize longer-range dependencies than the k=3 transitions used in prior work? A qualitative analysis of the learned filters or a targeted error analysis would have significantly strengthened the paper's contribution from a scientific perspective, moving beyond a pure engineering result.
3.  **Insufficient Discussion of a Key Related Work:** The authors dismiss the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014) by stating it achieved "very disappointing" results on their dataset. While this may be the case, dismissing a highly relevant neural baseline without further explanation is a weakness. A brief discussion of potential reasons for this discrepancy (e.g., differences in model architecture, suitability for longer documents in WSJ) would provide a more complete picture of the landscape of neural coherence models and add more context to the authors' own results.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the integration of entity-specific features, have you considered alternative methods to creating combined atomic symbols, such as concatenating the embedding for the grammatical role with embeddings for each feature type? This might allow for better parameter sharing and generalization.
2.  Could you provide any analysis or visualization of the patterns learned by the convolutional filters? It would be very insightful to see if the model learns linguistically meaningful long-range transitions that were difficult to capture with previous discrete models.
3.  The model treats each entity's transition column independently. Have you considered architectures that could model interactions between entities, for example, by applying a 2D convolution over the entire grid or adding a subsequent layer to compose features across the different entity columns?
