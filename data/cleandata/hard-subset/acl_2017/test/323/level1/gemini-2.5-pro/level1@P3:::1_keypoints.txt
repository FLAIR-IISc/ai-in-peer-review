- Proposes a CNN-based neuralization of the entity grid that embeds grid symbols and applies 1D convolutions over entity columns to model transition patterns end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective.
- Uses distributed representations and convolutional filters to address sparsity and enable modeling of longer-range entity transitions.
- Reports state-of-the-art results on three coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating), outperforming strong non-neural baselines.
- Primary contribution: an elegant, well-motivated adaptation of CNNs to the entity grid addressing limitations of discrete n-gram transitions.
- Strong empirical contribution: thorough evaluation on WSJ with statistically significant gains (up to ~4%) and experiments including pre-training for low-resource tasks.
- Paper is clear, well-written, includes helpful figures, detailed hyperparameters, and promises code release for reproducibility.
- Weakness: Limited model analysis and interpretation—paper lacks qualitative exploration of learned filters and embeddings, leaving the model as a black box.
- Weakness: Inherits a strong simplifying assumption of entity-independence by processing each entity column separately, ignoring cross-entity interactions.
- Weakness: Comparison to the extended grid model is not fully controlled—neural model uses only 3 of 9 features, making it hard to isolate architecture vs. feature effects.
- Suggestion/question: Why did the distributed sentence model of Li and Hovy (2014) perform so poorly on WSJ—possible causes include document length, domain mismatch, or experimental setup differences.
- Suggestion/question: Have the authors tried multiple convolution filter widths in parallel (as in Kim 2014) to capture transition patterns of varying lengths?
- Suggestion/question: Would training directly for the insertion task (negatives with single misplaced sentences) better align training and test objectives and improve insertion performance?
