**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a neural network-based model for local text coherence. The model is built upon the well-established entity grid representation of Barzilay and Lapata (2008). The core of the proposed architecture is a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates on columns of the entity grid, where each column represents the sequence of grammatical roles for a single entity throughout the text. By learning distributed representations (embeddings) for grid symbols and using convolutional filters to detect transition patterns, the model aims to overcome the "curse of dimensionality" that limits traditional, feature-based grid models to short-range transitions. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective, allowing it to learn task-specific features. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, outperforming strong non-neural entity grid baselines.

**Contributions**

1.  **A novel and effective "neuralization" of the entity grid model.** The primary contribution is the application of a convolutional neural network to the entity grid. This is a well-motivated approach that directly addresses known limitations of discrete n-gram-style transition features (i.e., data sparsity for long transitions). The architecture is elegant in its use of 1D convolutions to model transition patterns for each entity independently, leveraging the power of distributed representations and end-to-end learning for this classic coherence representation.
2.  **Strong empirical results establishing a new state of the art.** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on standard benchmarks, showing significant and consistent improvements over strong baselines, including the highly competitive extended entity grid model of Elsner and Charniak (2011). The gains are demonstrated across three distinct tasks, underscoring the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant model architecture.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the shift to a neural architecture. It clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models (sparsity of long-range transition features, task-agnostic feature engineering) and proposes a solution that directly targets these weaknesses. The use of a CNN is a natural fit for modeling location-invariant sequential patterns (entity transitions) and is a clever adaptation of a now-standard neural component to a structured, non-sentential input like the entity grid.
2.  **Comprehensive and rigorous evaluation.** The experimental setup is strong. The authors evaluate on three different tasks of varying difficulty, using a large, standard dataset (WSJ) that allows for more reliable conclusions than the smaller, domain-specific corpora used in some prior work. The comparisons are made against appropriate and strong baselines. The reported results, showing absolute gains of up to 4% on key tasks, are statistically significant and compelling. Furthermore, the inclusion of experiments with pre-training for the low-resource summary rating task is a thoughtful addition that showcases the model's flexibility.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The model is described clearly, and the inclusion of Figure 2 is very helpful for visualizing the architecture and training process. The authors provide sufficient details on hyperparameter tuning and experimental settings. The commitment to releasing the code is a major strength that will benefit the community and facilitate future research building on this work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of model analysis and interpretation.** While the model achieves impressive quantitative results, the paper offers little qualitative insight into what the network learns. The "Why it works" section provides only a high-level intuition. A more in-depth analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. For instance, what kinds of transition patterns do the learned convolutional filters capture? Visualizing the learned embeddings for the grid symbols (e.g., 'S-PERSON', 'O-ORG', 'X', '-') could reveal interesting relationships. Without such analysis, the model remains a black box, and a key opportunity to connect the neural model's success back to linguistic theories of coherence is missed.
2.  **A simplifying assumption about entity independence.** The model assumes that columns in the entity grid are independent and processes each entity's transition history separately. This is inherited from previous grid models, but it is a strong simplification. Coherence is often a function of the interplay *between* entities (e.g., a smooth transition from entity A as the subject to entity B as the subject). A model that could capture cross-column dependencies (perhaps via a 2D convolution or an attention mechanism across entities) might model coherence more powerfully. This limitation should be acknowledged and discussed as a direction for future work.
3.  **The comparison with the extended grid model is not perfectly controlled.** The authors state that their "Extended Grid-CNN" uses only three of the nine entity-specific features from the Elsner & Charniak (2011) model. While outperforming the full non-neural model with fewer features is impressive, it makes it difficult to isolate the source of the improvement. The gain could be attributed purely to the neural architecture, but it could also be that the three selected features are overwhelmingly the most important ones. A more direct comparison using the exact same feature set for both the neural and non-neural models would be more conclusive. Alternatively, an ablation study on the three features used in the Extended Grid-CNN would help clarify their individual contributions within the neural framework.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  You report that the distributed sentence model of Li and Hovy (2014) performed very poorly on your dataset (19.34% on discrimination). This is quite surprising and stands in stark contrast to the results in the original paper. Could you speculate as to why this might be? Could it be related to document length in the WSJ corpus, domain mismatch, or other factors in the experimental setup?
2.  The model employs a single, fixed window size for the convolutional filters (e.g., 5 or 6). Have you explored using multiple filter widths simultaneously, as is common in many CNN architectures for text (e.g., Kim, 2014)? This would allow the model to learn to detect transition patterns of different lengths in parallel, which seems intuitively beneficial for a task like coherence modeling.
3.  You correctly note that the model for the insertion task may be suboptimal because it is trained on the discrimination task (original vs. a full random permutation). Have you considered training a model specifically for insertion, using training pairs where the negative example is a document with only a single sentence misplaced? It seems this would align the training objective more closely with the test condition and could potentially lead to further gains on this more challenging task.
