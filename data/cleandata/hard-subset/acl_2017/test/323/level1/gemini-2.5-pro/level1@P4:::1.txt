### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, designed as a successor to the classic entity grid model. The proposed architecture uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that operates on the entity grid representation of a text. First, the discrete syntactic roles (e.g., Subject, Object) in the grid are converted into continuous vector representations via an embedding layer. Then, a convolutional layer with multiple filters is applied to each entity's transition sequence (i.e., each column of the grid) to learn to identify salient, location-invariant transition patterns. A max-pooling layer selects the most important features, which are then used to compute a final coherence score. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective, which allows it to learn features specifically optimized for distinguishing between more and less coherent texts. The authors evaluate their model on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and show that it achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming strong non-neural entity grid baselines by a significant margin.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for the Entity Grid:** The primary contribution is the successful application of a convolutional neural network to the entity grid representation for coherence modeling. This approach effectively addresses the main limitations of previous grid-based models, such as the data sparsity and curse of dimensionality associated with modeling long-range transitions using discrete features. By operating on dense embeddings, the model can learn complex patterns over longer sequences of entity transitions.
2.  **An End-to-End Learning Framework for Coherence:** The paper introduces a method for training a coherence model in an end-to-end fashion. By using a pairwise ranking loss, the model's parameters—from the initial role embeddings to the final scoring weights—are jointly optimized for the specific task of ranking texts by coherence. This contrasts with traditional methods where feature extraction is a separate, task-agnostic step.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The work provides a strong empirical validation across three different coherence evaluation tasks. The proposed models consistently and significantly outperform established baselines, including sophisticated extensions of the entity grid. These results establish a new state-of-the-art and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the neural approach.

### Strengths
1.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly motivated. The proposed model is a logical and well-designed "neuralization" of the entity grid. The choice of a CNN is well-justified for capturing location-invariant transition patterns, and the pairwise training objective is appropriate for the task. The architecture is explained with sufficient detail and a helpful diagram, making the work easy to understand and appreciate.
2.  **Strong and Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental setup is robust. The authors evaluate on three standard tasks and use the large WSJ corpus for their primary sentence ordering experiments, which provides a more challenging and generalizable testbed than smaller, domain-specific corpora used in some prior work. The inclusion of multiple strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art extended entity grid model, makes the significant performance gains highly convincing. Statistical significance testing further solidifies the claims.
3.  **Addresses Key Limitations of Prior Work:** The work directly tackles well-known weaknesses of the entity grid framework. By using distributed representations and a convolutional architecture, it avoids the combinatorial explosion that prevented previous models from considering transition lengths greater than k=3. The end-to-end training allows for the learning of task-specific features, moving beyond the fixed, hand-engineered features of earlier models.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors provide detailed hyperparameter settings and state that the code was released with the submission. This commitment to open science is a significant strength and facilitates future research building upon this work.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Simplistic Integration of Entity-Specific Features:** The method for incorporating entity-specific features (Section 3.1) involves creating new composite symbols (e.g., `PERSON-S`) for each combination of a feature and a grammatical role. While shown to be effective, this approach can lead to a combinatorial explosion in the vocabulary size if multiple features with high cardinality are used, potentially reintroducing sparsity issues at the embedding layer. A more scalable approach, such as concatenating separate embeddings for the role and the features, was not explored.
2.  **Incomplete Comparison with Alternative Neural Models:** The authors report being unable to reproduce the results of a contemporary neural coherence model by Li and Hovy (2014), which operates on sentence representations instead of an entity grid. Consequently, this important class of models is absent from the comparative results tables. While technical difficulties in reproducing prior work are understandable, its exclusion leaves a gap in the analysis, as it is unclear how this entity-centric neural model compares to sentence-centric neural approaches.
3.  **Lack of Model Analysis:** The paper provides a strong quantitative evaluation but lacks a qualitative analysis of what the model has learned. For instance, there is no investigation into the learned filters to see what kinds of transition patterns (e.g., `S-S-O-`, `-X-S-S`) are deemed important by the model. An analysis of the learned embeddings for syntactic roles could also have provided insight. This omission makes it difficult to understand *why* the model performs well, beyond the general power of neural networks.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the incorporation of entity-specific features: Did you consider alternative methods, such as learning separate embeddings for each feature type and concatenating them with the role embedding before the convolution layer? This might provide a more scalable solution than creating composite symbols.
2.  The performance improvement on the insertion task was notably smaller than on the discrimination task. You hypothesize this is because the model was not trained for insertion. Have you considered or experimented with formulating a training objective more aligned with the insertion task (e.g., training the model to assign the highest score to the correct insertion point out of all possible points)?
3.  Could you provide any analysis or visualization of the learned model components? For example, what is the effect of different filter window sizes on performance? Do the learned embeddings for {S, O, X, -} exhibit any interesting geometric relationships in the vector space?
