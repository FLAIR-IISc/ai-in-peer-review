**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) model for assessing local text coherence. The model operates directly on the entity grid representation, a classic structure for tracking entity roles across sentences. It uses an embedding layer to create distributed representations of grammatical roles (and optional entity-specific features), followed by a convolution layer to capture long-range entity transition patterns. The entire architecture is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective to learn task-specific features.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective application of a CNN architecture to the entity grid representation. This approach elegantly overcomes key limitations of prior discrete models, such as the curse of dimensionality when modeling longer entity transitions, by leveraging distributed representations and learned convolutional filters.
2.  A strong empirical validation on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary rating). The experiments are thorough, conducted on a large-scale dataset, and demonstrate state-of-the-art results, significantly outperforming strong, established entity-grid baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Sound and Novel Method:** The core idea of applying convolutions over entity transition columns in the grid is well-motivated and technically sound. It provides a principled way to learn complex, location-invariant transition patterns (e.g., 'S-S-O-X') that were previously difficult to model due to data sparsity. This represents a significant conceptual advance over traditional feature-counting methods.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The model achieves substantial and statistically significant improvements across all evaluation tasks. For instance, the absolute gains of approximately 4% on the discrimination task and over 4% on summary coherence rating (Table 3 & 4) over strong non-neural counterparts (Elsner & Charniak, 2011) clearly demonstrate the superiority and practical utility of the proposed neural approach.
3.  **High-Quality Evaluation and Reproducibility:** The experiments are conducted on the large and diverse WSJ corpus, which is more robust than smaller, domain-specific corpora used in past work. The methodology is clearly described, and the authors' decision to release their code significantly strengthens the paper and benefits the research community.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Contemporary Neural Models:** The paper's primary comparison is against non-neural baselines. A key contemporary neural baseline (Li and Hovy, 2014) was excluded due to reported difficulties in reproducing its performance. Without a successful comparison to other neural approaches, it is difficult to fully contextualize this work's contribution within the current landscape of neural coherence models.
2.  **Lack of Model Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the model works, but provides little insight into *why*. An analysis of the learned convolutional filters could reveal what kinds of linguistic patterns the model captures. Furthermore, an ablation study on key hyperparameters, such as the filter window size `k`, would provide a deeper understanding of what contributes to the model's success beyond the general power of neural networks.
3.  **Simplistic Feature Integration:** The method for incorporating entity-specific features—by creating composite symbols like 'PERSON-S'—is functional but basic. This approach conflates role and entity type into a single embedding and may not be optimal. Exploring more sophisticated fusion techniques, such as concatenating separate embeddings for roles and features, could lead to further performance gains and a more robust model.
