This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on entity grids. The core idea is to move beyond discrete representations of grammatical roles and entity features used in traditional entity grid models, and leverage distributed representations and convolutional filters to capture long-range entity transitions and task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion. The authors evaluate their model on three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), achieving state-of-the-art results.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of a successful neural network architecture for local coherence modeling that improves upon existing entity grid-based approaches. The use of convolutional neural networks to learn representations over entity grids is a conceptually sound approach, allowing for the capture of longer-range dependencies and potentially more nuanced coherence patterns than traditional methods. The end-to-end training approach, while not entirely novel in neural NLP, is valuable in this context as it allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically. A second, supporting contribution lies in the thorough evaluation of the model across multiple datasets and tasks. Achieving state-of-the-art results on three different coherence assessment tasks provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing baselines on all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), with improvements ranging from 4% to 2.5% and over 4% respectively. This represents a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art. The authors clearly present and compare the results.
2. **Effective Neural Architecture:** The proposed architecture, combining entity grids with convolutional neural networks, is a sensible and potentially powerful approach to modeling local coherence. Utilizing distributed representations and convolutional filters addresses some of the limitations of traditional, discrete-based entity grid models. The use of pooling layers effectively reduces dimensionality and focuses on salient features.
3. **End-to-End Training:** The end-to-end training method, enabled by the neural architecture, is a positive aspect of the work. It allows the model to learn task-specific features directly from the data, rather than relying on hand-engineered features.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluated their model on a diverse set of tasks designed to assess different aspects of coherence, strengthening the validity of the results. Using standard datasets like the Wall Street Journal and DUC’03 increases comparability and reproducibility.
5. **Code Release:** The release of the code is a valuable contribution, facilitating further research and replication of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of entity grids and CNNs is effective, neither component is novel in itself. The novelty primarily lies in applying CNNs to the specific problem of entity grid-based coherence modeling. A more detailed discussion comparing this work to other uses of CNNs in discourse analysis would be helpful.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While the authors mention hyperparameter tuning, the process isn't described in sufficient detail. For example, the search space for each hyperparameter is specified, but the criteria used for selecting the optimal values (beyond DEV set accuracy) are not discussed. More insight into the tuning process would increase confidence in the reported results.
3. **Limited Analysis of Learned Features:** The paper focuses primarily on performance gains but provides limited analysis of *what* the model is learning. Visualizing the learned convolutional filters or analyzing the activation patterns could provide valuable insights into the model’s internal workings and how it captures coherence.
4. **Summary Coherence Rating Dataset Size:** The summary coherence rating task suffers from a small dataset size. The reported gains on this task, while positive, should be interpreted cautiously due to the limited statistical power. The authors acknowledge this, but it remains a weakness.
5. **Comparison to Recent Neural Coherence Models:** The related work section does not adequately address recent neural approaches to coherence modeling beyond the Li and Hovy (2014) work, which was ultimately excluded from the comparisons. A more comprehensive overview of related work would help position this contribution more effectively.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the specific optimization strategy used (e.g., grid search, random search) and the stopping criteria?
2. Have you explored techniques for visualizing the learned convolutional filters? If so, could you share some examples and discuss what they reveal about the model’s behavior?
3. Beyond the entity-specific features used in your experiments, have you explored other types of features that could be incorporated into the model?
4. How does the performance of your model vary with the length of the input document? Are there any limitations in capturing long-range coherence in very long texts?
5. While you mention the limited performance of the Li and Hovy (2014) model, could you briefly discuss other recent neural coherence models and why they were not included in your comparisons?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The use of figures and tables is effective in presenting the results.
*   The related work section could be expanded to include more recent advances in neural coherence modeling.
*   Consider adding an ablation study to assess the contribution of the entity-specific features.
