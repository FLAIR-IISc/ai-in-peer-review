This paper presents a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on entity grids. The authors aim to overcome limitations of traditional entity grid models, specifically their reliance on discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation. They propose an end-to-end trainable model that learns task-specific features and captures long-range entity transitions. The model is evaluated on three coherence assessment tasks, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Neural Entity Grid Representation:** The paper’s core contribution is the adaptation of the entity grid coherence model to a neural architecture.  Replacing discrete representations with distributed embeddings and utilizing convolutional layers to process entity transitions is a significant step. This allows the model to capture longer-range dependencies than traditional methods.
2. **End-to-End Trainability:** The authors’ formulation allows for end-to-end training, meaning the model learns features directly optimized for the coherence task. This contrasts with prior work that often relies on hand-engineered features. This is particularly impactful because it sidesteps the need for extensive feature engineering.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results:**  The experimental results demonstrate consistent and substantial improvements over existing models on three standard coherence evaluation tasks. Achieving state-of-the-art performance is a strong indicator of the model's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models and provides a compelling motivation for the proposed neural approach. The introduction effectively highlights the importance of coherence modeling in downstream NLP tasks.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation on three distinct coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) provides a comprehensive assessment of the model’s capabilities. Using established datasets and comparing against strong baselines strengthens the validity of the results.
3. **Well-Explained Architecture:** The neural network architecture is described in a clear and understandable manner, with a helpful diagram (Figure 2) illustrating the key components and data flow. The explanations of the convolutional layer and pooling operations are particularly well-written.
4. **Code Release:** The authors' release of their code is commendable and facilitates reproducibility and further research. This is particularly valuable in the rapidly evolving field of deep learning.
5. **Significant Performance Gains:** The reported improvements of 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating represent substantial gains, especially in a field where incremental improvements are common.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Components:** While the *combination* of neural networks and entity grids is valuable, the individual components (CNNs, embeddings) are not novel. The paper doesn’t deeply explore the specific advantages of using a CNN over, for example, an RNN for modeling entity transitions. A more thorough discussion of design choices and their impact would be beneficial.
2. **Entity-Specific Feature Incorporation:** The implementation of entity-specific features appears somewhat simplistic (concatenation of type with grammatical role).  A more sophisticated integration of these features—perhaps via attention mechanisms or specialized embedding layers—could potentially lead to further improvements. The paper acknowledges this as future work, but expanding on potential approaches would be valuable.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Tuning:** While the authors mention hyperparameter tuning, the report lacks detail on the search space and rationale for the chosen hyperparameters. A more detailed description of the tuning process would increase confidence in the reported results. It’s unclear how robust the performance is to changes in these parameters.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on overall performance metrics.  A qualitative error analysis—examining examples where the model fails—would provide insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.  Understanding *why* the model makes mistakes could lead to better model design.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was a CNN chosen over an RNN for modeling entity transitions?  Were RNNs considered, and if so, why were they rejected?
2.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and rationale for the final choices?
3.  Can you provide a few examples where the model fails and discuss potential reasons for these failures?
4.  The paper mentions pre-training on WSJ and fine-tuning on the summary coherence rating task. What specific layers were fine-tuned, and how did that affect performance compared to fine-tuning all layers?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper references several related works, but a more detailed discussion of recent advances in neural discourse modeling (e.g., transformer-based models) would be helpful.
*   The notation used in Equation 5 could be clarified. Specifically, it's unclear why φ(Gi) and φ(Gj) are calculated with shared parameters θ, given that Gi and Gj are distinct inputs.
