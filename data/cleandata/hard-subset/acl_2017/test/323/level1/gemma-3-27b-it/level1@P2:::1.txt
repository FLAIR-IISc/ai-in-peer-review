This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on entity grids. The core idea is to move beyond discrete representations used in traditional entity grid models and leverage distributed representations to capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss. The authors report state-of-the-art results on three coherence assessment tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

**Contributions:**

1. **Neural Entity Grid Representation:** The most significant contribution is the application of CNNs to the entity grid framework. This allows for learning distributed representations of entity transitions and overcoming the limitations of discrete representations in traditional approaches. This allows capturing longer range dependencies than previously possible.
2. **End-to-End Training:**  The paper proposes an end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking. This contrasts with previous approaches where feature extraction was decoupled from the task. This allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates substantial performance improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods on three established coherence assessment tasks, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The consistent outperformance across different tasks is a strong signal.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models (discrete representations, task-agnostic feature extraction) and convincingly motivates the use of a neural approach.
2. **Well-Designed Architecture:** The proposed architecture is well-motivated and leverages the strengths of CNNs for capturing local patterns and long-range dependencies in the entity grid. The use of convolution and max-pooling is appropriate for the task.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering three different coherence assessment tasks with established benchmarks. This provides a strong basis for comparing the proposed model with existing methods.
4. **Ablation Study & Feature Incorporation:** The inclusion of entity-specific features and the ablation study demonstrating their impact (Extended Grid-CNN) is commendable. It highlights the benefits of incorporating additional information into the model.
5. **Code Release:** Releasing the code is a significant positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN Application:** While applying CNNs to entity grids is a reasonable approach, the core technique of using CNNs for sequence modeling is not entirely novel. The main contribution lies in *applying* this well-established technique to a specific domain (coherence modeling with entity grids), but the core innovation of the CNN itself isn't present. 
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Details:** While the authors mention hyperparameter tuning, the report on hyperparameter settings feels cursory. The search space described is relatively broad, but more detail on the specific rationale for choosing those values would be helpful. The effect of each hyperparameter on performance isn't discussed.
3. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on reporting overall performance gains. A more in-depth analysis of specific error cases (e.g., types of texts where the model struggles, common errors in sentence ordering) would provide valuable insights into the model's limitations and potential areas for improvement.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The reliance on the WSJ corpus, while standard, might limit the generalizability of the results. Exploring the model’s performance on more diverse datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
5. **Comparison to Recent Neural Coherence Models:** The related work section could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of recent neural coherence models beyond Li and Hovy (2014). There has been significant progress in neural discourse modeling in recent years, and a more thorough comparison would better position the contribution of this work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters, and discuss the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings?
2. Can you provide examples of error cases where the model fails, and analyze the reasons for these failures?
3. Have you experimented with other neural architectures besides CNNs (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) for modeling coherence? If so, what were the results?
4. How does the performance of your model vary across different genres or domains of text? Have you evaluated it on datasets beyond the WSJ corpus?
5. How does the performance compare to recent transformer-based language models fine-tuned for coherence tasks, given their strong general language understanding capabilities?

**Additional Comments:**

* The figure is a bit small and could be improved for clarity.
* It would be helpful to include the number of parameters in the model for comparison.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a promising approach to coherence modeling. While the core idea of applying CNNs to entity grids may not be groundbreaking, the strong empirical results and the end-to-end training approach demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
