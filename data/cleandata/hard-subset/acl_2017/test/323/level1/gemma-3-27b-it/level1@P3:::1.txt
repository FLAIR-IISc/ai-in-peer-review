This paper presents a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on entity grids. The authors aim to address limitations of traditional entity grid models, specifically their discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation, by leveraging distributed representations and end-to-end training. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on three coherence assessment tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful application of convolutional neural networks to the established entity grid framework for coherence modeling. While the idea of neuralizing existing NLP approaches isn't novel in itself, applying CNNs to the entity grid representation, and demonstrating substantial improvements over strong baselines, is a significant advancement. The end-to-end training approach, while conceptually straightforward, is a valuable component given the limitations of task-agnostic feature engineering in prior work. A secondary contribution is the publicly released code, which facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods across all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The reported gains (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating) are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The statistical significance testing strengthens these claims.
2. **Effective Neural Architecture:** The use of CNNs is well-motivated given the task. The ability to capture long-range entity transitions using convolutional filters, coupled with the benefits of distributed representations, addresses a key limitation of traditional entity grid models. The architecture’s modularity (lookup layer, convolution, pooling, scoring) allows for easy extension and modification.
3. **End-to-End Training:**  The pairwise ranking training approach is a significant improvement over the feature engineering required in previous entity grid models. It allows the model to learn task-specific features directly from data, leading to improved performance.
4. **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The inclusion of a diagram illustrating the architecture and training process is helpful. The release of the code is a strong positive, enabling other researchers to reproduce the results and build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Neural Network Application:** While effective, the application of CNNs to this problem is not groundbreaking in the broader context of deep learning. The novelty is primarily in the *application* to the specific domain of entity grid coherence modeling, not the neural network architecture itself. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of this point and a more detailed comparison to other neural sequence modeling approaches (e.g., LSTMs, Transformers) that could be applied to the same problem.
2. **Feature Incorporation Limited:** The extension to incorporate entity-specific features feels somewhat rudimentary. While it improves performance, the authors acknowledge using only a limited subset of features from Elsner and Charniak (2011). Exploring a wider range of entity features and more sophisticated integration methods could potentially yield further gains. The reliance on pre-defined features also limits the model’s ability to learn representations directly from the data.
3. **Evaluation Dataset Limitations:** The reliance on relatively small datasets (particularly for the summary coherence rating task) raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. The summary coherence rating task is especially susceptible to the limitations of the dataset size.  Reporting confidence intervals or conducting more robust statistical analyses could strengthen the claims.
4. **Ablation Studies are Needed:** The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study. For example, it is not clear how much each component of the neural architecture (e.g., convolutional layers, pooling, embedding size) contributes to the overall performance. Investigating the impact of these components would provide valuable insights into the model’s behavior.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you considered using other neural network architectures, such as LSTMs or Transformers, for modeling entity transitions? If so, what were the results, and why did you choose CNNs over these alternatives?
2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing only three entity-specific features in the "Extended Grid-CNN" model? What were the challenges of incorporating a larger set of features?
3.  What steps were taken to ensure the quality of the coreference resolution used to generate the entity grids? How might errors in coreference resolution affect the model’s performance?
4.  What is the computational cost of training and applying your model compared to the baseline entity grid models?




**Additional Comments:**

*  The references section is comprehensive.
*  The discussion of related work is generally thorough, but could benefit from a more focused discussion on recent advances in neural coherence modeling.
*  Consider clarifying the hyperparameter search process in more detail. For example, what search strategy was used (e.g., grid search, random search)?
