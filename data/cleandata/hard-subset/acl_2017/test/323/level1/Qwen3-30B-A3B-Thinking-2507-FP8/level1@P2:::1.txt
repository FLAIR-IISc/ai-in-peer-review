

# Review for ACL 2017: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a neural network-based approach to local coherence modeling that operates over the entity grid representation of text. The authors address two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) discrete representation of grammatical roles that leads to the curse of dimensionality (limiting transition length to k≤3), and (2) task-agnostic feature extraction that prevents learning task-specific features. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A neural architecture that uses distributed representations and convolutional layers to model longer-range entity transitions (k=5-8) without suffering from dimensionality issues.
2. An end-to-end pairwise ranking training method that allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically.
3. Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing significant improvements over existing entity grid models across three different coherence tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Technical Contribution to a Well-Known Limitation**: The paper directly addresses a fundamental limitation of entity grid models (the curse of dimensionality limiting transition length to k≤3) that has been acknowledged in prior work (Bengio et al., 2003). By using distributed representations and convolutional layers, the authors demonstrate that their model can effectively capture longer-range transitions (k=5-8) without overfitting, which is a substantial technical advancement. The paper clearly explains how their approach overcomes the dimensionality issue through distributed representations, making this a strong contribution to the coherence modeling field.

2. **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation**: The authors evaluate their model on three distinct coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) across two different datasets (WSJ and DUC'03). This is a significant strength compared to prior work that often evaluates on only one or two tasks. The results consistently show significant improvements over existing models (4% absolute gain in discrimination, 2.5% in insertion, and 4%+ in summary coherence rating), with statistical significance testing. The choice of WSJ corpus (larger and more diverse than AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES) for the main experiments is well-justified and appropriate for deep learning models.

3. **Clear and Well-Motivated Architecture**: The paper provides a thorough explanation of the neural architecture, including how the convolutional layers model entity transitions in a location-invariant manner. The relationship between the entity grid representation and the neural model is clearly articulated, and the authors provide detailed technical descriptions of the layers and training procedure. The explanation of why their approach works (each filter detecting specific transition patterns) is particularly compelling.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Detail on Entity-Specific Feature Integration**: While the paper mentions incorporating entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention), it lacks sufficient detail on how these features are integrated into the grid representation. The authors state they use "only three entity-specific features from (Elsner and Charniak, 2011) that are easy to compute," but don't explain how these features are encoded or how they compare to the full set of features used in the original work. This makes it difficult to assess the full contribution of incorporating entity-specific features.

2. **Unexplained Exclusion of Li and Hovy (2014)**: The paper dismisses Li and Hovy's neural coherence model as "very disappointing" (19.34% accuracy) without providing a clear explanation for the poor performance on their WSJ corpus. This weakens their claim of "state-of-the-art" results, as they don't compare against the most recent neural approach. The exclusion of this model without analysis or explanation creates a gap in the evaluation that needs to be addressed.

3. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Models**: The paper claims to achieve "state-of-the-art results" but doesn't compare against other neural coherence models published after 2014. The Li and Hovy (2014) model they mention is actually the most recent neural approach at the time, but they exclude it without justification. More recent neural coherence models (e.g., from Li et al., 2014) might have addressed the limitations they identify, and the paper should explain how their model differs from and improves upon these.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the Li and Hovy (2014) model performed poorly on your WSJ dataset (19.34% accuracy), but you excluded it from results without explanation. Could you clarify why this model performed so poorly on your data, and whether you believe this is due to dataset differences or implementation issues?

2. You mention using "only three entity-specific features" from Elsner and Charniak (2011). Could you provide more detail on how these features are encoded in the grid representation, and why you chose these particular three features over the full set?

3. The paper claims "state-of-the-art" results, but doesn't compare against neural coherence models published after 2014. How does your model compare to more recent neural approaches to coherence modeling, and why do you believe your approach is superior?

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to coherence modeling by addressing a clear limitation of existing entity grid models. The technical approach is sound, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the results are impressive. However, the exclusion of the Li and Hovy model without explanation and the insufficient detail on entity feature integration are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. With these clarifications, this would be a strong paper for ACL.
