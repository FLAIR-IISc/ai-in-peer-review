

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes a neural coherence model based on a convolutional neural network operating over the entity grid representation of text. The main contributions are:
1. A neural architecture using CNNs to model long-range entity transitions in the entity grid, addressing the curse of dimensionality problem in traditional entity grid models.
2. An end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking to learn task-specific features automatically.
3. A method to incorporate entity-specific features into the neural model.

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical results**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results on all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), with significant improvements over existing entity grid models (4% absolute gain on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating). The results are consistent across tasks and well-documented with statistical significance testing.

2. **Clear identification and solution of key limitations**: The paper effectively identifies two major limitations of existing entity grid models (curse of dimensionality and task-agnostic feature extraction) and proposes a principled solution using distributed representations and end-to-end training. The neural architecture directly addresses these limitations by enabling longer transition modeling and task-specific feature learning.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The evaluation covers three well-established coherence assessment tasks using appropriate baselines (including the best-performing entity grid models and a graph-based approach). The use of the WSJ corpus for sentence ordering tasks provides a larger and more generalizable dataset than previous studies, and the authors justify this choice thoroughly.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty**: While the neuralization of the entity grid is a reasonable extension, the approach (using CNNs to model entity transitions) is not particularly novel in NLP. Similar architectures have been used for text classification (e.g., Kim, 2014) and other sequence modeling tasks. The paper does not sufficiently argue why a CNN is the best choice for this specific task compared to other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs or transformers).

2. **Lack of detailed analysis**: The paper demonstrates that the neural model performs better but provides little insight into *why* it performs better. There is no analysis of the specific patterns the neural model learns to detect in the entity grid, nor a comparison of the features learned by the neural model versus those used by traditional entity grid models. This limits the understanding of the model's mechanisms.

3. **Inadequate handling of previous neural approach**: The authors mention that a neural model from Li and Hovy (2014) performed poorly on their dataset (19.34% accuracy in discrimination), yet they do not explore this discrepancy in depth. This is a significant weakness as it raises questions about the general applicability of neural approaches to coherence modeling, and the paper fails to address why their approach succeeds where others failed.

## Questions to Authors
1. The authors mention that a neural model from Li and Hovy (2014) performed poorly on their dataset (19.34% accuracy in discrimination). Could you elaborate on why their approach works better than the Li and Hovy model, and what specific aspects of the neural architecture or training procedure made the difference?

2. Could you provide more detailed analysis of the patterns detected by the convolutional filters in the neural model? For example, what specific entity transition patterns (e.g., "SS-O-X") are most predictive of coherence according to your model?

3. The paper notes that insertion is a harder task than discrimination, and the gains are smaller for insertion. How would you expect the model to perform if specifically trained on insertion data rather than using the discrimination task as the primary training objective?
