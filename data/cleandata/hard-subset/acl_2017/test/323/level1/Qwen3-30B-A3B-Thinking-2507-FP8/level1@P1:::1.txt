

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on a convolutional neural network operating over the entity grid representation of text. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a neural architecture that captures long-range entity transitions using distributed representations without sacrificing generalization, (2) an end-to-end training method for learning task-specific high-level features through pairwise ranking, and (3) state-of-the-art performance on three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural architecture that successfully extends the traditional entity grid model by using distributed representations to capture long-range entity transitions (up to length 8), overcoming the "curse of dimensionality" limitation of discrete representations.
2. An end-to-end training approach that learns task-specific features automatically through pairwise ranking, addressing the limitation of existing models where feature extraction is decoupled from the target task.
3. Comprehensive empirical validation demonstrating consistent and significant improvements over non-neural entity grid models across three different coherence assessment tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem identification and strong motivation**: The paper effectively identifies two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (a) discrete representation leading to the curse of dimensionality, limiting transition modeling to short lengths (k ≤ 3), and (b) task-agnostic feature extraction that prevents learning of task-specific features. The authors provide compelling evidence for these limitations through citations to prior work and clear explanations of the technical constraints.

2. **Strong empirical results with thorough evaluation**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over non-neural baselines across three distinct coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), with absolute gains of 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating. The evaluation design is well-justified, with clear explanations for corpus selection (WSJ vs. AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES) and careful ablation studies showing the impact of different model components (Grid-CNN vs. Extended Grid-CNN).

3. **Effective model design that builds on existing work**: The authors successfully leverage the well-established entity grid framework while introducing neural components that address its limitations. The convolutional architecture is well-suited for modeling transitions in a location-invariant manner, and the use of distributed representations for grammatical roles enables longer transition modeling without the dimensionality issues of discrete representations. The pairwise ranking training approach is well-motivated and consistent with prior work on coherence assessment as a ranking problem.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient theoretical justification for the improvements**: While the paper reports significant empirical gains, it lacks a clear theoretical explanation for *why* the neural approach outperforms the non-neural models. The authors claim that distributed representations enable better generalization, but they don't provide evidence beyond the empirical results. A more thorough analysis of how the neural model's feature learning differs from the non-neural approach would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Lack of comparison with the most recent neural coherence model**: The authors mention that they tried the Li and Hovy (2014) model but "results on our dataset were very disappointing," yet they don't provide a detailed comparison or explanation for this discrepancy. This is particularly problematic since Li and Hovy also proposed a neural approach for coherence modeling, and the authors' model appears to be a different neuralization of the entity grid approach. The paper should either provide a thorough comparison or explain why their approach is fundamentally different and why Li and Hovy's model performed poorly on this dataset.

3. **Underdeveloped discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the proposed approach. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the model performs on very short texts (which might not have enough entities to form meaningful transitions), or how it handles texts with complex discourse structures that might not fit the entity grid representation. The authors also don't explore why the model's gains are smaller on the insertion task compared to discrimination (only 1% vs. 4% improvement), despite acknowledging insertion is a harder task.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the Li and Hovy (2014) model performed poorly on your dataset (19.34% accuracy in discrimination), but the paper doesn't explain why this might be the case. Could you provide more details on why their model performed so poorly on your dataset compared to their reported results on other datasets?

2. The paper claims that the neural approach allows for capturing "sufficiently long entity transitions" (e.g., 5-8 in experiments), but it doesn't clearly explain why this is possible with the neural approach but not with existing grid models. Could you elaborate on the theoretical advantages of the neural approach for modeling long-range transitions?

3. The Extended Grid-CNN model incorporates only three entity-specific features from the Extended Grid model. How did you select these specific features, and what would be the impact of including all features from the Extended Grid model (which uses 9 features)? Would the performance improve further with more features?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a strong foundation in existing coherence modeling work. The implementation details are thorough, and the authors have released their code, which is a valuable contribution to the community. The experimental results are convincing, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the theoretical justification for the improvements and providing a more thorough comparison with the Li and Hovy (2014) model. The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art results" is well-supported by the empirical evidence, but the theoretical grounding for why these results are achieved needs to be more explicit.
