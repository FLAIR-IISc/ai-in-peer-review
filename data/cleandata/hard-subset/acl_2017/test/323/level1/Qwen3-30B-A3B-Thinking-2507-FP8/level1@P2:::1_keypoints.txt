- Proposes a neural local coherence model using distributed representations and convolutional layers over the entity grid to model longer-range entity transitions (k=5–8).  
- Introduces end-to-end pairwise ranking training to learn task-specific coherence features automatically.  
- Provides comprehensive evaluation on three coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) across WSJ and DUC'03, showing significant, statistically tested gains.  
- Strength: directly addresses the curse of dimensionality in entity-grid models, enabling longer transition modeling without overfitting.  
- Strength: rigorous, multi-task/multi-dataset evaluation with consistent improvements (e.g., +4% discrimination, +2.5% insertion).  
- Strength: clear, well-motivated architecture and explanations (convolutional filters as detectors of transition patterns).  
- Weakness: insufficient detail on how the three entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention) are encoded and how they compare to the full feature set.  
- Weakness: unexplained exclusion and poor reported performance (19.34% accuracy) of Li & Hovy (2014), weakening the state-of-the-art claim.  
- Weakness: lack of comparison to more recent neural coherence models post-2014 and unclear positioning relative to other neural approaches.  
- Question: clarify why Li & Hovy (2014) performed poorly on WSJ—dataset differences or implementation issues?  
- Question: provide encoding details and justification for the three Elsner & Charniak entity features used.  
- Question: explain comparisons to more recent neural coherence models and why this approach is superior.  
- Overall: meaningful and technically sound contribution with strong results, but needs clarifications on entity-feature integration and omitted comparisons to be a definitive state-of-the-art paper.
