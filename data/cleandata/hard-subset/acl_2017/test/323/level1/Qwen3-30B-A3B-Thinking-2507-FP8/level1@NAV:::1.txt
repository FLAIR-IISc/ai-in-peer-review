

This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that extends the entity grid framework using convolutional neural networks to capture long-range entity transitions while incorporating entity-specific features. The authors argue that existing entity grid models suffer from the curse of dimensionality due to discrete representations and task-agnostic feature extraction, and their neural approach addresses these limitations through distributed representations and end-to-end training.

The main contributions are: (1) a neural architecture that uses convolutional layers to model long-range entity transitions (up to length 8) while maintaining generalization, (2) a pairwise ranking training method that enables end-to-end learning of task-specific features, and (3) state-of-the-art performance across three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), with absolute gains of 4-5% over existing non-neural baselines.

Strengths: The paper provides a compelling motivation for neuralizing the entity grid model and demonstrates clear improvements over strong baselines. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering multiple coherence tasks and using appropriate datasets. The implementation details are well-described, and the model's effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated with statistically significant results. The pairwise ranking approach is well-suited for coherence assessment as it naturally handles the ranking nature of the problem.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks a detailed justification for choosing convolutional networks over other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs or Transformers) that might better model sequential dependencies. The improvements over baselines, while statistically significant, are relatively modest (4-5% absolute gain), raising questions about the practical impact of the method. Additionally, the paper provides minimal analysis of what the model actually learns, making it difficult to assess whether the model has captured meaningful linguistic patterns or simply memorized task-specific features.

Questions to Authors: How does your model compare to sequence-based neural models (e.g., LSTM or Transformer) that directly process text sequences rather than entity grids? Could you provide examples of entity transitions that your model identifies as particularly important for coherence?
