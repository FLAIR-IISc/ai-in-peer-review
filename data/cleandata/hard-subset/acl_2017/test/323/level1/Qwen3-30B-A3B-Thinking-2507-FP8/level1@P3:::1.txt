

# Peer Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors argue that traditional entity grid models suffer from discrete representations leading to the curse of dimensionality (limiting transition lengths to k≤3) and task-agnostic feature computation. Their contribution is a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates on the entity grid, using distributed representations to capture longer transitions (k=5-8) and incorporating entity-specific features. They also propose a pairwise ranking training method for end-to-end task-specific learning.

My assessment of the paper's main contributions (in order of strength):

1. **Demonstrating state-of-the-art results on coherence assessment tasks** through neural extension of the entity grid model. The model achieves consistent improvements (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, 4%+ on summary coherence) over the best prior entity grid models.

2. **Enabling modeling of longer entity transitions** (k=5-8) through distributed representations and convolutional architecture, which overcomes a key limitation of traditional entity grid models.

3. **Providing an end-to-end training method** using pairwise ranking, though this approach is not particularly novel as it's a standard technique in ranking problems.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of limitations in existing work**: The paper correctly identifies two key limitations of entity grid models (curse of dimensionality due to discrete representations, and task-agnostic feature computation) and proposes a sound neural solution. The authors effectively demonstrate how distributed representations address the dimensionality problem by allowing longer transition modeling (k=5-8 vs. k≤3 in prior work).

2. **Strong empirical results**: The model consistently outperforms prior work across three standard coherence assessment tasks with statistically significant gains. The improvements are meaningful (4-5% absolute gains), which is substantial in this field. The use of multiple evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence) strengthens the evaluation.

3. **Well-motivated and well-structured presentation**: The paper clearly situates its contribution within the existing literature, providing a thorough explanation of the entity grid model and its limitations. The architecture is well-explained with appropriate technical details, and the experimental setup is standard and fair.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of proper comparison to discrete representation baseline**: The paper claims the distributed representation is key to their success, but fails to compare against a version that uses the same entity grid features with discrete representations (e.g., a CNN applied to discrete entity transitions). Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether the improvements come from the neural architecture itself or the distributed representation approach.

2. **Insufficient engagement with competing neural approaches**: The paper dismisses Li and Hovy (2014) as "disappointing" without providing a clear comparison, yet Li and Hovy also proposed a neural coherence model. The authors claim their approach is "fundamentally different," but don't convincingly demonstrate why their approach is superior to Li and Hovy's neural model on the same tasks.

3. **Limited domain evaluation**: The evaluation is restricted to the WSJ corpus (newswire articles), which is a specific domain. The paper doesn't discuss how well the model generalizes to other domains (e.g., conversational text, student essays) or provide any cross-domain experiments, which is a significant limitation for a coherence model.

4. **Insufficient detail on entity-specific feature integration**: The paper mentions incorporating three entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention), but provides no details on how these features are encoded or integrated into the neural model. This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess the novelty of this aspect of their contribution.

5. **Misleading claim about "automatic" feature learning**: The paper claims to "learn task-specific high-level features automatically," but the entity-specific features are hand-selected from prior work. The "automatic" learning is limited to the neural architecture's ability to combine these features, not to discovering new features, which misrepresents the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide results comparing your neural model with a version that uses discrete representations of entity transitions (e.g., a CNN applied to the same entity grid features but without distributed representations)? This would clarify whether the improvements come from the neural architecture or the distributed representation.

2. How do your results compare to Li and Hovy (2014) on the same evaluation tasks and datasets? The paper dismisses their model as "disappointing" but doesn't provide a direct comparison.

3. Have you evaluated your model on other text domains beyond WSJ (e.g., conversational texts or student essays)? If not, how do you plan to address the domain-specificity limitation?

4. Could you provide more details on how the entity-specific features are encoded and integrated into the neural model? Specifically, how are the named entity type, salience, and proper mention features represented as part of the input to the neural network?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a solid empirical contribution by demonstrating that neural extensions of the entity grid model can achieve state-of-the-art results on coherence assessment tasks. The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. However, the paper's claims about the importance of distributed representations and "automatic" feature learning are not adequately supported by the experiments. The lack of comparison to a discrete representation baseline and insufficient engagement with competing neural approaches weaken the paper's contribution. With revisions addressing these weaknesses, particularly the comparative experiments and clearer explanation of the neural architecture's novelty, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
