- Proposes a convolutional neural extension of the entity grid model using distributed representations to model longer entity transitions and end-to-end pairwise ranking training.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art results: ~4% gain on discrimination, ~2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence tasks.  
- Claims longer transition modeling (k=5–8) via distributed representations overcomes discrete-model dimensionality limits.  

- Clearly identifies limitations of prior entity-grid models (curse of dimensionality, task-agnostic features) and motivates a neural solution.  
- Strong empirical gains across three standard coherence tasks with statistically significant improvements.  
- Well-presented paper with clear architecture description and fair experimental setup.  

- No comparison to a discrete-representation baseline, so gains may not be solely due to distributed representations.  
- Insufficient comparison to prior neural approaches (e.g., Li and Hovy 2014) despite dismissing them.  
- Evaluation limited to WSJ news domain; no cross-domain or diverse-genre tests provided.  
- Lacking details on how the three entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention) are encoded and integrated.  
- Overstates "automatic" feature learning since key entity features are hand-selected from prior work.  

- Request results comparing to a discrete-representation version (e.g., CNN on discrete entity transitions).  
- Ask for direct performance comparison to Li and Hovy (2014) on the same tasks/datasets.  
- Inquire about evaluation on other domains (conversational text, student essays) or plans to address domain generalization.  
- Request detailed description of how named entity type, salience, and proper mention features are represented in the model.  

- Overall: solid empirical contribution and well-motivated approach, but claims about distributed representations and automatic feature learning are under-supported without comparative experiments and clearer methodological details.
