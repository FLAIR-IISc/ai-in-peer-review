Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that operates on the entity grid representation. It replaces discrete transition counts with learned embeddings and applies a convolutional neural network over entity transition columns to capture longer-range patterns. The model is trained with a pairwise ranking loss and can incorporate entity-specific features by tokenizing role-feature combinations (e.g., PERSON-S). Experiments on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating show consistent improvements over entity-grid baselines and a graph-based model. Pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning improves summary rating.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical contribution: Strong results on three coherence assessment tasks, with statistically significant gains over strong entity-grid baselines on WSJ and improved summary rating on DUC’03, using a relatively simple neural architecture and limited feature engineering.
2) Methodological contribution: A CNN over entity-grid columns with learned role/feature embeddings and pairwise ranking, enabling longer transition modeling and end-to-end, task-specific feature learning.
3) Practical feature integration: A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features into the neural grid (concatenated tokens), avoiding combinatorial feature explosion of traditional grids.

Strengths
- Empirical performance and robustness: The model outperforms Extended Grid (Elsner & Charniak) and a graph-based method on discrimination and insertion, and improves human-judgment alignment in summary coherence rating. Results are averaged over multiple runs with significance testing and show non-trivial absolute gains (about 3–4% on discrimination; up to ~2.5% reported for insertion; 3–4% on summary rating with pretraining), which is meaningful for these standard benchmarks.
- Sound training objective and alignment with task: Pairwise ranking is a natural fit for coherence assessment. End-to-end training allows the model to learn task-specific filters that detect useful transition patterns, improving over task-agnostic transition counting.
- Addresses sparsity and long-range transitions: Distributed role embeddings and convolution mitigate the sparsity/cursed-dimensionality issues of discrete grids and allow larger receptive fields (k≈5–8), which previous grid models avoided due to parameter explosion.
- Feature extensibility with limited engineering: The extended neural grid includes only three simple entity features (NE type, salience, proper mention) yet surpasses the engineered Extended Grid that uses more features, indicating that learned composition reduces reliance on extensive feature design and external resources.
- Reproducibility practices: Hyperparameters, optimizer, and training protocol are described; results averaged across seeds; significance tested; code release is promised.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The main technical idea is a straightforward neuralization of entity grids using standard components (embeddings, 1D convolution, max pooling, pairwise ranking). While applying CNNs to entity transitions is reasonable, the conceptual advance over prior entity-grid models and over neural sentence-coherence models (e.g., Li & Hovy, 2014) appears incremental.
- Baseline coverage and fairness: The paper excludes the Li & Hovy (2014) neural baseline due to poor reproduction without deeper analysis or remediation, and does not compare against other strong alternatives that model discourse, e.g., learning-to-rank improvements (Feng & Hirst, 2012) or RST-based models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014). This limits the strength of the SOTA claim and makes it harder to assess gains relative to the broader literature.
- Reporting clarity/inconsistencies: Table 3 reports identical numbers for discrimination and insertion across all systems, which conflicts with the text (insertion is harder; gains “up to 2.5%”). Metrics and evaluation details for insertion are unclear, and the presence of an “F1” column in the same table is confusing given a separate summary-rating table is referenced later. These inconsistencies hinder interpretation and should be corrected.
- Insufficient detail on entity extraction and coreference: The approach relies on entity columns (all nouns and possibly coreference). It is unclear which coreference resolver (if any) is used, whether mentions are matched heuristically, and how errors propagate. Since grid quality is pivotal, missing details reduce reproducibility and make it difficult to compare fairly to prior work that uses specific pipelines.
- Modeling assumptions and analysis: Treating entity columns independently ignores interactions across entities that may carry coherence cues (e.g., thematic alternation across multiple entities). The effectiveness of longer windows is asserted but not analyzed (no ablation across k or pooling choices), and there is limited qualitative analysis (e.g., learned filter patterns). Training only on discrimination pairs for insertion evaluation is discussed but not experimentally examined; a task-matched training setup could be informative.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify Table 3: How exactly are discrimination and insertion evaluated, and why are the reported numbers identical? What does the “F1” column measure? Provide corrected results and definitions for each metric.
- What is the precise entity detection/coreference pipeline? Are “entities” all nouns (including non-head nouns) without coreference, or are you clustering mentions via a resolver? Which tool and settings? How sensitive is performance to this choice?
- Can you include or discuss stronger baselines (e.g., Feng & Hirst, 2012; Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014)? If reimplementation is infeasible, report the closest published numbers under comparable setups and discuss differences.
- What prevented reproducing Li & Hovy (2014) on WSJ? Did you try alternative hyperparameters, sentence encoders, or pretraining? A brief error analysis or ablation (e.g., sentence window size) would help contextualize your gains.
- Ablations: Please report the impact of transition window size k, pooling length/stride, number of filters, and inclusion of each entity-specific feature. Do longer windows materially help? Can you visualize or exemplify a few learned filters/patterns?
- Task-specific training: If you train with insertion-style pairs (varying the position of a held-out sentence), do insertion results improve? Similarly, does training on harder negative permutations (as in Feng & Hirst, 2012) further enhance discrimination?

Overall evaluation
The paper presents a clear, technically sound, and practically useful neural extension of the entity-grid coherence model with strong empirical results. The novelty is moderate, and some experimental/reporting gaps should be addressed, but the work is likely to be of interest to the community given its simplicity, effectiveness, and applicability.
