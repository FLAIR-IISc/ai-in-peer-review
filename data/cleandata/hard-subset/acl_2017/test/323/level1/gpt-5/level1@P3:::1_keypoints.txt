- Proposes a neural local coherence model that embeds entity-grid symbols and applies 1D convolutions with max-pooling to score document coherence via pairwise ranking.

- Contribution: neuralized entity grid with embedded grammatical-role symbols and convolution across entity transitions, trained end-to-end to capture longer-range patterns.

- Contribution: empirical state-of-the-art gains on sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) and summary coherence rating, including WSJ→DUC transfer.

- Contribution: lightweight way to include a few entity-specific features without external resources while outperforming richer extended-grid baselines.

- Strength: architecture addresses sparsity in long transitions by replacing discrete transition counts with shared embeddings and CNNs, enabling larger k without overfitting.

- Strength: strong, consistent empirical improvements with significance testing, multiple seeds, and code release for reproducibility.

- Strength: minimal external resources required; three simple entity features suffice to beat extended-grid methods that use more and external data.

- Strength: training objective (pairwise ranking) and pretrain–fine-tune setup align well with coherence tasks and small-data transfer.

- Weakness: limited novelty—straightforward application of standard neural methods and independence assumptions (columns treated independently, max-pooling losing inter-entity/positional info).

- Weakness: underspecified and potentially weak entity identification/coreference pipeline; unclear system, settings, and sensitivity to coref quality.

- Weakness: symbol-typing of feature-role combinations may not scale (vocabulary inflation and sparsity) and alternative compositional embeddings are not explored.

- Weakness: training on discrimination but evaluating on insertion creates a mismatch; lacks insertion-specific training and deeper ablations (k, pooling, embedding size).

- Weakness: dataset choices reduce comparability—omits common benchmarks like AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES, and provides insufficient analysis of Li & Hovy (2014) failure on WSJ.

- Question: Which POS tagger, parser, and coreference resolver were used, do you include non-head nouns, and how many entities per document vs. noun-matching?

- Question: What are the vocabulary sizes |V| for basic and extended settings, and how does performance scale as more entity features are added or modeled compositionally?

- Question: How do window size k, number of filters, and pooling length p affect performance, and does max-pooling’s position invariance hurt long/short documents?

- Question: Can the model be trained with an insertion-specific loss (ranking insertion positions) and does that improve insertion accuracy?

- Question: Provide more details on the Li & Hovy (2014) comparison—training setup, encoder/window choices, and any domain-pretraining attempts to ensure fairness.

- Question: Can you provide qualitative analysis—examples of learned filters/transition patterns and typical errors—to show what the model captures beyond frequency and salience?
