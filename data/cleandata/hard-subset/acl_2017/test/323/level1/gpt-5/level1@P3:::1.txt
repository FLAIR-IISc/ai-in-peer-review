Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that operates on the entity grid. It embeds grid symbols (grammatical roles and optionally entity features), applies 1D convolutions over per-entity sentence transitions with max-pooling, and learns a document-level coherence score via a pairwise ranking objective. Evaluations on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and summary coherence rating show consistent improvements over strong entity-grid baselines and a graph-based method.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple but effective neuralization of the entity grid: embedding grammatical-role symbols and using convolution across entity transitions to model longer-range patterns, trained end-to-end with pairwise ranking.
2) Empirical demonstration that the neural grid yields state-of-the-art results across three standard tasks, with consistent and significant gains over extended grid baselines, including transfer from WSJ pretraining to DUC summary rating.
3) A practical, lightweight mechanism for incorporating entity-specific features without external resources, while still outperforming richer extended-grid baselines.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture that addresses sparsity in longer transitions. Moving from discrete transition counts to distributed embeddings and convolution directly tackles the curse of dimensionality that limits k in classic grids. Allowing k≈5–8 while sharing parameters across documents is a principled way to capture longer dependencies without overfitting.

- Strong and consistent empirical improvements with careful experimental practice. On WSJ, Grid-CNN improves over Grid-all-nouns by ~4% absolute on discrimination and ~1% on insertion; Extended Grid-CNN adds a further ~3%–4% on discrimination and ~2.5% on insertion over Extended Grid, with significance testing and multiple random seeds. On DUC’03, pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning improves pairwise ranking by ~3–4% over baselines. Code availability further strengthens reproducibility.

- Minimal reliance on external resources yet competitive with richer feature sets. The Extended Grid-CNN uses only three easily computed entity features (NE type, salience, properness), yet outperforms the extended grid with nine features (some trained on external corpora). This supports the claim that learned representations over grids can substitute for substantial manual feature engineering.

- Sound training objective and evaluation alignment. Pairwise ranking with shared parameters on document pairs aligns with the inherent ranking nature of coherence assessment and is consistent with prior practice (e.g., SVM-rank in Barzilay & Lapata). The pretraining–fine-tuning setup for the small DUC dataset is appropriate and effective.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural coherence work; independence assumptions restrict expressivity. Using CNNs over embedded grid symbols is a relatively straightforward application of standard neural techniques to a well-known representation. Prior neural coherence models (e.g., Li & Hovy 2014) already learn coherence over sentence windows. Moreover, treating columns (entities) independently and using max-pooling discards inter-entity interactions and positional information that can be crucial for discourse phenomena (topic shifts, global structure).

- Entity identification/coreference pipeline is underspecified and potentially weak. The paper alternately mentions coreference resolution and noun matching (“They match the nouns to detect coreferent entities.”). The exact pipeline (which coref system, settings, inclusion of non-head nouns) is not clearly specified, and there is no analysis of sensitivity to coref quality. Given that the entire approach depends on grid quality, this is a significant gap in reproducibility and robustness claims.

- Incorporating entity-specific features by symbol typing is not obviously scalable. Encoding feature-role combinations as single symbols (e.g., PERSON-S) inflates the vocabulary V and may reintroduce sparsity for richer feature sets. The paper limits itself to three features; it is unclear whether the approach would remain effective with the nine features of Elsner & Charniak or beyond. Alternative designs (separate embeddings per feature and compositional combination) are not explored.

- Training–evaluation mismatch and limited analysis for the insertion task. The models are trained on discrimination pairs but evaluated on insertion without task-specific training; the authors acknowledge this likely hurts insertion gains. A fairer comparison would include training directly on the insertion objective or providing ablations to quantify the effect. More generally, the analysis is limited: there is no sensitivity study over k, pooling, or embedding sizes, nor qualitative error analysis.

- Dataset choices and comparability. While WSJ is a reasonable choice and larger than AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES, not reporting results on those common benchmarks reduces comparability with a substantial body of prior work. The reported failure of Li & Hovy (2014) on WSJ without deeper investigation is unsatisfying; stronger tuning or an explanation of domain mismatch would make the comparison more credible.

Questions to authors
- Entity pipeline: Which POS tagger, parser, and coreference resolver did you use? Do you include non-head nouns and how do you map mentions to entities (string match vs. coref chains)? Please quantify the number of entities per document and the effect of using coref vs. noun matching.

- Vocabulary size and feature scalability: What is |V| for the basic and extended settings? How does performance vary as you add more entity-specific features? Have you tried modeling features with separate embeddings (and concatenation or summation) rather than symbol-typing?

- Transition window and pooling: What is the impact of window size k, number of filters, and pooling length p on performance? Does performance degrade for very long or very short documents? Any evidence that max-pooling’s position invariance harms certain cases?

- Insertion-specific training: Can you train with a loss tailored to insertion (e.g., ranking candidate insertion positions) and report whether this narrows the gap?

- Comparison to Li & Hovy (2014): Can you provide more detail about the training setup that led to 19% discrimination accuracy? Did you explore different sentence encoders, window sizes, or domain pretraining to ensure a fair comparison?

- Qualitative analysis: Can you show examples of learned filters (e.g., transition patterns with high activation) and typical errors to better understand what the model captures beyond frequency and salience?
