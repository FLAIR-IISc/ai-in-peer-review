Summary
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a CNN over entity grids, embedding grammatical-role symbols (optionally augmented with entity-specific features) and learning transition patterns via convolution and max-pooling. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective. Evaluations on sentence discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating show consistent improvements over grid-based and graph baselines, with significance testing and multiple runs.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A neuralized, end-to-end entity-grid model that captures longer entity-transition dependencies via distributed representations and convolution, addressing sparsity and task-agnostic feature extraction in prior grid models.
2) Strong empirical results across three coherence evaluation tasks, including effective transfer via pretraining to a low-data summary rating setting.
3) A simple yet effective mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features without combinatorial blow-up by composing features with role symbols.

Strengths
- Addresses core limitations of classic grid models. Embedding roles and convolving over columns enables longer transition windows (k≈5–8) without sparsity, directly linked to improved performance.
- Consistent, significant SOTA gains across tasks. Reported absolute improvements of ~4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary rating (with pretraining). Significance tests and averaging over multiple seeds strengthen credibility.
- Sound training and evaluation protocol. Pairwise ranking aligns with coherence as preference; hyperparameter tuning on a dev set, early stopping, dropout, and RMSprop are appropriate. Use of a large WSJ corpus improves robustness; transfer to DUC is well-motivated.
- Practical integration of entity features. Achieves further gains using only three easily obtainable features, suggesting a favorable accuracy–complexity trade-off.
- Reproducibility. Public code and clear settings (filters, pooling, batch sizes) facilitate replication.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty. “Neuralizing” entity grids with CNNs is a natural extension; related neural coherence (e.g., sentence-level RNN/CNN models) dampens conceptual novelty.
- Limited ablations/analysis. No systematic study of transition window k, pooling, embedding size, or per-feature contributions; little interpretability (e.g., learned filters/patterns).
- Baseline coverage and fairness. The strongest non-neural extended grid uses 9 features vs. the neural model’s 3; a controlled comparison with matched feature sets would clarify gains attributable to modeling vs. features. Excluding Li & Hovy due to poor reproduction leaves a gap; stronger discourse-relation baselines (RST-based) could be added.
- Task mismatch. Training on discrimination but evaluating on insertion may understate potential; absence of insertion-trained results limits conclusions about task-specific learning.
- Coreference dependency and domain scope. Results rely on coreference quality; no error or domain transfer analysis beyond WSJ/DUC.

Questions to Authors
- How sensitive is performance to coreference errors and entity salience thresholds?
- What are the effects of k, pooling length, and embedding size? Any qualitative examples of filters detecting meaningful transition motifs?
- Can you train directly for insertion, and does it close the insertion gap?
- How do results change when matching entity feature sets across neural and non-neural grids?
- What is runtime/latency and model size for deployment scenarios?
