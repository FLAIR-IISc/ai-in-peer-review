Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a CNN over entity grids. The model replaces sparse, discrete transition features with learned distributed representations; convolutions along entity columns capture longer-range transitions; and a pairwise ranking objective enables task-specific end-to-end learning. The model is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) on WSJ and summary coherence rating on DUC’03, achieving state-of-the-art results and outperforming prior entity-grid variants and a graph-based baseline.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A neuralized entity-grid architecture (embeddings + convolution + pooling) that can model longer entity-transition patterns than traditional grids, trained with pairwise ranking. This is a clear, concrete modeling contribution with good empirical gains.
2) A practical recipe to incorporate simple entity-specific features into the neural grid by composing role-symbols with feature values (e.g., PERSON-S), avoiding combinatorial explosion and preserving generalization.
3) A thorough experimental setup on standard tasks with significance testing and pretraining/fine-tuning across domains (WSJ→DUC’03), showing robustness.

Strengths
- Clear advancement over strong grid baselines: On WSJ, the neural grid (Grid-CNN) improves over Grid-all-nouns by ~4% in discrimination and ~1% in insertion; Extended Grid-CNN further improves over Extended Grid by ~3.7% (discr.) and ~2.5% (ins.). These are meaningful margins over the strongest prior grid model (Elsner & Charniak, 2011). Significance testing supports the claims.
- Addresses core limitations of entity grids: By learning embeddings for grammatical-role+feature symbols and using convolutions, the model avoids the sparsity/curse-of-dimensionality that limits transition length (k ≤ 3) in prior work. The best settings use windows of 5–6, supporting the claim of capturing longer-range dependencies.
- End-to-end, task-specific learning with pairwise ranking: The training aligns with common evaluation formats (pairwise document preferences), providing a scoring function useful in generation/ordering. Early stopping and dropout are used, and five random seeds are averaged, improving reproducibility.
- Sensible feature design and ablation: The paper compares pure transition models (Grid-CNN) and feature-augmented ones (Extended Grid-CNN), showing incremental gains from simple, easily-computed entity features (NE type, salience, proper mention), while still outperforming a non-neural extended grid that uses more features.
- Cross-task robustness and pretraining: On DUC’03 summary coherence rating, the pre-trained WSJ models fine-tuned on small data yield further gains (up to +3.7% over the grid baseline), indicating reusability of the learned representations across domains and tasks where data is scarce.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparison to alternative neural coherence formalisms: The paper mentions Li & Hovy (2014) but reports very low reproduction accuracy without deeper analysis or hyperparameter sweeps. Given that model’s different paradigm (sentence-level composition), a stronger attempt at tuning or reporting a published number on the same dataset would strengthen the case that the proposed approach is superior among neural methods, not only among grid-based ones.
- Fairness and clarity of preprocessing: Performance of grid models is sensitive to coreference resolution, NP detection, and parsing. It is unclear whether the same pipeline and parameters were used for both the neural model and the Brown coherence toolkit baselines, and which coreference system was used. Without ensuring identical preprocessing, the improvements may partly reflect better coref/NP handling rather than modeling advances.
- Independence assumptions and locality: The model treats entity columns independently and composes over local sentence windows within columns. This misses cross-entity interactions (e.g., role alternation between entities) and global document structure. While local coherence is the target, some prior work (e.g., discourse relations, RST) captures complementary signals; the paper mentions this in future work but does not quantify the residual errors attributable to these design limits.
- Evaluation scope and analysis depth: While results are reported with significance tests, the paper lacks qualitative analysis/error analysis (e.g., examples of learned filters and transition patterns, where the model fails, impact of document length, or sensitivity to coref errors). Ablations isolating the effect of k (filter width), pooling length, and each entity feature would clarify where the gains come from.
- Notational/technical clarity: Some equations and dimensions are opaque or possibly inconsistent (e.g., feature map length “m.n+k−1” suggests confusion between sentences, entities, and padding; convolution axis and padding strategy per column should be clarified). Also, the insertion evaluation column labels in Table 3 are unclear (both discrimination and insertion listed as identical percentages), which needs correction.

Questions to authors
- Preprocessing: Which coreference resolver, parser, and NP extraction settings were used to build the entity grids for your model and for the baselines? Are they identical? If not, please quantify the effect.
- Reproducibility of Li & Hovy (2014): What hyperparameters and training data did you use to reproduce this baseline? Did you try larger windows L, pre-trained word embeddings, or curriculum strategies reported in that work?
- Convolutional axis and dimensions: Do filters slide only along the sentence axis within a single entity column (with wide padding across sentences), or also across entities? Please correct the feature map dimensionality and provide a schematic for one column.
- Insertion results: Table 3 shows identical numbers for discrimination and insertion; is this a typesetting error? Please provide the correct insertion accuracies and the evaluation metric definition.
- Robustness to coref noise: Have you measured performance when replacing gold vs. automatic coreference (or by simulating noise)? How sensitive is the model to the number of entities and document length?

Additional comments
- Consider adding ablations for (i) filter width k, (ii) pooling size, (iii) each entity feature, and (iv) training the pairwise loss specifically on insertion (using near-miss examples) to test task-specific learning.
- Minor: fix typos (“losing” not “loosing”), and clarify the DUC baseline training setup (why extended grid could not be trained pairwise).
