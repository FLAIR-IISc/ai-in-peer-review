Summary
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that operates on the entity grid representation. It replaces discrete transition counts with distributed embeddings of grammatical roles (and optional entity-specific features), applies 1D convolutions over column-wise entity transitions to capture longer patterns, and uses max-pooling and a linear layer to score coherence. Parameters are trained with a pairwise ranking objective. The model is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) on WSJ and summary coherence rating on DUC’03, achieving state-of-the-art performance over strong entity-grid baselines. Pretraining on WSJ helps on DUC’03.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A neuralized entity-grid model using CNNs that captures longer entity transition patterns and reduces sparsity via distributed role/feature embeddings, yielding consistent SOTA gains on standard coherence tasks.
2) An end-to-end pairwise ranking training regime that learns task-specific features from entity grids and supports transfer (pretrain on WSJ, finetune on DUC’03).
3) An empirical study showing the neural grid outperforms the extended grid despite using fewer handcrafted features, suggesting better generalization.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture: The move from discrete transition counts (which suffer from exponential sparsity with k) to embeddings + convolution is well-justified. Convolution across grid columns directly encodes longer transition dependencies (k≈5–8), while max-pooling provides location invariance to detect salient patterns. This design is faithful to the grid’s assumptions and addresses known limitations.
- Strong empirical gains with significance: On WSJ, Grid-CNN improves ≈4% absolute over Grid-all-nouns in discrimination and ≈1% in insertion; Extended Grid-CNN improves ≈3.7–3.8% over Extended Grid in discrimination and ≈2.5% in insertion, with p<0.01 against non-neural counterparts. On DUC’03, pretraining improves further (reported +3–4% over non-neural grid), demonstrating robustness across tasks.
- Sensible training objective and transfer: Pairwise ranking aligns with coherence scoring (and matches prior SVM-ranking setups). The pretrain-then-finetune result on DUC’03 is compelling given the small data, indicating the model learns task-relevant features from large WSJ training signals.
- Reproducibility and methodology: The paper provides implementation details (Theano, optimizer, hyperparameter search ranges), uses a dev set, early stopping, dropout, averages over multiple runs, and conducts approximate randomization tests. Code release further strengthens reproducibility.
- Fair baseline coverage in the entity-grid family: The paper reimplements/uses standard toolkits for the “all-nouns” and extended grid (E&C 2011) and a graph-based method (Guinaudeau & Strube 2013), reproducing strong non-neural references and surpassing them.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablations/analysis: The paper does not systematically analyze the impact of key architectural choices (transition window k, pooling length, number of filters), or the contribution of each entity-specific feature in the neural model. Qualitative analyses (e.g., what patterns filters detect, error types where the neural model helps/hurts) are missing, making it hard to interpret what the model learns beyond aggregate gains.
- Coreference and parsing dependency underexplored: Results hinge on coreference resolution and syntactic role labeling, yet the paper does not specify the tools used, their quality, or the model’s sensitivity to errors. Without robustness checks (e.g., alternative resolvers, subsets with better coref), generalization claims remain incomplete.
- Independence assumptions may be restrictive: Treating entity columns independently and using location-invariant max-pooling discards cross-entity interactions and positional cues (e.g., global topic shifts, beginnings/endings). The paper does not test whether modeling cross-entity patterns (e.g., 2D conv or attention across columns) or positional features would help, nor does it provide evidence that the independence assumption is harmless.
- Training/evaluation mismatch for insertion: The model is trained on discrimination-style pairs but evaluated on insertion, which is harder and structurally different. The paper acknowledges this but does not attempt training directly for insertion (e.g., constructing pairwise preferences for candidate insertion positions), leaving potential performance untapped and the evaluation less conclusive.
- Dataset choices and baseline gaps: Restricting to WSJ improves scale but reduces comparability with prior reported results on AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES; the rationale is plausible, yet showing at least some transfer would strengthen claims of generality. For DUC’03, the non-neural extended grid is omitted due to training mismatch; a fairer comparison (e.g., adapting it to pairwise training or reporting its standard non-pairwise performance) would contextualize gains. The Li & Hovy (2014) neural sentence model is excluded due to poor reimplementation results; more diagnostic analysis or reproducing their original setting on their datasets would avoid the impression of cherry-picking.
  
Questions to Authors
- Which coreference resolver and syntactic role extractor were used? Can you report the model’s sensitivity to switching resolvers or to noisy vs. high-quality coref (e.g., on an OntoNotes subset)?
- How does performance vary with transition window size k and pooling length? Is there evidence that longer k (>3) is responsible for the gains?
- Have you tried modeling cross-entity interactions (e.g., 2D convolutions over the grid or attention across columns)? Any preliminary results?
- Can you train the model directly on insertion-style pairs and report the effect? Similarly, can you include sentence position features or relative index to test positional effects?
- For DUC’03, can you provide variance/confidence intervals and significance tests, given the small sample size? Also, can you provide a comparable non-neural extended-grid baseline for rating (even if not pairwise)?

Overall assessment
This paper addresses a well-known limitation of entity-grid coherence models with a simple, well-motivated neural architecture and shows consistent, statistically significant improvements on standard tasks. While analysis is lighter than ideal and some comparisons are incomplete, the contribution is clear, useful, and timely for ACL 2017. With added ablations, robustness studies, and direct insertion training, the work would be even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
