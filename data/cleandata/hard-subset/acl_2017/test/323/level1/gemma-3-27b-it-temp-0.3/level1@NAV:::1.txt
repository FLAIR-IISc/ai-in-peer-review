## Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

This paper presents a neural network-based approach to local coherence modeling, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely, discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation – by employing convolutional neural networks to capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features through end-to-end training. The model is evaluated on three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), achieving state-of-the-art results.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful application of a convolutional neural network to the entity grid representation for coherence modeling. While neural networks have been applied to various NLP tasks, effectively integrating them with the established entity grid framework, and demonstrating performance gains across multiple evaluation tasks, is a significant step. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of end-to-end training for task-specific feature learning within this framework, allowing the model to adapt to different coherence assessment scenarios.

The strengths of this submission are considerable. The experimental results consistently outperform existing models on all three tasks, with statistically significant improvements reported. The inclusion of entity-specific features further enhances performance, demonstrating the model’s flexibility. The authors also provide clear motivation for their approach, outlining the limitations of previous work and positioning their contribution effectively. Releasing the code is a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The novelty of applying CNNs to this specific problem is somewhat incremental; the core idea isn't radically new, but rather a sensible adaptation of existing techniques. The description of the hyperparameter tuning process is brief, and more detail would strengthen the reproducibility claims. While the authors mention pre-training, the impact of different pre-training strategies isn’t thoroughly explored. Finally, the discussion of related work, while comprehensive, could benefit from a more critical analysis of existing neural coherence models beyond Li & Hovy (2014).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the criteria used for selection?
2.  Did you experiment with different pre-training strategies (e.g., using word embeddings trained on a large corpus)? If so, what were the results?
3.  The paper mentions limitations of the WSJ corpus. How might the model's performance generalize to other text genres or domains?
