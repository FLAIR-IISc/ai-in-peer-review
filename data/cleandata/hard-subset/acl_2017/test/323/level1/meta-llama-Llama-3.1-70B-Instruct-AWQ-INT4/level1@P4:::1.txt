**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates over the entity grid representation of a text. The model captures long-range entity transitions along with entity-specific features without losing generalization, thanks to the power of distributed representation. The authors present a pairwise ranking method to train the model in an end-to-end fashion on a task and learn task-specific high-level features. The evaluation on three different coherence assessment tasks demonstrates that the model achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming existing models by a good margin.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural Local Coherence Model**: The authors propose a neural architecture for coherence assessment that can capture long-range entity transitions along with arbitrary entity-specific features. The model obtains generalization through distributed representations of entity transitions and entity features.
2. **Pairwise Ranking Method**: The authors present an end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high-level features automatically in the model. The pairwise ranking approach allows the model to learn to distinguish between coherent and incoherent texts.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art results on three different coherence assessment tasks, outperforming existing models by a good margin.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective Use of Distributed Representation**: The authors effectively use distributed representation to capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features, which allows the model to obtain generalization.
2. **End-to-End Training**: The pairwise ranking method allows the model to learn task-specific high-level features automatically, which is a significant improvement over traditional coherence models.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art results on three different coherence assessment tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their model on three coherence assessment tasks, which may not be comprehensive enough to demonstrate the model's effectiveness.
2. **Lack of Comparison with Other Neural Models**: The authors only compare their model with traditional coherence models, but not with other neural models, which may be a limitation.
3. **Limited Analysis of Model Components**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the model components, such as the convolutional layer and the pooling layer, which may be useful to understand the model's behavior.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How did you choose the hyperparameters for the neural model, and what was the effect of different hyperparameters on the model's performance?
2. Can you provide more details on how the pairwise ranking method works, and how it allows the model to learn task-specific high-level features?
3. Have you considered evaluating the model on other coherence assessment tasks, such as coherence scoring or coherence classification?
