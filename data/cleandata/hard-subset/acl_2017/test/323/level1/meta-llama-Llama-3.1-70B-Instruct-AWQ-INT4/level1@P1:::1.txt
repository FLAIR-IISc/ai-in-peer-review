**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that uses a convolutional neural network (CNN) to capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features in text. The model operates on the entity grid representation of a text, which captures the distribution of discourse entities across sentences. The authors claim that their model achieves state-of-the-art results on three coherence assessment tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural local coherence model**: The authors propose a neural architecture that can capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features without losing generalization power.
2. **Convolutional feature learning**: The model uses a convolutional approach to compose local patches of entity transitions into higher-level representations, allowing it to capture longer transitional dependencies without overfitting.
3. **Pairwise ranking approach**: The authors present an end-to-end training method that uses a pairwise ranking approach to learn task-specific features automatically.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved performance**: The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art results on three coherence assessment tasks, outperforming existing models by a significant margin.
2. **Ability to capture long-range dependencies**: The convolutional architecture allows the model to capture longer transitional dependencies without overfitting, which is a significant improvement over traditional grid models.
3. **Flexibility and generalization**: The model can be trained on different target tasks and can incorporate entity-specific features without losing generalization power.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their model on three coherence assessment tasks, which may not be comprehensive enough to demonstrate the model's full potential.
2. **Dependence on pre-training**: The authors note that their model may not learn well for the summary coherence rating task without pre-training, which may limit its applicability.
3. **Lack of comparison to other neural models**: The authors only compare their model to traditional grid models and a graph-based model, but not to other neural models that may have similar architectures or objectives.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend their model to other forms of discourse, such as asynchronous conversations?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the pre-training process and how it affects the model's performance on different tasks?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting, which may occur when training the model on smaller datasets?
