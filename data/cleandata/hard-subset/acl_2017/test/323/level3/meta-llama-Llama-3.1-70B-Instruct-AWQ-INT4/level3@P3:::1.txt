This paper presents a novel neural architecture for modeling local coherence in text, which extends the entity grid model with a convolutional neural network (CNN) to learn entity-transition sequences and better generalize over longer sequences. The authors' approach is well-motivated, and their manuscript is well-written and fluent.

The key contributions of this paper are: (1) the proposal of a neural architecture for coherence assessment that can capture long-range entity transitions along with arbitrary entity-specific features; (2) the presentation of an end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high-level features automatically in the model; and (3) the evaluation of the approach on three different coherence assessment tasks, demonstrating state-of-the-art results.

The strengths of this paper include the innovative use of a CNN to model entity transitions, which allows for the capture of long-range dependencies without overfitting. The authors' decision to use a pairwise ranking approach to train the model is also well-justified, as it enables the model to learn task-specific features. The experimental results are impressive, with the proposed model outperforming existing models on all three evaluation tasks.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One major concern is the unexpected poor performance of the Li and Hovy model, which raises concerns about the experimental setup or corpus-dependence. The authors should have investigated this issue further and provided more insights into why the Li and Hovy model performed poorly. Additionally, the authors should have documented their efforts and difficulties encountered when running the Li and Hovy system. It would also be helpful to reproduce Li and Hovy's reported results on their original datasets as a sanity check.

Another weakness is the lack of comparison to other neural coherence models. The authors should have included more comparisons to other neural models to demonstrate the superiority of their approach. Furthermore, the authors could have provided more analysis on the learned features and the impact of different hyperparameters on the model's performance.

In terms of presentation, the paper could benefit from some minor revisions. There are several instances of missing definite articles throughout the paper, which should be corrected. Additionally, the authors should rephrase the recurring expression "limits the model to do X" to clearer alternatives like "limits the model's capacity to do X" or "stops the model from doing X."

Overall, this paper presents a novel and effective approach to modeling local coherence in text. With some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
