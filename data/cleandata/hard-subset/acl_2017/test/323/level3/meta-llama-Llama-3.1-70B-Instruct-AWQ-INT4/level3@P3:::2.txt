This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on a convolutional neural network that operates over the entity grid representation of a text. The model captures long-range entity transitions along with entity-specific features without losing generalization, thanks to the power of distributed representation. The authors present a pairwise ranking method to train the model in an end-to-end fashion on a task and learn task-specific high-level features.

The approach is well-motivated with clear discussion of design intuitions, such as why CNN is chosen and how it works. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their neural architecture, including the transformation of grammatical roles into feature vectors, modeling entity transitions, and coherence scoring. They also describe how to incorporate entity-specific features into their model.

The evaluation on three different coherence assessment tasks demonstrates that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming existing models by a good margin. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison with several existing models, including a random baseline, graph-based model, and entity grid models. They also present results on the summary coherence rating task, where their models outperform existing models and achieve further improvements with pre-training.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. The authors are unable to reproduce one prior result (Li/Hovy 2014), suggesting a possible implementation or modeling error that needs addressing. The method is largely a straightforward "neuralization" of the entity grid; it lacks substantial representational or task innovations expected for a full paper. The justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent four-dimensional grid entries is unclear, and the benefit of using longer transition chains (k=6 vs k=3) is questionable given that many WSJ articles are short.

The writing is verbose and repetitive, with introductory material and related work duplicated (notably section 6 repeats section 2) and should be reorganized/condensed. Minor language and proofreading issues, such as missing articles and phrasing problems, are present throughout the paper. Specific editorial fixes are needed, such as replacing 'Figure 2' with 'Figure 1' (p.1 bottom left), changing 'Firstly/Secondly' to 'First, Second', and rephrasing 'limits the model to' to 'prevents the model from considering...'.

In terms of contributions, the paper's primary contribution is the proposal of a neural local coherence model that achieves state-of-the-art results on several coherence assessment tasks. The paper's secondary contribution is the evaluation of the proposed method on multiple tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.

Strengths of the paper include:

1. The proposal of a neural local coherence model that achieves state-of-the-art results on several coherence assessment tasks.
2. The comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method on multiple tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness.
3. The clear discussion of design intuitions and the explanation of the neural architecture.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

1. The inability to reproduce one prior result (Li/Hovy 2014), suggesting a possible implementation or modeling error that needs addressing.
2. The lack of substantial representational or task innovations expected for a full paper.
3. The unclear justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent four-dimensional grid entries.
4. The questionable benefit of using longer transition chains (k=6 vs k=3) given that many WSJ articles are short.
5. The verbose and repetitive writing, with introductory material and related work duplicated.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the implementation of the Li/Hovy 2014 model and the possible reasons for the discrepancy in results?
2. How do you plan to address the lack of substantial representational or task innovations in future work?
3. Can you provide more justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent four-dimensional grid entries?
4. How do you plan to investigate the benefit of using longer transition chains (k=6 vs k=3) in future work?
5. Can you provide more details on the pre-training and fine-tuning process for the summary coherence rating task?
