**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates over the entity grid representation of a text. The model captures long-range entity transitions along with entity-specific features without losing generalization. The authors also present an end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high-level features automatically. The evaluation on three different coherence assessment tasks demonstrates that the proposed method outperforms existing approaches.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neuralization of Entity Grid Model**: The authors propose a neural extension of the basic entity grid model, which can capture long-range entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features without losing generalization power.
2. **Convolutional Architecture**: The authors use a convolutional architecture to model entity transitions, which allows for the capture of local patterns in the entity grid.
3. **Pairwise Ranking Approach**: The authors propose a pairwise ranking approach to train the model on a target task and learn task-specific features.

**Strengths**

1. **State-of-the-art Results**: The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on three different coherence assessment tasks, outperforming existing approaches.
2. **Effective Use of Convolutional Architecture**: The authors effectively use a convolutional architecture to model entity transitions, which allows for the capture of local patterns in the entity grid.
3. **End-to-end Training**: The authors propose an end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high-level features automatically, which simplifies the training process.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Innovation**: The method is largely a straightforward "neuralization" of the entity grid, lacking substantial representational or task innovations expected for a full paper.
2. **Unclear Justification**: The authors do not provide a clear justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent four-dimensional grid entries.
3. **Minor Language and Proofreading Issues**: The paper contains minor language and proofreading issues, such as missing articles and phrasing problems throughout.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the choice of hyperparameters and their impact on the performance of the model?
2. How do you plan to extend the model to other forms of discourse, such as asynchronous conversations?
3. Can you provide more insights into the failure to reproduce one prior result (Li/Hovy 2014), suggesting a possible implementation or modeling error that needs addressing?
