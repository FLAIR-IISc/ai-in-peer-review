## Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) applied to the entity grid representation to model text coherence. The core idea is to leverage the power of distributed representations and CNNs to overcome limitations of traditional, discrete feature-based entity grid models. The authors motivate this approach by arguing that CNNs can capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion. They evaluate their model on three coherence assessment tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of a CNN to the entity grid framework, demonstrating that a “neuralization” of this established approach can yield significant performance gains. A secondary contribution lies in the extensive evaluation across multiple tasks, providing a comprehensive assessment of the proposed model’s effectiveness. Finally, the authors provide a clear and well-motivated explanation of why a CNN is a suitable architecture for this task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing approaches on all three evaluation tasks, with statistically significant improvements reported. This demonstrates the practical benefit of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Motivation and Intuition:** The paper provides a compelling rationale for using a CNN, explaining how it addresses the limitations of traditional entity grid models (e.g., handling long-range dependencies, learning task-specific features). The "Why it works" section is particularly helpful.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating on three distinct tasks strengthens the validity of the findings and demonstrates the model’s generalizability.
*   **Code Release:** Releasing the code promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the results are strong, the approach is largely a straightforward application of a CNN to an existing framework. The paper lacks substantial representational or architectural innovations beyond this “neuralization”.
*   **Unjustified Dimensionality:** The choice of 100-dimensional vectors for representing four-dimensional grid entries is not adequately justified. A more thorough exploration of dimensionality and its impact on performance is needed.
*   **Questionable Benefit of Longer Transitions:** The benefit of using longer transition chains (k=6 vs k=3) is not convincingly demonstrated, particularly given the typically short length of WSJ articles.
*   **Repetitive Writing:** The paper suffers from significant redundancy, particularly in the introduction and related work sections (Section 6 largely repeats Section 2). This impacts readability and conciseness.
*   **Reproducibility Concern:** The inability to reproduce the Li/Hovy (2014) result raises concerns about potential implementation errors or modeling issues that need to be addressed.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of 100-dimensional vectors? Did you experiment with other dimensionalities, and if so, what were the results?
*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of transition chain length (k) on performance? Perhaps by showing results for different values of k on a subset of the data?
*   Can you clarify the discrepancies encountered when attempting to reproduce the Li/Hovy (2014) results? What steps have you taken to investigate and resolve this issue?
