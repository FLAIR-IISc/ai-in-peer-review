**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural model for assessing local text coherence by applying a convolutional neural network (CNN) to the entity grid representation. The model learns distributed representations for entity roles and their transitions, which are then used to produce a coherence score. The network is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective to distinguish between coherent and less coherent texts.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates that applying a standard CNN architecture to the classic entity grid representation achieves state-of-the-art performance on three established coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary rating), significantly outperforming previous methods.
2.  **A Well-Motivated Neural Framework:** The work presents a clear and effective "neuralization" of the entity grid. The architectural choices are well-motivated, particularly the use of a CNN to capture local, position-invariant entity transition patterns.

**Strengths**

1.  **Extensive and Compelling Evaluation:** The model's effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated through rigorous evaluation on multiple benchmarks. The consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines, including the non-neural extended entity grid model, provide strong evidence for the benefits of the proposed neural approach.
2.  **Clear Model Intuition:** The paper clearly explains the motivation behind its design. The rationale for using a CNN to learn salient transition patterns is intuitive, and the choice of a direct pairwise ranking objective for training is well-suited to the coherence assessment task.
3.  **Reproducibility:** The authors' decision to release their code with the submission is a significant strength, facilitating verification of their results and enabling future research to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The main weakness is that the method is a straightforward application of a well-known neural architecture (CNN) to a pre-existing representation (the entity grid). While empirically successful, this "neuralization" lacks the substantial representational or task-related innovation one might expect from a full conference paper.
2.  **Poor Writing and Organization:** The paper is verbose and poorly organized. The introduction and related work sections are highly repetitive, with Section 6 largely duplicating Section 2. The manuscript would benefit significantly from condensation and reorganization. There are also numerous proofreading and language errors (e.g., missing articles, awkward phrasing like "gain in insertion is less verbose"), and specific typos (e.g., "Figure 2" should be "Figure 1" on page 1).
3.  **Insufficient Justification for Model Parameters:** Several modeling choices are not well justified. It is unclear why 100-dimensional embeddings are used for what are essentially four-dimensional grid entries. Furthermore, the utility of learning long transition patterns (k=6) is questionable for a dataset like WSJ, where many articles are short.
4.  **Unresolved Comparison:** The authors report an inability to reproduce the results of a key prior work (Li/Hovy 2014), which they then exclude from comparison. This should be addressed, as it raises questions about the experimental setup or potential implementation errors.
