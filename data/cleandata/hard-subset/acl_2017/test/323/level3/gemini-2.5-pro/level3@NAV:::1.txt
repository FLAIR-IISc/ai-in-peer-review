**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that extends the classic entity grid representation. The primary contribution is a hybrid architecture where a convolutional neural network (CNN) operates on the entity grid to learn distributed representations of entity transition sequences. This approach effectively addresses a key limitation of traditional grid models—the inability to generalize over longer transition patterns due to feature sparsity. A secondary contribution is the strong empirical validation of this model, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on three standard coherence assessment tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  The proposed model is a novel and effective fusion of a structured linguistic representation (the entity grid) with a powerful neural learning architecture (a CNN). This pragmatic approach allows the model to capture long-range dependencies in entity transitions without suffering from the curse of dimensionality that plagues discrete feature-based methods.
2.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The motivation is well-argued, the model is explained intuitively, and the experimental setup is easy to follow, making the work and its contributions highly accessible.
3.  The empirical results are strong and convincing. The model consistently outperforms strong baselines, including its direct non-neural counterparts, across multiple evaluation tasks. The reported gains are statistically significant and provide compelling evidence for the model's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The paper's primary weakness is the inadequate comparison to the highly relevant neural coherence model by Li and Hovy (2014). The reported performance for this baseline is abnormally low (19.34% on a task with a 50% random baseline), which casts doubt on the validity of the comparison and weakens the state-of-the-art claim.
2.  The authors do not investigate or explain this failure. A deeper analysis is needed to understand if the issue stems from the experimental setup, corpus-dependence, or difficulties in running the baseline's code. This lack of analysis makes it difficult to fairly position the proposed work against prior neural approaches.
3.  The manuscript requires minor copy-editing. There are numerous missing definite articles and recurring awkward phrases (e.g., "limits the model to do X"), which should be corrected for clarity.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model, the reported performance is unexpectedly low. Could you elaborate on the difficulties you encountered while running their publicly available code?
2.  Have you considered performing a sanity check by attempting to reproduce their reported results on their original datasets to ensure the implementation is functioning correctly?
3.  Could you offer any hypotheses as to why their model might be particularly ill-suited for the WSJ corpus compared to the corpora on which it was originally evaluated?
