### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural extension to the well-established entity grid model for local coherence. The core idea is to apply a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) directly over the entity grid representation of a text. This approach aims to overcome a key limitation of traditional grid models: their inability to model long-range entity transitions due to the curse of dimensionality associated with discrete feature representations. By learning distributed representations for entity roles and using a CNN to compose features, the model can capture longer transition sequences and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence assessment tasks—discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating—and show that it achieves state-of-the-art results, significantly outperforming its non-neural counterparts.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Hybrid Architecture for Coherence Modeling:** The primary contribution is an effective and well-motivated hybridization of the classic, linguistically-grounded entity grid with a neural architecture. Applying a CNN to the grid is a clever way to leverage the structured representation of entity flow while using the power of neural networks to learn complex, long-range transition patterns that were previously intractable.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that this neural extension yields significant performance gains. The model sets a new state of the art on the WSJ sentence ordering benchmarks and the DUC summary rating task, outperforming strong baselines like the extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011) by a considerable margin.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model:** The proposed architecture is a natural and intelligent evolution of the entity grid model. The authors clearly identify a core weakness of prior work—the difficulty of modeling transitions of length k > 3—and propose a CNN-based solution that directly addresses this problem by learning from distributed representations. This combination of a structured linguistic input with a powerful neural learner is a compelling direction.
2.  **Strong and Consistent Experimental Results:** The model’s effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated across three distinct tasks. The absolute gains of ~4% on the discrimination task and ~2.5% on insertion over a very strong baseline (Extended Grid E&C) are impressive. The consistency of these improvements validates the authors' claims about the model's ability to learn more powerful features for coherence assessment.
3.  **Clear and Well-Written Manuscript:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation, model description, and experimental setup are presented with clarity, making the work highly accessible. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work within the existing literature and explaining the intuition behind their architectural choices.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Key Neural Baseline:** The treatment of the most relevant neural baseline, Li and Hovy (2014), is a major weakness. The authors report an alarmingly low accuracy of 19.34% on the discrimination task—far below the random baseline of 50%—and consequently exclude it from the main results. This is highly suspect and raises serious concerns about the experimental setup. A meaningful claim to state-of-the-art performance in neural coherence modeling requires a fair and thorough comparison to other prominent neural models. Simply dismissing a key competitor due to an unexplained, anomalous result is not sufficient.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Investigation into Baseline Failure:** Related to the point above, the paper lacks any investigation or discussion as to *why* the Li and Hovy model might have failed so catastrophically on the WSJ dataset. This could be due to implementation issues, hyper-parameter sensitivity, or fundamental differences in the corpora. A more rigorous analysis is needed. Documenting the difficulties encountered while running the provided code would be valuable for the community. A crucial sanity check would be to first reproduce Li and Hovy’s reported results on their original datasets before evaluating on WSJ.
3.  **Minor but Pervasive Grammatical Errors:** While generally fluent, the manuscript has numerous small grammatical errors that detract from its professionalism. The most frequent issue is the omission of definite articles (e.g., "in discrimination task," "in summary coherence rating task," "gain in insertion is less verbose"). Additionally, the recurring awkward phrasing "limits the model to do X" should be revised to clearer alternatives like "limits the model's ability to do X" or "prevents the model from doing X." Thorough proofreading is recommended.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the difficulties you faced when running the Li and Hovy (2014) system? Did you attempt to contact the authors or debug the cause of the extremely low performance? Could you confirm whether you were able to replicate their original results on the AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES corpora as a sanity check?
2.  Do you have a hypothesis for the discrepancy in the Li and Hovy model's performance? Could it be that their sentence-representation-based model is more sensitive to the domain or stylistic properties of the WSJ corpus compared to your entity-based approach? A discussion on this would significantly strengthen the paper's analysis.
