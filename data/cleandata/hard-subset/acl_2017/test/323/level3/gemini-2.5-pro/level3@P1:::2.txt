**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural model for local text coherence that applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) directly to the entity grid representation. The model learns distributed representations for grammatical roles and entity features, and the CNN is used to identify salient transition patterns of arbitrary length. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary rating), demonstrating that their model achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming previous entity-grid-based and other coherence models.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Strong Empirical Baseline:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical demonstration that a standard neural architecture (a CNN) can significantly improve upon the classic, feature-engineered entity grid model. The gains are consistent across multiple evaluation tasks, establishing a new, strong baseline for coherence modeling.
2.  **Effective Application of CNNs to a Symbolic Representation:** The work presents a well-motivated application of CNNs to the structured, symbolic entity grid. The authors clearly articulate the intuition behind using convolutions to learn location-invariant transition patterns, providing a solid example of "neuralizing" a classic NLP representation.

**Strengths**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The strongest argument for this paper is its performance. The proposed model achieves significant improvements over strong baselines, including the extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011). The absolute gains of ~4% on the discrimination task and over 2.5% on the more challenging insertion task are substantial. The consistent improvements across three distinct tasks demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed neural approach.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Explained Model:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating its design choices. The limitations of traditional entity grid models (curse of dimensionality, task-agnostic features) are clearly laid out, and the neural architecture is presented as a direct solution. The authors' explanation for "Why it works"—framing convolutional filters as detectors for specific transition patterns—is intuitive and adds to the paper's clarity.
3.  **Thorough and Reproducible Evaluation:** The evaluation is conducted on standard datasets (WSJ, DUC'03) and follows established protocols, making the results directly comparable to a large body of previous work. The authors compare against multiple relevant baselines and report results on several tasks that test different aspects of coherence. The decision to release the code further strengthens the paper's contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** The primary weakness is that the method itself is a relatively straightforward "neuralization" of a pre-existing technique. The core idea involves taking a classic representation (the entity grid) and applying a standard neural architecture (a CNN) on top of it. While the results are strong, the work lacks a substantial conceptual or representational innovation that one might expect from a top-tier conference paper. It consolidates and improves upon prior work rather than introducing a fundamentally new way of modeling coherence.
2.  **Unclear Justification for Key Hyperparameters:** Several key modeling choices lack clear justification.
    *   The use of 100-dimensional embeddings for a vocabulary of only four symbols ({S,O,X,-}) in the base model seems excessive. This high dimensionality could lead to an overly parameterized model without a clear benefit, and the authors do not provide an ablation or analysis to justify this choice over smaller, more efficient representations.
    *   The model uses long transition windows (k=5 or 6), which is claimed as an advantage over prior work (k≤3). However, given that the average document length in the WSJ dataset is around 22 sentences, it is questionable whether such long-range dependencies are prevalent or critical. The paper would be stronger if it included an analysis (e.g., ablation study on filter size) demonstrating a clear benefit from these longer windows.
3.  **Writing and Paper Organization:** The writing is often verbose and repetitive. In particular, Section 6 ("Related Work") largely repeats the background information already presented in detail in Section 2 ("Entity Grid and Its Extensions"). These sections could be condensed and merged to improve the paper's flow and conciseness. There are also minor but frequent language and proofreading issues (e.g., missing articles, awkward phrasing like "gain in insertion is less verbose"). The final paragraph of the introduction is unnecessary and could be removed.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014), you report being unable to achieve reasonable performance on your dataset. This is a strong claim against a related neural approach. Could you please elaborate on the potential reasons for this discrepancy? Was it an issue of dataset domain, document length, or a specific implementation detail? Clarifying this would help contextualize your model's performance.
2.  Could you provide a justification for using 100-dimensional embeddings to represent a very small vocabulary of grammatical roles? Have you experimented with smaller embedding dimensions, and if so, how did that affect performance?
3.  Your model is designed to capture long-range transitions (k=5, 6). Can you provide any analysis that demonstrates the model is truly benefiting from these longer dependencies compared to the shorter transitions (k=2, 3) used in prior work? For instance, an ablation study on the filter window size would be very informative.
