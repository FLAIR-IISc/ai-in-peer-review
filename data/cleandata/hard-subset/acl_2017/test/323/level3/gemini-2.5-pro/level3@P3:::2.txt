### **Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a neural model for assessing local text coherence. The approach applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) directly to an entity grid representation of a text. Each entry in the grid (representing an entity's grammatical role in a sentence) is mapped to a dense vector embedding. A 1D convolution is then applied to each column of the grid (representing the transition of a single entity across sentences) to extract higher-level features. These features are max-pooled and fed into a final layer to produce a coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective, learning to assign a higher score to an original document than to a permuted, less coherent version. The authors evaluate their model on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and demonstrate that it achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming previous entity grid-based methods.

### **Main Contributions**
1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The primary contribution is a thorough empirical demonstration that a standard neural architecture (a CNN) applied to a classic NLP representation (the entity grid) can significantly outperform prior feature-engineered models on multiple coherence assessment benchmarks. The consistent gains across all three tasks provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of this "neuralized" approach.
2.  **Reproducibility Analysis:** The paper provides a valuable, albeit brief, analysis of a prior neural coherence model (Li and Hovy, 2014), reporting an inability to reproduce its published performance on their dataset. This finding is an important contribution to the community, highlighting potential issues with prior work that warrant further investigation.

### **Strengths**
1.  **Excellent Motivation and Intuition:** The paper does a very good job of motivating the architectural choices. The authors clearly explain why the entity grid is a suitable representation and, more importantly, why a CNN is a natural fit for modeling it. The assumptions that entity columns are independent and that transition patterns are location-invariant are explicitly stated and directly lead to the convolutional approach. The "Why it works" paragraph provides a clear and intuitive explanation of what the filters and pooling layers are intended to capture.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering three different and well-established coherence tasks. The proposed models (Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN) are compared against strong and relevant baselines, including the non-neural models they are based on. The results are consistently strong, showing statistically significant improvements across the board (e.g., ~4% absolute gain in discrimination, ~2.5% in insertion over the extended grid). This provides robust evidence for the model's effectiveness.
3.  **Sensible Training Paradigm:** The use of a pairwise ranking objective is well-suited to the task of coherence modeling. As the authors and prior work note, coherence is better framed as a relative judgment than a binary classification. Training the model end-to-end with this objective allows it to learn representations and features that are directly optimized for discriminating between more and less coherent texts.

### **Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The main weakness is that the proposed method is largely a straightforward application of a standard neural architecture to a pre-existing representation. While effective, this "neuralization" of the entity grid does not introduce substantial innovations in representation learning or task formulation. The contribution lies more in the successful consolidation and modernization of prior work rather than in proposing a fundamentally new way to model coherence. For a full paper, one might expect a more novel architectural or representational component.
2.  **Unclear Justification for Key Hyperparameters:** Several important design choices are presented without sufficient justification.
    *   It is unclear why 100-dimensional vectors are used to embed the very small vocabulary of grid entries (e.g., {S, O, X, -} for the basic grid). This high dimensionality for a 4-symbol vocabulary seems excessive and could introduce unnecessary parameters.
    *   The benefit of using long transition windows (k=6 for Grid-CNN, k=5 for Extended Grid-CNN) is questionable for the WSJ dataset, where the average document length is ~22 sentences. It is not clear how many entities persist long enough for such transitions to be meaningful, and the model may be learning boundary effects from padding rather than true long-range dependencies.
3.  **Poor Paper Organization and Verbosity:** The writing and structure of the paper could be significantly improved. The text is often repetitive. Most notably, Section 6 (Related Work) largely repeats the background information already presented in Section 2 (Entity Grid and Its Extensions). These sections should be merged and condensed to improve readability and conciseness.

### **Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide a justification for using 100-dimensional embeddings for the small vocabulary of grammatical roles? Have you experimented with smaller embedding sizes (e.g., 10, 20), and how did they perform in comparison?
2.  Regarding the long convolution window sizes (k=5-6), can you provide any analysis on how frequently these long, non-padded transitions occur for single entities in the WSJ data? Is it possible that the performance gain comes primarily from the distributed representations and end-to-end training, rather than the model's ability to capture these specific long-range patterns?
3.  Your inability to reproduce the Li and Hovy (2014) result is an important finding. Could you elaborate on your experimental setup for that model? Do you have any hypotheses for the large performance discrepancy (e.g., differences in data preprocessing, entity grid generation, or hyperparameter sensitivity)?

### **Additional Comments**
The paper has several minor language and proofreading issues that should be addressed.
*   The writing often contains awkward phrasing or missing articles (e.g., "without loosing generalization," "gain in insertion is less verbose").
*   Page 1, bottom left column: "As shown in Figure 2" should likely be "As shown in Figure 1", as Figure 2 depicts the neural model, not the grid.
*   Throughout the paper, "Firstly" and "Secondly" should be replaced with "First" and "Second" for standard academic style.
*   In Section 1, a phrase like "limits the model to consider" would be better phrased as "prevents the model from considering".
*   The final paragraph of the introduction is a standard "road map" of the paper and could be removed to save space and improve flow.
