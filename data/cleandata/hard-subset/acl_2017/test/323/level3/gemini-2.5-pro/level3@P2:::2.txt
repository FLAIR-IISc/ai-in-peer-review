### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural model for assessing local text coherence. The model applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) directly to the entity grid representation of a text. By first embedding the grammatical roles (S, O, X, -) into a distributed vector space, the CNN can learn to identify salient transition patterns over long distances. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective, allowing it to learn task-specific features. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and demonstrate that their model achieves new state-of-the-art results, outperforming previous entity-grid and graph-based models.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for Coherence:** The primary contribution is the application of a convolutional neural network to an embedded entity grid representation. This "neuralization" of a classic coherence model effectively addresses the data sparsity and feature engineering limitations of previous discrete, count-based methods, allowing it to model longer entity transitions.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper presents a strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation across three distinct coherence tasks. The proposed model consistently outperforms prior work, including strong baselines like the extended entity grid model, by a significant margin (e.g., ~4% absolute gain in the discrimination task).

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model establishes a new state-of-the-art on the WSJ sentence ordering tasks and the DUC summary rating task. The gains over the non-neural counterparts (e.g., Extended Grid vs. Extended Grid-CNN) are consistent and significant, which strongly supports the effectiveness of the proposed neural architecture. The experimental setup is thorough, including hyperparameter tuning on a development set and reporting averaged results over multiple runs.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Explained Method:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work by clearly outlining the limitations of traditional entity grid models, namely the "curse of dimensionality" and the task-agnostic nature of feature extraction. It then provides clear intuition for how the proposed neural components—distributed embeddings and a CNN—are designed to overcome these specific limitations. The "Why it works" paragraph is a concise and effective explanation of the model's mechanism.
3.  **Reproducibility:** The authors use standard, publicly available datasets (WSJ, DUC) and compare against established baselines using a publicly available toolkit. Crucially, they state that their own code has been released with the submission, which is a major strength that will benefit the community by facilitating verification and future extensions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** While empirically successful, the proposed method is largely a straightforward combination of existing components: the entity grid (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008), word/symbol embeddings, a standard CNN architecture (Kim, 2014), and a pairwise ranking loss. This "neuralization" of a classic feature representation lacks a fundamental innovation in either the representation of coherence itself or the learning paradigm. It is a solid engineering contribution but may be borderline in novelty for a top-tier conference.
2.  **Poorly Justified Hyperparameter Choices:** The model uses 100-dimensional embeddings to represent a tiny vocabulary of just four grammatical roles ({S,O,X,-}) in the basic model. This seems excessively high and lacks justification. Such a high-dimensional space for a few discrete symbols risks overfitting and inefficiency. The paper would be much stronger if it included an analysis or ablation study on the impact of embedding dimensionality.
3.  **Unconvincing Motivation for Long-Range Transitions:** A key argument for the CNN is its ability to capture longer transition patterns (k=5-8) than prior models (k≤3). However, the experiments are run on WSJ articles with an average length of ~22 sentences. It is not clear that such long-range entity transitions are the primary driver of coherence in these relatively short texts. The performance gains could be due to other factors, such as better generalization from embeddings or the end-to-end training, rather than the increased context window. The paper provides no analysis to disentangle these effects.
4.  **Inability to Compare with a Key Baseline:** The authors report being unable to reproduce the results of a recent neural baseline (Li and Hovy, 2014), obtaining an accuracy of only 19.34% on the discrimination task. Excluding a key related work from the main comparison due to an apparent implementation issue is a significant weakness. This extremely poor result suggests a potential misunderstanding of the baseline model or a flaw in the experimental setup that should have been investigated more thoroughly.
5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The writing is often verbose and repetitive. In particular, Section 6 ("Related Work") largely restates information already presented in detail in Section 2 ("Entity Grid and Its Extensions"). These sections should be condensed and merged to improve the flow and make space for more substantive analysis, such as the ablation studies mentioned above. There are also several minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasings throughout the text (e.g., "gain in insertion is less verbose").

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide justification for using 100-dimensional embeddings for a vocabulary of only four symbols ({S, O, X, -})? Have you experimented with lower dimensions, and if so, how did that affect performance?
2.  Your motivation highlights the ability to model longer transitions (the optimal models use k=5 and k=6). Could you provide an analysis (e.g., by comparing models with different filter sizes k) to demonstrate that this specific capability, rather than other aspects of the neural model like end-to-end training, is a key reason for the performance gains on the WSJ dataset?
3.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model, the reported accuracy of 19.34% on a binary-choice task is puzzlingly low. Could you elaborate on your implementation and training setup? This result is anomalous and raises questions about the completeness of the experimental comparison. A more detailed explanation might help clarify the issue.
