### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling local text coherence. The model operates on the entity grid, a well-established representation for tracking entity mentions across sentences. Specifically, the authors apply a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to the entity grid to learn distributed representations of entity transition sequences. This approach is designed to overcome the "curse of dimensionality" that limits traditional, discrete entity grid models, allowing the model to capture longer-range dependencies and better generalize. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—demonstrating significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art models.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Hybrid Neural Coherence Model:** The primary contribution is the novel application of a CNN architecture to the entity grid representation. This is a well-motivated approach that directly addresses the known limitations of discrete feature representations in prior entity-based models, namely data sparsity and the difficulty of modeling long transition sequences. By learning dense vector representations for entity roles and transitions, the model can capture more complex patterns.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation across three distinct and challenging coherence tasks. The proposed model consistently and significantly outperforms strong baselines, including its non-neural counterparts (the Elsner & Charniak models). These state-of-the-art results provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed neural architecture.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Sound and Elegant Model Design:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating its architectural choices. Extending the successful entity grid framework with a neural component is a logical and effective strategy. Using a CNN is a natural fit for capturing local, position-invariant transition patterns within the grid, and the end-to-end training with a ranking loss allows the model to learn task-specific features directly. The overall approach is a strong example of integrating neural techniques to improve a classic, symbolic NLP model.

2.  **Convincing Experimental Results:** The empirical results are a major strength of the paper. The model achieves new state-of-the-art performance on all three evaluation tasks, with notable gains over the highly competitive extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011). The gains are consistent across tasks of varying difficulty (e.g., discrimination and the harder insertion task), which underscores the robustness and power of the learned representations.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the limitations of prior work and how their model addresses them. The inclusion of implementation details and the commitment to release the code are commendable and significantly enhance the paper's value for the community.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with a Key Neural Baseline:** My main concern is the treatment of the neural coherence model by Li and Hovy (2014). The authors report an extremely low accuracy (19.34%) for this model on their dataset and then exclude it from the main comparison table. This is problematic for a paper claiming state-of-the-art results. Such a strikingly poor result for a previously published strong baseline warrants a much deeper investigation. Without a proper analysis, it raises questions about the experimental setup, potential implementation errors, or corpus-specific sensitivities that are not discussed. A robust comparison requires either successfully running the baseline or providing a detailed analysis of why it failed, which is currently missing.

2.  **Hybrid Approach vs. End-to-End Learning:** The proposed model is a hybrid system that applies a neural network to a symbolic representation (the entity grid) generated by a complex upstream pipeline (parsing, coreference resolution, etc.). While effective, this approach inherits any errors and limitations from that pipeline. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of this design choice versus a more "fully neural" approach that might operate directly on raw text, and the potential impact of error propagation from the preprocessing steps.

### **Questions to Authors**

The most critical point to address is the comparison with the Li and Hovy (2014) model.

1.  Could you please elaborate on your efforts to run the Li and Hovy model? Specifically:
    a. Did you use the authors' publicly available code? What hyper-parameters and settings were used?
    b. Did you attempt to first reproduce their reported results on their original datasets (e.g., AIRPLANES, EARTHQUAKES) as a sanity check to ensure your setup was correct?
    c. Given the unexpectedly low performance on the WSJ dataset, do you have any hypotheses as to why the model failed so severely? A more thorough explanation is needed to substantiate your claim of outperforming other neural approaches.

### **Additional Comments**

The manuscript is well-written but could benefit from minor copy-editing.
*   Please proofread for missing definite articles (e.g., "the") throughout the paper.
*   The phrase "limits the model to do X" appears several times. Consider rephrasing for better fluency, such as "limits the model's capacity to do X" or "prevents the model from doing X."
