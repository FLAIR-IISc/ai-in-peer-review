- Proposes a CNN applied to the entity grid that learns distributed grammatical-role/entity embeddings and is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective, achieving SOTA on discrimination, insertion, and summary rating tasks.
- Contribution: provides a strong empirical baseline showing a standard CNN substantially improves classic entity-grid models across multiple tasks.
- Contribution: effectively "neuralizes" the symbolic entity grid, using convolutions to learn location-invariant transition patterns.
- Strength: achieves state-of-the-art empirical results (≈4% gain on discrimination, >2.5% on insertion) consistently across tasks.
- Strength: model is well-motivated and clearly explained, addressing dimensionality and task-agnostic feature limitations and interpreting filters as transition detectors.
- Strength: evaluation is thorough and reproducible—standard datasets (WSJ, DUC'03), multiple baselines, established protocols, and released code.
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—largely a straightforward application of a standard neural architecture to an existing representation.
- Weakness: unclear justification for key hyperparameters, notably 100-dimensional embeddings for a four-symbol vocabulary without ablation or analysis.
- Weakness: long transition windows (k=5,6) are used without analysis showing they provide clear benefits given average document lengths.
- Writing/organization issues: verbose and repetitive sections (Related Work repeats Entity Grid background), minor language/proofreading problems, and an unnecessary introductory paragraph.
- Question: please explain why the distributed sentence model of Li & Hovy (2014) could not be reproduced—dataset domain, document length, or implementation issues?
- Question: justify the use of 100-dimensional embeddings for grammatical roles and report results for smaller embedding sizes if available.
- Question: provide analysis (e.g., ablation on filter window size) demonstrating that long-range transitions (k=5,6) truly improve performance over shorter windows.
