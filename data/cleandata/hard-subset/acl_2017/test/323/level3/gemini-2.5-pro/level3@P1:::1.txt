### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, building upon the well-established entity grid representation. The core idea is to replace the traditional feature engineering approach of counting local entity transitions with a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates directly on the grid. Each grammatical role in the grid is mapped to a distributed vector representation (an embedding). The CNN then applies filters over the columns of the grid (representing entity transition sequences) to learn higher-level, task-specific features. The model is trained using a pairwise ranking objective to distinguish between coherent and less coherent texts. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and demonstrate that their neural model significantly outperforms previous entity grid models and other baselines, achieving new state-of-the-art results.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for the Entity Grid:** The primary contribution is the successful application of a convolutional neural network to the entity grid representation. This hybrid approach effectively "neuralizes" a successful symbolic model, leveraging the strengths of both paradigms. It uses the distributed representations and convolutional filters to overcome the "curse of dimensionality" that limited previous grid models to short-range transitions, enabling it to learn from longer entity sequences in a generalizable way.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The paper presents strong and convincing empirical results across three distinct coherence tasks. The proposed model consistently and significantly outperforms strong non-neural counterparts, including the sophisticated extended entity grid model from Elsner & Charniak (2011). This provides robust evidence for the model's effectiveness and its superiority over previous methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant Integration of Symbolic and Neural Approaches:** The model cleverly combines a linguistically-motivated, symbolic structure (the entity grid) with a powerful, data-driven learning architecture (a CNN). This design is a significant strength, as it grounds the neural model in a representation known to capture important aspects of coherence, while simultaneously using the neural components to learn more complex and longer-range patterns than was previously feasible. This is a well-motivated and effective modeling choice.

2.  **Strong and Thorough Experimental Results:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple standard tasks and datasets. The reported performance gains are substantial and statistically significant. For instance, the absolute improvements of around 4% on the discrimination task and over 2.5% on the insertion task over the strong Extended Grid baseline are impressive. The fact that the model achieves these gains with fewer hand-engineered features than its non-neural counterpart further underscores the power of the learned representations.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained clearly with a helpful diagram, the motivation is well-grounded in prior work, and the experimental setup is described in detail. The authors' decision to release their code is commendable and greatly enhances the reproducibility and potential impact of their work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with a Key Neural Baseline:** The biggest weakness is the treatment of the neural coherence model by Li and Hovy (2014). The authors report an accuracy of only 19.34% for this model on the discrimination task, which is far below the random baseline (50%). This result is highly anomalous and suggests a potential issue with the experimental setup, a failure to properly tune the model for the new dataset, or a fundamental misunderstanding of its application. As the most relevant prior work in neural coherence modeling, a more rigorous and convincing comparison is essential to properly contextualize the contributions of this paper. Without it, the claims of state-of-the-art performance in the neural space are difficult to fully evaluate.

2.  **Dependence on Upstream NLP Pipeline:** While the hybrid approach is a strength, it also means the model's performance is capped by the quality of the entity grid it receives. Errors from the upstream components (e.g., POS tagging, parsing, coreference resolution) will inevitably propagate and potentially limit the model's effectiveness. While this is a characteristic of the paradigm rather than a flaw in the model itself, a discussion of this dependency and its potential impact would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Minor Grammatical Errors:** The manuscript would benefit from a final proofreading pass. There are numerous instances of missing definite articles throughout the paper (e.g., "evaluation on three different coherence assessment tasks demonstrates", "in discrimination task"). Additionally, the recurring construction "limits the model to do X" is awkward and could be rephrased more clearly (e.g., "limits the model's ability to do X" or "prevents the model from doing X").

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the reported performance for the Li and Hovy (2014) model: Could you please provide more details on your experimental setup for this baseline? What efforts were made to tune its hyperparameters for the WSJ dataset? Did you encounter specific technical difficulties when running their publicly available code that might explain the unexpectedly poor result?

2.  To verify that the Li and Hovy code was functioning correctly, did you attempt to reproduce their reported results on one of the original datasets they used (e.g., AIRPLANES or EARTHQUAKES)? This would be a crucial sanity check to distinguish between an implementation issue and a genuine corpus-dependency of their model. Documenting these efforts in the paper would greatly strengthen your comparison.
