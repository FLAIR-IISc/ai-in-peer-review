- Proposes a convolutional neural local coherence model operating on the entity grid to capture long-range entity transitions with distributed representations.  
- Trains the model end-to-end via pairwise ranking and evaluates on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating tasks.  
- Shows state-of-the-art performance across the three coherence tasks, notably strong gains on the insertion task.  
- Main contribution: neuralization of the entity grid framework to learn task-specific features and model longer-range dependencies.  
- Secondary contribution: strong empirical results and public release of code for reproducibility.  
- Strength: effectively combines neural representation power with the theoretically grounded entity grid (Centering Theory).  
- Strength: end-to-end training removes reliance on manual feature engineering and enables task-specific feature learning.  
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation with an illustrative model figure.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to other neural coherence models; Li & Hovy (2014) baseline performance is surprisingly poor and under-investigated.  
- Weakness: lack of detailed documentation and diagnostics for reproducing Li & Hovy’s reported results.  
- Weakness: approach remains constrained by assumptions of the entity grid representation and doesn’t explore alternative representations.  
- Weakness: minor language/presentation issues that need proofreading.  
- Question: please detail the implementation challenges, parameter settings tried, and reasons for Li & Hovy’s poor performance.  
- Question: were alternative neural architectures (besides CNNs) explored, and why were convolutional networks chosen?  
- Question: why was only a subset of Elsner & Charniak (2011) entity-specific features used, and were larger feature sets tested?  
- Additional comment: investigate sensitivity to embedding size and filter window size.  
- Additional comment: include discussion of computational cost versus existing methods.  
- Additional comment: rephrase recurring awkward phrasing (e.g., “limits the model to do X”) for clarity.
