This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that extends the entity grid representation with a convolutional neural network. The model aims to capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion. The authors evaluate their approach on three coherence assessment tasks, reporting state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Neural Enhancement of Entity Grid Models:** The primary contribution is the successful integration of a convolutional neural network with the established entity grid framework. This allows the model to leverage the strengths of both approaches: the structured entity representation and the representational power of neural networks for capturing complex relationships.
2. **End-to-End Task-Specific Learning:** The authors present a pairwise ranking method for end-to-end training, which allows the model to learn features optimized for specific coherence assessment tasks, rather than relying on hand-engineered features.
3. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model demonstrates state-of-the-art performance across three different coherence evaluation tasks, indicating its effectiveness and generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Written and Fluent Manuscript:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is explained in a concise and understandable manner, and the experimental setup is well-defined.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The use of CNNs to model entity transitions is a logical and promising approach. The architecture effectively addresses the limitations of traditional entity grid models regarding long-range dependencies and discrete representations.  The pairwise ranking training objective is also well-suited for coherence assessment.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results are compelling, consistently outperforming existing models on all evaluated tasks. The reported gains (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4% on summary coherence rating) are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Code Release:** The release of the code is a valuable contribution, promoting reproducibility and facilitating future research in this area.
5. **Addressing Limitations of Existing Models:** The paper clearly articulates the shortcomings of existing entity grid models and effectively positions the proposed approach as a solution to these limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Recent Neural Models:** While the paper compares against several traditional entity grid models, the comparison to the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014) is insufficient. The reported performance of the Li and Hovy model (19.34% accuracy in discrimination) is unexpectedly low and raises significant concerns about the experimental setup, implementation, or corpus dependence. A more thorough investigation of this discrepancy is crucial.
2. **Potential Issues with Li and Hovy Replication:** The authors mention difficulties in running the Li and Hovy system but provide limited details about the challenges encountered. A more detailed description of these difficulties is necessary.  Furthermore, reproducing the reported results of Li and Hovy on their original datasets would serve as a valuable sanity check.
3. **Minor Language Issues:** The paper contains numerous instances of missing definite articles (“limits the model to do X” instead of “limits the model’s capacity to do X”). A careful proofread is needed to address these minor grammatical errors.
4. **Awkward Phrasing:** The recurring phrase "limits the model to do X" is somewhat awkward. Rephrasing to alternatives like “limits the model’s capacity to do X” or “prevents the model from doing X” would improve clarity.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Li and Hovy Performance:** Could you please provide a detailed account of the difficulties encountered while attempting to reproduce the results of the Li and Hovy model? What specific parameters and settings were used, and what were the observed issues?
2. **Li and Hovy Sanity Check:** Have you attempted to reproduce the reported results of Li and Hovy on their original datasets? If so, what were the results?
3. **Hyperparameter Search:** Can you elaborate on the justification for the specific hyperparameter settings chosen for the convolutional neural network? Was a more extensive search performed, and if so, what were the results?
4. **Ablation Studies:** Have you performed ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components of the model, such as the entity-specific features or the convolutional layers?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed model compared to traditional entity grid models.
*   It would be interesting to explore the impact of different pre-trained word embeddings on the model's performance.
*   The paper could benefit from a qualitative analysis of the learned features, providing insights into what the model is capturing.



**Final Recommendation:** 4 (Accept with minor revisions) - *Revised from a preliminary score of 3*. While the initial concerns regarding the comparison to Li and Hovy were significant, I acknowledge the inherent difficulties in replicating external code. The strong overall contributions of the paper, its clear presentation, and compelling empirical results justify acceptance, contingent on addressing the minor language issues and providing a more detailed account of the attempts to replicate the Li and Hovy model.
