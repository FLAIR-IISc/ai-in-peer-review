## Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network applied to the entity grid representation to model text coherence. The core idea is to "neuralize" the traditional entity grid approach, leveraging distributed representations and CNNs to capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on three coherence assessment tasks.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Consolidation of Entity Grid Approaches:** The paper provides a strong empirical baseline by consolidating and comparing various extensions of the entity grid model within a modern neural framework. This offers a valuable, reproducible resource for the coherence modeling community.
2. **Effective Neuralization of Entity Grid:** The application of CNNs to the entity grid representation proves effective, demonstrating improvements over traditional grid-based methods. This highlights the benefits of distributed representations and end-to-end training for coherence assessment.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three diverse tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) provides a robust assessment of the proposed model’s capabilities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model consistently outperforms existing methods across all three evaluation tasks, achieving statistically significant improvements on several metrics. The reported gains (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating) are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
2. **Clear Motivation and Design:** The paper provides a well-reasoned motivation for the use of CNNs, explicitly addressing the limitations of discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation in traditional entity grid models. The “Why it works” section clearly articulates the intuition behind the design choices.
3. **Reproducibility:** The authors have released their code, which is a significant positive aspect, fostering reproducibility and further research.
4. **Sound Experimental Setup:** The authors compare against a range of baseline models, including both traditional and recent approaches, providing a comprehensive and fair evaluation.
5. **Well-structured Presentation:** The paper follows a logical structure, clearly outlining the background, proposed method, experiments, and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the empirical results are strong, the core contribution feels incremental. The work largely represents a straightforward application of deep learning techniques to an established framework (entity grids) rather than a fundamentally new approach to coherence modeling. The representational and task innovations are relatively minor.
2. **Questionable Dimensionality Choice:** The justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent the four-dimensional grid entries (and slightly higher for the extended grid) is unclear. The authors do not provide sufficient reasoning for this dimensionality, raising concerns about potential information loss or redundancy. An ablation study exploring different embedding sizes would be beneficial.
3. **Uncertain Benefit of Longer Transitions:** The benefit of using longer transition chains (k=6 vs k=3) is not convincingly demonstrated, particularly given that many WSJ articles are relatively short. It’s plausible that longer chains introduce noise or require more data to train effectively.
4. **Verbose and Repetitive Writing:** The paper suffers from significant verbosity and repetition. The introductory material and related work are largely duplicated, particularly with Section 6 essentially repeating the content of Section 2. A more concise and streamlined presentation would improve clarity.
5. **Reproducibility Concern:** The authors report an inability to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014), indicating a potential implementation or modelling issue. While acknowledging this is a limitation is commendable, the authors should investigate this further and discuss potential reasons for the discrepancy.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of 100-dimensional embeddings for the grid entries? Did you experiment with different embedding sizes, and if so, what were the results?
2. What analysis did you perform to determine the optimal transition length *k*? Do you observe diminishing returns or negative effects with even longer transition chains?
3. Can you elaborate on the specific issues you encountered when attempting to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014)? What potential causes did you investigate?
4. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the entity-specific features used in the "Extended Grid-CNN" model and their individual contributions to performance?

**Additional Comments:**

* Replace 'Figure 2' -> 'Figure 1' (p.1 bottom left).
* Change 'Firstly/Secondly' -> 'First, Second' throughout the paper.
* Rephrase 'limits the model to' to 'prevents the model from considering...' where appropriate.
* Consider removing the unnecessary final paragraph in Section 1.
* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed model.
