This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating over the entity grid representation of a text. The core idea is to capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features using distributed representations, overcoming limitations of traditional, discrete representation-based entity grid models. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking method, aiming for task-specific feature learning. Evaluation on three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) demonstrates state-of-the-art results, outperforming existing models by a considerable margin.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the successful application of a neural architecture to the established entity grid framework, specifically addressing the limitations of discrete representations and task-decoupled feature extraction. By leveraging convolutional networks, the authors effectively model longer-range dependencies than previously possible and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion. A second, notable contribution is the strong empirical performance across three distinct coherence evaluation tasks, consistently achieving state-of-the-art results. Finally, the release of the code accompanying the submission promotes reproducibility and further research in the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Effective Neuralization of a Successful Framework:** The paper successfully integrates a neural network architecture with the well-established entity grid model. This approach leverages the strengths of both – the representational power of neural networks and the theoretically grounded foundation of the entity grid based on Centering Theory. This combination is a significant advancement over purely discrete approaches.
2. **State-of-the-Art Results:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on three diverse coherence tasks provides strong evidence for the validity and generalizability of the approach. The reported gains over existing methods are substantial and noteworthy, particularly the gains on the insertion task.
3. **End-to-End Training & Task-Specific Features:** The end-to-end training process, coupled with the ability to learn task-specific features, is a key strength. This contrasts with previous methods where feature engineering was decoupled from the learning process and often relied on manually designed features. The ability to automatically learn relevant features is a major advantage.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed model and experimental setup. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the technical details are presented in a logical and understandable manner. Figure 2 effectively illustrates the model architecture and training process.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Neural Baselines:** While the paper compares against several existing entity grid models, the comparison to other neural coherence models is limited. The mention of the Li and Hovy (2014) model is crucial, but the unexpectedly poor performance of this baseline raises significant concerns and requires more thorough investigation. The authors acknowledge the issue but the explanation offered—corpus dependence—feels insufficient without further diagnostic efforts.
2. **Performance Discrepancy with Li & Hovy:** The significantly lower performance of the Li and Hovy model compared to reported results warrants further scrutiny. The authors should document the exact experimental setup used for Li and Hovy's model, including any difficulties encountered during implementation and parameter tuning. Reproducing their original results on their datasets would provide a necessary sanity check.
3. **Dependence on Existing Entity Grid Framework:** The approach is inherently limited by the assumptions and constraints of the underlying entity grid representation. While the neural network addresses some of the limitations of the grid itself, it does not fundamentally challenge the core principles of the framework. Exploring alternative representations or architectures independent of the entity grid might lead to further improvements.
4. **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper suffers from a few instances of awkward phrasing and missing definite articles ("limits the model to do X"). A careful proofread would improve the overall clarity and polish of the manuscript.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the specific challenges you encountered when implementing and running the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Specifically, what parameter settings did you try, and what were the primary reasons for the observed performance difference?
2.  Did you explore any alternative neural architectures beyond convolutional networks for modeling entity transitions? If so, could you briefly discuss the rationale for choosing a convolutional approach?
3.  The paper mentions including only a subset of entity-specific features from Elsner and Charniak (2011). What was the rationale behind this selection, and did you experiment with including a larger set of features?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The authors should investigate the sensitivity of their model to the choice of embedding size and filter window size.
*   A discussion of the computational cost of the proposed model compared to existing methods would be beneficial.
*  The phrase “limits the model to do X” appears frequently and could be rephrased for greater clarity (e.g., “restricts the model’s ability to do X”).
