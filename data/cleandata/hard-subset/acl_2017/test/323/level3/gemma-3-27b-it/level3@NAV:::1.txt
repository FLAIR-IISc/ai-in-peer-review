## Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

This paper presents a novel approach to local coherence modeling by extending the entity grid model with a convolutional neural network (CNN). The core idea – leveraging the power of distributed representations and CNNs to capture long-range entity transitions – is promising and addresses limitations of traditional, feature-engineered entity grid models. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing methods on three standard coherence assessment tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating. The manuscript is well-written and easy to follow, showcasing a clear understanding of the existing literature. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the successful integration of a neural network with a well-established symbolic model (the entity grid). This hybrid approach demonstrates a practical way to benefit from both the interpretability of the grid representation and the generalization capabilities of neural networks. A secondary contribution lies in the end-to-end training methodology, enabling task-specific feature learning.

The strongest argument for acceptance is the consistently strong performance of the proposed model across all three evaluation tasks. The reported gains, particularly on the summary coherence rating task, are substantial and suggest a meaningful improvement in coherence assessment. The inclusion of entity-specific features further enhances the model's performance, validating the authors’ intuition.

However, the paper has some critical weaknesses. The unexpectedly poor performance of the Li and Hovy (2014) model raises serious concerns about the experimental setup. The authors mention this but don’t sufficiently investigate the discrepancy. A deeper dive is needed: what specific settings were used? Were there issues with running their code or data preprocessing? Reproducing Li and Hovy's results on their original datasets would be a crucial sanity check. The lack of detailed documentation regarding the difficulties encountered when running the Li and Hovy system is also problematic. 

Additionally, the manuscript contains numerous missing definite articles (e.g., "limits the model to do X" should be “limits the model’s capacity to do X”). While minor, this impacts readability and professionalism.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a detailed description of the settings and parameters used when running the Li and Hovy (2014) model, including preprocessing steps.
2.  Can you share the results of attempting to reproduce the Li and Hovy (2014) model on their original datasets?
3.  What specific challenges did you encounter when implementing and running the Li and Hovy (2014) system, and how did you attempt to address them?
