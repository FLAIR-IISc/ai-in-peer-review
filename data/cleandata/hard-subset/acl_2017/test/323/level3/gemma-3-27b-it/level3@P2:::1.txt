## Review for ACL 2017 - A Neural Local Coherence Model

This paper presents a neural local coherence model based on a convolutional neural network operating over the entity grid representation of text. The core contribution lies in replacing the discrete representation and hand-engineered features of traditional entity grid models with learned, distributed representations, enabling the capture of longer-range entity transitions and task-specific features through end-to-end training. A second, related contribution is the demonstration of state-of-the-art results on three standard coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). Finally, the release of code is a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Effective Neuralization of a Strong Baseline:** The authors successfully integrate a neural network architecture with the well-established entity grid framework. This approach builds upon a solid foundation and demonstrates the benefits of leveraging distributed representations and convolutional layers for modeling local coherence. The performance gains over traditional entity grid models – particularly the 4% absolute improvement on discrimination and 2.5% on insertion – are substantial and indicate a meaningful advancement.

2. **Sound Methodology and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper employs a rigorous evaluation methodology, utilizing three established coherence assessment tasks. The pairwise ranking approach for training is well-motivated, and the inclusion of both automatic and human-evaluated tasks (summary coherence rating) strengthens the validity of the findings. The comparison against several baselines, including a graph-based model and variations of the entity grid, provides a clear context for the improvements achieved by the proposed model.

3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, the proposed architecture is explained in detail (with a helpful diagram), and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The discussion of limitations and future work is also commendable.

4. **Practical Contribution via Code Release:**  Making the code publicly available is a valuable contribution to the research community, fostering reproducibility and facilitating further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Fully Neural Approaches:** While the paper acknowledges related work in neural coherence modeling (specifically, Li & Hovy, 2014), the comparison is surprisingly limited. The authors report unexpectedly poor performance for the Li & Hovy model, but the explanation offered is somewhat vague. The potential influence of corpus dependence and differences in experimental setup are mentioned, but further investigation is warranted.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Li & Hovy Results:** The unusually low performance of the Li & Hovy model raises significant concerns. The paper would benefit from a more detailed account of the challenges encountered when attempting to reproduce their results – including specific hyperparameter settings, implementation details, and potential reasons for the discrepancy. A sanity check by reproducing their reported results on the original datasets would be highly valuable. Without this, it's difficult to assess whether the reported improvement is truly substantial, or simply a consequence of differing experimental conditions.

3. **Minor Presentation Issues:** The manuscript contains several instances of missing definite articles (“limits the model to do X” instead of “limits the model’s capacity to do X”), which detract from the overall polish of the paper. While minor, addressing these issues would improve readability and professionalism.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific difficulties encountered when implementing and running the Li & Hovy (2014) model? Specifically, what hyperparameter settings were explored, and how did they compare to those reported in their original paper?

2.  Did you observe similar performance discrepancies for the Li & Hovy model on other coherence datasets?

3.  Could you provide more detail on the entity-specific features used in the "Extended Grid-CNN" model? What is the exact representation of these features, and how are they incorporated into the network?

**Additional Comments:**

* Consider rephrasing the recurring phrase "limits the model to do X" for greater clarity.
* Investigating the impact of different convolutional filter sizes and pooling strategies could provide further insights into the model's behavior.
