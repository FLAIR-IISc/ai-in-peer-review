This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on entity grid representations of text. The authors aim to capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features, addressing limitations of traditional entity grid models which rely on discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation. The core contribution lies in adapting the well-established entity grid framework to a neural architecture and training it end-to-end on coherence-related tasks. The model is evaluated on sentence discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance across all three tasks. The authors also release their code, promoting reproducibility.

The strengths of this submission are significant. Firstly, the empirical results are compelling. Consistently achieving state-of-the-art results on three different coherence assessment tasks, with improvements ranging from 4% to 2.5% depending on the task, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed neural architecture. The gains over existing, well-established entity grid models are substantial and provide strong evidence supporting the approach. Secondly, the motivation for the work is clear and well-articulated. The authors effectively identify limitations of previous entity grid models, such as the curse of dimensionality and task-agnostic feature computation, and position their work as a direct response to these challenges. Thirdly, the design intuitions behind the model are well-explained. The rationale for using a CNN, particularly the explanation of how it can capture long-range dependencies and generalize through distributed representations ("Why it works"), is convincing. Finally, the release of the code is a commendable practice that facilitates reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The primary weakness is a lack of substantial novelty beyond a straightforward neuralization of the existing entity grid approach. While the application of CNNs is effective, it doesn't introduce significant representational or architectural innovations. The paper largely consolidates prior work into a neural framework. Secondly, the choice of embedding dimension (100 for the basic grid, slightly higher for the extended grid) is not adequately justified. Mapping four-dimensional grid entries into 100-dimensional vectors seems excessive and raises questions about the efficiency of the representation. A more thorough ablation study or justification for this dimensionality would be beneficial. Thirdly, the authors report an inability to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014). This is concerning as it raises questions about the implementation details or potential modelling issues within the proposed approach, and warrants further investigation.

Furthermore, the writing is often verbose and repetitive. There’s substantial overlap between the introduction, related work (Section 6), and the description of entity grid limitations (Section 2.1). The paper could benefit significantly from reorganization and condensation. The length could be reduced without losing critical information. Finally, while minor, there are several language and proofreading issues throughout the paper, including missing articles and awkward phrasing. 

Here are some questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the embedding dimension (e.g., 100)? Did you explore different dimensionality settings and analyze their impact on performance?
2.  The paper mentions the potential for longer transition chains (k). However, given the typical length of the WSJ articles used for evaluation, is the benefit of using k=6 or k=8 substantial? Could you provide an analysis of the optimal transition length based on the dataset characteristics?
3. What specific aspects of the Li and Hovy (2014) model were challenging to reproduce? Were any attempts made to directly compare the internal representations or gradients of the two models to diagnose the source of the discrepancy?
4. Could you elaborate on the potential benefits of incorporating rhetorical relations, as mentioned in the conclusion, and outline any preliminary steps towards this integration?
