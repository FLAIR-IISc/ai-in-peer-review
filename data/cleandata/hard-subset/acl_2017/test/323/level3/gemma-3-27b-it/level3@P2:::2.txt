This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on the entity grid representation of text. The authors aim to capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features in a generalized manner, coupled with end-to-end task-specific training. The model is evaluated on three coherence assessment tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – demonstrating state-of-the-art results.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of a convolutional neural network to the entity grid representation for coherence modeling. While the entity grid itself is a well-established technique, framing it within a neural architecture and leveraging convolutional layers to learn feature representations directly from the grid demonstrates a valuable adaptation and improvement. This allows for the modeling of longer-range transitions than previously feasible and enables end-to-end task-specific training, both of which are significant advantages. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation across three diverse coherence tasks, firmly establishing the effectiveness of the proposed approach and providing a strong empirical baseline for future work. Finally, the release of the code is a positive step towards reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing methods on all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) by a considerable margin (up to 4% absolute gain). This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach and establishes a new state-of-the-art. The extensive evaluation across multiple tasks strengthens the validity of these findings.
2. **Effective Neuralization of Entity Grid:** The application of a convolutional neural network to the entity grid is a well-motivated and effective approach. The use of distributed representations and convolutional layers allows the model to learn complex relationships between entity transitions and to generalize better than traditional feature-based methods. The explanation of *why* this approach works ("Why it works" section) is particularly strong and clarifies the design choices.
3. **End-to-End Training:** The end-to-end training method allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically, eliminating the need for hand-engineered features. This simplifies the development process and potentially leads to better performance, as the model can optimize features directly for the target task.
4. **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models (discrete representations, task-agnostic feature extraction) and provides a compelling motivation for the proposed neural approach.
5. **Code Release:** The authors’ decision to release their code promotes reproducibility and encourages further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Innovation:** While the results are strong, the core innovation—applying a CNN to the entity grid—feels somewhat incremental. The paper largely "neuralizes" an existing approach rather than presenting fundamentally new representational or architectural ideas. This limits the overall impact of the work.
2. **Embedding Dimensionality Justification:** The choice of 100-dimensional embeddings for representing four-dimensional grid entries (and slightly higher for the extended grid) lacks clear justification. The paper does not explore the impact of different embedding sizes or provide a rationale for the selected dimensionality. It’s unclear whether a smaller embedding size could achieve comparable performance with reduced computational cost.
3. **Limited Benefit of Longer Transitions:** The authors claim the ability to model longer transitions (k=6 vs k=3) is beneficial, but the impact of this is questionable given the relatively short length of many WSJ articles. An ablation study specifically investigating the effect of transition length would strengthen this claim.
4. **Reproducibility Concern:** The authors acknowledge their inability to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014), indicating a potential issue with their implementation or modeling assumptions. This raises concerns about the reliability of the reported results and necessitates further investigation.
5. **Verbose and Repetitive Writing:** The paper suffers from verbosity and repetition, particularly in the Introduction and Related Work sections. The material in Section 6 largely duplicates information presented in Section 2, and the writing style could be more concise and focused.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the chosen embedding dimensionality? Have you explored the impact of different embedding sizes on performance?
2.  Did you investigate the impact of the transition length (k) on performance? If so, could you present these results?
3.  Could you elaborate on the discrepancies encountered when attempting to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014)? What specific aspects of their approach were difficult to replicate, and what steps have you taken to resolve these issues?
4.  Can you discuss the computational cost associated with the proposed model, particularly compared to traditional entity grid approaches?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Replace "Figure 2" with "Figure 1" on page 1, bottom left.
*   Consider revising "Firstly/Secondly" to "First, Second" for improved readability.
*   Rephrase "limits the model to" to "prevents the model from considering…" for a more accurate and nuanced description.
*   The final paragraph of Section 1 feels redundant and could be removed.



Overall, this paper presents a solid and well-executed contribution to the field of coherence modeling. While the innovation is incremental, the strong empirical results and clear presentation make it a valuable addition to the literature. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the justification for embedding dimensionality and the reproducibility concern, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
