- Proposes a CNN over the entity-grid with embedded grid cells and max-pooling, trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss to score document coherence.

- Contribution: neuralized entity-grid using CNNs to capture longer k-length entity transitions and include entity-specific features without combinatorial blow-up.

- Contribution: end-to-end pairwise ranking training that yields consistent state-of-the-art performance across discrimination, insertion, and summary-coherence tasks.

- Contribution: shows a relatively simple neural architecture with fewer handcrafted features can outperform stronger, feature-rich grid baselines.

- Strength: clear motivation and architecture; CNNs are an appropriate inductive bias for location-invariant, k-length transition patterns and distributed representations reduce sparsity.

- Strength: strong, consistent empirical gains (≈+4% discrimination, up to +2.5% insertion, >3–4% summary rating) with significance testing and benefit from WSJ pretraining.

- Strength: competitive performance using only three entity features versus baselines using nine, highlighting benefits of representation learning.

- Strength: sensible training/evaluation setup (pairwise ranking, WSJ pretraining, hyperparameter tuning, dropout, early stopping, multiple seeds, significance testing).

- Strength: practical relevance, simple implementation over standard entity grids, compatible with ranking use cases, and code release stated.

- Weakness: limited novelty—mainly a straightforward “neuralization” of entity grids that ignores cross-entity interactions and reuses established tasks/features.

- Weakness: insufficient analysis and ablations (embedding size, transition window k, pooling length, number of filters, dropout) to support design claims.

- Weakness: reproducibility/comparison concerns—Li & Hovy (2014) could not be reproduced and was excluded from main results, leaving a gap in comparisons.

- Weakness: modeling assumptions may be limiting—treats entity columns independently and lacks discussion of coreference/entity-extraction error impact.

- Weakness: writing and organization issues—verbose, repetitive background/related work and editorial/language problems that reduce clarity.

- Question: provide ablations for transition window k (e.g., 2–8), embedding dimension, number of filters/pooling size, and dropout—how sensitive are results?

- Question: impact of pretraining vs. training from scratch on summaries, and whether embeddings/filters are frozen during fine-tuning?

- Question: what entity extraction/coreference pipeline was used (head nouns vs. all nouns vs. full coreference), and how robust are results to coreference errors?

- Question: have you tried modeling cross-entity interactions (2D convs across columns, self-attention), and what were the results?

- Question: can you disentangle the effect of the pairwise ranking loss from the CNN architecture (e.g., SVM-ranker over CNN features or a linear model with same loss)?

- Question: regarding Li & Hovy (2014), which code version/settings and dataset splits were used and can you share diagnostics for the performance gap?

- Comment: reorganize and condense background vs. related work to avoid duplication and move detailed surveys later.

- Comment: provide more learning/compute details (training time, hardware, parameter counts) and clarify size of role+feature vocabulary and embedding table.

- Comment: explicitly report transition window k used in final models and include length-bucket performance (short vs. long documents).

- Comment: fix language/formatting issues (wording suggestions, figure cross-references, proofreading) and consider removing repetitive final paragraph of Section 1.

- Comment: add stronger analysis (qualitative filter visualizations, error analysis on insertion, examples where longer k helps) to substantiate modeling claims.

- Comment: report Li & Hovy (2014) results even if low, with caveats and diagnostics, to provide transparent comparison.
