Summary
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) over the entity grid representation. By embedding grid symbols (roles and optionally entity-specific features) and learning convolutional filters over entity-transition sequences, the model aims to capture longer-range transitions and mitigate sparsity inherent in discrete grid features. Training uses pairwise ranking to learn task-specific features end-to-end. The model is evaluated on three coherence assessment tasks (WSJ discrimination and insertion, and DUC’03 summary coherence rating) and achieves consistent improvements over strong entity-grid baselines. Code is released.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: An effective neuralization of the entity grid that learns distributed representations of entity transitions with convolution and pairwise ranking, yielding consistent SOTA gains on three standard coherence tasks.
- Secondary: A simple, extensible mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features into the neural grid without the combinatorial explosion of feature engineering.
- Tertiary: A thorough empirical evaluation with significance testing and released code, making the approach reproducible and useful for downstream research.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance across tasks. The model outperforms both basic and extended grid models by notable margins (≈4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, >3–4% on summary rating with pretraining), with statistical significance demonstrated. Results are consistent across five random seeds and include careful dev-tuning.
- Sound, well-motivated architecture. Convolution over embedded transition sequences is a natural fit to location-invariant local patterns, enabling longer transition windows (e.g., 5–8) without the curse of dimensionality faced by discrete grids. Pairwise ranking aligns training with the task objective.
- Practical extensibility. The method cleanly integrates entity-specific features (e.g., NE type, salience, proper mention) by composing them with role symbols; this replaces brittle feature conjunctions with learned distributed representations.
- Good experimental hygiene. The paper uses a large, appropriate corpus (WSJ), provides clear training details (optimizer, early stopping, dropout, hyperparameter sweeps), and conducts significance testing. Releasing code further supports reproducibility and adoption.
- Clear writing and positioning. The motivation and relation to prior coherence models are well laid out, with honest discussion of limitations of discrete grid models and how the proposed approach addresses them.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete/nebulous comparison to Li and Hovy (2014). The reported replication of their neural sentence-based coherence model yields surprisingly low discrimination accuracy (~19%). This raises concerns about setup mismatches (data domain, preprocessing, hyperparameters, negatives, sentence encoders) rather than a definitive negative result. As is, it is hard to draw fair conclusions about the relative merits of grid-CNN vs. sentence-based neural coherence.
- Limited cross-domain validation. Results focus on WSJ (and DUC’03 for summaries). The paper argues AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES are small and stylistically constrained, but these corpora are widely used; reproducing prior results there (even if smaller) would strengthen claims of generality and help interpret the Li & Hovy discrepancy.
- Training-task mismatch for insertion. The model is trained only on discrimination pairs. Insertion is substantially harder and structurally different; a task-aligned training setup (e.g., pairwise/ranking examples tailored to single-sentence reinsertions) might yield further gains. An ablation or training-on-insertion experiment would clarify headroom.
- Limited ablations/analysis. While hyperparameters are reported, additional analyses would be valuable: impact of transition window length, pooling size, and number of filters; contribution of each entity-specific feature; sensitivity to coreference errors; and qualitative inspection of learned filters/patterns.
- Clarity/style issues. There are numerous missing definite articles and recurring phrasing such as “limits the model to do X,” which reduces readability. Proofreading and minor rephrasing will improve clarity.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Li & Hovy replication: Please detail the exact code version, data splits, preprocessing (tokenization, sentence segmentation), embeddings, sentence encoder type/size, negative sampling or window labeling, training epochs, and hyperparameters used. Did you reproduce their reported numbers on their original datasets as a sanity check? If not, can you run that to verify your setup?
- Cross-domain tests: Can you include results on AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES (even if only for a subset) and/or on a subset of Li & Hovy’s original data to assess robustness and help diagnose the replication gap?
- Insertion training: What happens if you fine-tune the model with training pairs specifically constructed for insertion (e.g., correct vs. incorrect insertion positions)? Any measurable gains?
- Error analysis and robustness: How sensitive is performance to coreference quality? Do errors cluster around particular entity types or discourse configurations? Any examples of filters capturing interpretable transition motifs?
- Model size and efficiency: Please report parameter counts, training time, and inference speed compared to extended grid baselines.

Additional comments
- Proofreading: Fix missing definite articles throughout the paper. Rephrase instances of “limits the model to do X” to “limits the model’s capacity to do X” or “prevents the model from doing X.”
- Citations/formatting: Clean up reference entries (years and venues are inconsistent; some entries have placeholders). Ensure consistent naming (e.g., “wide convolution”) and clarify zero-padding details.
- Minor suggestions: Add ablations on transition window length and feature contributions; include a short qualitative visualization of top-activating filters/patterns; discuss applicability to tasks like sentence ordering for generation systems.

Overall recommendation
4 — Weak Accept

Post-rebuttal note
Given the authors’ clarification that there were objective difficulties running others’ code, I do not penalize them for the Li & Hovy replication issue; however, documenting the replication attempts and, if possible, adding sanity-check experiments would further strengthen the paper.
