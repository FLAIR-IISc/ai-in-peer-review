Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a CNN to entity grids. Each grid cell (grammatical role possibly combined with entity-specific features) is embedded; convolution and max-pooling over entity transition columns produce features used in a pairwise ranking objective to score coherence. The model is evaluated on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A neuralized entity-grid model: end-to-end learning with embeddings and CNN filters over entity transitions, trained with pairwise ranking.
2) Empirical gains and SOTA results across three benchmarks (WSJ discrimination/insertion and DUC’03 summary rating), with significance testing and sensible training protocol.
3) A simple mechanism to incorporate a subset of entity-specific features without a combinatorial feature explosion, reducing feature engineering compared to prior extended grids.

Strengths
- Solid empirical improvements with careful evaluation: The model outperforms strong grid baselines by ~4% absolute on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and 3–4% on summary rating, with significance tests and multiple runs to mitigate randomness. Pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning for summary rating is a reasonable and effective strategy in the low-data setting.
- Clear modeling intuition and design: The rationale for CNNs (location-invariant detection of transition patterns, longer receptive fields, shared parameters across columns) is articulated and aligns well with the entity-grid structure. The “Why it works” explanation improves readability.
- End-to-end, task-aligned training: Using a ranking objective directly on the target task addresses a known limitation of earlier grid models where features were task-agnostic. The approach readily accommodates longer transition windows without combinatorial feature growth.
- Practicality and reproducibility: Implementation details, hyperparameter search ranges, training regimen, significance testing, and code release are reported. The model operates with commonly available preprocessing (coreference, POS/NE), which increases usability.

Weaknesses
- Limited conceptual novelty: The core idea is primarily a straightforward neuralization of the entity grid with standard components (embeddings, convolution, max-pooling, margin ranking). While useful, the representational innovation is modest for a full conference paper, and the method does not address broader discourse phenomena beyond entity transitions.
- Insufficient analysis/justification of design choices: The use of 100-dimensional embeddings for a very small role vocabulary (and modest growth with added features) is not justified; a parameter-efficiency analysis or sensitivity study is missing. Similarly, the benefit of longer transition windows (e.g., k=6–8) relative to classic k≤3 is asserted but not rigorously ablated; given the relatively short WSJ articles, the gains may stem from other factors (e.g., training regime).
- Reproducibility gap w.r.t. Li & Hovy (2014): The inability to reproduce that baseline (reporting 19.34% accuracy) is noted but not investigated. Without diagnosis (data processing, windowing, training settings, or tokenization/pretraining differences), the comparison may be unfair and leaves uncertainty about the competitive landscape with sentence-representation-based coherence models.
- Limited scope of evaluation and ablations: While WSJ is a standard benchmark, broader domains or robustness tests (e.g., AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES, other news domains) would strengthen generality claims. The insertion task shows smaller gains and the model is not trained specifically for it; a task-aligned training experiment would be instructive. Ablations isolating the contribution of each entity-specific feature and each architectural component (embedding size, filter width, pooling size) are missing.
- Writing and organization issues: The paper is verbose, with repetition between Sections 2 and 6. Some phrasing is awkward, and there are minor editorial errors (figure reference, use of “Firstly/Secondly,” etc.), which detract from clarity.

Questions to authors
- Embedding dimension: How sensitive is performance to the embedding size for the role inventory and for the extended feature set? Can smaller embeddings (e.g., 10–50 dims) achieve similar results?
- Transition window size k: Please provide an ablation showing accuracy vs. k (and vs. pooling size), including k=2,3,4,5,6,8, to substantiate the claim about modeling longer transitions.
- Feature contributions: What are the incremental gains from each entity-specific feature (NE type, salience, properness)? Any interactions with k?
- Insertion task training: If trained with a pairwise objective tailored to insertion (i.e., differing by one sentence placement), does the model improve beyond the current 1–2.5% gains?
- Coreference robustness: How sensitive is performance to coreference quality? Have you tried simpler mentions-as-strings heuristics or oracle coreference to bound the impact?
- Pretraining and domain transfer: For DUC’03, how much of the improvement comes from WSJ pretraining vs. fine-tuning? Did you observe negative transfer in any cluster?
