Summary
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) to entity grid representations. Grid entries (grammatical roles and optional entity-specific features) are embedded, convolved over sentence-level transition windows, max-pooled, and combined into a document-level coherence score trained with a pairwise ranking objective. The method is evaluated on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating, reporting state-of-the-art performance and statistically significant gains over non-neural entity-grid baselines. The paper motivates the CNN choice and provides an intuitive “Why it works” rationale.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A neuralized entity-grid architecture with pairwise ranking that achieves strong, state-of-the-art results across three standard coherence tasks.
2) Clear, task-driven motivation for using CNNs over grid columns to capture longer transitions while maintaining generalization via distributed representations.
3) An empirical consolidation of coherence evaluation on larger WSJ data and DUC’03, with public code and significance testing.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across multiple tasks: The model consistently outperforms graph-based and classic/extended entity-grid baselines on discrimination, insertion, and summary rating, with reported significant improvements. Using a single framework across tasks increases the result’s credibility.
- Sensible model design and training: Applying 1-D convolutions over entity-transition columns aligns with the locality and position-invariance assumptions of grid-based coherence. Pairwise ranking directly optimizes the desired ordering objective and is a good fit for discrimination/rating tasks.
- Effective use of distributed representations: The approach addresses sparsity of R^k transition counts by embedding grid symbols and learning filters across documents, enabling longer transitions than traditional k ≤ 3 settings while avoiding combinatorial explosion when adding entity features.
- Clear intuition and design discussion: The “Why it works” section and the step-by-step architectural description articulate how filters detect salient transition patterns and why pooling is appropriate.
- Reproducibility-oriented evaluation: Larger WSJ data, significance testing, and released code help reproducibility and fair comparisons with the most relevant baselines (including extended grids).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond neuralization: The core contribution is a straightforward neural reimplementation of the entity grid with CNNs. There is little representational or task innovation beyond combining known components (embeddings, convolution, ranking) and consolidating prior evaluation settings. For a full paper, stronger conceptual advances (e.g., new coherence signals, richer discourse structures, or new tasks) would be desirable.
- Questionable hyperparameter justification: Representing four categorical roles (and modestly extended grids) with 100-dimensional embeddings (and similar sizes for features) lacks justification. An ablation/sensitivity analysis on embedding dimensionality is needed, especially given the small symbol inventory and potential overparameterization.
- Unclear benefit of long chains on short documents: While longer transition windows (e.g., k=6) are a selling point, many WSJ articles are relatively short. The paper does not isolate how much of the gain comes from larger k vs. other factors, nor does it analyze performance as a function of document length or k. This raises questions about cost-benefit and generality.
- Inability to reproduce Li & Hovy (2014): The reported 19.34% discrimination accuracy using the authors’ code suggests a possible implementation or modeling mismatch. This undermines the comparative context and should be investigated more thoroughly (dataset splits, preprocessing, negative sampling, training regime).
- Writing and organization issues: The paper is verbose, with duplicated content (e.g., section 6 substantially repeats section 2). The introduction and related work should be tightened and reorganized; there are multiple minor language issues (articles, phrasing) that impede readability.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Embedding and filter sizes: Why 100-d embeddings for 4 roles (and slightly more for extended grids)? Please provide sensitivity analyses for embedding size, number of filters, and pooling length on DEV/TEST.
- Transition window length: How does accuracy vary with k (e.g., 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8)? Can you report results stratified by document length to justify long windows?
- Training objective vs. insertion: Did you try optimizing the pairwise loss on insertion-specific pairs (documents differing by a single sentence move)? If so, how does it compare to training on discrimination and testing on insertion?
- Li & Hovy (2014) replication: What exact preprocessing, sentence segmentation, and negative sampling were used? Did you verify the code’s settings match the published best configuration and dataset (including OOV handling and early stopping)?
- Alternative architectures: Did you compare CNNs to RNNs or attention over grid columns, or try multi-k filter banks with shared heads? Any evidence that CNNs are superior for this representation?

Additional comments and editorial suggestions
- Condense and reorganize: Remove repeated introductory/related work material (section 6 vs. section 2). Tighten the introduction and move some background to related work.
- Language/phrasing: There are missing articles and awkward phrasings throughout; a careful proofreading pass is needed.
- Specific fixes:
  - p.1 bottom left: replace “Figure 2” with “Figure 1”.
  - Replace “Firstly/Secondly” with “First,” “Second,” throughout.
  - Rephrase “limits the model to” as “prevents the model from considering …”.
  - Consider removing the final paragraph in section 1, which is unnecessary given later sections.
- Broaden contribution: To strengthen the paper, consider adding novel features (e.g., explicit discourse relations, rhetorical structures with learnable fusion, cross-entity interactions) or new tasks/datasets, and provide deeper analysis (ablation on k, embeddings, entity features, salience; error analysis; runtime/memory vs. k).

Overall assessment
A well-motivated, competently executed neuralization of the entity grid with solid empirical gains and clear intuitions. However, limited conceptual novelty and missing analyses (especially around embedding size and transition length) weaken the contribution. With added innovations or stronger analysis and cleaner writing, this would be a clear accept; as is, it sits on the borderline between consolidation and substantive advance.
