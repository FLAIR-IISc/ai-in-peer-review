Summary
This paper proposes a convolutional neural network operating over the entity grid to model local discourse coherence, trained with a pairwise ranking objective. The model “neuralizes” grid entries via embeddings, applies convolution over entity-transition windows, and uses max pooling to produce a document-level coherence score. Experiments on WSJ discrimination/insertion and DUC’03 summary ranking show consistent improvements over non-neural entity-grid baselines.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Strong empirical evaluation demonstrating consistent gains over competitive entity-grid baselines across multiple coherence tasks, with significance testing and multiple runs.
2) A clear architectural instantiation and motivation for applying CNNs to entity-transition patterns, coupled with an end-to-end pairwise ranking objective aligned with coherence scoring.
3) A practical consolidation of entity-grid features into a learnable framework (including some entity-specific features), with code release to facilitate adoption.

Strengths
- Empirical performance: The model outperforms extended entity-grid baselines on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, with reported absolute gains (e.g., ~4% discrimination, up to 2.5% insertion) and significance tests.
- Sensible training objective: Direct optimization for pairwise coherence ranking is well aligned with evaluation and improves over task-agnostic feature extraction.
- Clear design intuition: The “Why it works” section convincingly explains why CNN filters can capture salient transition patterns and why pooling confers location invariance.
- Robustness and generalization: Pretraining on WSJ transfers to DUC’03 summary ranking; multiple random seeds and dev-based early stopping mitigate variance.
- Transparency: The paper candidly reports difficulty reproducing Li & Hovy (2014), avoiding questionable comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The contribution largely neuralizes an established representation without introducing substantial representational or task innovations expected for a full-length paper.
- Hyperparameter justification: The choice of 100-d embeddings for a 4-symbol grid (and slightly more for the extended grid) lacks justification; no sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Transition length: Benefits of longer chains (e.g., k=6) are not convincingly demonstrated, especially given many WSJ articles are short; ablations for k are needed.
- Writing/structure: The paper is verbose and repetitive (notably Section 6 repeats Section 2); organization should be tightened.
- Reproducibility/context: Failure to reproduce Li & Hovy (2014) needs deeper analysis; excluding the result weakens comparative context.

Questions to Authors (Optional)
- Provide ablations for embedding size, k, number of filters, pooling length, and dropout. What is the best-performing minimal configuration?
- How does performance vary with document length and entity density? Do long k filters help mainly on longer articles?
- What is the incremental gain from each entity-specific feature in the Extended Grid-CNN?
- Can you diagnose the Li & Hovy (2014) reproduction failure (preprocessing, training regime, or data mismatch)?
