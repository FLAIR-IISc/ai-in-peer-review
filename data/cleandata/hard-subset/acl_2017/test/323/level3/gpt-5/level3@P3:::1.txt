Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) over the entity grid. This allows the model to learn distributed representations of entity-transition sequences and to generalize to longer transitions than traditional, discrete grid features.
- The model is trained end-to-end via pairwise ranking, enabling task-specific feature learning and improving over traditional task-agnostic feature extraction.
- Comprehensive experiments on WSJ discrimination and insertion, and DUC’03 summary coherence rating, show consistent gains over strong entity-grid baselines, with state-of-the-art performance reported.

Main contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A CNN-over-grid architecture that captures longer, entity-conditioned transition patterns while retaining generalization via distributed embeddings.
2) An end-to-end ranking setup that ties feature induction to the target coherence tasks.
3) Empirical evidence of gains over grid baselines across multiple coherence evaluations.

Strengths
- Methodological advance over the entity grid: By embedding grid symbols and convolving over columns, the model overcomes sparsity in long k-length transitions and leverages shared parameters across documents. The use of wide convolution and max-pooling to detect salient transition patterns is a good fit to the grid structure.
- Strong empirical results: On WSJ discrimination and insertion, both Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN outperform their non-neural counterparts, with statistically significant improvements. On DUC’03, pretraining on WSJ and finetuning further boosts performance, indicating robustness and transferability.
- Sensible hybrid design: Building on the proven entity-grid representation focuses learning on entity continuity—central to local coherence—rather than requiring fully neural discourse modeling from scratch. The approach cleanly incorporates entity-specific features.
- Clear presentation and fluent writing: The paper is generally easy to follow, with a well-structured exposition of the architecture, training, and evaluation. Hyperparameter choices and training details are reported, and code availability is noted, supporting reproducibility.
- Strong positioning within coherence literature: The paper situates itself well relative to grid extensions, graph-based models, and discourse-based alternatives, and explains the architectural choices (e.g., location-invariant convolution over columns).

Weaknesses
- Limited and troubled comparison to Li and Hovy (2014): The reported 19.34% discrimination accuracy for Li & Hovy on WSJ is far below expectations and prior reports. This raises red flags about either the experimental setup, preprocessing mismatches (e.g., sentence segmentation, tokenization, vocabulary, training objective), or corpus dependence. Excluding the model from the results table leaves a gap in comparative evaluation against fully neural coherence approaches.
- Potential corpus dependence: The evaluation emphasizes WSJ/PTB, where entity grids and coreference work well. Without testing on AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES (used by Barzilay & Lapata and Li & Hovy), it’s hard to assess generality or to reconcile why Li & Hovy performs so poorly here.
- Assumptions and ablations: The independence assumption across entity columns and the choice of convolutional window sizes are plausible but under-analyzed. More ablations (e.g., varying k, pooling, feature subsets, salience binning strategies, coreference quality sensitivity) would clarify what drives gains.
- Task mismatch for insertion: The model is trained on discrimination pairs but evaluated on insertion, which may limit gains on insertion. A training regimen directly targeting insertion preferences could be informative.
- Writing issues: There are numerous missing definite articles throughout. Also, the recurring expression “limits the model to do X” is awkward and should be rephrased to “limits the model’s capacity to do X” or “stops the model from doing X.”

Questions to authors
- Li & Hovy reproduction: What exact preprocessing, training hyperparameters, and datasets were used when running their code? Which variants (RNN vs. recursive) and window sizes were tried? Did you attempt to replicate their reported numbers on their original datasets (e.g., AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES) as a sanity check?
- Corpus sensitivity: Can you evaluate your model and Li & Hovy on the small, style-constrained AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES corpora to see whether the relative ranking reverses? If Li & Hovy succeeds there, what differs (document length, style, vocabulary, coreference quality)?
- Coreference robustness: How sensitive is performance to coreference errors? Have you tried different coreference systems or oracle mentions to bound potential gains?
- Architectural ablations: What is the impact of window size k, pooling length, and number of filters on each task? How does performance change if you drop salience or named-entity features from Extended Grid-CNN?
- Insertion-oriented training: Does directly training the pairwise ranker on insertion-derived pairs improve insertion accuracy?

Additional comments (writing and presentation)
- Please proofread for article usage; many definite articles are missing.
- Replace “limits the model to do X” with clearer alternatives: “limits the model’s capacity to do X” or “stops the model from doing X.”
- Document the concrete efforts and difficulties when running the Li & Hovy system (environment, versions, preprocessing, parameter sweeps), so readers can assess comparability.

Recommendation
- Final recommendation: 4 (after author response). The method is solid and results are strong. While the weak Li & Hovy baseline is concerning, objective difficulties running others’ code should not penalize the submission; however, please add a deeper investigation (including reproducing Li & Hovy on their original datasets) in the camera-ready to strengthen the comparative claims.
