Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a convolutional neural network over entity grids. The CNN composes distributed representations of entity-role sequences, enabling longer transition modeling and end-to-end, task-specific learning via pairwise ranking. The model is evaluated on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, achieving state-of-the-art performance.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A CNN architecture over entity grids that learns long-range entity transitions and integrates entity-specific features while avoiding the sparsity/curse of dimensionality inherent in discrete grids.
2) An end-to-end pairwise ranking training setup that learns task-specific features and delivers consistent, sizable improvements over strong grid baselines across three standard tasks.
3) Empirical demonstration that the neuralized grid with a small set of lightweight entity features matches or exceeds the extended grid that relies on a larger hand-engineered feature set, and that pretraining on WSJ benefits summary rating.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains over competitive baselines on three benchmarks. The model improves discrimination by ~4% absolute versus the basic grid and ~3–4% over extended grid, and improves insertion by up to 2.5%. On summary coherence rating, pretraining yields a further 2–4% improvement. These are non-trivial margins on widely-used datasets and settings, with significance testing.
- Sound modeling addressing known limitations of entity grids. Mapping roles/features into embeddings and applying convolutions directly tackles the exponential blow-up in R^k transitions and enables modeling longer patterns (k up to 5–8) without overfitting. Pairwise ranking aligns the learning objective with downstream ranking tasks.
- Practical, modular approach that is easy to extend. Operating over the entity grid preserves an interpretable abstraction and makes it straightforward to attach entity-specific features, while the convolutional filters offer a clear intuition (pattern detectors over transitions). The method should plug in seamlessly wherever grids are already used.
- Careful experimental setup and transparency. Hyperparameters are tuned on dev, early stopping and dropout are used, and multiple random seeds are averaged with significance testing. The paper compares against several strong, publicly available baselines and releases code, which aids reproducibility.
- Clear exposition and motivation. The paper is well-structured, with a convincing intuition about how filters capture salient transition patterns and why max pooling aggregates them effectively.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and potentially unfair comparison to Li and Hovy (2014). The paper reports unexpectedly poor performance (e.g., ~19% discrimination) when re-running their model, but does not analyze why or attempt to replicate their reported results on the original datasets/settings. Given that Li & Hovy is a principal neural baseline, the lack of a robust head-to-head comparison weakens claims about the superiority of the proposed neuralization strategy.
- Limited exploration of generalization beyond WSJ and DUC’03. The rationale for preferring WSJ is reasonable, but the model’s dependence on high-quality coreference and domain-specific discourse patterns raises the question of portability. Results on corpora with different styles and coref quality (e.g., AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES, or more recent diverse datasets) would better substantiate robustness.
- Analysis could be deeper. There is little ablation on transition window size, number of filters, pooling width, or on the contribution of each entity feature. An insertion-specific training variant is suggested but not attempted; this leaves open whether the smaller gains on insertion reflect a limitation of training or of the architecture.
- Dependence on coreference quality is not quantified. Since the model’s inputs are entity grids, errors from coreference and syntactic parsing propagate directly. An analysis of performance versus coref accuracy or using different coref systems would help understand failure modes and practical deployment concerns.
- Minor writing issues. There are numerous missing definite articles and repeated phrasings such as “limits the model to do X,” which reduce readability.

Questions to authors
- Li & Hovy replication: Which exact code version, preprocessing, word embeddings, and hyperparameters were used? Did you try their recommended settings on their original corpora to verify sanity? Can you report results when training and evaluating on the same datasets they used?
- Corpus dependence: How does the model perform on AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES and other short-document corpora? Are the gains preserved when documents have fewer sentences and noisier coreference?
- Analysis: What is the effect of transition window size k and pooling size p on each task? How many filters are actually activated at test time (evidence for pattern specialization)?
- Insertion training: If trained directly on insertion pairs (rather than discrimination), does the model’s insertion score improve? Does a multi-task setup help?
- Coreference robustness: How sensitive are results to the underlying coreference resolver? Have you tried alternate resolvers or oracle coreference?
- Runtime and resources: What are training and inference times relative to extended grid models, and how do they scale with document length and number of entities?

Additional comments
- Please expand the comparison to Li & Hovy with a deeper investigation; at minimum, reproduce their reported results on their datasets as a sanity check, document the hurdles you faced, and discuss the likely causes of divergence (domain, preprocessing, optimization).
- Consider adding ablations on entity-specific features and transition window length, and an insertion-trained variant to test the hypothesis that task-specific training matters.
- Proofreading: fix missing definite articles throughout; and rephrase “limits the model to do X” to “limits the model’s capacity to do X” or “prevents the model from doing X.”
- The hybrid nature of the contribution (neuralizing an existing abstraction rather than proposing an end-to-end discourse model) makes novelty moderate, but the empirical and practical impact are convincing.

Overall recommendation
4 (accept). Novelty is moderate but well-justified; the method is principled, simple, and yields consistent improvements across standard tasks. After the author response clarifying the difficulties with Li & Hovy replication, I do not think those issues should penalize this submission, but the final version should include a more careful comparison and analysis.
