Summary
This paper proposes a local coherence model that applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) to the entity grid representation. Each grid cell (grammatical role and optionally entity features) is embedded, convolutional filters scan columns to capture k-length entity transitions, max-pooling selects salient patterns, and a linear layer produces a document-level coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss. Experiments on three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) show consistent improvements over prior grid-based methods and a graph-based model; the authors also report gains when pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning on summaries.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A neuralized entity-grid model that uses learned embeddings and CNNs to capture longer transition patterns and incorporate entity-specific features without combinatorial blow-up.
2) An end-to-end pairwise ranking training scheme that learns task-specific features and yields consistent state-of-the-art performance across three standard coherence tasks.
3) An empirical finding that a relatively simple neural architecture, even with a reduced set of entity-specific features, outperforms stronger feature-rich grid baselines.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and architecture design. The paper explains well why CNNs are an appropriate inductive bias for location-invariant, k-length transition patterns over entity columns, and how distributed representations alleviate sparsity in long transitions. The “Why it works” intuition aligns with the model structure.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains on multiple tasks. The model improves absolute accuracy by ~4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and >3–4% on summary coherence rating, with significance testing. Pretraining on a larger corpus further boosts summary rating, supporting the claim that feature learning generalizes across tasks.
- Competitive with fewer handcrafted features. The extended Grid-CNN uses only a subset (3) of entity features yet outperforms the extended grid baseline with 9 features (some from external corpora), highlighting the benefit of representation learning.
- Sensible training objective and evaluation setup. Pairwise ranking is a natural fit for coherence scoring; the use of WSJ (larger and stylistically broader than AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES) strengthens the evidential base. Hyperparameter tuning, regularization (dropout, early stopping), multiple random seeds, and significance testing enhance experimental rigor.
- Practical relevance and potential for reuse. The approach is simple to implement over standard entity grids, is compatible with downstream ranking-based use cases, and the authors state code is released.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior entity-grid work. The core advance is a straightforward “neuralization” of the entity grid with CNNs. While effective, representational and task innovations are modest for a full-length conference paper; the model ignores cross-entity interactions and mostly reuses established tasks and features.
- Insufficient analysis of design choices. The choice of 100-dimensional embeddings for a tiny role vocabulary (and for the extended grid) is not justified; there is no ablation on embedding size, k, pooling length, number of filters, or dropout. The claim that longer transitions help would be stronger with controlled k-ablations and length-bucket analyses.
- Reproducibility/comparison concerns. The authors were unable to reproduce Li & Hovy (2014), yet exclude it from the main results. This leaves a gap in comparative evidence versus another neural coherence approach and risks biasing the comparison set. More careful troubleshooting or reporting (e.g., alternative settings, dataset differences) is needed.
- Modeling assumptions may be limiting. Treating entity columns independently ignores potential cross-entity interactions (e.g., coordinated topical shifts). There is no discussion of how coreference errors or entity extraction choices affect results; given reliance on entity tracking, this is important.
- Writing and organization issues. The paper is verbose, with noticeable repetition between background and related work, and several editorial/language issues (see Additional comments). This detracts from clarity and makes key contributions less crisp.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Can you provide ablations for: (a) transition window k (e.g., 2–8), (b) embedding dimension, (c) number of filters and pooling size, and (d) dropout? How sensitive are results to these?
- What is the impact of pretraining vs. training from scratch on summaries? Do you freeze embeddings/filters when fine-tuning?
- What entity extraction/coreference pipeline did you use (head nouns vs. all nouns vs. full coreference)? How robust are results to coreference errors?
- Have you tried modeling cross-entity interactions (e.g., 2D convolutions across neighboring columns, self-attention over entities)? If yes, how did they fare?
- Can you disentangle the effect of the pairwise ranking loss from the CNN architecture? For example, how does an SVM-ranker over CNN features or a linear model with the same loss perform?
- Regarding Li & Hovy (2014): which code version/settings and dataset splits did you use? Can you share diagnostics explaining the large performance gap you observed?

Additional comments (minor/editorial)
- Reorganize and condense background vs. related work to avoid duplication; move detailed surveys to Section 6, keep Section 2 focused on the entity grid essentials and concrete limitations.
- Provide more learning/compute details (training time, hardware, parameter counts).
- Clarify the size of the role+feature vocabulary V in the extended model and the resulting embedding table size.
- Explicitly report transition window k used in final models and include length-bucket performance (short vs. long docs).
- Fix language/formatting:
  - Replace “Firstly/Secondly” with “First/Second.”
  - Rephrase “limits the model to consider” to “prevents the model from considering.”
  - Correct figure cross-reference: “Figure 2” → “Figure 1” at p.1 bottom left.
  - Address missing articles and awkward phrasing; do a careful proofread.
  - Consider removing the final paragraph of Section 1 (it is repetitive).
- Consider adding a stronger analysis section: qualitative filter visualizations (e.g., top n-gram of roles activating each filter), error analysis on insertion, and examples where longer k helps. This would better substantiate the modeling claims.
- For completeness, report results for Li & Hovy (2014) even if low, with caveats and attempted fixes, to provide a transparent comparison landscape.

Overall, this work is solid empirically and practically useful, but would benefit from deeper analysis, clearer writing, and stronger evidence for specific design claims and comparative positioning.
