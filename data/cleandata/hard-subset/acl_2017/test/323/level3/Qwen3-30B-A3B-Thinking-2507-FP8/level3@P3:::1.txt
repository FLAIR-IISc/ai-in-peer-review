

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary
The paper proposes a neural extension to the entity grid model for local coherence assessment. The authors replace the discrete feature extraction process of traditional entity grid models with a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates over distributed representations of entity transitions. This allows the model to capture longer-range entity transitions (up to length 5-8) while avoiding the curse of dimensionality. The model is trained end-to-end using pairwise ranking, enabling it to learn task-specific features. The authors evaluate their approach on three coherence tasks: sentence discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating, demonstrating state-of-the-art results across all tasks.

## Main Contributions
1. The paper introduces a neural architecture that extends the entity grid model with a CNN to learn entity-transition sequences, enabling better generalization over longer sequences without suffering from the curse of dimensionality.
2. The authors present a hybrid approach that leverages the strengths of the established entity grid framework while incorporating neural network capabilities, rather than proposing a fully neural system from scratch.
3. The work demonstrates significant improvements over existing non-neural entity grid models across three coherence assessment tasks, with absolute gains of 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4% on summary coherence rating.

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates clear and consistent improvements over existing entity grid models across three different coherence assessment tasks. The gains are statistically significant (p < 0.01) and substantial (4% absolute improvements), making a compelling case for the proposed approach. The results on the summary coherence rating task are particularly impressive, as neural approaches often struggle with smaller datasets.

2. **Well-structured and well-written**: The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and flows logically from motivation to model description to evaluation. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing entity grid models and explain how their neural approach addresses these limitations.

3. **Thoughtful hybrid approach**: The paper takes a pragmatic approach by building upon the well-established entity grid framework rather than discarding it entirely. This approach allows the authors to leverage existing work while incorporating neural capabilities, making their model more accessible to researchers familiar with the entity grid framework.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient comparison to Li and Hovy (2014)**: The paper mentions that the Li and Hovy model performed poorly (19.34% accuracy in discrimination) but does not provide a thorough comparison. The authors should address why their results differ from Li and Hovy's reported results, which were significantly higher. This is a critical omission given that Li and Hovy's work is a direct neural approach to coherence modeling.

2. **Lack of investigation into Li and Hovy's poor performance**: The authors mention that they "were not sure what could go wrong" with Li and Hovy's model but did not investigate further. They should have attempted to reproduce Li and Hovy's reported results on their original datasets as a sanity check and documented any difficulties encountered. This is especially important since the Li and Hovy model's performance on their dataset appears unexpectedly low.

3. **Proofreading issues**: The manuscript contains numerous missing definite articles throughout (e.g., "a model" instead of "the model"). This is a significant distraction from the otherwise strong technical content and suggests insufficient proofreading.

4. **Inconsistent phrasing**: The paper repeatedly uses the phrase "limits the model to do X" which is grammatically incorrect. This should be rephrased to "limits the model's capacity to do X" or "stops the model from doing X" for clarity and correctness.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more details about the specific difficulties you encountered when attempting to run the Li and Hovy system, including any error messages or unexpected behavior? This would help clarify whether the poor performance was due to implementation issues or corpus-specific factors.

2. Have you attempted to reproduce Li and Hovy's reported results on their original datasets (AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES) to verify their performance? If so, what were your results, and how do they compare to the authors' original findings?

3. Could you clarify why the Li and Hovy model performed so poorly on your WSJ corpus compared to their reported results? Is this due to differences in the corpus characteristics, model configuration, or implementation details?

## Recommendation
I recommend a final decision of 4 (weak accept) after considering the author response. The paper makes a strong contribution to the coherence modeling field with well-justified improvements over existing approaches. While the lack of thorough comparison to Li and Hovy is a significant weakness, the authors' explanation that they were unable to properly run the Li and Hovy system due to implementation difficulties is reasonable and should not be penalized in the final decision. The technical contribution is sound and well-executed, and the results are compelling. With proper attention to the proofreading issues and a more thorough comparison to Li and Hovy in the final version, this would be a strong paper.
