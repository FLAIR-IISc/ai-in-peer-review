

Summary: This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment, using a convolutional neural network to model longer entity transitions (k=6-8) compared to previous work (k≤3). The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a neural architecture that captures long-range entity transitions without suffering from the curse of dimensionality, (2) an end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking that learns task-specific features, and (3) state-of-the-art results across three coherence assessment tasks.

Strengths: The paper's most compelling strength is its clear demonstration of how neural approaches can overcome limitations of traditional entity grid models. By using distributed representations and convolutional layers, the authors successfully model longer entity transitions (k=6-8) without the exponential parameter growth that plagues discrete representations. This is well-justified in the "Why it works" section, where the authors explain how filters detect specific transition patterns. The experimental results are strong, with the proposed Extended Grid-CNN achieving absolute gains of 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating over the best prior non-neural model (Extended Grid). The pairwise ranking training approach is sensible and well-executed, allowing the model to learn task-specific features end-to-end. The authors also provide a thoughtful analysis of why their model performs better on discrimination than insertion, noting that insertion is inherently more difficult and the model wasn't specifically trained for it.

Weaknesses: The most significant weakness is that the approach is largely a straightforward "neuralization" of existing entity grid models without substantial representational or task innovations. The paper doesn't introduce new coherence features or modeling approaches beyond applying CNNs to the entity grid, which is a modest contribution for a full ACL paper. The justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent four-dimensional grid entries (and higher for the extended grid) is unclear and unconvincing, especially since the authors don't demonstrate any benefit from this high-dimensional representation. The benefit of longer transition chains (k=6 vs k=3) is questionable given the average WSJ article has only 21.5 sentences, and the paper fails to adequately address why longer transitions are beneficial in practice. The writing is verbose and repetitive, with significant duplication between section 2 (Entity Grid and Its Extensions) and section 6 (Related Work), which could have been condensed into a more focused discussion. The authors also report being unable to reproduce Li and Hovy's (2014) results on their dataset, which suggests potential implementation or modeling issues that need addressing.

Questions to Authors: 1) Can you clarify why you use 100-dimensional vectors to represent the four grammatical roles (S,O,X,-) in the entity grid? How does this higher dimensionality benefit coherence modeling compared to lower-dimensional representations? 2) Given that WSJ articles average only 21.5 sentences, why do you believe modeling transitions of length k=6-8 is beneficial over shorter transitions (k=3-4) that would better match the typical document length? 3) The paper claims to outperform Li and Hovy (2014), but you report failing to reproduce their results. Could you elaborate on why their model performed poorly on your dataset and whether this indicates a fundamental issue with their approach or a specific implementation problem?
