

**Review**

This paper proposes a neural coherence model that extends the entity grid framework with a convolutional neural network to better capture long-range entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features. The primary contribution is the neural extension of the entity grid model, which allows for learning longer transition patterns (up to length 8) while maintaining generalization through distributed representations. A secondary contribution is the pairwise ranking training approach that enables end-to-end learning of task-specific features. The third contribution is the demonstration of state-of-the-art results across three coherence assessment tasks.

Strengths: The technical approach is well-executed and addresses a clear limitation in existing entity grid models. The paper is exceptionally well-written and fluent, making complex concepts accessible. The empirical results are strong, showing consistent improvements over non-neural baselines (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating).

Weaknesses: The most significant weakness is the insufficient comparison with Li and Hovy's neural coherence model (2014), which performed unexpectedly poorly (19.34% accuracy) on the authors' dataset. This raises serious concerns about experimental setup or corpus dependence that require deeper investigation. The paper should reproduce Li and Hovy's reported results on their original datasets as a sanity check. Additionally, the manuscript contains numerous grammatical issues with missing definite articles throughout. The expression "limits the model to do X" appears repeatedly and should be rephrased for clarity (e.g., "limits the model's capacity to do X"). Finally, the authors should document challenges encountered when running the Li and Hovy system.

Questions to authors: Could you explain why the Li and Hovy model performed so poorly on your dataset compared to their reported results, and whether you tested it on their original corpora? How did you handle the differences in experimental setup between your work and theirs?

The paper makes a solid technical contribution with strong empirical results, but the unresolved issues with the Li and Hovy comparison and grammatical errors prevent it from being fully compelling. With the suggested revisions, this could be a strong paper. After considering the author response, I adjust my recommendation to a 4 (weak acceptance).
