

**Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"**

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a neural coherence model that applies a CNN on top of the entity grid representation. The primary contributions are: (1) a neural architecture for modeling local coherence using distributed representations of entity transitions, (2) an end-to-end pairwise ranking training approach to learn task-specific features, and (3) extensive empirical validation across three coherence assessment tasks. However, the core innovation is largely a neuralization of existing entity grid approaches rather than a substantial advancement.

**Strengths**  
1. The evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates consistent improvements over prior entity grid models (4% absolute gain in discrimination, 2.5% in insertion, and 4% in summary coherence).  
2. The motivation for using CNNs (to model long-range transitions and capture distributed representations) is well-justified in Section 3.1.  
3. The pairwise ranking training method is sensible and aligns with coherence assessment as a ranking problem.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The method is a straightforward neuralization of existing work (entity grid) without significant representational or task innovations expected for a full paper. The core contribution appears incremental.  
2. The paper fails to reproduce Li and Hovy (2014), suggesting potential implementation or modeling errors that undermine confidence in the results.  
3. The writing is verbose and repetitive (e.g., Sections 2 and 6 duplicate content), and key design choices lack justification (e.g., 100-dimensional vectors for 4D grid entries).  
4. The benefit of using longer transition chains (k=6) is questionable given many WSJ articles are short (average 21.5 sentences), and the paper does not address this limitation.  
5. Minor but pervasive language issues (missing articles, phrasing errors) detract from professionalism.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Can you clarify why 100-dimensional vectors were chosen for grid entries (Section 3.1) rather than a smaller dimension?  
2. How does the model address the questionable benefit of k=6 transitions for short documents (Section 5.3)?  
3. What caused the inability to reproduce Li and Hovy (2014) results, and how would you address this?  

The paper demonstrates solid empirical results but lacks sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance as a full paper. The authors should either incorporate more substantial innovations or significantly condense the manuscript to focus on the evaluation.
