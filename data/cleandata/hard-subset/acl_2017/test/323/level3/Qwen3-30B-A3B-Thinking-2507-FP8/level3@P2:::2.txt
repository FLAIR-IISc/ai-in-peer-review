

# Review for "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) to the entity grid representation of text. The authors argue that their approach addresses two limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) the use of discrete representations that leads to the curse of dimensionality, limiting transition modeling to short lengths (k ≤ 3); and (2) task-agnostic feature extraction that prevents learning task-specific features. Their model uses distributed representations of entity transitions and incorporates end-to-end training via pairwise ranking. The authors evaluate their model on three coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), reporting state-of-the-art results.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural extension of the entity grid model that uses distributed representations to model longer entity transitions (k=5-8) without suffering from the curse of dimensionality.
2. An end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking that allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically.
3. Empirical demonstration of consistent improvements over non-neural entity grid models across multiple coherence tasks.

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over prior entity grid models across three different coherence tasks (discrimination: +4%, insertion: +2.5%, summary coherence: +4.3%). These improvements are statistically significant (p<0.01) and represent the best results reported to date for these tasks. The evaluation on the larger WSJ corpus (vs. smaller AIRPLANES/ EARTHQUAKES datasets) provides more robust evidence of the model's effectiveness.

2. **Clear motivation and design rationale**: The authors effectively motivate their approach by clearly articulating the limitations of existing entity grid models (discrete representations causing curse of dimensionality, task-agnostic feature extraction). The "Why it works" section provides a strong intuitive explanation for why the CNN architecture is suitable for modeling entity transitions.

3. **Practical utility and reproducibility**: The authors release their code, making their work easily reproducible. The model's ability to incorporate entity-specific features (e.g., named entity types) without requiring external resources is a practical advantage over some prior work.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of substantial innovation**: The approach is largely a "neuralization" of existing entity grid models without introducing fundamentally new representations or modeling approaches. The authors acknowledge this ("method is largely a straightforward 'neuralization' of the entity grid"), and the paper fails to clearly articulate how this neuralization provides novel insights beyond what existing grid models could achieve with better feature engineering.

2. **Questionable design choices**: The justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent four discrete grammatical roles (S, O, X, -) is unclear. Additionally, the benefit of modeling longer transition chains (k=6) is questionable given that most WSJ articles are relatively short (average 21.5 sentences), making such long transitions statistically rare and potentially uninformative.

3. **Excessive repetition and poor organization**: The paper contains significant redundancy, particularly between Sections 2 and 6 (which duplicate content on entity grid models and their extensions). The writing is verbose and repetitive, with unnecessary duplication of introductory material. This undermines the paper's impact and makes it difficult to follow the core contributions.

4. **Unaddressed implementation issues**: The authors note they were unable to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014) using their code, yet they do not address this potential issue in their own work. This raises questions about the reliability of their experimental setup and the validity of their comparison.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims the model can "capture long range entity transitions" (k=5-8), but given the average WSJ article length of 21.5 sentences, how many actual transitions of length 6 or longer exist in the training data? How does the model avoid overfitting to rare transition patterns?

2. The paper uses 100-dimensional vectors to represent a 4-symbol discrete space (S, O, X, -). What is the theoretical or empirical justification for this dimensionality choice? How does this compare to standard embedding practices for small discrete spaces?

3. The authors state that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." What specific features were omitted, and how would including all features affect the results?

4. The paper duplicates content between Sections 2 and 6. How would you revise the paper to eliminate this redundancy while maintaining clarity?
