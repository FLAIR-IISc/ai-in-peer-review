- Summary: Proposes a CNN-based neural local coherence model over entity grids using distributed entity-transition representations and pairwise ranking, reporting state-of-the-art results on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence tasks.

- Contribution 1: Neural extension of the entity grid enabling modeling of longer entity transitions (k=5–8) without the curse of dimensionality.

- Contribution 2: End-to-end pairwise ranking training that learns task-specific coherence features automatically.

- Contribution 3: Empirical demonstration of consistent improvements over non-neural entity grid models across multiple coherence tasks.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical gains (discrimination +4%, insertion +2.5%, summary +4.3%) with statistical significance and robust WSJ evaluation.

- Strength 2: Clear motivation and rationale explaining why CNNs suit modeling entity transitions.

- Strength 3: Practicality and reproducibility — code released and model can use entity-specific features without external resources.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty — largely a straightforward "neuralization" of existing entity grid models without fundamentally new representations.

- Weakness 2: Questionable design choices — unclear justification for 100-dimensional embeddings for four grammatical roles and dubious benefit of very long transitions given short article lengths.

- Weakness 3: Excessive repetition and poor organization, with redundant content (notably Sections 2 and 6) and verbose writing.

- Weakness 4: Unaddressed implementation concerns — inability to reproduce prior work (Li and Hovy 2014) is noted but not resolved, raising reliability questions.

- Question 1: How many transitions of length ≥6 exist in the WSJ training data and how does the model avoid overfitting to such rare patterns?

- Question 2: What is the theoretical or empirical justification for using 100-dimensional vectors for a 4-symbol space, and how does this compare to standard embedding practices?

- Question 3: Which Extended Grid features were omitted in experiments, and what would including all features do to performance?

- Question 4: How would the authors revise the paper to remove redundancy between Sections 2 and 6 while keeping clarity?
