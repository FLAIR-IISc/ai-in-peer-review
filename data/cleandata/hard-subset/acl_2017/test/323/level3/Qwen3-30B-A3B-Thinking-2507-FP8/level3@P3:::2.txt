

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model" (ACL 2017)

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for local coherence assessment, using a convolutional neural network (CNN) to operate over the entity grid representation. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a neural architecture for coherence assessment that captures long-range entity transitions with distributed representations; (2) an end-to-end training method to learn task-specific features; and (3) state-of-the-art results on three coherence assessment tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Clear motivation for the CNN architecture**: The paper provides a strong justification for using a convolutional approach, explaining that it allows modeling entity transitions in a location-invariant way while capturing longer-range dependencies (k=6 vs. k=3 in prior work). The "Why it works" section effectively explains how convolutional filters detect specific transition patterns.

2. **Strong empirical evaluation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments across three distinct coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), demonstrating consistent improvements over prior entity grid models. The results are statistically significant, with absolute gains of up to 4% on discrimination and 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating.

3. **Effective implementation of neuralization**: The paper successfully demonstrates how to neuralize the entity grid model while preserving its core advantages. The authors carefully address limitations of the original grid model, particularly the curse of dimensionality, by using distributed representations and convolutional layers to model longer transitions without overfitting.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of substantial innovation**: The proposed method is largely a straightforward neuralization of the existing entity grid model, with minimal representational or task innovations. The paper consolidates prior work rather than introducing fundamentally new concepts or approaches, which is insufficient for a full paper at ACL.

2. **Unjustified design choices**: The authors use 100-dimensional vectors to represent four-dimensional grid entries (and 32 dimensions for the extended grid), but provide no justification for these specific dimensions. This arbitrary choice weakens the methodological rigor of the paper.

3. **Questionable use of longer transition chains**: The paper uses k=6 for transition chains, but many WSJ articles are relatively short (average 21.5 sentences), making the benefit of longer transition chains questionable. The authors fail to justify why k=6 is optimal for this dataset.

4. **Poor writing and organization**: The paper is verbose and repetitive, with significant duplication between sections 2 and 6 (introduction and related work). The writing contains numerous minor language issues, missing articles, and awkward phrasing that detract from the paper's clarity.

5. **Unreproducible prior result**: The authors report being unable to reproduce the Li and Hovy (2014) result, which is a significant issue that suggests either an implementation error or a fundamental problem with the prior work that needs addressing.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses 100-dimensional vectors for grid entries, but provides no justification for this specific dimensionality. Can you explain why this dimension was chosen and whether it significantly impacts performance?

2. Given that most WSJ articles have around 20 sentences (Table 1), how does the k=6 transition length provide meaningful advantages over k=3, considering the limited document length?

3. The authors mention they were unable to reproduce the Li and Hovy (2014) result. Could you elaborate on what specific implementation issues you encountered, and whether this suggests a problem with their approach?

4. The writing contains several repetitive sections and minor language issues. Could you provide a revised version of the paper with improved organization and language?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound extension of the entity grid model for coherence assessment, with strong empirical results. However, the lack of substantial innovation, questionable design choices, and poor writing undermine its contribution. The method is essentially a "neuralization" of prior work rather than a novel approach. While the results are promising, the paper does not demonstrate sufficient novelty to warrant publication at ACL, particularly given the numerous weaknesses identified. The authors should consider adding more substantial innovations, such as incorporating new features or tasks beyond the entity grid framework, before resubmitting.
