- Proposal: neural extension of the entity grid using a CNN to model longer entity transitions (k=6–8) with three claimed contributions (neural architecture, end-to-end pairwise ranking, SOTA results).  
- Strength: Neural approach overcomes discrete entity-grid parameter explosion by using distributed representations and convolutional filters to detect transition patterns.  
- Strength: Clear "Why it works" explanation linking filters to specific transition pattern detection.  
- Strength: Strong empirical gains—~4% on discrimination, ~2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating over prior non-neural best.  
- Strength: Pairwise ranking training is sensible and enables end-to-end learning of task-specific features.  
- Strength: Thoughtful analysis noting better performance on discrimination than insertion and that insertion is inherently harder.  
- Weakness: Largely a straightforward "neuralization" of existing entity-grid models, offering modest novelty for a full ACL paper.  
- Weakness: Unclear and unconvincing justification for using 100-dimensional vectors to represent four grammatical roles; no demonstrated benefit.  
- Weakness: Questionable benefit of long transitions (k=6–8) given average WSJ article length (~21.5 sentences); insufficient practical justification.  
- Weakness: Verbose and repetitive writing with duplication between methods and related work sections.  
- Weakness: Inability to reproduce Li and Hovy (2014) results on the dataset raises concerns about implementation or modeling issues.  
- Q: Please clarify why 100-dimensional vectors are used for four grammatical roles and how they help versus lower-dimensional representations.  
- Q: Why model transitions of length k=6–8 rather than shorter k=3–4 that better match typical document lengths?  
- Q: Why did Li and Hovy (2014) perform poorly on your dataset—does this indicate a fundamental problem with their approach or an implementation issue?
