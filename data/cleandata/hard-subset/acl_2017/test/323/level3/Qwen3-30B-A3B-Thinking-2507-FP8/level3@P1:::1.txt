

This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that extends the entity grid representation with a convolutional neural network to better capture long-range entity transitions while maintaining generalization. The main contributions are: (1) a neural architecture that models entity transitions using distributed representations to overcome the dimensionality limitations of traditional entity grid models; (2) a pairwise ranking training approach that enables end-to-end learning of task-specific features; and (3) state-of-the-art results across three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating).

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a well-motivated problem by extending a widely used entity grid model with neural techniques to overcome key limitations. The authors clearly articulate how discrete representations in existing models limit transition length (k ≤ 3) and prevent task-specific feature learning. Their CNN-based approach effectively models longer transitions (k = 5-8) while maintaining generalization through distributed representations, which is a significant technical contribution to the field.

2. The experimental results are compelling and consistent across three different evaluation tasks. The model achieves absolute gains of 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating over existing entity grid models. These improvements are statistically significant (p < 0.01), demonstrating the robustness of the approach. The evaluation on the larger WSJ corpus (vs. smaller AIRPLANES/ EARTHQUAKES datasets) is particularly valuable for deep learning models that require substantial training data.

3. The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a thorough explanation of the model architecture and its relationship to existing work. The neural architecture is presented in a logical manner, with clear descriptions of the lookup, convolutional, and pooling layers. The authors effectively demonstrate how their approach differs from prior work, particularly the Li and Hovy (2014) neural model, which they acknowledge as an important comparison point.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison with Li and Hovy's neural coherence model is insufficiently explored. The authors report disappointing results for this model (19.34% accuracy in discrimination), yet they do not adequately investigate why this occurred. This raises concerns about whether the poor performance was due to implementation issues, corpus differences, or other factors. The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of why Li and Hovy's model underperformed on their dataset and how their own approach overcomes these limitations.

2. The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, particularly missing definite articles throughout (e.g., "we propose a local coherence model" should be "we propose a local coherence model"). These errors detract from the professionalism of the paper and suggest insufficient proofreading. While minor, such issues undermine the paper's credibility and readability.

3. The discussion of the Li and Hovy model's limitations could be strengthened. The authors note that Li and Hovy's approach "uses a recurrent or a recursive neural network to compute the representation for each sentence," but don't sufficiently explain why their CNN-based approach is better suited for coherence modeling. A more detailed comparison of the architectural differences and why they matter for coherence would strengthen the paper's contribution claim.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered when implementing Li and Hovy's model on your dataset? Did you reproduce their reported results on their original datasets as a sanity check?

2. The paper states that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." What specific entity features from Elsner and Charniak (2011) were excluded, and how might including them further improve performance?

3. The authors mention using "pre-trained models from discrimination task on WSJ corpus" for the summary coherence task. How does the performance of these pre-trained models compare to training from scratch on the summary dataset, and what are the implications for transfer learning in coherence modeling?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to coherence modeling by effectively neuralizing the entity grid approach. While the experimental results are strong and the approach is well-motivated, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with Li and Hovy's model and improved proofreading. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
