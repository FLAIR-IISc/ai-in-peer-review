

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The main contributions are:

1. A convolutional neural network architecture that operates over the entity grid representation to model long-range entity transitions (up to length 8) without losing generalization power, addressing the curse of dimensionality problem in traditional entity grid models.

2. An end-to-end pairwise ranking training approach that allows the model to automatically learn task-specific high-level features, overcoming the limitation of previous models where feature extraction was decoupled from the target task.

3. Comprehensive evaluation across three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) demonstrating consistent improvements over existing entity grid models, with absolute gains of 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a significant technical contribution by successfully extending the entity grid model with a CNN architecture to capture longer-range entity transitions. This addresses a key limitation of previous work (k ≤ 3) and is well-motivated by the theoretical limitations of discrete representations. The empirical results (particularly the 2.5% improvement on the more challenging insertion task) clearly demonstrate the value of this extension.

2. The end-to-end training approach using pairwise ranking is well-justified and effectively implemented. This method allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically, which is a meaningful advancement over previous models where feature extraction was task-agnostic. The training methodology is clearly explained and aligned with the problem formulation.

3. The evaluation is thorough and well-designed, using three different coherence assessment tasks that provide complementary perspectives on model performance. The choice of WSJ corpus for sentence ordering tasks (over smaller specialized corpora) is particularly valuable due to its size and diversity, which is crucial for neural models. The results consistently show improvements over strong baselines across all tasks.

## Weaknesses
1. The comparison with Li and Hovy's neural coherence model (2014) is inadequate. The authors report that their implementation of this model performed poorly (19.34% accuracy), but they don't sufficiently investigate why this might be the case. This is a major weakness as it leaves doubt about whether the comparison is fair or if there's a corpus-specific issue. The authors should document their implementation efforts and ideally reproduce Li and Hovy's reported results on their original datasets as a sanity check.

2. The manuscript contains numerous grammatical issues, particularly with the use of definite articles throughout the text. Phrases like "limits the model to do X" appear repeatedly and should be rephrased to "limits the model's capacity to do X" or "prevents the model from doing X." This detracts from the professionalism of the paper and suggests insufficient proofreading.

3. The paper could better justify why a convolutional approach was chosen over other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs or LSTMs) that might also be appropriate for modeling sequential entity transitions. A brief comparison would strengthen the methodological justification.

4. The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why the neural model achieves better results on the insertion task, which is more challenging than discrimination. The authors mention that insertion is harder and that the model wasn't trained specifically for this task, but a deeper exploration of what features the model learns for this task would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more details about your implementation of Li and Hovy's model (2014) on your dataset? Specifically, what changes did you have to make to their code, and did you successfully reproduce their reported results on their original datasets as a sanity check?

2. The paper states that "the model that is trained to distinguish an original document from its random permutation may learn features that are not specific enough to distinguish documents when only one sentence differs." Could you elaborate on how you would adapt the model to better handle the insertion task directly?

3. Could you provide more details about your selection of entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention)? Why were these three features chosen over others mentioned in Elsner and Charniak (2011)?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper makes a meaningful contribution to coherence modeling by extending the entity grid framework with a neural architecture that effectively captures longer-range entity transitions. The empirical results are strong and well-documented. While the comparison with Li and Hovy's model is insufficient and the manuscript needs better proofreading, these are fixable issues. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field and would be a good fit for ACL 2017. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above.
