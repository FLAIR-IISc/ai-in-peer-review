**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on a convolutional neural network that operates over the entity grid representation of a text. The model captures long-range entity transitions along with entity-specific features without losing generalization, thanks to the power of distributed representation. The authors present a pairwise ranking method to train the model in an end-to-end fashion on a task and learn task-specific high-level features. The evaluation on three different coherence assessment tasks demonstrates that the model achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming existing models by a good margin.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural Local Coherence Model**: The authors propose a novel neural architecture for modeling local coherence, which captures long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features without losing generalization.
2. **Pairwise Ranking Method**: The authors present a pairwise ranking method to train the model in an end-to-end fashion on a task and learn task-specific high-level features.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The evaluation on three different coherence assessment tasks demonstrates that the model achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming existing models by a good margin.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective Use of Distributed Representation**: The authors effectively use distributed representation to capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features without losing generalization.
2. **End-to-End Training**: The pairwise ranking method allows for end-to-end training, which enables the model to learn task-specific high-level features.
3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The evaluation results demonstrate that the model achieves state-of-the-art results on three different coherence assessment tasks.
4. **Well-Motivated Architecture**: The neural architecture is well-motivated and effectively captures the entity transitions and entity-specific features.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Evaluation**: The evaluation is limited to three coherence assessment tasks, and it would be beneficial to evaluate the model on more tasks to demonstrate its generalizability.
2. **Comparison to Other Neural Models**: The authors only compare their model to non-neural models, and it would be beneficial to compare it to other neural models to demonstrate its effectiveness.
3. **Lack of Analysis on Hyperparameters**: The authors do not provide an analysis on the hyperparameters and their effect on the model's performance.
4. **Limited Discussion on Future Work**: The authors do not provide a detailed discussion on future work and potential extensions of the model.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend the model to other forms of discourse, such as asynchronous conversations?
2. Can you provide an analysis on the hyperparameters and their effect on the model's performance?
3. How do you plan to evaluate the model on more coherence assessment tasks to demonstrate its generalizability?
4. Can you compare your model to other neural models to demonstrate its effectiveness?
