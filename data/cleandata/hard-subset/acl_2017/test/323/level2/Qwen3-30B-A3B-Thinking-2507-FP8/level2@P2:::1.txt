

Summary: This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating over entity grids. The authors claim to address two key limitations of existing entity grid models: discrete representation leading to dimensionality issues, and task-agnostic feature extraction. Their main contributions are a neural architecture for coherence assessment that captures long-range entity transitions with distributed representations, an end-to-end training method for task-specific feature learning, and state-of-the-art results on three coherence tasks.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a clear and well-structured extension of the entity grid model, which is a well-established approach in coherence modeling. The authors effectively demonstrate how their neural model overcomes specific limitations of prior work, particularly the curse of dimensionality problem.
2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive across three distinct coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating), which is a strong point for the paper's validation. The consistent improvements over non-neural baselines across multiple tasks is noteworthy.
3. The paper makes a good effort to explain the neural architecture and its relation to the entity grid framework, with sufficient technical details about the convolutional layers and training procedure.

Weaknesses:
1. The claimed advantages of the neural model over existing approaches are not sufficiently substantiated. The paper states that their model can "capture long range entity transitions" (k=5-8), but provides no justification for why these specific transition lengths are optimal or how they were determined. The actual gains are modest (4% absolute on discrimination), and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why these gains represent a meaningful advance over prior work.
2. The authors mention they attempted to use the Li and Hovy (2014) neural coherence model but "results were very disappointing" (19.34% accuracy), yet they don't explain why their implementation failed or whether they properly followed the original methodology. This omission undermines their experimental comparison and raises questions about their evaluation setup.
3. The paper lacks a thorough comparison with recent neural coherence models that may have been published by 2017. The authors only compare with the entity grid variants and the graph-based model, but don't address more recent neural approaches that might have been presented at ACL or other top venues in the preceding year.
4. The paper claims "state of the art results" but doesn't properly contextualize these results within the broader literature. For example, the summary coherence rating task results don't compare with the most recent neural approaches, and the paper doesn't discuss whether the gains are statistically significant beyond the reported p<0.01.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper states that the neural model can capture "long range entity transitions" with k=5-8, but provides no justification for these specific values. How did you determine the optimal transition length, and how does this compare with what would be needed for traditional grid models?
2. The authors mention they tried Li and Hovy (2014) but got poor results. Could you elaborate on why your implementation failed, and whether you followed the original paper's methodology correctly?
3. The paper claims "state of the art results" but doesn't compare with more recent neural coherence models. Could you provide additional comparisons with models published after 2014 that might be more relevant to your approach?
