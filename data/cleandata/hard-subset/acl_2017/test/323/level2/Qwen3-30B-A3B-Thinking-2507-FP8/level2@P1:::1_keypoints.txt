- Paper introduces a CNN-based neural coherence model over entity grids using distributed representations, modeling long-range entity transitions (k=5–8) and trained with pairwise ranking, achieving 2.5–4% absolute gains on three coherence tasks.  
- Novel architecture addresses the curse of dimensionality by replacing discrete transition probabilities with learned distributed representations and models longer transitions without overfitting.  
- Strong empirical results: consistently outperforms baselines (including Elsner & Charniak 2011) across discrimination, insertion, and summary rating tasks with statistically significant gains.  
- Practical implementation and transparency: code released, clear hyperparameter details, and useful pre-training strategy demonstrating transfer potential.  
- Weakness: insufficient dataset comparability—WSJ used but AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES (prior benchmarks) were excluded without justification, limiting direct comparison.  
- Weakness: incomplete ablation—only three entity-specific features used versus nine in prior work; need experiments with the full feature set or justification for the subset.  
- Weakness: insertion-task gains are small (≈1%) and underexplored; no ablation/fine-tuning to explain or improve performance on insertion.  
- Question: Why was Li & Hovy (2014) excluded and why did it perform poorly on WSJ—could this be due to hyperparameters or data mismatch?  
- Question: How do entity-specific features (e.g., named entity type) affect model attention—would saliency or filter visualizations reveal driving features?  
- Comment: WSJ justification (size/style) is reasonable but should be made explicit to support claims of generalizability.  
- Comment: Naming is confusing—clarify what "Grid-CNN" vs. "Extended Grid-CNN" denotes (basic grid vs. neuralized version).  
- Comment: Paper should better distinguish its CNN approach from Li & Hovy (2014)’s window-based neural method.  
- Overall: Strong technical contribution with robust results and suitability for acceptance; addressing dataset comparability and feature ablation would significantly strengthen the paper.
