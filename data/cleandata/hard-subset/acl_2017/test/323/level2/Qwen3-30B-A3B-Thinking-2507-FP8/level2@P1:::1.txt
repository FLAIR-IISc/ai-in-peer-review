

Summary: The paper proposes a neural coherence model using convolutional networks over entity grids, aiming to overcome limitations of discrete entity grid models (e.g., curse of dimensionality) by leveraging distributed representations. Key contributions: (1) A CNN-based architecture that models long-range entity transitions (length 5–8) while incorporating entity-specific features end-to-end; (2) A pairwise ranking training method that learns task-specific features; (3) State-of-the-art results on three coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) with absolute gains of 2.5–4% over prior entity grid models.

Strengths:  
1. **Novel architecture addressing core limitations**: The CNN approach directly tackles the "curse of dimensionality" by replacing discrete transition probabilities with distributed representations (Section 2.1). The model achieves longer transition modeling (k=5–8 vs. k≤3 in prior work) without overfitting, as evidenced by the 4% gain on discrimination (Table 3). This is a meaningful technical contribution.  
2. **Strong empirical results with thorough evaluation**: The model consistently outperforms all baselines (including the extended grid model of Elsner & Charniak, 2011) across three distinct tasks. The use of pairwise ranking (Section 3.2) aligns well with coherence assessment as a ranking problem (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008), and the results are statistically significant (p<0.01).  
3. **Practical implementation and transparency**: The authors release code and provide clear hyperparameter tuning details (Table 2). The pre-training strategy for the summary coherence task (Table 4) is insightful and demonstrates transfer learning potential.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Insufficient comparison on standardized datasets**: The paper uses WSJ (Table 1) but excludes AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES (used in prior work) without justification. This limits comparability with prior SOTA results (e.g., Elsner & Charniak, 2011). *Recommendation*: Report results on AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES to confirm generalizability and enable direct comparison.  
2. **Incomplete ablation study**: The model incorporates only three entity-specific features (Section 5.1), while Elsner & Charniak (2011) used nine. The paper claims "this demonstrates effectiveness" (Section 5.3), but a fairer comparison would test the full feature set or justify the subset. *Recommendation*: Add an ablation showing performance with all nine features (or explain why the subset suffices).  
3. **Underexplored insertion task results**: The model gains only 1% on insertion (vs. 4% on discrimination), yet the authors note insertion is harder (Section 5.3). However, they do not investigate *why* gains are smaller (e.g., insufficient task-specific training). *Recommendation*: Add an ablation where the model is fine-tuned on insertion data to isolate task-specific factors.

Questions to Authors:  
1. Why was Li & Hovy (2014) excluded despite being a neural coherence model? Their poor performance (19.34% accuracy) on WSJ suggests potential dataset-specific issues—could this be due to hyperparameter choices or data mismatch?  
2. How do the entity-specific features (e.g., named entity type) influence the model's attention? Could a saliency analysis (e.g., filter visualization) show which features drive coherence decisions?  

Additional Comments:  
- The justification for WSJ over AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES (Section 4.1) is reasonable but insufficient; explicitly state how WSJ’s larger size and natural style support the model’s generalizability.  
- The "Grid-CNN" vs. "Extended Grid-CNN" naming is slightly confusing; clarify whether "Grid-CNN" refers to the basic grid (all nouns) or the neuralized version.  
- The paper correctly avoids claiming novelty for neuralizing entity grids (Section 6), but could better distinguish its CNN approach from Li & Hovy (2014)’s window-based method (Section 7).  

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear technical innovation and robust results. Addressing the weaknesses (especially dataset comparability and feature ablation) would significantly strengthen the paper. The work is well-positioned to advance coherence modeling and is suitable for acceptance.
