

Summary: This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that operates over the entity grid representation of text using a convolutional neural network. The primary contribution is a neural architecture that captures long-range entity transitions while incorporating entity-specific features through distributed representations. The secondary contribution is an end-to-end pairwise ranking training method that learns task-specific features. The tertiary contribution is state-of-the-art performance on three coherence assessment tasks.

Strengths:
The paper makes a clear and well-motivated contribution to coherence modeling by addressing two key limitations of existing entity grid models: their discrete representation that causes the curse of dimensionality and their task-agnostic feature extraction. The neural approach successfully enables modeling longer entity transitions (5-8 vs. previous models' 3) while maintaining generalization through distributed representations. The experimental results demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over non-neural entity grid models across three different coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), with absolute gains of 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4% on summary coherence rating. The paper provides a thorough evaluation with appropriate baselines and demonstrates the effectiveness of incorporating entity-specific features within the neural framework. The implementation details and hyperparameter tuning are well-documented, and the released code enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
The paper does not sufficiently justify why a convolutional neural network is the most appropriate architecture for this task compared to other neural approaches, particularly recurrent architectures that might be better suited for sequential data. While the paper mentions that the Li and Hovy (2014) neural model performed poorly on their dataset, it does not provide enough detail to understand why this occurred or whether it was due to the specific implementation or dataset characteristics. The paper also does not adequately address how the model handles texts with very long coherence dependencies that might exceed the maximum transition length considered in the convolutional layer. Additionally, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the specific features learned by the neural model, which would help understand why the approach works better than previous methods.

The paper's claim that it achieves "state of the art results" is somewhat overstated since it does not compare against the most recent neural coherence models. The Li and Hovy (2014) model is mentioned as having "very disappointing" results, but the paper does not provide sufficient evidence or analysis to support this characterization. It would be valuable to know whether the poor performance was due to the specific implementation of the Li and Hovy model on this dataset or whether the model is fundamentally unsuitable for coherence assessment.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the model handle texts with coherence dependencies that exceed the maximum transition length considered in the convolutional layer (e.g., 5-8), and what impact would extending this length have on performance?
2. What specific entity-specific features were most influential for the model's performance, and how did their importance compare to the features used in previous work (Elsner and Charniak, 2011)?
3. The paper mentions that the Li and Hovy (2014) model performed poorly on this dataset. Could the authors provide more details about the implementation and potential reasons for this poor performance, particularly in comparison to the results reported in their original paper?
