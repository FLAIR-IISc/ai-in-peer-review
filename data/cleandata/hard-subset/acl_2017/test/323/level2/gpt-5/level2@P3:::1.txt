Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that operates over the entity grid. Grammatical role symbols (S/O/X/-) and optionally entity-specific features are embedded, and convolutional filters over column-wise transitions capture long-range patterns; max pooling and a linear layer produce a document coherence score. Training uses pairwise ranking with shared weights to learn task-specific features end-to-end. Experiments on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating show consistent improvements over strong grid-based baselines, with statistically significant gains on WSJ.

As seen by this reviewer, the main contributions are:
1) A CNN-based neuralization of the entity grid that supports longer transition windows and integrates entity-specific features through learned embeddings, trained with a pairwise ranking loss.
2) Empirical evidence of state-of-the-art performance across three standard coherence evaluations, including improvements over the extended entity grid despite using only a subset of entity features and without external corpora.
3) A practical pretraining strategy (from WSJ discrimination) that enhances performance on the low-resource summary rating task.

Strengths
- Solid, task-relevant neuralization of a widely used representation: Modeling entity transitions with convolution over distributed role/feature embeddings addresses two known limitations of grid-based models: sparsity with longer transitions and task-agnostic feature extraction. The architecture is well-motivated for location-invariant local patterns and is simple enough to be adopted widely.

- Consistent and significant empirical gains on standard benchmarks: On WSJ, the neural grid improves over Grid-all-nouns by ~4% absolute in discrimination and ~1% in insertion, and over the extended grid by ~3.7% and ~2.5% respectively (Table 3), with significance tests. On DUC’03, the pre-trained Extended Grid-CNN further improves summary ranking accuracy by ~3–4% over grid baselines. Multiple random seeds, a development set for hyperparameter tuning, and significance testing strengthen the empirical credibility.

- Sensible training objective and protocol: Pairwise ranking directly optimizes the comparative nature of coherence assessment. Training details (optimizer, regularization, early stopping, search ranges) are clearly specified, which supports reproducibility.

- Interpretable inductive bias: Filters can be viewed as pattern detectors for salient entity-transition motifs (e.g., SS→O→X), and max pooling captures their presence regardless of position. This preserves the intuition behind the grid while benefiting from distributed representations.

- Data and feature economy: The model achieves gains without heavy reliance on numerous external features; incorporating only three simple entity-specific features yields further improvements, suggesting robustness and practicality.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior art: The proposal is a straightforward application of CNNs to the entity grid with a ranking loss. While effective, the conceptual advance over earlier neural coherence work (e.g., Li & Hovy, 2014, which models coherence with neural sentence representations) and over grid extensions is moderate. A clearer positioning versus discourse-relation transition models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) would help.

- Limited baseline coverage on WSJ: Results omit comparisons to discourse-relation-based ordering models (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) that are cited as strong alternatives. Including them, or explaining incompatibilities/dataset mismatches, would better substantiate the state-of-the-art claim.

- Dependence on preprocessing not analyzed: The approach hinges on coreference resolution and syntactic role assignment to build the grid. The paper does not specify the coreference and parsing tools used, nor analyze robustness to their errors. Given known sensitivity of grid models to coreference quality, this is a notable gap.

- Limited ablations and analysis: There is no ablation on transition window size k, the contribution of each entity feature, the impact of pretraining vs. training from scratch, or the effect of pooling choices. Qualitative inspection of learned filters/patterns or failure cases would increase insight.

- Domain scope and training-task mismatch: Experiments are confined to WSJ and DUC’03. Although the choice of WSJ is justified, a small cross-domain test (e.g., AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES) would strengthen generality claims. For insertion, the model is trained only on discrimination pairs; directly training with insertion-specific negatives might yield further gains and is not explored.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Preprocessing details: Which coreference resolver and parser/role labeler were used to construct the grids? How are pronouns handled? Are all nouns and pronouns included as entities, and are non-head nouns included by default?

- Vocabulary size and sparsity: After concatenating entity-specific features with grammatical roles (e.g., PERSON-S), what is the vocabulary size |V| for the extended model? Are rare composite symbols pruned or smoothed?

- Ablation and sensitivity: How does performance vary with transition window size k (e.g., 3–8), pooling size, and number of filters? What is the contribution of each entity-specific feature individually?

- Pretraining protocol: For summary rating, which parameters are transferred (embeddings only vs. full network), and are all layers fine-tuned? How sensitive are results to the pretraining corpus/task?

- Robustness to coreference errors: How does performance change when replacing the coreference system with a weaker/stronger model, or when using gold vs. automatic coreference (if available)?

- Computational aspects: What are training/inference times and memory requirements compared to extended grid baselines, especially for insertion where many candidates must be scored?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add discourse-relation baselines (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) on WSJ or provide a principled rationale if not feasible. Report statistical significance for the summary task.

- Provide ablations on k, features, and pretraining, and include qualitative analyses (e.g., top-activating transition patterns) and error analysis for insertion.

- Clarify preprocessing choices and assess sensitivity to coreference/role assignment quality.

- Consider training with insertion-style pairs to align training with that evaluation task; this seems feasible within the existing framework.
