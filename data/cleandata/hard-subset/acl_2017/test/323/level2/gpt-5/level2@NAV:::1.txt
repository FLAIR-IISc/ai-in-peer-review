Summary
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that applies a CNN over entity grids, learning distributed embeddings for grammatical roles and entity-specific features and training end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective. The model claims to capture longer-range entity transitions while retaining generalization, and achieves state-of-the-art results on sentence discrimination, sentence insertion (WSJ), and summary coherence rating (DUC03), with statistically significant gains over strong entity-grid baselines.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A neuralized entity-grid with convolution over columns that enables longer transition patterns and end-to-end learning, yielding consistent, significant improvements across three coherence evaluation tasks.
2) A simple, effective way to incorporate entity-specific features in the neural grid that outperforms a feature-rich non-neural extended grid while using fewer features.
3) A practical training recipe (pairwise ranking, dropout, pretraining/fine-tuning) that improves robustness and sample efficiency for low-data tasks (summary rating).

Strengths
- Strong empirical evidence: Clear, statistically significant gains over competitive baselines on discrimination (+~4%), insertion (+ up to 2.5%), and summary rating (+>4% with pretraining). Multiple runs and randomization tests increase confidence.
- Sound modeling: CNN over distributed role embeddings directly addresses sparsity/curse of dimensionality for longer k; wide conv + max-pooling plausibly capture position-invariant transition motifs.
- Reproducibility: Public code, hyperparameter search details, and reporting of seeds/runs are commendable for a conference paper.
- Sensible comparisons: Both “grid-only” and “grid+entity-features” variants isolate where improvements come from; pretraining adds a useful cross-task transfer angle.

Weaknesses
- Coreference dependence: The paper does not specify the resolver or analyze sensitivity to coref errors, which are known to materially impact grid-based coherence.
- Limited notion of global coherence: Columns are modeled independently; cross-entity interactions and discourse relations are not captured, potentially limiting performance on phenomena beyond local entity continuation.
- Evaluation scope: WSJ-only for ordering (in-domain) and small DUC03 for summaries; no out-of-domain transfer or robustness tests. More ablations (transition length k, pooling p, entity-feature choices) would clarify what drives gains.
- Training-task mismatch: Models are trained with random permutations; insertion requires finer distinctions, and the paper notes smaller gains—direct training on insertion or harder negatives would strengthen the claim.

Questions to Authors
- Which coreference system was used, and how sensitive are results to coref quality?
- What are the effects of varying k and pooling length p on each task?
- Can the architecture model cross-entity patterns (e.g., via 2D conv or attention) and does that help?
- What is the runtime and memory footprint compared to extended grids and graph-based models?
