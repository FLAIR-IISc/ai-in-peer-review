Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that operates on the entity grid representation of a document. Each grid cell (grammatical role possibly conjoined with entity features) is embedded; a 1D convolution along each entity column over sentences composes longer-range transition patterns; max-pooling and a linear layer yield a document-level score. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective.
- Contributions, as I see them:
  1) A neuralized entity-grid model using CNNs to model longer entity-transition patterns than traditional discrete transition probabilities allow, while incorporating entity-specific features without combinatorial blow-up.
  2) A task-tuned, end-to-end pairwise ranking framework over grids, including pretraining for low-resource tasks.
  3) Empirical results on three tasks (WSJ discrimination, WSJ insertion, DUC’03 summary coherence rating) with consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong entity-grid baselines.

Strengths
- Methodological clarity and sensible architecture choices: The mapping from discrete grid roles to embeddings, convolution along entity transitions, and pairwise ranking is well-motivated. The use of wide convolution and max-pooling is appropriate for location-invariant pattern detection in grids.
- Addresses a real limitation of entity grids: The approach directly tackles the sparsity/curse-of-dimensionality of long transition modeling in discrete grids, enabling k up to 5–8, a regime that is infeasible with count-based features. This is a substantial conceptual advance over prior grid variants.
- Strong empirical gains on standard tasks: On WSJ discrimination and insertion, the neural models outperform both the “all nouns” grid and the extended grid (with multiple entity features). Gains are statistically significant and non-trivial, especially considering the neural model uses only a subset of extended features. Pretraining further improves DUC’03 summary rating.
- Careful experimental protocol: Clear train/dev/test splits (PTB sections), multiple random runs averaged, hyperparameter search, early stopping, and significance testing. The authors also release code.
- Practicality: The model is simple, efficient, and compatible with existing preprocessing pipelines (POS/coref), making it easy to adopt.

Weaknesses
- Baseline coverage and fairness: The paper excludes Li & Hovy (2014) from tables due to disappointing replication results. This deprives readers of a key neural baseline comparison. Given model sensitivity, more effort to tune/adapt their code to WSJ, or at least a deeper diagnosis (data mismatch, windowing, negatives, embeddings, sentence representation choices), is warranted. Similarly, discourse-relation-based systems (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) are only discussed but not compared on WSJ; even if datasets differ, some cross-task or approximate comparisons would contextualize the claimed SOTA.
- Limited analysis and ablations: The paper argues that longer transitions and feature embeddings are beneficial, but provides no ablations on transition window size k, number of filters, or the contribution of each entity-specific feature. An analysis of sensitivity to pooling length/stride, or replacing max-pooling by average/dynamic pooling, would strengthen causal claims. Insight into learned filters/patterns (e.g., illustrative n-gram role sequences that activate filters) would substantiate “why it works.”
- Preprocessing dependence and robustness: The model depends on coreference and syntactic analysis; unlike some recent coherence models, there is no evaluation of robustness to coref errors, nor a comparison of different coref settings or a “surface-only” variant (e.g., noun matching vs. resolver). This is important to understand practical performance and portability.
- Insertion training mismatch: The insertion task is notably harder, and the model is not trained specifically for it. The authors acknowledge this; however, to fairly claim improvements on insertion, it would be valuable to include a variant trained with insertion-style pairs (varying a single sentence’s position) and report the impact.
- Clarity issues in notation and results: The convolution/pooling indexing is difficult to follow (e.g., hi length “m.n+k−1” and zero-padding across both sentences and entities). It seems convolution is strictly along sentences within each entity column; please clarify. Table 3’s headers/figures are confusing (identical numbers for discrimination and insertion in several rows; “Acc F1” labels without clear definition). The insertion metric needs a crisp definition and alignment with prior work.

Questions to authors
- Can you report an ablation on transition window size k and the effect of including/excluding each of the three entity-specific features? How large can k grow before performance saturates or degrades?
- What exactly is the convolution shape? Is it 1D along sentences per entity column, with no interaction across entities? If so, did you try 2D convolution that can capture inter-entity interactions?
- How are entities obtained: with a coreference resolver or noun matching? The text seems to suggest both. Which resolver and settings were used? How sensitive are results to coreference quality?
- For insertion, can you train with insertion-style pairs and report the delta? Also, please clarify the insertion metric and fix Table 3 to avoid confusion.
- Could you provide a more thorough comparison to Li & Hovy (2014)? What variants did you try (RNN vs. RecNN, window size, embeddings, negative sampling)? Even if results are poor, a diagnostic table would be informative.

Additional comments
- Consider evaluating against more challenging negative permutations (e.g., local swaps, reversal, discourse-aware reorders) to reduce the gap between training negatives and real errors.
- Provide qualitative analysis: error cases where the grid-CNN helps/hurts, and examples of high-activation transition patterns.
- Reporting runtime and parameter counts would help practitioners.
