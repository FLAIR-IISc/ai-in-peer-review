**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, building upon the well-established entity grid representation. The model uses a convolutional neural network (CNN) to analyze entity transition patterns. Specifically, it transforms the discrete grammatical roles in the entity grid into continuous vector embeddings and then applies 1D convolutions along each entity's transition sequence to learn salient patterns. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective to assign a higher score to more coherent texts. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating—and show that it achieves new state-of-the-art results, outperforming strong entity-grid-based baselines.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective neural architecture for local coherence modeling. It is one of the first works to successfully apply deep learning directly to the entity grid representation, overcoming key limitations of prior discrete models, such as the curse of dimensionality when modeling long-range transitions.
2.  Strong empirical evidence of the model's effectiveness. The proposed approach achieves statistically significant, state-of-the-art results across three different and well-established coherence evaluation tasks, demonstrating its robustness and power.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Novelty and Principled Design:** The paper presents a novel method for "neuralizing" the classic entity grid model. This is a well-motivated approach that directly addresses known weaknesses of previous grid-based methods. Using distributed representations for grammatical roles and a CNN to capture transition patterns allows the model to learn from sparse data and model longer-range dependencies (e.g., transition windows of size 5-6) than was previously feasible. The end-to-end training via a ranking loss is also a key advantage, allowing the model to learn features optimized for the coherence assessment task.
2.  **Comprehensive and Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on three standard tasks using a large, standard dataset (WSJ). The gains over strong, publicly available baselines like the extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011) are substantial and statistically significant (e.g., ~4% absolute improvement on the discrimination task). The fact that the model excels on the difficult sentence insertion task and the human-judgment-based summary rating task further validates its effectiveness.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, clearly explaining the limitations of prior work, the architecture of the proposed model, and the experimental setup. The inclusion of Figure 2 is particularly helpful for understanding the model and training process. The authors' decision to release their code is commendable and greatly enhances the reproducibility and potential impact of this work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Exploration of Feature Integration:** The "Extended Grid-CNN" is compared against the "Extended Grid (E&C)" model, but it only uses 3 of the 9 entity-specific features that the E&C model uses. While the neural model still wins, this makes the comparison slightly less direct. The proposed method of combining features and roles into a single symbol (e.g., `PERSON-S`) may not scale well as more features are added, as it could lead to a combinatorial explosion in the vocabulary size for the embedding layer. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the scalability of this approach or explored alternative feature integration methods (e.g., concatenating feature embeddings).
2.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the model works, but provides little insight into *why* it works or *what* it learns. The primary claim is that the CNN learns task-specific, high-level features from entity transitions. This claim could be substantiated with a qualitative analysis of the learned filters or embeddings. For example, do the filters learn to recognize patterns that correspond to known linguistic phenomena described in Centering Theory? Or do they capture novel, complex patterns? Such an analysis would significantly deepen the paper's contribution beyond just an empirical result.
3.  **Incomplete Comparison to Contemporary Neural Models:** The paper dismisses the contemporary neural model of Li and Hovy (2014) after reporting very poor performance on the WSJ dataset. While this may be due to implementation or dataset issues, simply excluding it from comparison is a missed opportunity. Li and Hovy's model operates on sentence-level representations rather than entity grids, representing a fundamentally different approach to neural coherence modeling. A more in-depth discussion of the potential reasons for its failure (e.g., sentence-level models struggle with longer documents) and the relative advantages of the entity-grid approach would have provided valuable context and strengthened the paper's positioning.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your method for incorporating entity-specific features involves creating composite symbols for the embedding layer. Did you consider the potential for vocabulary explosion if you were to include all 9 features from the Elsner & Charniak (2011) model? Have you experimented with alternative methods, such as using separate embedding vectors for the grammatical role and each entity feature, and then concatenating them before the convolution layer?
2.  Could you provide any qualitative analysis or visualization of the learned convolutional filters? It would be very insightful to see what kinds of entity transition patterns (e.g., 'S-S-O', 'S-X-S-S-S') the model learns to associate with coherent text. This could help connect your model's learned representations back to the linguistic theories that inspired the entity grid.
