**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, building upon the well-established entity grid representation. The core idea is to apply a convolutional neural network (CNN) directly to the entity grid. This approach learns distributed representations for grammatical roles and entity transitions, which allows the model to capture longer-range dependencies than previous methods without suffering from data sparsity. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective, enabling it to learn task-specific features.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective CNN-based architecture for modeling the entity grid. This provides an elegant, end-to-end framework that learns coherence features automatically, moving beyond the handcrafted feature engineering of prior models.
2.  Strong empirical validation demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. The paper presents convincing results on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating), showing significant and statistically significant improvements over strong, established baselines.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Soundness of the Model:** The paper successfully "neuralizes" the classic entity grid model. The application of a CNN to capture patterns in entity transitions is intuitive and well-motivated, directly addressing the curse of dimensionality that limited previous grid-based models to short n-grams. The end-to-end training approach is a clear advantage over prior methods where feature extraction was decoupled from the learning task.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and the results are compelling. The proposed `Extended Grid-CNN` achieves new state-of-the-art performance on all evaluation tasks, with substantial absolute gains (approx. 4% on discrimination and summary rating) over the strong extended grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011). The fact that this is achieved with fewer entity-specific features highlights the power of the neural architecture.
3.  **High-Quality Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient detail on their model architecture, training procedure, and hyperparameter tuning to facilitate reproducibility. The inclusion of a link to the code is a commendable step that benefits the community.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Simplistic Integration of Entity-Specific Features:** The method for incorporating entity-specific features—by creating new composite symbols like 'PERSON-S'—is rather basic. This approach greatly expands the vocabulary size and treats each feature-role combination independently, potentially missing deeper compositional interactions. A more sophisticated method, such as using separate embedding channels for roles and features, might offer better generalization and performance.
2.  **Insufficient Discussion of a Key Baseline:** The paper dismisses the neural model of Li and Hovy (2014) after failing to reproduce its performance on the WSJ dataset. While excluding a faulty baseline is reasonable, a brief analysis of *why* it might have failed (e.g., differences in model assumptions, suitability for long documents) would provide valuable context and strengthen the paper’s positioning relative to other neural approaches.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the integration of entity-specific features, have you considered alternative fusion methods, for example, concatenating separate embeddings for the grammatical role and the entity features before feeding them to the convolutional layer?
2.  You hypothesize that the smaller performance gain on the insertion task is due to the training objective. Have you experimented with training a model directly on an insertion-style task, and did this close the performance gap?
