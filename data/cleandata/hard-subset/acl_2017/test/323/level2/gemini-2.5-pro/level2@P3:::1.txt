**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel neural architecture for modeling local text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The model utilizes a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that operates over distributed vector representations of entity roles within the grid. This approach is designed to capture longer-range entity transitions than traditional discrete models and to learn task-specific feature representations in an end-to-end fashion using a pairwise ranking objective. The model is evaluated on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating—and is shown to achieve state-of-the-art results, significantly outperforming strong non-neural entity grid baselines.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and well-motivated neural coherence model that applies a CNN to the entity grid. This architecture directly addresses the data sparsity and task-agnostic feature engineering limitations of previous entity-based models by leveraging dense representations and end-to-end training.
2.  A strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating that the proposed neural approach yields significant and consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-art non-neural models on multiple benchmark tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model:** The paper does an excellent job of identifying the core limitations of traditional entity grid models, namely the curse of dimensionality for modeling long transitions and the decoupled, task-agnostic nature of feature extraction. The proposed CNN-based architecture is an elegant and natural solution to these problems. Applying convolutional filters to learn salient transition patterns over a continuous vector space is a compelling way to model local coherence.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The experimental results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model. The neural models (`Grid-CNN` and `Extended Grid-CNN`) consistently outperform their direct non-neural counterparts on three different tasks. The reported gains are statistically significant and practically meaningful (e.g., ~4% absolute improvement in accuracy on the discrimination task). The fact that the `Extended Grid-CNN` model achieves superior performance while using a smaller, simpler set of entity-specific features than its non-neural baseline further highlights the power of the neural feature learning approach.
3.  **Sound and Reproducible Experimental Methodology:** The evaluation is conducted rigorously. The work uses a large, standard dataset (WSJ) for the primary experiments, provides clear justification for this choice, and compares against strong, widely-used baselines. The inclusion of details on hyperparameter tuning, the reporting of averaged results over multiple runs, and the commitment to release the code are all commendable practices that enhance the reproducibility and credibility of the research. The use of pre-training for the summary rating task, which has a small dataset, is also a methodologically sound choice.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Analysis of Learned Representations:** A central claim of the paper is that the neural model learns task-specific, high-level features. However, the work provides little qualitative analysis to support this claim or to offer insight into what the model has learned. For example, an analysis of the learned embeddings (e.g., via t-SNE visualization to show relationships between roles like `S` vs. `PERSON-S`) or an inspection of the patterns captured by the most active convolutional filters would greatly strengthen the paper. Such an analysis would provide a deeper understanding of *why* the model works well, moving beyond just showing *that* it works.
2.  **Incomplete Comparison with other Neural Models:** The paper compares its model primarily to non-neural entity grid baselines. While it mentions the neural model of Li and Hovy (2014), it is excluded from the main results due to difficulties in reproduction. While the honesty is appreciated, this leaves a gap in the paper's positioning relative to other contemporary neural approaches to coherence. A more thorough discussion of the architectural differences and a potential attempt to compare on the dataset used in that prior work could have provided a more complete picture of the landscape of neural coherence modeling.
3.  **Simple Feature Integration Scheme:** The method for incorporating entity-specific features is to create new composite symbols in the vocabulary (e.g., `PERSON-S`). While effective, this approach increases the vocabulary size for every new feature combination and may not generalize well to unseen combinations. A more scalable alternative would be to use separate embeddings for each feature type (e.g., role, named-entity type) and combine them through concatenation or addition. A brief discussion of this alternative and the rationale for the chosen method would improve the paper.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The `Extended Grid-CNN` outperforms the `Extended Grid (E&C)` baseline while using only three of the nine features used by the latter. Was the full set of nine features tested with the neural model? If so, what was the impact on performance? This would help clarify whether the neural architecture benefits from a simpler feature set or if there is potential for further gains by incorporating more information.
2.  In the summary rating task, pre-training the model on the WSJ discrimination task provided a significant boost. Was the reverse experiment considered, i.e., fine-tuning a model trained for discrimination on the sentence insertion task? Given that insertion is a more difficult task, it might benefit from a more tailored training objective, and insights here could be valuable.
