**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel neural architecture for modeling local text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The proposed model uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that operates on columns of the entity grid, treating each column as a sequence of grammatical roles for a specific entity. The core idea is to replace the discrete, count-based features of traditional entity grid models with learned distributed representations for grammatical roles and convolutional filters that can capture longer-range transition patterns. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective, allowing it to learn task-specific features.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and well-motivated neural model for local coherence that directly addresses the key limitations (data sparsity, task-agnostic features) of the widely-used entity grid framework. The application of a CNN to the entity grid is an elegant way to model entity transitions.
2.  Strong empirical results on three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating). The model achieves new state-of-the-art results, demonstrating significant improvements over strong non-neural baselines.
3.  A straightforward method for incorporating entity-specific features into the neural architecture, which is shown to further improve performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Model Elegance and Motivation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the shift from discrete feature-based entity grid models to a neural approach. The limitations of prior work, namely the curse of dimensionality for modeling long transitions and the decoupled nature of feature extraction, are clearly articulated. The proposed CNN architecture is a very natural and clever solution to these problems, leveraging dense embeddings for generalization and convolutional filters to learn location-invariant transition patterns.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive and convincing. The authors evaluate their model on three different tasks with varying difficulty, using standard datasets. The choice of baselines is appropriate and includes the previous state-of-the-art non-neural models. The authors strengthen their empirical claims by running experiments multiple times to account for randomness and performing statistical significance tests.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed models consistently and significantly outperform all baselines across all tasks. The absolute improvements of ~4% in the discrimination task and ~2.5% in the insertion task over the strong Extended Grid model by Elsner & Charniak are impressive. These results provide compelling evidence for the superiority of the proposed neural approach.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The model, training procedure, and experiments are described with sufficient detail to allow for replication. The authors' decision to release their code is a commendable step towards fostering reproducible research.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Model Analysis:** While the model's strong performance is clearly demonstrated, the paper offers little insight into *why* it works so well. The power of neural models lies in their ability to learn meaningful representations, but there is no analysis of what the model has learned. For example, what kinds of salient transition patterns do the convolutional filters capture? A qualitative analysis of the learned embeddings (e.g., via t-SNE) or a visualization of filter activations on specific examples could have provided valuable insights into the model's inner workings and strengthened the paper's contribution beyond just an empirical result.
2.  **Inconclusive Comparison to a Key Neural Baseline:** The authors report being unable to achieve good performance with the recent neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014) and consequently exclude it from the main results. This model represents an alternative neural approach that is not based on the entity grid. Dismissing this important related work with the brief explanation "we were not sure what could go wrong" feels incomplete. A more detailed discussion of potential reasons for the poor performance (e.g., challenges in adapting the model to the longer documents in the WSJ corpus) would have made the comparison to related neural approaches more thorough.
3.  **Potentially Sub-optimal Feature Integration:** The method for incorporating entity-specific features—by creating new vocabulary items like 'PERSON-S'—is simple and effective. However, this approach conflates two distinct pieces of information (entity type and grammatical role) into a single atomic unit. This may hinder the model's ability to learn compositional representations and generalize to rare or unseen feature-role combinations. The paper would benefit from a discussion of this choice and a brief consideration of alternative, more compositional methods (e.g., concatenating separate embeddings for the feature and the role).

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide some analysis of the learned representations? For example, a visualization of the embeddings for the combined role/feature vocabulary could be very insightful. Do roles associated with the same entity type (e.g., PERSON-S, PERSON-O) cluster together? Do the same roles for different types (e.g., PERSON-S, ORG-S) show similar patterns?
2.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model, could the performance issues be related to document length or domain differences between the WSJ corpus and the datasets they used? Since their model operates on sentence representations, it might be more sensitive to these factors than an entity grid model.
3.  Have you experimented with or considered alternative ways of incorporating entity-specific features, such as having separate embedding lookups for roles and features and then combining the resulting vectors (e.g., by concatenation or addition)? This could potentially offer better generalization.
