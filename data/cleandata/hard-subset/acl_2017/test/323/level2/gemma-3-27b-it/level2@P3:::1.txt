This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on entity grids. The core idea is to move beyond discrete representations traditionally used with entity grids and leverage distributed representations to capture long-range entity transitions and task-specific features. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking approach and evaluated on three standard coherence assessment tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results. The primary contribution lies in the successful application of a neural architecture to the entity grid framework, improving performance across multiple coherence tasks. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of effective end-to-end training for learning task-specific features within this framework.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing entity grid-based methods, and achieves state-of-the-art results on all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The reported gains, particularly on the summary coherence rating task (over 4%), are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2.  **Addresses Limitations of Existing Models:** The paper directly tackles the limitations of traditional entity grid models, specifically the reliance on discrete representations and the decoupling of feature computation from the target task. The use of distributed representations and end-to-end training effectively addresses these issues.
3.  **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper provides a clear rationale for the proposed architecture, connecting it to established theories of coherence (Centering Theory) and highlighting the advantages of neural networks for representing complex relationships. The presentation of the model is also quite clear, with a helpful diagram illustrating the architecture and training process.
4.  **Code Release:** Releasing the code is a valuable contribution that facilitates reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper presents results for both Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN, a more detailed ablation study would be beneficial. For example, the impact of different convolutional filter sizes, pooling strategies, or entity-specific features could be explored more systematically. Determining the contribution of each element of the architecture would strengthen the analysis.
2.  **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The hyperparameter tuning process is described somewhat briefly. Providing more details about the search space and the rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters would increase the reproducibility of the results. The influence of each hyperparameter on the final performance could also be discussed.
3.  **Comparison to Non-Grid Based Models:** The paper primarily compares against other entity grid-based models. A comparison to more recent neural coherence models that *don't* rely on entity grids (e.g., those using transformers) would help contextualize the performance of the proposed approach and identify potential areas for improvement.
4.  **Dataset limitations:** The summary coherence rating task uses a relatively small dataset. The performance improvements on this task, while encouraging, should be interpreted cautiously given the limited data.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choices made during hyperparameter tuning, specifically regarding the filter sizes and pooling strategies used in the convolutional layers?
2.  The paper mentions that the pre-trained models improve performance on the summary coherence rating task. What is the extent of this improvement, and how does it vary depending on the size of the pre-training dataset?
3.  Have you experimented with different methods for incorporating entity-specific features, beyond the three features mentioned in the paper? If so, what were the results?
4.  How does the model perform when evaluated on datasets with more complex or diverse writing styles?
