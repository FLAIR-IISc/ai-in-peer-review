This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on entity grids. The authors claim to capture long-range entity transitions and entity-specific features, achieving state-of-the-art results on three coherence assessment tasks.

**Contributions:**

1. **Neural Entity Grid Representation:** The primary contribution is the adaptation of the entity grid formalism to a neural network architecture. This allows for distributed representations of entity transitions, potentially mitigating the curse of dimensionality inherent in traditional discrete representations.
2. **End-to-End Training:** The authors propose an end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking, enabling the model to learn task-specific features automatically. This is a significant improvement over previous approaches where feature computation was decoupled from the target task.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing entity grid models and achieves state-of-the-art results on three coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses Limitations of Existing Models:** The paper clearly identifies and addresses key limitations of traditional entity grid models – discrete representations and task-agnostic feature computation. The proposed neural approach directly tackles these issues.
2. **Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The model is evaluated on three diverse tasks, providing a comprehensive assessment of its performance. The consistent improvements over strong baselines demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. Reporting absolute gains, rather than just relative improvements, is commendable.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The architecture is clearly explained with a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The motivation and related work are well-presented.
4. **Code Release:** Providing the code alongside the submission enhances reproducibility and facilitates further research.
5. **Statistical Significance:** The authors perform statistical significance tests which bolster the validity of their claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of techniques is valuable, individual components like convolutional neural networks and pairwise ranking are well-established. The core novelty lies in applying these to the entity grid framework. The paper doesn't sufficiently emphasize *why* this combination is particularly effective beyond stating it overcomes limitations of the grid.
2. **Entity-Specific Feature Incorporation is Basic:**  The incorporation of entity-specific features (Section 3.1) seems somewhat limited. The authors acknowledge this by only including three features. Exploring more sophisticated feature engineering or learning entity embeddings could potentially yield further improvements.  The impact of these features is not deeply analyzed.
3. **Insufficient Detail on Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, the justification for specific choices is lacking. Providing more detail on the tuning process (e.g., learning curves) would strengthen the empirical analysis.
4. **Comparison to More Recent Neural Coherence Models:** The related work section, while adequate, could benefit from discussing more recent neural approaches to coherence modeling *beyond* Li & Hovy (2014). There's a rapidly evolving landscape in neural discourse modeling, and situating the work more precisely within that context is important.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on quantitative results. A qualitative error analysis – examining specific examples where the model fails – could provide valuable insights into its strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Why were convolutional neural networks chosen over recurrent neural networks (RNNs) for modeling entity transitions? Did you experiment with RNNs, and if so, what were the results?
2. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind selecting the specific entity-specific features used in Section 3.1? Were other features considered, and if so, why were they excluded?
3.  The paper mentions pre-training on the WSJ corpus. How sensitive are the results to the pre-training data? Would results degrade if the model were trained only on the target task dataset?
4. Could you provide some example cases where your model performs particularly well, and cases where it fails, with a discussion of the potential reasons for the successes and failures?
