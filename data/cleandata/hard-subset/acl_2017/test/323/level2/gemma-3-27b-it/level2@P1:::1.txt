This paper proposes a neural local coherence model based on convolutional neural networks operating on entity grids. The authors aim to address limitations of traditional entity grid models, specifically the use of discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation. They introduce an end-to-end trainable model that leverages distributed representations and convolutional layers to capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features. The model is evaluated on three coherence assessment tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – achieving state-of-the-art results.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of a convolutional neural network architecture to model local coherence using entity grids, demonstrating significant performance improvements over existing non-neural models. A secondary, yet valuable, contribution is the end-to-end training approach, allowing the model to learn task-specific features directly from the data, alleviating the need for hand-engineered features. Finally, the release of the code is a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art results:** The model consistently outperforms existing entity grid models across all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) by a considerable margin (up to 4% absolute gain on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary coherence rating). This is a strong indicator of the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported gains are substantial and demonstrate a meaningful improvement in coherence assessment.
2. **Effective use of CNNs for entity grid representation:** The adoption of convolutional neural networks to process the entity grid representation is a clever idea. CNNs are well-suited for capturing local patterns and dependencies, which aligns well with the concept of local coherence. The use of filters to model entity transitions of varying lengths addresses the limitations of traditional grid models that struggle with longer-range dependencies.
3. **End-to-end training:** Training the model in an end-to-end fashion allows it to learn task-specific features, which is a significant advantage over traditional approaches where feature extraction is decoupled from the target task. This approach simplifies the modeling process and potentially leads to better performance.
4. **Code release:** The authors have released their code, which encourages reproducibility and further research in this area. This is a valuable contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited explanation of hyperparameter choices:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning using a development set, but the justification for the specific values chosen (e.g., filter sizes, dropout rates) is lacking. A more detailed discussion of how these parameters were selected and their impact on performance would strengthen the paper.
2. **Comparison with recent neural coherence models:** While the authors compare their model to traditional entity grid methods, they do not adequately address more recent neural approaches to coherence modeling (e.g., those based on recurrent neural networks or transformers). A comparison with these methods would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach. The mention of Li & Hovy (2014) and its subsequent dismissal feels incomplete. More detail on *why* it performed poorly would be valuable.
3. **Limited ablation study:** The paper presents results for both Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN, but a more thorough ablation study would be helpful to understand the contribution of each component (e.g., the impact of entity-specific features). Specifically, analyzing the impact of *different* entity-specific features, rather than a bundled set, would provide more insight.
4. **Dataset limitations:** The reliance on relatively small datasets (particularly for summary coherence rating) raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, exploring the performance of the model on larger and more diverse datasets would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters?
2. How does your model compare to more recent neural coherence models based on recurrent neural networks or transformers?
3. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to assess the contribution of each component of the Extended Grid-CNN model, specifically analyzing different entity-specific features?
4. Have you considered evaluating your model on larger and more diverse datasets? If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the context of recent advances in neural coherence modeling.
* The figures in the paper are helpful, but could be improved by providing more detailed explanations of the different components of the model.
* The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are a bit convoluted and could be rephrased for clarity.
