## Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

This paper presents a neural network architecture for assessing text coherence, building upon the well-established entity grid framework. The core idea is to leverage distributed representations and convolutional neural networks to capture long-range entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features, trained end-to-end. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence assessment tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – reporting state-of-the-art results.

The most significant contribution of this work is the successful application of a convolutional neural network to the entity grid representation. This allows for modeling longer-range dependencies than traditional methods that rely on discrete probabilities and fixed-length transitions. A second noteworthy contribution is the end-to-end training approach, which enables the model to learn task-specific features automatically, rather than relying on hand-engineered features. Finally, the empirical results demonstrate a clear performance improvement over existing models across all three evaluation tasks.

The strengths of this paper lie in its solid experimental evaluation and clear presentation. The authors provide a thorough comparison against several baselines, including the widely-used extended entity grid model. The reported gains on all three tasks are substantial and statistically significant. The inclusion of code availability is also a major positive aspect. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The motivation for *why* certain hyperparameters were chosen is lacking. While the authors mention tuning hyperparameters on a development set, the reasoning behind the specific values selected isn’t discussed. Furthermore, the impact of different entity-specific features isn't thoroughly investigated. The authors mention incorporating only three features for simplicity, but a more detailed ablation study could reveal their relative importance. Finally, the discussion of limitations is brief.  A more nuanced discussion of potential failure cases and the model's sensitivity to coreference resolution errors would strengthen the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more insight into the hyperparameter tuning process? What were the key observations that led to the chosen values?
2. Did you experiment with different combinations of entity-specific features? If so, could you share the results?
3. How robust is your model to errors in the coreference resolution step? Have you considered incorporating uncertainty from the coreference resolver into your model?
