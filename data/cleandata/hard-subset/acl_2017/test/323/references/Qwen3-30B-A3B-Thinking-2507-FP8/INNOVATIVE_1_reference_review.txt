

# ACL Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural network-based extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008) represents text as a grid capturing how entities transition across sentences, using discrete representations for grammatical roles. The authors argue this model has limitations: discrete representation leads to the curse of dimensionality (limiting transition length to k≤3), and feature computation is decoupled from the target task. Their solution uses a CNN-based architecture that:
1. Replaces discrete representations with distributed vectors
2. Models longer entity transitions (k=5-8) using convolutional layers
3. Incorporates entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention)
4. Trains end-to-end using pairwise ranking

They evaluate on three standard tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence) and report consistent improvements over non-neural entity grid models (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, 4%+ on summary coherence).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Neural extension of entity grid model**: Demonstrating how to use CNNs to model longer entity transitions (k=5-8) than previous discrete-based approaches (k≤3)
2. **End-to-end training framework**: A pairwise ranking approach for coherence scoring that learns task-specific features automatically
3. **Consistent performance improvements**: Demonstrating measurable gains across multiple coherence assessment tasks

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The neural architecture is well-motivated and technically sound. The use of convolutional layers to model entity transitions is a natural fit for the problem, and the paper provides a clear explanation of how the architecture works. The pairwise training approach aligns well with the ranking nature of coherence assessment. The paper also provides a thorough ablation study (Grid-CNN vs Extended Grid-CNN) to demonstrate the value of different components.

2. **Clear Evaluation Framework**: The paper uses the standard evaluation tasks for coherence assessment (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) and provides a thorough comparison with multiple baselines. The justification for using the WSJ corpus (larger size, more natural style) over smaller, constrained corpora (AIRPLANES, EARTHQUAKES) is convincing.

3. **Consistent Performance Gains**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements (4-5%) over the state-of-the-art entity grid models across multiple tasks. While the magnitude of improvement is modest, the consistency across different evaluation metrics is noteworthy. The statistical significance testing (p<0.01) adds credibility to the results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty**: The paper's main contribution is a neural extension of an existing framework (entity grid model). While technically sound, this is a relatively straightforward application of neural networks to an existing problem rather than a truly novel or transformative approach. The authors don't convincingly argue why this is a significant conceptual leap beyond previous work (e.g., Li and Hovy, 2014). The paper reads more as an incremental engineering improvement than a bold new direction for coherence modeling.

2. **Incremental Improvement Magnitude**: The reported improvements (4-5%) are modest in the context of NLP research. In many NLP tasks, improvements of this magnitude are considered incremental, especially when the baseline is already strong. The paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why this specific 4-5% gain represents a significant step forward rather than a marginal improvement.

3. **Lack of Conceptual Depth**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of what the neural model has learned. For example, it doesn't show which specific transition patterns the model identifies as coherent, or how these patterns compare to the patterns identified by human judges. This is particularly important given the paper's claim about modeling "long-range" coherence patterns. Without this analysis, it's difficult to assess whether the model is truly capturing meaningful coherence signals or simply learning superficial patterns.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their model can capture longer entity transitions (k=5-8), but doesn't clearly explain why this is a critical improvement for coherence modeling. Could you provide more detail on why transitions of length 5-8 are particularly important for coherence, and how they relate to existing theories of coherence?

2. The paper dismisses the Li and Hovy (2014) model as "very disappointing" (19.34% accuracy) without clear explanation. Given that Li and Hovy designed their model specifically for coherence assessment, could you provide more insight into why it performed so poorly on your specific dataset?

3. The paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model, but doesn't address whether the entity grid representation itself might be limiting. Are there cases where the entity grid representation might fail to capture important coherence signals, and how would your neural model handle those cases? Could a more fundamental shift in representation be more beneficial than just neuralizing the existing one?

## Additional Comments

- The paper would have been strengthened by providing more detailed analysis of the model's behavior (e.g., visualization of learned transition patterns, case studies of coherent/incoherent text examples).
- The comparison to Li and Hovy (2014) is somewhat problematic as they dismiss it without clear explanation, which could undermine their credibility.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently articulate why this work represents a significant step forward rather than just an incremental improvement to an existing framework.

## Final Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents a technically sound neural extension of the entity grid model with consistent (though modest) improvements over existing methods, it lacks the significant novelty and boldness that would justify acceptance at ACL. The contribution is an incremental improvement to an existing framework rather than a truly novel concept or direction. The improvements (4-5%) are valuable but not transformative, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this represents a significant step forward in coherence modeling.

The paper reads like a natural next step in the evolution of entity grid models rather than a groundbreaking new direction. Given ACL's emphasis on novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper falls short of the threshold for acceptance. The technical execution is good, but the conceptual contribution is insufficient to warrant publication at ACL.
