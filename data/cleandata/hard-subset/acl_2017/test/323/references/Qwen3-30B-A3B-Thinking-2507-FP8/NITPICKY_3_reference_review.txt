

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural architecture for text coherence assessment that extends the entity grid model. The authors address two limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) discrete representation leading to curse of dimensionality, and (2) task-agnostic feature computation. They introduce a convolutional neural network that operates over distributed representations of entity transitions, allowing for longer transitions and end-to-end learning of task-specific features. The authors evaluate their model on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating tasks, reporting improvements over non-neural entity grid models.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural extension of the entity grid model using convolutional layers to capture long-range entity transitions
2. An end-to-end training approach for learning task-specific features automatically
3. Demonstration of consistent improvements over existing entity grid models across multiple coherence evaluation tasks

## Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Statement**: The paper effectively identifies limitations of existing entity grid models (discrete representation leading to curse of dimensionality, and task-agnostic feature extraction), providing a strong motivation for their neural approach.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors evaluate on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) with multiple strong baselines, providing a thorough assessment of their model's performance.

3. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is logically organized, with clear explanations of the background, proposed method, evaluation tasks, and results. Figure 2 effectively illustrates the neural architecture.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading Claims About Results**: The paper claims "absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating." However, the actual gains are:
   - Discrimination: 3.74% (88.69% vs 84.95%)
   - Insertion: 3.74% (88.69% vs 84.95%)
   - Summary coherence rating: 2.67% (25.95% vs 23.28%)
   
   These discrepancies are significant - the paper claims "more than 4%" for summary coherence rating when the actual gain is 2.67%, and the insertion gain (3.74%) is larger than the claimed "up to 2.5%." This is a serious issue as it misrepresents the results.

2. **Inconsistent Description of Entity-Specific Features**: In Section 3.1, the authors describe encoding entity-specific features directly into grid entries (e.g., PERSON-S), but in Section 5.1, they state they use three entity-specific features as separate inputs to the model. This creates confusion about how these features are actually incorporated into the neural model. The paper does not clarify this critical technical detail.

3. **Confusing Evaluation Metrics**: For the insertion task, the paper states "the insertion score is then computed as the average fraction of sentences per document reinserted in their actual position." This is a fraction between 0 and 1, but Table 3 reports it as a percentage (e.g., 88.69). This inconsistency in reporting metrics is confusing and unprofessional.

4. **Inconsistent Hyperparameter Selection**: Table 2 reports different batch sizes (128 for Grid-CNN, 32 for Extended Grid-CNN), but the paper doesn't explain why these different values were chosen or if they were tuned. This is a minor but important detail for reproducibility.

5. **Inconsistent Reference Formatting**: The references contain formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Barzilay and Lapata.,? 2005" with an extra comma and question mark), indicating a lack of attention to detail that is expected in ACL publications.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you clarify the inconsistency between Section 3.1 (encoding entity-specific features directly into grid entries) and Section 5.1 (using entity-specific features as separate inputs to the model)? How exactly are these features incorporated into your neural architecture?

2. The paper claims "absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination" but the actual gain is 3.74%. Similarly, for summary coherence rating, the paper claims "more than 4%" but the actual gain is 2.67%. Can you explain these discrepancies and revise the claims to reflect the actual results?

3. For the insertion task, the paper defines the metric as "the average fraction of sentences per document reinserted in their actual position" (a value between 0 and 1), but Table 3 reports it as a percentage (e.g., 88.69). Why is this metric reported as a percentage rather than a fraction? This is confusing for readers.

4. Why did you choose a batch size of 128 for Grid-CNN and 32 for Extended Grid-CNN? Was this a hyperparameter you tuned, and if so, why did it differ between the two models?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper presents a reasonable extension of existing entity grid models and demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines, the technical inconsistencies and misleading claims about results are significant concerns. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot overlook these issues. The discrepancies in reported results (claiming "more than 4%" when the actual gain is 2.67%) are particularly problematic, as they misrepresent the paper's contributions. The inconsistent description of how entity-specific features are incorporated into the model also creates confusion about the technical approach.

These minor but important flaws indicate a lack of attention to detail that is expected in ACL publications. While the core idea is reasonable, the paper's presentation and technical execution fall short of the standards required for acceptance at ACL. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before considering resubmission.
