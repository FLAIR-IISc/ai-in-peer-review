

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural network-based extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors replace the discrete representation and feature extraction of the traditional entity grid model with a neural architecture that uses distributed representations and convolutional layers to model entity transitions. They evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) and report modest improvements over existing entity grid models.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural architecture for coherence modeling that addresses limitations of the traditional entity grid model by using distributed representations and convolutional layers to model longer entity transitions.
2. An end-to-end training method that learns task-specific features automatically.
3. Evaluation showing consistent improvements over non-neural entity grid models across multiple coherence tasks.

## Strengths
1. **Clear problem identification**: The paper effectively identifies the limitations of existing entity grid models (curse of dimensionality and task-agnostic feature extraction) and presents a logical solution to address them. The authors provide a clear motivation for why the neural approach is needed.

2. **Thorough evaluation**: The evaluation covers three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) with appropriate baselines and statistical analysis. The authors also provide careful analysis of the results across different tasks.

3. **Well-organized and clear presentation**: The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the methodology, results, and evaluation tasks. The technical details are presented in a logical flow that makes the paper easy to follow.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of significant novelty**: This is the most critical weakness. The paper proposes a straightforward application of convolutional neural networks to an existing coherence modeling framework. While the authors claim to address limitations of the entity grid model, they're simply applying a standard neural technique to a specific problem. This is not a bold new concept or direction but rather a standard incremental improvement. Many previous papers (e.g., Collobert et al., 2011; Kim, 2014) have applied CNNs to text modeling, and this work doesn't introduce any novel neural architecture or training technique. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty, I find this limitation particularly significant.

2. **Modest performance gains**: The reported improvements over existing models are modest (2-4% absolute gains), which is not particularly significant in NLP. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why these gains are meaningful in practical terms, especially considering the additional complexity of the neural model. For comparison, many state-of-the-art NLP papers report gains of 5-10% or more on standard benchmarks.

3. **No theoretical or conceptual advancement**: The paper doesn't provide new insights into how text coherence works or fundamentally change our understanding of coherence modeling. The neural approach is simply a different implementation of the same underlying concept as the entity grid model, not a new conceptual framework. This is a significant limitation for a paper that claims to advance the field.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your neural approach fundamentally change our understanding of text coherence compared to the existing entity grid models? What new insights does it provide about coherence that the previous models couldn't capture?

2. The paper claims to model "long range entity transitions" (up to 5-8 length), but doesn't provide strong evidence that these longer transitions are actually beneficial for coherence modeling. Can you provide more analysis on why longer transitions are important for coherence and how they contribute to the observed improvements?

3. The improvements over existing models are modest (2-4% absolute gains). How do you justify these gains as significant in practical terms, especially considering the computational cost of training neural models compared to the simpler entity grid approach?

## Recommendation
Reject

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper's contribution to be insufficiently novel. The approach is a straightforward application of existing neural techniques to a specific problem in coherence modeling, not a significant new concept or direction. While the methodology is sound and the results are consistent, the lack of substantial novelty means this work represents an incremental improvement rather than a breakthrough.

The paper doesn't introduce a new conceptual framework for coherence modeling, doesn't present a fundamentally different approach to the problem, and doesn't provide compelling evidence that the modest improvements justify the additional complexity of the neural model. For an ACL paper, this level of novelty is insufficient to warrant acceptance. The paper would be more appropriate for a venue that prioritizes methodological soundness over novelty, but not for ACL where we seek bold new directions in the field.
