

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network extension of the established entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors claim to address two limitations of the entity grid approach: (1) its discrete representation of grammatical roles that limits modeling of long transitions (k ≤ 3), and (2) its decoupled feature computation from the target task. They propose a convolutional neural network that operates on the entity grid to learn distributed representations of entity transitions and entity-specific features.

From my perspective as a skeptical reviewer, the primary contribution appears to be a minor extension of an established framework rather than a truly novel approach. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that a neural approach is fundamentally necessary for the coherence modeling task, nor does it provide sufficient evidence that the improvements over the entity grid model are substantial enough to warrant the increased complexity.

## Strengths

1. **Thorough evaluation on standard tasks**: The paper evaluates on three well-established coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) using standard datasets (WSJ and DUC'03). The experimental setup is clear, and the evaluation protocols follow established conventions in the field.

2. **Clear presentation of the neural architecture**: The authors provide a detailed explanation of how their neural model relates to the entity grid model, making it easy to understand the connection between the traditional approach and their proposed extension.

3. **Attention to practical implementation details**: The paper includes comprehensive hyperparameter tuning information and thorough ablation studies (Grid-CNN vs. Extended Grid-CNN), which demonstrates attention to methodological rigor.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of convincing evidence for neural necessity**: The authors claim that the entity grid model's limitation to k ≤ 3 is due to the "curse of dimensionality" (Bengio et al., 2003). However, they don't provide empirical evidence that this limitation is fundamental or that a neural approach is required to overcome it. The gains reported (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion) are modest, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that these gains would be consistently replicable across different datasets or tasks. The authors should provide a thorough analysis of whether a non-neural extension of the entity grid (e.g., with better feature engineering) could achieve similar results without the added complexity of a neural model.

2. **Inadequate comparison with relevant baselines**: The paper dismisses Li and Hovy (2014) without proper analysis, claiming their results "were very disappointing" (19.34% accuracy in discrimination). This is a significant oversight, as Li and Hovy (2014) represents a more recent neural approach to coherence modeling. The authors don't provide a detailed explanation for why their approach would work better than Li and Hovy's, nor do they compare their model to other neural coherence models. This weakens their claim of achieving "state-of-the-art" results.

3. **Overstated novelty and significance**: The paper claims that their approach "obtains generalization through distributed representations of entity transitions" and "presents an end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high level features automatically." These claims are overstated, as the end-to-end training approach is essentially the same as what Barzilay and Lapata (2008) used (SVM preference ranking). The paper fails to demonstrate how their neural approach fundamentally differs from the existing entity grid model in a way that justifies the additional complexity.

4. **Lack of ablation studies on key components**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to determine which components of their neural architecture are most responsible for the improvements. For example, it's unclear whether the improvements come from the convolutional layers, the distributed representations, or the combination of both. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of how each component contributes to the performance gains.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of their approach. For instance, they claim their model can handle longer transitions (k=5-8), but don't provide evidence that these longer transitions are actually meaningful for coherence assessment. They also don't discuss the computational cost of their approach compared to the entity grid model, which is a significant consideration for practical applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the entity grid model is limited to k ≤ 3 due to the "curse of dimensionality." Can you provide empirical evidence that this limitation is fundamental and that your neural approach is the only viable way to overcome it? Have you considered alternative non-neural approaches to model longer transitions?

2. You dismiss Li and Hovy (2014) because it performed poorly on your dataset. What specific issues do you think caused this poor performance? Could you provide more detailed analysis to explain why your approach would be expected to perform better, rather than simply dismissing their work?

3. How much of the performance gain (4% on discrimination) is attributable to the neural architecture versus simply using a larger k (which you claim is now possible with your approach)? Could you provide an ablation study comparing your model with k=5 to a non-neural model with k=5?

4. The paper claims "state-of-the-art results" on all three tasks, but doesn't compare against all relevant state-of-the-art models. Specifically, how does your model compare to the recent work by Feng et al. (2014) which also incorporates discourse relations? This is a significant omission for a paper claiming to achieve state-of-the-art results.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a clear and methodologically sound extension of the entity grid model, the claimed contributions are not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant publication in ACL. The modest performance gains (4% absolute improvement) do not justify the added complexity of a neural approach, especially when the authors don't provide convincing evidence that a neural approach is necessary. The paper would be significantly stronger if it provided a more rigorous analysis of why a neural approach is required, a more comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines, and a deeper understanding of which components of the model are driving the performance gains.

Given my preference for established methods and skepticism toward new approaches without extensive evidence, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. The authors should substantially strengthen their justification for the neural approach and provide more thorough validation before this work could be considered for publication.
