

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural architecture for text coherence modeling that extends the popular entity grid framework (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors replace the discrete, hand-crafted feature representation of traditional entity grid models with a neural network that uses distributed representations and convolutional layers to capture longer-range entity transitions (up to length 5-8, compared to the traditional limit of k ≤ 3). The model also incorporates entity-specific features (like named entity types) in a more flexible way than previous work. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), reporting consistent improvements of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating over existing entity grid models.

## Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by strength)

1. **Novel neural architecture for overcoming the curse of dimensionality in entity grid models**: The paper's most significant contribution is addressing the fundamental limitation of existing entity grid models (the curse of dimensionality that restricts transition length to k ≤ 3) by using distributed representations and convolutional layers. This enables modeling longer-range coherence patterns (k=5-8) without exponential parameter growth, which is a bold conceptual advance.

2. **End-to-end training for task-specific feature learning**: The authors present an end-to-end training method that automatically learns task-specific high-level features, moving beyond the task-agnostic feature extraction of previous work. This represents a significant methodological shift in coherence modeling.

3. **Effective incorporation of entity-specific features**: The paper demonstrates how to incorporate entity-specific features (like named entity types) without the exponential parameter growth that plagued previous extensions of the entity grid model.

## Strengths

1. **Bold conceptual contribution addressing a fundamental limitation**: The paper identifies and solves a clear, long-standing limitation of the entity grid framework (the curse of dimensionality limiting transition length to k ≤ 3). By using distributed representations and convolutional layers, the authors enable modeling of longer coherence patterns (k=5-8), which is a significant conceptual advance. This is not just an incremental improvement but a fundamental change in how coherence models can be designed.

2. **Strong empirical results with consistent improvements**: The model achieves state-of-the-art results across all three evaluation tasks with significant improvements (4-5% absolute gains) over previous entity grid models. The improvements are consistent across tasks, which provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.

3. **Clear evaluation framework across multiple tasks**: The paper evaluates the model on three different coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), which provides a comprehensive assessment of the model's capabilities. This is more thorough than previous work that often evaluated on only one or two tasks.

4. **Practical implementation and code release**: The authors have released their code with the submission, which is a significant contribution to the community and demonstrates their commitment to reproducibility.

5. **Addressing a meaningful problem**: Coherence modeling is a well-established problem with wide-ranging applications in text generation, summarization, and readability assessment. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to this important area.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental nature of the approach**: While the neural architecture is novel, the overall approach still builds on the entity grid framework that has been used for over a decade. The paper doesn't introduce a completely new way of modeling coherence but rather "neuralizes" an existing framework. This limits the novelty of the contribution, though the specific solution to the curse of dimensionality is significant.

2. **Lack of comparison with more recent neural approaches**: The paper doesn't compare with the neural coherence model proposed by Li and Hovy (2014), which the authors mention they tried but got poor results on. The paper doesn't explain why this happened or how their approach differs fundamentally from Li and Hovy's. This is a significant gap, as it leaves open the question of whether their approach is truly better than other neural approaches.

3. **Limited ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of each component of their model. For example, they don't show how much the improvement comes from the neural representation vs. the end-to-end training vs. the incorporation of entity-specific features. This makes it hard to determine which aspects of their approach are truly novel and beneficial.

4. **Questionable significance of improvements**: While the paper reports absolute gains of 4-5%, these gains are not particularly large in the context of coherence modeling. Previous work has shown that coherence modeling is a challenging task, and gains of 4-5% are not uncommon. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why these gains are significant enough to warrant a major contribution.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, it doesn't address how their model might perform on more challenging coherence tasks or whether the improvements are consistent across different genres of text.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that they tried the neural coherence model proposed by Li and Hovy (2014) but got poor results (19.34% accuracy in discrimination). Why did this happen? Is there something specific about their dataset or implementation that caused this, or is the Li and Hovy approach fundamentally not suitable for their task? If the latter, why?

2. The paper claims that their model can capture "long-range entity transitions" (up to k=5-8), but how do they justify that these longer transitions actually capture meaningful coherence patterns? What evidence do they have that these longer transitions are more informative than shorter ones?

3. Could the authors provide more insight into what the model has learned about coherence? For example, could they share some examples of entity transitions that the model identifies as particularly important for coherence?

4. How does the computational complexity of their approach compare to previous entity grid models? Given that they're using a CNN with multiple layers, how does the training and inference time compare to previous methods?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the approach builds on an existing framework (entity grid), the authors have identified and solved a fundamental limitation of that framework (the curse of dimensionality) through a bold and novel application of neural networks. The improvements over previous work are significant (4-5% absolute gains), consistent across multiple tasks, and the paper presents a clear conceptual advance in how coherence models can be designed.

The paper's main weakness is that it's building on a framework that's been around for over a decade, but the specific solution to the curse of dimensionality problem represents a meaningful conceptual contribution that is not just an incremental improvement. As a reviewer who values novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper to be a significant contribution to the field of coherence modeling.

The paper would be strengthened by additional ablation studies, comparisons with more recent neural approaches, and a deeper analysis of what the model has learned about coherence. However, these are minor issues that could be addressed in the camera-ready version, and they don't outweigh the paper's significant conceptual contribution.
