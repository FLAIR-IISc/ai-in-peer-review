- Paper proposes a neural local coherence model that extends the entity grid by using distributed representations and convolutional layers to model longer entity transitions and is trained end-to-end with pairwise ranking.  
- Main contributions: (1) neural architecture avoiding curse of dimensionality to model longer transitions (5–8), (2) end-to-end task-specific feature learning via pairwise ranking, (3) comprehensive evaluation on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating showing consistent gains.

- Strength: Clearly identifies the curse-of-dimensionality limitation of discrete entity grids and motivates distributed representations with theoretical support.  
- Strength: Comprehensive, well-designed evaluation on three standard tasks with a larger WSJ dataset and statistically significant improvements (≈4% discrimination, 2.5% insertion, >4% summary rating).  
- Strength: Strong empirical gains and evidence that pre-training on discrimination helps transfer to summary coherence.

- Weakness: Fails to thoroughly compare with or analyze related neural work (Li & Hovy 2014), weakening claims of novelty.  
- Weakness: Lacks qualitative analysis of what convolutional filters learn or concrete examples of detected transition patterns.  
- Weakness: Overstates "state-of-the-art" claims by comparing mainly to entity-grid and graph models and not to recent neural approaches.  
- Weakness: No discussion of computational complexity or training/inference cost implications of modeling longer transitions.

- Question: Why did the Li & Hovy (2014) implementation perform poorly (19.34%); were hyperparameters or alternative implementations tried?  
- Question: Can the authors provide examples/tables of transition patterns learned by convolutional filters and their correlation with coherence?  
- Question: Can the authors report discrimination-task performance as a function of transition length k (k=2..8) to demonstrate benefits of longer transitions?

- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — solid, well-motivated contributions and convincing results but needs better comparison to prior neural work and qualitative/complexity analyses.
