

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors address two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) discrete representation leading to the curse of dimensionality (limiting transition length to k ≤ 3), and (2) task-agnostic feature extraction. Their approach uses distributed representations of grammatical roles and convolutional layers to model longer entity transitions (k=5-8), while incorporating entity-specific features and enabling end-to-end training for task-specific features.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Neural architecture for entity grid modeling that enables longer-range transition modeling** - This is the most significant contribution, as it directly addresses a fundamental limitation of the entity grid model that has constrained the field for years. By using convolutional layers on distributed representations, the authors can model transitions of length 5-8 without exponential parameter growth.

2. **Incorporating entity-specific features in a neural framework** - The paper demonstrates how to include entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention) without the exponential parameter growth that plagues traditional entity grid models.

3. **End-to-end training for coherence assessment** - The model learns task-specific features automatically through pairwise ranking training, eliminating the need for task-agnostic feature engineering.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a fundamental limitation of the entity grid model**: The paper correctly identifies the "curse of dimensionality" as a key limitation of existing coherence models (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008; Elsner and Charniak, 2011), which has constrained transition lengths to k ≤ 3. By using distributed representations and convolutional layers, the authors enable modeling of longer entity transitions (k=5-8), which is a significant technical advance. This directly addresses a long-standing limitation in the coherence modeling field.

2. **Strong empirical results with consistent improvements**: The paper demonstrates clear and significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art models across all three evaluation tasks. The absolute gains of ~4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating are substantial in the coherence modeling field, where improvements are often incremental (typically <2%).

3. **Clear problem statement and well-designed evaluation**: The authors properly identify the limitations of existing approaches and evaluate on standard, well-established tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating). The evaluation is thorough, with appropriate baselines and statistical significance testing.

4. **Practical implementation and code release**: The authors have released their code with the submission, which is a significant contribution to the community and facilitates reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of fundamental novelty in the neural approach**: While the application of CNNs to the entity grid for coherence modeling is valuable, the neural architecture itself is not novel. Convolutional neural networks have been widely used in NLP for text classification (Kim, 2014) and sentence modeling (Kalchbrenner et al., 2014). The paper doesn't introduce any new neural architecture or training technique; it simply applies existing CNN techniques to a new problem. This is an incremental application of a known technique rather than a truly novel neural approach.

2. **Insufficient exploration of why the model works**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the specific contribution of each component (distributed representations vs. longer transitions vs. end-to-end training). It's unclear whether the improvements are due to the longer transition modeling, the distributed representations, or the end-to-end training. Without this, it's difficult to understand the true contribution of the work.

3. **Limited comparison to recent neural coherence work**: The paper mentions Li and Hovy (2014) but dismisses it without sufficient justification. The authors state that "the results on our dataset were very disappointing – accuracy of only 19.34% in discrimination," but they don't explain why this neural model performed poorly on their dataset. A more thorough comparison to this recent neural coherence model would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty.

4. **Failure to question the entity grid framework itself**: The paper focuses on extending the entity grid model rather than questioning whether this is the right approach for coherence modeling. A more bold approach would have explored whether the entity grid framework is fundamentally limited for coherence modeling and proposed a completely different approach to coherence assessment.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." How did you select which features to include, and what would be the impact of including all 9 features used by Elsner and Charniak (2011)?

2. The paper mentions that the Extended Grid-CNN uses only 3 entity-specific features while the Extended Grid uses 9. How would performance change if the Extended Grid-CNN used all 9 features, and would the gains still be significant?

3. The paper states that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." Could you elaborate on why the neural model can learn more effectively from fewer features compared to the traditional grid model? Is this due to the distributed representation, the convolutional layers, or the end-to-end training?

4. The authors mention that "our models were not trained specifically on the insertion task." How would you expect the model to perform if trained directly on the insertion task, and what changes would be needed in the training procedure?

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the neural architecture itself is not fundamentally novel, the paper makes a significant contribution by applying existing neural techniques to address a long-standing limitation of the entity grid model (the curse of dimensionality) with substantial empirical improvements across all evaluation tasks. The paper is well-written, clearly articulates the problem, and provides strong empirical evidence of its approach's effectiveness. Given my preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection, this paper's clear contribution to overcoming a key limitation of the entity grid model outweighs the lack of fundamental novelty in the neural approach. The consistent improvements of 4%+ across multiple tasks are impressive and demonstrate the practical value of the approach.
