

# Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors argue that existing entity grid models are limited by their discrete representation of grammatical roles (which leads to the curse of dimensionality) and their task-agnostic feature extraction. Their neural model uses a convolutional architecture to process entity transitions and incorporates entity-specific features. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) and report improvements over existing entity grid models, with absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural architecture for local coherence modeling that extends the entity grid model using convolutional networks
2. An end-to-end training approach for learning task-specific features
3. Empirical validation showing consistent improvements over established entity grid models

## Strengths
1. **Clear motivation and problem statement**: The paper thoroughly explains the limitations of existing entity grid models, particularly the curse of dimensionality problem when modeling longer transitions. This provides a solid foundation for their neural approach.

2. **Standardized evaluation framework**: The authors use well-established evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence) and compare against strong baselines (the entity grid model and its extensions). The use of statistical significance testing (p < 0.01) adds rigor to their claims.

3. **Consistent and meaningful results**: The neural models consistently outperform non-neural baselines across all three evaluation tasks, with statistically significant improvements. The absolute gains (2.5-4%) are meaningful in the context of coherence modeling, especially since coherence assessment is a challenging task with diminishing returns.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach**: The paper essentially applies a standard convolutional neural network architecture to an existing entity grid model. The core idea of using convolution to model transitions is not novel in NLP (as evidenced by Kim, 2014; Kalchbrenner et al., 2014). The authors don't convincingly demonstrate why this neural approach is necessary or significantly better than the existing entity grid model. The claimed advantage of handling longer transitions (k=5-8) is not substantiated with evidence that the traditional model would fail at these lengths.

2. **Insufficient evidence for claimed advantages**: The authors claim their model avoids the curse of dimensionality problem, but they don't provide a comparison between traditional entity grid models with longer transition lengths (k=5-8) and their neural model. Without this comparison, it's impossible to validate their claim that the neural model's advantage comes from handling longer transitions rather than just learning better features.

3. **Inadequate comparison with more recent neural approaches**: The authors mention they tried the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014) but got "very disappointing" results (19.34% accuracy), yet they don't provide a detailed analysis of why this approach failed or how their model specifically addresses its limitations. They should have compared against more recent neural approaches to coherence modeling rather than just the entity grid model.

4. **Lack of analysis on when the neural approach fails**: The paper acknowledges that the model performs less well on the insertion task (which is harder than discrimination), but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of why the neural model might fail in certain scenarios. For example, they don't examine performance differences across text lengths or genres, which would be important for understanding the model's limitations.

5. **Questionable significance of improvements**: The reported gains (2.5-4%) are modest, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these improvements would translate to meaningful gains in downstream applications (e.g., text generation or summarization). The authors don't demonstrate how these small improvements would impact practical applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the neural model can handle longer transitions (k=5-8) without suffering from the curse of dimensionality, but doesn't provide results comparing traditional entity grid models with k=5-8 to their neural model. Can you provide these results to validate your claim?

2. The authors mention that they tried the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014) but got poor results. What specific issues did you encounter with this approach, and how does your model specifically address these issues? Can you provide a more detailed comparison between these two neural approaches?

3. The paper uses the WSJ corpus as the main dataset, which is larger than other corpora. However, the authors don't analyze whether the neural model's advantage is simply due to the larger training set. Can you provide results showing that the neural model's advantage persists even when comparing models trained on the same number of examples from smaller corpora?

4. The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the computational cost of the neural model compared to the traditional entity grid model. Can you provide details on training and inference times, memory requirements, and scalability of the neural model?

5. The authors claim their model learns task-specific features through end-to-end training, but they don't demonstrate how these learned features differ from those of the traditional model. Can you provide examples of specific features that the neural model learns that are not captured by the traditional approach?

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a well-structured and rigorously evaluated extension of an established coherence model, the claimed advantages are not sufficiently supported by the evidence. The approach lacks sufficient novelty, and the improvements over existing methods are modest without compelling justification for the increased complexity. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent neural approaches to coherence modeling and a stronger argument for why a neural model is necessary for this task.

The authors should either substantially strengthen their claims with additional evidence or consider submitting to a venue that values incremental improvements over novelty. As it stands, the paper does not make a compelling case for a new methodological contribution to the field.
