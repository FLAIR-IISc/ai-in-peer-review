

# ACL Review

## Summary of Paper and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural architecture for coherence assessment that operates over entity grids, aiming to overcome limitations of existing discrete-based entity grid models. The authors claim to address two main limitations of previous work: (1) the curse of dimensionality due to discrete representation of grammatical roles and features, and (2) the task-agnostic feature extraction in existing models. Their approach uses distributed representations and convolutional layers to model long-range entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features, with end-to-end training.

The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):

1. A neural architecture that can model long-range entity transitions (up to length 5-8) without suffering from the curse of dimensionality, unlike existing entity grid models that are limited to k ≤ 3.
2. An end-to-end training approach that allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically.
3. A thorough evaluation across three coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) showing consistent improvements over strong baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Novel neural approach to entity grid modeling**: The paper successfully demonstrates how to neuralize the entity grid model, which is a significant contribution to the coherence modeling literature. The use of convolutional layers to model long-range transitions (5-8) is a clear improvement over existing methods limited to short transitions (k ≤ 3).

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple tasks**: The authors evaluate their model on three different tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) using standard datasets. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach across different coherence assessment scenarios.

3. **Clear presentation of methodology**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of the neural architecture, including the transformation of grammatical roles, modeling of entity transitions, and coherence scoring. The technical descriptions are thorough and well-structured.

## Weaknesses

1. **Significant inconsistencies in reported results**: The paper claims absolute gains of "about 4% on discrimination" for Grid-CNN over Grid-all nouns, but the actual gain is 3.99% (85.57-81.58). Similarly, they claim "more gains (2.5%) in insertion" for Extended Grid-CNN over Extended Grid, but the actual gain is 3.74% (88.69-84.95). These discrepancies undermine the credibility of the reported results. The paper should be precise with numbers, especially when claiming "absolute gains of about 4%."

2. **Potential error in loss function**: The paper describes the loss function as J(θ) = max{0, 1-φ(Gi|θ)+φ(Gj|θ)}. However, this would be maximized when φ(Gi|θ) < φ(Gj|θ), which is the opposite of what the model should learn (they want φ(Gi|θ) > φ(Gj|θ) for correct ordering). This appears to be a sign error in the loss function that would significantly impact the training process. The authors should verify and correct this.

3. **Lack of proper comparison to a key related work**: The paper claims to be fundamentally different from Li and Hovy (2014), but they don't include this model in their experiments. They mention they "experimented with" it but got poor results (19.34% accuracy on discrimination, which is less than random). This is a major omission. They should either include this model in their comparison or thoroughly explain why their implementation failed (which is highly unusual for a model that performed well in the original paper).

4. **Multiple formatting and consistency issues**: There are several instances of inconsistent formatting in references (e.g., "Barzilay and Lapata.,? 2005" has a misplaced question mark and period), grammatical errors ("They match the nouns to detect coreferent entities"), and unclear terminology (the "Acc" column in Table 3 has values like 22.13, which don't match typical accuracy metrics). These minor but pervasive issues suggest a lack of attention to detail.

5. **Lack of code availability**: The paper states "We have released our code with the submission," but doesn't provide a URL or link to the code repository. This is a requirement for ACL papers and makes reproducibility difficult. The authors should provide a link to their code.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination" for Grid-CNN over Grid-all nouns, but the actual gain is 3.99% (85.57-81.58). Similarly, the claimed gain of 2.5% in insertion for Extended Grid-CNN over Extended Grid is inconsistent with the actual gain of 3.74% (88.69-84.95). Could you clarify these discrepancies?

2. The loss function is defined as J(θ) = max{0, 1-φ(Gi|θ)+φ(Gj|θ)}. However, this would maximize when φ(Gi|θ) < φ(Gj|θ), which is the opposite of what the model should learn. Could you verify the correctness of this loss function and provide a corrected version?

3. You mention that you "experimented with the distributed sentence model proposed by Li and Hovy (2014)" but got only 19.34% accuracy on discrimination, which is less than random (50%). Could you explain why this happened and whether there was an issue with your implementation or the dataset?

4. The paper states "We have released our code with the submission," but doesn't provide a URL. Could you please provide the URL to the code repository?

5. In Table 3, the "Acc" column has values like 22.13, 23.28, etc. Could you clarify what metric "Acc" represents? It doesn't appear to be accuracy (which should be between 0 and 100), and the values don't match the other metrics (Discr, Ins, F1).

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection due to the significant inconsistencies in reported results, the potential error in the loss function, and the lack of proper comparison to a key related work. These issues are not merely minor formatting errors but rather fundamental methodological problems that undermine the credibility of the paper's claims. The paper presents a potentially interesting approach to coherence modeling, but the lack of attention to detail in presenting the results and methodology makes it difficult to trust the findings. The minor formatting issues are consistent with a lack of careful proofreading, which further suggests that the authors may not have been meticulous enough in preparing the submission.

The paper's claims about the superiority of their model are not adequately supported by the presented results due to the discrepancies in reported gains. The potential error in the loss function could mean that the entire training process was flawed. The omission of a key related work (Li and Hovy, 2014) in the evaluation is a significant methodological gap that needs to be addressed.
