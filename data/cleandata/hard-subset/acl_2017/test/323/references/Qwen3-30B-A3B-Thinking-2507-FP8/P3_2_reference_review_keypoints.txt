- Proposes a neural convolutional model extending the entity grid to model local text coherence with distributed entity-transition representations and end-to-end pairwise ranking training.  
- Claims to overcome the entity-grid curse of dimensionality by modeling longer transitions (k=5–8) with convolution and max-pooling.  
- Integrates entity-specific features (e.g., named entity types) in distributed form to improve performance over discrete features.  
- Trains end-to-end so task-specific coherence features are learned automatically via pairwise ranking.  
- Evaluated on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating tasks, showing consistent improvements (≈4% discrimination, 2.5% insertion, >4% summary rating).  
- Uses WSJ and DUC'03 datasets to balance large-scale training and task-specific evaluation.  
- Strong technical motivation for using CNNs to capture long-range entity transitions and avoid overfitting.  
- Comprehensive evaluation across the three standard coherence tasks with improvements over strong baselines.  
- Clearly demonstrates neural advantages over discrete representations for modeling longer transitions.  
- Provides code, hyperparameters, and uses standard NLP tools, aiding reproducibility.  
- Situates work within coherence literature and cites relevant theoretical foundations (e.g., Centering Theory, entity grid).  
- Lacks a thorough ablation study isolating the impact of each entity-specific feature on performance.  
- Omits analysis explaining why Li and Hovy (2014) performed extremely poorly on their dataset, raising questions about the comparison.  
- Uses WSJ for training but DUC'03 for summary evaluation without analyzing domain mismatch or transferability.  
- Provides no interpretability evidence for claimed filter patterns nor failure analysis to show what the model learns or where it fails.  
- Possibly overstates novelty by framing as “neuralizing” entity grid without adequately comparing to other neural approaches.  
- Asks authors to explain Li and Hovy’s poor performance, training-on-WSJ vs DUC'03 choices, provide detailed ablation of entity features, give examples of learned k=5–8 patterns, and report number of seeds used for significance tests.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions, contingent on addressing ablation, Li & Hovy comparison, and interpretability/analysis concerns.
