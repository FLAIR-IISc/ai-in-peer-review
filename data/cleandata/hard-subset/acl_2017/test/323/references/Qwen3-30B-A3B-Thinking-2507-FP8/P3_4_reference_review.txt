

# Peer Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network architecture to improve upon the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors argue that existing entity grid models suffer from the curse of dimensionality due to discrete representations and task-agnostic feature extraction. Their neural model uses distributed representations of grammatical roles, convolutional layers to model longer entity transitions (5-8), and end-to-end training to learn task-specific features.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A neural architecture that successfully models long-range entity transitions (5-8) while avoiding the curse of dimensionality of traditional entity grid models
2. An end-to-end training approach that learns task-specific features automatically, eliminating the decoupled feature extraction of prior work
3. Strong empirical results across three standard coherence tasks showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art baselines

## Strengths

1. **Effective Neuralization of Entity Grid Model**: The paper successfully demonstrates how to neuralize the entity grid approach, which is a significant contribution. The convolutional architecture allows modeling longer entity transitions (5-8) compared to the typical k≤3 in existing models. The distributed representations and convolutional layers effectively capture long-range dependencies without suffering from the curse of dimensionality. The authors convincingly show that their model can handle longer transitions without overfitting, which is a major limitation of previous approaches.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The results are compelling, with consistent improvements over non-neural baselines across all three evaluation tasks. The absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating represent meaningful improvements in a field where gains are often marginal. The paper also provides a thorough comparison with strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art entity grid extensions by Elsner and Charniak (2011).

3. **Well-Designed Evaluation**: The authors evaluate on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) using appropriate datasets. The WSJ corpus provides a sufficiently large training set for neural models, and the authors justify their choice over smaller corpora like AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES. The use of pairwise ranking for training is well-justified and aligns with previous work on coherence assessment.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Architecture Choice**: While the paper uses convolutional layers, it doesn't provide a strong theoretical justification for why convolution is the right choice for modeling entity transitions. The authors could have explored why convolution is superior to other architectures (e.g., RNNs, transformers) for this specific task. The paper would be stronger with a more detailed comparison of architecture choices and their theoretical underpinnings for coherence modeling.

2. **Limited Analysis of Model Behavior**: The paper presents strong empirical results but lacks a deeper analysis of *why* the neural model works better. For instance, they don't analyze what specific transition patterns the convolutional filters learn, or how the entity-specific features contribute to performance gains. A qualitative analysis of learned filters and feature importance would significantly strengthen the paper.

3. **Potential Overfitting Concerns**: The WSJ corpus, while larger than previous datasets, still contains only ~1,300 training documents. The neural model's performance might be sensitive to the specific dataset, and the paper doesn't discuss how the model would scale to smaller datasets or whether it would overfit. The authors mention that "the extended grid model does not use pairwise training, therefore could not be trained on the summarization dataset," suggesting the neural model might be more data-hungry than the baseline.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." What specific entity-specific features were most important for the performance gains? Could the authors provide a feature importance analysis?

2. The authors note that "insertion is a much more difficult task than discrimination." Why does the neural model's performance improvement on insertion (2.5%) seem smaller than on discrimination (4%)? Is this because the neural model is better at detecting gross structural differences (as in discrimination) than subtle position changes (as in insertion)?

3. The paper mentions "pre-trained models from discrimination task on WSJ corpus" for the summary coherence rating task. How much improvement does pre-training provide, and how does this compare to training a model specifically for the summary coherence task?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of text coherence modeling. The neuralization of the entity grid model is well-executed, the methodology is sound, and the empirical results are significant. The paper addresses a clear limitation of existing work and provides a robust solution. While the theoretical justification for the convolutional architecture could be strengthened, and a deeper analysis of model behavior would be valuable, these do not significantly undermine the paper's contributions.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, demonstrating that neural approaches can substantially improve upon established coherence models. The results are convincing, the evaluation is thorough, and the work has clear potential for applications in text generation and summarization. I recommend acceptance.
