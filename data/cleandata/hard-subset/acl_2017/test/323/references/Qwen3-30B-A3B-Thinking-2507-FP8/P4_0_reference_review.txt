

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling local text coherence by extending the entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008), which has been the standard approach for coherence assessment. The entity grid model represents text as a grid capturing transitions of discourse entities across sentences, but it is limited by discrete representation (leading to curse of dimensionality) and task-agnostic feature extraction.

**Main Contributions (as assessed by reviewer):**

1. **Overcoming the discrete representation limitation of entity grid models:** The authors successfully replace the discrete representation with distributed representations, enabling modeling of longer entity transitions (length 5-8) without the curse of dimensionality that plagues traditional entity grid models. This addresses a fundamental limitation of the prior state of the art.

2. **End-to-end training for task-specific feature learning:** The authors present a pairwise ranking approach that allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically through end-to-end training, rather than using task-agnostic feature extraction.

3. **Demonstrated improvements across multiple coherence tasks:** The model achieves consistent improvements over non-neural baselines across three established coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), with statistically significant gains (p < 0.01) on all tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Significant technical contribution addressing a known limitation:** The paper effectively addresses a fundamental limitation of the entity grid model that has persisted for over a decade (k ≤ 3 for transition length). By using distributed representations and convolutional layers, the authors enable modeling of longer transitions (k = 5-8) without the exponential increase in parameters that would occur in traditional grid models. This is a substantial technical improvement over prior work, as evidenced by the 4% absolute improvement on discrimination and 2.5% on insertion tasks.

2. **Strong empirical results across multiple evaluation tasks:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) with statistically significant gains over the best non-neural baselines. The improvements are consistent and substantial (4-4.5% absolute gains), demonstrating the practical value of the approach across different coherence assessment scenarios. The use of a large WSJ dataset (1,378 training documents) for training neural models is particularly important for the success of the approach.

3. **Well-motivated and standard neural architecture:** The authors use a convolutional neural network architecture that is well-suited for the task of modeling local transitions in entity grids. The architecture (with embedding layers, convolution, pooling, and scoring) is standard in NLP for sequence modeling tasks, and the paper provides clear explanations for each component's role. The pairwise training approach is also well-justified by the nature of coherence assessment as a ranking problem.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty compared to existing neural coherence models:** The paper claims to be the first to "neuralize" the entity grid model, but Li and Hovy (2014) already proposed a neural framework for coherence scoring based on sentence window representations. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently justify why their entity grid-based approach is fundamentally different or superior. The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of how their model handles longer transitions compared to Li and Hovy's approach, which uses a fixed window size (L=3). This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies to isolate key contributions:** The paper compares the neural model to non-neural baselines but does not provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components (e.g., the convolutional architecture vs. distributed representations). Without these, it's unclear whether the improvements are due to the neural architecture itself or simply the use of distributed representations. For example, it would be valuable to know if a simple MLP on the grid embeddings would yield similar results to the convolutional model.

3. **Limited discussion of model limitations and failure cases:** The paper does not discuss any failure cases or limitations of the model. It does not explore scenarios where the model might fail (e.g., in texts with complex discourse structures), nor does it analyze performance differences based on document length or genre. This omission limits the understanding of the model's practical applicability and boundaries.

4. **Inadequate exploration of the impact of entity-specific features:** The paper mentions that their Extended Grid-CNN uses only three of the nine entity-specific features from Elsner and Charniak (2011), but doesn't explain why these three were sufficient or how the model would perform with more features. This is particularly surprising since the Extended Grid model in the non-neural baseline used all nine features. The paper should clarify whether the neural model can incorporate all features without performance degradation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the approach is "fundamentally different" from Li and Hovy (2014), but doesn't provide a clear technical comparison. Can the authors elaborate on how their entity grid-based approach handles longer transitions (k=5-8) compared to Li and Hovy's fixed window approach (L=3), and why this is a significant advantage?

2. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies for the neural model components. Could the authors share results for a model that uses distributed representations but without the convolutional layers (e.g., a simple MLP on the grid embeddings)? This would help isolate the contribution of the convolutional architecture.

3. The Extended Grid-CNN uses only three entity-specific features while the non-neural Extended Grid uses nine. How would the model perform if all nine features were incorporated? Is there a specific reason why the additional features might not be beneficial for the neural model?

4. The paper states that the model achieves state-of-the-art results, but doesn't compare to more recent coherence models beyond the entity grid extensions. Could the authors clarify how their model compares to other recent neural coherence models (e.g., those using transformers) that might be better suited for long-range coherence modeling?
