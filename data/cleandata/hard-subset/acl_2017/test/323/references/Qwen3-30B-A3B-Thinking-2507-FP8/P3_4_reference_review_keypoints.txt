Proposes a neural entity-grid model using distributed grammatical-role embeddings, convolutional layers for long-range entity transitions (5–8), and end-to-end training.

Introduces a neural architecture that models long-range entity transitions while avoiding the dimensionality issues of discrete grids.

Presents end-to-end training that automatically learns task-specific coherence features, removing decoupled feature extraction.

Reports strong empirical gains across three coherence tasks, outperforming state-of-the-art baselines.

Effectively neuralizes the entity-grid approach, showing convolutional layers capture longer transitions without overfitting.

Demonstrates meaningful empirical improvements (≈4% discrimination, up to 2.5% insertion, >4% summary rating) over strong baselines.

Uses a well-designed evaluation on three standard tasks with WSJ training data and pairwise ranking aligned with prior work.

Provides limited theoretical justification for choosing convolutional architectures over alternatives like RNNs or transformers.

Lacks deeper analysis of model behavior—no qualitative inspection of learned filters or feature importance.

Raises potential overfitting concerns given the relatively small WSJ training set (~1,300 documents) and possible data hunger of the neural model.

Asks which entity-specific features drove performance gains and requests a feature-importance analysis.

Questions why improvement on insertion is smaller than on discrimination and whether the model favors gross structural differences.

Asks how much pre-training on the discrimination task helps for summary coherence versus training specifically for the summary task.

Overall: a strong, well-executed contribution with convincing results and recommend acceptance despite the noted analyses and theoretical gaps.
