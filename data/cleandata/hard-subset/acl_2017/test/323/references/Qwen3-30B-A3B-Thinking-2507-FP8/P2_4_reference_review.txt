

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) discrete representation leading to the curse of dimensionality, limiting transition modeling to short lengths (k ≤ 3), and (2) task-agnostic feature extraction that prevents learning task-specific features. The main contributions are:

1. A neural architecture that uses distributed representations and convolutional layers to model long-range entity transitions (k = 5-8) without suffering from the curse of dimensionality.
2. An end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking that automatically learns task-specific features.
3. Consistent improvements over non-neural baselines across three standard coherence evaluation tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Well-motivated neural extension of the entity grid model**: The paper clearly identifies the limitations of existing entity grid models and provides a compelling rationale for why neural approaches could address these issues. The convolutional architecture is a natural fit for modeling entity transitions across sentences, and the authors demonstrate how their approach overcomes the dimensionality problem by using distributed representations rather than discrete transition probabilities.

2. **Strong empirical results across multiple evaluation tasks**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over previous models on three distinct coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The absolute gains (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence) are statistically significant (p<0.01) and meaningful for the coherence modeling field. The use of the larger WSJ corpus (vs. smaller datasets like AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES) is particularly valuable for deep learning models.

3. **Thorough experimental design**: The authors provide careful ablation studies (comparing Grid-CNN vs. Extended Grid-CNN) and include multiple strong baselines (including the graph-based model and both basic and extended entity grid models). The experimental setup follows standard practice in the field and the evaluation is comprehensive.

4. **Clear technical presentation**: The neural architecture is well-described with sufficient mathematical details, and the implementation details (hyperparameters, training procedure) are transparent enough to allow for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to recent work**: The paper claims to be "the first to neuralize the entity grid model," but overlooks relevant work by Li and Hovy (2014), who also proposed a neural framework for coherence modeling. The authors dismiss this work as "very disappointing" without providing sufficient analysis of why their model outperforms it. This is a significant oversight that weakens the novelty claim. The paper should more thoroughly compare and contrast their approach with Li and Hovy's work.

2. **Lack of model analysis**: While the paper provides strong empirical results, it lacks detailed analysis of *why* the model works. The authors mention that "each filter detects a specific transition pattern (e.g., 'SS-O-X' for a coherent text)" but provide no examples or evidence of these patterns. A deeper analysis of the learned features would significantly strengthen the paper.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper reports strong performance but doesn't discuss cases where the model fails or limitations in its approach. Understanding the model's weaknesses would be valuable for future work and would provide a more balanced evaluation.

4. **Overstated claims about "end-to-end" learning**: The paper claims to present "an end-to-end training method to learn task-specific high level features automatically," but the feature extraction (entity grid construction) remains a separate step. The neural model is only applied to the entity grid, not to the raw text, so the approach isn't truly end-to-end from raw text to coherence score.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper dismisses Li and Hovy (2014) as "very disappointing" with only 19.34% accuracy on discrimination. Could you provide more details on why your model outperforms theirs on the same dataset? Did you use the same preprocessing, hyperparameters, or training procedure?

2. The Extended Grid-CNN model incorporates only three entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, and proper mention), while the Extended Grid model uses nine features. How did you select these three features, and what would be the impact of including more features? Would the neural model benefit from incorporating all nine features?

3. The paper mentions that "the model that is trained to distinguish an original document from its random permutation may learn features that are not specific enough to distinguish documents when only one sentence differs." How would you modify the training approach to better address the insertion task specifically?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a solid contribution to the field of text coherence modeling. The neural extension of the entity grid model is well-motivated and demonstrates consistent improvements over previous work. However, the paper's novelty claim is weakened by insufficient comparison to relevant prior work, and the lack of deeper model analysis limits the paper's impact.

The strengths of the paper (strong empirical results, clear motivation, and thorough evaluation) outweigh the weaknesses, but the paper would benefit from addressing the novelty concerns and providing more insight into the model's behavior. With these improvements, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ACL.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions to address the novelty concerns and provide more model analysis)
