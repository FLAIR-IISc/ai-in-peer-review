

# Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural architecture for local coherence assessment that extends the entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors identify two limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) discrete representation of grammatical roles leading to the curse of dimensionality, and (2) task-agnostic feature extraction. Their model addresses these limitations through distributed representations of entity transitions and an end-to-end training approach.

The main contributions of this work (as assessed objectively) are:

1. A neural architecture using distributed representations and convolutional layers to capture long-range entity transitions (primary technical contribution)
2. An end-to-end training method that automatically learns task-specific high-level features (primary methodological contribution)
3. Empirical demonstration of consistent improvements over non-neural entity grid models across three standard coherence evaluation tasks (primary empirical contribution)

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness of the Model Architecture**: The paper presents a well-motivated neural architecture that properly addresses the limitations of the entity grid model. The use of distributed representations (instead of discrete representations) to capture entity transitions is theoretically sound, and the convolutional approach is well-suited for capturing local patterns across the entity grid. The paper provides sufficient technical detail about the model layers (lookup, convolution, pooling, scoring), including the use of wide convolution to handle boundaries and max-pooling to extract salient features. The implementation details (embedding initialization, activation functions, optimization methods) are appropriate for the task and well-justified.

2. **Strong Empirical Results with Proper Evaluation**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over existing non-neural entity grid models across three different evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The reported gains (4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating) are statistically significant (p < 0.01) and represent state-of-the-art results. The evaluation protocol is clear and well-justified, using established tasks and datasets (WSJ for sentence ordering, DUC'03 for summary coherence) that have been previously used to evaluate coherence models. The use of pairwise ranking loss and proper statistical testing (approximate randomization test with 10,000 iterations) is appropriate for the task.

3. **Clear Comparison to Relevant Baselines**: The paper provides a comprehensive comparison against multiple relevant baselines, including the random baseline, graph-based model, and several variants of the entity grid model (Grid-all nouns, Extended Grid). The authors also explain why they chose to exclude Li and Hovy (2014) despite testing it (poor performance on their dataset). The comparison is fair and well-justified, with the authors explaining why they used the WSJ corpus instead of smaller corpora like AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES (larger size, more natural text).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Approach**: While the paper presents a neural model for coherence assessment, the approach of neuralizing existing feature-based models is not particularly novel. Many NLP tasks have seen similar neural extensions of traditional models (e.g., neural versions of CRFs, HMMs, etc.). The paper does not sufficiently articulate how their specific neural approach differs fundamentally from other neural NLP methods or why the neural approach is particularly well-suited for coherence assessment. The comparison to Li and Hovy (2014) is superficial, and the paper could have better articulated the theoretical differences between their approach and previous neural coherence models.

2. **Lack of Sufficient Ablation Studies**: The paper does not provide adequate ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components of the model. For example, it doesn't clearly demonstrate how much of the improvement comes from the distributed representations versus the convolutional architecture versus the end-to-end training approach. The paper claims that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features," but doesn't quantify how much of the improvement is due to the neural architecture versus the entity features. This makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the neural model.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Task Difficulty**: The paper notes that insertion is a harder task than discrimination (as expected) and that the gains on insertion are smaller (1% for Grid-CNN, 2.5% for Extended Grid-CNN) compared to discrimination (4% and 4.7% respectively). However, it doesn't provide a deeper analysis of why the neural model struggles more with insertion. A failure analysis or discussion of specific examples where the model fails would strengthen the paper's contribution and help clarify the model's limitations.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." Could the authors provide more specific details about which entity features contributed most to the improvement, and whether the neural model could potentially learn these features without explicit engineering?

2. The paper mentions that "our models were not trained specifically on the insertion task." How would the authors expect the model to perform if trained directly on the insertion task (rather than on the discrimination task)? Would they anticipate larger improvements on insertion performance?

3. The paper states that "the transition length k can be sufficiently large (e.g., 5-8 in our experiments) to capture long-range transitional dependencies without overfitting." What evidence do the authors have that the improvements are due to the neural architecture (allowing longer k) rather than other factors such as the use of more training data or different hyperparameters?
