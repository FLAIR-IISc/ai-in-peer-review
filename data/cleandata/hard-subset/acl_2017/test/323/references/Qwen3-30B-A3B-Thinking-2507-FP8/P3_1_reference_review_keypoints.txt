- Proposes a CNN-based neural extension of the entity grid that uses distributed representations and convolutions to model longer entity transitions (k=5–8).  
- Argues end-to-end training replaces task-agnostic feature extraction by learning task-specific coherence features.  
- Reports state-of-the-art, statistically significant gains on discrimination (+4%), insertion (+2.5%), and summary coherence rating (+4%+).  

- Strength: correctly identifies the entity grid’s curse of dimensionality and shows distributed embeddings overcome it.  
- Strength: strong, consistent empirical results with multiple seeds and proper significance testing.  
- Strength: justified use of WSJ dataset for larger, more natural text and fairness to competing systems.

- Weakness: model architecture is standard (embedding → CNN → pooling → linear), offering limited methodological novelty beyond prior CNN work.  
- Weakness: lacks thorough ablation studies to isolate contributions of embeddings, convolution, filter sizes, and entity-specific features.  
- Weakness: uses the same pairwise ranking loss for all tasks despite noting that insertion may need different training; no task-specific training explored.  
- Weakness: no analysis of learned convolutional filters or which transition patterns drive coherence judgments.  
- Weakness: incomplete comparison to recent neural/discourse models and insufficient explanation for excluding Li & Hovy (2014).

- Q: How many of the 9 Elsner & Charniak entity-specific features were used and what was their impact?  
- Q: How does performance change with narrow vs. wide convolution and what’s the theoretical justification for wide convolution?  
- Q: Why not train task-specific models (e.g., for insertion) and what performance gains are expected?  
- Q: Why does this model outperform Li & Hovy (2014) — which architectural or training choices explain the difference?  
- Q: How exactly do embeddings transform grammatical roles into distributed representations and improve generalization?

- Additional: related work should discuss more recent neural discourse models (e.g., Li et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2019).  
- Additional: paper should discuss limitations on complex discourse structures beyond simple entity transitions.  
- Additional: include code link and more development-set details/hyperparameter results.

- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, contingent on adding ablations, learned-feature analysis, and addressing comparisons and methodological questions.
