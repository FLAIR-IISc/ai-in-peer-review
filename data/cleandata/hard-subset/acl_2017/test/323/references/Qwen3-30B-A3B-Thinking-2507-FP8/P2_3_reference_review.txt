

# ACL Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural architecture extending the entity grid model for local coherence assessment. The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) discrete representations leading to the curse of dimensionality, restricting transition modeling to short lengths (k ≤ 3), and (2) task-agnostic feature extraction. Their contributions are:

1. A neural model using distributed representations to capture long-range entity transitions (k=5-8) without suffering from the curse of dimensionality
2. An end-to-end training method that learns task-specific features automatically through pairwise ranking
3. A method to incorporate entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention) without exponential parameter growth

## Strengths

1. **Well-motivated technical extension**: The paper clearly identifies the limitations of existing entity grid models (curse of dimensionality, task-agnostic features) and proposes a principled neural solution. The convolutional architecture is well-suited to the problem structure, as it can model arbitrary-length transitions while maintaining position-invariance. The explanation of how the model overcomes the limitations of previous work is thorough and convincing.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The model consistently outperforms all non-neural baselines across three established tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence). The gains are significant (4%+ on discrimination and summary coherence, 2.5% on insertion) and statistically validated. The use of multiple evaluation tasks strengthens the claims about the model's effectiveness.

3. **Clear technical exposition**: The paper provides a detailed description of the neural architecture, including the transformation of grammatical roles to distributed representations, the convolutional layer for modeling transitions, and the pairwise training approach. The explanation of how the convolutional filters learn to detect specific transition patterns is particularly well-articulated.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty**: While the neural extension is well-executed, the core idea of neuralizing existing coherence models is not novel. The authors acknowledge this by stating "Following this tradition, in this paper we propose to neuralize the popular entity grid models." There are multiple recent papers (e.g., Li and Hovy, 2014; Li et al., 2014) that have also proposed neural approaches to coherence. The paper would have been stronger with a more detailed discussion of how this work differs from and improves upon these prior neural approaches.

2. **Lack of comprehensive ablation studies**: The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components. For instance, it's unclear how much the improvements come from the neural architecture versus the incorporation of entity-specific features. The paper mentions that Extended Grid-CNN incorporates only a subset of the Extended Grid features, but doesn't compare the performance of the neural model with just entity transitions versus with all features.

3. **Incomplete comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper compares to entity grid models and a graph-based model but omits comparison to recent neural approaches for coherence modeling. The authors mention they tried Li and Hovy (2014) but got "very disappointing" results (19.34% accuracy), but don't provide any analysis of why this might be the case. This omission makes it difficult to assess how the proposed approach compares to the current state of the art.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach differ from Li and Hovy (2014), who also proposed a neural model for coherence assessment? Why did their approach perform poorly on your dataset?

2. Could you provide more detailed ablation studies to show the contribution of each component (neural architecture, entity-specific features, etc.)?

3. The paper states that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." Could you clarify which features were omitted and how this affects the model's performance?

## Final Recommendation

This is a well-executed paper that makes a solid incremental contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The neural extension of the entity grid model is technically sound and achieves strong empirical results. However, the novelty is limited, and the paper would be significantly strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons to recent neural approaches and additional ablation studies. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how it advances beyond existing neural coherence models and a clearer presentation of the relative contributions of different model components.
