

# Peer Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network-based approach to model local text coherence, building upon the entity grid model that has been the dominant approach for coherence assessment. The authors address two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) their discrete representation of grammatical roles leads to a curse of dimensionality problem, limiting transition lengths to k≤3, and (2) their feature extraction is task-agnostic rather than learning task-specific features. The proposed model uses distributed representations and convolutional neural networks to capture longer entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features in an end-to-end fashion.

The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A neural architecture that overcomes the discrete representation limitation of existing entity grid models, enabling the capture of longer-range entity transitions (up to 5-8) without the curse of dimensionality.
2. An end-to-end training method for coherence assessment that learns task-specific features automatically, rather than using task-agnostic feature extraction.
3. Comprehensive evaluation across three standard coherence assessment tasks showing significant improvements over existing methods.

## Strengths

1. **Novel neural architecture for coherence modeling**: The paper successfully addresses a fundamental limitation of the entity grid model by introducing a neural approach that uses distributed representations. This is a significant technical contribution as it enables modeling of longer entity transitions (5-8) compared to the typical k≤3 in existing models. The convolutional architecture is well-suited for capturing local patterns in the entity grid, and the paper provides a clear theoretical justification for this choice. The model's ability to handle longer transitions is particularly valuable for capturing more nuanced coherence patterns.

2. **Strong empirical results across multiple tasks**: The model achieves absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating compared to the best non-neural entity grid models. These improvements are statistically significant (p<0.01), and the authors provide a thorough ablation study showing the contributions of both the neural architecture and entity-specific features. The results on the insertion task are particularly impressive as it's a more challenging task than discrimination, demonstrating the model's ability to detect subtle coherence differences.

3. **Clear articulation of limitations and justification for approach**: The paper effectively identifies and explains the limitations of existing entity grid models, providing a strong theoretical foundation for their neural approach. They clearly connect their method to Centering Theory, which is the basis for the entity grid model, and explain how their neural approach overcomes specific limitations of the discrete representation. The evaluation on three different tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) provides comprehensive evidence of the model's effectiveness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent neural coherence models**: While the paper mentions Li and Hovy (2014) as a related neural approach, they excluded it from results because it performed poorly (19.34% accuracy) on their WSJ dataset. However, the authors don't provide a detailed explanation for this poor performance. A more thorough comparison with this recent neural approach would strengthen the paper by demonstrating why their method is superior. It's concerning that a neural approach to coherence modeling performed so poorly on the same dataset, and the authors should address why their model overcomes this limitation.

2. **Lack of analysis of what the model learns**: The paper claims the model learns "task-specific features automatically" but provides no evidence or analysis of what these features actually are. The paper would be significantly stronger with some visualization of the patterns detected by the convolutional filters in the entity grid. For example, showing which specific coherence patterns (e.g., "S-S-O") the filters are learning to recognize would provide valuable insights into the model's operation.

3. **Insufficient evidence of generalization**: The paper mentions using dropout and early stopping to prevent overfitting, but doesn't provide learning curves or validation performance across epochs to demonstrate that the model is generalizing well. Without this evidence, it's difficult to assess whether the model is truly learning meaningful patterns or just memorizing the training data. The authors should include validation curves to address this concern.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that Li and Hovy (2014) performed poorly on your WSJ dataset (19.34% accuracy on discrimination), but doesn't explain why. Could you provide more details on why this neural model performed so poorly on your WSJ corpus? Was it a data mismatch issue or a problem with the implementation?

2. Could you provide examples of the patterns detected by the convolutional filters in the entity grid? For instance, which specific entity transition patterns (e.g., "S-S-O") are the filters learning to recognize as coherent? A visualization would greatly strengthen the paper.

3. The paper uses a pairwise ranking approach for training, which is common for coherence tasks. However, it doesn't compare with other training approaches (e.g., using actual coherence scores as labels). Could you explain why pairwise ranking was chosen over other approaches, and how you would expect the model to perform with alternative training methods?

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The neural approach to coherence modeling is well-motivated, the architecture is sound, and the empirical results are strong across multiple evaluation tasks. The paper makes a clear technical contribution to the field of text coherence modeling, addressing a fundamental limitation of the dominant approach.

The weaknesses identified are not deal-breakers, and I believe the authors can address them in the camera-ready version (particularly with the additional analysis of what the model learns, which would significantly strengthen the paper). The paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the results are convincing.

The paper will be of interest to the ACL community, especially those working in discourse processing, text generation, and natural language understanding. The neural approach to coherence modeling could inspire future work in this area and potentially be applied to other discourse-level tasks. The authors have made a significant contribution to a well-established area of NLP with clear empirical evidence of their model's superiority.
