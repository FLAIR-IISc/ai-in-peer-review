1. Neural extension of the entity grid using distributed representations and CNNs to model longer entity transitions (k=5–8), trained end-to-end with pairwise ranking loss and evaluated on discrimination, insertion, and summary rating tasks.

2. Contribution: a neural architecture that models longer entity transitions without the curse of dimensionality.

3. Contribution: end-to-end training that automatically learns task-specific coherence features.

4. Contribution: comprehensive evaluation showing consistent improvements over prior state-of-the-art.

5. Strength: directly addresses the dimensionality limitation of entity-grid models using embeddings and convolutional layers.

6. Strength: thorough evaluation on three tasks with justification for using the WSJ corpus and comparisons to strong baselines.

7. Strength: clear distinction from prior neural approaches (e.g., Li & Hovy) and explanation of architectural differences.

8. Strength: provides implementation details and an ablation study that aid reproducibility.

9. Weakness: claims about incorporating arbitrary entity-specific features are weakened because only 3 of 9 features were used without justification.

10. Weakness: insufficient statistical reporting—exact p-values are not provided despite using randomization tests.

11. Weakness: lacks qualitative analysis or examples illustrating what the model finds coherent or incoherent.

12. Weakness: inadequate comparison to other recent neural coherence models, weakening the state-of-the-art claim.

13. Weakness: no exploration of task-specific training (e.g., training directly on insertion) despite applying a discrimination-trained model to other tasks.

14. Question: why were only three entity-specific features chosen, and what impact would adding the remaining features have?

15. Question: what is the exact p-value for the reported 2.5% insertion gain from the Extended Grid-CNN?

16. Question: why did Li and Hovy’s distributed sentence model perform poorly (19.34%) — dataset, architecture, or training differences?

17. Question: would training specifically on the insertion task yield further improvements, and if so, by how much?

18. Overall recommendation: Weak Accept — solid and meaningful contribution but requires revisions addressing statistical evidence, qualitative analysis, feature inclusion, and broader comparisons.
