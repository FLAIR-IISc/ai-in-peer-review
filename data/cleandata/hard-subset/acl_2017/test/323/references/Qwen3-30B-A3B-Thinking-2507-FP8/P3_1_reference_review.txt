

# Peer Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural architecture for local coherence assessment that extends the popular entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors argue that existing entity grid models suffer from two key limitations: 1) discrete representation of grammatical roles leads to the curse of dimensionality (limiting transition length to k≤3), and 2) feature extraction is decoupled from the target task. Their solution uses a CNN-based model that transforms grammatical roles into distributed representations, models entity transitions with convolutional layers, and learns task-specific features through end-to-end training. The model is evaluated on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, where it achieves state-of-the-art results.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Neural extension of entity grid model** that overcomes the curse of dimensionality by using distributed representations and convolutional layers to model longer entity transitions (k=5-8) without exponential growth in parameters.
2. **End-to-end training approach** that learns task-specific features automatically rather than using task-agnostic feature extraction.
3. **Demonstrated empirical improvements** across three standard coherence evaluation tasks with statistically significant gains over non-neural baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling Identification of a Real Limitation**: The authors correctly identify a genuine limitation of existing entity grid models—the curse of dimensionality that prevents modeling longer entity transitions. This is a well-documented problem in NLP (Bengio et al., 2003), and the paper clearly demonstrates how their approach addresses this limitation by using distributed representations rather than discrete probabilities. Their experimental results showing that they can use k=5-8 (vs. k≤3 in prior work) provide concrete evidence of this advantage.

2. **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results**: The paper presents robust experimental results across three different evaluation tasks, with statistically significant improvements over strong baselines. The 4% absolute gain on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating are substantial improvements in this field. The use of proper statistical significance testing (p<0.01) and multiple runs with different random seeds adds credibility to their claims. The fact that the model achieves state-of-the-art results across all three tasks is a significant contribution.

3. **Clear Justification for Dataset Choice**: The authors provide a well-reasoned argument for using the WSJ corpus over smaller, more constrained corpora like AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES. They correctly note that larger training sets are crucial for deep learning models, that WSJ represents more natural text, and that they could reproduce results for competing systems using a public toolkit. This strengthens the validity of their experimental setup.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Model Architecture**: While the application of CNNs to coherence modeling is novel, the actual neural architecture (embedding layer, convolutional layer, pooling layer, linear output) is quite standard in NLP. The architecture is nearly identical to Kim's (2014) CNN for sentence classification, which was published in 2014 and has been widely adopted since. The paper doesn't introduce any novel architectural components beyond applying existing techniques to a new domain. This limits the methodological contribution of the paper.

2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies**: The paper lacks thorough ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component of their model. For example:
   - How much of the improvement comes from the distributed representation vs. the convolutional layers?
   - What is the impact of using different filter sizes (k=5-8) compared to smaller k values?
   - How much do the three entity-specific features contribute individually to the final performance?
   
   Without these analyses, it's difficult to determine which aspects of the model are truly driving the improvements.

3. **Questionable Training Methodology for Different Tasks**: The paper acknowledges that "the model that is trained to distinguish an original document from its random permutation may learn features that are not specific enough to distinguish documents when only one sentence differs" for the insertion task. Yet they use the same pairwise ranking loss for all tasks. They don't explore task-specific training strategies for insertion, which is a more challenging task. This suggests a misalignment between their training methodology and the specific demands of different evaluation tasks.

4. **Lack of Analysis of Learned Features**: The paper doesn't provide any analysis of what the convolutional filters have learned. For example, they don't show which transition patterns (e.g., "S-S-O", "O-X-S") are most important for coherence. Without this analysis, it's difficult to understand how their model differs from existing entity grid models and why it's more effective. This is a missed opportunity to provide deeper insights into the model's behavior.

5. **Comparison with More Recent Coherence Models**: The paper compares with entity grid models and a graph-based model (Guinaudeau and Strube, 2013), but doesn't compare with more recent neural coherence models. For instance, they exclude Li and Hovy (2014) due to poor performance but don't explain why their model outperforms it. Additionally, they don't compare with more recent neural approaches to discourse modeling that might have been published after the submission deadline but before the review.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." How many of the 9 entity-specific features from Elsner and Charniak (2011) did you include? What was the performance impact of including additional features?

2. The paper mentions using "wide convolution" instead of narrow convolution to ensure filters reach entire columns of the grid. How did the performance change when using narrow convolution instead? What is the theoretical justification for using wide convolution in this specific context?

3. The authors note that insertion is a more difficult task than discrimination. Why didn't they train separate models for each task (e.g., a model specifically trained for insertion), rather than using the same model for both tasks? What would be the expected performance gain from task-specific training?

4. The paper states that they "excluded" the Li and Hovy (2014) model due to poor performance. Can you explain why your model outperforms their model, and what specific aspects of your model architecture or training methodology contributed to this difference?

5. The paper claims to use "distributed representations of entity transitions and entity features" but doesn't elaborate on how these distributed representations differ from the standard entity grid. Could you provide more details about how the embedding layer transforms discrete grammatical roles into distributed representations, and how this helps with generalization?

## Additional Comments

- The related work section is well-written and covers the key papers in the coherence modeling literature, but it could be strengthened by discussing how the authors' approach relates to recent neural approaches to discourse modeling (e.g., Li et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2019).

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach. For example, how does their model handle texts with complex discourse structures that go beyond simple entity transitions?

- The authors mention that they "have released our code with the submission," but it would be helpful to include a link to the code repository in the paper.

- The paper's experimental section could be strengthened by including more detailed results on the development set, rather than just reporting the optimal hyperparameters.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-executed extension of the entity grid model using neural techniques, with strong empirical results that demonstrate clear improvements over existing approaches. While the methodological contribution is somewhat limited by the use of standard neural architectures, the empirical results are solid and the application to a well-established coherence modeling problem is valuable.

**Recommendation: Accept** with minor revisions (addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the ablation studies and analysis of learned features).

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of coherence modeling, and the results are sufficiently strong to warrant publication. The authors have clearly identified a limitation of existing approaches and provided a solution that works well in practice. While the novelty of the model architecture is limited, the empirical validation and clear demonstration of improvements across multiple tasks provide a strong foundation for future work in this area.
