

# ACL Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural architecture for text coherence assessment that extends the popular entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) discrete representation of grammatical roles leading to the curse of dimensionality, limiting transition lengths to k ≤ 3, and (2) decoupled feature extraction from the target task. Their neural model uses distributed representations of entity transitions and entity features, with an end-to-end training approach that learns task-specific features automatically. They evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination (identifying correct sentence order), insertion (locating removed sentences), and summary coherence rating.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Neural extension of the entity grid model** that overcomes the curse of dimensionality by using distributed representations, allowing for longer transition lengths (5-8) without exponential growth in parameters.

2. **End-to-end training methodology** using pairwise ranking that learns task-specific features automatically, addressing the limitation of decoupled feature extraction in existing models.

3. **Consistent improvements across multiple coherence tasks**, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on discrimination (4% absolute gain), insertion (2.5% gain), and summary coherence rating (4%+ gain) compared to non-neural entity grid models.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical validation across multiple tasks**: The paper provides thorough evaluation on three distinct coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), which is a significant strength. The results consistently show improvements over existing entity grid models across all tasks, with statistically significant gains (p<0.01) for their neural models. This comprehensive evaluation approach strengthens the paper's claims about the model's effectiveness.

2. **Clear technical contribution addressing a well-identified limitation**: The paper effectively identifies and addresses a concrete limitation of existing entity grid models (the curse of dimensionality) with a principled neural approach. The use of convolutional layers to handle entity transitions of arbitrary lengths while avoiding exponential parameter growth is a well-motivated technical contribution that directly addresses the stated limitations.

3. **Good comparison with relevant baselines**: The authors provide a thorough comparison with multiple relevant baselines, including the graph-based model, grid-all-nouns, and extended grid models from previous work. The comparison with the state-of-the-art extended grid model (Elsner and Charniak, 2011) is particularly strong, showing clear improvements with their neural extensions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the neural architecture**: While the application to coherence modeling is reasonable, the core neural architecture (convolutional layers + max pooling) is quite standard in NLP and not particularly novel. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from other recent neural approaches to coherence modeling. For instance, they mention Li and Hovy (2014) but don't provide a meaningful comparison that explains why their approach is superior, which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient analysis of model behavior**: The paper reports improvements but lacks detailed analysis of *why* the model works better. There's no analysis of what specific transition patterns the filters learn to detect (as mentioned in the "Why it works" section), nor is there any linguistic analysis to confirm the model is learning meaningful coherence patterns rather than simply memorizing the training data. Without such analysis, it's difficult to assess whether the improvements reflect genuine linguistic understanding.

3. **Incomplete comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper states they tried Li and Hovy (2014) but found it "very disappointing" (19.34% accuracy on discrimination) and excluded it from results. However, they don't provide sufficient details about this experiment to justify why their model outperforms this neural approach. A more thorough comparison with recent neural coherence models would strengthen the paper's claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the neural model can detect specific transition patterns (e.g., 'SS-O-X' for coherent text), but doesn't provide examples of these patterns or their linguistic interpretation. Could you share specific transition patterns that your model learns to detect, and how these relate to linguistic coherence principles?

2. You mention that your Extended Grid-CNN uses only a subset of entity-specific features from Elsner and Charniak (2011). Could you elaborate on why you selected these particular features (named entity type, salience, and proper mention), and what would happen if you included additional features?

3. You state that the Li and Hovy (2014) model performed poorly on your dataset (19.34% accuracy). Could you provide more details about this experiment, including the exact hyperparameters used and why you believe the model failed on your specific dataset?

## Additional Comments

The paper would be strengthened by:
- A more detailed comparison with recent neural coherence models beyond the entity grid baseline
- Analysis of model behavior (what specific patterns does it learn?)
- More discussion of why the Li and Hovy (2014) model performed poorly on this dataset
- Clarification on whether the improvements are due to the neural architecture itself or simply the use of distributed representations

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak reject**. The paper makes a reasonable contribution to applying neural networks to coherence modeling, and the results are solid. However, the novelty of the core approach is limited, and the paper lacks sufficient analysis to demonstrate that the model is learning linguistically meaningful coherence patterns rather than simply memorizing patterns. The incomplete comparison with recent neural approaches also weakens the paper's claims. With significant revisions addressing these concerns, particularly providing a more thorough comparison with recent neural models and analysis of the model's learned patterns, this paper could be a strong contribution to the field.
