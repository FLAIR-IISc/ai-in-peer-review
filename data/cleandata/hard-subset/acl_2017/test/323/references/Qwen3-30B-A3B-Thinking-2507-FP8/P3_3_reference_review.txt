

# Peer Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling local coherence in text, building upon the entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: 1) discrete representation leading to the curse of dimensionality that restricts modeling to short transitions (k ≤ 3), and 2) task-agnostic feature extraction that prevents learning task-specific features. Their contributions are: (1) a neural model that uses distributed representations to capture longer-range entity transitions (up to k=5-8), (2) an end-to-end training approach that learns task-specific features automatically, and (3) state-of-the-art results across three coherence evaluation tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of limitations in prior work**: The paper effectively identifies the key shortcomings of entity grid models (curse of dimensionality, task-agnostic feature extraction) and correctly positions their neural approach as addressing these limitations. The authors provide a compelling theoretical argument for why distributed representations and convolutional layers can overcome the discrete representation problem.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The authors evaluate on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) with thorough comparison against multiple strong baselines, including the extended entity grid model (Elsner and Charniak, 2011). The consistent improvements (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, 4%+ on summary coherence) across all tasks demonstrate the model's robustness and practical value.

3. **Appropriate architectural choices**: The use of convolutional layers to model entity transitions is well-motivated and aligned with successful NLP applications (e.g., Kim, 2014). The authors correctly note that convolution allows modeling longer transitions without the combinatorial explosion of discrete representations, and the max-pooling strategy effectively captures salient features.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of ablation studies for transition length**: While the authors claim their model captures "long range entity transitions" (k=5-8), they provide no ablation studies to demonstrate how performance changes with different transition lengths. Without this, it's unclear whether the performance gains are truly due to modeling longer transitions or simply the neural architecture's general advantages. The paper should include experiments showing performance as a function of k.

2. **Insufficient analysis of data scarcity in summary coherence task**: The summary coherence rating task uses only 144 training pairs, which is extremely limited for neural models. The authors acknowledge this by reporting results with pre-trained models, but they don't thoroughly analyze how this data scarcity affects model performance or how their approach compares to simpler models that might be more appropriate for small datasets. The claim that their neural model "outperforms existing models" is somewhat misleading given the data constraints.

3. **Inadequate comparison with recent neural coherence models**: The authors briefly mention Li and Hovy (2014) as a different approach but don't provide a detailed comparison. A thorough analysis of how their convolutional approach differs from Li and Hovy's window-based neural model would strengthen their claim of novelty. The paper should clarify why their method is more suitable for coherence modeling than existing neural approaches.

4. **Overstated claim about "arbitrary" entity features**: The authors claim their model can incorporate "arbitrary entity-specific features," but they only use three features in their experiments. They don't demonstrate how the model scales to include more features or address the combinatorial explosion issue that their own paper identifies as a limitation of previous work. This claim needs qualification.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide ablation results showing how model performance changes with different transition lengths (k=2,3,4,5,6,7,8)? This would clarify whether the performance gains are due to modeling longer transitions.

2. Given the extremely small size of the summary coherence dataset (144 training pairs), how do you justify using a neural model instead of a simpler linear model? Could you provide a comparison of performance with a linear model trained on the same data?

3. The paper mentions "pre-trained models" for the summary coherence task, but doesn't explain how these pre-trained models (from the discrimination task) are fine-tuned. Could you clarify the fine-tuning procedure and whether it's a standard approach in the field?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that makes a solid contribution to the coherence modeling literature. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing approaches and propose a reasonable neural solution that demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple tasks. While the experimental results are strong, the paper would benefit from more thorough analysis of the key claims (particularly regarding transition length and data requirements). The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and represents a meaningful advancement over the state-of-the-art, but the claims about "long range" transitions and "arbitrary" features need to be more carefully qualified. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the ablation study and data scarcity issues.
