

# Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling local text coherence that builds upon the entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors argue that existing entity grid models suffer from two key limitations: (1) they use discrete representations for grammatical roles, leading to the curse of dimensionality that limits modeling to short entity transitions (k ≤ 3), and (2) their feature extraction is task-agnostic, decoupled from the target coherence assessment task. To address these limitations, they introduce a neural model that uses distributed representations of entity transitions and entity-specific features, with convolutional layers to model longer transitions (k=5-8) and an end-to-end training approach that learns task-specific features automatically. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, reporting improvements over non-neural entity grid models.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **A neural architecture for coherence modeling that overcomes the curse of dimensionality in entity grid models** by using distributed representations to capture longer entity transitions (k=5-8) without exponential parameter growth.
2. **An end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking** that allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically, rather than relying on hand-crafted features.
3. **Empirical validation showing consistent improvements across multiple coherence evaluation tasks** with statistically significant gains over the state-of-the-art entity grid models.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification and addressing of a well-established limitation in coherence modeling**: The paper effectively identifies the limitations of the entity grid model (curse of dimensionality and task-agnostic feature extraction), which have been well-documented in the literature. The proposed neural approach directly addresses these limitations in a technically sound way, which is a significant contribution to the coherence modeling field. The paper demonstrates that the neural model can capture longer-range transitions (k=5-8) while avoiding the exponential parameter growth problem that plagues the traditional grid model.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple standard tasks**: The authors evaluate their model on three different coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), which is a strong validation of their approach. The improvements over non-neural baselines are consistent and statistically significant across all tasks (4% absolute gain in discrimination, 2.5% in insertion, and 4%+ in summary coherence rating). The use of multiple evaluation tasks strengthens the claim that the model is genuinely improving coherence modeling.

3. **Strong comparison to relevant baselines**: The paper provides a thorough comparison to multiple strong baselines, including the extended entity grid model from Elsner and Charniak (2011), which is the state-of-the-art for coherence modeling. The neural models consistently outperform these baselines, and the authors provide a clear explanation for the improvements (demonstrating that the neural model can incorporate entity-specific features while avoiding the limitations of the traditional model).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of analysis of why the neural model works better**: While the paper reports significant improvements, it does not provide sufficient analysis to explain *why* the neural model outperforms the traditional entity grid model. For example, the authors don't show what specific patterns the convolutional filters are learning or how the distributed representations capture meaningful coherence information. Without this analysis, it's difficult to assess whether the improvements are due to the neural architecture itself or simply because the authors incorporated more entity-specific features (which were already known to improve performance). The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding visualizations of learned filters or ablation studies showing the contribution of the neural architecture versus the entity-specific features.

2. **Insufficient justification for dataset choice**: The authors prefer the WSJ corpus over the AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES corpora used in previous work, citing reasons such as larger size and more natural style. However, they don't provide evidence that the WSJ corpus is representative of the coherence challenges in the types of texts where coherence modeling is most needed (e.g., news articles vs. narrative texts). The paper would be stronger with a more detailed comparison of the corpora's characteristics and their relevance to coherence modeling.

3. **Limited discussion of limitations and failure cases**: The paper does not discuss the limitations of their approach or provide examples where the model fails. For instance, the authors note that insertion task gains are smaller than discrimination task gains, but don't analyze why this might be the case or what specific types of coherence challenges the model struggles with. A discussion of failure cases would help readers understand the model's boundaries and guide future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper reports that the Extended Grid-CNN model achieves 2.5% absolute improvement in the insertion task compared to the Extended Grid model. However, the authors note that insertion is a harder task than discrimination. Can you provide specific examples from your dataset where the neural model outperforms the traditional model in the insertion task, and what features or patterns might be contributing to this improvement?

2. In Table 4, the authors show that their pre-trained models (trained on WSJ for discrimination and fine-tuned on summary coherence) yield better results than models trained solely on the summary coherence task. Given that the summary coherence task has very limited training data (only 144 pairs), is it possible that the neural model is overfitting to the WSJ data when fine-tuned on the summary task? What evidence do you have that this is not the case?

3. The paper states that their neural model incorporates "only a subset of the Extended Grid features" (three entity-specific features). Could you elaborate on which features were excluded and why? How might including the full set of features affect the model's performance and the significance of the neural architecture itself?

## Additional Comments

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with Li and Hovy (2014), who also proposed a neural approach to coherence modeling. The authors mention that they "excluded" this model due to disappointing results, but it would be helpful to understand why their implementation differed from the original work and what specific issues arose.

- The authors claim their model "achieves state of the art results in all these tasks," but it would be helpful to provide a more precise comparison with the most recent work in the field (e.g., Feng et al., 2014), as the paper's evaluation is limited to the models from Elsner and Charniak (2011).

- The paper mentions they have "released our code with the submission," but it would be helpful to include a link to the code repository in the paper (or in the review) to facilitate reproducibility.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated extension to the entity grid model for coherence assessment. The neural architecture addresses a clear limitation of existing models, and the experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines. However, the paper lacks sufficient analysis to understand *why* the neural model works better, and the dataset justification is somewhat weak. 

**Decision: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses in a revised version, particularly by adding analysis of the model's learned features and addressing the dataset justification).

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of coherence modeling, but would be significantly stronger with more analysis of the model's behavior and a more thorough justification of the experimental setup. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, but the paper would benefit from addressing the identified weaknesses before publication.
