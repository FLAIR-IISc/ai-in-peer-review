

# ACL Peer Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling local text coherence, building upon the popular entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) they use discrete representations leading to the curse of dimensionality problem, limiting the modeling of longer transitions (k ≤ 3), and (2) they compute features in a task-agnostic way, decoupled from the target task. To address these limitations, they propose a convolutional neural network that operates on distributed representations of entity transitions, allowing for longer transition modeling (k = 5-8) and end-to-end learning of task-specific features.

The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination (distinguishing original from permuted text), insertion (locating the correct position for a removed sentence), and summary coherence rating (comparing model rankings against human judgments). Their neural models consistently outperform existing entity grid models by about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating.

## Main Contributions

1. **Neural Architecture for Modeling Entity Transitions**: The paper introduces a convolutional neural network that operates on distributed representations of entity transitions, allowing for modeling of longer-range transitions (5-8 length) without the curse of dimensionality problem inherent in traditional entity grid models.

2. **End-to-End Task-Specific Feature Learning**: The authors present a pairwise ranking approach for training the neural model end-to-end, enabling automatic learning of task-specific features rather than relying on hand-crafted features.

3. **Incorporation of Entity-Specific Features**: The paper demonstrates how to effectively incorporate entity-specific features (like named entity types) into the neural model without the exponential parameter growth seen in previous approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Performance Gains**: The paper demonstrates consistent and substantial improvements over existing entity grid models across three different evaluation tasks. The absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination and summary coherence rating are statistically significant (p < 0.01) and meaningful for the coherence modeling task. These results are convincing and well-supported by thorough experiments.

2. **Clear Technical Explanation**: The paper provides a detailed and well-structured explanation of the neural architecture, including the motivation for each design choice (e.g., using wide convolution to handle boundary entities, the role of max-pooling). The technical details are presented clearly, making the approach understandable for readers familiar with neural networks.

3. **Practical Implementation and Reproducibility**: The authors have released their code with the submission, which is a significant contribution to the community. This makes the work easily reproducible and adoptable by other researchers.

4. **Careful Evaluation on Standard Tasks**: The paper uses the standard evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) established by Barzilay and Lapata (2008), ensuring that results are comparable with previous work. The authors also provide appropriate baselines and thorough comparisons against relevant prior work.

5. **Effective Handling of Entity-Specific Features**: The paper successfully demonstrates how to incorporate entity-specific features (like named entity types) into the neural model without the exponential parameter growth problem seen in previous approaches, showing the practicality of this method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Approach**: While the application of neural networks to coherence modeling is interesting, the core idea of using convolutional networks to model entity transitions is not particularly novel. Similar approaches have been applied to other NLP tasks (e.g., text classification with CNNs). The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from these existing methods or demonstrate why this specific application is novel.

2. **Inadequate Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches**: The paper mentions Li and Hovy's (2014) neural approach but excludes it from results due to poor performance (19.34% accuracy on discrimination), without providing a detailed explanation or comparison. This is a significant omission, as it fails to demonstrate how the proposed method improves upon existing neural approaches to coherence modeling.

3. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis**: The paper provides no qualitative analysis of the model's behavior or what patterns the convolutional filters learn. Without examples of what the model has learned (e.g., specific entity transition patterns that correlate with coherence), it's difficult to understand why the model works well or how it differs from previous approaches.

4. **Overfitting Concerns with Small Datasets**: The paper mentions using dropout and early stopping to prevent overfitting, but the summary coherence rating task has only 144 training pairs. With such a small dataset, it's unclear how well the model generalizes, and the authors don't provide evidence that the model isn't simply memorizing the training data.

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show the contribution of each component of the model (e.g., the effect of using entity-specific features, the effect of different filter sizes). This makes it difficult to determine which aspects of the model are most important for the performance gains.

## Questions to Authors

1. You excluded Li and Hovy's (2014) neural coherence model due to poor performance (19.34% accuracy on discrimination), but you don't provide a detailed explanation for this poor performance. Did you use the same hyperparameters as in their paper, or did you tune them for your dataset? How does your model compare to Li and Hovy's when trained on the same dataset?

2. The paper states that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the features used in your model versus the Extended Grid model? Specifically, which features are included in your model, and which ones are omitted? How might including the other features affect performance?

3. The paper mentions that "our neural models may not learn well for this task" on the summary coherence rating task due to limited training instances. How much does pre-training help, and what would be the performance if you trained from scratch on the summary coherence task with more data (e.g., by including additional summaries from other corpora)?

4. The paper doesn't provide any qualitative analysis of the model's behavior. Could you share examples of what the convolutional filters are learning (e.g., specific entity transition patterns that correlate with coherence)?

5. The paper doesn't discuss how the model scales to longer documents. The WSJ corpus has an average of 21.5 sentences per document, but what happens when the document is much longer (e.g., 100+ sentences)? Does the model still work well, or would you need to modify the architecture?

## Recommendation

Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution by applying neural networks to the coherence modeling task in a way that addresses the limitations of existing entity grid models. The results are strong, the technical approach is well-explained, and the work is reproducible. While there are some weaknesses (particularly regarding novelty and comparison to recent neural approaches), the paper's strengths outweigh these concerns, and the results are sufficiently compelling for acceptance.

The authors should address the questions raised above in the author response, particularly regarding the comparison with Li and Hovy's (2014) model and providing qualitative analysis of what the model has learned.
