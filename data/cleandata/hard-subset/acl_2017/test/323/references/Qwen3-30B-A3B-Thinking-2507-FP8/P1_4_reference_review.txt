

# Peer Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for local coherence assessment, addressing two key limitations of existing approaches: (1) the use of discrete representations leading to the curse of dimensionality, and (2) decoupled feature computation from the target task. The authors introduce a convolutional neural network architecture that transforms grammatical roles into distributed representations, models entity transitions of arbitrary lengths, and learns task-specific features end-to-end.

My assessment of the paper's contributions, ordered by strength:

1. **The neural architecture that successfully addresses the limitations of entity grid models**: This is the core technical contribution, replacing discrete representations with distributed representations and using convolutional layers to model longer entity transitions without suffering from the curse of dimensionality.

2. **Demonstration of consistent improvements across multiple coherence evaluation tasks**: The model achieves significant gains (4%+ on discrimination and summary coherence rating) over non-neural entity grid models across three different evaluation tasks, providing strong empirical evidence for the approach's effectiveness.

3. **The end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking**: This approach allows the model to learn task-specific high-level features automatically, addressing the limitation of existing models that compute features in a task-agnostic way.

## Strengths

1. **Significant and consistent experimental results**: The paper demonstrates robust improvements over state-of-the-art baselines across three different coherence tasks. The 4%+ absolute gains on discrimination and summary coherence rating are substantial in this domain, and the statistical significance tests (p<0.01) provide strong evidence for the improvements. The results on insertion task (2.5% improvement) are also notable, especially considering this is a more challenging task than discrimination.

2. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow from problem statement to model architecture, evaluation methodology, and results. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing models and how their neural approach addresses these limitations. The description of the neural architecture is thorough and accessible to readers familiar with basic neural network concepts.

3. **Practical contribution with code release**: The authors have released their code with the submission, which greatly enhances the reproducibility of their work and provides a valuable resource for the community. This is particularly important for coherence modeling, where evaluation datasets and baselines can be difficult to reproduce.

4. **Appropriate choice of evaluation tasks**: The authors evaluate on three different coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) that have been previously used to evaluate coherence models. This comprehensive evaluation strategy strengthens the paper's claims about the model's generalizability.

5. **Strong connection to existing coherence literature**: The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature on coherence modeling, correctly identifying the limitations of the entity grid approach and explaining how their neural model addresses these limitations.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inadequate comparison with recent neural coherence models**: The authors briefly mention Li and Hovy (2014) but dismiss their results as "very disappointing" without providing a thorough explanation. A more detailed comparison with this recent neural approach would strengthen the paper, as it would demonstrate why their approach outperforms other neural methods.

2. **Limited analysis of the learned features**: While the authors provide some intuition about how their model works ("each filter detects a specific transition pattern"), they don't provide any deeper analysis of the features learned by the model. Understanding what specific patterns the convolutional filters detect would add significant value to the paper.

3. **Insufficient explanation for the performance gap between discrimination and insertion tasks**: The authors note that gains on insertion (2.5%) are less than on discrimination (4%), but provide only a superficial explanation about the relative difficulty of the tasks. A more detailed analysis of why the model struggles with insertion would be valuable, especially since insertion is a more challenging task.

4. **Lack of ablation studies on key model components**: The paper compares Grid-CNN to Extended Grid-CNN, but doesn't provide ablation studies on other critical components (e.g., filter size, number of filters, embedding dimension). This makes it difficult to determine which aspects of the model contribute most to the performance gains.

5. **Limited discussion of theoretical implications**: While the paper is grounded in Centering Theory, it doesn't deeply explore how the neural model relates to or extends these theoretical frameworks. A discussion of how the neural model aligns with or challenges existing coherence theories would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors mention that the Li and Hovy (2014) model performed poorly (19.34% accuracy) on their WSJ dataset. Could the authors provide more details on why this might be the case, and whether they used the same preprocessing and evaluation setup as in the original paper?

2. Could the authors provide examples of specific patterns detected by the convolutional filters that contribute to better coherence modeling? This would help readers understand what the model has learned.

3. The paper notes that insertion task gains (2.5%) are less than discrimination gains (4%). Could the authors elaborate on why their model might struggle specifically with the insertion task, and what modifications might improve performance on this more challenging task?

4. How does the use of "wide convolution" (as opposed to narrow convolution) specifically benefit the coherence modeling task, and what would be the impact of using narrow convolution instead?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of text coherence modeling. The neural extension of the entity grid model is well-motivated, well-executed, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches. The paper is clearly written and provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple coherence tasks. While there are some weaknesses in the comparison with recent work and analysis of the learned features, these do not substantially undermine the paper's core contribution. I recommend acceptance of this paper for ACL.
