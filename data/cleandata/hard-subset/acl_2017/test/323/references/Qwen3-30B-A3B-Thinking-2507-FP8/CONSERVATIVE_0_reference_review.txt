

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural extension of the established entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors replace the discrete transition probability calculation with a convolutional neural network that operates on distributed representations of entity transitions. The model is evaluated on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination (identifying the correct sentence order), insertion (locating the original position of a sentence), and summary coherence rating. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art results with absolute improvements of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating compared to non-neural baselines.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A neural architecture that extends the entity grid model to capture longer entity transitions (k=5-8) compared to previous models (k≤3)
2. An end-to-end training method for coherence assessment that incorporates entity-specific features without suffering from the curse of dimensionality
3. Empirical validation showing consistent improvements over established baselines across multiple coherence tasks

## Strengths

1. **Rigorous evaluation on established benchmarks:** The paper evaluates on three well-established coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) with strong baselines, including the current state-of-the-art model (Elsner and Charniak's extended grid model). The use of the WSJ corpus (larger than previous datasets) and proper train/dev/test splits strengthens the empirical validation.

2. **Clear ablation studies:** The authors provide a thorough comparison between neural and non-neural versions of the model (Grid-CNN vs. Grid-all nouns, Extended Grid-CNN vs. Extended Grid), showing consistent improvements across tasks. The ablation studies demonstrate that the neural approach is not just a random improvement but has a systematic effect on performance.

3. **Practical implementation and code release:** The authors have released their code with the submission, which is a significant contribution to the community and facilitates reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core neural approach:** The paper applies a standard convolutional neural network architecture (similar to Kim, 2014) to an existing coherence model. The CNN approach for text processing is well-established in NLP, and the paper doesn't provide a compelling justification for why this specific architecture is particularly suited for coherence modeling compared to other neural approaches (e.g., RNNs, Transformers). The "neural" aspect is largely a superficial application of a standard technique to an existing problem, not a novel method.

2. **Overstated claims about "long-range" transitions:** The paper claims their model can capture "long-range entity transitions" (k=5-8), but the entity grid representation itself fundamentally limits the maximum transition length to the number of sentences in a document. For example, in the WSJ dataset with an average of 21.5 sentences per document (Table 1), the maximum possible transition length is 21.5, not 8. The paper doesn't provide evidence that their model actually captures meaningful long-range dependencies beyond what's possible with the entity grid representation. The improvement in performance is marginal (about 4% absolute gain), which is unlikely to be due to capturing "long-range" effects.

3. **Insufficient comparison with recent neural approaches:** The authors mention they tried the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014) but got poor results (19.34% accuracy) and excluded it from the results. However, they don't provide a detailed analysis of why this approach performed poorly on their dataset. A more thorough comparison with this existing neural approach would have strengthened the paper's claims about the superiority of their method.

4. **Lack of qualitative analysis:** The paper lacks any qualitative analysis of the model's behavior. Without examples showing which entity transitions the model learns to detect, how these transitions relate to human coherence judgments, or concrete cases where the neural model outperforms the non-neural model, it's difficult to assess whether the model is learning meaningful representations or simply memorizing patterns.

5. **Overstated "end-to-end" training claim:** The paper claims their model can be trained in an end-to-end fashion, but the entity grid construction (coreference resolution, entity extraction) is still a separate step that requires external tools. This isn't truly end-to-end as the authors suggest, and they don't address how the model would perform if the entire pipeline (including coreference resolution) was trained jointly.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the model can capture "long-range entity transitions" (k=5-8), but the entity grid representation limits the maximum transition length to the number of sentences in a document. How does your model actually capture long-range dependencies beyond what's possible with the entity grid representation itself? Can you provide specific examples where your model identifies meaningful long-range coherence patterns that the previous models couldn't?

2. The paper mentions that the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014) performed poorly (19.34% accuracy) on your dataset. Why did this happen? Was it due to dataset differences, hyperparameter settings, or fundamental limitations of that approach? A more detailed analysis of this comparison would help justify your method's superiority.

3. The paper states that the neural model can incorporate "arbitrary entity-specific features" without suffering from the curse of dimensionality. However, the paper only uses three entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, and proper mention). How would the model scale to include more features (e.g., 10+ features), and what would be the impact on performance? The paper doesn't provide evidence that the model's scalability is truly better than the existing grid model.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a reasonable extension of an established coherence modeling approach, the contributions are not sufficiently novel or impactful to justify publication in ACL. The neural approach is a straightforward application of a well-known technique to an existing problem, and the performance gains are marginal (about 4% absolute improvement) compared to the increased complexity of the model. The paper fails to provide convincing evidence that the neural approach is fundamentally better than the existing grid model, and it overstates the significance of the improvements. The lack of qualitative analysis and insufficient comparison with recent neural approaches further weaken the paper's claims. The work represents a modest incremental improvement rather than a meaningful advance in the field.
