

# Peer Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for local coherence assessment. The authors address limitations of existing entity grid models (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008), which use discrete representations of grammatical roles and entity transitions, leading to the curse of dimensionality that restricts modeling to short transitions (k ≤ 3). Their neural model uses distributed representations and convolutional layers to model longer entity transitions (k = 5-8) while incorporating entity-specific features. The model is trained end-to-end using pairwise ranking loss to learn task-specific features. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, reporting improvements over non-neural entity grid models.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. A neural architecture that effectively models longer entity transitions (5-8) without suffering from the curse of dimensionality, enabling better capture of long-range coherence dependencies
2. An end-to-end training approach that automatically learns task-specific features for coherence assessment
3. Comprehensive evaluation across three standard coherence tasks demonstrating consistent improvements over previous state-of-the-art

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a Valid Limitation of Existing Models**: The paper correctly identifies a significant limitation of entity grid models (the curse of dimensionality) that has been noted in the literature (Bengio et al., 2003). The authors' solution—using distributed representations and convolutional layers to model longer transitions—is technically sound and directly addresses this limitation. Their demonstration that their model can effectively model transitions of length 5-8 (compared to the previous maximum of 3) is a meaningful technical contribution that validates their approach.

2. **Comprehensive and Well-Justified Evaluation**: The evaluation across three different coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) is thorough and well-motivated. The authors provide a strong justification for using the WSJ corpus over smaller datasets like AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES, emphasizing the importance of large training sets for neural models. The comparison against multiple strong baselines (including the previous state-of-the-art extended grid model from Elsner and Charniak, 2011) makes the results convincing.

3. **Clear Distinction from Previous Neural Approaches**: The paper properly contextualizes its work against recent neural approaches to coherence (specifically Li and Hovy, 2014), highlighting key differences in their approach (operating over entity grids vs. sentence windows, using convolutional architecture vs. recurrent/recursive networks). This distinction is important and well-explained, preventing the paper from being seen as merely another neural reimplementation.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The authors provide sufficient technical details about their implementation (Theano, hyperparameters, training procedures) to allow for replication. The ablation study showing the impact of different components (Grid-CNN vs. Extended Grid-CNN) is particularly valuable for understanding the contribution of each aspect of their model.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Incorporation of Entity-Specific Features**: While the paper claims the model can "incorporate arbitrary entity-specific features," the authors only include three of the nine features used in the extended grid model (Elsner and Charniak, 2011). They don't provide a clear explanation for why these specific three features were chosen over the others, nor do they demonstrate that adding more features would further improve performance. This weakens the claim about the model's flexibility and potentially limits the model's full potential.

2. **Insufficient Statistical Significance Evidence**: The paper reports "absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating" with "p-value < 0.01" for significance. However, it does not provide the exact p-values for the most significant results. The paper mentions using an approximate randomization test with 10,000 iterations, but without the specific p-values, it's difficult to assess the true statistical significance of the improvements, especially for the more modest gains.

3. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis**: The paper provides strong quantitative results but lacks any qualitative analysis of the model's behavior. There are no examples showing what the model considers "coherent" or "incoherent" and why, or what features it's using to make those decisions. A qualitative analysis would provide deeper insights into the model's strengths and limitations and help readers understand whether the improvements are meaningful or just statistical.

4. **Inadequate Comparison to Recent Neural Models**: While the paper briefly mentions Li and Hovy (2014), it doesn't provide a detailed comparison or explain why their model is superior. There are other recent neural approaches to coherence (e.g., Li et al., 2014; Guinaudeau and Strube, 2013) that could have been compared against. The paper's claim that their model "achieves state of the art results" would be stronger with a more comprehensive comparison to recent neural approaches.

5. **Lack of Task-Specific Training Justification**: The paper trains the model on the discrimination task and then applies it to insertion and summary rating tasks without fine-tuning. The authors acknowledge that "the model that is trained to distinguish an original document from its random permutation may learn features that are not specific enough to distinguish documents when only one sentence differs." However, they don't explore how the model would perform if trained specifically on the insertion task, which could yield further improvements and better validate their claim about learning task-specific features.

## Questions to Authors

1. You mention that you only included three entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, and proper mention) instead of the full nine features from Elsner and Charniak (2011). Could you provide more detail on how you selected these specific three features, and what impact including additional features would have on performance?

2. The paper states that the Extended Grid-CNN yields "more gains (2.5%) in insertion" compared to the Extended Grid. Could you provide the exact p-value for this result to better assess the statistical significance of the improvement?

3. The paper mentions that you tried using Li and Hovy's distributed sentence model but got poor results (19.34% accuracy). Could you explain why you think this happened? Was it due to differences in the dataset, model architecture, or training procedure?

4. How do you think your model would perform if trained specifically on the insertion task instead of the discrimination task? Would this lead to further improvements, and if so, by how much?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of coherence modeling by addressing a clear limitation of existing entity grid models and demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple evaluation tasks. The technical approach is sound, and the evaluation is comprehensive. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing more detailed statistical evidence, including a qualitative analysis of the model's behavior, and expanding the comparison to include more recent neural approaches.

With revisions addressing these concerns, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field of discourse processing and coherence modeling. As it stands, it's a strong paper that would be suitable for acceptance with minor revisions.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept**
