

# ACL Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural network-based approach to text coherence modeling that builds upon the established entity grid framework (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors claim their model can capture longer-range entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features without suffering from the "curse of dimensionality" that plagues traditional entity grid models. They evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, reporting absolute improvements of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating over the current state-of-the-art entity grid models.

## Main Contributions (As I See Them)
1. Application of convolutional neural networks to the entity grid representation for coherence modeling
2. End-to-end training of coherence models that can learn task-specific features
3. Incorporation of entity-specific features within a neural framework

## Strengths
1. **Evaluation on standard coherence tasks**: The paper properly evaluates on three established coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) that have been used in prior work, providing a clear benchmark for comparison.

2. **Comparison with strong baselines**: The authors compare against multiple strong baselines including the extended entity grid model (E&C), which represents the current state-of-the-art for coherence modeling, and the graph-based model (Guinaudeau and Strube, 2013).

3. **Adequate statistical significance testing**: The authors report statistical significance (p-value < 0.01) for their improvements, which is appropriate for the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of meaningful novelty**: The core contribution is not a new idea but rather the application of a standard neural architecture (CNN) to an existing framework. The paper fails to demonstrate why a neural approach is fundamentally better than the established entity grid approach. The claim that the neural model "avoids the curse of dimensionality" is unsupported - the paper doesn't show how the neural model actually avoids this issue in practice, and the model's parameter count (with 150 filters, embedding size 100, etc.) suggests it may actually be more prone to overfitting than the discrete model, especially given the relatively small datasets used.

2. **Insufficient evidence for claimed advantages**: The paper reports "absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination" but doesn't provide context on whether these gains are practically significant. The baseline entity grid model already achieves 84.95% accuracy on discrimination, so a 4% absolute improvement (to 88.69%) may not be as impressive as it appears. The paper also fails to provide ablation studies showing why their specific neural architecture is better than alternatives (e.g., RNNs or transformers), making it impossible to determine what aspect of their approach is driving the improvements.

3. **Questionable claim about task-specific feature learning**: The paper claims that their model "learns task-specific high level features automatically" through end-to-end training, but the evaluation doesn't provide evidence that these features are actually task-specific. The same neural model is used across all three tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence), and the paper doesn't show that the features learned for one task are different from those learned for another task.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper dismisses Li and Hovy (2014) as having "very disappointing" results (19.34% accuracy), but doesn't compare their approach to more recent neural coherence models. The paper states that Li and Hovy's approach is "fundamentally different," but doesn't provide evidence that their approach is better than other neural approaches. This is a significant omission given the field's recent shift toward neural methods.

5. **Minimal analysis of failure cases**: The paper provides no examples of where the model fails, which would help readers understand the limitations of the approach. Without this, it's impossible to assess the true scope of the model's applicability.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims the neural model "avoids the curse of dimensionality" but provides no evidence for this. Could you provide specific examples showing how the neural model handles longer transitions (e.g., k=5-8) without suffering from sparsity issues that plague the discrete entity grid model?

2. The paper reports improvements of about 4% on discrimination, but the baseline entity grid model already achieves 84.95% accuracy. Could you provide context on whether these gains are meaningful in practical applications (e.g., how would this translate to improvements in downstream tasks like text generation or summarization)?

3. The paper states that "our model achieves state of the art results in all these tasks," but doesn't compare against more recent neural approaches. Could you please compare your model against recent neural coherence models beyond those mentioned in the paper?

4. The paper uses a small dataset for the summary coherence rating task (144 training pairs). How does the performance of your model compare to the baseline when using the same amount of training data (144 pairs) for both the neural and non-neural models?

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a reasonable extension of the entity grid model with some modest performance improvements, it fails to demonstrate meaningful novelty or sufficient evidence for the claimed advantages of the neural approach. The improvements reported are marginal and not contextualized in a way that demonstrates practical significance. The paper is more of a straightforward application of neural networks to an existing framework rather than a substantial contribution to the field. Given the skeptical perspective I maintain as a reviewer, I require more substantial evidence and a clearer justification for why this neural approach is fundamentally better than the established methods before I would consider accepting this paper.
