

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling text coherence that extends the entity grid framework. The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) discrete representations for grammatical roles lead to the curse of dimensionality, limiting transition length to k ≤ 3; and (2) feature extraction is decoupled from the target task, preventing learning of task-specific features. Their neural model addresses these limitations by using distributed representations, convolutional layers to model longer entity transitions (k=5-8), and end-to-end training to learn task-specific features. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, reporting consistent improvements over non-neural entity grid models.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A neural architecture that models long-range entity transitions (k=5-8) using convolutional networks, overcoming the dimensionality limitations of existing entity grid models.
2. An end-to-end training method that learns task-specific high-level features automatically, addressing the decoupled feature extraction problem in existing models.
3. Significant improvements over non-neural entity grid models across multiple coherence evaluation tasks (4% gain on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating).

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness of the Proposed Architecture:** The paper presents a well-motivated technical solution to a clear limitation in the existing entity grid framework. The authors correctly identify that discrete representations lead to the curse of dimensionality (Bengio et al., 2003), and their neural approach using distributed representations and convolutional layers provides a principled solution. The architecture is technically sound, with clear explanations of the lookup layer, convolutional operations, and pooling layers. The use of wide convolution to handle boundary entities and the incorporation of entity-specific features through modified grid entries are thoughtful design choices.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation on Standard Tasks:** The paper evaluates the model on three well-established coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), which are standard in the field. The evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines including the graph-based model and various extensions of the entity grid model. The results show consistent improvements across all tasks (Table 3 and Table 4), with statistical significance testing (p-value < 0.01) supporting the claims. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of pre-training on the WSJ dataset for the summary coherence task.

3. **Reproducibility and Practical Impact:** The authors provide detailed implementation information (Theano, hyperparameters, training procedures) and commit to releasing their code with the submission. This enhances reproducibility and practical impact. The improvements over existing models are substantial enough to suggest real-world applicability for text generation, summarization, and coherence scoring systems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis to Explain Performance Gains:** While the paper demonstrates quantitative improvements, it fails to provide qualitative analysis to explain *why* the neural approach works better. The authors state that their model "can capture long range entity transitions" and "learn task-specific high level features," but they don't show examples of how the neural model identifies different transition patterns or compare the features learned by the neural model to those used in the entity grid model. Without this analysis, it's difficult to understand the fundamental advantage of the neural approach beyond the quantitative results.

2. **Inadequate Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches:** The paper mentions that they experimented with the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014) but excluded it due to poor performance (19.34% accuracy on discrimination). However, they don't provide a thorough comparison to other recent neural approaches to coherence modeling, such as the recursive deep models for discourse parsing by Li et al. (2014) or other neural coherence models. This limits the context of their contribution and makes it difficult to assess how much of an improvement they're actually making over the state of the art.

3. **Overstated "State-of-the-Art" Claim:** The paper claims to achieve "state of the art results in all these tasks," but this claim is not fully supported. The comparison is only against existing entity grid models and the graph-based model. The paper does not compare against other neural approaches to coherence modeling, which might represent the true state of the art. The exclusion of the Li and Hovy (2014) model without sufficient explanation undermines the claim of being state-of-the-art.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the neural model can "capture long range entity transitions without overfitting on the training data" (Section 3). Could the authors provide more details on how they prevent overfitting when using longer transition lengths (k=5-8) compared to the standard entity grid models (k ≤ 3)?

2. The paper mentions that the Extended Grid-CNN model incorporates only a subset of the entity-specific features used in the Extended Grid model. Could the authors elaborate on which features they omitted and why they believe the subset they used is sufficient for good performance?

3. The paper uses pairwise ranking loss for training (Equation 5). How does this training approach compare to other training approaches for coherence models (e.g., using a regression loss for coherence scoring), and why did the authors choose pairwise ranking?
