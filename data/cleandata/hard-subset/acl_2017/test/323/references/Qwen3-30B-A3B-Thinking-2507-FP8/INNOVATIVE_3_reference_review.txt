

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors argue that existing entity grid models suffer from two limitations: 1) discrete representation of grammatical roles leading to the curse of dimensionality (limiting transition lengths to k ≤ 3), and 2) task-agnostic feature extraction. Their solution applies convolutional neural networks to model longer entity transitions (k=5-8) while incorporating entity-specific features through distributed representations.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1. A neural architecture that extends the entity grid model to capture longer-range entity transitions (5-8 instead of ≤3)
2. An end-to-end training approach for learning task-specific features
3. Incorporation of entity-specific features within a neural framework

However, these contributions represent an incremental improvement rather than a novel concept or direction in the field.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Identification**: The paper effectively identifies limitations of existing entity grid models, particularly the "curse of dimensionality" problem that restricts transition lengths. This is a well-motivated problem that the authors address with a reasonable solution.

2. **Thorough Evaluation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments on three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) with appropriate baselines and statistical significance testing. The evaluation framework is solid and well-executed.

3. **Technical Implementation**: The neural architecture is well-designed and properly implemented, with careful hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies. The use of convolutional layers to model entity transitions is technically sound and appropriately applied to the problem.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Nature of Contribution**: The core contribution is an incremental improvement to an existing model (neuralizing the entity grid) rather than a novel concept or direction. While the authors claim to "capture long range entity transitions," this is achieved through a standard application of CNNs to an existing framework, not a fundamentally new approach to coherence modeling. The 2-4% absolute gains reported are modest and do not represent a significant leap forward in the field.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Novelty**: The paper does not introduce a new theoretical framework for coherence modeling. The neural approach is essentially an engineering improvement to an existing model, not a conceptual breakthrough. The paper fails to convincingly argue why the neural approach fundamentally changes our understanding of coherence rather than just providing better feature learning.

3. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The authors mention Li and Hovy (2014) as a related neural approach but exclude it from their results due to "disappointing results" without providing a clear explanation. This is problematic as it prevents readers from understanding why their approach outperforms a similar neural method. A more thorough comparison would have strengthened the novelty claim.

4. **Overstated Impact**: The paper claims to "achieve state of the art results in all these tasks" but the improvements are relatively modest (2-4% absolute gains). In many NLP tasks, such gains are considered incremental rather than transformative. The paper overstates the significance of these improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your neural model fundamentally change our understanding of coherence, beyond just providing better feature learning? What specific theoretical insight does it provide that the entity grid model lacked?

2. The paper claims to "capture long range entity transitions" but doesn't clearly articulate how this capability translates to a meaningful improvement in coherence modeling. What specific coherence phenomena does the model capture that the previous model couldn't, and how does this translate to better performance?

3. The paper states that "our model achieves state of the art results," but the gains are modest (2-4% absolute). In the context of the field, how significant do you believe these improvements are, and what would need to be true for these improvements to be considered substantial rather than incremental?

## Overall Recommendation

Reject

The paper presents a well-executed technical improvement to an existing coherence model, but the contribution is fundamentally incremental rather than novel. While the methodology is sound and the evaluation is thorough, the paper fails to introduce a significant new concept or direction that would justify acceptance at ACL. The modest performance gains (2-4% absolute) do not represent a meaningful advancement in the field, and the paper does not convincingly argue for the theoretical novelty of its approach.

I would be willing to accept this paper if it had introduced a fundamentally new conceptual framework for coherence modeling or demonstrated significantly larger performance gains that represented a clear step forward. However, as presented, it's an incremental engineering improvement to an existing approach, which is not sufficient for acceptance at ACL under the review criteria that prioritize novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection.
