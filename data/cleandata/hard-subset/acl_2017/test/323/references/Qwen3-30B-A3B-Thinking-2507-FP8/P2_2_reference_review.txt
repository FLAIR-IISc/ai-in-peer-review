

# ACL Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary
This paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling local text coherence that builds upon the entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors address two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) their discrete representation of grammatical roles leads to the curse of dimensionality, limiting modeling to short transitions (k ≤ 3), and (2) feature extraction is decoupled from the target task. Their neural model uses distributed representations of entity transitions and incorporates entity-specific features through a convolutional neural network architecture. The model is evaluated on three coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, showing consistent improvements over prior work.

## Primary Contributions
1. **Neural architecture for modeling longer entity transitions**: The paper successfully applies convolutional neural networks to model entity transitions of length k=5-8, significantly exceeding the k≤3 limitation of prior entity grid models.
2. **End-to-end learning of task-specific features**: The authors propose a pairwise ranking approach for training the model, enabling automatic learning of task-specific features rather than using task-agnostic feature extraction.
3. **Integration of entity-specific features in a neural framework**: The paper demonstrates how entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention) can be effectively incorporated into the neural model.

## Strengths

1. **Significant performance improvements**: The paper demonstrates consistent and substantial improvements over existing models across three different coherence tasks (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating). These gains are statistically significant (p < 0.01) and represent state-of-the-art results for all tasks, which is a strong contribution to the field.

2. **Clear technical explanation**: The paper provides a well-structured and detailed explanation of the neural architecture, including the transformation of grammatical roles into distributed representations, the convolutional modeling of entity transitions, and the end-to-end training approach. The figures and equations are clear and effectively illustrate the proposed method.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The evaluation covers multiple coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence) using standard datasets (WSJ and DUC'03), which provides robust evidence for the model's effectiveness. The paper also includes a thorough comparison with multiple baseline models (random, graph-based, entity grid extensions).

4. **Reproducibility**: The authors have released their code, which is a valuable contribution to the community and facilitates reproducibility of their results.

5. **Practical relevance**: The paper clearly connects their approach to important NLP applications like text generation, summarization, and coherence scoring, demonstrating the practical utility of their method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the neural architecture**: While the application of CNNs to coherence modeling is novel, the architecture itself (embedding layer, convolution, pooling) is standard and not particularly innovative. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why CNNs are better suited for this task than other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs, Transformers), which would strengthen the technical contribution.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper lacks detailed ablation studies to identify which components of the model contribute most to the performance gains. For example, it would be valuable to know how much of the 4% improvement comes from the neural representation versus the convolutional layers versus the incorporation of entity-specific features. The paper mentions that Extended Grid-CNN outperforms Extended Grid with only a subset of features, but doesn't analyze which specific features were most beneficial.

3. **Inadequate comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper mentions they tried the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014) but got disappointing results, yet they don't provide a thorough comparison with more recent neural approaches to coherence modeling. The related work section should have included more recent neural coherence models to better position their contribution.

4. **Limited discussion of model limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of their approach. For instance, how does the model scale to longer documents? What are the computational costs compared to existing models? How would the model perform on documents with very different structures or in domains with different coherence patterns?

5. **Vague explanation of why the neural model works better**: While the paper states that the neural model can capture longer transitions, it doesn't provide concrete evidence that these longer transitions actually capture meaningful coherence patterns. The "Why it works" section is somewhat superficial and would benefit from more detailed analysis of the learned features.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the neural model achieves better results with fewer entity-specific features than the Extended Grid model. Could you elaborate on which specific features were most important and why the neural model could achieve good results with a subset of features?

2. You mention that the insertion task is more difficult than discrimination, and the performance gains are smaller on insertion. Could you provide more detailed analysis on whether this is due to the inherent difficulty of the task or because the model isn't trained specifically for insertion? Would fine-tuning on insertion data improve performance?

3. The paper uses WSJ as the main dataset, which has longer documents on average (21.5 sentences) compared to AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES (10-11 sentences). How would your model perform on shorter documents where the benefit of modeling longer transitions might be less pronounced?

4. The paper uses a convolutional approach for modeling entity transitions. How would the performance change if you used other sequence modeling approaches (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) for the same task? What are the specific advantages of using convolution for this particular problem?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a clear, well-executed contribution to the field of text coherence modeling. The technical approach is sound, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate significant improvements over prior work. While the neural architecture itself is not highly novel, its application to the coherence modeling problem is innovative and effective.

The paper would be strengthened by more detailed ablation studies, a more thorough comparison with recent neural approaches, and a better explanation of why the neural model works better than prior work. However, these are minor concerns that could be addressed in the author response period.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of text coherence modeling and provides a solid foundation for future work in this area.
