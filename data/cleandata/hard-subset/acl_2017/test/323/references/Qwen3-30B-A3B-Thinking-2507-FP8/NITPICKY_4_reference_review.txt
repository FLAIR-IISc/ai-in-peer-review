

# Review for "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a neural model for local coherence assessment that extends the entity grid approach (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008) using convolutional neural networks. The authors claim that their model can capture long-range entity transitions and incorporate arbitrary entity-specific features without suffering from the curse of dimensionality that plagues existing grid-based models. They evaluate their approach on three tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

The main contributions as I see them, ordered by strength:
1. A neural architecture that extends the entity grid model with distributed representations and convolutional layers to capture longer-range entity transitions (up to length 5-8) without the exponential parameter growth of traditional grid models.
2. An end-to-end training method for learning task-specific features automatically through pairwise ranking, which addresses the task-agnostic feature extraction limitation of previous models.
3. State-of-the-art results on three coherence evaluation tasks, with significant improvements over previous non-neural grid models.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Neural Architecture for Coherence Modeling**: The paper proposes a well-motivated neural extension of the entity grid model that effectively addresses the limitations of discrete representations in traditional approaches. The use of convolutional layers to model entity transitions is theoretically sound and aligns with successful applications of CNNs in other NLP tasks. The model's ability to learn long-range transitions (up to length 8) while avoiding the curse of dimensionality is a significant technical contribution.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Multiple Tasks**: The authors evaluate their model on three different coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), which provides a more robust assessment of model performance than previous work that often focuses on a single task. The inclusion of the summary coherence rating task is particularly valuable as it's a more challenging, real-world evaluation scenario.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The results show consistent improvements over non-neural baselines (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating), with statistically significant gains. The fact that even the basic Grid-CNN model (without entity-specific features) outperforms the extended grid model demonstrates the value of the neural approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Lack of Reproducibility Details**: The paper fails to provide essential details for reproducing the results. They mention using a coreference resolver but don't specify which one or how it was configured. They also don't provide details on the WSJ preprocessing steps, including how they handled the "excluded permutations that match the original document" issue. Without these details, the results cannot be verified or built upon.

2. **Inconsistent Citation Formatting and Missing Figure**: The references contain numerous formatting errors with question marks (e.g., "Barzilay and Lapata.,? 2005"). More critically, the paper refers to Figure 2 multiple times but doesn't include the figure in the text, making it impossible to understand the neural architecture being proposed. This is a serious omission for a paper describing a new model architecture.

3. **Exclusion of Li and Hovy (2014) Without Justification**: The authors state they excluded the Li and Hovy (2014) model because "the results on our dataset were very disappointing – accuracy of only 19.34% in discrimination." This is a major omission from their results table. They fail to explain why this model performed so poorly on their dataset, or whether they attempted to diagnose the issue (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, different preprocessing). This omission undermines the paper's claim of "state-of-the-art results" since they don't compare with a relevant neural model.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component of their model. For example, they don't show how much the entity-specific features contribute to the performance gains, or how the convolutional layers compare to a simple feedforward network. Without these studies, it's unclear which aspects of their approach are truly responsible for the improvements.

5. **Inadequate Discussion of Limitations**: The paper makes strong claims about the model's effectiveness but provides no discussion of its limitations or failure cases. A proper discussion of where the model fails would be valuable for understanding its boundaries and guiding future work. The brief mention of "the model that is trained to distinguish an original document from its random permutation may learn features that are not specific enough to distinguish documents when only one sentence differs" is too vague to be useful.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the coreference resolver used for entity extraction? Specifically, what system was used, and how was it configured for this task? Without this information, the results cannot be reproduced.

2. Why was the Li and Hovy (2014) model excluded from the results table? Did you attempt to diagnose why it performed so poorly on your dataset, or were you unable to get it to work at all? If you did attempt to diagnose, could you share those findings?

3. The paper refers to Figure 2 multiple times but the figure is missing from the submission. Could you provide the figure to clarify the neural architecture?

4. The paper mentions "pre-trained models" but doesn't explain how the pre-training was done. Did you use the same hyperparameters for pre-training as for the discrimination task? How many epochs were used for pre-training?

5. The paper states "We use 20 permutations of each document in the test set," but doesn't explain why 20 was chosen specifically. How does this compare to other studies in the literature, and why wasn't a different number (e.g., 10 or 50) used?

## Additional Comments

- The reference formatting is inconsistent throughout the paper (e.g., "Barzilay and Lapata.,? 2005" vs. "Barzilay and Lapata, 2008").
- The paper uses "entity grid" and "grid" interchangeably, which creates confusion.
- The phrase "the model that is trained to distinguish an original document from its random permutation may learn features that are not specific enough to distinguish documents when only one sentence differs" is vague and lacks technical precision.
- The paper claims "absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating" but doesn't provide the exact numbers for the summary coherence rating task in Table 4.

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper presents a technically sound neural extension of the entity grid model with promising results, the numerous minor but critical flaws (particularly the missing figure, lack of reproducibility details, and exclusion of a relevant baseline without explanation) prevent me from recommending acceptance. These issues significantly undermine the paper's credibility and the ability of the community to build upon this work. I recommend rejection due to the lack of sufficient detail to verify the claims and the omission of a relevant baseline comparison.
