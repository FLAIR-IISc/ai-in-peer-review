

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local text coherence that builds upon the entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008), which has been the dominant approach for coherence assessment. The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) their discrete representation of grammatical roles leads to the curse of dimensionality, limiting transition modeling to short lengths (k ≤ 3), and (2) their task-agnostic feature extraction prevents learning task-specific features.

The main contributions of the paper, in order of strength, are:
1. A neural architecture that uses distributed representations and convolutional layers to model long-range entity transitions (up to length 5-8) while avoiding the curse of dimensionality
2. An end-to-end training approach using pairwise ranking that automatically learns task-specific features
3. Comprehensive evaluation on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) showing consistent improvements over existing entity grid models

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a meaningful limitation in existing approaches**: The paper correctly identifies the key limitation of the entity grid model - the curse of dimensionality problem that prevents modeling longer entity transitions. The authors provide a solid theoretical explanation for why discrete representations are problematic for longer transitions (R^k transition probabilities become sparse as k increases), which is well-supported by references to Bengio et al. (2003). Their solution using distributed representations and convolutional layers is a natural fit for the grid structure of the entity model.

2. **Comprehensive and well-designed evaluation**: The paper evaluates on three standard tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) that have been established in the coherence modeling literature. The results show consistent improvements over previous entity grid models across all tasks (4% absolute gain on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary coherence rating). The authors provide detailed statistics on their dataset (WSJ corpus), which is larger than previous datasets used in this field (AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES), addressing a key concern about model generalizability. The use of pairwise ranking for training is well-justified by prior work (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008).

3. **Strong empirical results with clear significance**: The results demonstrate that the neural model consistently outperforms all non-neural baselines across all three tasks. The improvements are statistically significant (p<0.01) and meaningful in the context of coherence modeling, where gains of 1-5% are considered substantial. The paper also shows that pre-training on the discrimination task provides additional gains for the summary coherence task, which is a valuable observation about model transferability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of thorough comparison with related neural approaches**: The paper dismisses Li and Hovy (2014) as "very disappointing" without providing a detailed analysis of why their neural approach failed on this dataset. The authors state they "were not sure what could go wrong" but don't attempt to understand why their implementation of Li and Hovy's model performed poorly. This weakens their claim of novelty, as Li and Hovy (2014) also proposed a neural approach to coherence modeling, though with a different architecture (sentence-level embeddings vs. entity grids). A more thorough comparison with this related work would strengthen the paper.

2. **Insufficient analysis of what the model learns**: While the paper demonstrates that the neural model outperforms previous approaches, it provides no insight into what specific patterns the convolutional filters learn. The authors state "each filter detects a specific transition pattern (e.g., 'SS-O-X' for a coherent text)" but don't provide actual examples of learned patterns or show how these patterns correlate with coherence. A qualitative analysis of the filters would significantly strengthen the paper by demonstrating the model's ability to learn meaningful coherence patterns.

3. **Overstated claims about novelty and state-of-the-art results**: The paper claims "state of the art results in all these tasks" but only compares against entity grid models and a graph-based model. Without comparing to more recent neural approaches (including Li and Hovy, which they dismiss without proper analysis), the claim of "state of the art" is questionable. The paper should acknowledge that their approach is the first to apply neural networks to the entity grid representation specifically, but not necessarily the first neural approach to coherence modeling.

4. **Lack of discussion on computational complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the neural model compared to the entity grid model. While the authors claim their model can handle longer transitions (5-8 vs. 3), they don't provide any analysis of how this affects training or inference time. This is important for practical applications of the model, especially since the entity grid model has a clear computational complexity (O(R^k)) that the neural model would need to address.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper dismisses Li and Hovy (2014) as "very disappointing" with only 19.34% accuracy on discrimination. Could you provide more details on why their implementation of Li and Hovy's model performed poorly on your dataset? Did you try different hyperparameters or modifications to their approach to see if it could be made to work better?

2. The paper states that "each filter detects a specific transition pattern (e.g., 'SS-O-X' for a coherent text)" but doesn't provide any examples of actual patterns detected by the filters. Could you include a table showing examples of transition patterns learned by the convolutional filters and how they correlate with coherence?

3. The paper mentions that the neural model can handle longer transitions (5-8) but doesn't show how performance changes with transition length. Could you provide a table showing performance (on discrimination task) as a function of transition length (k=2,3,4,5,6,7,8) to demonstrate the benefits of longer transitions?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper addresses a meaningful limitation of existing coherence models with a well-motivated neural solution. The evaluation is comprehensive and the results are convincing. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing a more thorough comparison with related neural approaches and a qualitative analysis of what the model learns. The current version has solid technical contributions but overstates its novelty in the context of neural approaches to coherence modeling.

**Rating: Accept (with minor revisions)** - The paper makes a valuable contribution to the coherence modeling field with a clear solution to a well-identified problem, and the results are strong. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent contribution to ACL.
