

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary
The paper proposes a neural network architecture that applies convolutional layers to entity grids for text coherence assessment. The authors claim their model can capture longer entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features more effectively than existing entity grid models, which use discrete representations. The paper evaluates their approach on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, reporting modest improvements (4-5%) over non-neural baselines.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A straightforward application of convolutional neural networks to the entity grid representation for coherence assessment
2. Empirical demonstration of modest improvements (4-5%) over existing entity grid models on standard coherence tasks
3. An end-to-end training approach that allows for task-specific feature learning

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation:** The paper effectively identifies limitations of existing entity grid models (discrete representation leading to curse of dimensionality, task-agnostic feature extraction) and positions their neural approach as addressing these limitations. The motivation is well-grounded in established coherence modeling literature.

2. **Thorough evaluation on standard tasks:** The paper evaluates on three well-established coherence assessment tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) using standard datasets (WSJ, DUC'03). The evaluation methodology is sound and follows established practices in the field.

3. **Statistically significant results:** The reported improvements over non-neural baselines are statistically significant (p<0.01) across all three tasks, which is a strong point for the paper.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core approach:** The paper's main contribution is a straightforward application of convolutional neural networks to entity grids. This is not a novel application of neural networks to coherence modeling - many papers have applied CNNs to NLP tasks (e.g., Kim, 2014; Kalchbrenner et al., 2014). The authors don't convincingly argue that this specific application is novel or significantly better than existing approaches. The paper claims "neuralizing" the entity grid model, but this is a minor extension rather than a substantial innovation.

2. **Inadequate comparison with relevant neural approaches:** The paper dismisses Li and Hovy (2014) without proper comparison, stating "the results on our dataset were very disappointing – accuracy of only 19.34% in discrimination. We were not sure what could go wrong, therefore, we excluded it from our table of results." This is a serious weakness - the authors fail to provide a fair comparison with a recent neural approach to coherence modeling. They don't explain why their dataset is so different from the one Li and Hovy used, and they don't attempt to understand why their results differ.

3. **Insufficient analysis of why the neural model works better:** The paper provides no analysis of what specific patterns the convolutional filters are learning or why the neural approach outperforms the grid model. The paper claims the model "can capture long range entity transitions," but doesn't provide evidence that longer transition lengths actually lead to better performance compared to standard grid models (which typically use k=3). Without this analysis, it's difficult to believe the neural approach is capturing something meaningful about coherence that the grid model couldn't.

4. **Overstated claims of "state of the art":** The paper claims to achieve "state of the art results in all these tasks," but the improvements over the best non-neural baselines are modest (4-5%). In the context of coherence modeling, these are not large improvements. The paper should be more modest in its claims.

5. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show which components of the neural model (distributed representations, convolutional layers, entity-specific features) are actually contributing to the improvements. This makes it difficult to determine what aspects of the model are truly beneficial.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you exclude the Li and Hovy (2014) model from your comparison? Can you explain why this model performed so poorly on your dataset (19.34% accuracy in discrimination) compared to its reported performance on its original dataset? How does this compare to the performance of other neural models on your dataset?

2. The paper claims the model "can capture long range entity transitions" by using k=5-8, but doesn't provide evidence that longer transition lengths actually lead to better performance. Can you provide a comparison of model performance with different transition lengths (k=2,3,5,8) to demonstrate that longer transitions are beneficial?

3. Can you provide more detailed analysis of what specific patterns the convolutional filters are learning? For example, what are the most salient features detected by the filters in the grid, and how do these relate to coherence?

4. Have you performed ablation studies to show which components of your neural model (e.g., the distributed representations, the convolutional layers, the entity-specific features) are actually contributing to the improvements over the grid models?

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper presents a well-structured evaluation of a neural extension to an existing coherence model, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty. The core approach is a straightforward application of a well-known neural architecture (CNNs) to an existing representation (entity grids), and the reported improvements are modest. The paper fails to adequately compare with relevant neural approaches, provide sufficient analysis of why the neural approach works better, or justify its claims of "state of the art" results. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's contribution to be incremental rather than substantial, and the evidence provided is insufficient to justify the claims made.
