- Proposes a neural, convolutional entity-grid model that uses distributed representations to capture longer entity transitions (up to ~5–8) and avoid the discrete role dimensionality issue.  
- Introduces end-to-end training that learns task-specific coherence features rather than relying on task-agnostic feature extraction.  
- Evaluates on three standard tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) and reports significant, statistically significant gains over non-neural baselines.  
- Novel neural architecture is a strong technical contribution, with CNNs well-suited to capture local entity-grid patterns and longer transitions.  
- Strong empirical results: ~4% absolute gain on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, >4% on summary rating, with ablation studies showing component contributions.  
- Clear framing of limitations of prior entity-grid models and connection to Centering Theory; comprehensive multi-task evaluation.  
- Limited comparison to recent neural coherence models (e.g., Li & Hovy 2014) and no explanation for that model’s poor performance on the WSJ dataset.  
- No analysis or visualization of what convolutional filters learn; lacks examples of detected entity-transition patterns (e.g., "S-S-O").  
- Insufficient evidence of generalization: no learning/validation curves or per-epoch performance to demonstrate against overfitting.  
- Asks authors to explain Li & Hovy’s poor WSJ results (data vs. implementation), provide filter pattern examples/visualizations, and justify the choice of pairwise ranking versus other training approaches.  
- Recommendation: accept — well-motivated, sound architecture with strong results; weaknesses are addressable in the camera-ready version.
