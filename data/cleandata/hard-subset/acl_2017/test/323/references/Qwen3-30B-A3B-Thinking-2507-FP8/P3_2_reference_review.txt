

# Peer Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local text coherence that extends the popular entity grid representation (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors argue that traditional entity grid models suffer from two limitations: 1) discrete representation of grammatical roles leads to the curse of dimensionality when modeling longer transitions (k ≤ 3), and 2) feature extraction is decoupled from the target task. Their neural model uses distributed representations of entity transitions and entity-specific features, along with a convolutional architecture that can capture longer transitions (k=5-8) without overfitting. The model is trained end-to-end using pairwise ranking loss. The authors evaluate on three standard coherence tasks: sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and summary coherence rating, reporting consistent improvements over previous entity grid models.

## Main Contributions

1. **Novel application of convolutional neural networks to model long-range entity transitions in coherence modeling** - The authors successfully overcome the curse of dimensionality limitation in traditional entity grid models by using distributed representations and convolutional layers to model transitions of length 5-8, rather than the typical k ≤ 3.

2. **Demonstration of effective integration of entity-specific features within a neural framework** - The authors show that incorporating entity-specific features (like named entity types) in a distributed representation leads to better performance than discrete feature representations used in prior work.

3. **End-to-end training approach that learns task-specific features automatically** - The pairwise ranking training method allows the model to adapt to specific coherence tasks without task-agnostic feature extraction.

## Strengths

1. **Strong technical justification for the neural approach** - The paper provides a clear and compelling argument for why convolutional neural networks are well-suited for coherence modeling. The authors correctly identify the curse of dimensionality problem in traditional entity grid models and demonstrate how their neural approach overcomes this limitation. The use of wide convolution and max-pooling is particularly well-motivated for capturing salient features across the entity grid.

2. **Comprehensive and well-designed evaluation** - The authors evaluate on all three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), which is the gold standard for coherence modeling. The results show consistent improvements over strong baselines (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating), which is significant for the field. The inclusion of both WSJ and DUC'03 datasets provides a good balance between large-scale training and task-specific evaluation.

3. **Clear demonstration of neural advantages over discrete representations** - The paper successfully demonstrates that their neural model can effectively model longer entity transitions (k=5-8) without overfitting, which is impossible with traditional entity grid models. This is a fundamental advancement that addresses a core limitation in the field.

4. **Practical implementation and reproducibility** - The authors have released their code, providing detailed hyperparameter settings, and using standard NLP tools (Theano, Brown coherence toolkit). This makes their work highly reproducible and useful for other researchers.

5. **Good theoretical grounding in coherence theory** - The paper properly situates its work within the broader literature on coherence modeling, correctly citing key papers like Centering Theory (Grosz et al., 1995) and the entity grid framework (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of thorough ablation study on entity-specific features** - The paper claims that entity-specific features are "crucial" for coherence modeling but fails to provide a proper ablation study showing the contribution of each feature. The authors state they "include only three entity-specific features" but don't demonstrate how performance changes as they add each feature. This is a significant omission because it's unclear whether the gains from Extended Grid-CNN over Grid-CNN are due to the neural architecture or just the additional features.

2. **Questionable comparison with Li and Hovy (2014)** - The authors exclude Li and Hovy's neural model because "the results on our dataset were very disappointing – accuracy of only 19.34% in discrimination." This is extremely low (well below the random baseline of 50%), yet they don't provide any analysis of why this model performed so poorly. This could be due to implementation issues, hyperparameter settings, or data preprocessing differences. Without this analysis, the comparison is not convincing.

3. **Inconsistent dataset usage across tasks** - The authors use WSJ for most experiments but DUC'03 for the summary coherence rating task. They justify this by stating WSJ is "larger and more like normal informative articles," but this makes the comparison between WSJ-trained models and DUC'03 evaluation questionable. There's no analysis of whether the coherence patterns in WSJ are representative of those in DUC'03 summaries, which is critical for evaluating transfer learning.

4. **Lack of interpretability and failure analysis** - The paper claims that "each filter detects a specific transition pattern (e.g., 'SS-O-X' for a coherent text)" but provides no evidence to support this claim. Without interpretability, it's hard to verify that the model is learning meaningful coherence patterns rather than just memorizing the training data. Similarly, there's no analysis of where the model fails, which would help identify limitations and guide future work.

5. **Overstatement of the novelty of neural approach** - The paper frames their work as "neuralizing" the entity grid model, but this is a relatively straightforward application of neural techniques to an existing framework. The core insight is that CNNs can model longer transitions, which is valuable, but the paper overstates the novelty by not adequately comparing to other neural approaches that might have similar capabilities.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that Li and Hovy (2014) performed poorly (19.34% accuracy in discrimination), which is far below the random baseline. Could you provide more details about why their model performed so poorly on your dataset? Was it due to implementation issues, hyperparameters, or data preprocessing?

2. For the summary coherence rating task, you train on WSJ but evaluate on DUC'03. How do you justify this approach given that WSJ is a different domain from DUC'03 summaries? Have you tried training directly on DUC'03 to see if performance improves?

3. The paper claims that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." Could you provide a more detailed ablation study showing how performance changes as you add each additional entity-specific feature (named entity type, salience, proper mention)?

4. The paper states that the model can capture transitions of length 5-8. Could you provide specific examples of coherence patterns that the model has learned for k=5-8 that are different from patterns learned for k=3 (the typical length in previous work)?

5. The paper claims "significant superiority" with p-value < 0.01 but doesn't specify how many random seeds were used for the statistical significance tests. Could you clarify the number of random seeds used for the significance tests?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the coherence modeling literature by successfully applying neural techniques to overcome a fundamental limitation of traditional entity grid models. The results are convincing and the evaluation is comprehensive. However, the lack of ablation studies on entity-specific features and the questionable comparison with Li and Hovy (2014) are significant weaknesses. The paper would be substantially strengthened with a more thorough analysis of the contribution of each entity-specific feature and a proper explanation for the poor performance of the Li and Hovy model.

**Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions)**

The paper makes a clear, valuable contribution to the field, and the weaknesses identified are not severe enough to warrant rejection. With the authors addressing the questions above (particularly the ablation study and the Li and Hovy comparison), this would be a strong paper for ACL.
