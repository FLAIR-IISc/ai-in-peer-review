

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for text coherence assessment. The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) they use discrete representations for grammatical roles and features, leading to the curse of dimensionality that limits transition modeling to short lengths (k ≤ 3), and (2) feature extraction is decoupled from target tasks, preventing learning of task-specific features.

The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):

1. **Neural architecture for modeling long-range entity transitions**: The paper presents a convolutional neural network that uses distributed representations of entity transitions to capture longer-range dependencies (k = 5-8) without suffering from the curse of dimensionality, addressing a fundamental limitation of prior entity grid models.

2. **End-to-end training approach for task-specific feature learning**: The authors introduce a pairwise ranking method that enables the model to learn task-specific features automatically through end-to-end training, rather than using task-agnostic feature extraction as in prior work.

3. **Empirical validation across multiple coherence tasks**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over existing entity grid models across three established coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), with state-of-the-art results on all tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification and solution of a well-motivated limitation**: The paper effectively identifies the "curse of dimensionality" problem in existing entity grid models (where transition length k is limited to ≤3 due to sparse probability estimation) and provides a technically sound solution using distributed representations and convolutional layers. The explanation of how their approach overcomes this limitation (allowing k=5-8 without overfitting) is clear and well-supported by the experimental results.

2. **Comprehensive and consistent empirical validation**: The evaluation across three distinct coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) provides strong, consistent evidence of the model's effectiveness. The reported absolute improvements (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, >4% on summary coherence rating) are significant and consistent across multiple tasks, demonstrating the robustness of the approach. The use of multiple baselines (random, graph-based, entity grid variants) strengthens the evaluation.

3. **Thorough experimental setup and implementation details**: The paper provides detailed hyperparameter settings (Table 2), training procedures, and evaluation protocols. The authors' decision to use the larger WSJ corpus instead of smaller specialized corpora (AIRPLANES, EARTHQUAKES) is well-justified and beneficial for deep learning models. The code release further enhances reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the neural architecture**: The core neural architecture (embedding lookup, convolutional layers, max-pooling, linear output) is standard in NLP sequence modeling (e.g., Kim, 2014; Kalchbrenner et al., 2014). While the application to coherence modeling is novel, the neural components themselves are not innovative. The contribution is primarily about applying an established architecture to a new domain rather than introducing a novel neural approach.

2. **Incomplete comparison to recent neural coherence models**: The authors mention they tried Li and Hovy (2014) but found poor results (19.34% accuracy in discrimination), yet they don't provide a thorough comparison or explanation for this discrepancy. The paper doesn't compare to more recent neural coherence models that might have been published after 2014, which weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" results.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components. For example, it's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the neural architecture itself versus the end-to-end training approach. The authors don't show experiments varying the convolution window size, number of filters, or other key hyperparameters to demonstrate their impact on performance.

4. **Limited analysis of model behavior**: The paper lacks qualitative analysis of what the model has learned. There are no examples showing which transition patterns the convolutional filters detect (e.g., "SS-O-X" as mentioned in the paper), nor any analysis of whether the learned patterns align with coherence theories. This makes it difficult to assess whether the model is learning meaningful coherence patterns.

5. **Performance limitations on smaller datasets**: The paper acknowledges that their neural models "may not learn well" for the summary coherence rating task due to the small training set (144 pairs), requiring pre-training from the discrimination task. This suggests the approach may not generalize well to tasks with limited training data, which is a significant limitation for real-world applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions poor results with Li and Hovy (2014) (19.34% accuracy in discrimination). Could you provide more details on why this model performed so poorly on your dataset? Were there specific implementation issues or hyperparameter choices that caused this discrepancy?

2. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the neural architecture versus the end-to-end training approach. Could you add experiments showing the performance impact of removing the neural components while keeping the pairwise training, and vice versa?

3. The authors use only three entity-specific features from Elsner and Charniak (2011) for simplicity. Could you explain why these particular features were chosen, and whether adding more features would yield further improvements?

4. How does the computational complexity (training time, inference time) of your neural model compare to the existing entity grid models? Given the standard nature of the neural architecture, this comparison is important for assessing practical applicability.
