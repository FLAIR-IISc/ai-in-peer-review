

# ACL Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

## Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a neural extension of the entity grid model for local coherence assessment. The authors identify two key limitations of traditional entity grid models: (1) their discrete representation of grammatical roles and features leads to the curse of dimensionality, limiting modeling of long-range transitions (k ≤ 3), and (2) their feature extraction is task-agnostic rather than learning task-specific features. They propose a convolutional neural network architecture that uses distributed representations to model longer entity transitions (k=5-8) and incorporates entity-specific features. The model is trained end-to-end using pairwise ranking loss. The authors evaluate their approach on three established coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, showing consistent improvements over existing entity grid models.

## Main Contributions

1. **A neural architecture that overcomes limitations of traditional entity grid models by using distributed representations to model longer entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features.** This is the primary technical contribution of the paper.

2. **An end-to-end training method using pairwise ranking that allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically.** This addresses a key limitation of previous grid-based models.

3. **Demonstrated improvements over existing entity grid models across multiple evaluation tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results on all three tasks.** The empirical results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation for the work:** The paper clearly identifies a meaningful limitation in existing coherence models (the curse of dimensionality due to discrete representations) and provides a well-justified solution. The authors effectively explain why the traditional grid model's limitations are problematic for modeling coherence.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple established tasks:** The paper evaluates the model on three different coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) using standard datasets (WSJ and DUC'03), which allows for fair comparison with prior work. The consistent improvements across all three tasks (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, 4%+ on summary coherence) provide strong empirical support for the approach.

3. **Technical soundness of the neural architecture:** The proposed CNN-based architecture is well-motivated and appropriately adapted to the coherence modeling problem. The use of distributed representations to overcome the curse of dimensionality is a natural extension of the entity grid model, and the implementation details (wide convolution, max pooling) are well-justified.

4. **Clear comparison with strong baselines:** The paper compares against multiple strong baselines, including the best-performing entity grid variants (Elsner and Charniak, 2011) and a graph-based model (Guinaudeau and Strube, 2013), which are all relevant to the coherence modeling community.

5. **Appropriate use of statistical significance testing:** The paper uses appropriate statistical tests (approximate randomization test with 10,000 iterations) to establish the significance of the improvements, which strengthens the empirical claims.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the neural approach:** While the application to coherence modeling is novel, the core neural architecture (CNN for sequence modeling) is not novel in NLP. Similar approaches have been used for sentence classification (Kim, 2014) and other NLP tasks. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how their specific application to coherence modeling differs from these prior works or why their approach is particularly suited to coherence.

2. **Lack of in-depth analysis of the neural model's behavior:** The paper demonstrates that the neural model outperforms traditional models but provides limited insight into *why* it works better. There are no examples of what specific patterns the convolutional filters are learning, no analysis of the learned embeddings, and no discussion of how the distributed representations capture coherence-relevant information. This limits the paper's theoretical contribution.

3. **Inadequate comparison with more recent neural approaches:** The paper mentions that they tried a distributed sentence model (Li and Hovy, 2014) but got disappointing results, so they excluded it from the main results. However, they don't compare with more recent neural coherence models or explain why their approach would outperform these newer methods. This weakens the claim that their approach is state-of-the-art.

4. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components of their model (e.g., the effect of using distributed representations vs. the CNN architecture vs. incorporating entity-specific features). This makes it difficult to understand which aspects of their approach are most important.

5. **Limited discussion of computational cost and scalability:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training and using their model compared to traditional grid models. For a model that claims to handle longer transitions (k=5-8 vs. traditional k≤3), it's important to understand if the increased model capacity comes with significant computational overhead.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the specific patterns that the convolutional filters are learning? For example, could you show examples of the filters that detect coherent patterns (e.g., 'SS-O-X') versus incoherent patterns?

2. The paper mentions that the Extended Grid-CNN incorporates only a subset of the Extended Grid features (only 3 out of 9). Could you elaborate on which features were excluded and why? How would including all 9 features affect the model's performance?

3. Could you explain why the distributed sentence model (Li and Hovy, 2014) performed so poorly on your WSJ dataset? Was this due to dataset characteristics (e.g., WSJ vs. the AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES datasets used in their work), or are there specific issues with applying that model to coherence assessment?

4. How does the training time and inference time of your model compare to the traditional entity grid models? Given that your model handles longer transitions (k=5-8 vs. k≤3), is there a significant computational cost increase?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend a **weak accept** for this paper. The paper makes a solid contribution to the coherence modeling literature by proposing a neural extension of the entity grid model that demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple evaluation tasks. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are compelling.

However, the paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of *why* the neural model works better, stronger comparison with more recent neural approaches, and additional ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components. Addressing these weaknesses would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and impact.

The paper would be stronger with a more nuanced discussion of the novelty of the approach, including a clearer comparison with how similar neural techniques have been applied to other NLP tasks. The authors should also provide more insight into the learned representations of the model to better explain why it outperforms traditional methods.

Overall, this is a well-executed study that advances the state of the art in coherence modeling, but the theoretical insights could be deeper and the comparison with other neural approaches more thorough.
