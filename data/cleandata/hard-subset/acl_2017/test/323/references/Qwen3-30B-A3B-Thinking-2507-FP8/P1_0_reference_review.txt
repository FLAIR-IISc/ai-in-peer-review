

# Peer Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local text coherence based on the entity grid representation, which has been a standard approach in coherence modeling. The authors argue that existing entity grid models are limited by their discrete representations of grammatical roles and features (leading to the curse of dimensionality) and by their task-agnostic feature extraction. Their proposed model uses distributed representations of entity transitions and convolutional layers to model longer-range entity transitions (up to 5-8 instead of the typical 3 in previous work), while also incorporating entity-specific features. The model is trained end-to-end using pairwise ranking loss to learn task-specific features. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination (identifying the correct sentence order), insertion (finding the correct position for a removed sentence), and summary coherence rating.

## Main Contributions

1. A neural architecture that effectively models long-range entity transitions (length 5-8) using convolutional layers, overcoming the curse of dimensionality limitation of existing entity grid models.
2. An end-to-end training method that learns task-specific high-level features automatically for coherence assessment.
3. State-of-the-art results across three standard coherence evaluation tasks, with absolute improvements of 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification and effective addressing of a genuine limitation in existing coherence models**: The paper correctly identifies that the discrete representation approach in entity grid models leads to the curse of dimensionality problem, limiting the modeling of long-range transitions. The authors' solution using distributed representations and convolutional layers is well-motivated and directly addresses this limitation. The ability to model transitions of length 5-8 (vs. the typical k≤3 in previous work) is a significant technical contribution that directly addresses this limitation.

2. **Comprehensive and well-designed experimental evaluation**: The paper evaluates the model on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) across multiple datasets. The results consistently show improvements over strong baselines, including the extended entity grid model (Elsner and Charniak, 2011). The authors also provide a good justification for using the WSJ corpus over smaller, more constrained corpora, which is important for deep learning models that require large training data.

3. **Strong empirical results with statistical significance**: The results demonstrate consistent improvements across all three tasks, with statistically significant gains (p<0.01) in most cases. The paper reports average results over five random seeds to avoid randomness, and provides detailed hyperparameter tuning information. The fact that their model outperforms previous state-of-the-art results on all tasks is a strong point.

4. **Good code release and reproducibility**: The authors have released their code, which is a significant contribution to the community and will facilitate reproducibility of their results.

5. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is well-written, with a logical flow from problem statement to proposed solution to evaluation. The architecture is clearly illustrated in Figure 2, and the technical details are well-explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the neuralization of the entity grid is a reasonable idea, the core components (convolutional layers, distributed representations) are standard in NLP. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate from prior work, particularly Li and Hovy (2014), who also proposed a neural approach for coherence assessment. The authors mention that they tried Li and Hovy's model but got poor results (19.34% accuracy), but they don't explain why. This is a significant weakness as it leaves the reader wondering whether their model is fundamentally better or if it's just a matter of implementation details or dataset differences.

2. **Insufficient analysis of why the model works**: The paper shows strong empirical results but doesn't provide enough insight into why the neural model works better. For example, they don't show examples of what specific patterns the convolutional filters learn to detect, nor do they analyze which features are most important. This limits the interpretability of their model and makes it harder to understand the underlying mechanisms.

3. **Lack of comparison to the most recent work**: While the paper compares to existing entity grid models, it doesn't provide a direct comparison with the latest coherence models beyond those that use entity grids. For example, there have been recent advances in neural coherence models (e.g., using transformers) that could provide a more relevant comparison.

4. **Questionable choice of entity-specific features**: The paper states that they incorporate entity-specific features but only use three of the nine features from Elsner and Charniak (2011). It's unclear why they chose these specific features and whether including more features would lead to further improvements. The paper doesn't provide any ablation study to show the contribution of each feature.

5. **No discussion of computational complexity**: The paper claims their model can handle longer transitions (5-8) but doesn't discuss the computational complexity compared to previous approaches. For a model that processes entity grids with potentially long transition sequences, this could be a significant issue in practice.

## Questions to Authors

1. You mention that you tried Li and Hovy's model (2014) but got poor results (19.34% accuracy). Could you elaborate on why you got such poor results with their model, and whether you believe your model is fundamentally different or if it's just a matter of implementation details or dataset differences?

2. The paper states that your model can handle transitions of length 5-8, but doesn't explain why these specific lengths work best. Could you provide more insight into how the convolutional filters learn to detect these longer transitions, and whether there's a pattern to the optimal transition lengths?

3. You mention incorporating entity-specific features but only use three of the nine features from Elsner and Charniak (2011). Could you explain your selection criteria for these specific features, and whether you've considered including more features (e.g., all nine) to see if performance would improve further?

4. The paper claims your model achieves state-of-the-art results on all three tasks, but doesn't directly compare to the most recent coherence models beyond the entity grid approaches. Could you clarify how your results compare to the latest neural coherence models (e.g., using transformers) that have been proposed since the entity grid work?

5. The paper mentions using pre-trained models from the discrimination task for the summary coherence rating task. Could you provide more details on how this pre-training works and whether it's necessary for good performance on the summary task, or if your model can achieve good results without pre-training?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend this paper for acceptance at ACL. The paper addresses a clear limitation in existing coherence models with a well-motivated neural approach, provides comprehensive experimental evaluation showing consistent improvements over strong baselines, and is well-written with code released for reproducibility. While there are some weaknesses in terms of novelty and analysis, the empirical results are strong and the contribution is meaningful for the coherence modeling community. The paper makes a solid case for the utility of neural methods in coherence modeling, and the results will likely be valuable for researchers working on text generation, summarization, and coherence assessment.
