

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a neural architecture for text coherence assessment that extends the traditional entity grid model. The entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008) represents text as a grid capturing entity transitions across sentences, but has limitations in handling longer transitions (k≤3) and incorporating entity-specific features due to its discrete representation. The authors propose a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates on distributed representations of entity transitions, allowing for modeling longer-range dependencies and incorporating entity-specific features without exponential parameter growth. They evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Neural architecture for coherence modeling:** The authors present a convolutional neural network that overcomes the limitations of discrete entity grid models by using distributed representations and convolutional layers to capture longer entity transitions (k=5-8) and incorporate entity-specific features without exponential parameter growth.

2. **End-to-end training method for coherence assessment:** They introduce a pairwise ranking approach that trains the neural model end-to-end, allowing it to learn task-specific features automatically rather than using a task-agnostic feature extraction process.

3. **Empirical validation of the approach:** The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over existing entity grid models on three different coherence evaluation tasks, with statistically significant gains across all tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification and solution of limitations in existing models:** The paper effectively identifies two key limitations of the entity grid model (discrete representation leading to curse of dimensionality, and task-agnostic feature extraction) and proposes a neural solution that directly addresses these issues. The authors correctly argue that the discrete representation limits the model to short transitions (k≤3), while the neural approach allows for longer transitions (k=5-8) without overfitting, which is a significant technical contribution to the field.

2. **Strong empirical results across multiple evaluation tasks:** The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over previous state-of-the-art models on three different coherence tasks. The improvements are substantial (4% absolute gain on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating) and statistically significant (p<0.01). This robust performance across multiple tasks strongly supports the effectiveness of their approach.

3. **Well-designed evaluation with appropriate baselines:** The authors use standard datasets (WSJ for sentence ordering, DUC'03 for summary coherence) and compare against multiple strong baselines, including the entity grid model and its extensions. The ablation study (Grid-CNN vs. Extended Grid-CNN) effectively demonstrates the contribution of incorporating entity-specific features.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to recent neural NLP approaches:** While the application to coherence modeling is novel, the core technique (using CNNs for sequence modeling) is a standard approach in NLP (e.g., Kim, 2014; Kalchbrenner et al., 2014). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from these prior works, and the contribution appears to be more of an application of existing neural techniques rather than a novel neural architecture. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their approach differs from or improves upon these standard neural NLP techniques.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent neural coherence models:** The authors mention Li and Hovy (2014) but dismiss their model as "very disappointing" without sufficient explanation or analysis. However, they don't compare their approach to other recent neural coherence models that might have been published after Li and Hovy. For example, the paper by Li et al. (2014) on "Recursive deep models for discourse parsing" might be relevant, but it's not directly compared. This omission weakens the claim that their model achieves "state of the art results."

3. **Insufficient analysis of model behavior and interpretability:** The paper claims improvements but doesn't provide enough insight into why the neural model outperforms the entity grid model. There is no analysis of what specific transition patterns the neural model learns that the entity grid model misses. This lack of interpretability limits the contribution to the field and makes it difficult to understand the mechanisms behind the improved performance.

4. **Questionable dataset choice for sentence ordering:** The authors justify using WSJ over AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES corpora because it's larger and more "normal" in style. However, this choice might not be fully justified. The WSJ corpus is news text, which has different coherence patterns than the domain-specific AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES corpora. The authors don't provide evidence that their model generalizes well across different domains, which is a key concern for coherence models. This limits the applicability of their findings.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors mention that they tried the Li and Hovy (2014) model but got "very disappointing" results (19.34% accuracy on discrimination). Can they provide more details on why their implementation failed, and whether they believe this is due to the model itself or implementation issues?

2. The paper states that the Extended Grid-CNN model incorporates only three entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, and proper mention), while the Extended Grid model uses 9 features. Why did the authors choose to use a subset of features for the neural model? What would be the impact if they included all 9 features?

3. The paper claims "pretraining gives further improvements" for the summary coherence task. However, the results show that the pre-trained models are only compared to the Grid model, not to the Extended Grid model. How do the pre-trained Extended Grid-CNN models perform compared to the Extended Grid model?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a clear and well-executed application of neural techniques to the coherence modeling task, with strong empirical results across multiple evaluation tasks. The core contribution is the neural architecture that overcomes limitations of the entity grid model, particularly by enabling longer entity transitions. However, the novelty is somewhat limited compared to the broader field of neural NLP, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent neural approaches and a deeper analysis of why their model works better.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions:
1. Address the limited novelty by better differentiating the approach from standard neural NLP techniques.
2. Expand the comparison to include more recent neural coherence models.
3. Provide more detailed analysis of model behavior and why the neural model outperforms the entity grid model.

With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling.
