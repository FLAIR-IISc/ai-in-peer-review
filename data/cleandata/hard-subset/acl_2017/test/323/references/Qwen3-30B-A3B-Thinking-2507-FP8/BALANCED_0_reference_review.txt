

# Review for "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural architecture for text coherence modeling that extends the entity grid framework (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) discrete representations of grammatical roles lead to the curse of dimensionality (limiting transition lengths to k ≤ 3), and (2) feature extraction is decoupled from the target task. Their model transforms grammatical roles into distributed representations, uses convolutional layers to model longer entity transitions (up to 5-8), and incorporates entity-specific features without exponential parameter growth. The model is trained end-to-end using pairwise ranking for coherence assessment tasks. The authors evaluate their approach on three tasks: discrimination (ordering sentences), insertion (locating removed sentences), and summary coherence rating, reporting improvements over non-neural entity grid models.

## Main Contributions
1. A neural architecture that overcomes the curse of dimensionality limitation of previous entity grid models by using distributed representations and convolutional layers, enabling modeling of longer entity transitions (5-8 instead of ≤3).
2. A method to incorporate entity-specific features (like named entity types) without exponential parameter growth, by embedding these features with grammatical roles.
3. An end-to-end training approach using pairwise ranking that allows automatic learning of task-specific features.

## Strengths

1. **Technical innovation addressing a clear limitation**: The paper identifies and successfully addresses a well-known limitation of entity grid models (the curse of dimensionality) by leveraging distributed representations and convolutional layers. The authors demonstrate this by showing their model can effectively use longer transition lengths (5-8) without overfitting, while previous models were limited to k ≤ 3. This is a significant technical contribution that directly advances the state of the art in coherence modeling.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple tasks**: The paper evaluates the model on three distinct coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), which is a strong practice for coherence modeling. The authors compare against multiple strong baselines (including the extended entity grid model by Elsner and Charniak, which is the best-performing non-neural model), and report consistent improvements across all tasks (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence). The statistical significance of these improvements is properly established.

3. **Clear technical description and comparison to related work**: The paper provides a well-organized and intuitive explanation of the neural architecture, building logically on the existing entity grid framework. The authors effectively contrast their approach with related work, particularly Li and Hovy (2014), highlighting key differences in how the models operate (entity grids vs. sentence window-based approaches). The explanation of how convolutional layers can model arbitrary-length transitions is particularly strong.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of justification for corpus choice**: The authors chose to use the WSJ corpus for their experiments, which is larger than the AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES corpora used in prior work. However, they don't adequately address the potential issue that WSJ articles might be more structured than the other corpora, which could affect the generalizability of their results. They don't provide any analysis of whether their model would perform similarly on the smaller, more constrained corpora used in previous work. This is a significant oversight given that prior coherence models were evaluated on these smaller datasets.

2. **Insufficient demonstration of the neural approach's advantage**: The authors claim their model can incorporate entity-specific features without exponential parameter growth, but they don't provide a clear comparison of model size or training time between their neural model and an extended entity grid model with the same number of features. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the neural approach actually provides a meaningful advantage in terms of model complexity and training efficiency.

3. **Lack of analysis of insertion task performance**: The authors note that their model achieves lower gains on the insertion task compared to discrimination (2.5% vs. 4%), and suggest this might be because insertion is a harder task. However, they don't provide any analysis of why this is the case or whether the features learned for discrimination are transferable to insertion. Without this analysis, it's hard to determine whether the performance gap is due to the task difficulty or limitations in the model architecture.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors chose WSJ over AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES corpora due to size, but the WSJ corpus is more structured and informative than the other corpora. Could the authors provide results on one of the smaller corpora (e.g., AIRPLANES) to demonstrate that their model generalizes to more constrained text types?

2. The paper claims the neural model avoids exponential parameter growth when incorporating entity-specific features, but doesn't provide a comparison of model size or training time between their neural model and an extended entity grid model with the same number of features. Could the authors provide this comparison to better demonstrate the practical advantages of their approach?

3. The model achieves lower gains on the insertion task compared to discrimination. Could the authors provide an analysis of whether the features learned for discrimination are transferable to insertion, or whether the model would benefit from task-specific fine-tuning on the insertion task? This would help clarify whether the performance gap is due to task difficulty or model limitations.

## Recommendation

The paper presents a solid technical contribution that addresses a clear limitation in coherence modeling. The neural architecture successfully overcomes the curse of dimensionality problem of existing entity grid models, and the evaluation across multiple coherence tasks is comprehensive and convincing. However, the lack of justification for corpus choice and insufficient demonstration of the neural approach's practical advantages are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

The paper has strong technical merit and makes a meaningful contribution to coherence modeling. While the weaknesses are significant, they are addressable through additional experiments and analysis that could be completed in a revision. The authors have already released their code, which is a positive sign of their commitment to reproducibility. With appropriate revisions addressing the concerns raised above, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
