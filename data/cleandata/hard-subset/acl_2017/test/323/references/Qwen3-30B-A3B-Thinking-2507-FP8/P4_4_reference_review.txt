

# Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural network-based approach to text coherence assessment that extends the traditional entity grid framework. The authors address two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) their discrete representation of grammatical roles limits them to short transitions (k ≤ 3), and (2) their task-agnostic feature extraction limits their ability to learn task-specific features. The proposed model uses distributed representations and a convolutional neural network to model longer entity transitions (k = 5-8) and learns task-specific features through end-to-end training.

## Main Contributions
1. A neural architecture that models long-range entity transitions (up to length 5-8) using convolutional layers, overcoming the "curse of dimensionality" problem in traditional entity grid models.
2. An end-to-end training method that learns task-specific features automatically, rather than using task-agnostic feature extraction as in previous models.
3. A method for incorporating entity-specific features (like named entity types, salience, and proper mentions) without the exponential growth in parameters that plagues previous approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness of the Neural Architecture**: The paper presents a well-structured neural architecture that effectively addresses the limitations of the traditional entity grid model. The use of convolutional layers to model entity transitions of arbitrary lengths (5-8) is technically sound and addresses the "curse of dimensionality" problem. The authors provide clear mathematical formulations for each layer (lookup layer, convolutional layer, pooling layer, and coherence scoring layer) and justify their design choices based on the problem constraints. The architecture is well-motivated by the need to handle longer transitions without overfitting, which is a genuine limitation of previous models.

2. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments on three established evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) using standard datasets (WSJ and DUC'03). The results are clearly presented in tables with statistical significance tests. The neural models consistently outperform the non-neural counterparts by significant margins (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4%+ on summary coherence rating), demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors also compare against multiple baselines including the graph-based model, entity grid with all nouns, and extended entity grid, which strengthens the evaluation.

3. **Practical Implementation and Reproducibility**: The authors have released their code, which is a significant strength for a computational linguistics paper. They provide detailed hyperparameter settings and experimental details (including the use of pre-trained models for the summary coherence task), making the work highly reproducible. The implementation in Theano with clear hyperparameter tuning on a development set shows careful attention to model development.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Idea**: While the paper's neural architecture is technically sound, the core idea of applying neural networks to the entity grid framework is not particularly novel. The paper acknowledges this by stating "Following this tradition, in this paper we propose to neuralize the popular entity grid models." Several recent papers have done similar things (e.g., neuralizing traditional NLP approaches like language modeling, sequence tagging, syntactic parsing, etc.). The paper doesn't sufficiently argue why the entity grid is a particularly good candidate for neuralization compared to other approaches, or why the specific convolutional architecture they use is necessary rather than other neural architectures.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for the Neural Approach**: The paper states that their neural approach can "capture long range entity transitions along with arbitrary entity-specific features" but doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why this neural approach is superior to the traditional entity grid approach. The main argument is empirical (the results show better performance), but the paper could have provided more insight into why the neural approach works better. For example, why does using distributed representations help with long-range transitions? Why is convolutional architecture particularly well-suited for this task? The paper mentions "generalization through distributed representations" but doesn't elaborate on this point.

3. **Limited Analysis of Model Behavior**: The paper provides good empirical results but lacks deeper analysis of why the model works better. The paper mentions that the model performs better on discrimination than insertion but doesn't analyze why. Additionally, the paper doesn't provide insight into which specific entity transition patterns the convolutional layer learns to detect, or how the entity-specific features contribute to the performance gain. The authors could have provided more detailed analysis of the model's behavior to strengthen their claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the neural model "obtains generalization through distributed representations of entity transitions and entity features." Could the authors provide more specific examples of what these distributed representations capture, and how they differ from the discrete representations used in traditional entity grid models?

2. The paper mentions that the Extended Grid-CNN achieves better results than the Extended Grid model despite using only a subset of the entity-specific features. Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of which features contribute most to the performance gain?

3. The paper states that the Extended Grid-CNN yields "absolute gains of about 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and more than 4% on summary coherence rating." However, the results in Table 3 show that the gain on insertion is 3.72% (88.69% - 84.95%), which is higher than the "up to 2.5%" mentioned in the abstract. Could the authors clarify this discrepancy?

4. The paper mentions that "the model that is trained to distinguish an original document from its random permutation may learn features that are not specific enough to distinguish documents when only one sentence differs." Could the authors provide more insight into why this is the case, and what specific features might be missing for the insertion task?
