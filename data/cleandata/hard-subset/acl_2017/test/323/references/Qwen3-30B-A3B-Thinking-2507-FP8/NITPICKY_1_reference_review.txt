

# ACL Review: "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

## Summary of the Paper

The paper proposes a neural architecture for coherence assessment that operates over entity grids. The authors claim their model can capture long-range entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features without the curse of dimensionality issues that plague traditional entity grid models. They evaluate their approach on three tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, reporting improvements over non-neural baselines.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. A neural architecture for coherence assessment that operates over entity grids with distributed representations
2. An end-to-end training method for learning task-specific features
3. Empirical evaluation showing improvements over existing entity grid models on standard coherence tasks

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of convolutional neural networks to coherence modeling**: The paper successfully applies convolutional approaches to the entity grid representation, which is a fresh perspective on an established problem. The use of convolution to model entity transitions of arbitrary lengths is well-motivated and theoretically sound.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple tasks**: The paper evaluates on three different coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), which is more thorough than many prior works that focus on just one task. The inclusion of both sentence ordering tasks and the summary coherence rating task provides a more holistic view of model performance.

3. **Good comparison to existing baselines**: The paper compares against multiple strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art entity grid extensions by Elsner and Charniak (2011). The experimental design is appropriate, with careful attention to hyperparameter tuning and statistical significance testing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical citation and formatting errors**: The paper contains numerous inconsistent and incomplete citations (e.g., "Barzilay and Lapata.,? 2005" and "Elsner and Charniak.,? 2011"). The references section also contains several formatting issues. ACL has strict formatting guidelines, and these errors indicate a lack of attention to detail that would be expected in a top-tier conference submission.

2. **Missing code link**: The paper states "We have released our code with the submission," but no link to the code repository is provided. This is a critical omission, as ACL requires that code be publicly available for reproducibility. Without a code link, the paper cannot be properly evaluated for reproducibility.

3. **Inconsistent results reporting**: The paper claims "more than 4% on summary coherence rating" (in the abstract) but Table 4 shows a 3.7% improvement for Pre-trained Extended Grid-CNN over the Extended Grid model. This discrepancy undermines the paper's credibility and suggests a lack of careful proofreading.

4. **Lack of comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art**: The paper claims to achieve "state of the art results" but does not compare against the most recent neural coherence models, such as Li and Hovy (2014) or more recent approaches. The related work section mentions Li and Hovy (2014) but does not adequately explain how their approach differs from the previous work.

5. **Inadequate explanation of the neural model's advantages**: The paper claims to solve the "curse of dimensionality" problem but doesn't adequately explain how their model avoids the exponential growth in parameters when incorporating entity-specific features. The explanation in Section 3.1 is insufficient, particularly regarding how they handle cases where an entity has multiple types.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "more than 4% on summary coherence rating" in the abstract but Table 4 shows a 3.7% improvement for Pre-trained Extended Grid-CNN over the Extended Grid model. Can you clarify this discrepancy?

2. The paper claims to have "released our code with the submission" but provides no link to the code repository. Could you please provide the code link?

3. In Section 3.1, you state "we include only three entity-specific features from (Elsner and Charniak, 2011) that are easy to compute and do not require any external corpus." What specific criteria were used to select these three features, and why were the other six features not included?

4. The paper claims to solve the curse of dimensionality problem, but doesn't adequately explain how the neural model avoids exponential parameter growth when incorporating entity-specific features. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of this mechanism?

## Additional Comments

- In Table 2, the hyperparameter "Win" is not defined (it's likely "Window size" but should be spelled out).
- In Table 3, "Acc" and "F1" are not defined in the table header, which is confusing for readers.
- The paper mentions "Short articles may produce many matches" but doesn't provide any statistics on article length or how they handled short articles.
- The figure caption for Figure 2 claims to show "the pairwise training method" but the figure only shows the neural architecture.

## Overall Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper presents a reasonable extension of the entity grid model using neural networks, the numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies in the paper would be unacceptable for an ACL submission. The citation errors, missing code link, and inconsistent results reporting are particularly concerning. The paper's claims about state-of-the-art performance are undermined by the lack of comparison to the most recent work in the field.

The authors have made a good effort to apply neural networks to a traditional coherence modeling task, but the paper's technical details and presentation are insufficiently polished for ACL. The minor methodological issues and formatting inconsistencies indicate a lack of attention to detail that would be expected in a top-tier conference submission.
