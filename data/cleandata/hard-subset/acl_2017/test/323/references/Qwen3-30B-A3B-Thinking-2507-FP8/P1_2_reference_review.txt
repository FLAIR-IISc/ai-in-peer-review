

# Peer Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling local text coherence that extends the popular entity grid model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: (1) their discrete representation of grammatical roles leads to the curse of dimensionality problem, limiting transition modeling to short lengths (k ≤ 3), and (2) feature extraction is decoupled from the target task, preventing learning of task-specific features. To address these limitations, they present a neural model that uses distributed representations of entity transitions and incorporates entity-specific features through a convolutional neural network architecture. The model is trained end-to-end using pairwise ranking loss. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) and demonstrate consistent improvements over non-neural counterparts, achieving state-of-the-art results.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Neural architecture for local coherence modeling**: The paper presents a novel neural network architecture that overcomes the limitations of the entity grid model by using distributed representations and convolutional layers to model long-range entity transitions without the curse of dimensionality.

2. **End-to-end training framework**: The authors introduce a pairwise ranking approach that enables the model to learn task-specific features automatically, addressing the decoupled feature extraction problem in previous models.

3. **Strong empirical results on multiple standard coherence evaluation tasks**: The model achieves consistent and significant improvements over the state-of-the-art on discrimination (4% absolute gain), insertion (2.5% absolute gain), and summary coherence rating (4%+ absolute gain) tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and well-motivated neural extension of the entity grid model**: The paper identifies a clear limitation of the entity grid model (the curse of dimensionality problem) and proposes a natural solution using neural networks. The convolutional architecture is particularly well-suited for modeling entity transitions, and the distributed representation approach elegantly solves the dimensionality problem. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their model works and why it's better than previous approaches.

2. **Strong empirical results on multiple standard tasks**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over the best existing models across three different coherence evaluation tasks. The results are statistically significant (p < 0.01), with absolute gains of 4-5% on discrimination and summary coherence rating, and 2.5% on insertion. This is a substantial improvement for a coherence modeling task, where gains are often marginal.

3. **Clear and comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their model on the three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) using the same datasets as previous work, enabling direct comparison. The experimental setup is well-documented, and the comparison with multiple baselines (including the best existing model, Extended Grid) is thorough.

4. **Practical contribution with code release**: The authors release their code with the submission, which is a significant practical contribution that will enable reproducibility and future work in the field. This is particularly valuable for coherence modeling, where datasets and evaluation frameworks can be complex.

5. **Strong theoretical grounding**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach by building on the well-established entity grid model and identifying its specific limitations. The connection to Centering Theory (Grosz et al., 1995) and existing coherence modeling approaches is well-maintained throughout.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of detailed comparison with other neural approaches**: The paper mentions that the authors tried to use Li and Hovy (2014) model but got poor results (19.34% accuracy on discrimination), yet they don't provide a detailed explanation for why their implementation failed. This is a significant weakness because it leaves open questions about whether the poor performance was due to the model architecture, dataset differences, or implementation details. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper's claims about the superiority of their neural approach.

2. **Limited analysis of what the model learns**: While the paper demonstrates that the model works better, it lacks any analysis of what the model actually learns. For example, the paper doesn't provide examples of entity transitions that the model finds most salient or how these transitions correlate with coherence. This would help understand the model's behavior and potentially improve it.

3. **Theoretical justification for the architecture is limited**: The paper doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why a convolutional architecture is particularly well-suited for this task compared to other neural architectures (e.g., RNNs or transformers). The justification is somewhat ad hoc, simply noting that "convolutional approach" is used for other NLP tasks. A more detailed theoretical argument would strengthen the paper.

4. **The insertion task shows less pronounced gains**: The improvements on the insertion task (2.5% absolute gain) are less substantial than on discrimination (4% absolute gain). The authors note that insertion is a harder task, but they don't provide any analysis of why their model might be less effective on this specific task or how it could be improved. This is a significant weakness because insertion is often considered a more challenging task.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the model. For example, how much does the convolutional layer contribute compared to the distributed representation? How much does adding entity-specific features help? This information would help readers understand what's essential to the model's success.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that you tried to use the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014) but got poor results (19.34% accuracy on discrimination). Could you elaborate on why you think this implementation failed? Was it due to the model architecture, the dataset, or the implementation details?

2. The paper states that "the Extended Grid-CNN yields these improvements considering only a subset of the Extended Grid features." How much of the performance difference is due to the neural architecture versus the specific features used? Could you provide an ablation study showing the contribution of the neural components versus the entity-specific features?

3. The insertion task shows smaller gains compared to discrimination. Could you provide more analysis of the insertion task results? For example, are there specific types of sentences or documents where the model struggles? How might the model be adapted to better handle the insertion task?

4. The paper uses WSJ corpus for sentence ordering tasks, but the dataset is larger than other corpora like AIRPLANES and EARTHQUAKES. How do you think the results would generalize to these smaller, more constrained corpora? Could you provide some results on these datasets for comparison?

5. The paper mentions that the model achieves state-of-the-art results, but doesn't compare to very recent work in the field. Have you considered comparing to more recent neural coherence models (e.g., those using transformers or other recent architectures)?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of text coherence modeling. The proposed neural model addresses a well-identified limitation of the entity grid model and achieves significant improvements over the state-of-the-art on multiple standard evaluation tasks. The paper is well-written, well-structured, and provides a comprehensive evaluation. While there are some weaknesses in the comparison with other neural approaches and the lack of analysis of what the model learns, these do not substantially undermine the main contribution of the paper.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for ACL. The work is novel, well-executed, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of text coherence modeling. The empirical results are strong and the code release is a significant practical contribution.
