### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) model for assessing local text coherence. The model operates on the entity-grid representation, a well-established structure that tracks the grammatical roles of entities across sentences. The authors' main argument is that a neural approach can overcome limitations of prior feature-based entity-grid models, namely the curse of dimensionality when modeling long entity transitions and the task-agnostic nature of feature engineering. The proposed architecture learns distributed representations (embeddings) for grammatical roles and uses convolutional filters to learn task-specific features for coherence scoring. The model is evaluated on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and is shown to outperform previous state-of-the-art entity-grid models.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  The application of a standard CNN architecture to the entity-grid representation for local coherence modeling. This serves as a "neuralization" of the classic model by Barzilay and Lapata (2008).
2.  An empirical demonstration that this neural approach achieves state-of-the-art results on several benchmark coherence tasks, outperforming strong, feature-engineered baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its experimental results. The proposed model consistently and significantly outperforms established baselines, including the strong extended entity-grid model of Elsner and Charniak (2011). The reported absolute gains of ~4% on the discrimination task, up to 2.5% on the insertion task, and over 4% on summary coherence rating are compelling. These results provide solid evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

2.  **Methodologically Sound Evaluation:** The authors follow a rigorous and well-established evaluation protocol. They use standard datasets (WSJ, DUC'03) and evaluation tasks that are common in coherence modeling literature. The inclusion of multiple tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) provides a comprehensive assessment of the model's capabilities. Furthermore, the use of significance testing adds credibility to the reported gains, and the public release of the code is a valuable contribution to the community, enhancing reproducibility.

3.  **Clear Motivation and Exposition:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate their work by articulating the specific limitations of prior entity-grid models (sparsity with long transitions, task-agnostic features). They then present their neural architecture as a principled solution to these problems. The connection between the traditional entity-grid and its neural counterpart is straightforward and well-explained.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the application is novel, the neural architecture itself is a standard CNN (embedding lookup, convolution, max-pooling, output layer) that has been widely used in NLP for several years for tasks like sentence classification. The primary contribution is therefore the adaptation of this existing architecture to the entity-grid data structure, rather than a fundamental innovation in neural modeling. The claims of capturing "long range" dependencies with filter sizes of 5-8 are also somewhat optimistic, as these still represent fairly local contextual windows.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison with Other Neural Baselines:** The paper positions itself as a "neural coherence model" but fails to provide a thorough comparison to other relevant neural approaches. The dismissal of the Li and Hovy (2014) model due to poor replication performance is unsatisfactory without a deeper investigation into the cause. A truly convincing argument for the superiority of this specific architecture would require comparison against other plausible neural baselines. For instance, a recurrent neural network (e.g., an LSTM or GRU) operating over sentence representations is a natural and strong baseline for sequence-level tasks like coherence assessment. Without such comparisons, it is unclear whether the performance gains stem from the use of a CNN on an entity-grid specifically, or simply from applying any modern neural network to the problem.

3.  **The "End-to-End" Claim is Overstated:** The authors claim their model is trained "end-to-end," which implies learning from raw input to final output. However, the model's input is not raw text but a pre-computed entity grid. The construction of this grid is a complex, multi-stage pipeline involving NP chunking, coreference resolution, and parsing to identify grammatical roles. This pipeline is a significant source of potential error, and the model's performance is fundamentally dependent on its quality. The training is end-to-end from the grid, not from the text, and this distinction is critical. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this dependency and discussed the impact of errors from this upstream pipeline.

4.  **Lack of Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper successfully demonstrates *that* the model works better, but provides very little insight into *why* or *how*. The strength of the traditional entity-grid model lies in its interpretable features (e.g., the probability of an entity transitioning from subject to subject). The neural model, while more powerful, is a black box. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an analysis of what the model has learned. For example, what kinds of transition patterns do the learned convolutional filters capture? Are they linguistically meaningful? A qualitative error analysis comparing the neural model's failures to those of the baseline would also provide valuable insights into its behavior. Without such analysis, the paper is more of an engineering report than a scientific contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model, could you provide more detail on why it failed to perform well on your dataset? Was this potentially due to differences in data preprocessing, hyperparameter settings, or is there a more fundamental reason why their sentence-level representation approach is less suitable for the WSJ corpus compared to the entity-grid representation?

2.  The model's performance relies heavily on the quality of the pre-computed entity grid. Have you conducted any analysis on the impact of errors from the upstream components (e.g., parser, coreference resolver) on your model's final performance? How robust is the model to noise in the grid representation?

3.  Could you provide some qualitative analysis to help understand what the model learns? For example, by examining the n-grams in the entity grid that maximally activate certain convolutional filters, it might be possible to interpret the specific coherence patterns (e.g., S-S-O, PERSON-S -> PERSON-S) that the model has learned are important. This would provide valuable insight beyond the aggregate accuracy scores.
