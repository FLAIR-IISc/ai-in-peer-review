### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network-based model for local text coherence, designed as a direct successor to the classic entity grid model. The core idea is to re-frame the problem of feature extraction from the entity grid. Instead of manually defining and counting discrete entity role transitions (e.g., Subject -> Subject -> Object), the authors use a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to automatically learn feature representations of these transitions. Grammatical roles are first mapped to dense embeddings, and a convolutional layer with subsequent max-pooling is applied over the transition sequences for each entity to produce a final coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors demonstrate that this approach outperforms previous entity-grid models on standard coherence tasks, including discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and compelling neural re-formulation of the entity grid model.** This is the primary contribution. The work elegantly translates the discrete, feature-engineered approach of Barzilay and Lapata (2008) into a modern deep learning framework. This move from counting explicit n-grams of roles to learning distributed representations via convolutions is a significant conceptual leap for this line of research.
2.  **An effective solution to the primary limitations of traditional grid models.** The proposed architecture directly addresses the "curse of dimensionality" that prevented prior work from modeling long-range entity transitions (k > 3). By using dense embeddings and shared convolutional filters, the model can capture longer patterns (k=5-8 in their experiments) without the parameter explosion of discrete models, leading to better generalization.
3.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the value of task-specific, learned features for coherence.** By training the model end-to-end, the features are optimized for the coherence task at hand, unlike the task-agnostic transition probabilities used in prior work. The consistent and significant performance gains across three different tasks validate this architectural choice.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and a Bold New Direction:** The paper's main strength lies in its conceptual novelty. While "neuralizing" classic NLP models has become common, this application to the entity grid is particularly well-motivated and impactful. It shifts the paradigm for entity-based coherence from hand-crafted feature engineering to representation learning. This is not an incremental tweak but a fundamental change in how to model information in the grid, opening up a promising new direction for research on local coherence. I highly value this kind of bold rethinking of a classic problem.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Architecture:** The choice of a CNN is a natural and elegant fit for the problem. The core assumption of the entity grid is that local transition patterns are indicative of coherence, regardless of where they appear in the text. This aligns perfectly with the location-invariant nature of convolutional filters. The architecture provides a principled way to learn robust representations of transition patterns that are shared across all documents in the training set, a clear advantage over calculating sparse transition probabilities from a single document.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The proposed model isn't just a theoretical exercise; it delivers state-of-the-art results across the board. The absolute gains of ~4% on discrimination and summary rating over the very strong extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011) are impressive. Notably, the Extended Grid-CNN achieves this while using fewer, more easily obtained entity-specific features, further highlighting the power of the learned representations. The consistent success across multiple tasks demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of the approach.

### Weaknesses

While I am very positive about this paper, there are a few areas that could be stronger. However, in my view, these are minor flaws that do not detract from the significance of the core contribution.

1.  **Lack of Model Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper claims that the filters learn to detect specific transition patterns, which is an intuitive explanation for why the model works. However, there is no analysis to support this. What kinds of patterns do the learned filters actually capture? Do they rediscover the simple, linguistically-motivated patterns from prior work (e.g., S-S-S continuity)? Or do they find more complex, non-obvious correlations? A qualitative analysis of the learned filters and embeddings would have provided deeper insight into *why* the neural model is superior and moved the paper from a strong engineering contribution to one with more scientific insight.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison with Other Neural Coherence Models:** The authors mention the distributed sentence model of Li and Hovy (2014) but dismiss it because they were unable to reproduce good results on their dataset. This is not a satisfying comparison. A more thorough discussion of the architectural differences (e.g., sentence-level composition vs. entity-centric transitions) and a more robust attempt to create a fair comparison would have better situated this work within the emerging landscape of neural coherence models. As it stands, the engagement with related neural approaches feels somewhat superficial.

3.  **The "End-to-End" Claim is Slightly Overstated:** The model is end-to-end *from the entity grid*, but it still relies on a significant pre-processing pipeline (parsing for grammatical roles, coreference resolution) to construct the grid in the first place. This is not a criticism of the method itself, which is focused on modeling the grid, but the framing could be more precise. The model does not learn coherence from raw text. This is a minor point, but worth noting.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide any qualitative analysis or visualization of the learned convolutional filters or role embeddings? For instance, can you identify filters that activate strongly on specific, known coherence patterns (e.g., repeated subjects) and show examples? This would greatly strengthen the claim that the model learns meaningful representations.

2.  Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for the poor performance of the Li and Hovy (2014) model on the WSJ dataset? Is it possible that sentence-representation models are less suited for the long, topically-diverse documents in WSJ compared to the entity-centric approach, which can track discourse participants over long distances?

3.  You hypothesize that performance gains on the insertion task were smaller because the model was trained on the discrimination task. Did you experiment with training a model directly on the insertion task (i.e., by creating training pairs where the model must rank the correct insertion point higher than incorrect ones)? This seems like a straightforward experiment that would directly test your hypothesis.
