### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling local text coherence, framed as an extension of the well-known entity grid model. The proposed model, named Grid-CNN, represents an entity grid's grammatical roles with learned embeddings and applies a one-dimensional convolutional neural network (CNN) along each entity's transition column. This allows the model to learn representations of long-range entity transitions. The output features from the CNN are pooled and fed into a final layer to produce a coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their model on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and report state-of-the-art results, showing consistent improvements over previous entity-grid-based models.

### Contributions
1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for Entity-Grid Coherence:** The primary contribution is the application of a convolutional neural network directly to the entity grid representation. This is a natural and well-motivated approach to overcome the "curse of dimensionality" that limited previous grid models to short, discrete transition features.
2.  **End-to-End Learning for Coherence Features:** The model learns task-specific feature representations directly from the data via backpropagation, in contrast to prior work where feature extraction from the grid was a separate, task-agnostic step.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper presents strong empirical evidence across three distinct and standard coherence tasks, consistently outperforming strong baselines, including various extensions of the original entity grid model.

### Strengths
1.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Model:** The core idea of "neuralizing" the entity grid is logical and elegant. The CNN architecture is a suitable choice for capturing local, position-invariant patterns (i.e., entity transitions) of varying lengths, directly addressing the key limitation of earlier models. The use of learned embeddings for grammatical roles is also a standard and effective technique for this type of problem.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive. The authors evaluate on three different tasks with varying difficulty, use a large and standard dataset (WSJ), and compare against appropriate baselines. Methodological rigor is demonstrated through practices like using a development set for tuning, averaging results over multiple random seeds, and performing statistical significance testing.
3.  **Strong and Consistent Performance Gains:** The proposed models (Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN) achieve significant absolute improvements over their non-neural counterparts across all tasks (e.g., ~4% on discrimination, >4% on summary rating). Achieving state-of-the-art results on multiple established benchmarks is a significant achievement and clearly demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed method.

### Weaknesses
Despite the novel idea and strong results, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor flaws, technical ambiguities, and a general lack of polish that collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Profound Lack of Attention to Detail:** The paper is marred by an unacceptable number of formatting errors, particularly in the bibliography. The reference list is filled with BibTeX compilation artifacts (e.g., `Barzilay and Lapata.,?`, `Bengio et al\.,?`, `Feng et al\.,?`). The Tieleman and Hinton (2012) reference contains `\Q2012\E`, and the Theano reference ends with `Team.,? 2016, Team.,`. This level of carelessness in the final presentation suggests a rushed submission and raises concerns about the diligence applied to the research itself.
2.  **Technical and Notational Imprecision:**
    *   In the convolution operation (Equation 2), the bias term is written as `bt`, indexed by the time step `t`. This is highly non-standard for a CNN, where a single scalar bias is typically learned for each filter `w`, not for each application of the filter. If this is an intentional, novel choice, it is completely unjustified and unexplained. If it is a typo, it is a significant one in the core model description.
    *   The method for incorporating entity-specific features (Section 3.1) by creating composite symbols (e.g., `PERSON-S`) is rather simplistic. This approach leads to a combinatorial explosion in the vocabulary size as more features are added, which is not scalable. This limitation is not discussed.
    *   The "Why it works" section is overly simplistic, offering a generic textbook explanation of CNNs without providing any specific analysis or insight into what kinds of coherence patterns the model might actually be learning.
3.  **Weaknesses in Experimental Comparison:**
    *   The dismissal of the Li and Hovy (2014) neural baseline is unconvincing. Stating that results were "very disappointing" and that "We were not sure what could go wrong" is insufficient diligence. This is a highly relevant piece of recent work, and a more rigorous effort to reproduce or debug its performance should have been made before excluding it. Its absence weakens the paper's claim to be a comprehensive evaluation of neural coherence models.
    *   The justification for not comparing against the Extended Grid (E&C) model on the summary rating task (Footnote 9) is confusing. The E&C model, like the authors' own, uses a preference ranking learner (SVM-rank), which is inherently pairwise. The stated inability to train it on the summarization dataset appears to be a tooling limitation rather than a fundamental incompatibility, and weakens the comparative analysis on that task.
4.  **Minor Clarity and Formatting Issues:**
    *   Footnote 1 ("They match the nouns to detect coreferent entities.") is abrupt and lacks context. It is unclear which "they" it refers to.
    *   There is a typo in Equation 3, which reads `h 1` instead of `h^1` or `h_1`.
    *   The caption for Figure 2 contains a slightly awkward clarification: "For clarification, pairwise input as shown in the figure is required only to train the model." This could be phrased more elegantly.

While the core ideas of this paper are strong, the execution and presentation are not up to the standards of a top-tier conference. The sheer number of small errors, combined with some more significant methodological and experimental gaps, leads me to recommend rejection in its current form. The work shows promise but requires a thorough round of revision and polishing.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the formulation of the bias term `bt` in Equation (2)? Is it indeed position-dependent, and if so, what is the motivation for this departure from the standard per-filter bias used in CNNs?
2.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) baseline: can you provide more detail on your attempts to reproduce their results? For instance, did you experiment with different sentence encoders or hyperparameters beyond what was reported in their paper, or attempt to contact the authors for clarification?
3.  Can you elaborate on the technical barrier that prevented you from training the Extended Grid (E&C) model on the pairwise summary coherence dataset? Given that it uses a preference ranking SVM, it seems that it should be compatible with the training data format.
