**Summary of the paper**
This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, framed as a neural extension of the well-established entity grid model. The proposed architecture, Grid-CNN, represents the grammatical roles in an entity grid using learned embeddings and applies a 1D convolutional neural network along each entity's column to learn salient transition patterns. This allows the model to capture longer-range dependencies than traditional n-gram-based grid models. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their model on standard sentence ordering and summary coherence rating tasks, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and significant improvements over non-neural counterparts.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is an effective and intuitive neuralization of the entity grid model. By applying a standard CNN architecture to the entity grid representation, the authors create a model that learns distributed representations of entity transitions, overcoming the "curse of dimensionality" that limits previous discrete feature-based approaches.
2.  A secondary contribution is the strong empirical validation of this approach. The paper shows that the proposed neural models, even a version using a simplified feature set, consistently and significantly outperform strong, feature-rich non-neural baselines across multiple standard coherence evaluation tasks.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper presents convincing results, achieving new state-of-the-art on all three evaluation tasks. The improvements over the non-neural counterparts are substantial (e.g., ~4% absolute on discrimination). The experimental setup is commendable: the authors use a large, standard dataset (WSJ), reproduce baselines themselves using a public toolkit, report averaged results over five runs to ensure stability, and perform significance testing.
2.  **Simple and Well-Motivated Model:** The proposed architecture is elegant in its simplicity. Applying a CNN to the columns of an entity grid is a natural fit for capturing position-invariant transition patterns of arbitrary length. The motivation for moving from discrete, task-agnostic feature extraction to a learned, end-to-end distributed representation is clearly articulated and well-justified by the results.
3.  **Clarity and Transparency:** For the most part, the paper is well-written and the model is described clearly. The authors are transparent about their hyperparameter tuning process and provide the optimal settings. Furthermore, their honesty in reporting the failure to get good results with a competing model (Li and Hovy, 2014), rather than omitting it, is a strength, though it could have been accompanied by more analysis.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Crucial Methodological Details are Missing:** The description of the best-performing model, `Extended Grid-CNN`, is critically underspecified. In Section 3.1, the authors state that entity-specific features are incorporated by creating composite symbols (e.g., `PERSON-S`). However, they fail to explain how a continuous feature like "salience as determined by occurrence frequency" is discretized to create such a symbol. This is a non-trivial decision that could significantly impact performance. Without this information, the most important model in the paper is not fully reproducible. The paper also neglects to state the resulting vocabulary size for the extended model, which is essential for understanding the model's complexity and whether it truly avoids sparsity issues.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Potentially Inaccurate Claims:** The paper presents results but often stops short of providing deeper analysis. For instance, Table 2 shows that the optimal batch size and filter window size differ notably between the `Grid-CNN` (128, 6) and `Extended Grid-CNN` (32, 5). There is no discussion as to why this might be the case. More concerningly, footnote 9 claims that the non-neural extended grid model "could not be trained on the summarization dataset" because it "does not use pairwise training". This appears to be incorrect; the Brown coherence toolkit implementation of that model uses an SVM preference ranking framework, which is fundamentally a pairwise learner. This unsubstantiated claim undermines the comparison on that task.
3.  **Lack of Professionalism in Manuscript Preparation:** The paper suffers from numerous minor errors that, in aggregate, suggest a lack of care.
    *   The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted, containing numerous artifacts like "Barzilay and Lapata.,? 2005, Barzilay and Lapata.,", "Bengio et al\.,?", and "\Q2012\E". This is unacceptable for a conference submission and makes it difficult to parse the references.
    *   Technical descriptions contain minor inaccuracies. In Section 3, the description of zero-padding for wide convolution states "t > {m,n}", which is ambiguous notation. It should likely be `t > m`, where `m` is the number of sentences.
    *   Linguistic precision is sometimes lacking. In Section 5.3, the authors write that the "gain in insertion is less verbose". The word "verbose" is incorrect here; "pronounced" or "significant" would be appropriate. While minor, these errors detract from the overall quality of the paper.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Please provide the exact methodology used to incorporate the entity-specific features in Section 3.1, especially the "salience" feature. How was this continuous value binned or otherwise converted into a discrete symbol for the grid? What was the final vocabulary size (`|V|`) for the `Extended Grid-CNN` model's embedding layer?
2.  Could you please clarify or correct the statement in footnote 9 regarding the non-neural extended grid model? The SVM-ranker used by Elsner and Charniak (2011) is a pairwise learning method, so it seems it should be trainable on the pairwise summary dataset. Was there a specific technical limitation that prevented this?
3.  Can you offer any insight into why the optimal hyperparameters for batch size and filter window size (Table 2) are so different between your two neural models? Does the richer input representation of the `Extended Grid-CNN` favor smaller batches and filters for some reason?
