**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a neural architecture for modeling local text coherence based on the well-established entity grid representation. The proposed model replaces the discrete, probabilistic features of traditional grid models with learned distributed representations (embeddings) for grammatical roles. A convolutional neural network (CNN) is then applied over the sequence of roles for each entity, allowing the model to capture long-range transition patterns. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective to produce a final coherence score for a document. The authors evaluate their model on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—demonstrating significant improvements over previous entity grid-based methods.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel neural architecture that successfully applies convolutional networks to the entity grid representation for coherence assessment. This approach effectively combines a linguistically-motivated structural representation (the grid) with the feature learning capabilities of deep learning, directly addressing the data sparsity and generalization issues of prior grid-based models that limit their ability to capture long-range dependencies.
2.  A strong empirical validation of the proposed model, which establishes a new state of the art on multiple standard coherence evaluation tasks. The experiments are conducted on a large, standard dataset and include comparisons against strong, directly comparable baselines, with results supported by statistical significance testing.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Principled Model Design:** The paper provides a clear and compelling motivation by identifying the key limitations of existing entity grid models: the "curse of dimensionality" that restricts the length of modeled entity transitions, and the decoupling of feature extraction from the end task. The proposed neural architecture directly and elegantly addresses these shortcomings. Using embeddings to create dense representations and a CNN to identify salient, location-invariant transition patterns is a well-founded and intuitive application of these techniques to the problem.

2.  **Methodological Rigor and Reproducibility:** The experimental evaluation is methodologically sound and thorough. The authors use a large, standard dataset (WSJ) and compare their model against relevant and strong baselines, including a reimplementation of the state-of-the-art non-neural grid model using a publicly available toolkit, ensuring a fair comparison. The reporting of hyper-parameter tuning on a dedicated development set, the use of statistical significance tests, and the averaging of results over multiple random seeds lend high credibility to the findings. The release of the source code is a significant strength that facilitates reproducibility and future work.

3.  **Significant and Consistent Performance Gains:** The proposed models achieve substantial and statistically significant performance improvements across all three evaluation tasks. The Extended Grid-CNN outperforms its direct non-neural counterpart (Extended Grid E&C) by nearly 4% absolute on the discrimination task and over 2.5% on the more challenging insertion task. This demonstrates that the neural approach provides more than just an incremental improvement, offering a tangible advancement in the state of the art for this style of coherence modeling. The gains are particularly notable given that the neural model uses fewer hand-crafted entity features than the baseline it outperforms.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Analysis of Model Internals:** While the paper convincingly shows that the model is effective, it offers little analysis of *what* the model has learned. The "Why it works" section provides only a high-level, generic intuition about CNNs. An analysis of the learned representations would have strengthened the paper significantly. For example, visualizing the embeddings could show whether related roles (e.g., 'PERSON-S' and 'ORGANIZATION-S') are clustered. Examining the filters learned by the convolutional layer could reveal the specific transition patterns (e.g., 'S-S-O-', '-S-O-X') the model finds indicative of coherence. Without such analysis, the work is primarily an engineering contribution that misses an opportunity to provide new linguistic insights into coherence.

2.  **Inadequate Comparison with Other Neural Approaches:** The paper dismisses a key piece of related work, the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014), on the grounds of poor reimplementation results. This comparison is unsatisfying. Given that Li and Hovy's model represents a different paradigm of neural coherence modeling (based on sentence representations rather than entity grids), a more thorough investigation into why it failed on the WSJ dataset would have been valuable. A deeper discussion of the conceptual differences between the two approaches and their potential domain or task suitability would provide a more complete contextualization of the paper's contribution within the broader landscape of neural coherence research.

3.  **Fixed Architectural Assumption of Entity Independence:** The model assumes that entity transitions (the columns of the grid) are independent, processing each one with a separate 1D convolution. This overlooks the possibility that coherence arises from the interactions *between* different entities' transition patterns. The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging this limitation and discussing alternative architectures that could capture such dependencies, such as using a 2D CNN over the entire grid or adding subsequent layers that integrate information across columns. While this might be out of scope for the current work, its absence as a point of discussion is a minor weakness.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The Extended Grid-CNN outperforms the non-neural Extended Grid model despite using only three entity-specific features compared to the baseline's nine. To what do you attribute this superior performance with less explicit information? Is it primarily due to the ability to model longer transitions, or does the end-to-end training produce a more effective representation of the three features used?
2.  Your model processes each entity column independently. Have you considered or experimented with architectures that could model interactions between entities, such as a 2D convolution over the grid? What are your thoughts on the potential trade-offs (e.g., performance vs. model complexity and data requirements) of such an approach?
3.  Regarding the comparison with Li and Hovy (2014), could you provide more detail on the difficulties encountered during reimplementation? Do you have any hypotheses as to why their sentence-representation-based model might be ill-suited for the WSJ discrimination task compared to your entity-grid-based approach?
