### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network-based model for local coherence assessment, framed as a "neuralization" of the well-established entity grid model. The proposed architecture uses a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates on an entity grid where grammatical roles are represented by learned embeddings. The authors claim this approach overcomes key limitations of traditional grid models, namely the data sparsity issues associated with modeling long entity transitions and the task-agnostic nature of feature extraction. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective and evaluated on standard sentence ordering and summary coherence rating tasks, where it reportedly achieves state-of-the-art results.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Successful Application of a CNN to a Classic NLP Representation:** The primary contribution is demonstrating that a standard CNN architecture can be effectively applied to the entity grid representation, outperforming strong, feature-engineered baselines.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results on Standard Benchmarks:** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation on multiple standard tasks and datasets, showing consistent and statistically significant improvements over prior state-of-the-art models.

### Strengths

1.  **Solid Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its results. The proposed models consistently outperform strong and widely-used baselines, including the extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011), across three distinct evaluation tasks. The reported improvements (e.g., an absolute gain of nearly 4% on the discrimination task and over 4% on summary coherence rating for the extended model) are substantial and statistically significant, establishing a new state-of-the-art on these benchmarks.
2.  **Rigorous and Reproducible Evaluation:** The experimental setup is methodologically sound. The authors use standard datasets (WSJ, DUC'03) and evaluation protocols, which allows for direct comparison with previous work. The inclusion of multiple strong baselines, particularly the Elsner & Charniak (2011) models implemented using the publicly available Brown coherence toolkit, ensures that the reported gains are meaningful. The decision to release the code further strengthens the paper by promoting reproducibility.
3.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper provides a clear and reasonable motivation for its neural approach. It correctly identifies a known limitation of traditional entity grid models: the "curse of dimensionality" that makes it difficult to model entity transitions of length k > 3 due to feature sparsity. The use of distributed representations and shared convolutional filters is a principled way to address this limitation and capture longer-range dependencies, and the experimental results appear to validate this design choice.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the application to coherence modeling is new, the neural architecture itself is entirely conventional. The sequence of embedding layer, 1D convolutions, max-pooling, and a final linear layer is a standard pattern for sequence and sentence classification tasks in NLP. The paper presents this as a novel contribution to coherence modeling, but it is more accurately an application of an off-the-shelf neural architecture to a classic structured representation. The core contribution is therefore more in engineering and empirical validation than in fundamental model innovation.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Interpretation:** A significant weakness is the lack of analysis into what the model learns. The traditional entity grid provides interpretable features (e.g., frequencies of specific S->O->X transitions). The authors claim their CNN filters learn to detect "specific transition patterns," but offer no evidence to support this. The paper would be substantially stronger with an analysis of the learned filters to see what patterns they capture. Is the model learning linguistically plausible long-range patterns that were missed by previous models? Or is the performance gain simply a result of a more powerful, non-linear function approximator? Without such analysis, the paper demonstrates that the model works but fails to provide insight into *why* it works, which is a missed opportunity for advancing our understanding of coherence.
3.  **Inconclusive Comparison of Extended Models:** The comparison between the proposed "Extended Grid-CNN" and the baseline "Extended Grid (E&C)" is potentially misleading. The authors state their neural model uses only three entity-specific features, whereas the E&C baseline uses nine. While the authors present this as a strength (achieving better performance with fewer features), it confounds the comparison. The observed performance difference could be attributed to the modeling architecture (CNN vs. SVM) or to the different feature sets. It is unclear if the three features chosen are simply more informative than the other six. A more rigorous comparison would require training and testing both models on the exact same set of features to isolate the impact of the modeling approach.
4.  **Unexplored Data Dependency:** The model is trained on the large WSJ corpus. The strong performance on the summary rating task, which has a very small training set, is achieved only after pre-training on this large corpus. This highlights a potential dependency on large training sets, which is characteristic of neural models but a potential drawback compared to feature-based methods that can perform well in lower-resource settings. The paper does not discuss or evaluate the model's performance as a function of training data size, which would be important for understanding its practical applicability.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your motivation for using a CNN is its ability to model longer entity transitions (k>3). Could you provide an ablation study on the filter window size? This would help quantify the contribution of modeling longer vs. shorter transitions and directly validate a central claim of the paper.
2.  The comparison between your Extended Grid-CNN (3 features) and the baseline Extended Grid model (9 features) is not an apples-to-apples comparison. Could you provide results for an experiment where the baseline model is also restricted to the same three features? This would help disentangle the effect of the model architecture from the effect of the feature set.
3.  Can you provide any qualitative analysis of the learned filters in your CNN? For example, can you show what input transition patterns (e.g., 'S-S-O-X-') maximally activate some of the learned filters? This would offer valuable insight into whether the model is learning linguistically meaningful patterns beyond what was explicitly engineered in previous work.
