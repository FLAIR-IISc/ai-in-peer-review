### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network model for local text coherence assessment. The model is a "neuralization" of the classic and successful entity grid representation. It addresses key limitations of prior grid-based models, namely the curse of dimensionality from discrete features and the task-agnostic nature of feature extraction. The proposed architecture represents the entity grid's grammatical roles and entity features as dense embeddings. A convolutional neural network (CNN) is then applied over the columns of this embedded grid (i.e., over each entity's transition sequence) to learn abstract, high-level features. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective to produce a coherence score. The authors evaluate their model on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and summary coherence rating tasks, demonstrating state-of-the-art results that significantly outperform strong non-neural entity grid baselines.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and well-motivated neural architecture for coherence modeling based on the entity grid.** The paper successfully translates a classic symbolic NLP representation into a deep learning framework, elegantly addressing the sparsity and feature engineering limitations of the original model by using embeddings and a CNN to capture long-range entity transition patterns.
2.  **Strong empirical validation of the proposed model.** The authors provide convincing evidence across three standard coherence tasks that their neural approach significantly outperforms strong, widely-used baselines, including the state-of-the-art non-neural entity grid model. The gains reported are substantial and statistically significant.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Model Design:** The paper does a very good job of identifying concrete weaknesses in the highly successful entity grid model (sparsity preventing long-range transition modeling, task-agnostic feature extraction) and designing a neural architecture that directly addresses these problems. The choice of a CNN is well-justified for learning location-invariant transition patterns of arbitrary length, and the end-to-end training with a ranking loss allows for the learning of task-specific representations. The overall approach is both elegant and intuitive.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. The model is tested on multiple standard tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) against relevant and strong baselines. The most important comparison is against the direct non-neural counterparts (E&C's grid models), which clearly isolates and demonstrates the benefit of the neural approach. The reported performance gains are impressive and consistently statistically significant, establishing a new state-of-the-art on these benchmarks.
3.  **High Standard of Scientific Reporting:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The experimental setup is well-detailed, including hyperparameter tuning on a dedicated development set, averaging results over multiple random seeds, and conducting significance tests. The authors' decision to release their code is commendable and greatly enhances the reproducibility and potential impact of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the model works, but offers little insight into *why* it works or *what* it has learned. The motivation for using a CNN is to capture meaningful transition patterns, but there is no analysis to confirm this. A qualitative analysis would have significantly strengthened the paper. For instance, visualizing the learned embeddings for grammatical roles or examining the filter activations on specific coherent/incoherent examples could reveal whether the model is learning linguistically plausible coherence strategies (e.g., patterns related to Centering Theory principles). This leaves the model as a bit of a "black box".
2.  **Limited Discussion of Architectural Novelty:** While the application of a CNN to the entity grid is novel, the architecture itself (embeddings -> convolution -> max-pooling -> output layer) is a standard pattern in NLP. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion acknowledging this and further justifying the specific architectural choices. For example, why is a 1D convolution over entity transitions superior to other possible architectures, like an RNN over the same sequences?
3.  **Incomplete Comparison to other Neural Models:** The authors mention the contemporary neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014) but relegate it to a brief remark, stating they could not reproduce good results. While this honesty is appreciated, dismissing a key related work in this manner weakens the paper's positioning. A more thorough discussion of the potential reasons for the discrepancy (e.g., differences in datasets, model sensitivity) or a more detailed analysis of the architectural differences would be necessary to properly contextualize the paper's contribution relative to other neural approaches. As it stands, it is unclear whether the entity grid representation itself provides the main advantage, or if there were other confounding factors.
4.  **Slightly Unfair Comparison for the "Extended" Model:** The proposed "Extended Grid-CNN" is compared against the E&C "Extended Grid" but uses only three of the nine features available to the baseline. While it is impressive that the model still wins with fewer features, this is not an apples-to-apples comparison. It leaves open the question of how much better the model could perform with the full feature set. Justifying this design choice as being "to keep the model simple" is not fully convincing from a research perspective.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your Extended Grid-CNN model uses a subset of features (3 out of 9) compared to its non-neural baseline. Could you elaborate on this decision? Have you experimented with including all 9 features? If so, does performance improve further, or does the model's effectiveness plateau with the three features you selected?
2.  Could you provide any qualitative analysis or examples that offer insight into the coherence patterns learned by the convolutional filters? For instance, can you identify specific filters that activate for known coherence phenomena, such as a consistent subject-position entity, and show how they contribute to the final score?
3.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model, could you speculate further on why it might have underperformed on the WSJ dataset? Do you believe this points to a fundamental advantage of grounding a neural model in an explicit linguistic representation like the entity grid, as opposed to operating on learned sentence vectors, for modeling local coherence?
