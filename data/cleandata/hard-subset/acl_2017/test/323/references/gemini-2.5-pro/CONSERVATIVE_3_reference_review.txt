### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, building upon the well-established entity grid framework. The authors argue that traditional entity grid models suffer from data sparsity due to their reliance on discrete feature representations, which limits their ability to model long-range entity transitions, and that their feature extraction is decoupled from the downstream task. To address this, they propose a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that operates on a distributed representation of the entity grid. The model learns embeddings for grammatical roles, applies convolutional filters to capture transition patterns, and is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their model on standard sentence ordering and summary coherence rating tasks, reporting state-of-the-art results that outperform previous entity grid models.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  The application of a standard CNN architecture to the entity grid representation for local coherence modeling.
2.  An empirical demonstration that this neural approach provides consistent, moderate improvements over strong, feature-engineered baselines on several established coherence evaluation tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this work lies in its experimental results. The proposed models, Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN, consistently outperform their non-neural counterparts on three different tasks. The reported gains of ~4% in discrimination accuracy and >4% in summary coherence rating over strong baselines from Elsner & Charniak (2011) are noteworthy and suggest the practical utility of the proposed method. The use of significance testing adds rigor to these claims.

2.  **Sound Evaluation Methodology:** The authors adopt a well-established evaluation protocol, using standard datasets (WSJ portion of the PTB, DUC'03 summaries) and tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) from prior work. This facilitates direct comparison and situates the work properly within the existing literature. The decision to use the larger WSJ corpus over smaller, genre-specific corpora is well-justified, particularly for training a data-hungry neural model.

3.  **Clear Motivation:** The paper provides a clear and compelling motivation for moving beyond traditional entity grid models. The limitations of discrete features, particularly the "curse of dimensionality" when modeling longer transitions or incorporating entity-specific features, are well-known problems. The proposed neural architecture is presented as a principled solution to these specific issues, which makes the paper easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The core of the proposed method is a standard CNN architecture (embedding layer, convolution, max-pooling, output layer) that has been widely applied in NLP for several years for tasks like sentence classification. While applying this architecture to the entity grid is a logical step, it cannot be considered a significant methodological innovation in itself. The paper presents this as a new architecture for coherence, but it is more accurately described as an application of an existing, well-understood neural architecture to a classic NLP representation.

2.  **Inadequate Comparison with a Key Neural Baseline:** The authors mention and then dismiss the contemporary work of Li and Hovy (2014), another neural approach to coherence, stating that their reimplementation yielded "very disappointing" results. This is a significant weakness. To claim state-of-the-art performance, a thorough comparison with competing methods is essential. Simply excluding a key baseline due to implementation difficulties is insufficient. The authors should have provided a more detailed analysis of why the model might have failed on their dataset or made a more robust attempt to reproduce the original work's performance. Without this comparison, the claim that this model represents the state of the art for neural coherence modeling is unsubstantiated.

3.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison of Extended Models:** The "Extended Grid-CNN" is compared against the "Extended Grid (E&C)" model. However, the neural model uses only three entity-specific features, while the baseline E&C model uses nine, some of which require external corpora. The authors frame this as a strength, but it obfuscates the source of the performance gain. It is unclear how much of the neural model's advantage comes from the architecture itself versus the different feature sets. A more controlled experiment, where the E&C baseline is restricted to the same three features, is necessary for a fair comparison of the models' representational power.

4.  **Lack of Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper demonstrates *that* the model works better, but provides no insight into *why*. Traditional grid models offer some interpretability through their feature weights (e.g., highlighting the importance of S->S transitions). The CNN, however, is treated as a black box. A qualitative analysis of the learned convolutional filters could have revealed what kinds of entity transition patterns the model learns to associate with coherence. Such analysis would have elevated the paper from a pure engineering result to a work that also provides new insights into the linguistic phenomenon of coherence.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model: Can you provide more detail on your implementation and the specific issues that led to its poor performance? Given that it is the most relevant neural baseline, its exclusion weakens your state-of-the-art claim. Was there any attempt to contact the original authors to debug the performance discrepancy?

2.  To ensure a fair comparison between your Extended Grid-CNN and the non-neural Extended Grid model, could you report the performance of the Elsner & Charniak (2011) model when restricted to the same three entity-specific features used in your neural model? This would help isolate the contribution of the CNN architecture itself.

3.  Could you provide any qualitative analysis of the learned convolutional filters? For instance, can you identify specific filters that respond strongly to known coherent transition patterns (e.g., a subject persisting across several sentences) or incoherent ones? This would add significant value by providing insight into what the model has learned.
