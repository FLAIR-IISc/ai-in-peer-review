### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, building upon the classic entity-grid representation. The model replaces the traditional feature engineering approach of calculating discrete entity transition probabilities with an end-to-end learned system. It first represents the symbolic roles in the entity grid (e.g., Subject, Object) as continuous vectors via an embedding layer. A convolutional neural network (CNN) is then applied over the columns of this embedded grid to learn salient entity transition patterns of varying lengths. Finally, a pooling layer and a linear scoring layer produce a single coherence score for the input text. The model is trained using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary rating—and demonstrate that their neural models significantly outperform strong non-neural entity-grid baselines and achieve state-of-the-art results.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and well-motivated neural architecture for entity-based coherence modeling.** The paper presents a novel application of convolutional networks to the entity-grid representation. This approach elegantly addresses a key limitation of prior work—the curse of dimensionality associated with modeling long-range, discrete entity transitions—by leveraging distributed representations and learned filters.
2.  **Strong empirical validation of end-to-end learning for a classic NLP task.** The work provides compelling evidence that learning task-specific features directly from data surpasses the traditional, decoupled pipeline of feature extraction and classification for coherence assessment. The consistent, significant gains over strong baselines on multiple benchmarks firmly establish the value of this approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Model Design.** The paper does a great job of identifying the core weaknesses of traditional entity-grid models (data sparsity for long transitions, task-agnostic feature engineering) and proposing a neural architecture that directly addresses them. The choice of a CNN is well-justified, as it is naturally suited to learning location-invariant patterns (i.e., specific entity transitions) over sequences. The end-to-end training with a ranking loss is also a principled way to optimize the model directly for the target tasks.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation.** The authors evaluate their model on three distinct and standard tasks, using widely accepted datasets (WSJ and DUC'03). The comparisons are made against strong and relevant baselines, including the state-of-the-art models from Elsner & Charniak (2011) implemented in the publicly available Brown coherence toolkit. The reporting of statistically significant improvements across all tasks provides robust evidence for the proposed model's superiority.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility.** The paper is clearly written, and the proposed architecture is explained well, aided by a helpful diagram. The authors provide sufficient detail about their experimental setup, including dataset statistics, hyperparameter tuning, and training procedures. The decision to release the code is commendable and significantly increases the value and impact of the work by facilitating reproducibility and future extensions.

4.  **Insightful Analysis.** The authors demonstrate that their extended neural model achieves superior performance with fewer hand-engineered, entity-specific features than its non-neural counterpart. This highlights the power of learned representations to capture relevant information more effectively. The inclusion of a pre-training experiment for the low-resource summary rating task is a thoughtful addition that further strengthens the results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an External NLP Pipeline.** The proposed model, while neural, still operates on a symbolic entity grid that is the product of a complex upstream pipeline (parsing for syntactic roles, coreference resolution). The performance is therefore fundamentally dependent on the quality of these tools. The paper does not analyze this dependency or investigate the model's robustness to potential errors (e.g., from parsing or coreference) that propagate from this pipeline. An analysis of how such errors affect the neural model compared to the traditional feature-based model would have strengthened the paper.

2.  **Limited Ablation and Architectural Analysis.** While the main results are strong, the analysis could be deeper. A key claim is that the neural model can capture longer-range transitions than traditional models, which are limited to k≤3 due to sparsity. However, this is not directly verified experimentally. An ablation study showing how the performance of the Grid-CNN and the traditional grid model varies as the filter/transition window size `k` increases would have provided direct evidence for this central motivation. Furthermore, the contribution of individual components (e.g., specific entity features in the extended model) is not ablated.

3.  **Ambiguity in the Pooling Mechanism.** A critical architectural detail seems underspecified. The entity grid has variable dimensions (number of sentences `m` and number of entities `n`). The paper states that a convolution is applied to each column, and then max-pooling is applied to each resulting feature map. It is unclear how features from a variable number of entities are aggregated into a fixed-size vector for the final scoring layer. The most likely interpretation is a global max-pooling operation over both the sentence and entity dimensions for each of the N filters, producing a fixed-size N-dimensional vector. Clarifying this point is important for a full understanding and reimplementation of the model.

4.  **Novelty of Neural Components.** While the application to coherence is novel, the core neural components (embeddings, CNN, max-pooling, ranking loss) are standard techniques that were widely used in NLP at the time of this work (e.g., for sentence classification). The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how the architecture is specifically tailored to the unique 2D, sparse structure of the entity grid, beyond simply applying 1D convolutions independently to each column.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the pooling mechanism used to produce the final fixed-size feature vector `m` before the scoring layer? Is it a global max-pooling operation performed over all positions in all columns for each of the N feature maps, resulting in an N-dimensional vector?

2.  One of the main motivations for your model is its ability to capture longer-range dependencies (e.g., k=5-6) compared to traditional models. Would it be possible to provide an empirical analysis comparing your Grid-CNN to the baseline Grid model as the window size `k` is varied (e.g., for k=2, 3, 4, 5)? This would directly substantiate this key claim.

3.  Given the model's reliance on upstream parsing and coreference resolution, have you performed any analysis on its sensitivity to the quality of these components? For instance, does the performance degrade gracefully if a less accurate parser is used to generate the grid?
