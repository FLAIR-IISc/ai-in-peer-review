### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper introduces a neural network architecture for modeling local text coherence, designed as a successor to the well-known entity grid model. The core idea is to represent the entity grid—a matrix of entities and their grammatical roles across sentences—using learned, distributed embeddings. A convolutional neural network (CNN) is then applied over the columns of this embedded grid (representing entity transitions) to extract higher-level features indicative of coherence. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective, which allows it to learn task-specific features.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  The application of a convolutional neural network directly to the entity grid representation for coherence modeling. This is a novel approach that addresses the "curse of dimensionality" limitation of previous discrete models, enabling the capture of longer-range entity transitions.
2.  The development of an end-to-end trainable framework for the entity grid, which moves away from the decoupled and task-agnostic feature engineering of prior work.
3.  An empirical demonstration that this neural approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Model Architecture:** The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant "neuralization" of the entity grid model. The justification for using a neural approach is clear: to overcome the sparsity and dimensionality issues that limited previous models to short entity transitions (k ≤ 3). The choice of a CNN is particularly well-suited for this problem, as it naturally captures local transition patterns in a position-invariant manner, which aligns with the underlying assumptions of the entity grid. The end-to-end training paradigm is a significant conceptual improvement.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed models (Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN) demonstrate substantial and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including the sophisticated extended entity grid model from Elsner & Charniak (2011). Achieving absolute gains of ~4% on the discrimination task and ~4% on summary rating is impressive and suggests the model is effectively learning powerful representations for coherence.

3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation Setup:** The authors validate their model on three different and well-established tasks, using standard datasets (WSJ and DUC'03). This allows for direct and meaningful comparison with a large body of previous work and demonstrates the model's versatility. The comparison between the basic `Grid-CNN` and the feature-enriched `Extended Grid-CNN` also serves as a useful ablation study.

### Weaknesses

Despite the novel idea and strong results, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws, ranging from critical methodological inconsistencies to a general lack of precision and care in presentation.

1.  **Major Inconsistency in Experimental Results:** The results presented for the sentence ordering tasks in Table 3 are highly suspect. The accuracy scores for the "Discrimination" and "Insertion" tasks are reported as being *identical* for every model, down to the hundredth of a percentage point. This is statistically improbable and contradicts the paper's own description of the insertion task as being "much more difficult." Furthermore, the text explicitly states the insertion metric is the "average fraction of sentences per document reinserted in their actual position," which is a single score, not an accuracy that can be directly compared to a pairwise discrimination task. This suggests a fundamental error in the evaluation methodology for the insertion task, which casts serious doubt on one of the paper's key claims of state-of-the-art performance.

2.  **Imprecise and Ambiguous Model Description:** The technical description of the model lacks the rigor expected for a top-tier publication.
    *   Equation (2), `ht = f(w TLt:t+k−1,j + bt)`, is dimensionally confusing. For a filter `w` of size `k.d` and an input patch of the same size, the transpose `w^T` is unconventional. The bias term `bt` is indexed by position `t`, which is non-standard for a CNN filter and implies a different bias at each application, which is likely not what was intended.
    *   Figure 2, while conceptually useful, is misleading. The output of the convolution is depicted as a single vector per feature map, whereas a feature map is a sequence of activations. The pooling operation is also vaguely represented.
    *   Details are sparse. The exact padding strategy for the "wide convolution" is not specified, and other architectural details are relegated to footnotes where they might be missed.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison with a Key Neural Baseline:** The authors dismiss the highly relevant neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014) in a brief remark, stating its performance was "very disappointing." Simply excluding a primary competitor because an off-the-shelf implementation did not perform well on a new dataset is scientifically unsound. A proper analysis would require investigation into the cause of this failure. This omission weakens the paper's claim to be state-of-the-art in the neural coherence modeling space.

4.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Errors:** The paper demonstrates a notable lack of attention to detail. The reference section is in disarray, with inconsistent formatting, stray punctuation (e.g., `Barzilay and Lapata.,?`), strange character sequences (e.g., `\Q2012\E`), and incomplete entries. This sloppiness extends to the main text, with awkward phrasing ("gain in insertion is less verbose") and inconsistent citation styles. While minor individually, the sheer volume of these errors suggests a hastily prepared manuscript and undermines the credibility of the work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Please clarify the results in Table 3. Why are the accuracy scores for the Discrimination and Insertion tasks identical? Can you please describe the exact evaluation protocol used for the insertion task and explain how the reported "Acc F1" metric was calculated from it?

2.  Could you provide a more precise, standard mathematical formulation for your convolution layer (Equation 2), clarifying the dimensions of the weight matrix and the nature of the bias term?

3.  Could you elaborate on your attempts to produce a fair comparison with the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Given its relevance, a more thorough analysis of why it failed on your dataset seems necessary to properly contextualize your own model's performance.

4.  The development set for the summary coherence rating task contains only 14 pairs. How do you justify tuning the hyperparameters of a complex neural network on such a small dataset, particularly for the models that were not pre-trained? How can you ensure the chosen parameters are not simply overfitting to this tiny validation set?
