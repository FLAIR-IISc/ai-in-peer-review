**Review**

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local text coherence, building directly upon the classic entity grid framework. The core idea is to replace the traditional sparse, discrete feature representation of entity transitions with learned, dense vector representations (embeddings). A convolutional neural network (CNN) is then applied over the columns of the entity grid (i.e., over the transition sequence for each entity) to learn salient, high-level features indicative of coherence. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking loss to learn to assign higher scores to more coherent texts.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and well-motivated neural architecture for entity-based coherence.** The application of a CNN to the columns of an entity grid is an elegant and effective solution to the "curse of dimensionality" that limited prior grid-based models. This approach allows the model to capture longer-range entity transition patterns without suffering from feature sparsity.
2.  **Strong and comprehensive empirical validation.** The paper presents state-of-the-art results on three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary rating) using a large, appropriate dataset (WSJ). The consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art entity grid model, provide convincing evidence for the proposed model's effectiveness.

**Strengths**

1.  **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The proposed model is technically sound and well-designed. Applying a 1D convolution over each entity's transition sequence is a natural and clever way to neuralize the entity grid. It directly addresses a key limitation of prior work—the inability to model long transition sequences (k > 3) due to data sparsity. The use of embeddings and convolutions is a principled method for learning robust, generalizable representations of these patterns.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive and convincing. The authors evaluate their model on three distinct tasks that test different aspects of coherence modeling. The comparison against strong and relevant baselines, particularly the Elsner & Charniak (2011) models, is crucial and well-executed. The consistent, significant gains reported across all tasks (e.g., ~4% absolute on discrimination, >4% on summary rating) strongly support the paper's claims.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established by outlining the limitations of previous models. The proposed architecture is explained lucidly, aided by an informative diagram (Figure 2). The experimental details are sufficient for reproducibility, which is further supported by the authors' decision to release their code.
4.  **Effective Incorporation of Features:** The paper demonstrates that the neural architecture can effectively incorporate entity-specific features (`Extended Grid-CNN`). Notably, this model achieves superior performance to its non-neural counterpart (`Extended Grid (E&C)`) while using only a subset of the original features, highlighting the power of the learned representations.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Non-Grid Coherence Models:** The evaluation is heavily focused on comparing the proposed model to other entity-grid-based approaches. While this is a necessary and important comparison, the paper would be stronger if it situated its results within the broader landscape of coherence models. For instance, comparisons to models based on discourse relations (e.g., Lin et al., 2011) or other neural sentence-level approaches would provide a more complete picture of the state of the art. The authors note that they were unable to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014), but a more detailed discussion of potential reasons for this failure (e.g., dataset characteristics, model architecture suitability) would be valuable for the community.
2.  **Lack of In-depth Model Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper offers limited qualitative analysis of *what* the model has learned. What kinds of transition patterns do the learned convolutional filters detect? For instance, do certain filters specialize in subject-object-subject chains, or patterns involving discourse-new entities? Visualizing filter weights or providing examples of texts where the neural model succeeds and the baseline fails would offer deeper insight into why the model works well and how it moves beyond the limitations of discrete n-gram features.
3.  **Task-Specific Training for Insertion:** The authors rightly note that the gains on the insertion task are smaller and hypothesize that this is because the model is not trained directly for this task. Given that insertion is arguably a more realistic and challenging measure of coherence than discrimination against a random permutation, it feels like a missed opportunity not to have explored training a model specifically on the insertion task. While this could be future work, presenting results from this experiment would have made the evaluation even more compelling.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model, could you elaborate on your hypothesis for its poor performance on the WSJ dataset? Do you believe this points to a fundamental limitation of sentence-vector-composition models for the permutation task, perhaps because they miss the explicit entity-linking information that grid models capture?
2.  Your `Extended Grid-CNN` outperforms the stronger `Extended Grid (E&C)` baseline while using only three of its nine entity-specific features. This is a very strong result. Do you have any insights from development experiments about the relative importance of these three features (named entity type, salience, proper mention) within your model?
3.  In your hyperparameter search, how sensitive was model performance to the filter window size (`k`)? An analysis showing performance as `k` varies would be interesting, as it would directly test the hypothesis that the neural model is better at capturing longer-range dependencies than traditional models, which are limited to `k`<=3.
