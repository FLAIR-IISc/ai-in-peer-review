### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) model for assessing local text coherence, framed as a "neuralization" of the well-known entity-grid model. The core idea is to represent the entity grid not with discrete symbols, but with learned, continuous embeddings for each entity's grammatical role. A CNN is then applied over the columns of this embedded grid (i.e., over entity transition sequences) to learn salient coherence patterns. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and effective neural architecture for local coherence:** The paper successfully adapts a standard CNN architecture to the entity-grid representation. This is a well-motivated approach that directly addresses key limitations of prior grid-based models, namely the curse of dimensionality associated with modeling long-range entity transitions and the task-agnostic nature of feature extraction.
2.  **Strong empirical results establishing a new state of the art:** The proposed model significantly and consistently outperforms previous entity-grid models and other baselines across three distinct and established coherence benchmarks. The gains are robust and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed neural approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant model:** The paper does an excellent job of identifying the core weaknesses of traditional entity-grid models (feature sparsity with long transitions, task-agnostic features) and proposes a neural solution that directly addresses them. Using embeddings to create distributed representations and a CNN to identify location-invariant transition patterns is a natural and sound application of deep learning techniques to this classic NLP problem. The end-to-end training via a ranking loss is also highly appropriate for coherence assessment tasks.
2.  **Comprehensive and rigorous evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on three different tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) that test different facets of coherence modeling. The comparison against strong and relevant baselines, particularly the non-neural "Grid-all nouns" and "Extended Grid" models, provides a clear measure of the specific contribution of the neural components. The use of statistical significance testing and reporting averages over multiple runs lends credibility to the results.
3.  **Significant performance improvements:** The model achieves substantial gains over prior state-of-the-art systems. For instance, the Extended Grid-CNN achieves an absolute improvement of nearly 4% on the discrimination task and over 2.5% on the more challenging insertion task compared to the strong Elsner & Charniak (2011) model. These are meaningful improvements on established benchmarks, clearly demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
4.  **Clarity and readability:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the model is explained in sufficient detail with helpful diagrams, and the experiments are well-described. The clear connection to the original entity grid framework makes the paper accessible to readers familiar with prior work in this area.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient comparison with other neural coherence models:** The paper's main competitor in the neural space is the sentence-representation-based model of Li and Hovy (2014). However, this model is dismissed with a brief note about poor replication results ("accuracy of only 19.34%"). This is a significant weakness. Without a more thorough comparison or at least a compelling analysis of *why* that model might have failed on this dataset, the claim to "state of the art results" is weakened. The paper convincingly shows it is the state-of-the-art *entity-grid-based* model, but its standing in the broader field of neural coherence models is less clear.
2.  **Lack of model analysis and interpretation:** The paper successfully demonstrates that the model works, but provides almost no insight into *what* it has learned. The "Why it works" section is a high-level intuition, but a deeper analysis is needed. For example, what filter sizes (transition lengths) were most effective? Can the learned filters be inspected to understand what kinds of transition patterns (e.g., 'S-S-S-', 'S-O-S-') the model finds most salient for coherence? Such analysis would elevate the paper from a strong engineering contribution to a more scientifically insightful one.
3.  **Crude integration of entity-specific features:** The method for incorporating entity features (e.g., creating a new vocabulary symbol "PERSON-S") feels simplistic and somewhat at odds with the distributed representation philosophy of the rest of the model. This approach expands the embedding vocabulary size |V| for every combination of role and feature, potentially re-introducing a form of sparsity. A more natural approach in a neural framework might be to learn separate embeddings for roles and entity features, and then combine them (e.g., via concatenation) before the convolutional layer. This was not explored or discussed.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model: Could you elaborate on your attempts to replicate their work? Given the stark difference in performance, is there a hypothesis for why a model based on sentence semantics would perform so poorly on the WSJ dataset compared to your entity-based one? This comparison seems crucial for positioning your work.
2.  The method for incorporating entity-specific features involves creating composite symbols (e.g., PERSON-S). Did you consider or experiment with alternative, more "neural" methods, such as having separate embedding lookups for grammatical roles and for entity features, and then concatenating these vectors before the convolution layer? This seems like it might offer better parameter sharing and generalization.
3.  Could you provide some analysis of the learned model? Specifically, does the performance vary significantly with the filter window size `k`? This would provide insight into the importance of modeling longer-range transitions, which is a key motivation for your work.
