### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a novel neural architecture for modeling local text coherence, building upon the entity grid representation of Barzilay and Lapata (2008). The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: the use of discrete features, which leads to data sparsity and restricts the modeling of long-range entity transitions, and the decoupling of feature extraction from the final task. To address this, they introduce a model that applies a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to the entity grid. The model first learns dense vector representations (embeddings) for grammatical roles and entity features. A convolutional layer then processes the sequence of roles for each entity to learn abstract features representing salient transition patterns. The entire network is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard tasks: sentence discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating. The results show that the proposed neural model significantly outperforms its non-neural counterparts and achieves new state-of-the-art performance on all tasks.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel neural architecture for coherence modeling that directly operates on the entity grid.** The primary contribution is the application of a Convolutional Neural Network to learn features from entity transition sequences. This method effectively replaces the traditional count-based feature engineering with a learned feature representation, allowing the model to capture longer-range dependencies (transitions of length 5-6 in experiments) without suffering from the data sparsity issues that limit previous models.
2.  **An end-to-end training paradigm for the entity grid model.** Unlike prior work where grid-based features are extracted in a task-agnostic manner, this model learns task-specific features via backpropagation from a pairwise ranking loss. This allows the representations and transition patterns to be optimized directly for the coherence assessment task at hand.
3.  **Strong empirical validation establishing a new state of the art on standard coherence benchmarks.** The paper provides a thorough evaluation on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and summary rating tasks. The proposed models demonstrate consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including the widely-used extended entity grid model of Elsner and Charniak (2011).

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Technical Soundness.** The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated neural architecture for a classic NLP task. The application of a CNN to the columns of an entity grid is an elegant way to model local, position-invariant transition patterns, directly addressing a core limitation (the curse of dimensionality) of previous grid-based models. The overall architecture, from embedding to convolution and pairwise training, is technically sound and clearly explained.
2.  **Compelling Empirical Results.** The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The model achieves absolute gains of around 4% on the discrimination task and over 4% on summary coherence rating compared to the previous state-of-the-art non-neural model. The fact that the extended neural model outperforms its stronger non-neural counterpart while using fewer entity-specific features further underscores the power of the learned representations. The use of significance tests adds rigor to these claims.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model is described with sufficient detail, and the connection to previous work is clearly articulated. The authors' decision to use a publicly available toolkit to reproduce baseline results and to release their own code significantly enhances the reproducibility of the work and provides a valuable resource for the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Analysis of Learned Representations.** While the model achieves strong performance, the paper provides little insight into *why* it works. The "Why it works" paragraph in Section 3 is speculative. An analysis of the learned representations would have strengthened the paper considerably. For instance, visualizing the learned convolutional filters could reveal whether the model is capturing linguistically meaningful transition patterns (e.g., subject-to-subject chains) or discovering novel patterns. An ablation study on the filter window size (`k`) would also provide empirical evidence for the claim that modeling longer transitions is beneficial.
2.  **Dependence on a Complex Pre-processing Pipeline.** The model's input is an entity grid, which requires a substantial pre-processing pipeline including sentence splitting, parsing (for grammatical roles), and coreference resolution. The quality of this input is critical to the model's performance, but the paper does not discuss the potential impact of errors from these upstream components. While this is a limitation shared by all entity grid models, a discussion of the model's robustness or sensitivity to pre-processing quality would be valuable.
3.  **Simplistic Handling of Entity-Specific Features.** The "Extended Grid-CNN" incorporates entity-specific features by creating new composite symbols for the embedding vocabulary (e.g., `PERSON-S`). While effective for the three features used, this approach may not scale well to a larger number of features or continuous features, as it would lead to a combinatorial explosion of the vocabulary size. The paper compares its model using three features to a baseline that uses nine, leaving it unclear how the model would perform or scale with a richer feature set. This makes the comparison between the extended models slightly uneven.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The Extended Grid-CNN uses only three of the nine features from the Elsner & Charniak (2011) model. Could you elaborate on the rationale for this choice? Does the proposed feature incorporation method (concatenating feature values and roles into single symbols) scale effectively if more features were added, or would it lead to vocabulary size and data sparsity issues for the embedding layer?
2.  Your paper is motivated by the ability of CNNs to model longer-range entity transitions than previous methods. The experiments report an optimal filter size of 5 or 6. Could you provide an analysis of how performance varies with different filter sizes? This would help quantify the benefit of modeling longer transitions and provide insight into the nature of the coherence patterns in the data.
3.  You report very poor performance when attempting to replicate the sentence-representation model of Li and Hovy (2014) on your dataset. Given that their model also uses a neural approach, this result is quite surprising. Can you speculate further on the reasons for this discrepancy? Could it be due to fundamental differences between entity-based vs. sentence-embedding-based approaches on this particular dataset, or perhaps a mismatch in model capacity or training requirements?
