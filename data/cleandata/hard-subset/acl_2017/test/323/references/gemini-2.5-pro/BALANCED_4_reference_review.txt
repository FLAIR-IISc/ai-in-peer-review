### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture for modeling local text coherence. The model is a neural adaptation of the well-known entity grid framework. It represents a document as an entity grid, learns distributed embeddings for the grammatical roles of entities (e.g., subject, object), and then applies a 1D convolution over each entity's transition sequence across sentences. This process is designed to automatically learn salient transition patterns indicative of coherence. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective, which forces it to assign higher scores to more coherent texts. The authors evaluate their model on three standard tasks—discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating—demonstrating state-of-the-art results that significantly outperform strong non-neural entity grid baselines.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and well-motivated neural architecture for local coherence assessment that effectively translates the discrete, feature-engineered entity grid model into a continuous, end-to-end learnable framework. The use of a CNN to capture location-invariant entity transition patterns is a natural and elegant fit for the problem.
2.  Strong and comprehensive empirical validation on multiple standard coherence tasks. The paper shows consistent and statistically significant improvements over competitive baselines, including the strong extended entity grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011), firmly establishing the practical value of the proposed neural approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating its approach by clearly identifying the limitations of prior entity grid models, namely the "curse of dimensionality" which restricts them to short transition lengths, and the task-agnostic nature of their feature extraction. The proposed neural model directly addresses these two shortcomings by using distributed representations to handle sparsity and by learning task-specific features via end-to-end training.
2.  **Sound and Effective Model Design:** The architecture is thoughtfully designed. The choice of a CNN is well-justified for learning local, position-invariant transition patterns, which aligns with the core principles of entity-based coherence. The overall pipeline—from role embeddings to convolution, pooling, and a final scoring layer trained with a ranking loss—is a standard and robust deep learning methodology applied effectively to this problem.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental setup is rigorous. The authors evaluate their model on three distinct and well-established tasks, using standard datasets (WSJ, DUC'03), which allows for clear comparison with previous work. The comparison against strong baselines, particularly the Elsner & Charniak (2011) models, makes the reported performance gains (e.g., ~4% absolute on discrimination) highly convincing. The diligence in reporting average results over multiple runs and conducting significance tests further strengthens the validity of the claims.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The model is explained lucidly with the help of a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The authors have also released their code, which is a commendable practice that enhances the paper's contribution to the community by facilitating reproducibility and future extensions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Other Neural Coherence Models:** The paper's claim to "state of the art results" is weakened by the omission of a proper comparison with the neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014). The authors mention trying to run the model and achieving poor results, but this is insufficient. A key part of research is understanding why a competing method fails or succeeds. Dismissing a highly relevant baseline without a deeper investigation or discussion (e.g., analyzing dataset differences, attempting hyperparameter retuning) leaves a significant gap in the evaluation. A comparative discussion of the architectural differences (entity-based vs. sentence-representation-based) and their potential implications would have greatly enriched the paper.
2.  **Lack of Model Analysis and Interpretability:** While the model achieves strong quantitative results, the paper offers no insight into *what* coherence patterns it has learned. The convolutional filters are meant to detect specific transition patterns, but we are given no examples. A qualitative analysis—for instance, identifying the input transition n-grams that maximally activate certain filters—could reveal whether the model has rediscovered principles from Centering Theory (e.g., preferring subject-to-subject transitions) or learned other, novel patterns. This lack of analysis is a missed opportunity to move beyond engineering a better number and contribute deeper linguistic or scientific understanding.
3.  **Incomplete Implementation of the "Extended" Model:** The "Extended Grid-CNN" is compared to the "Extended Grid (E&C)" model, but it only incorporates 3 of the 9 entity-specific features used by the E&C baseline. The authors state this choice was for simplicity, as the features were "easy to compute." While the model still performs well, this makes the comparison less direct. It is unclear if the performance gain comes from the neural architecture itself or the model's implicit preference for this specific feature subset. A more principled comparison would involve attempting to incorporate all features to create a true neural counterpart to the E&C model, which would provide a clearer measure of the architectural benefits.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on your experiments with the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Given its relevance as another neural approach to coherence, a more detailed discussion is needed. Did you attempt to re-tune its hyperparameters on the WSJ development set? Can you speculate on the potential reasons for its poor performance on this dataset compared to your entity-based approach?
2.  The strength of your CNN model lies in its ability to learn salient entity transition patterns automatically. Could you provide some qualitative analysis of the learned filters? For example, by examining the highest-weighted filters or finding the n-gram patterns that cause the highest activations, could you give us examples of what the model considers to be patterns of "good" or "bad" coherence?
3.  Your Extended Grid-CNN uses a subset of features from the Elsner & Charniak (2011) model. What were the technical challenges, if any, in incorporating the remaining six features? A more direct comparison using the full feature set would make the claims about the superiority of the neural architecture more conclusive. Did you run any experiments with a larger feature set?
