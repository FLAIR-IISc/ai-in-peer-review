### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel neural architecture for modeling local text coherence. The core idea is to apply a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) directly to the well-known entity grid representation of a text. Instead of using discrete symbols for grammatical roles (Subject, Object, etc.), the model first learns distributed vector representations (embeddings) for these roles. A CNN then operates over these embeddings to capture salient entity transition patterns of arbitrary length, which are used to produce a final coherence score. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel and principled neuralization of a classic symbolic model:** This is the paper's key strength. It doesn't simply replace the entity grid with a generic sentence encoder. Instead, it thoughtfully integrates a neural architecture (CNN) with a linguistically-motivated structure (the entity grid), preserving the core assumptions of the original model while overcoming its fundamental limitations. This provides a blueprint for how to bridge symbolic, theory-driven NLP with modern, data-driven deep learning.
2.  **An effective solution to the "curse of dimensionality" in coherence modeling:** By using distributed representations and convolutional filters, the model elegantly sidesteps the combinatorial explosion that limited previous grid-based models to very short transitions (k ≤ 3). The ability to model longer-range dependencies (k=5-8) is a significant practical advance.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and a Bold New Direction:** The paper's most significant strength is its conceptual leap. At a time when many neural approaches were focused on raw text, this work proposes a hybrid model that injects explicit linguistic structure into a deep learning framework. Applying a CNN to a grid of grammatical roles is a creative and non-obvious idea. It moves the field beyond hand-crafted feature engineering for this task and opens up a new and promising research direction. This is exactly the kind of bold, paradigm-shifting work that the community should encourage.

2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed architecture is not just novel, but also highly appropriate for the task. The use of embeddings to create a continuous space for grammatical roles is a natural way to enable generalization. The choice of a CNN is particularly clever, as its core properties—detecting local patterns in a location-invariant manner—perfectly align with the goal of identifying common, coherent entity transition patterns (e.g., subject-chaining) regardless of where they appear in a document.

3.  **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The model demonstrates strong and consistent performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on three distinct and standard coherence evaluation tasks. The gains over powerful non-neural baselines, including the extended entity grid model, are statistically significant and practically meaningful (e.g., ~4% absolute gain in discrimination). This provides compelling evidence that the proposed neural approach is not just conceptually interesting but also empirically superior.

4.  **Demonstrated Power of Learned Representations:** A subtle but important strength is that the `Extended Grid-CNN` achieves its SOTA results using only a subset of the entity-specific features required by its non-neural counterpart. This suggests that the end-to-end training process allows the model to learn more powerful and task-relevant feature combinations from simpler inputs, reducing the burden of manual feature engineering.

### Weaknesses

While the paper is strong, there are a few points that could be strengthened. In line with my philosophy, I see these as opportunities for future work rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Limited Comparison with Other Neural Approaches:** The authors honestly report their inability to replicate the results of the contemporary distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014). This is understandable, but it leaves a crucial comparison unanswered. The current experiments convincingly show that a neural grid model beats a non-neural grid model. However, it is less clear how this structured neural approach compares to unstructured neural approaches that operate on sentence representations directly. A successful comparison would have more definitively established the value of the entity grid structure itself in the neural era.

2.  **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The paper provides a strong "black-box" validation of the model's performance but misses an opportunity to provide insight into *why* it works. The central claim is that the convolutional filters learn to detect salient transition patterns. It would have been immensely valuable to see an analysis or visualization of the learned filters or embeddings. For example, do certain filters specialize in detecting subject-continuity patterns ('S-S-S')? Does the model learn that 'S' and 'O' are more similar to each other than to '-'? Such analysis would connect the model's learned components back to the linguistic theories that inspired the original entity grid.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The convolutional filters are the heart of your model's ability to learn transition patterns. Have you performed any qualitative analysis to interpret what these filters learn? For instance, by examining the grid patches that maximally activate certain filters, can you identify if they correspond to known linguistic patterns of coherent discourse (e.g., subject chains, object-to-subject transitions)?
