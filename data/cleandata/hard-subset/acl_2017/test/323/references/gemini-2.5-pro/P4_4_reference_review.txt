### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The model replaces the traditional discrete feature extraction from the grid with a series of learned transformations. First, it maps the grammatical roles and entity-specific features in the grid to dense vector embeddings. Then, a convolutional neural network (CNN) is applied over the columns of the grid (representing entity transitions across sentences) to learn abstract representations of transition patterns. These features are then passed through a max-pooling layer and a final linear layer to produce a single coherence score for the document. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their model on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and show that it achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming previous entity grid models.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel and technically sound neural architecture that applies convolutional networks to the entity grid representation for coherence modeling. This approach effectively addresses key limitations of prior grid-based models, namely the data sparsity and combinatorial explosion associated with modeling long-range transitions and complex features using discrete representations.
2.  A strong empirical demonstration of the proposed model's effectiveness across three standard coherence evaluation tasks. The experiments are thorough, comparing against strong baselines on a standard, large-scale dataset (WSJ), and the results show consistent and statistically significant improvements.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Design:** The proposed neural architecture is a direct and logical modernization of the highly successful entity grid model. The paper clearly identifies the weaknesses of previous discrete models (the "curse of dimensionality" and task-agnostic feature extraction) and presents a technically sound solution. Using embeddings for roles and a CNN to capture transition patterns is an elegant way to learn dense, task-specific features from the grid structure, enabling the model to capture longer-range dependencies (e.g., transitions of length 5-6) without suffering from data sparsity.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is a major strength of this work. The authors test their model on three distinct and well-established tasks that measure different facets of coherence assessment. The use of the WSJ corpus is appropriate, as it is larger and more stylistically diverse than other commonly used corpora, making it a suitable benchmark for training and evaluating neural models. The reported results are not only strong but are also shown to be statistically significant improvements over well-chosen and competitive baselines, including the state-of-the-art extended entity grid model.
3.  **High Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The model architecture is explained clearly, aided by an informative diagram (Figure 2). The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, including hyperparameter settings, training procedures, and statistical testing methods. The authors' commitment to reproducibility, demonstrated by using a public toolkit for baseline comparisons and releasing their own code, is commendable and adds significant value to the submission.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Analysis of Model Behavior:** While the paper demonstrates *that* the model performs well, it offers little insight into *why*. The primary motivation is the ability to capture longer-range transitions and learn better features, but this is not empirically verified beyond showing improved final scores. An analysis or ablation study on the impact of the convolutional filter size would have directly substantiated the claim about the importance of longer transitions. Furthermore, there is no qualitative analysis of what the learned filters capture (e.g., specific transition patterns like S-S-S-O vs. S-X-S-X) or what the learned embeddings represent. Such analysis would provide a deeper understanding of the model's mechanisms and the nature of neural coherence patterns.
2.  **Incomplete Comparison with Baselines and Related Work:** The comparison with the "Extended Grid (E&C)" model is potentially disadvantaged. The authors' "Extended Grid-CNN" uses only three of the nine entity-specific features from the E&C model, citing simplicity as the reason. While the neural model still wins, it is unclear how much of the performance gap is attributable to the architecture versus the feature set. A model using all nine features would have provided a more direct comparison. Additionally, the dismissal of another relevant neural approach (Li and Hovy, 2014) due to poor replication results, without further investigation or discussion, weakens the paper's positioning within the landscape of neural coherence models.
3.  **Lack of Task-Specific Training for Insertion Task:** The authors note that the performance gains on the insertion task are less pronounced than on the discrimination task. They speculate this is because the model was trained on the discrimination task (distinguishing original vs. globally permuted documents) rather than the insertion task (finding the correct position for a single sentence). This is a valid point, but it also highlights a limitation in the experimental design. Given that the insertion task is arguably a more realistic and fine-grained measure of local coherence, training and evaluating a model specifically for this task would provide a more complete picture of the architecture's capabilities.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The Extended Grid-CNN uses only a subset (3 out of 9) of the entity-specific features available in the non-neural Extended Grid baseline. Could you clarify if this decision was based on preliminary experiments? Would incorporating the remaining features (e.g., those requiring external corpora) be expected to further improve performance, or does it introduce prohibitive complexity or noise for the neural model?
2.  Your model uses convolutional filter sizes of 5 and 6, which are larger than the transition lengths (k ≤ 3) typically used in non-neural grid models. Could you provide an ablation study showing how performance on the discrimination task varies with different filter sizes (e.g., k=2, 3, 4, 5, 6)? This would directly support the hypothesis that the ability to model longer-range transitions is a key advantage of your architecture.
