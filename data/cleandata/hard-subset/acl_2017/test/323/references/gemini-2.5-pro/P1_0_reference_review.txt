**Summary and Contributions**
This paper presents a neural network-based model for local coherence assessment, designed as a direct successor to the popular entity grid model. The proposed method represents a document's entity grid by mapping its discrete symbols (e.g., Subject, Object) to continuous embeddings. A convolutional neural network (CNN) is then applied over the columns of this embedded grid to learn salient entity transition patterns. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary rating) using the WSJ corpus, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance that significantly surpasses previous entity grid models.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective "neuralization" of the entity grid model.** The paper successfully translates the core principles of the entity grid into a modern neural architecture. This approach elegantly addresses the key limitations of its predecessor, namely the curse of dimensionality and the inability to learn task-specific features, by leveraging distributed representations and end-to-end training.
2.  **Strong empirical validation and new state-of-the-art results.** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on standard benchmarks, comparing against strong and relevant baselines. The consistent and statistically significant improvements across all three tasks provide compelling evidence for the model's effectiveness.
3.  **A clear demonstration of how to model long-range dependencies in entity transitions.** By using a CNN, the model can utilize larger filter sizes (e.g., k=5-8) than the discrete entity grid (k<=3), allowing it to capture more complex and longer-range patterns of entity flow without suffering from data sparsity.

**Strengths**
1.  **Well-motivated and sound methodology.** The paper clearly identifies the weaknesses of existing entity grid models—data sparsity from discrete features and task-agnostic feature engineering—and proposes a solution that directly targets these issues. The choice of a CNN is well-justified, as it is a natural fit for detecting position-invariant local patterns (i.e., characteristic entity transitions) within the columns of the grid. The end-to-end training with a pairwise ranking loss is appropriate for coherence assessment tasks.
2.  **Thorough and convincing experimental setup.** The evaluation is robust. The authors use a large, standard dataset (WSJ), which is more challenging and general than smaller, domain-specific corpora used in some prior work. They compare against strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art non-neural entity grid models. The use of three distinct tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) demonstrates the model's versatility and effectiveness on problems of varying difficulty.
3.  **The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.** The motivation, model architecture (aided by a helpful diagram), and experimental results are presented with clarity and precision. The work is easy to follow and understand, and the authors' claims are well-supported by the evidence provided. The announced code release is a significant plus for reproducibility.
4.  **Practical insights on pre-training for low-data tasks.** The experiments on the summary coherence rating task show that pre-training the model on the larger discrimination task dataset significantly boosts performance. This is a valuable practical finding for applying complex neural models in data-scarce scenarios, which are common in NLP.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **The model inherits a key simplifying assumption from the original entity grid.** The paper states that "columns in a grid are treated independently." This means the model does not capture interactions *between* different entities (e.g., how the role of entity A influences the role of entity B in subsequent sentences). Coherence is often a product of such interactions. While improving upon the original grid is a valid contribution, the paper misses an opportunity to explore how a neural architecture could move beyond this limitation, for instance by using 2D convolutions or attention mechanisms across entity columns.
2.  **The handling of the Li & Hovy (2014) baseline is unsatisfying.** The authors report "very disappointing" results for this contemporary neural coherence model and relegate the finding to a brief remark, excluding it from the main results table. Simply stating "we were not sure what could go wrong" is insufficient. A more thorough analysis is needed. Is their model fundamentally better, or did the baseline fail due to implementation issues or specific properties of the WSJ dataset (e.g., longer documents) that their entity-centric approach handles better? A deeper discussion would have better situated their work within the landscape of neural approaches to coherence.
3.  **The comparison for the "Extended Grid-CNN" is not entirely fair.** The original Extended Grid by Elsner & Charniak (2011) uses nine entity-specific features, including four from external corpora. The authors' Extended Grid-CNN uses only a "simple" subset of three features that are easy to compute. While their model still outperforms the baseline, this setup potentially under-sells the neural model's ability to integrate rich features. A more direct comparison incorporating all nine features would have provided a stronger test of the architecture's capacity to leverage diverse information.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the assumption of entity independence: Have you considered or experimented with architectures that model interactions across the entity columns in the grid? Do you have intuitions on whether this is a promising direction for future work?
2.  Could you elaborate on the poor performance of the Li & Hovy (2014) model? Given that it is also a neural approach, a more detailed comparison or error analysis would be highly valuable. For example, does its sentence-level focus struggle on longer documents where entity-level continuity, as captured by your model, becomes more critical?
3.  For the Extended Grid-CNN, what was the reason for using only three of the nine features from the original extended model? Did you experiment with including more features, and if so, what were the results? A more direct comparison would strengthen your claims about the model's superiority.
