### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture to model local text coherence, building directly upon the classic entity grid representation. The model replaces the traditional feature engineering approach of counting discrete entity role transitions (e.g., S-O-X) with a learned, end-to-end system. It first learns distributed embeddings for grammatical roles (and optional entity-specific features), then applies 1D convolutions along each entity's transition sequence to learn higher-level features, which are subsequently pooled and used to produce a final coherence score. The model is trained using a pairwise ranking objective.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for a Classic Representation:** The primary contribution is the successful "neuralization" of the entity grid. The application of a CNN to learn features from entity transition sequences is a well-motivated and elegant solution to the "curse of dimensionality" that limited previous grid-based models to short transition lengths.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results Demonstrating the Value of End-to-End Learning:** The paper provides a convincing empirical demonstration that this learned approach outperforms its direct, feature-engineered non-neural counterparts (Elsner & Charniak, 2011) across three standard evaluation tasks. The gains are consistent and statistically significant, validating the hypothesis that learning task-specific features in an end-to-end manner is superior to a decoupled feature extraction and learning pipeline.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Novelty:** The proposed model is a very natural and clever evolution of the entity grid framework. The core limitations of the original model—sparsity and the inability to capture long-range dependencies—are directly addressed by using dense embeddings and convolutional filters. The modeling assumptions (independence of entities, location-invariance of transitions) are clearly stated and justify the choice of a 1D convolutional architecture. The method for incorporating entity-specific features by enriching the input vocabulary (e.g., 'S' -> 'PERSON-S') is simple but effective.

2.  **Rigorous and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength. The authors evaluate on multiple, standard tasks (Discrimination, Insertion, Summary Rating) using a large, appropriate dataset (WSJ) that is more general than older, domain-specific corpora. Crucially, the comparisons are made against the correct and strongest baselines: the non-neural entity grid models whose limitations this work aims to solve. The "apples-to-apples" comparison between `Grid-all nouns` and `Grid-CNN`, and between `Extended Grid` and `Extended Grid-CNN`, provides powerful evidence for the superiority of the neural approach. The reporting of state-of-the-art results is well-supported.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, aided by the helpful diagram in Figure 2. The authors provide sufficient detail about their experimental setup, hyperparameter tuning, and training procedure to inspire confidence in the results. The promise to release the code is a significant plus for the community and enhances the paper's potential impact.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope and Conceptual Framing:** While the paper honestly frames itself as a "Neural Local Coherence Model," it is still fundamentally constrained by the limitations of the entity grid itself. The model can only capture entity-centric coherence. It has no mechanism for modeling other crucial aspects of coherence, such as rhetorical structure, logical progression, or lexical cohesion beyond coreferent entities. The advance is therefore primarily architectural rather than conceptual in terms of what constitutes coherence. The paper would benefit from a more explicit acknowledgment of what types of coherence phenomena it *cannot* capture.

2.  **Lack of Model Analysis and Interpretation:** The paper demonstrates *that* the model works well, but provides little insight into *why*. The "Why it works" section is speculative and generic. A key advantage of neural models is their ability to learn complex features, but the paper makes no attempt to analyze what these features are. For instance, what kinds of transition patterns do the learned filters actually respond to? Do they rediscover known linguistic patterns (e.g., subject-chaining 'S-S-S...') or do they find novel, more complex ones? Visualizing filter weights or presenting examples of entity transitions that produce high activations would have moved the paper from a strong engineering contribution to one with deeper scientific insight.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Alternative Neural Approaches:** The dismissal of the Li and Hovy (2014) model is a significant weakness. Simply stating that "the results on our dataset were very disappointing" without further analysis is not a scientifically rigorous comparison. This is a missed opportunity, as Li and Hovy's model represents a fundamentally different neural approach to coherence (operating on sentence representations directly, without an entity grid). A more thorough comparison could have answered a key question: Is the entity grid still a valuable intermediate representation for coherence modeling in the neural era, or can we achieve better results by learning from raw text? The current paper implicitly argues for the former but fails to substantiate this claim against a key alternative.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Following up on Weakness #2, could you provide any qualitative analysis of the learned convolutional filters? For instance, can you identify specific filters that respond strongly to known coherence-inducing patterns like subject continuity ('S-S-S') or entity introduction followed by elaboration ('- - - S-O')? This would greatly strengthen the claim that the model learns meaningful features.

2.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model, could you elaborate on the difficulties you faced? Their model is trained on a classification task (coherent vs. incoherent sentence windows), whereas yours uses a ranking objective on full documents. Is it possible the poor performance was due to this mismatch in training objectives rather than a fundamental flaw in their approach? A more detailed discussion of the differences would help position your work more clearly.

3.  The model treats each entity column independently. This prevents it from learning patterns involving the interaction *between* different entities (e.g., a pattern where entity A is consistently the subject and entity B is the object). Have you considered architectures, such as 2D convolutions across the grid, that could capture such dependencies? What are your thoughts on the potential benefits or challenges of such an extension?
