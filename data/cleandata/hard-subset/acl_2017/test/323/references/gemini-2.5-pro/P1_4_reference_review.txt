**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) model to assess local text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The model aims to address key limitations of traditional entity grid models, namely their reliance on sparse, discrete features which limits their ability to capture long-range entity transitions, and their decoupled, task-agnostic feature engineering. The proposed architecture transforms the entity grid into a sequence of dense embeddings and applies 1D convolutions along each entity's transition column to learn abstract, task-specific features for scoring coherence. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their approach on sentence discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating tasks, demonstrating significant improvements over previous entity grid models and achieving new state-of-the-art results.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **An effective neuralization of the classic entity grid model.** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated application of a CNN architecture directly onto the entity grid structure. This successfully replaces hand-crafted, sparse feature extraction with a learnable, end-to-end system that captures richer transition patterns.
2.  **Strong empirical validation and new state-of-the-art results.** The work provides a comprehensive evaluation on three standard coherence tasks using established datasets. The consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong, publicly available baselines convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Technical Soundness:** The paper does an excellent job of identifying the core weaknesses of prior entity grid models—the curse of dimensionality for long transitions and the task-agnostic feature extraction. The proposed neural solution directly and elegantly addresses these problems. Using embeddings to create dense representations and a CNN to model location-invariant transition patterns is a very natural and technically sound approach for this problem.
2.  **Impressive and Consistent Empirical Gains:** The model achieves substantial improvements across all three evaluation tasks. The absolute gains of ~4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on the difficult insertion task, and >4% on summary rating are highly significant and push the state of the art forward. The fact that the basic `Grid-CNN` outperforms the feature-rich `Extended Grid (E&C)` on two of three tasks highlights the power of the learned representations.
3.  **Thorough and Reproducible Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is robust. The authors compare against relevant and strong baselines, including reproducing prior work using a public toolkit. The use of multiple diverse tasks (ordering and human rating) strengthens the claim of the model's general applicability. The paper provides sufficient detail on hyper-parameters and training, and the authors' commitment to releasing the code is a major asset for the community.
4.  **Well-Written and Easy to Follow:** The paper is exceptionally clear. The model is explained logically, and Figure 2 provides an intuitive visual summary of the architecture and training process. The motivation, methods, and results are presented in a way that is easy to understand and appreciate.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** While the application to the entity grid is novel, the core architecture (Embedding -> Conv -> Pool -> FC) is a standard pattern for many NLP tasks, heavily influenced by prior work in sentence classification (e.g., Kim, 2014). The paper would benefit from better situating its contribution in this context, acknowledging that the novelty lies in the creative application to a symbolic structure (the grid) rather than in the network design itself.
2.  **Lack of Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the model works, but offers little insight into *why* it works better. A key motivation is capturing longer-range dependencies (the paper uses filter sizes of 5 and 6), but there is no analysis to confirm that the model is indeed learning meaningful long-range patterns that shorter-range models cannot. Visualizing some of the learned filters or performing an ablation study on the filter size (k) would greatly strengthen the paper by providing insight into what the model has learned about coherence.
3.  **An Unbalanced Comparison for the Extended Model:** The `Extended Grid-CNN` is presented as the neural counterpart to the `Extended Grid (E&C)` model. However, the neural version uses only 3 simple entity-specific features, while the non-neural baseline uses 9 features, some of which require external corpora. This is an apples-to-oranges comparison. While it is impressive that the neural model wins with fewer features, it leaves open the question of how it would perform with the full feature set. A more direct comparison or an ablation study would provide a clearer picture of the relative contributions of the architecture versus the features.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the comparison between `Extended Grid-CNN` (3 features) and `Extended Grid (E&C)` (9 features): Can you clarify the reasoning for using only a subset of the features? Have you experimented with incorporating the other six features into your neural model? This would allow for a more direct comparison of the modeling paradigms.
2.  The paper highlights the model's ability to capture long-range transitions as a key advantage. Could you provide any analysis that sheds light on what these longer-range filters (e.g., k=5 or 6) are learning? For example, does performance degrade significantly if you restrict the filter size to 3, matching the limit of traditional models?
3.  The performance gain on the insertion task was smaller than on discrimination, which you speculate is due to a mismatch between the training task (discrimination) and the test task (insertion). Have you attempted to train a model specifically for the insertion task (e.g., by creating training pairs where the positive example is the original text and the negative example is the same text with one sentence misplaced)? It would be interesting to see if task-specific training closes the performance gap.
