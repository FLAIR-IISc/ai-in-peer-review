**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces a convolutional neural network (CNN) model for assessing local text coherence, built upon the classic entity-grid representation. The authors argue that traditional entity-grid models suffer from data sparsity and the curse of dimensionality, which prevents them from modeling long-range entity transitions, and that their feature engineering is decoupled from the downstream task. The proposed neural model addresses these issues by learning distributed representations (embeddings) for entity roles and using a CNN to learn features over arbitrarily long entity transitions. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on three standard coherence tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel and well-motivated application of a convolutional architecture to the entity-grid representation. This "neuralization" of a classic, symbolic NLP model effectively addresses its primary limitations (data sparsity and fixed, short-range features) in a principled manner.
2.  Strong and consistent empirical results across multiple standard benchmarks. The proposed model significantly outperforms strong baselines, including its non-neural counterparts, validating the effectiveness of the architecture for modeling local coherence.
3.  A flexible framework for incorporating entity-specific features directly into the representation, which are then learned in a task-specific, end-to-end fashion.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Elegant Model:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work by clearly outlining the limitations of prior entity-grid models. The proposed CNN-based solution is an elegant and intuitive way to address these limitations. Using embeddings overcomes the discrete representation problem, while the convolutional approach is a natural fit for capturing position-invariant transition patterns of varying lengths, directly tackling the core weakness of previous models.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, using three distinct and standard tasks that test different facets of coherence modeling (discrimination, insertion, summary rating). The choice of the WSJ corpus is well-justified due to its size and diversity compared to other commonly used corpora. The comparisons are made against appropriate and strong baselines, particularly the state-of-the-art models from Elsner and Charniak (2011).
3.  **Significant Performance Gains:** The proposed model achieves new state-of-the-art results on all evaluation tasks. The improvements are not marginal; for instance, the absolute gain of nearly 4% on the discrimination task over the strong Extended Grid baseline is impressive. The fact that the Extended Grid-CNN outperforms the E&C model while using fewer features further underscores the power of the architecture.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The paper provides sufficient details on the experimental setup, including dataset statistics and hyperparameter choices. The authors' statement that the code has been released with the submission is a significant strength, enabling verification and fostering future work in this area.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Other Neural Approaches:** The paper dismisses the contemporary neural model of Li and Hovy (2014) after reporting very poor performance (19.34% accuracy). The explanation—"We were not sure what could go wrong"—is inadequate for a scientific paper. This was a key alternative neural approach to coherence that operated on sentence representations rather than an entity grid. A deeper analysis is needed. Is there a fundamental reason why an entity-based inductive bias is superior for these tasks? Or could the poor result be due to implementation or hyperparameter issues? A more thorough comparison or at least a more detailed discussion of the potential reasons for this performance discrepancy would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about its place in the landscape of neural coherence models.
2.  **Unbalanced Comparison for the "Extended" Model:** The Extended Grid-CNN is shown to outperform the Extended Grid (E&C) model. However, the E&C model uses 9 entity-specific features (some from external corpora), whereas the proposed neural version uses only 3 simple ones. While this makes the result more impressive, it is not a direct, apples-to-apples comparison of the architectures. The performance gain could be even larger if all features were included, or the comparison would be fairer if the E&C baseline was re-run using only the same 3 features. As it stands, it is difficult to fully disentangle the contribution of the neural architecture from the difference in feature sets.
3.  **Lack of Model Interpretation:** The paper provides a good intuition for why the model works (i.e., filters detect specific transition patterns), but offers no analysis to support this claim. The work would be much more insightful if it included some qualitative analysis. For example, visualizing the learned embeddings for grammatical roles (e.g., via t-SNE) or examining the n-grams that maximally activate certain learned convolutional filters could provide concrete evidence of what coherence-indicating patterns the model has actually learned. Without this, the model remains a "black box," and we miss an opportunity to learn more about the nature of local coherence.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the results for the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Do you have any hypotheses for its failure on the WSJ dataset? For example, could it be that the sentence ordering tasks rely more heavily on local entity continuity, which is explicitly captured by the grid, than on semantic similarity between sentences, which their model focuses on?
2.  Regarding the Extended Grid-CNN, what was the reason for using only 3 of the 9 features from Elsner and Charniak (2011)? Would it be possible to include the others, and do you have any results from such an experiment? Alternatively, could you provide the performance of the non-neural E&C model when restricted to the same 3 features, to allow for a more direct architectural comparison?
3.  You hypothesize that filters learn specific transition patterns. Have you conducted any analysis to verify this? For instance, could you identify the entity transition sequences from the test set that produce the highest activations for some of the learned filters? This would provide valuable insight into the model's inner workings.
