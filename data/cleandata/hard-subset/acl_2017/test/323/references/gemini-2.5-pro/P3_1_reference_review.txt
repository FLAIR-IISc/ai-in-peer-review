### Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a neural network architecture to model local text coherence, directly extending the well-established entity grid model of Barzilay and Lapata (2008). The core idea is to replace the traditional, discrete feature engineering approach (i.e., counting fixed-length entity role transitions) with a learned, continuous representation. The model first represents a document as an entity grid, then looks up a distributed vector representation for each grammatical role (S, O, X, -). A 1D convolutional neural network (CNN) is applied over each entity's sequence of roles (i.e., each column of the grid) to learn representations of local transition patterns. Max-pooling is then used to select the most salient features, which are fed to a final layer to produce a single coherence score for the document. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective and well-motivated neural architecture for entity-based coherence modeling.** The paper successfully "neuralizes" a classic and influential NLP model. The application of a CNN to the columns of the entity grid is an elegant way to address the primary limitation of previous grid-based models: the inability to model long-range dependencies due to the curse of dimensionality.
2.  **State-of-the-art results on multiple standard coherence benchmarks.** The authors demonstrate convincing and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including the very models they are extending, on the discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating tasks. This provides strong empirical validation for their approach.
3.  **A demonstration of task-specific feature learning for coherence.** By training the model end-to-end, the architecture learns representations (embeddings and convolutional filters) that are optimized for the coherence assessment task, moving away from the task-agnostic feature extraction of prior work.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Grounding in Prior Work:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It starts from a widely-recognized and successful model (the entity grid), clearly articulates its specific limitations (discrete representations, sparsity for long transitions, task-agnostic features), and proposes a neural solution that directly targets each of these weaknesses. This makes the contribution easy to understand and situate within the field.
2.  **Sound and Elegant Methodology:** The proposed architecture is a natural and well-suited solution to the problem. Using a CNN to capture location-invariant transition patterns is a perfect fit for modeling entity role sequences, and the end-to-end pairwise training framework is appropriate for the task. The model design is simple, yet powerful, and avoids unnecessary complexity.
3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong. The authors evaluate on a large, standard dataset (WSJ) and across three different tasks that test different aspects of coherence modeling. The choice of baselines is excellent, particularly the inclusion of the direct non-neural counterparts from the Brown coherence toolkit (E&C models), which allows for a direct and fair comparison. The reported gains are consistent, substantial, and backed by significance testing, which lends high confidence to the results.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, with a clear logical flow. Figure 2 is particularly effective at communicating the model architecture and training setup. The authors' commitment to releasing their code is a significant strength that will benefit the community and facilitate future work in this area.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the model works, but provides little insight into *why* it works better. The "Why it works" section offers only a high-level intuition. A key advantage of the CNN is its purported ability to learn longer, more complex transition patterns than the k≤3 n-grams of previous models. However, there is no analysis to support this. The paper would be much stronger with a qualitative analysis of the learned filters. For instance, what kinds of transition patterns (e.g., 'S-S-S-X-', 'S-O---S') do the learned filters respond to most strongly? Does the model learn distinct filters for salient vs. non-salient entities? Without this, a central claim of the paper—capturing long-range dependencies—remains empirically demonstrated but not mechanistically understood.
2.  **Simplistic Integration of Entity-Specific Features:** The method for incorporating entity-specific features—by creating new composite symbols like 'PERSON-S'—is a potential weakness. This approach leads to a combinatorial explosion in the vocabulary size and treats symbols like 'PERSON-S' and 'ORGANIZATION-S' as completely independent, failing to capture the shared "subjectness". A more sophisticated approach, such as concatenating separate embeddings for the role and entity features before the convolutional layer, might offer better scalability and generalization. While the current method works well with just three features, its limitations should be acknowledged.
3.  **Insufficient Discussion of a Key Neural Baseline:** The authors' treatment of the Li and Hovy (2014) model is dismissive. They report that it performed very poorly on their data and then exclude it from the main results, stating "We were not sure what could go wrong." This is a missed opportunity for a valuable discussion. As a prominent neural coherence model from the same era, its failure is significant. Does this failure suggest that coherence is better captured by modeling explicit entity transitions (as this paper does) rather than holistic sentence representations? A more thorough attempt to diagnose the performance difference would have strengthened the paper's positioning against alternative neural approaches.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the integration of entity-specific features: Have you considered or experimented with alternative methods, such as using separate embedding channels for roles and features and concatenating them before the convolution? This might provide a more scalable and generalizable way to incorporate rich entity information.
2.  Following up on Weakness #1: Could you provide any qualitative analysis of what the model learns? For example, by identifying the n-gram transition patterns that maximally activate specific convolutional filters, you could give concrete examples of the coherence-indicating patterns your model has discovered, potentially highlighting patterns longer than the trigrams used in previous work.
3.  Could you elaborate further on the poor performance of the Li and Hovy (2014) model on your dataset? Does this result suggest a fundamental advantage of entity-centric models over sentence-vector-composition models for the types of local coherence measured by these tasks? A deeper analysis could provide valuable insights into the nature of coherence itself.
