### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The core idea is to replace the discrete feature engineering of traditional entity grid models with a learned representation. The proposed model uses a convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates on embeddings of grammatical roles in the entity grid. The CNN is designed to capture patterns in long-range entity transitions, and the entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their model on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and report state-of-the-art results, showing consistent improvements over previous entity grid models.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  The application of a convolutional neural network directly to the entity grid structure. This is a novel and well-motivated approach to learning features from entity transitions, addressing the "curse of dimensionality" that limited previous models to short transition lengths.
2.  An end-to-end training framework for an entity-based coherence model, which allows features to be learned in a task-specific manner, unlike the decoupled feature extraction in prior work.
3.  A strong set of empirical results on standard benchmarks, demonstrating that the proposed neural approach significantly outperforms its non-neural counterparts.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model:** The core contribution of applying a 1D CNN to the columns of an entity grid is elegant. It directly addresses the primary limitations of prior entity grid models—namely, their inability to model long-range transitions due to data sparsity. By moving to a distributed representation and a convolutional architecture, the model can learn robust features from longer patterns (k=5-6 in experiments vs. k<=3 previously), which is a clear and well-justified methodological advance.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents compelling results across three distinct and standard evaluation tasks. The proposed models, Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN, consistently and significantly outperform their respective non-neural baselines (Grid-all nouns and Extended Grid). Absolute gains of ~4% on discrimination and summary rating are substantial in these established tasks. This provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the neural approach.

3.  **Sound Evaluation Setup:** The authors largely follow established evaluation protocols, using the standard WSJ dataset for sentence ordering and the DUC'03 dataset for summary rating. Their decision to use the larger WSJ corpus over smaller, domain-specific corpora is well-justified, particularly for training a data-hungry neural model. The comparison against strong baselines from the publicly available Brown coherence toolkit enhances the credibility of the results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Flaws in Experimental Reporting:** The results presented in Table 3 are highly suspect. The accuracies for the Discrimination and Insertion tasks are reported as identical for every single model (e.g., 81.58 for Grid-all nouns, 88.69 for Extended Grid-CNN). Given that the paper itself describes Insertion as a "much more difficult task" than Discrimination, and that the tasks are procedurally very different (1-of-20 choice vs. 1-of-N sentence placement), it is statistically inconceivable for the results to be identical. This suggests a fundamental error in either the calculation or the reporting of these key metrics, which severely undermines the paper's primary empirical claims. Furthermore, Table 3 includes an "F1" column that is never defined, explained, or motivated anywhere in the text, leaving the reader to guess its meaning and relevance.

2.  **Ambiguous Methodological Description:** The description of the neural architecture lacks precision, making the model difficult to fully comprehend and reproduce.
    *   The convolution operation in Equation (2) includes a bias term `bt` indexed by position `t`, which implies a position-dependent bias. This is non-standard for a CNN, which typically uses a single shared bias per filter. This requires clarification.
    *   It is unclear how the feature maps are generated and combined. The text states that the filter is applied to transitions of "different entities," but the notation for the feature map `hi` suggests a single flattened vector. The paper should explicitly state whether convolution is performed independently on each entity column before pooling, or if transitions from all entities are processed jointly.
    *   The method for incorporating entity-specific features by creating new symbols (e.g., "PERSON-S") is glossed over. This could lead to a vocabulary explosion. The paper provides no details on the resulting vocabulary size or its impact on the embedding layer's complexity and trainability.

3.  **Unsatisfactory Treatment of a Key Baseline:** The authors' dismissal of the recent neural model by Li and Hovy (2014) is scientifically inadequate. Stating that the results were "very disappointing" and they "were not sure what could go wrong" is not a valid reason to exclude a key competitor from a comparative evaluation. For a paper claiming state-of-the-art, a more rigorous effort is expected, such as attempting hyperparameter tuning on the development set or providing a reasoned analysis for the model's failure. As it stands, the SOTA claims are weakened by the absence of this important comparison.

4.  **Lack of Professionalism in Presentation:** The paper suffers from numerous formatting and presentation errors that detract from its quality. The reference section is improperly formatted, appearing as a raw dump from a bibliography management tool, complete with extraneous characters like `\.,?` and BibTeX commands. In the introduction, Figure 2 (the proposed neural model) is incorrectly cited as an illustration of the standard entity grid model. While minor individually, the sheer number of such issues suggests a lack of care and polish, which unfortunately reduces confidence in the rigor of the work as a whole.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a detailed clarification for the results in Table 3? Specifically, why are the accuracies for the Discrimination and Insertion tasks identical for all models? Please also define the "F1" metric presented in this table.
2.  Regarding the convolution layer (Equation 2), is the bias term shared for a given filter (i.e., `b` instead of `bt`)? Can you also clarify the precise mechanics of the convolution and pooling stages: are they applied per entity column, with the results aggregated afterward, or in some other fashion?
3.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) baseline, can you provide more detail on your attempts to run it? Did you perform any hyperparameter tuning on your development set, or did you use the exact settings from their paper? Could you offer a more concrete hypothesis for its poor performance on the WSJ dataset?
4.  Footnote 9 claims the extended grid model (E&C) could not be trained on the pairwise summarization dataset. However, the E&C model uses an SVM preference ranker, which is a pairwise learner. Could you explain this apparent contradiction?
