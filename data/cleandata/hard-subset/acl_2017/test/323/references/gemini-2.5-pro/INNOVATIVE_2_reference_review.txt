### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel neural architecture for modeling local text coherence. The model operates on the classic entity grid representation, but replaces the traditional feature engineering approach (calculating transition probabilities) with a deep learning framework. Specifically, it learns distributed representations for grammatical roles and entity features, and then applies a convolutional neural network (CNN) over the entity transition sequences (the columns of the grid) to learn salient patterns indicative of coherence. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel and elegant "neuralization" of the entity grid model.** This is the core conceptual advance. By applying a CNN directly to the entity grid, the authors provide a principled way to overcome the "curse of dimensionality" that has historically limited grid-based models to very short entity transitions. This allows the model to capture longer-range dependencies in a robust, data-driven manner.
2.  **An end-to-end learning framework for coherence.** The model moves beyond the task-agnostic feature extraction of previous work. By training the entire architecture, from embeddings to the final scoring layer, on a ranking task, it can learn representations of entity transitions that are specifically optimized for judging coherence.
3.  **State-of-the-art empirical results.** The proposed model significantly outperforms strong entity-grid baselines and other competing methods on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Boldness:** This paper represents a significant and welcome departure from the incremental improvements that have characterized work on entity-grid models. Instead of engineering a few more features or tweaking the learning algorithm, the authors have fundamentally re-imagined how to extract signals from the grid structure. The application of a convolutional architecture to model location-invariant entity transitions is an elegant, insightful, and powerful idea. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-impact research that moves the field forward.

2.  **Directly Addresses Core Limitations of Prior Work:** The motivation for the neural architecture is exceptionally clear and well-grounded. It directly targets the two most significant weaknesses of the entity grid: the inability to model long transitions due to data sparsity, and the decoupling of feature extraction from the final task. The proposed model elegantly solves both problems through distributed representations and end-to-end training, respectively.

3.  **Impressive Empirical Performance and Generality:** The model doesn't just present a new idea; it demonstrates its superiority conclusively. Achieving consistent, statistically significant gains of up to 4% over strong, well-established baselines across three distinct tasks is a major achievement. The fact that the "Extended Grid-CNN" outperforms its non-neural counterpart using only three simple features (compared to the baseline's nine, some from external corpora) is a powerful testament to the efficacy of learned representations.

4.  **Simplicity and Potential for Extension:** Despite its strong performance, the architecture is conceptually clean and straightforward. This simplicity is a strength, making the work easy to understand, reproduce (code is provided), and build upon. The framework naturally allows for incorporating other sources of information, as the authors suggest in their conclusion, without the exponential feature growth that plagues traditional models.

### Weaknesses

While my overall assessment is extremely positive, there are areas where the paper could be strengthened. I view these as minor points that do not detract from the core contribution, but are worth mentioning.

1.  **Lack of Interpretability and Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows that the model works, but offers little insight into *why* it works. The convolutional filters are learning to detect salient transition patterns, but what are these patterns? Are they rediscovering linguistically-motivated principles from Centering Theory (e.g., subject-chaining), or are they capturing more complex, non-intuitive statistical regularities? Even a simple analysis or visualization of the most highly-activated filters or n-grams would have added significant value and connected this powerful neural model back to the linguistic theories that inspired the entity grid in the first place.

2.  **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough ablation study to tease apart the sources of its performance gains. For instance, how much of the improvement comes from the distributed representations alone versus the end-to-end training? An experiment with pre-trained, static embeddings could have isolated the effect of task-specific tuning. Similarly, while the authors report using a larger transition window (k=5-6) than previous work, a graph showing performance as a function of the filter window size would have provided stronger empirical evidence for their claim about capturing longer-range dependencies.

3.  **Handling of the Competing Neural Model:** The authors note that they were unable to get good performance from the Li and Hovy (2014) model and excluded it from the results table. While this is understandable from a practical standpoint, it leaves a gap in the comparison to related neural approaches. A more detailed discussion of the potential reasons for the model's failure on this dataset would have strengthened the paper's positioning.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide any analysis of what the learned convolutional filters are capturing? For example, by finding the input transition patterns (e.g., 'S-S-O-X-X') that maximally activate certain filters, can you offer any insight into whether the model is learning linguistically interpretable coherence strategies?

2.  The paper argues that end-to-end training to learn task-specific features is a key advantage. Could you run an experiment to quantify this advantage? Specifically, what is the performance if you only pre-train the embedding layer (e.g., using an autoencoder on the grid data) and keep it fixed during the final pairwise ranking training? This would help disentangle the benefit of the distributed representation itself from the benefit of task-specific fine-tuning.
