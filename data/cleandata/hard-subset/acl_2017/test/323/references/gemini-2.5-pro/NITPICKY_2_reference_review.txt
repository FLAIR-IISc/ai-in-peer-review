**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) model for assessing local text coherence. The model operates on an entity grid representation of a text, similar to the classic model of Barzilay and Lapata (2008). However, instead of using discrete features based on short transition probabilities, this work uses learned embeddings for grammatical roles and applies a convolutional architecture to capture longer-range entity transition patterns. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their approach on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and summary coherence rating tasks, reporting state-of-the-art results that significantly outperform previous entity-grid-based models.

**Contributions**

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel application of a standard CNN architecture to the entity grid representation for local coherence modeling. This provides an effective method for learning features automatically from the grid, avoiding the data sparsity issues that limit traditional models to short-range transitions.
2.  Strong empirical results demonstrating that this neural approach surpasses well-established non-neural counterparts on three standard coherence evaluation benchmarks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models, namely the curse of dimensionality from discrete representations and the task-agnostic nature of feature extraction. The proposed neural architecture is a principled and well-motivated solution to these specific problems, replacing hand-crafted, sparse features with learned, dense representations.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines, including the extended entity grid model of Elsner and Charniak (2011), across three distinct evaluation tasks. The reported gains of ~4% on discrimination and summary rating are substantial, and the model sets a new state-of-the-art on these benchmarks.
3.  **Sound Experimental Design:** The authors use standard, well-understood evaluation tasks and datasets (primarily the WSJ corpus), which allows for direct comparison with prior work. The use of a development set for hyperparameter tuning, reporting the average of multiple runs, and performing significance testing are all marks of a methodologically sound evaluation.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors state that they have released their code, which is a significant plus for reproducibility and will allow other researchers to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel idea and strong results, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its technical description, experimental comparison, and overall presentation.

1.  **Serious Presentation and Formatting Deficiencies:** The paper exhibits a concerning lack of attention to detail. There are multiple typographical errors (e.g., "loosing" instead of "losing" in Sections 2.1 and 7). More critically, the entire References section is malformed, appearing to be a raw, un-inspected BibTeX output with numerous extraneous characters (e.g., "Barzilay and Lapata.,?") and improperly formatted entries (e.g., the citation for Tieleman and Hinton, 2012). This level of carelessness in a submission to a top-tier conference is unacceptable and severely detracts from the paper's professionalism and credibility.
2.  **Insufficient and Unscientific Comparison to Prior Art:** The authors explicitly dismiss a highly relevant and recent neural baseline, the model of Li and Hovy (2014). They state that the results were "very disappointing" and "we were not sure what could go wrong, therefore, we excluded it". This is not a valid justification. A rigorous scientific paper must engage with competing methods fairly. The authors should have either reported the poor numbers and provided a thorough analysis of why the model might have failed on this specific dataset, or detailed their efforts to properly tune and run the baseline. Simply omitting a key competitor because of poor initial results is a major methodological flaw.
3.  **Ambiguity and Potential Errors in Technical Details:**
    *   The convolution operation in Equation (2) includes a bias term `bt` indexed by the position `t`. In a standard CNN, the bias is a scalar parameter associated with a filter `w`, not the location of its application. This is either a significant typo or a non-standard and unexplained deviation from the conventional CNN formulation.
    *   The mechanism for incorporating entity-specific features (Section 3.1) involves creating new atomic symbols (e.g., "PERSON-S"). This could lead to a combinatorial explosion in the vocabulary size `|V|`. The paper fails to discuss the consequences of this design choice, such as the final vocabulary size or potential data sparsity issues for the embedding layer, which undermines the initial motivation of avoiding sparsity.
4.  **Lack of Clarity in Results Presentation:** Table 3 is exceptionally confusing. The column headers ("Discr.", "Ins.", "Acc", "F1") are not explained. It is unclear what "Acc" and "F1" refer to. Furthermore, the accuracy scores for the Discrimination and Insertion tasks are identical or nearly identical for most models. Given that these are different tasks with different evaluation metrics (one is binary classification accuracy, the other is average positional accuracy), this similarity is highly suspect and requires a clear explanation, which is not provided. This makes the results in this crucial table difficult to interpret and trust.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Can you confirm the formulation of the bias term in Equation 2? Is it a standard scalar bias per filter, or is it indeed position-dependent as the notation `bt` suggests? If the latter, please provide a justification for this choice.
2.  For your Extended Grid-CNN model, what was the resulting vocabulary size `|V|` after combining grammatical roles with the three entity-specific features? Did you encounter any challenges in learning meaningful embeddings for this larger and presumably sparser vocabulary?
3.  Please clarify the header and content of Table 3. What do the columns "Acc" and "F1" represent? Can you explain the surprising similarity between the results for the Discrimination and Insertion tasks?
4.  Could you elaborate on your efforts to reproduce the results of Li and Hovy (2014)? Failing to include a comparison with this key baseline is a significant omission. Can you provide any hypothesis for its poor performance in your experimental setting?
