**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) model for assessing local text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation of Barzilay and Lapata (2008). The model processes an entity grid by first mapping grammatical roles and entity features to distributed embeddings. It then applies 1D convolutions over each entity's transition sequence (the columns of the grid) to learn abstract feature representations of state changes. A max-pooling layer selects the most salient features, which are fed into a final linear layer to produce a coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their approach on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and summary coherence rating tasks, reporting state-of-the-art results.

As I see them, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A successful application of a standard CNN architecture to the entity grid framework for coherence modeling. The work demonstrates that learning features directly from the grid representation in an end-to-end manner can outperform previous methods that rely on engineered, discrete features.
2.  Strong empirical evidence across three standard tasks showing that this neural approach surpasses well-regarded baselines, including the strong extended entity grid model of Elsner and Charniak (2011).

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents compelling results, achieving consistent and statistically significant improvements over several baselines on three different tasks. The reported absolute gains of ~4% on the discrimination task and >4% on summary coherence rating over strong non-neural counterparts are substantial and represent a meaningful advance.
2.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The work is motivated by clear and well-known limitations of traditional entity grid models, namely the sparsity issue ("curse of dimensionality") that prevents modeling long entity transitions and the task-agnostic nature of feature engineering. The proposed neural architecture directly addresses these limitations by using dense representations and end-to-end training.
3.  **Credible and Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors compare their model against strong, relevant baselines, crucially using the publicly available Brown coherence toolkit to reproduce the results for the Elsner & Charniak (2011) models. This ensures the baseline is not a strawman and makes the reported improvements highly credible. The use of standard datasets and evaluation metrics further grounds the work in the existing literature.
4.  **Efficiency of Feature Representation:** The extended version of the proposed model (Extended Grid-CNN) achieves its state-of-the-art performance using only three simple entity-specific features, whereas its strongest competitor (Extended Grid E&C) relies on nine features, some of which require external corpora. This suggests the proposed model is more effective at learning useful representations from a smaller feature set, which is a desirable property.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While effective, the core methodology involves applying a standard CNN architecture (embedding layer, convolutions, max-pooling) to a pre-existing representation (the entity grid). This "neuralization" of an established model, while a valid engineering contribution, lacks significant methodological novelty. The paper does not provide a compelling justification for the choice of a CNN over other neural sequence models (e.g., LSTMs) that are also well-suited for capturing dependencies in sequences like entity transitions.
2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Interpretation:** The paper claims superiority but offers little insight into *why* the model performs better. A central motivation is the model's ability to capture longer-range entity transitions (k > 3). However, this claim is not substantiated with analysis. There is no ablation study on the effect of the filter window size, which would be crucial for demonstrating that longer-range patterns are indeed being learned and are beneficial. Without this, the model remains a black box, and the source of its performance gains is unclear. Do the learned filters capture linguistically plausible transition patterns? This is left unexplored.
3.  **Unconvincing Dismissal of a Competing Neural Model:** The authors mention that the neural model of Li and Hovy (2014) performed extremely poorly in their experiments and was therefore excluded from the results table. Simply stating that "we were not sure what could go wrong" is insufficient for a rigorous scientific paper. This model represents a different and important direction in neural coherence modeling (operating on sentence vectors rather than entity grids). A more thorough investigation and discussion of this result are necessary to properly contextualize the current work and to fairly assess the state of the art in neural approaches to coherence. As it stands, the paper primarily demonstrates superiority over non-neural grid models, not over alternative neural architectures.
4.  **Incomplete Comparison of Extended Models:** The comparison between the Extended Grid-CNN (using 3 features) and the Extended Grid E&C (using 9 features) is not entirely direct due to the differing feature sets. While achieving better performance with fewer features is impressive, it leaves a question unanswered: how does the CNN architecture handle a larger, more complex set of entity-specific features? An experiment training the Extended Grid-CNN on all 9 features would provide a more complete and informative comparison of the two modeling paradigms (feature engineering vs. end-to-end learning).

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide an analysis of the model's sensitivity to the convolutional filter size (i.e., the length of the entity transition, `k`)? This would directly support your central motivation that modeling longer-range transitions is a key advantage of your approach.
2.  Can you provide more details regarding the failure of the Li and Hovy (2014) model on your dataset? This is a critical comparison point for understanding where your model fits within the landscape of neural coherence models. Was there a specific reason to believe the model is unsuited for the WSJ dataset, or could there have been an issue with the implementation or experimental setup?
3.  What is the justification for choosing a CNN architecture over a recurrent one (e.g., an LSTM or GRU) for modeling the entity transition sequences in the grid columns? Both are standard choices for sequence modeling, and a brief discussion of the trade-offs would strengthen the paper's methodological contribution.
