### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel neural architecture for modeling local text coherence. The model builds upon the classic entity grid representation (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008), which captures the transitions of grammatical roles (subject, object, other) for entities across sentences. The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid models: 1) the use of discrete features leads to data sparsity, preventing the modeling of long-range entity transitions, and 2) feature extraction is decoupled from the downstream task. To address this, they propose a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that operates directly on the entity grid. Grammatical roles are first mapped to dense embeddings, and a convolutional layer with max-pooling is then used to learn task-specific features from entity transition patterns. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective neural adaptation of the entity grid model for coherence.** The paper presents a well-motivated method for applying a standard CNN architecture to the structured entity grid representation, successfully overcoming the sparsity and feature engineering limitations of previous grid-based approaches. This allows the model to learn from longer entity transition patterns in a task-specific, end-to-end manner.
2.  **A strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation.** The proposed model is tested on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) using a large, standard dataset (WSJ). The experiments show consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong non-neural baselines, achieving new state-of-the-art results on all tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Model.** The paper provides a clear and compelling motivation for moving beyond traditional entity grid models. The proposed solution is elegant: it retains the theoretically grounded entity grid structure while leveraging the power of neural networks to learn dense, task-specific representations. Applying a CNN to the columns of the grid (representing entity transitions over time) is a very natural and clever way to capture local patterns of coherence of varying lengths without suffering from the curse of dimensionality.
2.  **Strong and Reproducible Empirical Results.** The experiments are thorough and the results are impressive. The proposed models (`Grid-CNN` and `Extended Grid-CNN`) consistently and significantly outperform their direct non-neural counterparts (`Grid-all nouns` and `Extended Grid`) across all tasks. For instance, the absolute gain of nearly 4% on the discrimination task and over 4% on summary coherence rating demonstrates the clear superiority of the neural approach. The authors' decision to use the publicly available Brown Coherence Toolkit for baselines makes their comparisons fair and their results easily verifiable. The release of their own code further enhances the paper's contribution to the community.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The background on the entity grid model and its limitations is presented clearly, setting the stage perfectly for the introduction of the neural model. Figure 2 provides an excellent visualization of the model architecture and the pairwise training setup, greatly aiding comprehension. The overall structure and narrative are logical and persuasive.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on a Brittle Pre-processing Pipeline.** The neural model, while trained end-to-end on the coherence task, is not end-to-end in the broader sense. It still fundamentally relies on a pre-computed entity grid, which requires a complex and error-prone pipeline involving parsing, entity identification, and coreference resolution. Errors from these upstream components will inevitably propagate and affect the model's performance. The paper does not include any analysis of the model's robustness or sensitivity to the quality of this pipeline, which is a key practical consideration. This dependency also means the model cannot learn to extract coherence cues beyond those explicitly captured by the entity grid structure.
2.  **Limited Comparison with Other Neural Approaches.** The paper compares its model primarily to non-neural entity grid baselines from the early 2010s. While this comparison is essential for showing the benefits of "neuralizing" the grid, the paper's claim to state-of-the-art is weakened by the limited engagement with other neural coherence models. The authors mention dismissing the contemporary model of Li and Hovy (2014) due to poor performance on their dataset, but a deeper discussion of potential reasons would have been valuable. A comparison against models that operate directly on sentence representations without an explicit entity grid would provide a more complete picture of where this work stands in the modern landscape of coherence modeling.
3.  **Novelty is in Application, Not Architecture.** The core neural architecture (embedding layer -> convolution -> max-pooling -> output layer) is a standard design for text classification, popularized by work such as Kim (2014), which the authors cite. The main novelty here lies in the creative *application* of this architecture to the entity grid representation, not in the development of a new neural architecture itself. The paper could be strengthened by positioning its contribution more precisely in this regard, emphasizing the novel problem formulation that allows a standard CNN to effectively model a classic linguistic structure.

### Questions to Authors

1.  You note that your model outperforms its non-neural counterpart partly because it can capture longer-range entity transitions (k > 3). Your hyperparameter tuning found optimal filter window sizes of k=5 and k=6. Could you provide any analysis or visualization of the learned filters to give insight into what kinds of long-range patterns (e.g., 'S-S-O-X-X') the model finds indicative of coherence? This would provide concrete evidence for your claim.
2.  Could you elaborate on the poor performance of the Li and Hovy (2014) model on your dataset? Given that it is also a neural approach, understanding why it failed where yours succeeded would be very insightful for the community. Do you believe the issue is architectural (CNN on grid vs. RNN on sentences), related to the dataset characteristics, or something else?
3.  Your model is dependent on the quality of the upstream NLP pipeline used to generate the entity grid. How do you expect performance would be affected by using a different parser or coreference resolver? Have you considered the possibility of making the grid generation process itself differentiable or learning it jointly with the coherence scoring?
