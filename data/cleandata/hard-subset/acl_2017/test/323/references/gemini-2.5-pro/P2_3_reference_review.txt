### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural network model for assessing local text coherence, building directly upon the well-established entity-grid representation. The authors identify two key limitations of traditional entity-grid models: 1) the "curse of dimensionality" from using discrete features, which restricts the length of entity transitions that can be modeled, and 2) the decoupling of feature extraction from the end task.

To address this, the proposed model first represents the entity grid's grammatical roles ({S, O, X, -}) as learned embeddings. It then applies a 1D convolutional neural network (CNN) along each entity's transition column, followed by a max-pooling layer, to learn abstract, task-specific features from these transitions. The final score is produced by a linear layer. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and demonstrate state-of-the-art results, significantly outperforming previous entity-grid models.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective application of a convolutional architecture to the entity-grid representation.** This is a well-motivated "neuralization" of a classic, successful NLP model, which directly addresses the sparsity and fixed-context limitations of its predecessors by leveraging distributed representations and convolutional filters to capture longer-range transition patterns.
2.  **Strong empirical validation demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on multiple standard benchmarks (WSJ and DUC'03) against strong baselines. The consistent and statistically significant improvements across all tasks provide compelling evidence for the model's effectiveness.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating its approach. It clearly articulates the weaknesses of prior entity-grid models and proposes a neural architecture that is a natural and elegant solution. Using a CNN to slide over entity transition sequences is a very intuitive way to model local patterns of arbitrary length, neatly overcoming the feature sparsity problem that plagued earlier n-gram-style approaches on the grid. The end-to-end training further allows the model to learn representations that are optimized for the coherence task itself.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental setup is rigorous and follows best practices. The authors evaluate on multiple, standard tasks and datasets, making their results directly comparable to a large body of previous work. They compare against relevant and strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art non-neural models from Elsner & Charniak (2011). The reported gains are substantial and statistically significant, leaving little doubt about the superiority of the proposed method.

3.  **High Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The model architecture is explained clearly, aided by the helpful diagram in Figure 2. The authors provide sufficient detail about their experimental setup, including dataset statistics (Table 1) and hyperparameter settings (Table 2), which instills confidence in the results. The statement that the code has been released with the submission is a major strength, greatly facilitating reproducibility and future work in this area.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Neural Architecture Itself:** While the *application* of the CNN to the entity grid is novel, the core architecture (embedding layer -> convolution -> pooling -> fully-connected layer) is a standard pattern used widely in NLP since at least Kim (2014) for sentence classification. The paper is transparent about this, citing relevant work, but it means the primary technical contribution lies in the framing of the problem rather than in the development of a new neural network component.

2.  **Incomplete Comparison to Other Neural Models:** The paper mentions the contemporary neural model of Li and Hovy (2014) but dismisses it with a brief note about poor replication performance ("We were not sure what could go wrong"). This is a significant weakness. A more in-depth discussion and comparison are needed. Is the model of Li & Hovy fundamentally less suited to the WSJ dataset? Do the two models capture different aspects of coherence (sentence-level semantics vs. entity-level transitions)? Without a proper comparison to other relevant neural approaches, the claim to "state-of-the-art" is weakened, as it primarily establishes dominance over non-neural predecessors.

3.  **Simplistic Integration of Entity-Specific Features:** The proposed method for incorporating entity-specific features—by creating composite symbols like "PERSON-S"—is simple but lacks scalability and expressive power. This approach leads to a combinatorial explosion in the vocabulary size as more features are added. A more standard and flexible approach would be to learn separate embeddings for each feature type and concatenate them with the grammatical role embedding before feeding them to the convolutional layer. This would allow the model to learn interactions between features and might lead to better performance and generalization.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more insight into the failure to replicate the Li & Hovy (2014) results? Given that your model operates on a symbolic entity grid while theirs operates on distributed sentence representations, could you hypothesize why your entity-centric approach might be more robust or better suited for these particular tasks and datasets? A more detailed architectural comparison would strengthen your paper's positioning.

2.  You integrate entity-specific features by expanding the vocabulary (e.g., creating a token for "PERSON-S"). Did you consider alternative methods, such as concatenating the embedding of the grammatical role ('S') with embeddings for the entity features ('PERSON', 'salient', etc.)? This approach might scale better to a larger number of features and allow the model to learn finer-grained interactions. What was the reasoning behind choosing the composite-symbol method?
