### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel neural architecture for modeling local text coherence, building directly upon the well-established entity grid representation. The core idea is to replace the traditional, discrete feature engineering approach of entity grid models with a learned, end-to-end system. The authors represent the grammatical roles in the grid (e.g., Subject, Object) as continuous vectors (embeddings) and apply a convolutional neural network (CNN) over the transition sequences for each entity. This allows the model to learn salient, long-range transition patterns automatically. The model is trained using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and demonstrate that their neural models significantly outperform strong non-neural entity-grid baselines, achieving new state-of-the-art results.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Neural Architecture for a Classic Discourse Representation:** The primary contribution is the conceptual leap of applying a convolutional neural network directly to the entity grid. This is an elegant and powerful method for capturing long-range entity transitions, which was a major limitation of previous grid models due to the curse of dimensionality. It reframes the problem from counting discrete transition patterns to learning distributed representations of them.
2.  **An End-to-End, Task-Specific Coherence Model:** The paper introduces a fully differentiable model that learns coherence-related features automatically via backpropagation. This is a significant departure from prior work where feature extraction from the grid was a separate, task-agnostic step. This allows the model to learn features that are optimized for the specific coherence task at hand.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper's core idea is genuinely novel and compelling. Instead of abandoning the theoretically-grounded entity grid, the authors "neuralize" it in a non-trivial way. Using a CNN to scan entity transition columns is a clever and well-motivated architectural choice that directly addresses the key weakness of the original model (modeling long transitions). This represents a bold and promising new direction for entity-based coherence modeling, moving beyond incremental feature engineering.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results across three distinct and standard evaluation tasks. The gains over the strong non-neural counterparts from the Brown coherence toolkit are consistent and statistically significant. This provides convincing evidence that the proposed neural approach is not just conceptually interesting but also practically effective.
3.  **Principled and Well-Justified Method:** The approach is sound and builds logically on a large body of previous work. The authors clearly identify the limitations of existing entity grid models and design their neural architecture specifically to overcome them. The use of convolutions for location-invariant pattern detection and max-pooling to identify the most salient features is well-justified and provides an intuitive explanation for why the model works.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors state they have released their code, which is a commendable practice that strengthens the submission and will benefit the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incomplete Comparison in the Extended Model:** The Extended Grid-CNN is compared against the Extended Grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011), but only incorporates three of the nine entity-specific features used by the baseline. While the neural model still wins, this is not an apples-to-apples comparison and slightly weakens the claim of being able to incorporate "arbitrary entity-specific features". It raises the question of whether the performance gains would hold, or perhaps even increase, if all features were included, or if there were technical challenges in doing so.
2.  **Missing Comparison to Contemporary Neural Models:** The authors report their inability to reproduce the results for the Li & Hovy (2014) model, which represents a different and important strand of neural coherence modeling (based on sentence representations). While difficulties in reproducing prior work are understandable, its absence leaves a notable gap in the experimental landscape. The current experiments convincingly show the proposed model is better than *non-neural grid models*, but its positioning relative to other *neural* approaches is less clear.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Architectural Alternatives:** The choice of a CNN is well-motivated, but it is not the only possible architecture for processing the sequential data in the grid's columns. A brief discussion or comparison with alternatives, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), could have strengthened the paper by further justifying the specific architectural choices made.

Despite these weaknesses, I view them as minor limitations on what is otherwise a very strong and innovative paper. The core contribution is significant enough to stand on its own, and these points can be addressed in future work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more insight into the decision to use only a subset of the entity-specific features from Elsner & Charniak (2011) in your Extended Grid-CNN? Was this for simplicity, or did you encounter challenges or diminishing returns when trying to incorporate more complex features into the vocabulary (e.g., `PERSON-FREQUENT-PROPER-S`)?
2.  The paper frames the coherence scoring problem as detecting salient local transition patterns, for which a CNN is a natural fit. Did you consider or perform any preliminary experiments with alternative architectures for processing the entity transition sequences, such as an LSTM or GRU, which might be more suited to modeling stateful dependencies? I would be interested in your thoughts on the trade-offs between these architectural choices for this specific problem.
