### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) model for assessing local text coherence, built upon the entity grid representation. The authors identify two key limitations of prior entity grid models: their reliance on discrete, sparse feature representations which restricts the modeling of long-range entity transitions, and the decoupling of feature extraction from the final task. To address this, the proposed model learns distributed representations (embeddings) for grammatical roles and entity features. A 1D convolutional layer is then applied to the transition sequence of each entity (i.e., each column of the grid), enabling the model to learn abstract features from longer transition patterns. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective, allowing for the learning of task-specific features. The authors evaluate their model on three standard tasks—discrimination, sentence insertion, and summary coherence rating—demonstrating state-of-the-art performance and significant improvements over strong non-neural entity grid baselines.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel neural architecture for modeling the entity grid:** The application of a 1D CNN over embedded entity transition sequences is an effective and well-motivated method for overcoming the feature sparsity and limited context window issues inherent in previous grid-based models.
2.  **Strong and comprehensive empirical validation:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on multiple standard tasks and datasets. By showing consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong, widely-used baselines, the work establishes a new state-of-the-art for entity grid-based coherence modeling.
3.  **A simple yet effective method for integrating discrete entity-specific features into the neural framework:** The approach of creating combined symbols for role and feature types (e.g., PERSON-S) and learning embeddings for them is shown to be effective, providing a clear recipe for extending the base neural model.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Technically Sound Method:** The paper provides a clear and compelling motivation by identifying concrete weaknesses in a popular and successful line of prior work. The proposed neural architecture is a direct and logical solution to these identified problems. Using embeddings to create dense representations and a CNN to capture local, position-invariant patterns are well-established and appropriate techniques for this problem structure. The end-to-end training via a pairwise ranking loss is a standard and suitable choice for the task. The model is explained clearly and logically.
2.  **Robust and Significant Experimental Results:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering three distinct tasks with varying levels of difficulty. The choice of baselines is excellent, including the strong extended entity grid model of Elsner and Charniak (2011), which represents the previous state of the art in this paradigm. The reported gains are substantial and statistically significant across all tasks (e.g., an absolute improvement of ~4% on discrimination and >4% on summary rating). This provides strong evidence for the superiority of the proposed neural approach over its non-neural counterparts.
3.  **High Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is clearly depicted in Figure 2, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to allow for replication. The authors' decision to use a publicly available toolkit to generate baseline results and to release their own code significantly strengthens the paper's contribution to the community and enhances the credibility of the results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with other Neural Coherence Models:** The paper’s main comparison is between the proposed neural grid model and non-neural grid models. While it mentions another prominent neural coherence model (Li and Hovy, 2014), it is excluded from the main results table due to poor performance in the authors' reimplementation. This is a missed opportunity for a broader contextualization of the work. A more detailed analysis of why that model might have failed (e.g., differences in architectural assumptions, data suitability) would have been more insightful than a brief dismissal. As it stands, the paper primarily demonstrates the benefit of neuralizing the entity grid, but makes a less compelling case for its superiority over alternative neural approaches to coherence that do not use the grid.
2.  **Lack of Analysis of Learned Representations:** The paper claims that the model learns "high-level features" and that convolutional filters can detect specific transition patterns. However, no qualitative analysis is provided to support these claims or to offer insight into what the model has learned. For example, an analysis of the learned filters or an error analysis showing specific examples where the ability to model longer transitions (k > 3) leads to a correct prediction would have substantially strengthened the paper's central argument. Without such analysis, the model's improvements remain somewhat of a "black box" phenomenon.
3.  **Uneven Comparison for Entity-Specific Features:** The Extended Grid-CNN model is compared against the Extended Grid (E&C) model, but the neural version uses only three of the nine features employed by the non-neural baseline. While the neural model's superior performance is still impressive given this handicap, the comparison is not direct. It leaves open the question of how the proposed feature integration method (concatenating roles and feature values into single symbols) would scale with a larger set of features. A potential vocabulary explosion could introduce new sparsity issues, a point that is not addressed. This slightly weakens the claim that the model can incorporate "arbitrary" entity-specific features.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more detail on the experimental run of the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Given that their approach is based on sentence representations rather than entity transitions, could its poor performance be attributed to the nature of the WSJ dataset, and if so, what characteristics of the data might favor an entity-centric model?
2.  The "Extended Grid-CNN" uses a subset of the features from the non-neural "Extended Grid (E&C)" baseline. Have you experimented with incorporating more of the nine features into your neural model? How does your feature encoding scheme (e.g., creating symbols like `PERSON-S`) scale as the number of feature types and values increases, and does this re-introduce data sparsity challenges for the embedding layer?
3.  The current model applies a 1D convolution to each entity column independently, preserving the assumption of entity independence from prior work. Have you considered alternative architectures, such as a 2D convolution over the full embedded grid, that might be able to capture interactions *between* different entities across adjacent sentences?
