### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for assessing local text coherence. The model is a "neuralization" of the well-established entity grid model. It addresses limitations of traditional entity grid models, such as their reliance on discrete features and their inability to model long-range entity transitions due to data sparsity. The proposed architecture represents the grammatical roles in an entity grid using learned, distributed embeddings. A convolutional neural network (CNN) is then applied over the columns of this embedded grid (i.e., over the transition sequences for each entity) to learn high-level, task-specific features. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their model on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and demonstrate that it achieves new state-of-the-art results, outperforming its non-neural counterparts.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Application of CNNs for Coherence Modeling:** The primary contribution is the elegant and effective framing of the entity grid as a structure amenable to convolutional processing. By treating entity transition sequences as input channels, the model can learn complex, long-range transition patterns from data, directly addressing a key limitation of previous grid-based methods.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results Demonstrating the Power of End-to-End Learning:** The paper provides compelling evidence that learning features automatically within a neural architecture can outperform models based on extensive, hand-crafted features. The proposed model surpasses the strong Elsner & Charniak (2011) baseline, even when using a smaller set of entity-specific features, highlighting the effectiveness of the distributed representations and end-to-end training.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work by clearly identifying the weaknesses of existing entity grid models (curse of dimensionality, task-agnostic feature extraction). The proposed CNN-based architecture is a logical and elegant solution to these specific problems. Using embeddings for grammatical roles and a CNN to capture transition patterns is a natural and well-justified modeling choice.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and follows established best practices. The authors test their model on three different standard tasks, using widely accepted datasets (WSJ, DUC'03). They compare against strong and appropriate baselines, including the state-of-the-art entity grid models from the publicly available Brown coherence toolkit. The experimental setup is sound, including the use of a development set for hyperparameter tuning, reporting average results over multiple runs, and performing statistical significance testing.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The model achieves significant and consistent improvements over strong baselines across all tasks. The absolute gains of ~4% on discrimination and >4% on summary rating are substantial in this well-studied area. The fact that the extended neural model outperforms its much more feature-rich non-neural counterpart is particularly impressive and underscores the paper's central claims.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is clearly explained, aided by the helpful Figure 2. The authors provide sufficient detail on their experimental settings and hyperparameter choices. Crucially, they state that they have released their code, which is a major strength that enables verification and facilitates future work in the area.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Dismissal of a Key Neural Baseline:** The authors mention experimenting with the distributed sentence model of Li and Hovy (2014), a highly relevant neural competitor, but exclude it from the main results due to poor performance on their dataset ("accuracy of only 19.34%"). Simply stating "we were not sure what could go wrong" is insufficient. This feels like a significant gap in the comparison. A more thorough attempt to debug the issue or a more detailed analysis of why that model might fail on the WSJ dataset would be necessary to make a convincing case that it is not a competitive baseline. As it stands, it looks like a potentially strong competitor was omitted due to implementation challenges.
2.  **Slightly Uneven Comparison of Extended Models:** The proposed "Extended Grid-CNN" is compared to the "Extended Grid (E&C)" model. However, the neural model uses only three simple entity-specific features, whereas the E&C baseline uses nine, including four computed from external corpora. While it is impressive that the neural model wins with fewer features, this is not a direct, apples-to-apples comparison of the underlying architectures. It leaves open the question of how much more performance could be gained if the neural model were equipped with the full feature set. This framing slightly conflates the benefit of the neural architecture with the benefit of feature parsimony.
3.  **The "End-to-End" Claim is Qualified:** The paper claims the model is trained "end-to-end," but this is only true starting from the pre-computed entity grid. The model still relies on a complex upstream pipeline involving parsing and coreference resolution to generate the grid in the first place. While this is a common shorthand in NLP, it's worth noting that the feature engineering has been shifted to the grid creation step rather than being completely eliminated.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model: Could you please elaborate on your efforts to reproduce their results on the WSJ dataset? Is it possible that their model, which operates on sentence representations, is fundamentally less suited to the WSJ news domain compared to the corpora they originally used? A more detailed discussion would strengthen your paper's positioning relative to other neural approaches.
2.  Regarding the comparison of extended models: What was the motivation for using only three of the nine entity-specific features from Elsner & Charniak (2011)? Have you considered experiments incorporating the full feature set into your Extended Grid-CNN to provide a more direct comparison and potentially push performance even higher?
3.  The CNN architecture is excellent at finding local, position-invariant patterns. Have you considered an alternative like an RNN/LSTM operating over the entity transition sequences (the grid columns) to model stateful, sequential dependencies? It would be interesting to know your thoughts on the relative merits of these different architectures for this specific task.
4.  A major benefit of neural models is their potential for interpretation. Have you performed any analysis of the learned convolutional filters? For example, can you identify filters that correspond to well-known coherent transition patterns from Centering Theory (e.g., a subject-to-subject transition, or "CONTINUE")? This would provide valuable insight into *what* the model is learning and connect it back to the linguistic theories that inspired this line of work.
