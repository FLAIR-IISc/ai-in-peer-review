### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture for modeling local text coherence, designed as a neural successor to the popular entity grid model. The core idea is to represent an entity grid not with discrete transition counts, but with distributed embeddings for grammatical roles. A CNN is then applied over the columns of this embedded grid (i.e., over each entity's transitions through the text) to learn salient transition patterns. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective to score a coherent document higher than an incoherent one. The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—demonstrating significant improvements over strong non-neural entity grid baselines and achieving state-of-the-art results.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Effective Neural Architecture for the Entity Grid:** The primary contribution is the successful application of a CNN to the entity grid representation. This is an elegant solution to the "curse of dimensionality" that limited previous grid models, allowing the model to learn longer-range transition patterns over continuous representations without feature sparsity issues.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation on Multiple Coherence Tasks:** The paper presents a robust evaluation across three distinct and well-established tasks. The consistent and statistically significant gains over strong baselines, particularly the extended entity grid model from the widely used Brown coherence toolkit, provide compelling evidence for the model's effectiveness.
3.  **An End-to-End Framework for Learning Coherence Features:** By training the model with a pairwise ranking loss, the architecture learns task-specific representations of entity transitions, moving away from the task-agnostic feature engineering of prior work.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Model Design:** The proposed architecture is a natural and well-justified neural counterpart to the classic entity grid. Treating each entity's role transitions as a sequence and applying a 1D convolution is an intuitive way to capture local transition patterns. The use of embeddings and end-to-end training directly addresses the key limitations of previous discrete, count-based models, namely feature sparsity and task-agnostic feature extraction. The model is explained clearly, and Figure 2 is particularly helpful for understanding the architecture and training process.

2.  **Convincing and Comprehensive Experimental Results:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on standard datasets (WSJ and DUC'03) and tasks. The results in Table 3 show clear and significant improvements over strong baselines like the Elsner & Charniak (2011) models. An absolute gain of nearly 4% on the discrimination task and 2.5% on the more challenging insertion task is impressive. The success on the summary coherence rating task further demonstrates the model's generalizability.

3.  **Good Scientific Practice and Reproducibility:** The authors compare against appropriate and strong baselines, using publicly available toolkits to generate results, which makes the comparisons fair and credible. They perform hyper-parameter tuning on a dedicated development set, report average results over multiple runs to account for randomness, and use significance testing. The stated intention to release their code is a major strength that will benefit the community.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparison with Contemporary Neural Models:** The paper's primary comparisons are against non-neural entity grid models. The only other neural model mentioned, Li and Hovy (2014), is dismissed after achieving poor results on the WSJ dataset. This is a significant weakness. It is crucial to understand *why* this other model failed—was it a dataset mismatch, an implementation issue, or a fundamental flaw in that approach for this task? Simply excluding a key contemporary neural baseline weakens the claim of achieving state-of-the-art results in a broader sense and leaves the reader wondering how this model compares to other neural approaches beyond the entity grid paradigm.

2.  **Lack of Model Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper demonstrates *that* the model works well but provides little insight into *why*. The "Why it works" section is brief and high-level. A key advantage of the original entity grid was its interpretability (e.g., we can see that S->S transitions are frequent). It would have greatly strengthened the paper to include an analysis of the learned model components. For example, what kinds of transition patterns do the convolutional filters learn to detect? Is it possible to visualize the learned embeddings for the grammatical roles (S, O, X, -) to see if they capture meaningful relationships? Without this analysis, the model remains a black box.

3.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison for the Extended Model:** The "Extended Grid-CNN" is compared against the "Extended Grid (E&C)" baseline. However, the authors state their neural model uses only three simple entity-specific features, whereas the E&C baseline uses nine, including some derived from external corpora. While the neural model's victory is still impressive, the comparison is not entirely apples-to-apples. It would be more conclusive if the authors had either (a) incorporated all nine features into their model to show its full potential or (b) run a baseline E&C model trained on only the same three features used by the CNN.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the issues encountered with the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Given that it is a prominent neural coherence model from the same time period, a more thorough investigation or discussion of why it performed poorly on the WSJ dataset seems necessary to properly position your work relative to other neural approaches. Did you reach out to the original authors or investigate potential reasons for the discrepancy in performance?

2.  Have you conducted any qualitative analysis of the learned filters in your CNN? It would be very insightful to understand what specific n-gram transition patterns (e.g., 'S-S-O', 'O-X-S', etc.) the model learns are indicative of coherence. This would help bridge the gap between the performance of your neural model and the interpretability of traditional grid models.

3.  Regarding the comparison between the Extended Grid-CNN and the Extended Grid (E&C), why was only a subset of the entity-specific features used in your neural model? Does your architecture present challenges for incorporating more complex features, especially those from external resources?
