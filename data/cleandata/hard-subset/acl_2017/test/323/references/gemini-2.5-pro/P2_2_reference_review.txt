### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural network-based model for local text coherence that modernizes the classic entity grid model. The authors identify two key limitations of existing entity grid approaches: the curse of dimensionality, which prevents modeling long entity transitions, and the decoupling of feature extraction from the end task. The proposed model, "Grid-CNN," addresses these by applying a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) directly to the entity grid. The grid's grammatical roles are first converted into learned embeddings. A convolutional layer then acts as a feature detector for transition patterns across sentences for each entity, followed by a max-pooling layer to identify the most salient patterns. The entire model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective "neuralization" of the entity grid model.** The core contribution is the successful application of a standard CNN architecture to the entity grid representation. This is a well-motivated approach that directly addresses the known limitations of discrete, feature-engineered grid models by learning distributed representations of long-range transition patterns in a task-specific manner.
2.  **Strong empirical results establishing a new state of the art.** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary rating), consistently outperforming strong non-neural baselines, including the models it directly extends. The reported gains are significant and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Elegant Model Design:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work by clearly articulating the weaknesses of the successful but limited entity grid model. The proposed CNN architecture is an elegant and intuitive solution to these problems. Using convolutions to detect location-invariant transition patterns of arbitrary length is a natural fit for the problem and directly tackles the issue of modeling long-range dependencies without feature sparsity.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is a major strength. The authors use standard, well-established datasets (WSJ) and evaluation tasks, allowing for direct comparison with a rich body of prior work. The choice of baselines is appropriate and strong, critically including the non-neural entity grid models (E&C) that this work aims to improve upon. The gains are not marginal; an absolute improvement of ~4% on the discrimination task over a strong baseline is impressive.

3.  **High Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow that is easy to follow. The model architecture is explained clearly, aided by a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The authors' explicit statement that they have released their code is a significant plus, strengthening the paper's contribution to the community and facilitating future work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** While the paper convincingly demonstrates *that* the model works well, it offers little insight into *why* it works better than its predecessors. The "Why it works" section is very high-level. A qualitative analysis of the learned filters would have been highly beneficial. For instance, what specific long-range transition patterns (e.g., `S-S-X-O-S`) do the learned filters capture that were missed by the trigram-based discrete models? An error analysis comparing the mistakes made by the Grid-CNN and the traditional grid model could have provided valuable insights into their respective strengths and weaknesses. Without this analysis, the model remains somewhat of a black box.

2.  **Simplistic Handling of Entity-Specific Features:** The proposed method for incorporating entity-specific features—creating composite symbols like `PERSON-S`—is a simple solution that expands the vocabulary size. This approach conflates the grammatical role and the entity type into a single atomic unit. A more sophisticated method, such as using separate embedding channels for roles and features and then combining them (e.g., via concatenation), might allow the model to learn more compositional representations and generalize better. This feels like a missed opportunity to explore a more powerful feature integration scheme within the neural framework.

3.  **Unsatisfying Dismissal of a Key Neural Baseline:** The authors mention that they were unable to get good results with the distributed sentence model of Li and Hovy (2014) and thus excluded it. While this diligence is appreciated, its simple exclusion is a weakness. As another prominent neural coherence model, it represents a crucial point of comparison. The statement "We were not sure what could go wrong" is insufficient. A more detailed discussion or hypothesis is needed. Is the sentence-averaging approach fundamentally flawed for this task/dataset compared to the entity-centric view? Was there a specific implementation challenge? Leaving this comparison unresolved weakens the claim of being the definitive state-of-the-art neural coherence model.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the integration of entity-specific features: Did you experiment with alternative methods besides creating composite symbols? For example, one could maintain separate vocabularies for roles and entity types, look up their respective embeddings, and concatenate these vectors before feeding them into the convolutional layer. Do you have any intuition on whether such a method would offer benefits over your current approach?

2.  Could you provide any qualitative analysis of the learned convolutional filters? It would greatly strengthen the paper to show a few examples of the specific multi-step entity transition patterns that the model learns are indicative of coherent (or incoherent) text. This would help bridge the gap between the model's strong performance and our understanding of what it has learned.

3.  The performance gain on the insertion task, while significant, is smaller than on the discrimination task. The paper hypothesizes that this is because the model was not trained specifically on insertion. Have you considered training a model on the insertion task directly (i.e., where the pairwise objective is to score the correct insertion higher than incorrect ones)? This would validate your hypothesis and could further demonstrate the model's robustness.
