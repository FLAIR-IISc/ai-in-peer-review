**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a neural network architecture for modeling local text coherence based on the entity grid representation. The proposed model uses a 1D convolutional neural network (CNN) that operates directly on the entity grid. It first learns distributed vector representations (embeddings) for grammatical roles of entities. Subsequently, convolutional filters are applied over the sequence of roles for each entity (i.e., each column of the grid) to learn abstract features representing entity transition patterns. These features are then pooled and used to compute a final coherence score for the text. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective. The authors evaluate their approach on three standard tasks—discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating—and demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art results, significantly outperforming previous entity grid models.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel neural architecture that successfully applies a convolutional network to the entity grid representation for coherence modeling. This method effectively models longer-range entity transitions using learned distributed representations, directly addressing the feature sparsity and "curse of dimensionality" problems that limited previous discrete, feature-based grid models to very short transitions.
2.  An extensive empirical evaluation on three established coherence tasks. The results provide strong evidence that the proposed neural approach significantly outperforms strong non-neural baselines, including the state-of-the-art extended entity grid model, setting a new benchmark for these tasks.
3.  An end-to-end learning framework for coherence assessment where task-specific features are learned automatically from the raw entity grid, contrasting with prior work where feature extraction and model training were decoupled.

**Strengths**

1.  **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The application of a CNN to the entity grid is a well-motivated and technically sound contribution. The core idea of treating each entity's transition sequence as a channel for a 1D convolution is an elegant way to capture local transition patterns. This approach provides a principled solution to the limitations of prior grid models, which struggled to model transitions of length k>3 due to feature sparsity. By learning continuous representations and filters, the model can capture more complex and longer-range dependencies, as evidenced by the optimal filter sizes of 5 and 6 found during tuning. The overall architecture is clearly explained and logically constructed.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Results:** The paper presents a robust evaluation across three distinct and standard coherence tasks. The proposed models, Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN, achieve consistent and statistically significant improvements over all baselines, including strong competitors like the extended entity grid model from Elsner and Charniak (2011). The reported gains are substantial (e.g., an absolute improvement of approximately 4% in accuracy on the discrimination task and over 4% on the summary coherence rating task), which strongly supports the effectiveness of the proposed neural architecture. The use of the large WSJ corpus for the main experiments provides a more reliable assessment than smaller, domain-specific corpora used in some prior work.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for a neural approach is clearly articulated by outlining the limitations of existing models. The proposed architecture is described in sufficient detail, and the accompanying Figure 2 provides an excellent visual summary of the model and training process. The experimental setup is thorough, detailing the datasets, baselines, and hyperparameter tuning process. The authors' commitment to releasing their code further strengthens the paper by ensuring that the results are verifiable and the method can be built upon by the community.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with a Key Neural Baseline:** The paper dismisses the contemporary neural coherence model of Li and Hovy (2014) based on a single, extremely poor performance number (19.34% accuracy), which is then excluded from the main results table. This comparison is inadequate. A state-of-the-art claim requires a more thorough engagement with competing neural methods. The authors should have provided more analysis on why the model failed so severely (e.g., issues with the public code, architectural mismatch for the WSJ dataset, lack of hyperparameter tuning). Without this, it is difficult to assess how the proposed Grid-CNN compares to other neural paradigms for coherence (e.g., recurrent models operating on sentence vectors).

2.  **Lack of Analysis and Interpretation:** The paper successfully demonstrates *that* the model performs well, but offers no insight into *what* it learns or *why* it is effective beyond high-level claims. An analysis of the learned model components would have significantly strengthened the paper. For instance, a qualitative inspection of the patterns captured by the most influential convolutional filters, or a visualization (e.g., using t-SNE) of the learned embeddings for grammatical roles, could provide valuable insights. Such analysis could help bridge the gap between the model's data-driven success and the linguistic theories of coherence that originally motivated the entity grid, showing whether the model rediscovers known coherence patterns or finds novel ones.

3.  **Simplistic Integration of Entity-Specific Features:** The method for incorporating entity-specific features in the "Extended Grid-CNN" is to create new composite symbols in the vocabulary (e.g., PERSON-S). While effective, this approach is quite basic and does not fully exploit the power of neural networks for feature composition. It leads to a combinatorial explosion in the vocabulary size and prevents the model from sharing knowledge between related roles (e.g., PERSON-S and ORG-S). A more sophisticated approach, such as using separate embedding channels for grammatical role and entity type and combining them with a composition function, would be more scalable and potentially more powerful.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the experimental results with the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Were hyperparameters retuned for the WSJ dataset, or were the original paper's settings used directly? A more detailed discussion on why you believe their architecture might be ill-suited for this task or dataset would be valuable for situating your work within the landscape of neural coherence models.

2.  The paper argues that the model can capture "long range entity transitions". The optimal filter window sizes were 5 and 6. Did you experiment with even larger window sizes, and if so, what was the impact on performance? Is there a clear performance plateau or degradation that suggests a natural limit to the length of local transition patterns that are useful for this task?

3.  While a full analysis is beyond the scope of a short paper, could you provide any qualitative insight into the learned representations? For example, in the learned embedding space for grammatical roles, are roles like Subject (S) and Object (O) closer to each other than to the Absent (-) role? This would provide evidence that the model is learning linguistically plausible representations.
