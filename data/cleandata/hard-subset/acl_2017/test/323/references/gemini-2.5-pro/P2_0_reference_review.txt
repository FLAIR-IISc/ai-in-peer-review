### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural network-based model for assessing local text coherence, designed as a direct successor to the popular entity grid model. The core idea is to apply a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) directly onto the entity grid representation of a text. The model first transforms the discrete grammatical roles (Subject, Object, etc.) in the grid into continuous vector representations via an embedding layer. A convolutional layer then slides filters over the columns of this embedded grid (representing entity transition sequences) to learn salient transition patterns. The resulting features are pooled and used to compute a final coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end using a pairwise ranking objective.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective neural formulation of the entity grid model.** The primary contribution is the successful application of a CNN architecture to the problem of modeling entity transitions. This approach elegantly addresses a key limitation of prior grid models—the curse of dimensionality—by using distributed representations and shared convolutional filters, allowing it to capture longer and more complex transition patterns than was previously feasible.

2.  **Demonstration of strong empirical performance and new state-of-the-art results.** The proposed model significantly outperforms strong, established baselines (including the extended entity grid model) on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating. These consistent and substantial gains validate the effectiveness of the neural approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Sound Methodology.** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models (sparsity from discrete representations, inability to model long transitions, task-agnostic feature engineering) and proposes a neural architecture that directly addresses each of these points. The choice of a CNN is well-justified, as its properties of local connectivity and location invariance are a natural fit for detecting recurring transition patterns anywhere within an entity's history. The end-to-end training via a pairwise ranking loss is appropriate for the task and follows best practices from prior work.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results.** The experimental results are a key strength. The paper shows consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including the state-of-the-art extended entity grid model by Elsner & Charniak (2011). Achieving absolute gains of ~4% on the discrimination task and ~2.5% on the more difficult insertion task on a large, standard dataset (WSJ) is impressive. The fact that the `Extended Grid-CNN` outperforms the `Extended Grid (E&C)` model while using a smaller, more easily obtainable set of entity features further highlights the power of the neural feature learning approach.

3.  **High-Quality Evaluation and Reproducibility.** The evaluation is thorough and follows established protocols. The authors use standard tasks, metrics, and a large, well-justified dataset (WSJ). The inclusion of multiple strong baselines makes the comparison robust. The authors' attention to detail, such as running experiments multiple times with different random seeds and performing significance testing, inspires confidence in the results. The statement that the code has been released with the submission is a major plus for reproducibility and future work in the field.

4.  **Excellent Clarity and Presentation.** The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. Figure 2 provides an intuitive and helpful visualization of the model architecture and training process. The authors effectively explain complex concepts (e.g., the entity grid, the CNN layers) in an accessible manner, making the paper a pleasure to read.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Neural Architecture Itself.** While the *application* of a CNN to the entity grid is novel and insightful, the architecture itself (embedding layer -> convolution -> max-pooling -> linear layer) is a standard design widely used in NLP, particularly for sentence classification (e.g., Kim, 2014). The paper applies this existing architecture to a different data representation (the grid instead of a sentence). While this is a valid and successful contribution, the work could be strengthened by acknowledging the architectural precedents more directly and focusing the claims of novelty on the application and problem formulation.

2.  **Simplistic Integration of Entity-Specific Features.** The method for incorporating entity-specific features—by creating new composite symbols like 'PERSON-S' and learning a distinct embedding for each—is a straightforward but potentially suboptimal approach. This method dramatically increases the vocabulary size of the embedding layer and fails to explicitly model any shared properties between, for example, 'PERSON-S' and 'PERSON-O'. A more compositional approach, such as learning separate embeddings for grammatical roles and entity types and combining them (e.g., via concatenation or a small MLP), might be more parameter-efficient and offer better generalization.

3.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis.** The paper presents strong quantitative results but offers little insight into *what* the model has actually learned. The "Why it works" paragraph provides a high-level intuition, but the paper would be much more impactful with a qualitative analysis of the learned components. For example, an inspection of the learned convolutional filters could reveal whether the model has rediscovered linguistically meaningful transition patterns (e.g., patterns corresponding to the 'continue' or 'shift' transitions from Centering Theory). This analysis would move the paper from simply showing that "it works" to explaining "how and why it works."

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the integration of entity-specific features, your method creates an expanded vocabulary of composite symbols (e.g., 'PERSON-S'). Did you experiment with or consider more compositional methods, such as learning separate embeddings for roles (S, O, X, -) and entity features (PERSON, ORG, salient, etc.) and then combining their vectors before the convolution layer? It seems this could lead to better parameter sharing and generalization.

2.  The quantitative improvements are very clear. To better understand the model's behavior, could you provide any qualitative analysis of the learned filters? For example, by finding the input patches that maximally activate certain filters, can you determine if the model is learning to identify specific, interpretable coherence patterns (e.g., a subject-to-subject chain 'S-S-S', or a shift from object to subject 'O-S')?

3.  You report that you were unable to get good performance from the Li and Hovy (2014) model. While acknowledging the difficulty of reproducing others' work, could you speculate on why a model based on distributed sentence representations might underperform compared to your entity-centric model on these tasks? Does this suggest that explicit entity tracking is more crucial for local coherence than general sentence semantics?
