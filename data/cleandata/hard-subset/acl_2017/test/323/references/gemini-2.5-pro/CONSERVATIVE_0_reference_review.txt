**Summary of the paper and claimed contributions**

This paper proposes a neural network model to assess local text coherence, building upon the established entity grid representation (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008). The authors argue that traditional entity grid models suffer from data sparsity when modeling long-range entity transitions and use task-agnostic feature engineering. To address this, they introduce a model that first creates distributed representations (embeddings) for the grammatical roles in the grid and then applies a 1D Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) along each entity's transition column to learn abstract features. The model is trained end-to-end (from the grid representation) using a pairwise ranking objective. The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  The application of a standard CNN architecture to the entity grid representation for coherence modeling.
2.  An empirical demonstration that this neural approach achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming strong non-neural entity grid baselines on three standard evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Baselines and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper's greatest strength is its thorough and well-grounded empirical evaluation. The authors compare their model against the highly relevant and strong entity grid model and its extensions by Elsner & Charniak (2011). Crucially, they use the publicly available Brown coherence toolkit to generate these baseline results, which lends significant credibility and reproducibility to their comparisons. The evaluation is conducted on standard tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating) using a widely accepted dataset (WSJ), allowing for clear positioning of the work within the field.

2.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The authors provide a clear and compelling motivation for their work by identifying specific, well-understood limitations of the classic entity grid model—namely, the curse of dimensionality for discrete transition features and the decoupled nature of feature extraction. The proposed neural architecture is a direct and plausible approach to mitigate these specific problems.

3.  **Significant Empirical Improvements:** The proposed models demonstrate consistent and statistically significant improvements over their non-neural counterparts. An absolute gain of around 4% on the discrimination task and over 4% on summary coherence rating over the extended grid model of Elsner & Charniak (2011) is a non-trivial result. This suggests that the neural approach is genuinely effective at capturing more robust coherence patterns from the grid representation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** While the application is novel, the neural architecture itself is entirely standard. The model is a straightforward implementation of a 1D CNN with embedding, convolution, and max-pooling layers, an architecture well-established for various NLP tasks (e.g., Kim, 2014). The contribution lies in "neuralizing" an existing, successful representation rather than proposing a new coherence modeling paradigm or a novel neural architecture. As such, the work feels more like a successful application of existing tools than a fundamental advance in coherence modeling.

2.  **Lack of Analysis and Interpretability:** The paper demonstrates *that* the model works better but offers little insight into *why*. The "Why it works" section provides a high-level intuition, but the paper would be much stronger with a qualitative analysis of the learned representations. What kinds of entity transition patterns do the learned convolutional filters actually detect? Are they capturing linguistically intuitive phenomena (e.g., subject-to-subject chains) or more complex, non-local patterns that traditional models miss? Without such analysis, the model remains a black box, and it is difficult to draw deeper conclusions about the nature of coherence beyond the improved performance numbers.

3.  **Inadequate Treatment of a Key Neural Baseline:** The dismissal of the related neural model by Li and Hovy (2014) is concerning. The authors state that the results were "very disappointing" and they were "not sure what could go wrong." This is insufficient justification for excluding a highly relevant contemporary neural approach. A rigorous comparison would require a more thorough effort to replicate the baseline, including hyperparameter tuning, or a well-reasoned argument for why that model is fundamentally unsuited to this particular dataset or task. As it stands, the state-of-the-art claim feels incomplete, as it is only validated against non-neural models while sidestepping a direct comparison with another neural alternative.

4.  **Overstated "End-to-End" Claim:** The paper claims to present an "end-to-end training method." This is somewhat misleading. The model is end-to-end from the entity grid to the final score, but it is not end-to-end from raw text. The generation of the entity grid itself is a complex, multi-stage pipeline involving parsing and coreference resolution, and errors from these upstream components are not modeled. The phrasing should be more precise to reflect that the feature learning, not the entire coherence assessment process, is end-to-end.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Can you provide a qualitative analysis of the learned filters in your CNN? For example, could you identify specific filters that respond strongly to known coherent patterns (e.g., subject chains) and provide examples of input grids that activate them? This would greatly improve the interpretability of your model.

2.  Could you elaborate on your efforts to run the Li and Hovy (2014) model? Given its relevance as another neural approach to coherence, a more detailed discussion of why it failed on this dataset is necessary to substantiate your SOTA claims. Was the issue possibly related to differences in pre-trained embeddings, sentence length, or other dataset-specific characteristics?

3.  How sensitive is your model to the quality of the entity grid? Have you performed any analysis on how errors from the coreference resolution system affect your model's performance compared to the traditional entity grid model?
