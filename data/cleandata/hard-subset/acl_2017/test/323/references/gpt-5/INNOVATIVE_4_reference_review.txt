Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neuralized version of the entity grid model for local coherence. It maps grammatical roles (and optionally entity-specific features) to embeddings, applies convolution over entity transitions to capture longer-range patterns, aggregates with max pooling, and trains end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss. The model is evaluated on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, reporting consistent improvements over non-neural entity-grid baselines.

Key contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, effective neural architecture that operates on entity grids, learning distributed role embeddings and convolutional filters to model long-range entity transitions; this bridges classic entity-based coherence theory with end-to-end representation learning.
2) Empirical evidence that such neuralized grids yield state-of-the-art performance across three standard tasks, with sizable absolute gains over strong grid baselines and reasonable transfer to summary rating via pretraining.
3) A practical recipe for incorporating entity-specific features into the neural grid without exploding feature dimensionality.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty with a clear direction shift: Moving from sparse, hand-engineered transition counts to learned embeddings and convolutional composition over entity transitions is a substantial step that retains the interpretability of entity grids while enabling longer contexts and task-specific feature learning. This is a meaningful new direction for coherence modeling grounded in discourse theory.
- Consistent, material gains on established benchmarks: The model improves over strong baselines by ~4% absolute on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary rating. Results are statistically significant, and the method achieves state-of-the-art performance on all tasks considered.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility: The architecture, loss, and training details are clearly specified (embeddings, filter sizes, pooling, optimization, early stopping). Hyperparameter search space and chosen settings are reported; significance testing is performed; code is released. This facilitates verification and future extensions.
- Simplicity and interpretability: Using convolution over entity transitions is intuitive—filters correspond to transition motifs and max pooling captures salient occurrences. The approach stays close to the entity-grid intuition while addressing sparsity and feature explosion.
- Transfer across tasks: Pretraining on WSJ for discrimination followed by fine-tuning improves summary coherence rating, supporting the claim that the model learns reusable coherence-sensitive features.

Weaknesses
- “End-to-end” claim is overstated: The approach depends on external preprocessing (coreference resolution, syntactic roles, named entities), and assumes independence across entity columns. While the learned representation is task-trained, the pipeline is not end-to-end from raw text and inherits errors/biases from upstream tools. An analysis of sensitivity to coreference quality is missing.
- Limited ablations and analysis: The paper lacks ablations on transition length k, filter numbers, pooling, and the contribution of each entity-specific feature. There is no error analysis (e.g., in which discourse phenomena gains arise or fail). This makes it hard to assess which components matter most and why insertion gains are smaller.
- Comparisons and fairness issues: The Li & Hovy (2014) neural model is excluded due to poor reproduction, but no alternative comparison (e.g., reported numbers on similar datasets) is given. Also, the strongest non-neural baseline uses more features (some external), while the neural extended grid uses only three—useful to the paper’s advantage but complicates a fully controlled comparison. Including numbers with matched feature sets and an “entity-only vs entity+features” ablation would strengthen claims.
- Modeling assumptions may cap ceiling: Treating entity columns independently ignores cross-entity interactions that often drive coherence (e.g., role alternation across participants). Max pooling also removes positional information beyond local windows; long-distance ordering effects or global discourse structure are not modeled. A brief discussion or experiment probing these limitations would help bound the contribution.
- Dataset and generalization scope: Main experiments focus on WSJ; the paper argues AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES are limited, but some cross-domain evidence (even small-scale) would be valuable. For summary rating, data are very small; the approach benefits from pretraining, but a careful examination of overfitting or variance (beyond average over seeds) is not provided.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to coreference quality? Did you test different coreference systems or perturb coreference to quantify impact?
- Can you provide ablations on transition length k, number of filters, and pooling size, and a plot of performance vs k to substantiate the “long-range transitions” claim?
- What is the effect of each of the three entity-specific features in the Extended Grid-CNN? A leave-one-out analysis would clarify their contribution.
- How exactly is convolution applied: strictly along the sentence axis within each entity column, or also across entities? Does pooling aggregate across all entities, and if so, does it remove useful positional information?
- Could you train directly on the insertion task (pairwise within-document comparisons) and report whether that narrows the discrimination/insertion gap?
- What are the parameter counts and runtimes compared to the extended grid baseline? Any practical bottlenecks for longer documents?
- Did you inspect learned filters to identify interpretable transition motifs (e.g., sustained subject continuity)? Examples would support the intuitions in “Why it works.”
- Any results on out-of-domain test sets (even small) to assess robustness beyond WSJ?
