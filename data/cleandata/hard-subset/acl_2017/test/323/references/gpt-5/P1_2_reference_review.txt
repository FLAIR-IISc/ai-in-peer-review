Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that operates over entity grids. It replaces discrete transition probabilities with distributed embeddings and a convolutional architecture over per-entity transition sequences, trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss. The model can incorporate entity-specific features by expanding the vocabulary of grid symbols (e.g., PERSON-S). The approach is evaluated on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating, showing consistent improvements over entity-grid baselines and a graph-based method.

Main contributions (as I see them), strongest first:
1) A simple, effective neuralization of entity grids: embedding grammatical-role symbols and using CNN+max-pooling to detect longer, location-invariant transition patterns, trained with pairwise ranking.
2) Empirical improvements and transfer: consistent, statistically significant gains over strong grid baselines on WSJ discrimination/insertion and further gains on DUC’03 via pretraining on WSJ.
3) A practical framework to incorporate entity-specific features without combinatorial blow-up by embedding symbol-feature conjunctions; code release for reproducibility.

Strengths
1) Clear, well-motivated architecture that addresses known limitations of entity grids. The use of embeddings and convolutions directly targets the sparsity/curse-of-dimensionality of long transitions and allows k up to 5–8. The max-pooling captures location-invariant patterns, aligning with the intuition that specific transition motifs signal coherence independent of column position.

2) Solid and consistent empirical gains. On WSJ discrimination, Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN improve by roughly 4% and ~3.7% absolute over their non-neural counterparts; on insertion, gains up to 2.5% are reported. Improvements are significant (approximate randomization), and the neural models beat both all-noun and feature-extended grids, despite using only a subset of features in the extended variant.

3) End-to-end task-focused training with pairwise ranking. Unlike classical grid models where feature computation is task-agnostic, the proposed training couples representation learning with the evaluation task. The pretraining + fine-tuning on DUC summaries is a sensible and effective transfer setting, yielding further improvements when labeled data are scarce.

4) Reproducibility and experimental transparency. The paper details hyperparameter search, optimization choices (RMSProp), early stopping, multiple random seeds, and significance testing. Using a large WSJ split is appropriate for training neural models, and leveraging public toolkits for baselines strengthens replicability.

5) Practical extensibility. The symbol-conjunction trick makes it straightforward to inject additional entity features without exploding feature spaces; this is a pragmatic path to richer models without complex architecture changes.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior neural coherence work. While applying CNNs over entity grids is neat, the conceptual leap—from discrete transition counts to learned filters over distributed representations—is incremental. Prior neural coherence models (e.g., Li & Hovy 2014) already learn coherence from distributed representations at the sentence level. The main novelty lies in the input representation choice (entity grid) and convolutional parameterization, not in a fundamentally new coherence objective or modeling of discourse structure.

2) Incomplete comparisons and SOTA claims. The empirical comparisons omit strong discourse-relation and RST-based models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014), which have been competitive on ordering/coherence. The exclusion of Li & Hovy 2014 results due to disappointing reproduction (reported briefly in prose) leaves uncertainty about relative standing. As written, “state of the art” is not fully substantiated across all task variants.

3) Evaluation design and reporting issues. The insertion metric/reporting is unclear: the table headers (Discr., Ins., Acc, F1) and values (e.g., ~23.xx) do not match the earlier description of insertion as “average fraction reinserted in actual position.” Please clarify whether accuracy or F1 is used for insertion and ensure consistency across text and tables. More rigorous ordering metrics (e.g., Kendall’s tau, perfect match rate across multiple permutations) and harder baselines (e.g., near-neighbor permutations) would strengthen conclusions.

4) Strong dependence on preprocessing with no robustness analysis. The approach inherits errors from parsing and coreference resolution; yet there is no sensitivity or ablation study (e.g., with/without non-head nouns, different coref systems, error simulated noise). Given the centrality of the grid, it would be important to quantify how coref quality impacts performance.

5) Modeling assumptions may limit expressivity. Treating entity columns independently ignores inter-entity interactions that may jointly signal coherence (e.g., alternating focus between protagonists). Aggressive max-pooling discards positional information about where patterns occur. The paper argues location invariance is desirable, but an analysis showing whether absolute/relative positions matter (or adding positional encodings) is missing.

Questions to authors
- Metrics/reporting: Please clarify the insertion evaluation metric precisely and fix the table header inconsistencies. If F1 is used, define positive/negative classes and averaging.
- Baselines: Can you add comparisons to discourse-relation/RST-based methods (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) under your WSJ setup? If not feasible, discuss differences that prevent fair comparison.
- Sensitivity/ablation: What is the effect of varying transition length k, filter count, and pooling size on performance? How much of the gain comes from longer k (>3)? Include an ablation comparing k=3 vs 5–8.
- Preprocessing: Which coreference resolver and parser are used? How sensitive are results to using only head nouns vs all nouns? Any evidence on robustness to coref errors?
- Training objective: Have you tried training directly for insertion (e.g., pairwise preferences over insertion positions)? If so, how does it compare to discrimination-trained models?
- Interpretability: Can you provide examples of high-activation filters and the transition patterns they capture, and qualitative error analyses where the model succeeds/fails?

Overall recommendation
This is a solid, well-executed paper with a simple idea that yields consistent gains and is likely to be useful for practitioners. The novelty is moderate and the comparisons could be broader, but the empirical contribution and clarity justify acceptance. I recommend accept (weak).
