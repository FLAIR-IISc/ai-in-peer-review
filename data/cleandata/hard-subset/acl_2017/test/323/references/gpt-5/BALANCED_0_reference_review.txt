Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neuralized version of the entity grid for modeling local coherence. Each grid cell (grammatical role possibly augmented with entity-specific features) is embedded; 1D convolutions are applied down each entity column to capture transitions of length k; max pooling selects salient transition patterns; a linear layer outputs a coherence score. The model is trained with pairwise ranking. Evaluations on WSJ discrimination and insertion tasks and DUC’03 summary coherence rating show consistent improvements over non-neural entity-grid variants and a graph-based baseline. Code is released.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple and effective CNN over entity grids that learns distributed representations of entity transitions and yields consistent gains over strong grid baselines across three standard tasks, with significance testing and multi-run averaging.
2) An end-to-end pairwise-ranking training procedure that enables task-specific feature learning, including incorporation of entity-specific features without a combinatorial explosion.
3) Empirical findings that longer transition windows (enabled by embeddings + CNN) help, and that pretraining on sentence ordering transfers to summary coherence rating.

Strengths
- Empirical effectiveness and breadth of evaluation: The model improves over the widely used entity grid and the extended grid across discrimination (+~4% abs.) and insertion (up to +2.5% abs.) on WSJ, and on DUC’03 summary rating (>4% with pretraining). Results are averaged over multiple seeds and tested for significance against non-neural counterparts, which increases confidence in robustness.
- Conceptual clarity and practicality: The architecture is a natural, well-motivated extension of the grid: embeddings reduce sparsity; convolutions over columns capture longer local transitions in a position-invariant way; max pooling selects salient patterns. The independence assumption across entity columns mirrors classic grid models, making the design easy to understand and implement.
- End-to-end, task-specific learning: Unlike traditional grid features computed in a task-agnostic way, the pairwise-ranking setup trains embeddings and filters for the target task. The approach learns useful features even when only a subset of entity-specific features is included, suggesting better parameter efficiency.
- Reproducibility details: The paper provides hyperparameter ranges, optimization choices, dev-set tuning, early stopping strategy, multiple runs, and significance testing. It also states that code is released and that baselines were reproduced with public toolkits, which facilitates verification.
- Sensible transfer to low-data setting: Pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning on DUC’03 improves summary coherence rating, a common and practical strategy when supervision is scarce.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: Neuralizing the entity grid with CNNs is intuitive and incremental; similar ideas (distributed representations, neural scoring over local windows) have appeared in coherence modeling (e.g., Li & Hovy, 2014) and other grid extensions. The main novelty is architectural instantiation rather than a new theoretical perspective or modeling assumption.
- Baseline coverage and fairness: The comparison omits strong discourse-based baselines (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014 RST-based) in the reported tables. The paper notes these in related work but does not present empirical comparisons. The Li & Hovy model is excluded due to disappointing results on the authors’ setup; more diagnosis or hyperparameter search might be necessary to rule out implementation issues.
- Ablation and analysis are thin: There is no ablation on (i) transition window size k, (ii) pooling length, (iii) embedding dimension, (iv) contribution of each entity-specific feature, or (v) the effect of all-nouns vs head-nouns in the neural setting. Without these, it’s hard to attribute gains to specific design choices or to understand model sensitivity.
- Coreference and preprocessing details: Results depend critically on entity detection and coreference. The paper does not specify the resolver (or noun-matching heuristic), quality, or its impact on performance. It is unclear how non-head nouns are linked and how grammatical roles are assigned for them; potential noise could affect conclusions.
- Generalization and task mismatch: Training is centered on discrimination via random permutations. Insertion is arguably more diagnostic and different in nature. The authors acknowledge not training directly for insertion; doing so would strengthen claims. Domain robustness is not demonstrated beyond WSJ; AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES or other genres would help assess generalization.

Questions to authors
- Which coreference system and syntactic parser were used to build the grids? How sensitive are results to these components? Any quantitative estimate (or ablation) on using gold vs. automatic coreference?
- How large is the symbol vocabulary when concatenating entity features (e.g., PERSON-S, salience bins, proper noun flags)? What is the OOV rate and its effect on training stability?
- Can you report ablations on k (transition window) and pooling length, and the contribution of each of the three entity-specific features in the Extended Grid-CNN?
- Did you attempt training the model directly on insertion pairs (contrastive positions) and, if so, how did it compare?
- Could you include comparisons against RST/discourse-relation baselines or combine discourse features as an additional channel?

Additional comments
- The convolution/pooling indexing notation is a bit opaque (e.g., hi length m·n + k − 1). A small schematic clarifying the exact convolution path over columns (and how padding is applied) would help reproducibility.
- Consider reporting runtime and parameter counts to contextualize the cost vs. grid models.
- Error analysis (e.g., characteristic failures on insertion, entity types where gains are largest) would strengthen insights.
- The references section appears to contain formatting issues; please fix for camera-ready.

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed, and practically useful neuralization of a classic approach with solid empirical gains. While the conceptual novelty is moderate and some comparisons/ablations are missing, the paper meets a reasonable bar for contribution and reproducibility. Strengthening the experimental analysis (ablations, stronger baselines, insertion-trained variant, and coreference details) would further improve the work.
