- Proposes a neuralized entity-grid: embed grammatical-role symbols, apply 1D convolutions over entity transitions, max-pool, and linear layer with pairwise ranking to score coherence.
- Shows a simple method to incorporate entity-specific features by conjoining feature values with grammatical roles and embedding resulting symbols.
- Evaluates on WSJ discrimination and insertion plus DUC’03 summary rating, reporting consistent, significant gains over non-neural grid variants and a graph baseline.

- Main contribution 1: Practical neural architecture that models longer transition lengths without combinatorial sparsity, achieving state-of-the-art coherence results.
- Main contribution 2: End-to-end pairwise-ranking training that learns task-specific features and outperforms hand-crafted transition-probability features.
- Main contribution 3: Simple integration of entity signals (NE type, salience, properness) into the neural grid, improving performance with fewer features.

- Strength: Empirically strong and broad — consistent, significant improvements across multiple coherence tasks with multiple runs and significance testing.
- Strength: Method is conceptually simple and well-motivated — embeddings + CNNs capture long-range patterns; hinge loss matches ranking objective.
- Strength: Experimental setup is reproducible and clearly described (hyperparameters, optimizer, early stopping, significance protocol).
- Strength: Fair feature usage — improvements hold despite using fewer entity features and no external corpora.
- Strength: Method is modular and extensible, suitable as a drop-in for grid-based pipelines and compatible with richer signals.

- Weakness: Limited novelty — largely a “neuralization” of existing grids and incremental relative to prior neural coherence work.
- Weakness: Independence assumptions and max-pooling may discard cross-entity interactions and positional nuances, potentially missing topic-shift phenomena.
- Weakness: Evaluation gaps — discrimination uses random permutations only (no hard/local perturbations); insertion training is mismatched to task; Li & Hovy (2014) baseline excluded without deeper analysis.
- Weakness: Grid construction and preprocessing are under-specified (coreference and NP detection unclear), limiting assessment of robustness to coref quality.
- Weakness: Limited ablations and analysis — no per-feature, k/pooling, filter-count ablations, no qualitative filter analysis, and limited cross-domain robustness checks given small DUC’03 data.

- Q: Which coreference and NP tools/settings were used; do you resolve pronouns/nominal coref or only head-noun matching, and how sensitive are results to coref errors?
- Q: How does performance vary with transition window size k and pooling length p; is there an optimal k or do long k values over-smooth?
- Q: Did you train specifically for insertion (task-specific negatives or listwise losses) and how did that affect insertion results?
- Q: Can you provide qualitative examples of high-activation filters and the transition patterns they capture, and do they map to known coherence patterns?
- Q: Exactly how are entity-specific features encoded (e.g., concatenated symbols vs. multi-hot) and are embeddings shared across roles and feature values?
- Q: What is the computational cost relative to the extended grid (training time, inference per document), and how does it scale with document length and entity count?

- Overall: Method is technically sound and clear, with strong empirical gains but moderate originality and incomplete comparisons/analyses.
- Recommendation inclination: Accept, contingent on strengthening comparative breadth, preprocessing clarity, and ablation/analysis.
