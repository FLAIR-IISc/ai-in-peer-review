Summary and main contributions
- A convolutional neural network over entity grids: The paper “neuralizes” the entity grid by embedding grid symbols (grammatical role plus optional entity-specific features), applying 1D convolutions over per-entity sentence transitions, max-pooling, and a linear scoring layer, trained with pairwise ranking. This directly addresses data sparsity of discrete transition features and allows longer transition windows.
- Incorporating entity-specific features in a simple, extensible way: The paper proposes to attach features (e.g., NE type, salience, proper name) to role symbols, letting the embedding learn useful interactions.
- Empirical results on sentence ordering and summary coherence rating: On WSJ discrimination and insertion, and DUC summary rating, the proposed models outperform entity-grid baselines, with additional gains from pretraining.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity of the core idea: Using distributed representations for grid symbols and CNNs over transition windows is a natural, well-motivated remedy for the curse of dimensionality in entity grids while staying faithful to the grid paradigm (columns treated independently).
- Training objective matches use-case: Pairwise ranking with shared weights closely mirrors the common evaluation protocols for coherence. The use of five runs and an approximate randomization test is appropriate and strengthens claims of robustness.
- Sensible, large-scale dataset choice for sentence ordering: WSJ PTB (Sections 00–13 train, 14–24 test) is substantially larger and more varied than AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES; this supports the deep model and improves external validity.
- Evidence of transfer: Pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning on DUC improves summary coherence rating, which is plausible and practically useful given the small summary dataset.
- Competitive results with fewer engineered features: The neural “extended” variant uses only three easy-to-compute features yet reportedly outperforms the much more feature-rich extended grid of Elsner & Charniak (2011), suggesting the learned representations capture informative patterns.

Weaknesses
- Serious presentation and formatting problems impede comprehension and trust:
  - Tables/results are inconsistent: In Table 3, the discrimination and insertion numbers are identical for each system, which is implausible given the text and typical behavior of these tasks. This undermines the results. Table 4 (summary rating) is referenced but not included.
  - Metrics are unclear: Table 3 has a third column labeled “Acc F1” with values (e.g., 12.60, 25.95) that do not match standard metrics for either task; the text later refers to summary rating improvements without showing the actual numbers or metric (e.g., Kendall’s τ or pairwise accuracy).
  - Numerous footnotes and asides are misplaced in the main text (“Short articles may produce many matches.” “For clarification, pairwise input …”); references have missing/garbled years (“? 2014”), inconsistent formatting, and broken citations; there are stray typographical artifacts. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for ACL and raises concerns about carefulness elsewhere.
- Insufficient methodological detail and notational ambiguity:
  - Convolutional shape/aggregation is unclear. The feature map length formula hi = [h1,…,hm.n+k−1] mixes sentence and entity dimensions. What exactly is convolved (per column across sentences) and how are the n columns aggregated (concatenation then pooled across entities and time? separate pooling then concatenation)? How is document length variability handled (fixed pooling length p across different m)? What padding is applied (the text oddly says t > {m,n})?
  - Entity recognition/coreference: The coreference method is unspecified. Do you use the Brown toolkit’s heuristics (string/head matches) or an external resolver? For “all nouns,” how are mentions grouped into entities? This choice materially affects the grid and thus results.
  - Feature encoding and vocabulary size: Attaching features to roles (e.g., PERSON-S) implies a combinatorial explosion. How are salience values discretized (bins?), how many NE types, and what is the resulting |V|? Without this, it’s hard to assess generalization and training stability.
  - Hyperparameters: You report one window size per model; do you use multiple filter widths (common in CNNs) or a single width? How is the max-pooling region p chosen relative to m? Is there any normalization for document length or number of entities?
- Baselines and fairness:
  - The strongest non-neural baseline (Extended Grid) reportedly uses nine features (some from external corpora), whereas your neural version uses only three; this comparison is interesting but asymmetric. Conversely, you exclude the Li & Hovy (2014) neural sentence model due to poor performance on your setup without diagnosing or adapting it; given that your main claim is “neuralizing” coherence, a stronger effort to include and tune competing neural baselines is needed.
  - More recent sentence ordering models (e.g., permutation-ranking or neural order models) are not included; only one graph model (Guinaudeau & Strube) is considered.
- Limited analysis/ablation:
  - No ablations for convolution window size k, pooling length p, embedding initialization, or feature subsets; no error analysis; no training on insertion (despite acknowledging this might help). Given the simplicity of the architecture, analysis would be straightforward and would substantiate claims about long-range transitions and feature learning.
- Reproducibility issues:
  - Code is “released with the submission” but no link is provided. Important details (preprocessing pipeline, tokenization, POS/parse resources, exact entity feature inventories and bins, random seeds, hardware/time) are missing.

Questions to authors
- Please correct Table 3 (discrimination vs. insertion) and include the missing Table 4 with exact metrics. What metric do you use for summary rating (pairwise accuracy, τ, ρ)? Provide full numerical results and significance tests.
- Clarify the convolution/pooling pipeline precisely: tensor shapes at each layer; whether you convolve separately per entity column across sentences and then how you aggregate across entities; how pooling is applied over variable-length documents; whether multiple filter widths are used.
- How are entities detected and coreferred for “all nouns”? Do you rely on the Brown toolkit’s pipeline, a particular coref system, or simple string matching? What is the effect of coref errors (any robustness checks)?
- How exactly is “salience” encoded when attached to roles? Raw counts, binned categories (specify bins), or normalized ranks? What is the final vocabulary size |V| for the extended model?
- Can you provide controlled ablations: (i) effect of k (2–8), (ii) effect of p, (iii) without entity features, (iv) training on insertion pairs? This would validate your claims about long-range transitions and task-specific learning.
- Please provide a working code repository link and a precise description of preprocessing and training settings to ensure reproducibility.

Overall, while the core idea is promising and results appear strong, the paper’s presentation and several methodological gaps are substantial. I cannot recommend acceptance without corrected/complete results and clarifications.
