Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid by (i) embedding grid symbols (grammatical roles optionally bundled with entity features) and (ii) applying 1D convolution over entity-specific columns (i.e., sentence-wise transitions) followed by max pooling and a pairwise ranking objective. The model is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) on WSJ and summary coherence ranking on DUC’03, achieving consistent improvements over traditional entity grid baselines and a graph-based model.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1) A simple, effective neural architecture over entity grids that learns distributed representations of entity transitions and supports longer transition windows than traditional sparse-count models, yielding consistent state-of-the-art results on standard coherence tasks.
2) An end-to-end pairwise ranking training scheme with transfer to a low-resource setting (pretraining on WSJ, fine-tuning on DUC’03 summaries), demonstrating practical benefits.
3) A straightforward mechanism for incorporating entity-specific features into the neural grid via compositional symbols, showing gains even with a subset of features compared to a strong extended grid baseline.

Strengths
1) Strong, consistent empirical gains with careful evaluation. The model improves over: (a) the basic entity grid (all nouns) by ~4% absolute on discrimination and ~1% on insertion, and (b) the extended grid by ~3.7% on discrimination and up to 2.5% on insertion, with statistical significance. On DUC’03, pretraining further boosts summary ranking performance by 2.5–3.7%. Multiple runs, dev tuning, and significance testing are reported.

2) Well-motivated design that addresses known limitations. By replacing sparse discrete transition counts with embeddings and convolution, the model can use longer transition windows (k≈5–8) without combinatorial explosion, and learns task-specific features via end-to-end training rather than task-agnostic grid statistics.

3) Fairly strong baselines and replication effort. The paper compares to both graph-based and extended entity grid models (via the Brown coherence toolkit), and acknowledges previous datasets/design choices. Reporting of hyperparameters, training procedure (RMSProp, dropout, early stopping), and use of pairwise ranking loss is clear.

4) Practicality and extensibility. The approach is modular (embedding-lookup + conv + pooling), easy to extend with additional entity-level attributes (named entity type, salience, proper mention), and potentially compatible with richer discourse features in future work.

5) Clarity of the core method. The architecture and assumptions (independent columns, location-invariant convolution along sentence dimension, wide convolution with zero-padding) are clearly articulated, with intuitive rationale (filters detect salient transition patterns).

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty beyond a straightforward neuralization. Conceptually, applying CNNs to embedded entity-grid symbols is a direct translation of the classic grid into a neural template; the independence assumptions and reliance on local entity transitions remain unchanged. While the empirical gains are meaningful, the modeling novelty is incremental.

2) Insufficient detail and analysis of preprocessing and feature construction. The results hinge on entity detection, coreference resolution, and grammatical role assignment (S/O/X), yet the paper does not specify the parser/coreference resolver, how non-head nouns and pronouns are treated, or mapping from syntax to S/O/X. This affects reproducibility and fairness of comparison; it also precludes understanding the sensitivity to coreference/parser errors.

3) Limited breadth of evaluation and analysis. The main sentence-ordering results are only on WSJ; the alternative AIRPLANE/EARTHQUAKE datasets are not included, and DUC’03 is small. There is no robustness analysis across domains, document lengths, or different permutation difficulties. No error analysis or qualitative insights into what patterns the filters learn are provided.

4) Missing ablations and hyperparameter impact. The paper claims benefits from longer k, pooling, and feature inclusion, but does not quantify the effect of transition window length, pooling size, number of filters, or individual entity features. An ablation isolating each factor (e.g., embeddings only, conv only, features only, varying k) would strengthen causal claims.

5) Baseline comparison gaps. The authors report a very poor result for Li & Hovy (2014) but exclude it without in-depth investigation or hyperparameter tuning details, which weakens the comparative narrative. The extended grid baseline uses more handcrafted features than the neural model, yet the learning objectives differ (SVM vs neural ranking); matching the training paradigm (e.g., re-training extended grid with the same pairwise objective or adding a simple neural MLP over the traditional features) would clarify where gains come from.

Questions to authors
- Preprocessing: Which coreference resolver and parser were used? How are S/O/X roles derived (dependency mapping or phrase-structure heuristics)? How are non-head nouns, pronouns, and multi-mentions per sentence handled? What proportion of entities are pronouns, and do you filter singleton entities?
- Vocabulary construction: When concatenating entity features with roles (e.g., PERSON-S), how large does V become? Do you cap rare feature-role combos or use any hashing/regularization to mitigate sparsity?
- Ablations: What is the effect of transition window length k and pooling size p on accuracy? Please include a sensitivity plot for k and p. How much of the gain comes from embeddings alone (no conv), conv with narrow vs wide padding, and from each of the three entity features?
- Task-specific training: If you train the model directly on insertion pairs (rather than permutations), does insertion performance increase as hypothesized?
- Interpretability: Can you provide examples of high-activation filters and the transition patterns they capture? Do they align with Centering-theoretic expectations (e.g., subject continuity)?
- Runtime/model size: What are training/inference times and parameter counts for the best models? How does it scale with document length and number of entities?
- Li & Hovy baseline: What settings were used, and did you attempt domain-specific tuning? Can you release your scripts/results to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall: A solid, well-executed neural rethinking of the entity grid with clear empirical benefits and good potential for extension. The main concerns are limited novelty, missing preprocessing details, and lack of ablation/analysis. Addressing these would strengthen the paper.
