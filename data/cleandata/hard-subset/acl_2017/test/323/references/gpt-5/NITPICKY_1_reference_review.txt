Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid. Each grid cell (grammatical role and optional entity-specific features) is embedded; 1D convolutions run along entity columns to capture transitions of length k; max-pooling aggregates features; a linear layer outputs a coherence score trained with a pairwise ranking loss. The authors report consistent gains over non-neural grid baselines on WSJ discrimination and insertion, and on DUC’03 summary coherence rating; they also show further improvements via pretraining.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A CNN-based architecture over entity grids for learning distributed transition features and task-specific scoring via pairwise ranking, yielding improvements over classic and extended grids.
2) A simple (if somewhat ad hoc) way to incorporate entity-specific features by composing them with grammatical role symbols, and the demonstration that even a small subset of features yields gains.
3) An empirical study across three evaluation tasks with multiple runs and statistical testing on WSJ sentence ordering.

Strengths
1) Empirical improvements on strong baselines. The model improves over “grid-all-nouns” and the extended entity grid on both discrimination (~+4% and ~+3.7% absolute, respectively) and insertion (up to +2.5% absolute), with significance testing for WSJ. This suggests the neuralized transition representation is effective even with limited features.

2) End-to-end pairwise training on the target task. Using a hinge ranking loss and shared parameters for document pairs is appropriate for coherence ranking and aligns with how entity grid models are used. The pretraining-to-finetuning setup for the DUC task is sensible and appears to help.

3) Sensible inductive bias and simple architecture. Convolving along entity columns encodes the key grid intuition (local transitions) while enabling longer windows (k up to 5–8) without the explicit combinatorial explosion of discrete transition tuples.

4) Evaluation practice details. The authors tune hyperparameters on a dev set, run multiple random seeds, and report approximate randomization tests. They also describe search ranges and give final chosen hyperparameters, which helps reproducibility.

5) Minimal feature engineering. The approach achieves gains while using only three easily computable entity features for the extended neural model, supporting the claim that distributed representations reduce the need for elaborate feature design.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior neural coherence work and incremental methodological contribution. Replacing discrete transition counting with a CNN over embedded role symbols is conceptually straightforward and closely parallels prior neural coherence methods (e.g., Li & Hovy 2014’s neural coherence with sentence encoders). The paper does not clearly articulate what is fundamentally new beyond applying CNNs to grid columns, and lacks analysis showing that longer transitions (k) are truly responsible for gains.

2) Reproducibility and preprocessing under-specified. The paper does not specify which tools/cascades are used to build grids (tokenization, sentence splitting, POS/parse, role assignment, and especially coreference). Confusingly, it both mentions use of a coreference resolver and “matching nouns” to detect coreference. How are non-head nouns assigned roles? Are pronouns treated as entities? This matters greatly for grid quality and comparability to prior work.

3) Feature composition contradicts stated advantages. The “entity-specific features” are attached by concatenating feature and role into a single symbol (e.g., PERSON-S), which expands the vocabulary and partially reintroduces sparsity—the very limitation the authors decry. An alternative (and more principled) approach would embed each feature separately and combine via concatenation/sum. The paper provides no quantification of vocabulary growth, no ablation comparing composite-symbol vs. compositional embeddings, and no analysis of the trade-off.

4) Incomplete baselines and comparisons. The paper excludes Li & Hovy (2014) due to poor reproduction without deeper investigation, parameter sweeps, or re-implementation; this weakens the claim of state of the art. It also omits comparisons to RST/discourse-based coherence models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) on the same WSJ split. The graph-based baseline is included, but configuration details are minimal. Overall, the comparative picture is incomplete.

5) Methodological and presentation issues. 
- Convolution indexing seems inconsistent: after stating columns are independent, the feature map length is given as “m.n+k−1,” which is dimensionally odd; per entity it should be (m+k−1), aggregated over n entities. Clarify implementation.
- No ablations: effect of k, pooling length, number of filters, dropout location, and entity features are not analyzed. No error analysis or examples.
- DUC results are on a tiny test set (80 pairs); no significance is reported. 
- Several formatting problems: references contain “?, \Q2012\E” placeholders; figure/table cross-references are incomplete (Figure 2 is crucial but not shown; Table 4 referenced but not included); footnotes are oddly placed; some prose is informal (“We were not sure what could go wrong.”).
- No discussion of runtime/memory or sensitivity to document length/#entities.

Questions to authors
1) Preprocessing: Precisely which tools and configurations were used for sentence splitting, parsing, role assignment, and coreference? Are all nouns (including non-heads and pronouns) treated as entities? How are grammatical roles assigned to non-head nouns?

2) Convolution details: Is the convolution strictly along the sentence axis within each entity column? Please correct/clarify the feature map length expression and how maps across entities are aggregated before pooling.

3) Entity features: How many unique composite symbols are created in Extended Grid-CNN (vocabulary size)? Did you try compositional embeddings (separate embeddings per feature plus role) rather than symbol concatenation? Any ablation on which of the three features drive gains?

4) Longer transitions: Provide ablations varying k and show performance vs. transition length. Are the gains due to longer k or simply to distributed smoothing over short transitions?

5) Baselines: Can you include stronger neural baselines (e.g., re-implemented Li & Hovy with modern optimization), and RST/discourse-based baselines on the same splits? If not, justify the “state of the art” claim more cautiously.

6) DUC significance: Are the improvements on DUC significant? Please report p-values, and detail the finetuning setup (epochs, learning rate, regularization).

7) Resources: What is the training/inference time and memory footprint for WSJ? How does performance scale with document length and number of entities?

Overall, the idea is plausible and results are promising, but the paper has many missing details, incomplete baselines, and presentation issues that, taken together, undermine confidence in the contribution.
