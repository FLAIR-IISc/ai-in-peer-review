Summary
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that operates over entity grids. Each grid cell (grammatical role of an entity in a sentence, optionally augmented with entity-specific features) is embedded into a vector; convolutional filters slide along entity transitions to capture longer-range patterns; max pooling summarizes salient patterns; and a linear layer outputs a document coherence score. The model is trained with pairwise ranking. The approach is evaluated on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating, showing consistent gains over entity-grid baselines and a graph-based model. Pretraining on WSJ improves performance on the small DUC’03 task.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A neural convolutional model over entity grids that learns task-specific, distributed representations of entity transitions and can model longer transition windows than traditional grid models.
2) Empirical state-of-the-art results on standard coherence evaluations (WSJ discrimination and insertion; DUC’03 summary coherence), with consistent, statistically significant gains over strong entity-grid baselines.
3) A simple, practical mechanism to include entity-specific features within the neural grid framework, and evidence that even a subset of features suffices to outperform richer non-neural grids.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture that addresses known limitations of entity grids. Using embeddings and convolution over entity transitions directly targets sparsity and short-transition constraints in classic grid models. The choice of wide convolution and max pooling yields position-invariant detectors for salient transition patterns, which is appropriate for local coherence cues.

- Strong empirical results with careful experimental protocol. On WSJ, Grid-CNN outperforms the basic all-nouns grid by ~4% (discrimination) and ~1% (insertion); Extended Grid-CNN improves over Extended Grid by ~3.7% (88.69 vs 84.95) in discrimination and ~2.5% in insertion, with significance testing. On DUC’03, pretraining on WSJ notably improves summary rating results, supporting the claim that learned representations transfer across tasks.

- End-to-end training aligned with the target tasks. Pairwise ranking is appropriate for coherence scoring and integrates feature learning with the downstream objective. Using multiple random seeds and early stopping on a held-out dev set increases reliability of reported numbers.

- Practicality and simplicity. The model requires only an entity grid and standard neural components; it avoids complex structured objectives. The inclusion of only three easily computable entity features still outperforms a richer non-neural baseline, suggesting robustness and a favorable accuracy/complexity trade-off. Code availability is a plus for reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work; incremental advance. The contribution is largely a “neuralization” of the entity grid with a straightforward CNN. While effective, the architectural novelty is modest, and related neural coherence work (e.g., Li & Hovy 2014) is only briefly discussed. A deeper analysis clarifying why convolution over entity transitions (vs. other neural architectures) is specifically advantageous, or stronger conceptual links to discourse theory, would strengthen the paper’s intellectual contribution.

- Insufficient analysis and ablations. There is little exploration of what the model learns: no ablations on transition length k, pooling size, number of filters, embedding initialization, or the impact of each entity-specific feature. No qualitative analysis (e.g., visualizing filters or examples) is provided. This makes it hard to assess which aspects drive the gains and how generalizable/sensitive the model is.

- Dependence on preprocessing (coreference and parsing) is not examined. The approach inherits the known sensitivity of grid models to coreference quality and syntactic role assignment. The paper does not specify the tools used, report error rates, or test robustness to noise. This is important for downstream deployment and cross-domain generalization.

- Evaluation scope and comparators. Results are presented primarily on WSJ; AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES are dismissed for size/style, but additional domains or languages would improve claims of generality. The exclusion of Li & Hovy (2014) due to poor reproduction is noted but unsatisfying; at minimum, report your reproduced numbers with caveats or include a discussion of mismatches (data, preprocessing, hyperparameters). Comparisons to discourse-relation-based models (e.g., RST-informed) are discussed but not experimentally contrasted on the same setup.

- Task mismatch for insertion. The model is trained on discrimination pairs but evaluated on insertion, which likely underutilizes its capacity for the insertion task; the authors note this but do not attempt insertion-specific training or adaptation. A direct training/evaluation alignment could yield stronger evidence and possibly larger gains.

Questions to Authors
- What coreference resolver and parser were used to build the grids? Do your results change materially with a different resolver or with degraded coreference quality?

- How sensitive is performance to transition window size k and pooling length p? Please provide an ablation varying k and p, and report the best k you found, especially since you cite using k up to 5–8.

- What is the per-feature contribution of the three entity-specific features in Extended Grid-CNN? Do they remain helpful when combined with longer k?

- Does max pooling over the entire transition map overly discard positional information? Have you tried k-max pooling or attention to preserve more structure?

- For insertion, did you try fine-tuning the model on insertion-specific pairs or augmenting training with “near-miss” permutations? If yes, how did it affect results?

- Can you provide qualitative examples where CNN filters capture interpretable transition patterns, and failure cases where the model is misled?

- How many entities per document are included on average, and do you prune low-salience entities? How does performance vary with document length and number of entities?

Suggestions for Improvement
- Add ablation studies (k, p, filters, embeddings; per-feature analysis), robustness checks to coreference noise, and qualitative examinations of learned filters/patterns.

- Align training with insertion (e.g., curriculum with increasingly similar permutations) and report gains.

- Provide a careful comparison or at least a reconciled reproduction discussion with Li & Hovy (2014); optionally add a discourse-relation baseline where feasible.

- Explore lightweight mechanisms to incorporate discourse relations (RST cues) into the CNN, and/or attention over entities, to move beyond incremental neuralization.
