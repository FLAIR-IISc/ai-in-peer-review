Summary and contributions
- Primary: A conceptually clean neuralization of the entity grid: the paper replaces discrete transition counts with learned embeddings and a 1D convolution + max-pooling over entity-transition columns, trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss. This enables modeling longer transitions without sparsity and learning task-specific features.
- Secondary: A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features by composing them with roles into the grid vocabulary, and transfer/fine-tuning across tasks (WSJ→DUC summaries).
- Tertiary: Strong empirical results on three benchmarks (WSJ discrimination/insertion, DUC’03 summary coherence rating), with consistent, statistically significant gains over strong grid baselines and a graph-based method, and solid experimental practices (multiple runs, significance testing, hyperparameter details).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: Casting local coherence modeling as convolution over embedded entity-role transitions is a compelling, bold reframing of the popular entity grid paradigm. It addresses the core limitations of discrete transition counts (sparsity with longer k; task-agnostic feature engineering) with a minimal, elegant architecture that learns distributed representations and long-range patterns. The independence assumption by columns keeps the model faithful to the original grid while enabling deep learning benefits.
- Consistent, meaningful gains across tasks: On WSJ, the neural grid improves over the “all-nouns” grid by ~4 points on discrimination (81.58→85.57) and ~1 point on insertion (22.13→23.12), and over the extended grid by ~3.7–3.8 points on discrimination (84.95→88.69) and ~2.7 points on insertion (23.28→25.95). On DUC’03 summary rating, fine-tuning pre-trained models yields additional improvements (reported 2.5–3.7 points). The results are averaged over seeds and supported by significance tests.
- End-to-end, task-specific training: The pairwise ranking objective and differentiable pipeline (embeddings, convolutional filters, pooling) allow the model to learn features directly optimized for the downstream task, avoiding the task-agnostic counting of previous grids. The transfer from WSJ to DUC shows practical utility of pretraining.
- Simplicity and extensibility: The model is easy to implement, requires no elaborate discourse parsing, and naturally accommodates longer transitions and arbitrary entity features. The authors also report practical hyperparameters and training choices and use widely available toolkits; this bodes well for reproducibility and adoption.
- Experimental rigor and baselines: The paper replicates strong grid baselines using a public toolkit, includes a graph-based comparator, tunes on a held-out dev set, reports detailed settings, and runs multiple seeds. The dataset choice (large WSJ) is justified for learning neural models.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling of inter-entity interactions and global discourse: The model assumes independence across entity columns and only models local transitions per entity. Many discourse phenomena hinge on interactions between entities and higher-level relations; the architecture cannot capture cross-entity dependencies or global structure beyond max-pooling. This is a conceptual limitation compared to models that integrate discourse relations or joint entity dynamics.
- Reliance on coreference quality without analysis: The approach inherits all biases and errors of the coreference resolver and NP detection. There is no sensitivity study (e.g., different resolvers, gold vs. automatic) or error analysis to quantify the impact, which is important for robustness and domain transfer.
- Feature encoding strategy may reintroduce sparsity: Encoding entity-specific features by collapsing them into composite symbols (e.g., PERSON-S) inflates the vocabulary V and could counteract the generalization benefits of embedding, especially as more features are added. The paper does not explore more principled, factorized alternatives (separate embeddings per feature and role with composition) nor ablates the contributions of each feature.
- Task mismatch and missing learning objectives: Models are trained on discrimination pairs but evaluated on insertion; gains on insertion are smaller, and the paper itself notes this. There is no attempt to train directly on an insertion-oriented objective or to incorporate candidate-position contrastive training, which could strengthen claims about task generality.
- Incomplete baseline coverage and analysis: The comparison to sentence-representation neural coherence (Li & Hovy, 2014) is dismissed due to a failed replication with poor performance but without deeper investigation. Additional modern baselines (e.g., RNN/CNN over sentence order windows, or discourse-relation-based neural models) and qualitative analyses (interpreting learned filters/patterns, error cases) would solidify the contribution. Computational efficiency and parameter counts are also not reported.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice and quality of the coreference resolver? Can you report results with an alternative resolver or gold coref (if available) to bound the impact?
- Have you tried training with an insertion-oriented ranking objective (e.g., contrast among candidate insertion positions per sentence)? Does this improve insertion beyond the current gains?
- When composing entity-specific features into the vocabulary, what is the resulting |V| for Extended Grid-CNN, and how does performance vary as you add features? Have you tried factorized embeddings (e.g., role embedding + NE-type embedding + salience embedding) instead of concatenated symbols?
- Do multiple filter widths (k) in parallel improve results over a single best k? Can you provide an ablation over k and pooling size p to validate the “longer transition” claim quantitatively?
- Can you provide qualitative analyses: examples of transition patterns captured by high-activation filters, and common failure modes?
- What are the parameter counts, training times, and inference throughput compared to the SVM grid? How does the approach scale with document length and number of entities?
- Have you evaluated out-of-domain robustness (e.g., non-WSJ news or different genres), given the model’s reliance on coref and syntax?

Overall, this is a timely and novel rethinking of entity-grid coherence modeling with a simple, effective neural architecture. Despite some analysis gaps and modeling simplifications, the conceptual contribution and consistent empirical gains make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
