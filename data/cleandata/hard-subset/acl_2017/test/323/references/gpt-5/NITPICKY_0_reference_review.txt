Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a neuralized entity-grid model for local coherence. It embeds grid symbols (grammatical roles and optionally entity features), applies 1D convolution across sentence-wise transitions per entity column, max-pools, and uses a pairwise ranking objective to score documents. It evaluates on WSJ discrimination and insertion, and DUC’03 summary coherence rating, reporting consistent improvements over classic entity-grid baselines and a graph-based method. Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple CNN-over-entity-grid architecture that enables longer transition modeling and end-to-end training with pairwise ranking.
2) Empirical results showing consistent gains over non-neural entity-grid baselines on three tasks; pretraining on WSJ helps transfer to summary coherence rating.
3) A practical way to incorporate a subset of entity-specific features by composing them with role symbols (e.g., PERSON-S).

Strengths
- Conceptually clear neuralization of a well-known paradigm. Recasting entity grids in a distributed, convolutional framework is natural and addresses sparsity for longer transitions (k up to 8), while preserving the grid’s inductive bias. The pairwise ranking loss is appropriate for coherence scoring.
- Consistent empirical gains and reasonably careful training. The model improves 4% absolute on discrimination and up to 2.5% on insertion relative to non-neural grids; summary rating also improves, further boosted by pretraining. Multiple runs with different seeds and approximate randomization tests are reported.
- Sensible transfer learning result. Pretraining on WSJ discrimination followed by fine-tuning on DUC’03 improves summary coherence scoring in a low-data setting, supporting the claim that the model learns reusable coherence features.
- Practicality. The approach avoids explosion in feature space even when adding a few entity-specific attributes, and training details (optimization, dropout, early stopping, hyperparameter search) are provided at a high level.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient preprocessing detail and reproducibility concerns. Critical components are underspecified:
  • How are entities extracted? Do you rely on true coreference resolution or lexical matching? The text alternates between “NPs + coref” and “all nouns,” but the experiments use Brown toolkit settings without specifying the coref/NP detection pipeline (parser, coref system, NER tool, sentence splitter), thresholds, or parameterization.
  • For the extended features: which NER model and tagset? How is “proper mention” operationalized? How is salience discretized/encoded? What is the final vocabulary size V after composing features with roles?
  • Without these, results are hard to reproduce and comparisons to prior entity-grid results may be confounded by preprocessing differences.
- Ambiguities and inconsistencies in the model description:
  • The convolution indexing and dimensions are unclear (e.g., hi length “m.n+k−1” is nonsensical—do you mean n columns each with length m+k−1, then concatenated?).
  • You assert columns are independent but then pool across feature maps that aggregate across all columns; clarify whether pooling mixes columns or stays within-column before aggregation.
  • The model uses a dedicated symbol “-” for entity absence, but boundary padding is done with zero vectors, not the “-” embedding, leading to two semantically different “absences.” This discrepancy can bias filters near boundaries.
  • Max-pooling with large receptive fields destroys positional information, which seems at odds with insertion; this is acknowledged but not ablated.
- Baselines and fairness:
  • Li & Hovy (2014) is excluded because it “performed badly” with your reimplementation; this should be reported as a baseline with analysis, not omitted. Alternatively, collaborate with authors or use their reported settings/datasets.
  • For summary coherence, the extended grid baseline is missing (“could not be trained pairwise”) even though SVM preference ranking is standard; at minimum, include a non-pairwise trained grid baseline on that task for parity.
  • Stronger discourse-informed baselines (e.g., RST-based transitions) are not included on WSJ, despite citing them; the “state of the art” claim is thus overstated.
- Limited analysis/ablation:
  • No ablations on filter width k, multi-scale filters, pooling size, or the contribution of each added entity feature. No analysis of sensitivity to grid noise (coref errors), or to k beyond a single best choice.
  • No qualitative analysis of learned filters/patterns or error cases (e.g., insertion failures).
- Presentation and formatting issues that impede clarity:
  • Numerous typos and grammar (“loosing,” “gain in insertion is less verbose”), broken or incomplete citations with “?,” missing Table 4 in the text, repeated/misplaced figure references, footnotes inserted mid-paragraph, and unclear table headers (Table 3 has “Discr. Ins. Acc F1” but only two tasks are described).
  • Code is “released with the submission” but no anonymized link or artifact description; this conflicts with anonymity and is unhelpful for reproducibility.

Questions to authors
1) Precisely specify the preprocessing pipeline for WSJ and DUC: sentence segmentation, POS/parse, entity extraction, coreference resolution (system, version, settings), NER (model/tagset), and how you cluster mentions into entities when using “all nouns.”
2) How do you encode extended features: what values, how composed with roles, vocabulary size |V|, and do you share embeddings across feature types?
3) Clarify convolution: over which dimension(s) is it applied, how are columns handled, what is the exact shape of each feature map, and does pooling aggregate across columns or remain per-column before concatenation?
4) Why use zero-padding instead of the learned “-” embedding for out-of-range positions? Did you try padding with the “-” vector?
5) Provide full ablations: (a) k ∈ {2..8}, (b) multi-k filters, (c) pooling sizes, (d) with/without each entity feature, (e) training on insertion pairs. Does preserving some positional information (e.g., k-max pooling, attention) help insertion?
6) Include Li & Hovy (2014) results with careful tuning or explain, with evidence, why it fails on your setup. For DUC summary rating, include a grid baseline trained as in the original work.
7) Please correct and complete tables/figures and references, and provide an anonymized code/data artifact sufficient to reproduce results.

Decision: Weak Reject. The core idea is sound and the empirical gains are promising, but the paper has too many methodological ambiguities, incomplete baselines, and presentation problems to meet ACL standards without substantial revision.
