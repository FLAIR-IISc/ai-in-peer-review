Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neuralized variant of the entity grid for modeling local coherence. The model embeds grid symbols (grammatical roles, optionally augmented with entity-specific features) and applies 1D convolutions over per-entity transitions to learn higher-level coherence patterns; a pairwise ranking loss is used for end-to-end, task-specific training. The approach is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) using WSJ/PTB and on summary coherence rating (DUC’03), showing consistent gains over non-neural grid baselines and a graph-based method; pretraining on WSJ further boosts summary rating.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength:
1) A simple, effective neural architecture that “neuralizes” the entity grid: distributed role/feature embeddings with convolution over entity transitions, enabling longer transition modeling and task-coupled learning via pairwise ranking.
2) Empirical evidence of consistent improvements and new SOTA on three standard coherence evaluations, including transfer via pretraining from sentence ordering to summary rating.
3) A practical path to incorporate entity-specific features into the neural grid without external resources, plus a transparent training/evaluation setup (multiple runs, significance testing), and code release.

Strengths
1) Clear empirical improvements across tasks and settings. On WSJ discrimination, Grid-CNN improves ~4 points over Grid-all-nouns (81.58→85.57), and Extended Grid-CNN improves ~3.7 points over Extended Grid (84.95→88.69), with significance testing. Gains persist for insertion and for DUC’03 summary rating (with further boosts from pretraining). Consistency across datasets and tasks supports the robustness of the approach.
2) Addresses key limitations of classic entity grids. The use of distributed representations and convolution directly tackles sparsity and short-transition constraints (k ≤ 3) in prior grids. The model trains end-to-end toward the ranking objective, alleviating the task-agnostic feature extraction issue highlighted by prior work.
3) Methodological clarity and reproducibility. The paper details training choices (RMSprop, dropout, early stopping), hyperparameter search ranges and selected values (Table 2), multi-seed runs, and approximate randomization tests. It also reimplements/replicates standard baselines (browncoherence and cohere toolkits) on WSJ, an established, larger benchmark than AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES, which strengthens comparability.
4) Simplicity and extensibility. The architecture (lookup → conv → max-pool → linear scoring) is easy to implement, efficient, and modular. It provides a straightforward mechanism to add entity-level features without heavy engineering or external corpora, yet already outperforms richer non-neural feature sets.
5) Positive transfer across tasks. The pretraining from WSJ sentence ordering to DUC’03, followed by fine-tuning, is a useful demonstration that the learned coherence representations carry over beyond the original domain/task.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty is moderate. While neuralizing the grid with convolution is sensible and effective, the conceptual step from classic grids to embedded symbols + 1D conv is relatively incremental, given prior neural coherence models (e.g., Li & Hovy 2014) and extensive use of CNNs for local pattern detection in NLP. A stronger case for novelty would come from modeling cross-entity interactions or moving beyond strictly local transitions.
2) Independence across entity columns may miss important interactions. The model treats each entity’s transition stream independently and pools locally, ignoring patterns involving multiple entities (e.g., role exchanges, bridging, parallelism). This design choice likely limits the capture of richer discourse phenomena and global coherence.
3) Incorporation of entity-specific features may reintroduce sparsity. Concatenating features to role symbols (e.g., PERSON-S) expands the vocabulary V and could partly negate the benefits of distributed parameter sharing. The paper does not quantify the resulting |V|, OOV handling, or compare this design with alternative feature fusion (e.g., separate embeddings and composition).
4) Evaluation scope and baselines. While WSJ is appropriate and large, the evaluation omits direct comparisons to the strongest RST/discourse relation models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) on the same split, even though they are discussed as state-of-the-art. Insertion gains are smaller, and the model is not trained explicitly for insertion. A task-specific training variant would be a fairer comparison. DUC’03 is small; more domains (e.g., scientific articles, Wikipedia) would better test generalization.
5) Clarity/analysis gaps. Table 3 appears to report identical values for discrimination and insertion, which is unexpected given their different metrics; this needs clarification. There is limited qualitative/error analysis (e.g., what patterns the filters capture, failure cases, coreference error sensitivity), and limited ablation on transition length k, pooling length p, and the contribution of each entity feature. Implementation details for coreference and parsing tools (and their accuracy) are not specified, yet they are critical for grid quality.

Questions to authors
- What coreference resolver, parser, and NP head rules were used to build the grids? How sensitive are results to coreference quality (e.g., using a weaker/stronger resolver)?
- How large is the vocabulary V when entity-specific features are appended to roles? Did you try separate embeddings for role and feature types with learned composition, and how did that compare?
- What is the exact transition length k chosen per task, and how do results vary with k and pooling length p? Any evidence that longer k (e.g., 7–8) improves beyond 5–6?
- For insertion, did you try training with an insertion-specific ranking objective (e.g., contrasting candidate insertion positions) and does it improve over discrimination-trained models?
- Can you clarify Table 3: are the discrimination and insertion numbers indeed identical, or is this a formatting error? What metrics were used for insertion?
- Did you attempt to include simple cross-entity interactions (e.g., 2D convolutions across entities and sentences, or attention across columns)? If so, what were the findings?
- For DUC’03, how many parameters were fine-tuned vs. frozen during transfer? Any domain adaptation strategies beyond fine-tuning?

Overall, the paper presents a well-executed, practically useful neural coherence model with consistent gains. Addressing the analysis gaps, clarifying evaluation metrics, and exploring cross-entity/global structures would strengthen both novelty and impact.
