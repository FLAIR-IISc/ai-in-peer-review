Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid: grammatical roles and entity-specific features are embedded; a 1D convolution over column-wise entity transitions composes higher-level patterns; max-pooling and a linear layer produce a coherence score. Training uses pairwise ranking.
- It shows how to incorporate entity-specific features in a distributed way (e.g., NE type, salience, properness) without the combinatorial explosion of traditional grids.
- It reports consistent improvements over strong grid baselines on three established tasks (WSJ discrimination and insertion; DUC’03 summary coherence rating), with code release.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, effective convolutional architecture over entity-grid columns that enables longer transition modeling and end-to-end learning for coherence.
2) A principled way to encode entity-specific features into the grid vocabulary and learn their embeddings jointly.
3) Strong empirical results with careful training/tuning and improvements over state-of-the-art entity-grid baselines.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and plausibility: The convolution over entity-transition columns directly targets the core intuition behind grid models (patterns of role transitions), while addressing sparsity/curse-of-dimensionality by learning distributed representations and filters. The use of wide convolutions and pooling is well motivated to capture position-invariant patterns and longer dependencies than k ≤ 3 in traditional grids.
- Empirical performance and breadth of evaluation: The model consistently improves over multiple, relevant baselines (grid-all-nouns and extended grid) on discrimination (+~4% absolute) and insertion (+up to 2.5%), and on summary coherence rating (+>4% with pretraining). Results are averaged over multiple random seeds with significance testing. The WSJ train/test split is substantially larger than prior small-domain corpora, supporting robustness claims.
- End-to-end training and task adaptivity: Pairwise ranking over document pairs ties feature learning to the downstream scoring objective, addressing a key limitation of hand-engineered grid features. Pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning for summaries is a sensible demonstration of transfer.
- Practicality and reproducibility: The architecture is lightweight (lookup, conv, pool, linear), hyperparameters are tuned on a held-out dev set, and implementation details are transparent (Theano, RMSprop, dropout, Glorot init). Code release is a strong plus for reproducibility.
- Strong baselines and fair comparisons within the grid family: The paper compares against both grid-all-nouns and the extended grid with 9 features (the top published grid variant), as well as a graph-based unsupervised method, making the claims within the entity-grid paradigm credible.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope of baselines beyond entity grids: The evaluation omits competitive discourse-structure-based models (e.g., RST-based coherence metrics) and does not include a strong sentence-based neural coherence model as originally published (the attempt to reproduce Li & Hovy 2014 yielded poor performance and was omitted). This makes it harder to situate gains relative to non-grid families.
- Modeling assumptions restrict interactions: Treating columns independently ignores cross-entity interactions (e.g., competition or co-reference chains across entities) that can be informative for coherence. A brief justification is given, but there is no empirical analysis of the impact of this independence assumption or attempts to model cross-entity patterns.
- Analysis and ablations are limited: While the paper shows “grid-CNN” versus “extended grid-CNN,” it lacks ablations on key design choices (transition window size k, pooling size, number of filters, effect of each entity-specific feature, or the benefit of pretraining embeddings vs. random). Moreover, there is no qualitative analysis of learned filters/patterns to substantiate interpretability claims (e.g., which transitions are captured).
- Reliance on coreference and parsing without robustness checks: The approach depends on entity detection and coreference resolution; yet the paper does not specify which coreference resolver/parser is used, nor assess robustness to their errors. This is a known confounder for grid models and could affect the generality of results.
- Task/data limitations for insertion and summaries: The model is trained on discrimination but evaluated on insertion; the authors themselves note this mismatch. A dedicated training regime for insertion might yield larger gains and should be included. The summary rating dataset is small; while pretraining helps, effect sizes and significance are not fully documented, and the choice of features for summaries is not analyzed.

Questions to authors
- What coreference resolution and parsing tools/settings were used to build the grids? How sensitive are results to different coreference systems or to coreference quality (e.g., on noisy domains)?
- Did you try modeling cross-entity interactions (e.g., 2D convolutions over the full grid, attention across columns), and if so, how did they perform?
- Can you provide ablations on (i) transition window size k, (ii) pooling size, (iii) number of filters, and (iv) the contribution of each entity-specific feature in the extended model?
- For the summary rating task, are the improvements statistically significant? What is the impact of pretraining the embeddings only vs. all parameters?
- Have you experimented with training the model directly on insertion-style pairs (same document with different single-sentence positions)? If yes, how does that compare?
- How long does training/inference take, and what is the memory footprint relative to extended grids? Any issues with very long documents?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-motivated paper that advances entity-grid coherence modeling by leveraging neural representations and convolution to overcome sparsity and short-range limitations. The improvements over strong grid baselines and the clean architecture make it a valuable contribution. The main reservations concern the breadth of comparisons beyond grids, lack of deeper analysis, and limited handling of cross-entity interactions and coreference robustness. I lean toward acceptance, especially for its clear step forward within a widely used paradigm and its practical impact.
