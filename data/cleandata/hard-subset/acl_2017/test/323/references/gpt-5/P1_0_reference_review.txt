Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neuralized variant of the entity grid for modeling local discourse coherence. The model embeds grid symbols (grammatical roles optionally augmented with entity features) and applies 1D convolutions over columns (entity transitions) with max-pooling, trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss. Experiments on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating show consistent improvements over entity-grid baselines and a graph-based method.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical: A simple CNN-over-grid architecture that consistently and significantly improves over strong entity-grid baselines on three established coherence evaluations, including on WSJ where scale matters. The gains are robust (multiple seeds, significance tested) and code is released.
2) Methodological: A task-trained, distributed representation of entity transitions that supports longer transition windows than traditional discrete grids and can incorporate entity-specific features without combinatorial explosion.
3) Transfer: A practical demonstration that pretraining on sentence-ordering transfers to summary coherence rating, improving performance in low-data settings.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements with careful protocol. The model yields absolute gains of ~4% (discrimination), up to 2.5% (insertion), and >3–4% (summary rating), with significance testing and averages over multiple seeds. Hyperparameters are tuned on a held-out dev set; early stopping and dropout are used; RMSprop training is standard and appropriate. Using the large WSJ split improves reliability over smaller, stylistically constrained corpora and enables a fairer assessment of learning.
- Simple, well-motivated architecture with clear inductive bias. Convolving over entity transitions directly addresses the sparsity that afflicts discrete transition counts beyond k≈3, enabling longer-range patterns (k up to 5–8). Max pooling provides location invariance over columns and highlights salient local patterns. End-to-end pairwise ranking aligns with evaluation tasks and the traditional framing of coherence as ranking.
- Incorporation of entity-specific features with minimal complexity. Encoding features by symbol composition (e.g., PERSON-S) plus embeddings avoids feature explosion. Despite using only a subset (3) of the extended grid features, the neural model surpasses the full extended grid, indicating the value of distributed representations and shared parameters across documents.
- Practical transfer to low-resource evaluation. Pretraining on WSJ discrimination followed by fine-tuning on DUC summary rating boosts performance further, illustrating reusability of learned transition representations across coherence tasks.
- Reproducibility. The paper reports dataset splits, permutations protocol, model choices, search ranges, optimization details, and significance testing, and states that code is released.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond “neuralizing” a well-known model. The core idea—embedding grid symbols and using standard CNNs with pooling—is a straightforward application of widely used components. While the empirical gains are solid, the methodological advance is incremental. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis explaining what the model learns that discrete grids cannot.
- Incomplete and uneven baselines. The paper excludes the Li & Hovy (2014) sentence-embedding model due to poor replication without analysis, and does not include other strong, relevant baselines: (a) learning-to-rank improvements over grids (Feng & Hirst, 2012), and (b) discourse-relation-based models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) that have reported competitive or superior results in some settings. Given the SOTA claim, comparisons to those baselines (or a substantiated explanation for omission) are necessary.
- Missing ablations and analysis supporting key claims. The paper argues the ability to model “longer transitions” and the usefulness of entity features, but provides only two system variants (with/without features). There is no systematic study varying transition window size k, pooling strategy (overlap vs non-overlap), number of filters, embedding size, or the contribution of each entity feature. An analysis of learned filters/patterns and error cases would strengthen the understanding of what drives the gains.
- Dependency on coreference and parsing not discussed. The approach relies on accurate NP identification and coreference resolution, yet the paper does not specify which tools are used nor quantify sensitivity to their errors. This affects generality and practical deployment, especially across domains.
- Task mismatch and modeling assumptions. The model assumes independence across entity columns and location-invariance within columns. These assumptions may hurt tasks where precise positional information and inter-entity interactions matter (e.g., insertion). Moreover, despite emphasizing end-to-end task training, the insertion results are reported using a model trained on discrimination. Training directly on insertion-style pairs would better support the “task-specific” claim.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you include (or discuss) results against Feng & Hirst (2012) and the discourse-relation-based models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) on the same WSJ split? If not feasible, please justify and contextualize your SOTA claim.
- Core NLP pipeline: Which parser and coreference resolver did you use? How sensitive are results to these choices? Any experiments with noisier or alternative systems?
- Ablations: Please report performance as a function of transition window size k and pooling stride/overlap, and ablate each entity feature (NE type, salience, proper mention). Do multiple filter widths in parallel help?
- Insertion training: If you train the model with pairwise examples specific to insertion (placing a removed sentence at candidate slots), do results improve? This would substantiate the end-to-end, task-specific learning claim.
- Analysis: Can you provide qualitative examples of learned filter patterns (e.g., prototypical transition n-grams) and failure cases to illuminate model behavior and limitations?
- Summary rating: What exact metric and absolute numbers are achieved (and significance)? Does pretraining on WSJ always help across clusters, or only some?

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed paper with meaningful empirical gains and a simple, practical method. Addressing the missing baselines and providing ablations/analysis would solidify the contribution and the SOTA claim.
