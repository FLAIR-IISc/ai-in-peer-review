Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neuralized variant of the entity grid for local coherence modeling. Grammatical roles and entity-specific features are embedded; a 1D convolution is applied independently over each entity’s column to detect long-range transition patterns; max-pooling yields salient features used in a pairwise ranking objective to score coherence. The model is evaluated on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence ranking, achieving consistent gains over non-neural grid models and a graph-based baseline.

Main contributions as I see them:
1) Method: A simple, effective CNN over entity-grid columns that replaces sparsity-prone discrete transition counts with distributed representations, enabling longer transition windows and end-to-end training via pairwise ranking.
2) Empirical evidence: Strong, consistent improvements on standard coherence evaluations (WSJ discrimination/insertion; DUC’03 summary ranking), including transfer/fine-tuning from WSJ to DUC.
3) Practicality: Demonstrates that modest entity-specific features (NE type, salience, proper mention) suffice within the neural framework to outperform stronger non-neural extended grids.

Strengths
- Clear, motivated architecture that targets known limitations of entity grids. The move from discrete transition histograms to convolution over learned embeddings directly addresses sparsity and the k-length transition explosion, and the independence-by-column assumption matches the original grid philosophy, making the extension principled and easy to interpret.
- Strong baselines and fair experimental setup on widely used datasets. The paper reimplements the classic and extended entity grid using a public toolkit and compares against a graph-based method. Improvements are consistent: roughly +4% absolute on discrimination, up to +2.5% on insertion, and >3–4% on summary coherence (with pretraining). Statistical testing is reported for WSJ.
- End-to-end training with pairwise ranking. Using pairwise hinge loss mirrors prior learning-to-rank formulations while allowing joint optimization of embeddings, filters, and scoring. The transfer learning experiment (pretrain on WSJ discrimination, fine-tune on DUC) is appropriate for data-scarce coherence rating and shows clear benefit.
- Sensible engineering choices and reporting. Hyperparameter searches, dropout, wide convolution with zero-padding to capture boundaries, and multiple random seeds with averaging are good practice. The discussion of window sizes (k ≈ 5–8) and pooling is aligned with the intended capacity to capture longer transitions.
- Simplicity and extensibility. The model is compact, uses only dependency/coref-derived grids plus a few entity features, yet still beats stronger feature-rich baselines. It should be easy to adopt and extend (e.g., integrating discourse relations in future work).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural coherence work; missing ablations. While operating on entity grids rather than sentence embeddings differentiates this from Li & Hovy (2014), the contribution is an incremental neuralization of a classic representation using standard CNNs. The paper would benefit from deeper ablations: effect of transition window k, pooling length, embedding size, number of filters; contribution of each entity-specific feature; and sensitivity to wide vs narrow convolution.
- Independence across entities discards important cross-entity interactions. Treating columns independently ignores phenomena like entity competition or coordination between entity chains that often underlie coherence. A 2D architecture (across sentences and entities) or attention across columns may capture cross-entity patterns; at minimum, a discussion or analysis showing when independence suffices vs fails would help.
- Evaluation scope and task realism. Discrimination against random permutations favors models exploiting shallow cues and length effects; insertion is harder but still synthetic. The DUC summary task is very small (6 clusters), risking instability; stronger evidence on more realistic downstream tasks (e.g., neural sentence ordering benchmarks, coherence-aware generation evaluation) or domain transfer would strengthen the claims.
- Coreference and preprocessing dependencies are under-specified. The approach depends critically on accurate coref and syntactic roles, especially when using all nouns and non-head mentions. The paper does not specify the coreference resolver/parsing pipeline, nor analyze robustness to coref noise. Error analysis on cases with coref mistakes would be useful.
- Baseline coverage and fairness concerns. The exclusion of Li & Hovy (2014) because of poor replication without deeper investigation is unsatisfying—please include results and diagnostic discussion. For the DUC task, you note the extended grid “does not use pairwise training” and thus could not be trained, yet earlier the grid models are trained with a ranking SVM; this inconsistency needs clarification. Also, ensure hyperparameter tuning budgets and training objectives are comparable across systems.

Questions to authors
- Preprocessing: Which parser and coreference system were used? How were non-head nouns linked into entity chains (“we match the nouns to detect coreferent entities”)—is it string/lemma matching or full coref? Any constraints on entity salience thresholds?
- Architecture details: Are embeddings shared across role-feature composite symbols (e.g., PERSON-S) or composed from separate role and feature embeddings? Did you try factoring role and feature embeddings rather than concatenating into a larger vocabulary?
- Ablations: What is the effect of varying k, pooling length, and number of filters on WSJ tasks? How much of the gain comes from embeddings vs convolution vs pairwise loss?
- Cross-entity modeling: Did you experiment with 2D convolutions or inter-column attention to capture interactions between entity chains? Any preliminary observations?
- DUC evaluation: What metric is reported (accuracy/F1/Kendall’s tau)? Table labeling appears inconsistent (Table 3 lists Acc/F1 under sentence ordering). Please clarify metrics and provide significance tests for DUC results.
- Training-task mismatch: Given insertion is harder and differs from discrimination, did you try training directly on insertion-style pairs (e.g., near-miss permutations or single-sentence moves)? If so, how did that affect performance?

Additional comments
- Please add qualitative analyses: top activated filters/patterns (e.g., prototypical SS→S→O chains), and error cases contrasting the extended grid vs Grid-CNN.
- Reporting parameter counts and runtime would aid reproducibility.
- Consider normalizing for document length when comparing to random permutations to mitigate length-driven discrimination advantages.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed incremental contribution that reliably improves over strong grid baselines with a simple, extensible neural architecture. Addressing the ablations, baseline fairness, and clarity issues would further strengthen the paper.
