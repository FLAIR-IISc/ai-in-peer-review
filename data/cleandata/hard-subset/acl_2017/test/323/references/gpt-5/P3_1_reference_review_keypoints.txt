- Proposes a neuralized entity grid: replace discrete transition counts with learned embeddings and a 1D convolution over per-entity sentence-role sequences, trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss to model longer transition windows and task-specific features.
- Introduces a simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features (NE type, salience, proper-name flag) by conjoining them with grammatical roles and learning distributed representations to avoid combinatorial count explosion.
- Shows empirical gains: new state-of-the-art on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and improved summary coherence ranking on DUC’03, with transfer learning from WSJ helping small datasets.
- Strength: strong, consistent empirical improvements over classic grid baselines, with significance testing and averaged runs.
- Strength: clean, well-motivated architecture—convolving along entity columns operationalizes entity-role transitions and supports longer windows (k≈5–8) with a ranking objective matched to evaluation.
- Strength: practical and simple pipeline requiring only standard NLP preprocessing, modest hyperparameters, and straightforward feature conjoining.
- Strength: sensible training/evaluation practices—held-out dev set, early stopping, dropout, multiple runs, approximate randomization significance testing, and inclusion of competitive baselines.
- Strength: extensible convolutional framework that can incorporate additional discourse signals or alternative parameterizations without changing the training objective.
- Weakness: limited novelty—direct application of CNNs to grids with bounded fixed-window k and no modeling of true long-distance or cross-entity dependencies.
- Weakness: incomplete baselines and unclear treatment of prior neural work (Li & Hovy 2014); absence of RST/discourse-model comparisons weakens the SOTA claim.
- Weakness: reliance on preprocessing and under-specified pipeline—parser/coref systems, mention selection heuristics, and pronoun handling are unspecified, harming reproducibility and error analysis.
- Weakness: modeling assumptions restrict expressivity by treating entity columns independently, preventing learning of cross-entity interactions (parallelism, joint topical shifts).
- Weakness: limited analysis and ablations—no sensitivity studies for window size, pooling, embedding size, per-feature contributions, or qualitative inspection of learned filters.
- Weakness: clarity issues in exposition and metrics—confusing convolution/padding notation, unspecified salience encoding, and unclear meaning of “F1” in Table 3.
- Question: Which parser and coreference system were used, how are mentions detected and clustered for “all nouns” vs “entities,” are pronouns included, and how does coref accuracy affect performance?
- Question: How many NE types are used, how is salience encoded (continuous vs buckets), how large does the vocabulary become after conjoining features, and what measures control vocabulary blowup?
- Question: Were 2D convolutions across sentences and entities, attention across entity columns, or recurrent models along columns tried to capture cross-entity patterns?
- Question: Can you report ablations/sensitivity to window size k, pooling length p, embedding size, per-feature contributions, effects of dropout, and use of pretrained embeddings?
- Question: Can you provide comparisons to RST/discourse-relation transition models on WSJ and diagnose the Li & Hovy (2014) replication failure or preprocessing mismatches?
- Question: Have you tried training the pairwise ranker directly on insertion-specific pairs (documents differing by one sentence position) to improve insertion performance?
- Question: Can you provide qualitative examples, visualizations of filters that capture canonical patterns, and an error analysis contrasting gains and losses versus the Extended Grid?
