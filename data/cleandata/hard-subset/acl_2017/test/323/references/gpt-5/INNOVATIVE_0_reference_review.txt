Summary and contributions
- A neuralized entity-grid model for local coherence: The paper replaces discrete transition counts with distributed embeddings and a convolutional architecture over entity-transition columns, trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss. This enables modeling longer transitions without combinatorial sparsity and learning task-specific features.
- Empirical validation across three tasks: The model achieves consistent, statistically significant gains over strong entity-grid baselines on WSJ discrimination and insertion, and on DUC03 summary coherence rating (with and without pretraining), establishing new state-of-the-art results in these setups.
- Simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features: By concatenating entity attributes with roles and embedding them jointly, the neural model surpasses the extended entity-grid baseline while using only a subset of handcrafted features, suggesting reduced reliance on manual feature engineering.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance with clear motivation: Neuralizing the entity-grid is an appealing bridge between a well-established discourse representation and modern representation learning. The use of CNNs over entity transitions is a neat, principled way to capture longer patterns without the exponential blow-up of discrete transition probabilities.
- End-to-end, task-aware training: Using a pairwise ranking objective tightly couples representation learning with the evaluation tasks. The pretraining-and-finetuning result on summary rating is a nice demonstration of transferability across domains/tasks, which is uncommon for coherence models.
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements: On widely used WSJ discrimination and insertion tasks, as well as DUC03 summary coherence rating, the gains over well-implemented entity-grid baselines are robust and statistically significant. Multiple runs with different seeds and significance testing add credibility. The authors use standard toolkits for competitor implementations and release code, aiding reproducibility.
- Simple, extensible design: The architecture is modular (lookup → conv → pooling → scoring) and readily accommodates entity-specific features through embedding, potentially reducing the engineering burden. The wide-convolution design addressing boundary effects is a thoughtful detail.
- Clarity and thoroughness of setup: Data splits, hyperparameter search ranges, optimization details, and early stopping are clearly laid out. The choice of WSJ for scale and generality is well argued.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope to local coherence and strong independence assumptions: Treating entity columns independently and using location-invariant convolution ignores cross-entity interactions and positional/global discourse structure. While this matches the traditional grid assumptions, it potentially leaves performance on harder coherence phenomena on the table. The paper would benefit from an ablation or discussion of 2D convolutions or mechanisms that capture inter-entity coupling.
- Evaluation focused on synthetic ordering tasks: Discrimination and insertion are proxies and can overstate progress. The summary rating evaluation is small; improvements are encouraging but modest. A richer human evaluation or additional real-world tasks (e.g., sentence ordering on narrative datasets, essay scoring) would strengthen claims of practical impact.
- Incomplete comparative picture with other discourse models: The paper mentions discourse-relation-based approaches (e.g., Lin et al. 2011; Feng et al. 2014), but does not include them in the empirical comparison on the same dataset. This makes it harder to position the contribution relative to alternative coherence formalisms beyond grids and a graph baseline.
- Handling of alternative neural baselines is unsatisfying: The Li and Hovy (2014) sentence representation model performed poorly in the authors’ replication and was excluded from tables. Even if results are disappointing, including them with diagnostic analysis (data preprocessing, windowing, sentence encoders) would provide a fairer, more transparent comparison to contemporary neural coherence models.
- Lack of analysis: There is little qualitative/error analysis or interpretability of learned filters (e.g., what transition motifs are captured, how window size k affects performance). Sensitivity to hyperparameters (transition window length, pooling length), and to coreference quality, is not explored. Given the strong reliance on coreference, an analysis using different resolvers or oracle coref (if available) would be valuable.

Questions to authors
- Coreference sensitivity: How does performance change with different coreference systems or with improved coreference (e.g., stronger modern resolvers)? Do errors from coref dominate failure cases?
- Inter-entity interactions: Did you try 2D convolutions or any mechanism to jointly consider multiple entity columns (e.g., attention across entities) rather than assuming independence? If so, what happened?
- Ablations and hyperparameter sensitivity: Can you report the effect of transition window size k, pooling length, number of filters, and embedding dimension on each task? What is the trade-off between longer windows and overfitting?
- Feature incorporation: As you concatenate entity-specific features with roles, the vocabulary grows. How large does V become in Extended Grid-CNN, and how does this affect training speed and data efficiency? Any sparsity issues?
- Broader baselines and transfer: Can you include comparisons to discourse-relation-based models on WSJ, and/or run your model on AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES to assess cross-domain robustness? For DUC03, how much does pretraining contribute vs finetuning alone?
- Negative result with Li & Hovy (2014): Could you elaborate on the setup that led to 19.34% discrimination accuracy? Were pretrained word embeddings used? Any indication that domain mismatch or sentence encoder capacity was the issue?

Overall, I view the paper as a meaningful and timely step that brings a classic, theory-backed representation (entity grids) into an end-to-end neural framework, with consistent empirical gains. Despite some limitations in breadth of evaluation and analysis, the conceptual move is clear and promising. I lean toward acceptance.
