- Proposes a neural local coherence model over entity grids using embedded grammatical-role symbols, CNNs over per-entity transition sequences, and pairwise ranking loss; shows gains on WSJ ordering/insertion and DUC’03 summary coherence.

- Main contribution 1: Neuralized entity grids—embed grid symbols and apply CNN+max-pooling to capture longer, location-invariant transition patterns learned end-to-end.

- Main contribution 2: Empirical improvements and transfer—consistent, statistically significant gains over grid baselines on WSJ discrimination/insertion and further gains on DUC’03 via WSJ pretraining.

- Main contribution 3: Practical way to add entity-specific features by expanding symbol vocabulary (e.g., PERSON-S) without combinatorial explosion, with code released.

- Strength 1: Clear, well-motivated architecture addressing sparsity of long transitions and enabling larger k (5–8) with location-invariant pattern detection.

- Strength 2: Solid, consistent empirical gains (∼4% discrimination, ∼2.5% insertion improvements) with significance testing and comparisons to strong grid baselines.

- Strength 3: End-to-end, task-focused training with pairwise ranking and sensible pretraining+fine-tuning for transfer when labeled data are scarce.

- Strength 4: Reproducibility and transparency—hyperparameter search, optimization details, multiple seeds, early stopping, and public toolkits used for baselines.

- Strength 5: Practical extensibility via symbol-conjunction trick to incorporate richer entity features without architectural complexity.

- Weakness 1: Limited novelty—incremental shift from discrete counts to learned filters; prior neural coherence models operate at sentence-level and reduce conceptual novelty.

- Weakness 2: Incomplete comparisons and unsupported SOTA claims—omits strong RST/discourse-relation baselines and lacks full accounting for Li & Hovy (2014) results.

- Weakness 3: Evaluation/reporting issues—unclear insertion metric and inconsistent table headers; more rigorous ordering metrics and harder baselines needed.

- Weakness 4: Reliance on preprocessing with no robustness analysis—no ablation on parser/coreference errors or sensitivity to different preprocessing choices.

- Weakness 5: Modeling assumptions limit expressivity—independent entity columns and aggressive max-pooling may ignore inter-entity interactions and positional information.

- Question: Clarify insertion evaluation metric and fix table header inconsistencies; if F1 used, define classes and averaging.

- Question: Can authors add comparisons to discourse-relation/RST methods or explain why fair comparison isn’t possible?

- Question: Provide sensitivity/ablation results for transition length k, filter count, pooling size, and quantify gains from longer k.

- Question: Specify coreference resolver and parser used; report sensitivity to using only head nouns vs all nouns and robustness to coref errors.

- Question: Have you tried training directly for insertion (pairwise preferences over insertion positions) and how does that compare?

- Question: Provide interpretability analysis—examples of high-activation filters, transition patterns captured, and qualitative success/failure cases.

- Overall recommendation: Solid, well-executed paper with moderate novelty but clear empirical benefits and reproducibility—recommend accept (weak).
