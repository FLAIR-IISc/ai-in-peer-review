Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a neuralized entity-grid model for local coherence: each grid cell (grammatical role and optional entity features) is embedded, per-entity transitions over sentences are composed with 1-D convolutions and max pooling, and a linear layer with pairwise ranking yields a document-level coherence score.
- It shows that this approach enables longer transition windows and end-to-end, task-specific learning, addressing sparsity and task-agnostic limitations of classic (discrete) grid models. The model also accommodates entity-specific features by embedding composite symbols.
- Empirically, it achieves consistent and statistically significant gains over strong entity-grid baselines on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) and transfers to DUC summary coherence rating, yielding new SOTA within the compared set.

Strengths
- Addresses clear, well-motivated limitations of prior work with a simple, effective architecture. Moving from discrete transition probabilities to distributed representations and convolution directly targets the curse-of-dimensionality (longer k) and enables task-tuned features via pairwise ranking. The method is conceptually clean and easy to implement.
- Strong empirical results with careful evaluation protocol. On WSJ: +4% absolute in discrimination and up to +2.5% in insertion over the corresponding non-neural grid variants, with approximate randomization significance, multiple runs averaged, and tuned hyperparameters on a held-out dev set. The gains hold even though the neural “extended” version uses only 3 entity features and still outperforms the 9-feature extended grid.
- Demonstrated transfer to a small, human-rated summary coherence task. Pretraining on WSJ then fine-tuning on DUC yields further improvements, supporting the claim that the model learns reusable coherence features.
- Reproducibility and fair baselines. The authors use public toolkits for baselines (Brown, cohere), report settings, and release code with the submission, which increases confidence and facilitates future use.
- Practicality. The method uses widely available preprocessing (POS/NPs, simple entity features) and does not require heavy external resources, yet yields consistent improvements.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty relative to prior lines. The core idea is “CNN over the entity grid with embeddings.” While this is a useful contribution, it is a fairly direct neuralization of a well-known representation. A deeper modeling advance (e.g., interactions across entities, explicit discourse constraints, or global structure) is not explored. The method also assumes entity columns are independent and position-invariant, which is a strong simplification for coherence.
- Evaluation breadth and external validity. Sentence ordering (especially discrimination) is a synthetic proxy and can overestimate practical gains. The summary rating dataset is tiny, and there is no evaluation on modern generation outputs or diverse domains. No cross-domain tests (e.g., outside WSJ) are reported. This limits claims about robustness and applicability.
- Limited analysis and ablations. There is no systematic study of:
  - Transition length k, pooling length p, and their effect on insertion vs discrimination.
  - Contribution of each entity feature (NE type, salience, proper mention).
  - Sensitivity to coreference/NP detection errors and to using “all nouns” vs coref-resolved entities.
  - Alternative architectural choices (e.g., without pooling, with position encodings, or with cross-entity attention).
  These analyses would substantiate the modeling claims and explain where gains come from.
- Preprocessing and baselines need clearer specification. It is unclear whether a coreference resolver is used or whether noun matching is employed (the text suggests both at different points). The exact pipeline (parser/coref tool, versions, quality) and its impact on results are not reported. The comparison to Li & Hovy (2014) is brief (very low accuracy reported) without thorough tuning or a fair reproduction; stronger discourse-based baselines (RST-based or more recent neural coherence models) are not presented, making “state of the art” claims contingent on a limited baseline set.
- Modeling assumptions may hurt fine-grained tasks. Treating entity columns independently and using max pooling (losing location) could discard useful cross-entity alignment and positional cues, which may explain the smaller insertion gains. A discussion or experiment training directly for insertion (as the authors suggest) would clarify this.

Questions to authors
- Preprocessing: Which coreference system and parser were used (versions, settings)? Or was noun matching used? How sensitive are results to coref quality? Please quantify performance deltas when switching between “all nouns” and coref-based entities.
- Model details: What is the effective vocabulary size V when composing role with entity features (e.g., PERSON-S) and how does it affect training time/parameters? Did you try embedding features separately and composing them instead of concatenating into a single symbol?
- Ablations: How do performance and overfitting vary with k and pooling length p? Can you report ablations removing each of the three entity features?
- Insertion objective: Did you attempt training with an insertion-specific ranking loss (varying the position of one sentence)? If so, how does it change performance?
- Transfer: How much of the DUC improvement stems from pretraining vs simple regularization? Did you try zero-shot transfer to DUC without fine-tuning?

Additional comments
- Provide qualitative analyses (e.g., top-activating transition patterns per filter) and a small error analysis contrasting cases where the neural model succeeds but the grid fails.
- Reporting runtime/memory and model size would help practitioners.
- Minor clarity: tighten Section 3.1 to avoid ambiguity about coreference vs noun matching; specify whether pooling is applied per-entity column or across columns, and how features from different entities are aggregated before the final linear layer.

Overall: A solid, well-executed neuralization of the entity grid with consistent empirical gains and good reproducibility. The contribution is primarily practical and incremental; broader evaluation and deeper analysis would strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance if space allows, given the empirical improvements and simplicity.
