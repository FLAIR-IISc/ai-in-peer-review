Summary and Contributions
- The paper proposes a neuralized version of the entity grid for local coherence assessment. It maps grid symbols (grammatical roles and optionally entity-specific features) to embeddings and applies convolution over within-entity sentence transitions, followed by max-pooling and a linear scoring layer trained with pairwise ranking.
- Empirically, the model improves over standard entity-grid baselines on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and on DUC’03 summary coherence rating; pretraining on WSJ further helps the summary task.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) An end-to-end CNN over entity-grid transitions that allows longer transition windows and task-specific feature learning, showing consistent improvements over established entity-grid variants.
  2) A practical demonstration that limited entity-specific features, when embedded and composed with CNNs, can match or surpass a richer, hand-engineered extended grid.
  3) Release of code and reporting of dev tuning protocol and multiple-run averages.

Strengths
- Solid improvements over strong, established baselines on standard tasks. The Extended Grid-CNN yields +3.7 points in discrimination and +2.7 in insertion over Extended Grid (E&C), with significance reported. These are meaningful margins on well-used setups, lending credence to the claim that distributed representations and convolution can mitigate sparsity in longer transitions.
- Clear, task-aligned training objective. Pairwise ranking is appropriate for coherence scoring; using it end-to-end to learn embeddings and filters is a sensible modernization of the original SVM-ranker approach.
- Sensible architectural choices and tuning protocol. The model design (lookup → convolution over transitions → max-pooling → linear scoring) is straightforward and well-motivated for location-invariant pattern detection; hyperparameters are tuned on a held-out dev set, training uses multiple random seeds, and early stopping/dropout are employed.
- Pragmatic and fair feature setup. The neural “extended” model uses only three readily available entity features yet surpasses a more feature-heavy non-neural model, suggesting robustness and reduced reliance on external resources.
- Transfer to a distinct task. Pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning for summary coherence yields additional gains on a very small dataset, consistent with expected benefits of representation learning under data scarcity.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond a direct neuralization of a well-known model. The core idea—embedding discrete grid symbols and applying CNNs over transitions—is a fairly straightforward adaptation of standard neural components. There is little theoretical analysis or new modeling insight beyond this architectural substitution.
- Incomplete comparison to relevant strong baselines. The experiments omit comparisons to (i) improved learning-to-rank schemes over grids (e.g., Feng & Hirst 2012), and (ii) discourse-relation-based coherence models (Lin et al. 2011; Feng et al. 2014), which have reported strong results. The attempt to include Li & Hovy (2014) is noted, but simply dropping a known baseline due to reproduction difficulties weakens the empirical case.
- Evaluation tasks are narrow and have known limitations. Heavy reliance on discrimination with random permutations (particularly on longer WSJ articles) can inflate scores and may not reflect real-world coherence distinctions. For insertion, only exact-position accuracy is reported; no rank-based or near-miss metrics (e.g., Kendall tau, distance-based accuracy) are provided to gauge practical utility.
- Insufficient methodological detail for reproducibility and fairness. The paper does not clearly specify the coreference resolution pipeline (system, settings, whether identical across neural and non-neural models), which is critical for entity grids. It is also unclear whether the non-neural baselines received comparable dev-set tuning. The graph-based baseline appears minimally tuned (“projection=3”), which may understate its performance.
- Lack of ablations and analysis. No ablation of transition window size, pooling, number of filters, or entity features is provided; there is no analysis of which transition patterns the filters learn; and no study of how much of the gain comes from longer k versus end-to-end training. The independence assumption across entity columns is strong; there is no exploration of modeling interactions between entities or incorporating positional signals, which could matter for insertion.

Questions to Authors
- Coreference: Which coreference resolver is used for all models (neural and baselines)? Are the mention detection, head rules, and resolution decisions identical across systems? Please report coref quality (if available) and its sensitivity.
- Baselines: Did you tune the E&C baselines on the same dev set with comparable effort? Can you include results for Feng & Hirst (2012) and for discourse-relation models (Lin et al. 2011; Feng et al. 2014) on your WSJ setup?
- Li & Hovy (2014): What exact training protocol, embeddings, and hyperparameters were used in your reproduction attempt? Could you share logs/configs and, if space permits, include the negative result (with analysis) rather than omitting it?
- Metrics: For insertion, can you add rank-based/near-miss metrics (e.g., average displacement, Kendall tau)? For discrimination, can you control for length and report performance by document length bins?
- Ablations/Analysis: Please provide ablations for transition length k, pooling size, number of filters, and feature sets; and a qualitative analysis of learned filters (e.g., prototypical high-activation patterns). Does adding simple positional encodings or cross-entity interactions help insertion?
- Summary task: Are the DUC improvements statistically significant? How sensitive are the results to pretraining domain mismatch and to the choice of fine-tuning data size?
- Efficiency: What are parameter counts, training time, and inference time compared to E&C? How does k impact runtime and memory?
