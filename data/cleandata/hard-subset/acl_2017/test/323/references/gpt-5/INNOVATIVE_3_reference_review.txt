Summary and claimed contributions
- Introduces a neuralized entity-grid model for local coherence: grammatical roles (and optionally entity-specific features) are embedded, and 1D convolutions over entity transitions (columns of the grid) with max-pooling yield a document-level coherence score trained with pairwise ranking. This addresses sparsity and task-agnostic feature extraction in classic grid models and enables longer transition modeling.
- Demonstrates consistent, statistically significant improvements over strong non-neural grid baselines on three standard tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating), with simple feature engineering and code release. Shows transfer via pretraining from WSJ to summary rating.
- Presents a practical recipe (embeddings + CNN + pairwise ranking) that is simple, extensible, and compatible with adding entity features without blowing up parameters.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance linking discourse-theoretic entity grids with distributed representation learning. Moving from discrete transition counts to learned embeddings and convolution filters directly addresses the curse of dimensionality and the task-agnostic nature of prior grids. The use of wide convolution over entity-transition columns is a clean way to capture long-range local coherence patterns.
- Strong empirical gains with statistical rigor. The neural model outperforms both the all-noun grid and the extended grid on discrimination and insertion, and improves on summary rating (further boosted by pretraining). Significance testing, multiple seeds, and detailed hyperparameter reporting increase confidence in the results.
- Low-feature, end-to-end training. Compared to extended grids that rely on a larger set of engineered features (some from external corpora), the proposed model achieves better results with only three simple entity features (or none), highlighting the power of learned representations and reducing reliance on feature engineering.
- Transferability and generality. The pretraining experiment (WSJ → DUC summaries) is a useful proof-of-concept for transfer in coherence modeling, a desirable property for practical deployment where labeled comparisons are scarce.
- Reproducibility and clarity. The architecture is straightforward, the training objective is standard yet appropriate for coherence, and the authors release code and provide sufficient training details.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate. While the paper makes a useful step, it can be seen largely as a straightforward neuralization of the entity grid with a standard CNN and pairwise ranking. The choice of CNN over other sequence models (RNN/attention) is not contrasted theoretically or empirically, which somewhat limits the methodological contribution.
- Missing or downplayed baselines. Results for other strong non-grid coherence models (e.g., Feng & Hirst 2012’s improved ranking among permutations; Lin et al. 2011 and Feng et al. 2014 discourse-relation/RST-based models) are not included in the main comparisons. The Li & Hovy (2014) neural baseline is excluded due to poor performance without deeper analysis, leaving questions about fairness and completeness of empirical context.
- Limited analysis and ablations. There is no systematic study of the impact of transition window size, pooling size/stride, filter counts, or the contribution of each entity-specific feature. An ablation isolating embeddings vs convolution vs pooling vs ranking loss would strengthen causal claims. Qualitative analysis (e.g., visualizing filters/activations as learned transition patterns) is missing.
- Dependence on preprocessing with no sensitivity study. The model is only as good as the coreference and syntactic role labeling that define the grid. There is no robustness analysis to coreference errors, domain shift, or alternative mention/role definitions. This is important for practical use and for understanding where the gains come from.
- Evaluation scope and task mismatch. The main results are on WSJ discrimination/insertion, which are synthetic tasks known to correlate imperfectly with downstream coherence needs. The summary dataset is small. Moreover, the model used for insertion is trained on discrimination pairs, which may underutilize the proposed framework; training on insertion-specific pairs would be a natural and important experiment.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide ablations: (a) varying transition length k and pooling size, (b) removing convolution (bag of embedded transitions), (c) removing entity features, and (d) training with classification rather than pairwise ranking? Which components drive the gains?
- How sensitive is performance to the coreference resolver and syntactic role assignment? Have you tried different coref systems or oracle vs predicted mentions to quantify robustness?
- Why favor CNN over alternatives like RNNs or self-attention over transition sequences? Did you run comparative experiments?
- Can you add comparisons to Feng & Hirst (2012) and RST-informed models (Lin et al., Feng et al., 2014) on WSJ, or justify their omission?
- For the extended features, you encode combinations like PERSON-S as single symbols. Did you consider a factorized embedding (separate embeddings for role, NE type, salience bin, properness) to avoid vocabulary blow-up and improve generalization?
- For insertion, can you train with insertion-specific pairwise constraints (or listwise training) and report whether performance improves?
- Do you have qualitative analyses of learned filters (e.g., top-activating transition patterns), or error analyses contrasting where the neural grid helps vs hurts relative to the extended grid?
- What is the runtime/parameter footprint compared to extended grid models, and how does performance scale with document length and number of entities?

Overall assessment
This work is a solid and timely step that brings entity-grid coherence modeling into the neural era with a clean, end-to-end design and measurable gains. While the methodological novelty is modest, the direction is meaningful and likely to influence subsequent research; with stronger baselines and ablations, this could be a clear accept. I lean toward acceptance given the conceptual bridge, simplicity, and consistent improvements.
