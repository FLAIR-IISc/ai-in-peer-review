Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid: grammatical role symbols (e.g., S/O/X/−, optionally augmented with entity features) are embedded; per-entity transitions over sentences are processed with 1D convolutions (wide conv) and max-pooling; a linear layer outputs a coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking (hinge) loss.
- It shows consistent improvements over strong entity-grid baselines on three established tasks: sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion on WSJ) and summary coherence rating (DUC), achieving new state-of-the-art results. Limited pretraining transfer from WSJ to DUC further improves performance.
- It demonstrates that distributed representations and convolutional composition over longer entity transitions (k≈5–8) can overcome sparsity limits of classic grid models and that a simplified set of entity-specific features suffices within this framework.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and principled neuralization of a widely used framework. The design choices (embedding lookup for grid symbols, local conv over transitions, pairwise ranking) are well motivated by the limitations of discrete, task-agnostic counts in classic grids and map cleanly to the problem assumptions.
- Empirical gains are consistent and meaningful across tasks and datasets. The model improves over “Grid-all nouns” by ~4% (discrimination) and ~1% (insertion), and over “Extended grid” by ~3.7% (discr.) and up to 2.5% (insertion), with significance testing. On summary ratings, the model outperforms prior methods even without pretraining; with pretraining, gains increase further (reported >3% absolute). This breadth strengthens the claim of general utility.
- Ability to handle longer transitions and incorporate entity features without combinatorial blow-up. Using embeddings and shared convolutional filters allows modeling longer k than previous grid models (k≤3), tackling a longstanding sparsity issue, while making feature augmentation straightforward (e.g., PERSON-S).
- Good experimental hygiene. Hyperparameters are tuned on a held-out dev set; multiple runs with different seeds are averaged; significance is tested via approximate randomization; a large, standard WSJ split is used, and the choice is justified. Implementation details (optimizers, init, dropout, early stopping) are transparent.
- Practical relevance and usability. The model is end-to-end trainable for ranking, produces a scalar coherence score for downstream use, and code is reportedly released. The architecture is lightweight compared to full-blown discourse parsers and thus attractive for generation and ordering pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond a straightforward application of CNNs to the entity grid. While effective, the main technical step—embedding grid symbols and applying per-column convolutions with pooling—is an incremental extension of prior grids and standard CNN practice. The independence assumptions (columns/entities independent; location invariance via pooling) mirror classic grids rather than advancing discourse modeling capacity.
- Independence assumptions may cap modeling power. Treating entity columns independently ignores interactions among entities (e.g., role alternation across entities within a sentence) and longer-distance cross-entity discourse phenomena. Max-pooling erases positional information, which may be important for fine-grained insertion decisions. These choices could explain the smaller gains on insertion and risk plateauing against stronger recent baselines.
- Incomplete comparisons and analysis against the strongest alternatives. Results include Guinaudeau & Strube and the E&C grid variants, but not the discourse relation models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) that often perform strongly on ordering tasks. The Li & Hovy sentence-level neural baseline is dismissed due to low performance on the authors’ setup; however, no deeper analysis or tuning effort is reported. This leaves open whether the improvements are state-of-the-art relative to all relevant modern baselines.
- Sparse ablation/diagnostic analysis. The paper lacks ablations on transition window size k, pooling size/stride, effect of entity feature subsets, and sensitivity to the coreference resolver. No qualitative analysis of learned filters or error breakdown is provided (e.g., what patterns are captured; typical failure cases in insertion). Such analysis would substantiate claims about “long-range transition modeling” and task-specific feature learning.
- Evaluation and reporting gaps. For insertion, the paper notes the model is trained only on discrimination and speculates this limits gains; however, no experiment trains directly on insertion. Metrics presentation is occasionally unclear (e.g., Table 3 shows “Acc F1” without consistent explanation; the discrimination vs insertion columns appear to duplicate “Acc”). Runtime, memory, and preprocessing robustness (e.g., effect of parsing/coref errors) are not discussed. DUC results are based on a very small training set; variance/CI are not reported.

Questions to the authors
- Coreference and parsing: Which coreference resolver and syntactic pipeline are used to construct the grid? How sensitive are results to coref errors (e.g., using gold vs automatic, ablating coref, or swapping resolvers)?
- Modeling choices: What is the empirical effect of transition window size k and pooling length p on the three tasks? Do larger k keep helping, or is there a sweet spot?
- Interaction among entities: Did you try convolutions that span across columns (e.g., 2D conv over rows×entities) to capture inter-entity interactions? If so, what were the results?
- Insertion training: Can you train the pairwise ranking directly on insertion candidates (or a listwise loss) and report whether insertion improves materially?
- Baselines: Can you provide results or citations comparing against discourse-relation-based models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) on the same WSJ split, and clarify whether your method remains SOTA?
- Analysis: Can you show exemplar high-activation patterns learned by filters (e.g., typical transitions), and provide an error analysis for insertion?

Additional comments
- Clarify metrics and table labels (Acc vs F1; ensure insertion metric matches prior work). Report standard deviations over runs, and, if possible, confidence intervals on DUC.
- Since you claim end-to-end, discuss computational costs and training/inference speeds versus E&C and graph-based methods.
- The reference list has formatting artifacts (question marks, missing years/pages). Please fix for camera-ready.
