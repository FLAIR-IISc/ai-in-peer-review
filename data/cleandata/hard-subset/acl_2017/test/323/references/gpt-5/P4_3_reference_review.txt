Summary
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid framework. Each grid cell (grammatical role, optionally augmented with entity features) is embedded; 1-D convolutions are applied along each entity column to capture transitions of length k; max-pooling selects salient patterns; and a linear layer outputs a coherence score. The model is trained with a pairwise ranking loss on (original, permuted) document pairs. Experiments on WSJ (discrimination and insertion) and DUC’03 (summary coherence rating) show consistent improvements over entity grid baselines and a graph-based method. The approach reports state-of-the-art results on these tasks, with code released.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A CNN-based, end-to-end ranking model over entity grids that learns distributed representations of grammatical roles and entity-specific features, enabling longer transition modeling than traditional discrete entity-grid features.
2) Empirical evidence that the neuralized grid yields consistent, statistically significant gains over strong entity-grid baselines on sentence ordering and summary coherence rating, including when using only a subset of entity-specific features.
3) A simple procedure to incorporate entity-specific features into the neural grid and a demonstration of transfer via pretraining on WSJ for summary coherence rating.

Strengths
- Clear performance gains over strong baselines: On WSJ, Grid-CNN improves over Grid-all-nouns by ~4% absolute on discrimination and ~1% on insertion; Extended Grid-CNN improves over the Extended Grid by ~3.7% on discrimination and 2.5% on insertion (p<0.01). On DUC’03, pretraining further boosts summary coherence rating by 3–4% absolute. These are meaningful improvements on standard benchmarks.
- Sound training and evaluation methodology: The pairwise ranking loss aligns with prior work on coherence scoring; the model is tuned on a held-out dev set, trained with multiple seeds, and evaluated with approximate randomization tests. Pretraining and fine-tuning for the low-resource summary rating task are appropriate and effective.
- Addresses limitations of discrete grids: By embedding roles and using convolution, the model alleviates sparsity and can use longer transitions (k up to 5–8), which are infeasible with R^k discrete features. Learning is end-to-end and task-specific rather than task-agnostic feature counts.
- Practicality and simplicity: The architecture is straightforward, computationally reasonable, and compatible with existing grid extraction pipelines. The proposed way to add entity features is simple to implement, and code availability enhances reproducibility.
- Broad evaluation: Results are reported on two sentence ordering tasks and summary coherence rating, covering both document ordering and human-judged coherence.

Weaknesses
- Modeling independence across entities: The architecture treats entity columns independently and only models within-entity temporal transitions. Interactions across entities and cross-sentence structural patterns (e.g., multiple entities co-evolving, topic shifts) are not modeled. This limits expressivity beyond the traditional grid’s assumptions.
- Feature composition strategy may limit generalization: Encoding entity-specific features by concatenating them with role tokens (e.g., PERSON-S) increases the vocabulary and loses factorization across features. This design prevents the model from sharing parameters across feature dimensions and may be sample-inefficient, especially as more features are added.
- Evaluation limited to permutation-based training: Training predominantly on (original vs. random permutation) pairs risks learning cues particular to random shuffles rather than general coherence. The insertion gains are comparatively small and the model is not trained for this task. No experiments report training with task-specific negatives (e.g., near-miss insertions) to assess robustness.
- Baseline coverage and ablations: The graph-based baseline is relatively weak. Strong discourse-structure-based baselines (e.g., RST-based coherence models) are cited but not included in comparisons. The Li & Hovy (2014) model is excluded due to poor performance without deeper analysis. Ablations on transition length k, pooling, and entity features are limited; it is unclear how much each component contributes.
- Missing implementation details and minor clarity issues: The coreference resolver, parser, and settings (e.g., mention detection choices, treatment of non-head nouns) are not specified, yet are crucial for grid quality. The notation for convolution feature map dimensions (e.g., “m.n+k−1”), and the insertion metric labeling in the results table are confusing. Vocabulary size and parameter counts when entity features are included are not reported.

Questions to authors
- What coreference resolver, parser, and mention detection pipeline are used for WSJ and DUC? What are their versions and settings? How sensitive are results to coreference quality?
- What is the size of the role/feature vocabulary V in each setting (basic vs. extended), and the total parameter count? Did you try factorizing features (separate embeddings per feature type concatenated or summed) instead of composite tokens?
- Did you experiment with training using task-specific negatives for insertion (e.g., near-miss positions) or curriculum strategies? If so, how did performance change?
- What are the effects of varying transition length k and pooling size p? Please provide ablations on k, number of filters, and pooling to quantify their contributions.
- Did you explore interactions across entities (e.g., 2D convolutions over the grid or attention across columns)? If attempted, what were the results?
- For summary coherence rating, what is the exact pretraining protocol (number of frozen vs. fine-tuned layers, learning rates), and how stable are gains across seeds?

Overall assessment
The paper is clear, technically sound, and demonstrates consistent improvements with a simple, well-motivated neuralization of the entity grid. Novelty is moderate but sufficient: the main value lies in adapting CNNs to model entity transitions and showing that this alleviates sparsity and improves performance. Addressing the modeling limitations (cross-entity interactions), clarifying implementation details, and expanding ablations/baselines would further strengthen the work.
