Summary
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid framework. It embeds grid symbols (grammatical roles and optionally entity-specific features) and applies 1D convolution over per-entity sentence-role sequences, followed by max pooling and a linear layer trained with a pairwise ranking loss. The model is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) using WSJ sections and on summary coherence rating (DUC’03), showing gains over non-neural grid baselines and a graph-based baseline. A variant with a small set of entity-specific features further improves performance; pretraining on WSJ helps on DUC’03.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A CNN-based architecture over embedded entity-grid symbols for coherence scoring, enabling longer transition windows than classic discrete grids and end-to-end learning for the target task (within the grid abstraction).
2) Empirical improvements over strong entity-grid baselines on discrimination, insertion, and summary rating, with modest additional features and without external corpora for feature derivation.
3) A pairwise ranking training setup for the neural grid model and preliminary transfer (pretraining) from ordering to summary rating.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and plausible novelty: Using CNNs over embedded entity-role sequences is a natural, compact way to capture longer transition patterns without the sparsity of enumerated discrete transitions. This fills a gap between discrete grids and sentence-level neural coherence models.
- Strong baselines and experimental scope: The paper compares against classic and extended grid models and a representative graph model; it evaluates on three established tasks (WSJ ordering and DUC summary rankings), including transfer via pretraining, which is appropriate given DUC’s limited size.
- Consistent improvements: The neuralized basic grid improves over the non-neural basic grid, and the extended neural model improves over the extended grid, with statistically significant gains reported. The finding that pretraining helps on DUC is also useful for the community.
- Reasonable training protocol: Pairwise ranking, dropout, early stopping, multiple runs with different seeds, and significance testing reflect good experimental hygiene, at least at a high level.
- Practical framing: Limiting entity features to a few easy-to-compute ones (NE type, salience, proper mention) is pragmatic and suggests the gains are not due to feature engineering or external corpora.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity issues and inconsistencies in the convolution/pooling description: The paper claims columns are independent yet mentions padding for t > {m,n}, which is nonsensical (should be t > m if t indexes sentences). Equation and dimensionality issues (e.g., feature map length “m.n+k−1”) are incorrect; it is unclear how outputs across columns are combined into feature maps before pooling. Without a precise tensor shape specification, replication is difficult.
- Preprocessing pipeline under-specified: Results depend critically on entity extraction, grammatical role assignment, coreference, and NE tagging. The paper alternates between using a coreference resolver and “matching nouns to detect coreferent entities,” but does not name tools, versions, or configurations, nor how “all nouns” are incorporated (POS tags? dependency vs constituency roles?). Salience bucketing, NE tag inventory, and discretization of numeric features are not specified. This severely limits reproducibility and may confound comparisons with baselines if pipelines differ.
- Evaluation reporting errors and missing results: Table 3 shows identical numbers for discrimination and insertion for every model, contradicting both intuition and the paper’s own statement that insertion is harder. The third column header “Acc F1” is unexplained in a table purportedly for discrimination and insertion, and the summary rating results (Table 4) are referenced but not presented here. These inconsistencies cast doubt on the reported gains.
- Baseline fairness and incomplete comparisons: The Li & Hovy (2014) neural coherence model is a direct comparator. The paper reports a failed replication (19.34% discrimination) and then excludes it “not sure what could go wrong,” which is not acceptable. At minimum, report numbers from the paper, consult authors, or present a careful ablation explaining the discrepancy. Also, the paper claims SOTA without comparing to discourse-relation-based models (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) under matched conditions.
- Overstated “end-to-end” claim and analysis gaps: The approach is end-to-end only from grid to score; it still relies on a full external NLP pipeline (parser, coref, NER). No ablations show the effect of window size k, filter count, pooling, or embedding size; no analysis of errors or coref quality impact; no runtime/efficiency comparisons; no discussion of vocabulary explosion when concatenating features into role symbols and whether embeddings mitigate rare symbol sparsity in practice.

Questions to Authors
- Please provide a precise description (with shapes) of the convolution and pooling over the grid: along which axis is convolution performed, how are outputs across columns aggregated into feature maps, and what is the exact dimensionality at each layer? Clarify the padding statement “t > {m,n}.”
- What tools (name/version/config) were used for: coreference resolution, syntactic role extraction (how are S/O/X derived?), POS tagging, and NER? Are these identical across your models and the Brown coherence toolkit baselines?
- How is salience represented when “attached” to role symbols (raw count, buckets)? What is the final vocabulary size |V| for the extended model, and how frequent are rare symbols? Any UNK handling?
- Please correct and report the true discrimination and insertion numbers (separately) for all systems, and include the full summary rating results with the exact metric (pairwise accuracy/Kendall/Spearman?) and variance over seeds.
- Can you include a fair comparison to Li & Hovy (2014) (either by reproducing their reported settings on their datasets or explaining the mismatch) and to discourse-relation models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) on WSJ if feasible?
- How sensitive are results to the transition window k and to the choice of pooling length? Any evidence that longer k drives the gains vs. simply more parameters?
