Summary and main contributions
- A neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the classic entity grid: grammatical roles (and optionally entity-specific features) are embedded and processed with a 1-D convolution over per-entity sentence transitions, followed by max pooling and a pairwise ranking objective. This removes sparsity, enables longer transition modeling, and learns task-specific features end-to-end.
- A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features (e.g., NE type, salience, properness) by composing them with roles into the grid symbol space, maintaining a unified architecture.
- Consistent state-of-the-art results on WSJ discrimination and insertion, and DUC’03 summary coherence rating, including gains even with a reduced feature set; code is released.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Neuralizing the entity grid in a way that is faithful to the theory while addressing its core limitations (sparsity, short transitions, task-agnostic features). Using embeddings plus convolution to learn distributed transition patterns is a clean, general idea with clear upside beyond the specific tasks here. This is more than a mere reimplementation: it enables longer-range transitions (k up to 8) and end-to-end learning under a ranking loss aligned with coherence scoring.
- Solid empirical improvements across tasks: On WSJ, ~4% absolute on discrimination and up to 2.5% on insertion vs. the strongest non-neural grid; on DUC’03, >3% over prior models with pretraining/fine-tuning. Improvements are reported over multiple runs with significance testing, and the neural model beats the extended grid even using only three entity features, supporting the claim that learned representations reduce feature engineering needs.
- Methodological simplicity and practicality: The architecture is simple (lookup → conv → max-pool → linear scorer), easy to reproduce (hyperparameters, optimizer, early stopping, dropout specified), and trains with a standard pairwise hinge loss. The pretraining strategy for low-resource summary rating is sensible and effective. Released code further strengthens impact.
- Alignment to downstream usage: Pairwise ranking delivers a usable scoring function for generation/selection and matches the evaluation tasks’ comparative nature. The pooling provides location invariance that plausibly reflects the intuition that coherent patterns can appear anywhere in the document.
- Clarity: The paper is well written, with transparent modeling assumptions (independence across entity columns; location invariance) and clear connection to prior grid work and alternative approaches.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling of inter-entity interactions: The core assumption that columns (entities) are independent leaves out cross-entity phenomena (e.g., parallel structures, topic shifts involving multiple entities, role alternations across entities). This is a notable limitation for a coherence model, and it leaves performance potentially capped. Even lightweight 2-D convolutions or attention across columns might capture important patterns.
- Dependence on preprocessing with no robustness analysis: The model relies on accurate coreference and syntactic role labeling. There is no study of sensitivity to errors, or variants with weaker preprocessing (e.g., no coref, head nouns only). Given the potential brittleness of coreference systems, especially outside news, this omission matters for practical use and generalization.
- Baseline fairness and coverage: The reproduced Li & Hovy (2014) baseline scores (19.34% discrimination) seem implausibly low and out of line with prior reports; without careful re-tuning or stronger neural sentence-based baselines, it’s hard to assess how much of the gain is due to the entity-grid inductive bias versus generic neural capacity. Similarly, strong discourse (RST-based) baselines on the same splits are not included, despite being discussed in related work.
- Evaluation scope: The primary results are on WSJ; DUC’03 has very limited training data. There is no cross-domain evaluation, and insertion is not trained directly. More ablations (transition length k, number of filters, effect of pretraining, with/without entity features) and qualitative analyses (e.g., learned filter patterns) would strengthen the case that the model learns meaningful transition regularities beyond capturing surface heuristics or document length effects.
- Feature composition scaling: Encoding entity-specific features by concatenating them with roles expands the vocabulary V. The paper does not quantify the resulting size, OOV handling for unseen combinations, or compare against alternative, more factorized encodings (e.g., separate embeddings combined via composition), which might generalize better.

Questions to authors
- How large does V become when adding the three entity-specific features? How are unseen role–feature combinations at test time handled? Did you try factorized embeddings (separate role and feature embeddings combined by concatenation/projection) to reduce sparsity?
- Did you explore capturing inter-entity interactions (e.g., 2-D convolutions across sentence × entity axes or attention across columns)? Any preliminary results?
- Can you provide a robustness analysis to coreference/parsing errors (e.g., varying coref system quality, ablating coref, or using head nouns only)?
- The Li & Hovy baseline result appears extremely low. What tuning and data processing did you attempt? Can you report a sanity check (e.g., training on your splits until convergence, matching tokenization/sentence segmentation) or include a stronger neural sentence-window baseline?
- Do you have qualitative visualizations of learned filters/max-pooled activations corresponding to interpretable transition patterns?
- For insertion, did you attempt task-specific training (e.g., pairwise comparisons over candidate insertion positions)? If so, how does it compare?

Recommendation
Accept. While the architecture is conceptually simple, it is a meaningful and well-executed step that brings a classic, theory-driven coherence model into the neural era, enabling longer-range transitions and task-specific learning with clear gains. The idea is likely to influence subsequent work on discourse modeling and sentence ordering. I would not block acceptance on the noted limitations, but encourage adding robust baselines, sensitivity analyses, and qualitative insights.
