Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid: each grid cell (grammatical role possibly augmented with entity features) is embedded; 1D convolutions are applied along sentence order within each entity column to detect longer transition patterns; max-pooling and a linear layer produce a document coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss.
- The authors show consistent improvements over non-neural entity grid baselines on three tasks: sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) on WSJ PTB and summary coherence rating on DUC’03, with additional gains from pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning on summaries.
- They demonstrate that their approach can incorporate a subset of entity-specific features without blowing up feature space, supporting the claim that distributed representations mitigate sparsity and allow longer transition windows.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, effective CNN over entity grids that enables longer-range transition modeling and task-specific end-to-end learning, addressing sparsity and task-agnostic feature extraction in classic grid models.
2) Empirical validation on standard coherence evaluations with statistically significant and consistent gains, including transfer (pretraining + fine-tuning) for the low-resource summary rating task.
3) A practical recipe to incorporate entity-specific features in a neural grid without exponential feature blow-up.

Strengths
- Methodological clarity and soundness: The model tightly targets known limitations of entity grids (sparsity from discrete transitions; decoupled feature extraction) with a natural neural design. Using embeddings and convolutions along entity columns is a principled way to model longer transitions (k up to 5–8) while sharing parameters across documents, which should improve generalization. The pairwise ranking objective directly optimizes the intended comparison task.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains with robust evaluation practices: On WSJ, Grid-CNN improves over Grid-all-nouns (~+4% discrimination, ~+1% insertion), and Extended Grid-CNN improves over Extended Grid (~+3.7% discrimination, +2.5% insertion), with p<0.01 vs the non-neural counterparts. The summary rating task also improves, especially with pretraining. The authors average over 5 random seeds and use approximate randomization tests, lending credibility to the reported gains.
- Sensible dataset choices and baselines: Using the larger WSJ PTB with a clear section split increases statistical power relative to smaller, stylistically constrained datasets. The authors reproduce and compare against widely used baselines via public toolkits (Brown coherence toolkit, cohere framework), which enhances reproducibility and fairness.
- End-to-end training and transfer: The pretrain-on-WSJ then fine-tune-on-DUC setting is a valuable demonstration that the model learns task-specific features and transfers across domains/tasks, improving performance where labeled data are scarce.
- Interpretability and connection to theory: The model preserves the entity-centric perspective from Centering Theory and grid-based methods. The intuition that filters detect salient transition patterns (e.g., SS-O-X) aligns with prior analyses, aiding interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural coherence work: While operating over entity grids is different from Li & Hovy (2014), the core idea—learn distributed sentence-level or entity-level patterns and score windows/documents—is conceptually incremental. The paper’s decision to omit a substantive comparison to Li & Hovy (beyond reporting a failed replication) weakens claims of superiority; more careful reimplementation/tuning or a diagnostic comparison would strengthen the case.
- Independence assumptions and limited modeling of cross-entity interactions: Treating entity columns independently and aggregating via max-pooling likely misses interactions across entities and global discourse structure (e.g., topic shifts mediated by multiple entities). The paper does not analyze whether ignoring cross-entity patterns limits performance, nor explore lightweight cross-column composition.
- Analysis and ablations are sparse: Beyond “grid vs extended grid,” there is little investigation of where gains come from. The paper would benefit from ablations on transition window size k, pooling length p, number of filters, and each entity-specific feature. Error analysis (e.g., failure cases by document length or coreference quality) is also missing.
- Pipeline dependence not quantified: The approach relies on coreference and NP detection quality, yet the paper does not specify the exact coreference system or quantify sensitivity to coreference errors. This raises questions about robustness across domains and coreference tools.
- Evaluation coverage and clarity: There is no WSJ comparison to discourse-relation-based models (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) that are strong on coherence tasks; this leaves open whether gains hold against those alternatives. The insertion model is tested without task-specific training, which may understate or misstate its true capabilities. Also, Table 3 appears to include an unexplained “F1” column for sentence ordering; metric definitions and table labeling need clarification.

Questions to authors
- Which coreference resolver and NP extraction pipeline are used to construct grids? How sensitive is performance to different coreference systems and to precision/recall trade-offs?
- Can you provide ablations on transition window size k, pooling size p, and number of filters, as well as on each entity-specific feature? Where do most of the gains originate?
- Did you attempt any cross-entity composition (e.g., 2D convolutions across columns, or attention across entities)? If so, what were the outcomes?
- Why not train directly on the insertion task (e.g., pairwise preferences over candidate insertion positions)? Do gains increase when trained for insertion explicitly?
- Can you clarify Table 3’s last column and ensure metric definitions align with task descriptions? Also, can you add comparisons against discourse-relation models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) on WSJ?

Overall assessment
A well-motivated, clear, and practically useful neuralization of the entity grid with solid empirical gains and good experimental rigor. The methodological novelty is moderate, and additional analysis/comparisons would strengthen the paper, but the results and simplicity argue for acceptance.
