Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid. Each grid entry (grammatical role, optionally augmented with entity-specific features) is embedded; 1D convolutions over entity transitions (column-wise windows across sentences) capture longer-range patterns; max pooling aggregates salient patterns; a linear layer yields a document coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss (original vs. permuted documents). The authors report state-of-the-art results on WSJ discrimination and insertion tasks and on DUC’03 summary coherence rating, including transfer learning from WSJ to DUC.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, effective neural architecture for local coherence that operates over entity-grid embeddings with convolution over transition windows, enabling longer transition modeling and end-to-end, task-specific learning.
2) A practical way to incorporate entity-specific features within the neural grid by expanding the symbol space and learning embeddings, reducing manual feature engineering while outperforming strong entity-grid baselines.
3) Strong empirical results across three standard benchmarks (WSJ discrimination/insertion, DUC’03 summary coherence rating), with consistent gains and evidence that pretraining is beneficial for data-scarce settings.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and well-motivated design: The model directly addresses two widely acknowledged limitations of classic entity grid models: sparsity with long transitions and task-agnostic feature extraction. Embeddings mitigate sparsity; convolution over transitions captures longer local dependencies in a position-invariant way; pairwise ranking enables end-to-end learning tailored to each task.

- Strong empirical performance with reasonable comparisons: On WSJ, the neural grid improves over both the “all nouns” and extended grid baselines by ~4% absolute on discrimination and up to 2.5% on insertion, despite using only a subset of entity-specific features. On DUC’03, the model outperforms prior reported results, and pretraining on WSJ further improves performance, supporting the model’s robustness and transferability.

- Sound training setup and reproducibility: The paper provides training details (optimizers, hyperparameter search, early stopping, dropout), uses standard splits (WSJ sections), performs multiple runs with different seeds, and reports significance testing. Code availability is a plus for reproducibility.

- Minimal reliance on manual features: The fact that the neural model with only three entity-level features outperforms a feature-rich extended grid highlights the value of learned distributed representations, suggesting reduced engineering burden and better generalization.

- Compatibility with established coherence theory: The approach remains grounded in entity-based coherence and centering-inspired transitions, making it interpretable and aligning with prior analyses, while bringing the benefits of neural modeling.

Weaknesses
- Limited modeling of cross-entity interactions and global discourse structure: Treating columns independently assumes no interactions among entities. Many coherence phenomena involve interplay between multiple entities and global topical shifts. Ignoring cross-entity relations may limit ceiling performance, particularly for insertion where subtle interactions matter. An ablation or variant that includes cross-entity convolution or attention would strengthen the case.

- Loss of positional information via max pooling: The model uses location-invariant pooling over feature maps, which discards where patterns occur. While helpful for discrimination, this may hurt tasks like insertion where relative position is critical. Results corroborate this: gains on insertion are smaller, and the model was not trained specifically for this task. Exploring k-max pooling, positional encodings, or per-column pooling could mitigate this.

- Preprocessing and coreference dependence under-specified: The approach relies on accurate NP detection, grammatical role assignment, and coreference. The paper does not specify which parser/coreference system is used, whether gold or automatic annotations are employed, and how errors affect performance. Without this, it is hard to assess robustness and comparability with baselines (e.g., if different preprocessing was used).

- Baseline coverage and fairness: The exclusion of the Li and Hovy (2014) model due to reproduction issues leaves out a relevant neural baseline; more effort to reproduce or at least report sensitivity analyses would be valuable. Moreover, other competitive baselines (e.g., discourse-relation based models on WSJ, or more recent sentence-ordering neural models) are not included in the WSJ comparison. For DUC’03, extended grid training is noted as incompatible with pairwise training; still, some effort to adapt or include stronger non-neural baselines would clarify the margin.

- Analysis depth: The paper would benefit from ablations showing sensitivity to transition window size k, number of filters, pooling length, and the contribution of each entity-specific feature. Qualitative analysis of learned filters/patterns and error analysis (e.g., where insertion fails) would provide insight into what the network captures and where it falls short.

Questions to authors
- Preprocessing: Which tools and settings were used for NP detection, syntactic role assignment, and coreference resolution? Were gold PTB annotations used at any point, or fully automatic pipelines? Are baselines run with exactly the same preprocessing? How sensitive are results to the coreference system?

- Sensitivity and ablations: How do performance and overfitting vary with k (transition window), pooling length, and number of filters? Can you report curves on DEV to justify chosen hyperparameters? What happens if you remove entity-specific features entirely?

- Positional information: Did you try alternatives to max pooling (e.g., k-max pooling, average pooling, per-column pooling) or adding positional encodings to preserve some location information? Does that help insertion?

- Task-specific training: Have you tried constructing pairwise training instances for insertion (e.g., documents differing only in a sentence position) and does that improve insertion performance?

- Cross-entity modeling: Have you explored convolutions across columns (i.e., across entities), or attention mechanisms that capture interactions between entity transitions?

- Efficiency: What is the parameter count and typical training time? How well does the model scale with document length and number of entities?

Overall, this is a well-executed and timely neural reinterpretation of a classic coherence model with clear empirical gains. Addressing the concerns above—especially preprocessing clarity, stronger baselines, and deeper analysis—would further solidify the contribution.
