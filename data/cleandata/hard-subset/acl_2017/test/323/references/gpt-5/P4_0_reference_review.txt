Summary
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid framework by learning distributed embeddings for entity-role symbols and composing them with a convolutional neural network (CNN) over entity transition sequences. The model is trained with a pairwise ranking loss to score documents by coherence. It aims to (i) capture longer entity-transition dependencies than traditional grid models (which suffer from sparsity with discrete features), and (ii) learn task-specific features end-to-end. The approach is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion on WSJ/PTB) and summary coherence rating (DUC’03), showing consistent improvements over standard entity grid baselines and a graph-based method. A variant including simple entity-specific features (named entity type, salience, proper mention) further improves performance. Code is released.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A CNN-based coherence model over entity grids with learned embeddings for grid symbols, enabling longer transition modeling and end-to-end task-specific training via pairwise ranking.
2) A simple, scalable mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features by composing them with grammatical roles into a unified symbol vocabulary, avoiding combinatorial explosion inherent to discrete transition counts.
3) Empirical improvements over strong entity grid baselines on standard coherence evaluations (WSJ discrimination/insertion and DUC’03 summary rating), including significance testing and transfer via pretraining.

Strengths
- Clear and technically sound architecture: The model is well-specified (lookup embeddings, wide convolution over k-length transitions, max pooling, linear scoring) with a standard, appropriate ranking loss. The independence assumptions (across entity columns and location invariance) are explicitly stated and consistent with the entity grid paradigm.
- Addresses known limitations of grid models: By replacing discrete transition counts with embeddings and CNN filters, the approach mitigates sparsity and supports longer k (reported k≈5–8), directly tackling the “curse of dimensionality” in R^k transitions and combinatorial growth when adding features.
- Strong empirical results with fair comparisons: The neural models outperform both the “all nouns” and the extended grid baselines, with absolute gains reported of ~4% on discrimination and up to ~2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating (with pretraining). Significance testing via approximate randomization is provided. Hyperparameter search and early stopping practices are appropriate.
- Practicality and simplicity of feature integration: Encoding entity-specific attributes (NE type, salience, proper mention) as augmented symbols (e.g., PERSON-S) is straightforward, avoids complex feature engineering, and still yields gains relative to the extended grid that uses more features.
- Reproducibility efforts: Use of standard datasets (WSJ PTB, DUC’03), clear training protocol (pair construction, 20 permutations, DEV split), public toolkits for baselines (Brown coherence toolkit, cohere) and a code release for the proposed method support replicability.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: The main technical move is applying standard CNNs and embeddings to the entity grid representation with a pairwise hinge loss. While effective, this is an incremental extension of a well-known framework rather than a fundamentally new modeling direction for coherence.
- Reporting clarity issues in results: Table 3 appears to mix or duplicate metrics. Discrimination and insertion columns are identical for some systems, and additional “Acc” and “F1” columns are presented without clear linkage to the described tasks (in contrast to the later description of a separate Table 4 for summary rating). This ambiguity makes it difficult to validate the exact magnitude and interpretation of improvements.
- Missing or incomplete baselines: The paper reports excluding Li & Hovy (2014) due to poor reproduced performance and does not include results from discourse-relation-based models (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) in the main tables. Given these are recognized strong approaches for coherence, their absence limits the strength of the comparative claim, especially for insertion and summary rating.
- Insufficient analysis/ablation: The paper does not include analyses on (i) the effect of transition length k, (ii) filter number/embedding dimension, (iii) the contribution of each entity-specific feature, or (iv) the impact of pretraining vs. random initialization beyond a brief mention. There is also no error analysis to characterize when the model succeeds/fails relative to baselines.
- Dependence on coreference/entity detection remains under-specified: The construction of the grid requires entity identification and (often) coreference resolution. The paper does not specify the exact pipeline or its accuracy (especially for “all nouns”), nor does it quantify sensitivity to errors, which is important for robustness and portability beyond WSJ news.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Grid construction and coreference: What specific tools/settings were used for NP/mention detection and coreference resolution? For the “all nouns” setting, are mentions coreferentially clustered into entity columns, or is each noun lemma its own column? How sensitive are results to coreference errors?
- Vocabulary growth and symbol composition: What is the typical size of the symbol vocabulary V when combining grammatical roles with entity features (e.g., PERSON-S, etc.)? Does |V| materially affect training stability or performance? Any OOV handling?
- Transition length and ablation: What is the effect of varying k (e.g., 3, 5, 7) on discrimination and insertion? Could you provide ablations for (i) removing entity-specific features, (ii) different pooling lengths/strides, and (iii) embedding size?
- Baselines: Can you include (or discuss) results for discourse-relational baselines (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) on the same splits? For Li & Hovy (2014), what training details differed from their setup, and can you provide a controlled comparison even if performance is low?
- Metrics and tables: Please clarify the metrics in Table 3 (which columns correspond to which task, units, and whether insertion scores in the table are the average fraction correctly inserted). If there is a separate Table 4 for summary rating, please include it and specify the evaluation measure (e.g., pairwise accuracy, F1).
- Transfer to summary rating: What are the exact pretraining/fine-tuning protocols (frozen vs. updated embeddings/filters, learning rates)? How many summary pairs are used for fine-tuning, and what is the variance across runs?
- Efficiency and scalability: What are training/inference times and memory footprints compared to the extended grid SVM ranker? How do these scale with document length and number of entities?

Overall, the paper presents a clear and technically sound neural adaptation of the entity grid that achieves consistent gains on standard tasks. The main limitations are incremental novelty, incomplete baseline coverage, and some reporting/clarity gaps that can be addressed in rebuttal.
