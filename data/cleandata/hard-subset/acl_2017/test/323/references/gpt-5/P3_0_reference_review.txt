Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes a neuralized variant of the entity grid for local coherence modeling. Grid entries (grammatical roles and optional entity-specific features) are embedded, 1D convolutional filters slide over per-entity transitions to capture longer-range patterns, max-pooling selects salient patterns, and a linear layer scores coherence. The model is trained with a pairwise ranking loss.
- The approach is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) using WSJ and on DUC’03 summary coherence rating. The neural models outperform non-neural grid baselines, with additional gains from pretraining on WSJ before fine-tuning on DUC.
- A simple way to include entity-specific features is proposed by fusing feature values with roles (e.g., PERSON-S), yielding further improvements.

Contributions as I see them
1) A straightforward but effective neural architecture that “neuralizes” the entity grid and mitigates sparsity, enabling longer transition windows and end-to-end, task-specific learning.
2) Empirical evidence that this yields consistent, statistically significant gains on standard coherence evaluations, including a low-data transfer setting for summary rating.
3) A practical recipe for incorporating entity features into the neural grid with minimal engineering.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea with good motivation. Moving from discrete transition counts to learned embeddings plus convolution is a natural way to address sparsity and to learn task-specific features. The per-entity 1D CNN over transitions is an appropriate architectural choice under the stated assumptions.
- Consistent improvements with significance. The neural models outperform their non-neural counterparts in both discrimination (+~4% absolute) and insertion (+~1–2.5% absolute), and on DUC’03 summary rating (+>4% with pretraining). The comparison to the strongest grid baseline (Elsner & Charniak 2011) is particularly informative, showing gains even though the neural model uses fewer features.
- Sensible training protocol and hyperparameter search. Pairwise ranking, use of a held-out dev set, multiple random restarts, dropout, and significance testing are sound choices. Showing that pretraining on WSJ helps on DUC addresses data scarcity pragmatically.
- Simplicity and applicability. The model is compact, requires only entity grids, and is easy to integrate in broader systems. The idea should transfer to coherence-sensitive applications (summarization, generation) with little overhead. Code release is a plus for adoption.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental. The core idea—embedding discrete grid symbols and applying a CNN over local sequences—is a direct adaptation of standard CNNs for sequences to a well-known representation. The contribution is more an effective instantiation than a conceptual advance in coherence modeling.
- Modeling assumptions and information loss. Columns (entities) are treated independently, and max-pooling removes even relative positional information along the document. Important cross-entity interactions and position-dependent phenomena (e.g., introductory vs concluding patterns) are not modeled. A 2D convolution or attention across entities, or k-max pooling preserving order, could potentially capture richer structure.
- Limited ablation and analysis. There is no systematic study of the effect of transition length k, pooling size, number of filters, or the separate contribution of each entity-specific feature. Claims about modeling “sufficiently long transitions” would be stronger with a k-sweep and direct comparison to k≤3. Likewise, qualitative analysis of learned filters/patterns and error analysis are missing.
- Preprocessing details and reproducibility. The paper alternately mentions using a coreference resolver and matching nouns to detect coreferent entities; this is a crucial source of variance. The specific parser/coreference system, mention extraction, how non-head nouns are handled, average number of entities per document, and any capping or pruning are not described. Without these details, replication and fair comparison are difficult, and the impact of coreference accuracy on results is not assessed.
- Evaluation scope and baselines. Heavy reliance on random permutations is a known limitation of coherence evaluation; the insertion setup is more realistic but the model is not trained for it. The DUC’03 setting is very small. The exclusion of Li & Hovy (2014) due to poor reproduction (19% on WSJ) without deeper diagnosis is unsatisfying; at minimum, their numbers should be reported and discussed, and potential dataset/task mismatches clarified. More recent discourse-aware or neural ordering baselines are not considered.

Questions to authors
- Coreference and entity extraction: Which coreference resolver and syntactic analyzer are used? Do you include non-head nouns, and how are nominal variants (e.g., “the president” vs “Obama”) unified? Please reconcile the apparent inconsistency between using a resolver and “matching nouns.”
- Transition modeling: Do filters operate strictly within one column (entity) at a time? Have you tried 2D convolutions or mechanisms that capture cross-entity interactions?
- Position information: Did you experiment with k-max pooling or positional encodings to retain some location information? How sensitive are results to pooling size?
- Long-range transitions: Please report performance as a function of k and compare to k≤3 to substantiate the “long transition” advantage.
- Feature integration: Concatenating role and entity features into a single symbol increases |V|. Did you try factorized embeddings (e.g., separate embeddings for role and feature type with composition)? How does vocabulary size affect training time and performance?
- Insertion training: If trained directly for insertion with pairwise candidates differing by one sentence position, does performance improve?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed incremental contribution. The method is simple, effective, and likely useful to practitioners, but the paper would benefit from clearer preprocessing details, stronger ablations/analysis, and a more complete comparison set.
