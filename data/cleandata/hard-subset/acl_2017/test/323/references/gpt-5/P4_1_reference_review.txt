Summary
The paper proposes a neural model for local coherence assessment that operates over entity grids. Each grammatical role (e.g., S/O/X/-), optionally augmented with entity-specific features (e.g., named entity type, salience, proper mention), is embedded into a continuous vector space. Convolutional filters are applied along columns of the grid (i.e., entity transitions across sentences) to detect salient transition patterns, followed by max pooling and a linear projection to produce a document-level coherence score. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective using (original, permuted) sentence order pairs. Experiments on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence ranking show consistent improvements over non-neural entity-grid baselines and a graph-based method. The authors provide implementation details, hyperparameter tuning, statistical significance testing, and report that pretraining on WSJ further helps on the small DUC dataset.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A neuralized entity-grid coherence model that uses CNNs over embedded entity transitions, enabling longer transition modeling and end-to-end task-driven learning. This is the primary technical contribution.
2) A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features within the neural grid via compositional symbols (e.g., PERSON-S) embedded jointly, reducing sparsity relative to discrete feature engineering.
3) An empirical demonstration of consistent gains over strong entity-grid baselines across three evaluation tasks, with training and implementation details that facilitate reproducibility.

Strengths
- Clear problem setup and architecture: The paper is clear about limitations of discrete entity grids (sparsity, task-agnostic feature extraction) and motivates how embeddings + convolution address them. The model components (lookup layer, wide convolution along entity columns, max pooling, linear scoring) and the pairwise hinge loss are standard and well specified. Hyperparameters, optimization choices (RMSProp), regularization (dropout, early stopping), and significance testing are documented.

- Technical soundness and appropriate modeling assumptions: Treating columns independently mirrors the entity-grid assumption of per-entity transitions, and using wide convolution with zero padding ensures filters cover entire transitions. The choice of length k up to 5–8 addresses the curse of dimensionality in discrete grids. The pairwise ranking objective matches prior work on coherence assessment.

- Strong empirical results on established tasks: On WSJ, the neural models outperform non-neural counterparts: Grid-CNN improves over Grid-all-nouns by ~4% absolute in discrimination and ~1% in insertion; Extended Grid-CNN improves over Extended Grid by ~3.7% in discrimination and ~2.5% in insertion (Table 3), with significance testing (p<0.01). On DUC’03 summary ranking, pretraining on WSJ yields further gains, which is reasonable given the small training size.

- Simplicity and generality: The approach requires only the entity grid and optional simple entity features; it avoids reliance on external discourse parsers. The mechanism to add features by composing them with roles is straightforward and yields gains even with only three features, while outperforming a non-neural extended grid that used more features.

- Reproducibility: Use of public toolkits for baselines, clear dataset splits (WSJ sections), number of permutations, multiple random seeds, and released code support repeatability.

Weaknesses
- Limited baseline coverage for claimed state of the art: While the paper compares to graph-based coherence and extended entity grids, it does not include results for discourse-relation-based models (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) or multiple-rank learning (Feng & Hirst, 2012), which have reported competitive performance. The exclusion weakens the SOTA claim and makes it difficult to assess relative standing to discourse-structure methods.

- Omitted neural baseline: The authors attempted to reproduce Li & Hovy (2014) and obtained very low accuracy, then excluded it from the results table. Excluding a published neural baseline due to implementation difficulties is problematic; even a negative but reported number, along with diagnostic analysis (e.g., dataset mismatch, hyperparameters), would improve fairness and transparency.

- Modeling independence assumptions: The model treats entity columns independently and applies location-invariant max pooling, ignoring interactions among entities and positional information across the document. This may limit capturing coherence phenomena involving multiple entities or global transitions. The paper does not explore alternatives (e.g., attention across entities, positional encoding) or ablation on pooling choice.

- Feature-composition trade-offs and ablations: Encoding entity features by concatenating them with roles increases the vocabulary (e.g., PERSON-S), which could introduce sparsity in low-resource settings. The paper does not quantify the trade-off or report ablations isolating the impact of: (i) embedding vs. convolution, (ii) transition length k, (iii) each entity-specific feature, and (iv) pooling size. Such analyses would strengthen claims about why the model works.

- Dependence on preprocessing quality and error analysis: The approach relies on coreference resolution and syntactic roles to build the grid. There is no analysis of robustness to coreference or parsing errors, nor qualitative error analysis on insertion vs. discrimination to explain where the model succeeds/fails. Also, the model is trained on discrimination but evaluated on insertion; training on insertion could be explored to test task specificity.

Questions to Authors
1) Baselines: Can you include results or discuss why discourse-relation-based coherence models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) and multiple-rank learning (Feng & Hirst, 2012) were not compared on WSJ? If not feasible, please contextualize your results relative to their reported numbers.

2) Li & Hovy (2014): Please report your reproduced number explicitly and provide details (hyperparameters, data preprocessing, sentence encoders, window size) and any hypotheses for the discrepancy. Did you try the authors’ recommended settings or reach out to them?

3) Ablations and sensitivity: 
- How does performance vary with transition length k and pooling length p? 
- What is the contribution of embeddings alone vs. adding convolution? 
- What is the incremental gain of each of the three entity-specific features?

4) Positional and cross-entity information: Did you try adding positional encodings or filters over multiple entity columns (e.g., 2D convolutions) to capture interactions across entities? If so, with what effect?

5) Preprocessing robustness: Which coreference resolver and parser were used? Can you quantify the impact of coreference errors (e.g., by swapping in a weaker/stronger resolver) on your model vs. discrete grids?

6) Training for insertion: Have you tried training the pairwise ranker on insertion-specific pairs (documents differing by a single sentence position)? If so, does it improve insertion accuracy?

Overall assessment
Clarity: High. Technical soundness: Solid, using standard neural components appropriately. Novelty: Incremental but meaningful—neuralizing entity grids with CNNs to capture longer transitions and learn task-specific features. Empirical improvements are convincing within the chosen baseline set; broader comparisons and ablations would strengthen the case.
