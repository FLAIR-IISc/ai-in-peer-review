Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid by replacing discrete transition counts with learned distributed representations and a convolutional architecture trained with a pairwise ranking objective. The approach aims to (i) capture longer range entity transitions while avoiding sparsity, and (ii) learn task-specific features end-to-end. The model is evaluated on three established tasks: sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) on WSJ and summary coherence rating on DUC’03. Reported gains over strong entity-grid baselines are 3–4 points on discrimination, up to 2.5 points on insertion, and ~3–4 points on summary rating, with significance tests and some discussion of hyperparameters.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Method: A CNN over embedded entity-grid entries with pairwise ranking, enabling longer transition windows and end-to-end learning. This is a clear, focused, and practically effective refinement of the entity-grid paradigm.
2) Empirics: Consistent, statistically significant improvements over strong grid baselines on three coherence tasks, including an ablation-like comparison between grid-only and feature-augmented neural models. Use of a larger WSJ corpus strengthens conclusions.
3) Reproducibility assets: A relatively transparent description of architecture, training, and hyperparameter search; claim of code release (though no link is provided in the paper).

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated architecture that addresses known limitations of entity grids. The switch from discrete transition probability tables to embeddings plus convolution directly tackles the curse of dimensionality for longer k and enables learning richer local patterns. The assumption of location-invariant filters and max pooling is appropriate for grid-column transitions and aligns with the intuition behind detecting salient transition patterns.
- Strong empirical gains on standard tasks with proper significance testing. Improvements over “extended grid” baselines (E&C) are consistent across discrimination and insertion; notably, the neural model with only three entity features outperforms the E&C variant using nine features, suggesting the value of learned representations. On DUC’03 summary coherence rating, the pretraining/fine-tuning setup is sensible and yields further gains despite limited data.
- Solid experimental setup and reporting. The paper uses a large WSJ split, reports development tuning, multiple random seeds, early stopping, dropout, and adaptive optimizers. Baselines include both simple and stronger grid-based systems, and statistical significance is reported with an appropriate test.
- Conceptual simplicity with practical utility. The method stays close to a widely used model (entity grid), making it easy to adopt in pipelines that already rely on coreference and syntactic roles, while providing measurable improvements.
- Potential for impact and extension. The approach is modular (embeddings, filters, features), and the end-to-end ranking framework is adaptable to downstream generation and scoring tasks; the discussion about adding discourse relations is plausible and straightforward to integrate.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural coherence work and CNNs in NLP. The contribution is primarily an application of standard CNN + embeddings + pairwise ranking to the entity grid. While effective, it is an incremental advance rather than a fundamentally new modeling idea for coherence. The discussion contrasts with Li & Hovy (2014), but a deeper analytical comparison is lacking.
- Incomplete baseline coverage and replication concerns. The paper reports an unsuccessful attempt to reproduce Li & Hovy (2014) and omits it from the results. This weakens the comparative picture; at minimum, a careful analysis of why the model underperformed (domain mismatch, hyperparameters, training objective differences) or comparison to published numbers on a shared dataset would be appropriate. Also, RST/discourse-based baselines (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) are discussed but not included in the WSJ experiments here.
- Evaluation scope and task limitations. Sentence permutation discrimination is known to be easy and sometimes weakly correlated with human coherence judgments; insertion is better but still synthetic. The DUC’03 summary rating set is very small (6 clusters), and the gains rely partly on pretraining from a different domain/task. A broader evaluation (e.g., more domains, full ordering metrics, or additional human judgments) would strengthen claims.
- Modeling assumptions may miss important interactions. Treating columns (entities) independently and pooling over locations ignores cross-entity interactions and positional trends that can matter for coherence (e.g., topic shifts, entity introduction/retirement patterns across the document). While inherited from the grid, the neural model could in principle capture cross-column interactions; the current architecture does not.
- Analysis and ablations are light. There is little insight into what the model learns: no ablation on transition window size k, pooling choices, embedding initialization, or the impact of individual entity features. No error analysis or sensitivity to coreference quality is provided, yet the approach depends critically on accurate coreference and syntactic roles.

Questions to authors
- Which coreference resolver and syntactic role assignment pipeline were used for WSJ and DUC? How sensitive are the results to coreference quality (e.g., using a weaker/stronger resolver)?
- How large were the optimal transition window sizes k and pooling lengths p across tasks, and how do results vary with k? Any evidence that longer transitions (k > 3) drive the gains?
- Can you provide a head-to-head comparison with an RST-based model (e.g., Feng et al., 2014) on the same WSJ split? If not, why?
- For Li & Hovy (2014), what settings/datasets were used in your reimplementation, and what diagnostics suggest why the replication failed? Could you report their published results on a shared benchmark for context?
- Do multiple occurrences of a pattern in a document matter? Max pooling selects only the strongest activation. Did you try k-max pooling or average pooling to retain multiple signals?

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed, and practically useful paper that advances a widely used modeling line with a simple neural architecture and demonstrates consistent, statistically significant improvements across standard tasks. While the novelty is incremental and the evaluation scope could be broader, the methodological clarity, empirical rigor, and potential for adoption merit acceptance. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the camera-ready should strengthen baseline comparisons and add analysis/ablations to enhance insight and reproducibility.
