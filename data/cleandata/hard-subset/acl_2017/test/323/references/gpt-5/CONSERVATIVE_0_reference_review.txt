Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a neuralized variant of the entity grid for modeling local coherence. The model embeds categorical role/feature symbols, applies 1D convolutions over column-wise entity transitions with max pooling, and trains end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective. It evaluates on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence ranking, reporting improvements over classic entity-grid baselines and a graph-based model.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A straightforward neural reformulation of the entity grid using CNNs and learned embeddings, enabling longer transition windows and end-to-end training with task-specific objectives.
2) Empirical results on multiple benchmarks (WSJ discrimination/insertion; DUC’03 ratings), with statistically significant gains over conventional grid baselines; a simple cross-task pretraining/fine-tuning strategy for the small DUC dataset.
3) A practical recipe to incorporate a few entity-specific features into the neural grid.

Strengths
- Sound, task-aligned objective and clean architecture: Pairwise ranking is standard for coherence assessment; the network mirrors the grid pipeline (lookup → convolution → pooling → scoring), and the location-invariant convolution matches the local pattern-detection goal. The approach is simple and reproducible (Theano code reportedly released), and the training details (optimizer, early stopping, multiple seeds, significance testing) are reasonably thorough.
- Consistent gains over established grid baselines: On WSJ, the neuralized grid (with all-nouns) improves ~4% absolute on discrimination and ~1% on insertion; the neural extended grid further improves both tasks (up to +2.5% insertion), with significance vs. non-neural counterparts. On DUC’03, pretraining on WSJ followed by fine-tuning yields additional improvements. These are meaningful deltas against widely used baselines.
- Data and setup largely standard: WSJ sections split as in prior work; 20 random permutations for discrimination; insertion as in Elsner & Charniak. This makes comparison to the entity-grid literature more credible.
- Efficiency and simplicity: The method avoids sentence-level encoders and relies on compact, interpretable inputs (entity grids). Implementation/training costs appear modest.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond “neuralizing” an established model: Using embeddings and CNNs over categorical grids with a ranking loss is a relatively direct application of standard neural components. The conceptual assumptions (independent columns; local, position-invariant patterns) mirror the classic grid, and there is little theoretical or analytical justification for why CNNs capture “long-range” entity transitions better than carefully tuned higher-order transitions or alternative sequence models. The contribution is mainly engineering rather than conceptual.
- Incomplete and uneven baselines: The comparison excludes several strong coherence/order baselines that do not rely on the entity grid, e.g., sentence/paragraph-level neural coherence (Li & Hovy 2014; other sentence-ordering models), discourse relation transition models (Lin et al. 2011; Feng et al. 2014, RST-based), or more recent graph/order models. The authors tried Li & Hovy but obtained poor performance and then omitted it. That is not satisfactory: either diagnose/tune to a reasonable reproduction or include a careful discussion. Claims of state-of-the-art are therefore not well supported.
- Sparse ablations and analysis: The paper does not probe what drives the gains. Missing ablations include: effect of transition window size k (especially to support the “long-range” claim), pooling/window choices, number of filters, and the contribution of each entity-specific feature. There is no analysis of the sensitivity to coreference errors, which are known to critically shape entity grids, nor any alternative coref settings. No qualitative analysis of learned filters/patterns is provided.
- Potential reintroduction of sparsity via compositional features: The method encodes feature-role pairs as atomic symbols (e.g., PERSON-S). As more features are added, the vocabulary |V| can explode, undermining the stated advantage over discrete grids. An additive/compositional embedding of separate feature slots (role, NE type, salience bin, proper flag) would share parameters and likely generalize better; this is neither proposed nor evaluated.
- Evaluation clarity and metrics: Some tables/metrics are unclear (e.g., “Discr., Ins., Acc, F1” in Table 3). For the DUC task, the exact evaluation measure (pairwise accuracy vs. Kendall’s τ/ρ) and comparison protocol to Barzilay & Lapata (2008) are not clearly specified. Given the small DUC dataset, overfitting risks are high; stronger reporting (confidence intervals, exact metric) is needed.
- Scope of coherence: All tasks are primarily sensitive to local entity continuity in permutations. The method claims “long-range” modeling with k up to 8, but this is still local and does not engage discourse relations or global structure. This is acknowledged as future work, but the claims should be tempered.

Questions to authors
- What coreference system and syntactic pipeline were used to build the grids? Did you vary the coreference system or evaluate robustness to coref errors?
- For DUC’03, what is the exact evaluation metric and protocol (pairwise accuracy, Kendall’s τ)? How do your numbers align with the original setup in Barzilay & Lapata (2008)?
- Can you report ablations on: (i) transition window size k; (ii) removing each entity-specific feature; (iii) replacing atomic feature-role tokens with compositional embeddings? This would clarify the source of gains and whether “long-range” transitions matter.
- Can you provide a fair, tuned comparison to Li & Hovy (2014) (and/or other non-grid neural order models) on WSJ? If reproduction failed, what configurations were tried, and can you collaborate with the authors for a reliable baseline?
- Have you tried training directly on insertion-style pairs (i.e., moving one sentence) to assess task-specific benefits versus discrimination-trained models?

Overall, the paper is a solid, well-executed neural reimplementation of a classic approach with consistent empirical gains on standard tasks. However, the conceptual novelty is modest, the baseline coverage is insufficient for strong SOTA claims, and the analysis is too light to substantiate the central claims (especially about longer transitions and feature integration).
