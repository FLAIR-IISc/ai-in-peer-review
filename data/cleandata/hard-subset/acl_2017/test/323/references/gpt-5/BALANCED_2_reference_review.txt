Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neuralized variant of the entity grid for modeling local coherence. Grammatical role symbols in the grid are embedded and composed with 1D convolutions along each entity column to capture longer-range transition patterns. The model is trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking objective, and can incorporate simple entity-specific features by expanding the symbol vocabulary. Experiments on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating show consistent gains over standard and extended entity-grid baselines and a graph-based model.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple, effective neural architecture (embedding + convolution + pooling over entity transitions) that outperforms strong entity-grid baselines across three coherence evaluation tasks, yielding state-of-the-art results on the reported setups.
2) An end-to-end pairwise ranking training regime that learns task-specific features and allows pretraining on WSJ to transfer to summary ranking.
3) A practical way to incorporate entity-specific features within the neural framework (via symbol augmentation), achieving further gains even with a subset of prior engineered features.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over strong baselines. The neural grid consistently outperforms the “all-nouns” and “extended” grid models:
  • Discrimination: Grid-CNN 85.57 vs 81.58 (all-nouns) and Extended Grid-CNN 88.69 vs 84.95 (extended), with significance at p < 0.01.
  • Insertion: up to +2.5 absolute over extended grid. These are meaningful improvements on well-established tasks with many prior attempts.
  • Summary rating: additional gains, further improved by pretraining on WSJ.
  Using multiple runs with different seeds and significance testing strengthens the empirical case.

- Addresses key limitations of entity grids in a principled way. The distributed representation + convolution directly tackles sparsity and short-transition limits (k ≤ 3) of discrete grids. The ability to use longer windows (k ≈ 5–8) without overfitting is well-motivated and supported by results.

- End-to-end, task-specific training and transfer. The pairwise ranking objective is appropriate for coherence scoring and mirrors prior practice; pretraining on WSJ for summary rating is a judicious design that shows the framework’s flexibility under limited supervision.

- Conceptual simplicity and practicality. The model is easy to implement, computationally light compared to sentence-level compositional models, and integrates cleanly with existing preprocessing (NP extraction and coreference). Releasing code increases reproducibility.

- Solid experimental setup. The WSJ split, use of 20 permutations, dev tuning, and reporting of hyperparameters are appropriate. Comparisons include the strongest non-neural baselines commonly used for these tasks.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty. The core technical move—embedding symbolic grid entries and applying 1D convolutions with max pooling—is a straightforward application of standard CNNs to an existing representation. While effective, the methodological novelty is limited relative to prior neural coherence work (e.g., Li & Hovy 2014), and the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of why CNNs over grids work and what patterns the filters learn.

- Independence assumptions and limited modeling capacity. Treating columns (entities) independently ignores cross-entity interactions and global discourse signals (e.g., entity co-occurrence patterns, topic shifts). The architecture is strictly 1D along columns; no mechanism models inter-entity dependencies or cross-sentence features outside entity transitions. This may cap performance and limits theoretical appeal.

- Incomplete comparative evaluation and diagnostics. The comparison to Li & Hovy (2014) is inconclusive; the reimplementation performed very poorly (19.34% discrimination), but without analysis or hyperparameter exploration. Stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., coherence via discourse relations or recent coref-based models) are cited but not fully compared on the same splits. Ablations are limited: we do not see the effect of transition window length, pooling size, number of filters, or each entity-specific feature; nor the impact of pretraining vs training from scratch on each task.

- Heavy reliance on coreference without analysis. Performance of entity-grid methods is sensitive to coreference quality. The paper does not report which resolver is used, nor an ablation or sensitivity analysis across resolvers or with oracle mentions. This makes it hard to assess robustness and portability.

- Presentation issues and minor inconsistencies. There are notational inaccuracies (e.g., feature map length “m.n+k−1”), and the results tables appear inconsistent: Table 3 header includes “Acc F1” though insertion is not typically reported with F1; Table 4 (summary rating) is referenced but not shown in the excerpt. Clarifying metrics (e.g., insertion metric definition) and fixing table references would improve clarity.

Questions to authors
- Training data generation: For discrimination, do you train on all 20 permutations per document as negative instances? Do you weight negatives or perform any hard-negative mining? For insertion, did you attempt task-specific training (e.g., constructing pairwise examples from candidate insertions), and if so, how did it compare?

- Normalization and length effects: Is the coherence score normalized wrt document length or number of entities? How sensitive is performance to document length (e.g., shorter articles where discrimination is harder)?

- Hyperparameters and ablations: Can you report ablations on k (transition window), pooling length, and number of filters? How much of the gain comes from longer windows vs simply having embeddings and convolution? What is the performance of the CNN without max pooling?

- Entity-specific features: When augmenting symbols (e.g., PERSON-S), how large does the vocabulary get, and how does it affect training stability? Did you try alternative incorporations (e.g., concatenating role and feature embeddings rather than symbol conflation)?

- Coreference: Which coreference system and settings are used? Any experiments with different resolvers or gold mentions? How does performance change if you restrict to head nouns vs all noun mentions?

- Comparison to Li & Hovy (2014): What hyperparameters and pretrained embeddings did you try for their model? Could you provide validation performance and a brief error analysis to substantiate the claim that their method underperforms on WSJ in your setup?

- Efficiency and scalability: What are training/inference runtimes and memory usage on WSJ? How does complexity scale with number of entities, and do you subsample low-salience entities?

- Robustness and transfer: Have you tried cross-domain evaluation (e.g., training on WSJ, testing on AIRPLANE/EARTHQUAKE) to assess generalization? Any analysis by genre or topic?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed application of CNNs to the entity grid with consistent empirical improvements and a clear training protocol. While the novelty is moderate and analysis could be deeper, the work is likely to be of interest to the coherence modeling community and seems publishable with clarifications and expanded diagnostics.
