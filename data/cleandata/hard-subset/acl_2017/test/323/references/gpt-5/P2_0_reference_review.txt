Summary and claimed contributions (as I see them)
- The paper proposes a neuralized entity-grid model for local coherence: grammatical-role symbols in the grid are embedded; 1D convolutions over entity transitions capture longer-range patterns; max-pooling and a linear layer produce a document coherence score trained with pairwise ranking.
- It shows how to incorporate simple entity-specific features by conjoining feature values with grammatical roles and embedding the resulting symbols.
- Experiments on three standard tasks (WSJ discrimination and insertion; DUC’03 summary coherence rating) report consistent, statistically significant gains over non-neural entity grid variants and a graph-based baseline, with relatively simple features and end-to-end training.

Main contributions, in order of strength
1) A practical, task-trained neural architecture over entity grids that enables modeling of longer transition lengths without combinatorial sparsity, yielding state-of-the-art results across multiple coherence evaluations.
2) An end-to-end pairwise-ranking training setup for coherence that learns task-specific features from data, with clear gains over hand-crafted transition-probability features.
3) A simple method to fold entity-specific signals (NE type, salience, properness) into the neural grid, achieving further improvements despite using fewer features than prior extended grids.

Strengths
- Empirical effectiveness and breadth: The model consistently outperforms strong, widely used baselines (basic entity grid with “all nouns” and the extended grid of Elsner & Charniak) by substantial margins (+4% absolute on discrimination; up to +2.5% on insertion; +3–4% on summary rating with pretraining). Statistical significance testing and multiple runs mitigate variance concerns. Evaluating on three tasks strengthens the claim that the approach generalizes across coherence settings.
- Soundness and simplicity of the method: Mapping discrete grid symbols to embeddings and applying convolution across transitions is a well-motivated way to capture longer patterns (k=5–8) without explosion of parameters. The pairwise hinge-loss training aligns with the ranking nature of coherence assessment and directly optimizes the evaluation objective.
- Reproducibility and clarity of experimental setup: The paper provides hyperparameter search spaces, chosen settings, optimizer details, early stopping, and significance testing protocol. Baselines are reimplemented with public toolkits; datasets and split details follow prior work. The architecture is clearly described with equations and a figure.
- Fairness of feature usage: The neural model improves over the extended grid even though it uses only a subset (3/9) of entity features and no external corpora, suggesting the gains come from the learned representations rather than extra supervision.
- Potential impact and extensibility: The approach is modular (drop-in for grid-based pipelines), data-driven, and compatible with richer signals (e.g., discourse relations), making it a strong foundation for future coherence modeling.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The paper is largely a “neuralization” of a well-known representation. While effective, the conceptual advance is incremental relative to prior grids and to existing neural coherence work (e.g., Li & Hovy 2014), differing mainly in the input representation (entities vs. sentence windows) and the use of CNNs. The paper would benefit from deeper theoretical or analytical insights into what is learned beyond “longer transitions.”
- Independence assumptions and loss of interactions: Treating entity columns independently and using max-pooling can discard cross-entity dependencies and positional nuances (e.g., interactions among multiple salient entities across adjacent sentences). This may limit modeling of phenomena like topic shifts or coordinated referential moves. No experiments test whether modeling inter-entity interactions helps.
- Evaluation design gaps: 
  - Discrimination uses 20 random permutations; no “hard” perturbations (local swaps, reverse order, one-swap) are reported, making it hard to gauge sensitivity to subtle local coherence breaks.
  - Insertion is evaluated but the model is trained only on discrimination pairs; unsurprisingly, gains are smaller. A version trained/listwise-tuned for insertion (or with task-specific negative sampling) would be a fairer comparison.
  - The paper excludes Li & Hovy (2014) due to poor reproduction without deeper analysis or collaboration to obtain competitive settings; this weakens the comparative claims versus contemporary neural baselines.
- Grid construction and preprocessing clarity: It is unclear which coreference resolution and NP detection pipeline is used; one note suggests noun matching rather than full coreference. Coherence models are sensitive to coref quality; lack of detail and ablations (e.g., with/without pronoun resolution) limit conclusions about robustness.
- Limited analysis and ablations: Beyond showing “Grid-CNN” vs. “Extended Grid-CNN,” there is no ablation on individual features, convolution window size k, number of filters, pooling length, or the contribution of pretraining. Qualitative analyses (e.g., what filters detect) are missing. The small DUC’03 training set raises overfitting concerns; pretraining helps but cross-domain robustness is not explored.

Questions to authors
- What coreference and NP identification tools/settings were used to build the grids? Do you resolve pronouns and nominal coreference, or only match head nouns? Please quantify coref accuracy or show sensitivity to coref errors.
- How does performance vary with transition window size k and pooling length p? Is there an optimal k; do very long k hurt due to over-smoothing?
- Did you try training specifically for insertion (e.g., pairwise/listwise losses over insertion positions or sampled near-miss negatives)? If yes, how did results change?
- Can you provide qualitative examples of high-activation filters and the transition patterns they capture? Do they correspond to known coherent patterns (e.g., sustained subjects, reintroductions)?
- How are entity-specific features encoded exactly (e.g., concatenated symbol PERSON-S vs. multi-hot features)? Are embeddings shared across roles and feature values?
- What is the computational cost relative to the extended grid (training time, inference per document), and how does it scale with document length and number of entities?

Overall assessment
- Soundness/correctness: Strong; method is technically solid and aligned with objectives.
- Originality: Moderate; an effective but incremental neural adaptation of a standard representation.
- Meaningful comparison: Adequate vs. entity-grid and graph baselines; incomplete vs. other neural coherence models.
- Clarity: Good; architecture and training are clear; some preprocessing details need clarification.
- Reproducibility: Good; code promised; hyperparameters and protocols provided.

Given the strong empirical gains across tasks and clear, well-executed method, I lean toward accept, with the caveat that comparative breadth and analysis could be strengthened.
