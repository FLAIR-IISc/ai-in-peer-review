Summary and main contributions
- Proposes a neuralized entity-grid model for local coherence: grammatical roles and entity-specific features are embedded; a 1D convolution over per-entity sentence transitions with max-pooling yields a document score trained via pairwise ranking.
- Demonstrates consistent gains over traditional (non-neural) entity-grid baselines on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating; shows that modest pretraining on WSJ further helps on DUC.
- Provides an end-to-end framework that can, in principle, model longer transitions than count-based grids and can incorporate entity-level attributes by token-augmenting the grid.

Strengths
- Grounded extension of an established framework. Building on the entity grid is methodologically sound and makes the modeling assumptions transparent (local transitions, entity salience). The move to distributed representations and convolution is a natural way to mitigate sparsity and handle longer k.
- Clear, simple architecture and training objective. The model is easy to reproduce conceptually (embedding lookup → per-entity convolution → pooling → linear scoring; hinge loss with shared weights), with reasonable regularization (dropout, early stopping) and multiple random seeds.
- Consistent improvements over strong grid baselines. On WSJ, the Grid-CNN improves over “all nouns” and the Extended Grid-CNN improves over the Extended Grid, with reported statistical significance. It is notable that the neural model with only three entity features outperforms the extended grid that uses nine features.
- Sensible use of pretraining for low-resource setting. Pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning on DUC yields further gains, suggesting the learned transition filters generalize.
- Code release and details. The use of standard toolkits (Brown coherence toolkit for baselines; Theano) and reporting of hyperparameter search and significance testing support reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. A CNN over embedded entity-role symbols to model longer local transitions is a relatively straightforward application of standard neural components to an established representation. The contributions are incremental rather than conceptual advances in coherence modeling.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair baseline coverage. The paper omits comparisons to discourse-relation-based approaches known to be competitive (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014 with RST). The Li and Hovy (2014) sentence-representation baseline is excluded due to poor replication, but the result is an empirical picture centered largely on entity-grid methods and one graph-based system.
- Metric/reporting issues for insertion/discrimination. Table 3 appears inconsistent: discrimination and insertion figures are identical per method, and the random baseline shows 50% for insertion, which contradicts the definition (expected ≈ 1/avg #sentences). This raises concerns about metric computation, labeling, or a copy/paste error. The paper must clarify and correct these results.
- Weaknesses of the evaluation protocols not addressed. Random permutation discrimination is a coarse proxy; insertion as implemented here is harder but still synthetic. There is no evaluation on more realistic downstream tasks (e.g., sentence ordering datasets with human order, generation reranking), nor cross-domain tests (AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES are dismissed rather than used to assess robustness).
- Missing ablations and analysis. The paper lacks diagnostics on: effect of transition length k; contribution of each entity feature; impact of pretraining vs. training from scratch; comparisons to simple neural alternatives (e.g., MLP over transition histograms or RNN over per-entity sequences); interactions across entities (the model assumes column independence). No error analysis or qualitative pattern inspection is provided to substantiate the “filter detects transition pattern” claim.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the evaluation metrics and numbers in Table 3. Are the discrimination and insertion scores both accuracies? Why are they identical across methods, and why is the random insertion score 50%? If insertion is “fraction correctly reinserted,” random should be ≪ 50%. Provide corrected results and definitions.
- Which coreference and syntactic tools were used to build the grids? Are they trained on WSJ (potential domain advantage), and are their errors controlled for? Did you use gold parses anywhere?
- Why not include RST/discourse-relation baselines (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) that have reported strong performance on similar tasks? If unavailable, can you at least compare to published numbers on the same WSJ split?
- How sensitive are results to the transition window size k, pooling size, and embedding dimension? Please provide an ablation table and a plot showing performance vs. k.
- What is the vocabulary size |V| when including entity-specific features via concatenated tokens (e.g., PERSON-S)? Does this lead to sparsity for rarer feature combinations, and how do embeddings mitigate it? Any OOV handling for unseen feature-role combinations at test time?
- Did you try modeling interactions across entities (e.g., 2D convolutions across columns) or attention over entities? Given the independence assumption, do you observe cases where cross-entity interactions would help?
- For DUC’03, how many parameters are fine-tuned, and do you freeze embeddings or filters? What is the performance without fine-tuning (purely pretrained), to separate transfer from adaptation?

Additional comments
- The bibliography has multiple malformed/incomplete entries; please fix for camera-ready.
- Consider reporting computational cost (training time, inference speed) compared to grid baselines.
- A brief qualitative analysis (filters activating on interpretable patterns) would strengthen the methodological claims.
