Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural architecture for local coherence assessment that operates over entity grids. The model learns distributed embeddings for grid entries (grammatical role plus optional entity-specific features), composes transition patterns via 1D convolution across sentence-wise columns (entities), and aggregates with max pooling to yield a document-level coherence score trained with a pairwise ranking loss. The approach aims to (i) capture longer-range entity transitions without sparsity and (ii) learn task-specific features end-to-end. The model is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) using WSJ and on summary coherence rating (DUC’03), showing consistent gains over entity grid baselines and a graph-based model.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A neuralized entity grid: replacing discrete transition count features with learned embeddings and convolutional composition to capture longer-range transitions and avoid combinatorial sparsity. This is the primary technical contribution.
2) End-to-end, task-specific training via pairwise ranking, applied across discrimination, insertion, and summary rating, with state-of-the-art results versus non-neural entity grid baselines.
3) A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features into the neural grid and a demonstration that even with a reduced feature set, the neural model outperforms a stronger non-neural baseline.

Strengths
- Clear architectural design with sound learning objective: The mapping from grid symbols to embeddings, convolution across transition windows, max pooling, and a linear scoring layer with a hinge loss is coherent and technically solid. The use of wide convolution with zero-padding appropriately handles boundary effects and varying column lengths. Pairwise ranking aligns with the nature of coherence scoring and prior work.

- Addresses known limitations of entity grids: The paper clearly motivates the move from discrete transition features (which suffer from sparsity as transition length/features increase) to distributed representations. By learning embeddings and filters across the corpus, the model plausibly captures longer dependencies (k up to 5–8) and reduces the parameter explosion from feature cross-products, which is confirmed by improved results.

- Empirical improvements and careful comparisons: On WSJ discrimination and insertion, the neural basic grid (Grid-CNN) improves over the all-nouns grid; the extended neural model further improves and surpasses the extended grid baseline despite using fewer entity features. Statistical significance testing is reported for major comparisons. The summary rating task shows additional gains, with reasonable use of pretraining given the small supervision.

- Reproducibility practices: The paper details model hyperparameter search ranges, optimization settings (RMSprop, dropout, early stopping), and claims code release. Baselines are reproduced using standard toolkits (Brown coherence toolkit, cohere), which helps ensure fair comparisons.

- Justified dataset choice and broad evaluation: The choice of WSJ (larger, less stylistically constrained) is justified, and the evaluation covers both sentence ordering (including the more challenging insertion) and human-ranked summary coherence, increasing the external validity of the findings.

Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of feature design and vocabulary explosion for entity features: Encoding entity-specific features by concatenating tags with grammatical roles increases the symbol vocabulary |V| and may reintroduce sparsity at the embedding layer. The paper does not analyze the impact of this design versus factorized embeddings (separate embeddings for role and feature types) or report the size of |V| and its effects on learning speed/overfitting.

- Insufficient ablations and sensitivity studies: The paper does not present ablations on transition window size k, pooling parameters, or the contribution of each entity feature. There is no sensitivity analysis to coreference or parsing errors, although the approach fundamentally depends on accurate entity identification and coreference resolution.

- Scope limited to entity-wise independent transitions: Columns (entities) are modeled independently, ignoring cross-entity interactions that could carry coherence signals (e.g., parallel structures, topical shifts). While consistent with traditional entity grids, this assumption limits modeling capacity. The paper does not discuss or experiment with mechanisms to capture interactions across entities (e.g., 2D convolutions or attention over columns).

- Comparisons omit some strong alternatives: The evaluation does not include learning-to-rank improvements for grid models (e.g., Feng and Hirst 2012) or discourse-relation-based models (e.g., RST-informed approaches) under the same setup, which have been competitive in past work. The paper cites them but does not provide direct empirical comparisons, making it harder to quantify the incremental gain over the best non-entity-grid baselines.

- Limited cross-domain evidence and insertion-specific training: Results are largely on WSJ; domain robustness is not assessed (e.g., AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES or other news domains). For insertion, the model is trained on discrimination pairs, which may explain only modest gains; training directly on insertion-style pairs is suggested but not explored.

Questions to authors
- Entity grid construction details: What tools and settings were used for POS tagging, parsing, NER, and coreference resolution? Are they identical for baselines and your model? Please clarify any differences and their potential impact.

- Vocabulary and embedding factorization: What is the size of the symbol vocabulary |V| when including the three entity features? Did you consider factorizing role and feature embeddings (e.g., sum/concat of separate embeddings) to control |V| and improve generalization? Any empirical comparison?

- Transition length and pooling: How sensitive are results to the convolution window size k and pooling length p? Can you provide an ablation showing performance vs. k to substantiate the “long-range transition” claim?

- Error and robustness analysis: How does performance change with noisier coreference or with different coreference tools? Do the learned filters correspond to interpretable transition patterns (e.g., frequent subject continuity)? Any qualitative analysis?

- Additional baselines: Can you compare against Feng and Hirst (2012) and an RST-based model under your WSJ setup? If not feasible, please explain constraints.

Overall assessment
The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and provides a reasonably novel neuralization of the entity grid with consistent empirical gains. The contribution is incremental but meaningful for coherence modeling. Addressing the analysis gaps and adding stronger baselines would further strengthen the case.
