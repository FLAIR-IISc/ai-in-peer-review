Summary
The paper “A Neural Local Coherence Model” proposes a convolutional neural network (CNN) operating over entity grids to score local coherence. Each grid cell (grammatical role +/- entity attributes) is embedded; 1D convolutions over columns model k-length entity-role transitions; max pooling and a linear layer produce a document coherence score. The model is trained with a pairwise ranking loss. The approach is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) using WSJ/PTB and on DUC’03 summary coherence ranking, with consistent improvements over entity-grid baselines and a graph-based model. The authors also show that pretraining on WSJ improves performance on DUC summaries.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A neuralized entity-grid model using CNNs to capture longer entity transition patterns with distributed representations and pairwise ranking, delivering consistent gains over established entity-grid baselines on standard tasks.
2) A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features by augmenting grid symbols (e.g., PERSON-S), and evidence that even a subset of features suffices to outperform stronger non-neural entity-grid variants.
3) Evidence of transfer: pretraining on WSJ improves performance on a small DUC summary-coherence dataset.

Strengths
1) Builds on a well-established modeling framework. The entity grid is a strong, interpretable baseline for coherence; the paper’s choice to reuse it and replace sparse transition counting with distributed representations is methodologically sensible and minimally invasive, which increases the credibility of the gains.

2) Clear improvements on standard tasks with appropriate training protocol. On WSJ discrimination and insertion tasks, the CNN variants outperform both “all-nouns” and extended entity-grid baselines, with reported significance, multiple random seeds, and a development-based hyperparameter search. The transfer to DUC with pretraining is also a practical and defensible setup given the limited DUC supervision.

3) End-to-end ranking objective aligned with downstream use. Pairwise ranking is standard in coherence evaluation and allows task-specific feature learning. The paper’s training objective is consistent with prior work yet leverages modern optimization (dropout, RMSProp).

4) Simplicity and potential reproducibility. The architecture is straightforward (embedding + conv + pooling + linear), and the authors state they will release code. This lowers barriers to adoption and follow-up work.

5) Scalability to longer transitions/features. Using embeddings and filters alleviates the sparsity/parameter growth of R^k discrete transitions and feature cross-products—an often-cited limitation of traditional grids.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty and underdeveloped modeling insight. Applying a 1D CNN over entity-grid columns is a relatively straightforward modernization; the paper does not offer deeper modeling advances (e.g., interactions across entities/sentences, 2D convolutions, attention, or principled treatment of global discourse structure). The independence assumptions (columns treated independently; location invariance) are strong and likely suboptimal for coherence, yet are not probed empirically.

2) Incomplete baseline coverage and fairness. The comparisons omit strong discourse-relation baselines known to perform well on ordering (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) on the same corpus. The paper cites them but does not report numbers or attempt reproduction. The Li & Hovy (2014) neural model is excluded due to disappointing results without a thorough diagnosis. As a result, it is hard to conclude state of the art beyond entity-grid families.

3) Insufficient analysis/ablation to support key claims. The central claim is that longer transitions can be modeled effectively. However, there is no analysis of transition length k, pooling size, or number of filters, nor a comparison to short-k settings. Similarly, the contribution of each entity-specific feature is not disentangled, and there is no study isolating the benefit of end-to-end task-specific training versus fixed features.

4) Preprocessing and data details are underspecified. The quality of grids depends on coreference, parsing, and NER. It appears that coreference may be approximated by noun matching in some settings; if so, this conflates entities and can distort transitions. The paper does not specify which coreference resolver/NER/parser is used, nor quantify error sensitivity. This weakens the methodological soundness and external validity.

5) Evaluation clarity and metrics. The presentation of results contains ambiguities (e.g., Table 3 column labels; use of “Acc F1” in a context that appears to be ordering). For DUC, rank correlation (Kendall’s tau/Spearman) is standard; the paper’s exact metric and its comparability to prior work should be clearer. Also, the model is trained on discrimination but evaluated on insertion without task-specific training; while the authors acknowledge this, it complicates interpretation of relatively modest insertion gains.

Questions to Authors
1) Coreference and preprocessing: Which coreference resolver, parser, and NER did you use for WSJ and DUC? Did you rely on noun matching anywhere? How sensitive are results to these components?

2) Transition length and ablations: How does performance vary with k (e.g., 2, 3, 5, 8)? Please provide an ablation isolating k, pooling size, and number of filters. Do longer transitions concretely help?

3) Feature contributions: For Extended Grid-CNN, what is the vocabulary size after augmenting symbols (e.g., PERSON-S), and how do each of the three features contribute individually? How do results change if you include the full nine features of Elsner & Charniak (2011)?

4) Cross-entity/sentence interactions: Did you try 2D convolutions or mechanisms that capture interactions across columns (entities)? If so, what were the results?

5) Baselines: Can you report results for Lin et al. (2011) and Feng et al. (2014) on your WSJ setup, or explain concretely why not? For Li & Hovy (2014), can you provide diagnostics (training curves, hyperparameters) to substantiate the failed reproduction?

6) DUC evaluation: What metric do you report and how does it compare to Barzilay & Lapata (2008)? Please provide rank correlations and confidence intervals. How much does WSJ pretraining help per cluster/domain?
