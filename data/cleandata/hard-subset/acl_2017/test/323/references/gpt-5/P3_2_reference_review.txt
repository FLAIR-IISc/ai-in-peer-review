Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid: it embeds grid symbols (grammatical roles and optionally entity-specific features), applies 1D convolutions down each entity column to capture transitions of length k, max-pools to select salient patterns, and learns a coherence scoring function via pairwise ranking. The model is evaluated on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and DUC’03 summary coherence rating. Reported results outperform non-neural entity grid variants and a graph-based baseline; pretraining on WSJ improves transfer to summary rating.

Main contributions as I see them:
1) A simple, end-to-end CNN architecture over entity grids that enables longer transition modeling and task-specific representation learning, yielding state-of-the-art results on standard local coherence benchmarks.
2) A practical mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features into the grid in a neural framework and empirical evidence that the neuralized model outperforms strong entity-grid baselines even with a subset of features.
3) Demonstration of transfer from sentence-ordering pretraining to summary coherence rating, indicating the model learns reusable local coherence cues.

Strengths
- Empirical gains and robustness: The model consistently improves over strong non-neural entity grid baselines on discrimination (+~4% absolute) and insertion (up to +2.5%), with significance testing and averaging over multiple seeds. The transfer to summary rating with pretraining is a nice touch, showing the learned features are not task-specific artifacts.
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: The design—embedding grid entries, convolution over per-entity transitions, and max pooling—directly addresses the sparsity/curse of dimensionality limitations of discrete transition counts and naturally supports longer k without exploding parameters.
- End-to-end training on the target objective: Pairwise ranking directly optimizes the scoring function for discrimination (and is appropriate for ranking applications), unlike task-agnostic feature extraction in classic grid models.
- Sensible engineering and reproducibility: The paper describes hyperparameter search, regularization (dropout, early stopping), optimizer settings, and significance testing. Code release is promised; experiments are run with multiple seeds.
- Coverage of multiple benchmarks: Using the larger WSJ split is prudent for neural models; including both ordering tasks and summary rating provides a broader picture.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Methodologically, this is a straightforward application of CNNs to an existing representation with strong independence assumptions (columns treated independently, location-invariant pooling). The primary advance is empirical neuralization rather than a new modeling idea for coherence. The paper would be stronger with deeper modeling of cross-entity interactions or global discourse structure.
- Independence of entity columns and loss of positional information: Treating columns independently prevents modeling interactions among entities (e.g., topic shifts involving multiple entities). Max pooling further discards where a transition occurs, which may matter for beginnings/endings of documents. A 2D architecture or attention across columns could address this.
- Encoding entity-specific features as cross-product tokens: Collapsing role and features into atomic symbols (e.g., PERSON-S) inflates the vocabulary and loses compositionality. It undercuts the stated motivation to avoid combinatorial explosion. Composing separate embeddings for role and features (and possibly salience as a scalar) would be more principled. An ablation comparing cross-product vs compositional embeddings is missing.
- Evaluation gaps and baselines: The insertion task is evaluated with a model trained on discrimination pairs, likely underestimating its potential; training directly for insertion (e.g., pairwise preferences over candidate positions) is not explored. The authors dismiss Li & Hovy (2014) based on a failed reproduction, but stronger sentence-based/semantic baselines (e.g., discourse relation models Lin et al. 2011; Feng et al. 2014) are not re-run in the same setting. It would help to include at least one discourse-relation baseline or a recent sentence-representation baseline for balance.
- Dependence on preprocessing quality and unclear settings: The approach hinges on coreference and syntactic role assignment, but the paper does not specify whether gold parses/coref are used on WSJ or which off-the-shelf systems are applied (and how pronouns are handled). Results may overestimate performance in realistic noisy settings. Moreover, some notation/details are ambiguous (e.g., the length of feature maps hm.n+k−1, padding “t > {m,n}”, pooling stride) and Table 3’s headers are confusing (Acc/F1 with discrimination/insertion?).

Questions to authors
- Preprocessing: What coreference system and syntactic role extractor are used? Are pronouns included as entities? Are WSJ parses/coref gold or automatic? For DUC summaries, what tools and settings are used?
- Feature composition: Why use cross-product tokens for role+features rather than composing separate embeddings? What is |V| with feature augmentation? Did you try factorized representations, and how do they compare?
- Convolution/pooling specifics: Please clarify the convolution axis (strictly along the sentence dimension per column), the exact length of feature maps per column, and how you aggregate across n columns. How is max pooling applied (window and stride) relative to column boundaries?
- Insertion training: Did you attempt training directly on insertion (e.g., pairwise ranking between true vs. alternative insertion positions)? If so, how did it perform?
- Analysis: Can you provide ablations on k (transition window), pooling length, and number of filters, as well as an analysis by document length? Any qualitative inspection of learned filters (e.g., top-activating transition patterns) to support the “why it works” intuition?
- Generalization: How sensitive is the model to coreference errors? Have you tested cross-domain robustness beyond WSJ (e.g., AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES or more diverse news/forums)?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper that modernizes a well-known coherence model with neural techniques and delivers consistent gains. Its novelty is moderate, and some design/evaluation choices could be strengthened, but the results, clarity of the core idea, and potential impact on downstream generation/ordering tasks support acceptance.
