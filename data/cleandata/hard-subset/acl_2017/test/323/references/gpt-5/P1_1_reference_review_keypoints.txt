- Paper proposes a neuralized entity-grid: embed grammatical-role symbols (±entity features), apply 1D conv over entity columns, max-pool, linear score, train with pairwise ranking; evaluated on WSJ (discrimination, insertion) and DUC (summary rating) with consistent gains.

- Main contribution 1: Simple convolutional architecture over grid columns that models longer-range transitions and yields SOTA on standard coherence benchmarks.

- Main contribution 2: End-to-end pairwise ranking training that jointly learns symbol embeddings and convolution filters, with effective pretraining transfer (WSJ→DUC).

- Main contribution 3: Practical composition of entity-specific features with grammatical roles into a shared embedding space, improving performance with fewer handcrafted features.

- Strength: Strong empirical improvements over non-neural entity-grid baselines and a graph-based baseline, with significance testing.

- Strength: Addresses sparsity of classic grids by embedding role/feature symbols and enabling larger k (5–8) without combinatorial parameter growth.

- Strength: Simple, modular architecture (lookup → conv → max-pool → linear), implemented in Theano with standard optimizers and code reportedly released.

- Strength: Pairwise ranking objective aligns with coherence assessment and permits fair comparisons to prior grid setups.

- Strength: Pretraining on WSJ then fine-tuning on DUC is sensible and yields better performance under limited DUC supervision.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—direct application of standard CNN techniques to entity-grid columns without deeper modeling innovations.

- Weakness: Independence assumption across columns and max-pooling discard positional/global information and likely miss cross-entity interactions.

- Weakness: No analysis showing whether the model captures cross-entity patterns (topic shifts, multi-entity dynamics) or global positional cues.

- Weakness: Relies on external NLP (entity detection, syntactic roles, coreference) with unclear coref pipeline details and no robustness analysis to upstream errors.

- Weakness: Evaluation scope limited—discrimination and insertion tasks have ecological validity concerns, and model trained on discrimination may not optimize insertion performance.

- Weakness: Inconclusive or missing comparisons to prior work (e.g., Li & Hovy 2014) and to stronger modern sentence-representation baselines like RNNs/Transformers.

- Weakness: Missing key ablations (window size k, pooling length p, embedding size, number of filters, per-feature contributions) and limited qualitative/error analysis.

- Weakness: Notational clarity, computational-efficiency, training time, parameter counts, and memory usage are not reported.

- Question: What exact coreference/entity identification pipeline was used (string match vs coref resolver), and how sensitive are results to coref quality (gold vs automatic)?

- Question: Were convolutions or other mechanisms tried across multiple columns to model cross-entity dependencies, and what were the findings?

- Question: Ablation requests—how do k, embedding size, pooling length, and filter count affect performance and overfitting; which entity features matter most?

- Question: If trained directly on insertion (pairwise insert-position comparisons), does performance improve over the reported numbers?

- Question: Beyond WSJ→DUC, was transfer tested on other domains or languages to assess robustness?

- Additional suggestions: Preserve positional information (k-max pooling, positional encodings, attention), visualize top-activating transition patterns per filter, and report training time/parameter/memory stats.
