Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a neural reformulation of the entity grid for local coherence assessment. The model maps discrete grid symbols (grammatical roles and optional entity features) to embeddings, applies convolution over per-entity transitions to capture longer-range patterns, max-pools to select salient features, and trains with a pairwise ranking loss tailored to the target task. The approach is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) using WSJ and on summary coherence rating using DUC’03, showing consistent improvements over standard entity-grid baselines and a graph-based model.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A neuralized entity-grid model (Grid-CNN/Extended Grid-CNN) that learns distributed representations and convolutional features over entity transitions, enabling longer-range patterns and end-to-end, task-specific training.
2) An empirical demonstration of consistent, statistically significant gains over strong non-neural entity-grid baselines on multiple coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary rating), with careful training and significance testing.
3) A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features into the neural model (feature tagging at grid entries), showing additional gains without external resources.

Strengths
- Clear, task-aligned neural architecture with plausible inductive biases. Mapping grid symbols to embeddings and applying convolution over entity-wise transitions directly targets local coherence phenomena the grid encodes, while pairwise ranking optimizes the right objective (relative coherence). This is a principled modernization of a classic model.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains over relevant baselines. On WSJ, the neural models improve over Grid-all-nouns and Extended Grid, with absolute gains reported around +4% (discrimination) and up to +2.5% (insertion). On DUC’03 summary rating, pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning gives additional gains. Multiple runs and approximate randomization tests strengthen the results’ reliability.
- Sensible data choices and setup. Using WSJ (larger and less stylistically constrained than AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES) is appropriate for training neural models; the paper follows established evaluation protocols (20 permutations, insertion scoring). Development sets, early stopping, dropout, and hyperparameter search are used appropriately.
- Practical extensibility. The simple feature composition scheme (e.g., PERSON-S) makes adding entity attributes straightforward, and the model can, in principle, incorporate richer features or pretraining. The approach also reduces manual feature engineering compared to extended grids.
- Reproducibility attention. The paper provides implementation details (Theano, optimization, hyperparameters), reports averaging over five seeds, and claims to release code, which aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work; missing key baselines. The core contribution is “neuralizing” the entity grid with standard CNN and embeddings, an incremental architectural change. Several relevant, strong baselines are not reported in the tables, notably RST/discourse-based models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014) and Li & Hovy (2014). The latter is mentioned as underperforming in the authors’ re-run and thus omitted; however, as a published model, it should be included, with best-effort replication or consultation, to ensure a fair comparison.
- Modeling assumptions may undercut coherence phenomena. Treating columns independently ignores interactions among entities within and across sentences (e.g., role exchanges or coreferential chains that jointly signal coherence). The model uses 1D convolutions along each column only; a 2D convolution or attention over the grid could capture cross-entity patterns. There is no analysis of whether independence harms specific cases.
- Training/evaluation mismatch for insertion. The model is trained on discrimination (original vs. random permutations) but evaluated on insertion, a more fine-grained task where only one sentence is moved. While the paper acknowledges this, it does not train a version directly on insertion or provide ablations to quantify the mismatch’s impact.
- Insufficient analysis and ablations. The paper lacks: (i) ablations on transition length k, pooling size, embedding size, and number/type of entity features; (ii) error analysis or qualitative inspection of learned filters; (iii) sensitivity to coreference quality and NE tagging; and (iv) domain robustness (e.g., AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES or other out-of-domain tests).
- Clarity and reporting issues. Table 3’s headers/metrics are confusing (the identical numbers in Discrimination and Insertion columns suggest a formatting error). Details about the coreference system, NE tagger, and exact feature encoding vocabulary are missing. Some references are incomplete/misformatted. The statement about code release should ensure anonymization.

Questions to authors
1) Coreference and entity detection: Which coreference resolver and noun/NE taggers were used? Are pronouns included? How sensitive are results to coreference quality? Please report performance with an off-the-shelf vs. an oracle (if feasible) to bound the impact.
2) Feature encoding: How exactly are salience and “proper mention” encoded in the vocabulary V (e.g., discretization bins)? What is the resulting |V| with entity features, and how does that affect parameter count?
3) Transition length and pooling: What values of k were optimal across tasks, and how does performance vary with k and pooling length p? Please provide an ablation.
4) Insertion training: Can you train the ranking loss on insertion pairs (i.e., correct position vs. sampled alternative positions) and report the effect?
5) Baselines: Can you include results (yours or reported) for Li & Hovy (2014) and RST-based models (Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014)? If your reimplementation underperforms, can you consult authors or use reported numbers with appropriate caveats?
6) Cross-entity interactions: Did you try 2D convolutions or attention across columns? Any preliminary results or constraints that argued against this?

Additional comments
- Please fix Table 3’s headers/metrics and ensure insertion is reported with the standard metric. Clarify if “Acc/F1” is applicable and consistent across tasks.
- Include parameter counts, training time, and memory usage for a typical WSJ model.
- Provide qualitative examples of high-activation filters (e.g., learned patterns akin to SS–O–X across 5–8 sentences), and common failure cases.
- Verify that code release complies with double-blind policies (e.g., anonymous repository).

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper presents a solid, well-executed modernization of entity-grid coherence modeling with consistent empirical gains and clear practical value. To strengthen the camera-ready, please add stronger baselines, ablations, and analysis, and address clarity issues.
