Summary and claimed contributions
The paper presents a neural model for local coherence that “neuralizes” the entity grid. It embeds grammatical-role symbols (and optionally entity-specific features) and applies 1D convolutions over entity transition sequences (grid columns), followed by max pooling and a linear scoring layer. The model is trained with a pairwise ranking loss. It is evaluated on sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) on WSJ and on DUC summary coherence rating, achieving consistent gains over prior (non-neural) entity-grid models and a graph-based baseline.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple convolutional architecture over entity-grid columns that alleviates sparsity and enables longer-range transition modeling, yielding state-of-the-art results on standard coherence benchmarks (WSJ discrimination, insertion; DUC summary rating).
2) An end-to-end, task-specific training setup (pairwise ranking) that jointly learns symbol embeddings and convolutional filters, with evidence of transfer: pretraining on WSJ improves DUC summary ranking.
3) A practical way to incorporate entity-specific features by composing them with grammatical roles into a shared embedding space, achieving improvements while using fewer handcrafted features than prior work.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results across tasks and settings:
  • WSJ discrimination: Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN provide absolute gains of ~4% and ~3.7% over their non-neural counterparts; insertion: up to 2.5% gains. DUC summary rating also improves, further boosted by pretraining. Significance tests are reported.
  • The model outperforms a widely used extended entity-grid baseline despite using only a subset of its features, suggesting the learned representations capture useful regularities beyond sparse transition counts.

- Addresses key limitation of entity grids (sparsity for longer transitions):
  • By embedding role/feature symbols and applying convolutions, the model can use longer k (5–8) without combinatorial blow-up. This directly tackles the R^k parameter growth in classic grids and is supported by results.

- Simple, modular, and reproducible:
  • The architecture is straightforward (lookup → conv → max-pool → linear), implemented in Theano with standard optimization (RMSprop, dropout, early stopping), and the authors state they released code. This facilitates adoption and extension.

- Sound training objective and evaluation protocol:
  • Pairwise ranking matches the nature of coherence assessment and mirrors prior entity-grid setups, enabling apples-to-apples comparisons on WSJ with the Brown coherence toolkit baselines and graph-based alternatives.

- Sensible use of pretraining and transfer:
  • Given limited DUC supervision, pretraining on WSJ then fine-tuning improves performance, indicating learned representations generalize across related coherence tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond a straightforward neuralization:
  • The core idea is applying convolution and embeddings to entity-grid columns with max pooling. While effective, this is a relatively direct application of standard CNN architectures and independence assumptions from prior grid models. A deeper modeling advance (e.g., cross-entity interactions, attention, or discourse structure) is not explored.

- Independence assumptions and loss of structural information:
  • Columns (entities) are treated independently and max pooling discards positional information beyond local windows, imposing location-invariance that may miss document-level structure and inter-entity interactions known to matter for coherence.
  • No analysis of whether/how the model captures cross-entity patterns (e.g., topic shifts involving multiple entities) or positional/global cues.

- Reliance on upstream NLP and unclear coreference details:
  • The approach is not fully end-to-end: it requires entity detection, syntactic roles, and coreference/linking. The paper suggests noun matching is used in places; the exact coref setup and its error rates are not fully specified. Robustness to coref/parsing errors and domain transfer are not analyzed.

- Evaluation scope and task mismatch:
  • Discrimination via random permutations and insertion are standard but have been criticized for limited ecological validity. The model is trained on discrimination but evaluated on insertion without task-specific training; authors acknowledge smaller gains on insertion. More realistic ordering baselines or generation settings would strengthen impact.
  • The comparison to Li & Hovy (2014) is inconclusive (very low performance and excluded). A careful reproduction or alternative sentence-representation baselines (e.g., modern RNN/Transformer sentence encoders) would contextualize gains.

- Missing ablations/analyses:
  • No ablation on window size k, pooling length p, or the contribution of each entity-specific feature.
  • Limited qualitative/error analysis (e.g., what transition patterns filters learn, where the model fails).
  • Some notational clarity issues (e.g., feature map length over m sentences and n entities), and details on computational efficiency are absent.

Questions to authors
- Coreference and entity identification: What exact pipeline is used (string match vs. coref resolver)? How sensitive is performance to coref quality? Any results with gold vs. automatic coref?
- Cross-entity interactions: Did you try convolutions across multiple columns or mechanisms to capture dependencies between entities? If so, what were the findings?
- Ablations: How do performance and overfitting vary with k (transition window), embedding size, pooling length, and number of filters? Which entity-specific features matter most?
- Task-specific training: If trained directly on insertion (pairwise comparisons of candidate insert positions), does performance improve substantially over the reported numbers?
- Transfer and domain robustness: Beyond WSJ→DUC, did you test on other domains or languages? How well do embeddings/filters transfer?

Additional comments
- Consider preserving some positional information (e.g., k-max pooling, learned positional encodings, or attention) to mitigate location-invariance issues.
- A qualitative visualization of top-activating transition patterns per filter would improve interpretability.
- Reporting training time, parameter counts, and memory use would aid reproducibility.
