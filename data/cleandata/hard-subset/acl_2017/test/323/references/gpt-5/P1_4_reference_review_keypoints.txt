- Paper proposes a neural local coherence model that embeds entity-grid symbols and applies 1D convolution over entity columns with max pooling and pairwise ranking, improving sentence-ordering and summary ranking tasks.

- Contribution: A simple, effective neural architecture over entity grids that learns distributed entity-transition representations and supports longer transition windows, achieving SOTA on coherence tasks.

- Contribution: An end-to-end pairwise ranking training scheme with pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning on DUC’03, demonstrating transfer to low-resource settings.

- Contribution: A mechanism for compositional entity-feature symbols (e.g., role+entity attribute) that yields gains over strong extended-grid baselines.

- Strength: Strong, consistent empirical gains vs basic and extended entity grid baselines and a graph-based model, with multiple runs and significance testing.

- Strength: Well-motivated design addressing sparsity of discrete transitions by using embeddings and convolutions to handle longer k without combinatorial explosion.

- Strength: Fair baselines and replication effort, with reported hyperparameters, training details (RMSProp, dropout, early stopping), and clear use of pairwise ranking loss.

- Strength: Practical, extensible modular architecture (embedding + conv + pooling) that can incorporate additional entity attributes.

- Strength: Clear exposition of model assumptions (independent columns, location-invariant convolution, wide conv with zero-padding) and intuition for filters detecting transition patterns.

- Weakness: Limited novelty — primarily a straightforward neuralization of the entity grid without changing independence assumptions or reliance on local transitions.

- Weakness: Insufficient detail on preprocessing and feature construction (coreference resolver, parser, S/O/X mapping, treatment of non-head nouns/pronouns, singleton filtering), harming reproducibility.

- Weakness: Limited evaluation breadth — main results only on WSJ and small DUC’03, no AIRPLANE/EARTHQUAKE datasets, domain/length/perm difficulty robustness, or qualitative error analysis.

- Weakness: Missing ablations and hyperparameter analysis (effects of k, pooling size, number of filters, individual features; embeddings-only vs conv-only).

- Weakness: Baseline comparison gaps — poor Li & Hovy (2014) result unexplored and training paradigms differ between extended-grid (SVM) and neural model, lacking matched comparisons.

- Question: Which coreference resolver and parser were used, and how are S/O/X roles derived (dependency vs phrase-structure heuristics)?

- Question: How are non-head nouns, pronouns, and multiple mentions per sentence handled; are singleton entities filtered and what proportion are pronouns?

- Question: When concatenating entity features with roles, how large is the vocabulary V and do you cap/regularize rare feature-role combos or use hashing?

- Question: What is the effect of transition window length k and pooling size p on accuracy; please provide sensitivity plots for k and p.

- Question: How much of the gain comes from embeddings alone, conv specifics (narrow vs wide padding), and each individual entity feature (ablation requested)?

- Question: If trained directly on insertion pairs instead of permutations, does insertion performance improve?

- Question: Can you provide examples of high-activation filters and the transition patterns they capture; do filters align with Centering-theoretic expectations?

- Question: What are training/inference times and parameter counts for best models, and how does performance scale with document length and number of entities?

- Question: What settings were used for the Li & Hovy baseline; was domain-specific tuning attempted, and can scripts/results be released to aid reproducibility?

- Overall: Solid, well-executed neural rethinking of the entity grid with clear empirical benefits and extensibility, but limited novelty and missing preprocessing/ablation/analysis that should be addressed.
