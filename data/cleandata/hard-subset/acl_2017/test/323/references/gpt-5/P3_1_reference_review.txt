Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a neuralized version of the entity grid for local coherence modeling. It replaces discrete transition counts with learned embeddings and a 1D convolution over per-entity sentence-role sequences, trained end-to-end with a pairwise ranking loss. This allows modeling longer transition windows and task-specific feature learning.
- It shows a simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features (e.g., NE type, salience, proper-name flag) by conjoining them with grammatical roles and learning distributed representations, avoiding explicit combinatorial feature explosion in counting models.
- Empirically, the model establishes new state-of-the-art results on WSJ sentence ordering (discrimination and insertion) and improves summary coherence ranking on DUC’03; transfer learning from WSJ helps on the small summary dataset.

Strengths
- Strong, consistent empirical gains over well-known, competitive non-neural grids. On WSJ, the neural grid improves discrimination by about 4 points over Grid-all-nouns and 3.7 points over the Extended Grid; insertion also improves (up to 2.5 points). Summary ranking benefits from pretraining. Results are averaged over multiple seeds and tested for significance.
- Clean, well-motivated architecture. Convolving along entity columns directly operationalizes the central intuition of grid-based coherence—entity-role transitions—while enabling longer transition windows (k≈5–8) and end-to-end learning with a ranking objective that matches the evaluation protocol.
- Practicality and simplicity. The model needs only standard NLP preprocessing (POS/parse + coref), a manageable set of hyperparameters, and trains with common optimizers. Incorporating entity features by conjoining symbols is simple to implement yet effective once embedded.
- Sensible training and evaluation practice. The authors hold out a dev set, apply early stopping and dropout, report averages over five runs, and use approximate randomization for significance. They include a competitive unsupervised graph baseline and the strongest classic grid baselines, and they attempt cross-task transfer to address small-data settings.
- Potential for extensibility. The convolutional framework is flexible and may incorporate additional discourse signals (e.g., RST or coreference attributes) and alternative parameterizations (e.g., different filters, pooling) without changing the overall training objective.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior neural coherence and grid work. The core idea is a fairly direct application of CNNs to a grid representation; the paper would benefit from a deeper contrast to Li and Hovy (2014) and to discourse-transition models (e.g., Lin et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2014). The claim of “sufficiently long” modeling is still bounded by a fixed convolutional window; it does not capture truly long-distance dependencies beyond k or cross-entity interactions.
- Incomplete baselines and unclear handling of prior neural model. The authors exclude Li and Hovy (2014) due to disappointing replication on their data, but do not diagnose the failure or seek assistance. Strong discourse baselines (e.g., RST-based models) are not directly compared on WSJ, yet the paper claims state-of-the-art. This weakens the strength of the empirical claim.
- Reliance on preprocessing and under-specified pipeline. Performance depends on parsing and coreference resolution quality; however, the coref system, parsing setup, and mention selection heuristics are not specified, reducing reproducibility and obscuring error sources. The “all nouns” vs “coreferent entities” distinction needs clarity: do all nouns get clustered via coref or become separate columns?
- Modeling assumptions restrict expressivity. Treating columns independently precludes learning cross-entity interactions (e.g., parallelism across entities or joint patterns like topical shifts involving multiple entities). A 2D conv across sentences and entities or attention across entities might capture richer coherence signals.
- Analysis and ablations are limited. The paper lacks ablations for transition window size, pooling length, embedding size, and per-feature contributions in the Extended Grid-CNN. No error analysis or qualitative inspection of learned filters/patterns is presented. This makes it hard to attribute where gains come from and to generalize design recommendations.
- Some clarity issues in technical exposition and metrics. Notation around convolution indices and padding (e.g., m.n+k−1; padding “for t > {m,n}”) is confusing. The encoding of salience into the vocabulary V (bucketing?) is not specified. In Table 3, the meaning of “F1” is unclear for the sentence-ordering tasks.

Questions to authors
- Preprocessing: Which parser and coreference system are used? How are mention detection and coreference clustering handled for “all nouns” vs “entities”? Are pronouns included? What is the impact of coref accuracy on performance?
- Feature encoding: How many NE types are used? How is salience encoded (continuous value, buckets)? How large does |V| become after conjoining features with roles? Any measures to control vocabulary blowup?
- Architecture choices: Did you try 2D convolutions (across sentences and entities) or attention across entity columns to capture cross-entity patterns? Any experiments with recurrent models along columns?
- Ablations: Can you report sensitivity to window size k and pooling length p, and per-feature ablations in the Extended Grid-CNN? How do results change without dropout or with pretrained embeddings?
- Baselines: Can you provide a careful comparison to RST/discourse relation transition models on WSJ? For Li and Hovy (2014), what configuration differences or data preprocessing mismatches might explain the low accuracy?
- Task training: Have you tried training the pairwise ranker directly on insertion-specific pairs (documents differing by one sentence position)? Does that close the gap and further improve insertion performance?
- Analysis: Can you provide qualitative examples or visualizations of filters that fire on canonical patterns, and an error analysis contrasting gains/losses vs Extended Grid?

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution that neuralizes a widely used coherence representation and delivers consistent gains with a simple, extensible design. Clarifying the pipeline, expanding baselines, and adding ablations/analysis would strengthen the empirical claims and scientific insight. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the missing details and comparisons.
