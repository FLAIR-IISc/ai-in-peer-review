Summary and claimed contributions
- Proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid: grammatical-role symbols (S/O/X/–) and optionally entity-specific features are embedded; a 1D CNN over columns (entity transitions) with max pooling and a linear layer produces a document coherence score, trained with pairwise ranking.
- Demonstrates improvements over entity-grid baselines on WSJ sentence ordering tasks (discrimination and insertion) and on DUC’03 summary coherence rating, with pretraining on WSJ benefiting the small summary dataset.
- Argues the approach can model longer transition patterns than discrete transition probabilities and can be trained end-to-end for task-specific features.

Strengths
- Sound grounding in an established, well-understood framework. Building on the entity grid keeps the model interpretable and connected to prior theory (Centering). The modeling choices (embeddings + convolution + max-pooling + pairwise ranking) are standard and robust.
- Consistent empirical gains on a reasonably large corpus. On WSJ, the CNN improves over both “all-nouns” and extended grid baselines for discrimination and insertion, with significance testing and averaging over multiple seeds. This addresses common concerns about instability of neural results.
- Practical training/evaluation setup. Pairwise ranking is appropriate for coherence scoring; the use of DEV for hyperparameter tuning, dropout, early stopping, and multiple seeds shows attention to methodology. Releasing code aids reproducibility.
- Sensible transfer to low-resource setting. Pretraining on WSJ and fine-tuning on DUC’03 to improve summary coherence ranking is a reasonable and effective strategy given the small data size.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. Conceptually, the model replaces discrete transition counts with a standard CNN over the same grid representation. The architectural assumptions (independent columns, local convolutions with max-pooling) are straightforward and similar in spirit to longer n-gram transition modeling. The paper lacks deeper algorithmic innovations or new theoretical insights beyond this neuralization.
- Incomplete and possibly uneven baselines. The paper excludes the Li & Hovy (2014) neural sentence-representation baseline due to disappointing results without sufficient analysis; this weakens the claim of state of the art. For DUC’03, the extended grid baseline is not trained in a pairwise manner (authors state it “could not be trained”), but pairwise preferences could be handled with a simple preference SVM/logistic regression on grid features, making the comparison potentially unfair. More broadly, the graph baseline is relatively weak compared to stronger recent variants.
- Missing ablations and diagnostics. There is no ablation on key design choices: transition window size k, pooling size/stride, number of filters, the effect of each entity-specific feature (NE type, salience, properness), or the contribution of embeddings vs. convolution vs. pooling. It is also unclear how sensitive results are to the dev-tuned hyperparameters or to random seeds beyond the five reported. Error analyses (e.g., per-document-length breakdown, typical failure cases, entity density) are absent.
- Dependence on upstream tools not specified. Coreference resolution, NER, and syntactic role extraction heavily affect grid quality. The paper does not specify which tools/models were used, whether gold vs. predicted annotations were employed, or how errors propagate. Without this, it is hard to assess robustness and reproducibility across domains or with different preprocessing pipelines.
- Evaluation scope and training-objective mismatch. Results are confined largely to WSJ; the rationale for not including AIRPLANES/EARTHQUAKES is understandable but limits comparability with prior work. For insertion, the model is trained only on discrimination-style pairs (random permutations) and not on insertion-specific objectives; the reported gains might not reflect the method’s full potential, and the comparison is thus less conclusive. Also, using only 20 random permutations per doc is standard but does not probe “hard negatives.”

Questions to authors
- Preprocessing: Which coreference resolver, parser, and NER system were used? What are their versions and approximate performance on WSJ? Are grammatical roles derived from dependency or constituency parses? Are results robust to swapping these tools?
- Features: Please provide ablations quantifying the contribution of (i) embeddings vs. one-hot CNN, (ii) convolution vs. a simple MLP over k-gram transitions, (iii) each entity-specific feature (NE type, salience, properness), and (iv) different transition lengths k. How does performance vary with k from 2 to 8?
- Baselines: Can you include a stronger comparison to Li & Hovy (2014) by investigating why it failed (tokenization, pretraining, optimization, input sentence windows, or domain mismatch)? For DUC’03, can you train a pairwise preference model over extended-grid features to ensure a fair comparison?
- Analysis: Please report performance as a function of document length and entity density, and provide error examples illustrating what patterns the filters learn (e.g., top-activated transition snippets).
- Training: Did you tune the ranking margin? Did you try harder negative sampling (e.g., near-miss permutations) or training directly on insertion pairs? What is the parameter count, training time, and inference time per document?
- Generalization: Have you tested cross-domain transfer (train on WSJ, test on another news source) to evaluate robustness to domain shift and preprocessing variation?

Overall, this is a careful, incremental application of standard neural components to a classic representation, with solid empirical gains on WSJ. The contribution is more engineering than conceptual, and stronger baselines, ablations, and clarity about preprocessing would significantly strengthen the case.
