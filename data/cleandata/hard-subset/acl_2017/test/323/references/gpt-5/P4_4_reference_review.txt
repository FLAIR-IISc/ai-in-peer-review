Summary
The paper proposes a neural local coherence model that “neuralizes” the entity grid representation. Grid cells (grammatical roles and optionally entity-specific features) are embedded, and 1D convolutional filters operate over per-entity transitions of length k across sentences. Max-pooling and a linear layer yield a document coherence score. The model is trained with a pairwise ranking loss. Evaluations on sentence ordering (discrimination, insertion) using WSJ and on summary coherence rating (DUC’03) show consistent improvements over traditional entity-grid baselines and a graph-based method; pretraining on WSJ further boosts performance on the small DUC dataset.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A neural CNN-based formulation of the entity grid that supports longer entity-transition contexts via distributed representations and end-to-end task-specific training. This addresses sparsity and task-agnostic feature engineering in prior grid models.
2) Empirical evidence of state-of-the-art performance on standard coherence benchmarks (WSJ discrimination/insertion; DUC coherence rating), with statistically significant gains over strong, widely used entity-grid baselines.
3) A simple mechanism to incorporate entity-specific features in the neural grid via symbol expansion and embeddings, achieving improvements even with a reduced feature set compared to prior extended grids.

Strengths
1) Clear, technically sound architecture that directly targets known limitations of entity grids. Using embeddings and convolution over transitions enables modeling longer contexts (k up to 5–8) without exponential sparsity. The pairwise ranking objective is appropriate for coherence scoring and consistent with prior work.
2) Strong empirical results with appropriate baselines and statistical testing. The model improves over both the “all nouns” and the extended grid variants, with significant gains reported for discrimination and insertion, and additional gains on DUC with pretraining. Reporting averages over multiple random seeds and approximate randomization tests improves reliability.
3) End-to-end training and feature learning. Unlike traditional grid feature extraction, the proposed approach learns task-specific representations (embeddings and filters) directly from data, addressing the task-mismatch of hand-crafted transition probabilities.
4) Efficiency and simplicity of incorporating entity-specific features. The approach uses only three easily available features (NE type, salience by frequency, proper mention) yet surpasses an extended grid using a larger set of features, suggesting effective generalization through embeddings.
5) Reproducibility. The paper details hyperparameter ranges, training procedures, and significance testing; code is stated to be released, which strengthens reproducibility.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to prior neural coherence models; primarily an application of standard CNNs to an existing representation. While the neuralization of the entity grid is useful, the core technical components (embeddings, 1D convolution, max-pooling, pairwise ranking) are standard. The paper would benefit from deeper architectural innovation (e.g., modeling cross-entity interactions or positional/global document structure).
2) Independence assumptions and potential information loss. Columns (entities) are treated independently, and max pooling discards positional information beyond local windows. Coherence often involves interactions among multiple entities and global discourse structure; the model as presented cannot capture such cross-entity patterns.
3) Limited ablations and analysis. There is no systematic study of transition length k, pooling strategy, embedding size, or the contribution of each entity-specific feature. An ablation on pretraining vs. from-scratch for DUC is provided, but broader analyses (e.g., effect of coreference quality, error types, sensitivity to document length) are missing.
4) Baseline coverage and fairness. The paper excludes Li & Hovy (2014) due to unexpectedly poor performance on the authors’ setup; however, the exclusion weakens comparative context with other neural coherence approaches. Additional baselines (e.g., discourse-relation-based models or stronger sentence-ordering methods) would better situate contributions. For DUC, some competing models could not be trained in pairwise fashion; an explicit discussion of comparability and significance on the small dataset is needed.
5) Clarity and reporting details. Some reference formatting is inconsistent, and implementation details critical for replication are underspecified (e.g., coreference resolver used; exact construction/size of the expanded vocabulary when adding features; whether convolution operates strictly within columns only; handling of documents with very few entities; runtime and model size). These omissions may hinder exact reproducibility despite code availability.

Questions to Authors
1) What coreference system and settings were used to construct the grids? How sensitive are results to coreference quality (e.g., using a weaker/stronger resolver)?
2) How large is the symbol vocabulary V when incorporating entity features (NE type, salience bins, proper mention), and how are continuous salience values discretized? Please provide counts and examples.
3) Can you report ablations on transition length k, pooling length p, and number of filters, and their effects on all tasks? Does longer k consistently help?
4) Did you explore modeling cross-entity interactions (e.g., 2D convolutions or attention across columns)? If so, how did they compare?
5) For DUC’03, are the improvements statistically significant given the small test set? Please report significance and variance across seeds.
6) Why did Li & Hovy (2014) perform poorly under your setup? Can you provide diagnostics (e.g., data preprocessing differences, sentence segmentation) to clarify the discrepancy?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, technically sound neural reformulation of a widely used coherence model and demonstrates consistent empirical gains. The main limitations concern novelty beyond applying standard CNNs to the grid representation and the lack of analysis and stronger baselines.
