## Review for "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

**Summary:** This paper proposes a neural network architecture to model local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models—discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation—by employing distributed representations and end-to-end training. The model is evaluated on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) and demonstrates state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Neuralization of Entity Grid Models:**  The paper successfully translates the entity grid concept into a neural network framework. This is not merely a re-implementation but a thoughtful adaptation, leveraging convolutional networks to handle long-range dependencies and learn distributed representations.
2. **Demonstrated Empirical Improvement:** The consistent and statistically significant performance gains across three different evaluation tasks provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model.  Achieving state-of-the-art results is a notable accomplishment.
3. **Practical Contribution (Code Release):** The release of code is valuable for reproducibility and encourages further research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The paper thoroughly compares against relevant baselines, including both traditional entity grid models and a recent deep learning approach (Li & Hovy, 2014, though they note issues with its reproduction). This provides a clear understanding of the model’s advantages.
2. **Well-Motivated Design Choices:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods and justify their design choices, such as using convolutional networks for capturing long-range dependencies and end-to-end training for task-specific feature learning. The reasoning is sound and contributes to the paper's clarity.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluation on three diverse tasks provides a robust assessment of the model’s generalization ability.  The inclusion of both sentence ordering and summary coherence rating tasks is commendable.
4. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.  The architecture is explained clearly with the help of Figure 2.  The experimental setup and results are presented in a concise and organized manner.
5. **Effective Incorporation of Entity-Specific Features:** The method for incorporating entity-specific features, while relatively simple, demonstrates a significant performance boost. This highlights the importance of such features and the flexibility of the neural framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Neural Architecture:** While the *application* to the coherence modeling domain is novel, the underlying neural network components (CNNs, embeddings, max pooling) are standard. The paper doesn’t present any novel architectural innovations within the neural network itself.  The core contribution lies in the successful *adaptation* and *application* of established techniques to a new problem.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Learned Representations:** The paper primarily focuses on performance gains. A deeper analysis of what the network *learns* would be valuable. Visualizing learned embeddings, or analyzing filter weights, could provide insights into the model's behavior and understanding of coherence.
3. **Dependence on Pre-processing (Coreference Resolution):** The performance relies heavily on the accuracy of the coreference resolution system. Errors in coreference will propagate through the entity grid and potentially impact the results. The paper does not discuss the potential impact of coreference errors or explore methods to mitigate them.
4. **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While hyperparameters were tuned using a development set, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the model's sensitivity to different hyperparameters.  A more thorough investigation could reveal optimal settings and provide insights into the model's robustness.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Coreference Error Analysis:**  Could you provide any analysis of how errors in the coreference resolver might affect your model's performance? Have you considered any techniques to mitigate the impact of coreference errors (e.g., using multiple coreference systems, incorporating confidence scores)?
2. **Representation Visualization:**  Would you be willing to share any visualizations of the learned embeddings or convolutional filter weights? This would help us understand what features the model is learning to identify coherent text.
3. **Ablation Study on Entity Features:** Beyond the three entity features you included, did you experiment with other potential features, and if so, what were the results? Were there diminishing returns with the addition of more features?
4. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of training and running your model compared to the traditional entity grid methods? This is important for practical applications.



**Additional Comments:**

* The citation to Li and Hovy (2014) regarding reproduction issues is helpful.
* While the code release is appreciated, providing pre-trained models would further enhance reproducibility and accessibility.
* It would be useful to explicitly state the coreference resolution system used in the experiments.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The neuralization of the entity grid framework is a significant step forward, and the demonstrated empirical improvements are compelling. While the novelty of the underlying neural architecture is limited, the successful application and adaptation of established techniques, coupled with the comprehensive evaluation and code release, warrant acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
