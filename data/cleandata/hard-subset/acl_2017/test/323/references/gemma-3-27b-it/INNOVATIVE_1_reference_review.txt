This paper presents a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The primary contribution is the introduction of a convolutional neural network to capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion, a departure from the discrete representations and decoupled feature engineering of traditional entity grid models. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of consistent performance gains across three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of convolutional neural networks to the entity grid framework is a genuinely novel idea. While neural networks have been applied to various NLP tasks, bridging them with a well-established, symbolic approach like entity grids is a promising direction. The potential to learn representations directly from data, rather than relying on hand-crafted features, is a significant advantage.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates improvements over existing baselines, including the strong extended entity grid model. Achieving state-of-the-art performance across multiple tasks is compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported gains, particularly in insertion and summary coherence rating, are noteworthy.
3. **End-to-End Training:** The end-to-end training methodology is a key strength. Decoupling feature extraction from the target task is a common limitation in traditional coherence modeling. The ability to learn task-specific features directly from the data is a significant advancement.
4. **Code Release:** The authors have thoughtfully released their code, which greatly facilitates reproducibility and further research in this area. This is a crucial practice for promoting scientific progress.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares the full model (Extended Grid-CNN) to its non-neural counterpart, a more detailed ablation study would be beneficial. Specifically, it's unclear how much each component contributes to the overall performance – the convolutional layers, the distributed representations, and the incorporation of entity-specific features. A systematic analysis of these components would provide a deeper understanding of the model's inner workings.
2. **Task Specificity of Improvements:** The gains on the discrimination task are relatively small compared to insertion and coherence rating. It would be useful to investigate whether the model is less sensitive to subtle coherence differences in the discrimination task, or if the task itself is particularly challenging for all models.
3. **Simplification of Entity-Specific Features:** The authors acknowledge that they only used a subset of the entity-specific features from Elsner and Charniak (2011). While this simplifies the model, it's possible that incorporating more features could further improve performance. The reasons for this simplification aren't fully explained.
4. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on quantitative results. A qualitative analysis of the model's predictions, perhaps with examples of coherent and incoherent texts, could provide valuable insights into its strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, systematically evaluating the contributions of each component of your model (e.g., convolutional layers, distributed representations, entity-specific features)?
2. Why were only a limited number of entity-specific features included in the Extended Grid-CNN model? Are there limitations that prevented you from incorporating more features?
3.  Can you provide a few examples where your model correctly identifies a coherent text that previous models failed to recognize, and vice-versa? This qualitative analysis would help understand the nuances of your model's predictions.
4. The paper states that the model is trained with pairwise ranking. What is the source of the (di, dj) pairs for the training set? Is it created automatically, or does it require human annotation?
