This paper presents a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, aiming to improve upon traditional entity grid-based approaches. The authors claim contributions in (1) capturing long-range entity transitions through distributed representations, (2) incorporating arbitrary entity-specific features, and (3) end-to-end training for task-specific feature learning. As I see it, the primary contribution lies in demonstrating the feasibility of applying a convolutional neural network to the entity grid framework, achieving state-of-the-art results on established coherence evaluation tasks. The successful integration of entity-specific features within this neural framework is a secondary, though valuable, contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms existing entity grid models on all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The reported gains, particularly the 2.5% improvement on insertion and over 4% on summary coherence rating, are statistically significant and represent a genuine advancement in the field. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed neural architecture.
2. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The description of the neural architecture, while technically detailed, is accessible and well-illustrated (though Figure 2 is somewhat cramped). The authors provide sufficient context regarding the limitations of existing entity grid models.
3. **Practical Implementation and Release:**  The authors’ release of their code is commendable and facilitates reproducibility and further research. This demonstrates a commitment to open science.
4. **Sound Methodology:** The use of pairwise ranking for training and the inclusion of a development set for hyperparameter tuning are good practices. The ablation study (comparing Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN) provides valuable insights into the contribution of entity-specific features.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Architecture:** While the application of convolutional neural networks to the entity grid framework yields positive results, the core architectural components (convolutional layers, max-pooling) are not novel in themselves. The paper does not sufficiently articulate *why* a convolutional architecture is particularly well-suited to this problem beyond a general statement about capturing local transitions. A more detailed justification, perhaps referencing relevant work in sequence modeling or signal processing, would strengthen the contribution.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper presents quantitative results but lacks a qualitative analysis of the model’s errors. Understanding *where* the model fails (e.g., specific types of coherence violations, challenging entity types) would provide valuable insights and guide future development.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Search Space:** The hyperparameter search space, though described, appears relatively limited. A more extensive exploration of different architectures and regularization techniques could potentially yield further improvements. The authors mention early stopping, but the criteria for stopping could be more clearly defined.
4. **Task-Specific Performance Discrepancies:** The improvements are more pronounced on the summary coherence rating task compared to the sentence ordering tasks. A discussion of potential reasons for this discrepancy (e.g., task difficulty, suitability of the model for different types of coherence assessment) is missing.
5. **Minor Formatting Issues:** Throughout the paper there are instances of inconsistent spacing, and occasionally awkward phrasing (e.g., “We preferred WSJ corpus for several reasons.”).  While minor, these detract from the overall polish of the manuscript.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why a convolutional architecture is particularly well-suited for modeling entity transitions in the context of coherence assessment? What specific properties of convolution make it advantageous over alternative approaches like recurrent neural networks or transformers?
2.  Can you provide examples of error cases where the model fails to accurately assess coherence? What types of coherence violations does the model struggle with most?
3.  Did you experiment with different embedding initialization strategies beyond random initialization or pre-trained vectors? If so, what were the results?
4.  The paper mentions training the neural model only on WSJ for discrimination and insertion tasks and then evaluating on the other corpora. Is this an intended experimental design? If so, what is the rationale? Wouldn't it be better to fine-tune the model on each dataset separately?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is quite extensive, but inconsistent formatting is observed.
*   Figure 2 is difficult to read due to the compact layout. Consider increasing the figure size or simplifying the diagram.
*   The phrase “neuralizing the basic entity grid model” feels slightly informal for an academic paper.



Overall, this is a solid paper with promising results. However, the limited novelty of the core architecture and the lack of in-depth error analysis prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit.
