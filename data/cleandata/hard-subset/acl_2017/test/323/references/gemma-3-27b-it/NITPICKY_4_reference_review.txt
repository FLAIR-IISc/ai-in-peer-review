This paper presents a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the entity grid framework. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of convolutional neural networks to learn distributed representations of entity transitions within the entity grid, effectively addressing limitations of the discrete representation used in prior work. A secondary contribution lies in the end-to-end training approach, allowing for task-specific feature learning. A minor contribution is the demonstration of improved performance across three standard coherence evaluation tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Limitation of Existing Models:** The core strength of this work lies in tackling the limitations of the discrete representation inherent in traditional entity grid models. By leveraging distributed representations and convolutional layers, the authors demonstrably allow for modeling longer-range dependencies and potentially mitigating the curse of dimensionality. The argument that discrete representations hinder the ability to model longer transitions is sound and well-motivated.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates improvements over existing non-neural and neural baselines across all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The reported gains (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating) are significant, particularly given the established nature of this research area. Achieving state-of-the-art results is a positive indicator.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work, and each section builds upon the previous one in a coherent manner. The figures (specifically Figure 2) effectively illustrate the proposed architecture.
4. **Availability of Code:** The release of the code is a commendable practice that promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Neural Architecture:** While the *application* of CNNs to the entity grid framework is a valid contribution, the CNN architecture itself is fairly standard. The paper doesn’t present any novel CNN components or techniques. The architecture feels almost “plug-and-play” and lacks specific innovation within the neural network design itself. Simply applying a well-known technique to a new problem isn’t inherently groundbreaking.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The paper presents results for “Grid-CNN” and “Extended Grid-CNN,” but lacks a comprehensive ablation study to dissect the contribution of each component (e.g., embedding size, filter size, the inclusion of entity-specific features). It’s unclear which aspects of the model are driving the performance gains. The jump from Grid-CNN to Extended Grid-CNN is substantial, and a more granular analysis is needed.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (batch size, embedding size, dropout rate, etc.) is crucial for the performance of neural networks. The paper mentions a development set was used for tuning, but does not provide sufficient details regarding the search process or the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings. Reporting a sensitivity analysis or justification for the chosen parameters would strengthen the work.
4. **Minor Formatting Issues & Repetition:** There are some minor stylistic inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of abbreviations) and a degree of redundancy in the text (particularly in the introduction and related work sections). For instance, the limitations of the entity grid model are repeatedly stated in slightly different ways.
5. **Dataset limitations and transferability:** The experiments are largely limited to the WSJ corpus. While this allows for comparison to prior work, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings. Do the improvements observed on WSJ translate to other text genres or domains?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the criteria used to select the optimal settings?
2. What is the computational cost of the neural model compared to the traditional entity grid models? (e.g., training time, inference time).
3. Can you elaborate on the impact of different entity-specific features on performance? Are some features more informative than others?
4. Did you explore other neural network architectures besides CNNs (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) for this task? If so, what were the results? If not, why?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please ensure consistent formatting throughout the paper (e.g., citation style, abbreviations).
*   The reference list is extensive, but a few entries appear incomplete.
*   The text mentioning the fourth column of Table 1 needs rephrasing for clarity. ("The fourth column (# Pairs) in Table 1 shows the resulting number of (original, permuted) pairs used for training our model and for testing in the discrimination task.") is a little clumsy.
* The citation of Guinaudeau and Strube (2013) in the experimental setting section is not needed. It can be safely removed.

Despite the paper’s strengths, the limited novelty of the neural architecture and the lack of a thorough ablation study raise concerns. While the empirical results are positive, a more in-depth analysis is needed to fully understand the contributions of the proposed model. I lean towards **rejection** due to the lack of significant architectural innovation and the insufficient analysis of the experimental results. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration.
