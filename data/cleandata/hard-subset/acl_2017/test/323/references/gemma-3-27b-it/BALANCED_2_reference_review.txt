This paper presents a neural coherence model designed to overcome limitations of traditional entity grid approaches. The authors propose a convolutional neural network (CNN) that leverages distributed representations of entity transitions and features, enabling end-to-end training for task-specific coherence assessment. The model is evaluated on three standard tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – and achieves state-of-the-art results. 

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of a CNN architecture to the task of local coherence modeling, demonstrating improvements over established entity grid methods. The use of distributed representations allows for capturing longer-range dependencies and incorporating entity-specific features more effectively than discrete representations. A second valuable contribution is the end-to-end training approach, enabling task-specific feature learning and further enhancing performance. Finally, the comprehensive evaluation on three diverse tasks with clear comparisons to existing baselines provides strong evidence of the model's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently reports improvements across all three evaluation tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance. The gains of 4% on discrimination, up to 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary coherence rating are significant and demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed model.  The statistical significance tests further support the robustness of these findings.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models – namely, the use of discrete representations and the decoupling of feature computation from the target task. The proposed neural model directly addresses these issues, making the approach logically sound and compelling.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough, using three standard tasks commonly employed in the coherence modeling literature. This allows for direct comparison with existing methods and provides a well-rounded assessment of the model's capabilities. The use of publicly available datasets (WSJ, DUC’03) also enhances reproducibility.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized.  The architecture is clearly explained with the help of Figure 2, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail. This makes the paper easy to understand and follow.
5. **Code Release:** Releasing the code is a significant strength, promoting reproducibility and enabling further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN Application:** While applying CNNs to entity grid representations is a sensible approach, the novelty of using CNNs in NLP is diminishing.  The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this application specifically overcomes the challenges of coherence modeling compared to other recent CNN-based approaches in related fields.
2. **Entity-Specific Feature Incorporation:** The authors mention incorporating entity-specific features, but only utilize a limited subset (named entity type, salience, proper mention). While acknowledging this limitation, the paper doesn’t fully explore the potential benefits of a more comprehensive feature set, or investigate strategies for automatically learning relevant features.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper would benefit from an ablation study that systematically evaluates the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., the convolutional layers, the embedding layer, the entity-specific features). This would provide further insight into the relative importance of each component and help understand the model's inner workings.
4. **Minor Dataset Details:** The summary coherence rating task dataset is relatively small. While this is standard for the task, the authors should explicitly acknowledge the limited statistical power and potential impact on the reported results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on why you chose to use a max-pooling layer after the convolutional layers, as opposed to other pooling strategies like average pooling?
2. In the experiment section, you mention a failed attempt with the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014).  Could you provide more details about the specific settings and challenges encountered during that experiment? Was it a matter of hyperparameter tuning, or fundamental incompatibility with the dataset?
3. What are your plans for exploring more sophisticated entity-specific features in future work? Do you envision using pre-trained embeddings for these features, or learning them jointly with the rest of the model?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference formatting is somewhat inconsistent (e.g., some include URLs, others do not).
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of potential limitations of the model, such as its reliance on accurate coreference resolution.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The proposed neural model demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods, and the comprehensive evaluation provides compelling evidence of its effectiveness. While some aspects of the approach could be further explored, the paper is well-written, clearly presented, and provides a solid foundation for future research. I recommend acceptance.
