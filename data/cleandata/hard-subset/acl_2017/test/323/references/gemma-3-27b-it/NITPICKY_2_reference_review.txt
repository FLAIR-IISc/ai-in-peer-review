This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The core contribution is the application of convolutional neural networks to learn distributed representations of entity transitions and features, enabling end-to-end training for task-specific coherence assessment. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating improved performance across three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) compared to existing entity grid models.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper consistently achieves state-of-the-art results on the evaluated tasks, offering tangible improvements over existing methods. The reported gains, while not dramatic, are consistent and statistically significant, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Addressing Limitations of Entity Grids:** The authors directly address known limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely, the discrete representation of features and the decoupling of feature computation from downstream tasks. The neural approach offers a principled way to overcome these issues through distributed representations and end-to-end learning.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The description of the neural architecture and the training procedure is clear and easy to follow. The inclusion of a diagram (Figure 2) is helpful for understanding the model’s structure.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on a comprehensive suite of tasks using established datasets and evaluation protocols. The comparison against several baselines, including the strong extended grid model, strengthens the validity of the results. The release of code is a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of CNNs to entity grids is a reasonable approach, the architectural choices themselves are relatively standard. The paper doesn’t introduce novel convolutional techniques or attention mechanisms that significantly differentiate it from existing neural NLP models. The core innovation resides more in the *application* of established techniques to a specific problem than in the techniques themselves.
2. **Limited Analysis of Feature Importance:** The paper mentions incorporating entity-specific features but provides limited analysis of their contribution. It is unclear which features are most important for performance, and whether the neural network is effectively utilizing them. A more detailed ablation study examining the impact of different feature combinations would strengthen the analysis.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameter tuning process, while described, lacks sufficient detail. The sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings (e.g., filter size, embedding dimension) is not adequately investigated. Without a clearer understanding of this sensitivity, it's difficult to assess the robustness of the results.
4. **Inconsistent Reporting & Minor Errors:** Table 2 states a batch size, but this value is not referenced in the experimental setup. More critically, the formatting of Table 3 is inconsistent; the inclusion of '†' symbol seems arbitrary and lacks clear explanation within the text, simply indicating "significant superiority." The statistical tests used (approximate randomization test) aren't detailed enough to understand the significance claim.
5. **Dataset Limitations & Generalizability:** The primary evaluation is conducted on the WSJ corpus, which may not fully represent the diversity of real-world text. The transferability of the model to other domains or text types is not investigated, raising concerns about its generalizability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of different entity-specific features on model performance? Specifically, which features contribute the most to the observed gains?
2.  Can you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? What strategies were used to select the optimal values, and how sensitive is the model's performance to different settings?
3. What is the specific statistical significance threshold used for the '†' symbol in Table 3? Please provide details of the randomization test.
4. Have you investigated the performance of the model on datasets beyond the WSJ corpus? If not, what steps would be necessary to assess its generalizability to other domains?
5. The paper mentions pre-trained models - were these models pre-trained *specifically* on the coherence tasks described, or on a larger, more general corpus?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive but inconsistent in formatting.
*   Consider expanding the related work section to discuss recent advances in neural coherence modeling beyond those explicitly cited.
*   The description of the pairwise ranking objective (Equation 5) could be clarified. It is not immediately obvious why maximizing the margin between the coherence scores is equivalent to the stated goal.



Despite achieving strong empirical results, the lack of significant architectural novelty and the presence of several minor flaws raise concerns. While the paper demonstrates a competent application of neural networks to a classic NLP problem, I am inclined towards **rejection** due to the lack of substantial innovation and the need for further refinement and analysis.
