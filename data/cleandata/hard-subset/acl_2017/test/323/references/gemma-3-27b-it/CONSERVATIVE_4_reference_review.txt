This paper presents a neural network-based model for local coherence assessment, building upon the established entity grid framework. The primary contribution is the application of convolutional neural networks to learn representations of entity transitions, aiming to overcome limitations of discrete representations in traditional entity grid models. A secondary contribution is the end-to-end training approach, intended to learn task-specific features. Finally, the paper demonstrates empirically that this neural model achieves state-of-the-art results on three standard coherence evaluation tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirically Sound Improvements:** The reported gains across all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) are consistent and non-trivial, particularly the 4% absolute improvement on discrimination and 2.5% on insertion. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on established benchmarks is a significant positive.
2. **Well-Established Baseline:** The authors convincingly situate their work within the established literature of entity grid models and clearly articulate the limitations they address. The comparison against strong, pre-existing baselines (including the extended grid model of Elsner & Charniak) strengthens the validity of the reported improvements.
3. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The explanation of the neural architecture and the training process is sufficiently detailed, and the inclusion of Figure 2 is helpful.
4. **Code Release:** The release of the code is commendable and will facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Idea:** While the application of CNNs to the entity grid representation is a reasonable step, the core idea itself – replacing hand-engineered features with learned representations – is not fundamentally novel in the context of NLP. The paper doesn’t sufficiently emphasize *why* CNNs are particularly well-suited for this task beyond a generic statement about capturing long-range dependencies.
2. **Overstated Claims of “State-of-the-Art”:** The claim of “state-of-the-art” needs qualification. The improvements are incremental and are achieved on a relatively small dataset (WSJ) and a somewhat dated set of tasks. While statistically significant, the practical impact of a 2-4% improvement warrants cautious interpretation. The exclusion of more recent and potentially competitive models (e.g., transformer-based approaches) weakens this claim.
3. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** While the authors present results for a basic and extended version of their model, a more thorough ablation study is needed to understand the individual contribution of different components (e.g., entity-specific features, different convolutional filter sizes). This would help to justify the architectural choices made.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any discussion of *where* the model fails. A qualitative analysis of the types of coherence errors the model makes would provide valuable insights and guide future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of CNNs over other neural network architectures (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) for modeling entity transitions?
2. The paper mentions a distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014) that performed poorly in your experiments. Can you elaborate on the specific settings and challenges encountered when attempting to integrate or compare against this approach?
3.  Could you provide examples of cases where the neural model succeeds while the baseline entity grid model fails, and vice versa? This would help illustrate the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
4. What are the computational costs associated with the neural model compared to the traditional entity grid models?



**Additional Comments:** The reference list is extensive. It would be helpful to indicate which papers were directly compared to in the experiments (e.g., by adding a short phrase like “compared in Section 5”). The description of the pairwise ranking objective (Equation 5) could be slightly clearer.
