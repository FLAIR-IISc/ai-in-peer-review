## Peer Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

**Summary:**

This paper presents a neural network architecture for assessing local text coherence, building upon the widely used entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely their reliance on discrete representations and task-agnostic feature computation – by using distributed representations and end-to-end training.  They evaluate their model on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, reporting state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Neuralization of Entity Grid:** The primary contribution is a novel neural architecture that effectively replaces the discrete representations within the traditional entity grid model with learned distributed representations. This allows for capturing longer-range dependencies and mitigating the curse of dimensionality.
2. **End-to-End Training for Coherence:** The authors demonstrate the benefit of end-to-end training, allowing the model to learn task-specific features automatically, rather than relying on manually engineered features.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents comprehensive experimental results across three established evaluation tasks, consistently outperforming existing entity grid models and achieving state-of-the-art performance.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core Limitation of Entity Grid Models:** The paper directly tackles a significant drawback of the entity grid approach - the limitations of discrete representations. Moving to distributed representations is a natural and promising direction for improving the model's capacity and generalization ability. The convolutional architecture is a sensible choice for capturing transitions in the grid.
2. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation on three standard datasets and tasks. The consistent improvements over strong baselines, including the well-established extended entity grid model, provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.  The inclusion of statistical significance tests adds further rigor.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, model architecture, and experimental setup. The inclusion of Figure 2 is helpful for understanding the model's inner workings.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention releasing their code, which is a significant positive aspect, enabling further research and validation by the community.
5. **Effective Incorporation of Entity-Specific Features:** Showing that the model can easily incorporate entity-specific features (as demonstrated in Section 3.1 and the experimental results) is a valuable addition, bridging the gap between the neural approach and prior work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Neural Architecture:** While the application of a CNN to the entity grid representation is a logical step, the architecture itself is fairly standard. The core novelty lies more in the *application* of neural networks to this specific problem rather than a groundbreaking architectural innovation. A deeper discussion of related neural sequence modeling approaches and how this work differs would be beneficial.
2. **Ablation Study is Limited:** While the results show the benefit of the extended grid version, a more detailed ablation study would be valuable. For instance, it would be helpful to see results with different embedding sizes, filter numbers, or pooling strategies to better understand the contribution of each component.  What happens if entity-specific features are *removed* from the extended version?
3. **Dataset Limitations & Task Specificity:** The reliance on the WSJ corpus for the discrimination and insertion tasks could limit the generalizability of the findings. These tasks may be more suited to journalistic text and may not translate well to other domains (e.g., conversational text, creative writing). The authors acknowledge this in their discussion, but it remains a concern. The summary coherence rating task suffers from small data size.
4. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper focuses solely on quantitative results. Providing some qualitative examples of how the model identifies coherent vs. incoherent text would strengthen the understanding of *why* the model works.  Are there specific entity transitions or features the model particularly focuses on?




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of a convolutional neural network for modeling entity transitions?  How does this compare to other potential architectures like recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or transformers, and why did you believe a CNN was most appropriate?
2.  You mention the potential for including rhetorical relations in future work. Could you provide more details on how you envision incorporating this information into the model? Would it be added as additional entity-specific features, or would it require a more significant architectural modification?
3.  Regarding the limited size of the summary coherence rating dataset, have you considered techniques like data augmentation or transfer learning to improve the model's performance on this task?
4.  The ablation study is limited. Could you present a more detailed breakdown of the impact of different components (embedding size, filter number, etc.) on the model’s performance?
5. Can you provide examples of text segments where your model correctly identifies coherence while previous models fail, and vice versa? This qualitative insight would be extremely valuable.




**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*   The use of readily available tools like Brown coherence toolkit is appreciated.
*   The phrasing "pairwise input… is required only to train the model" in Section 3 is a little awkward. Rewording for clarity would be helpful.
* The paper would be stronger if it discussed the computational complexity of the proposed model compared to the traditional entity grid approaches.
