## Peer Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

**Summary:**

This paper presents a neural network architecture for assessing local text coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely, their reliance on discrete representations and task-agnostic feature computation – by employing distributed representations and end-to-end training. They evaluate their model on three tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, demonstrating state-of-the-art results and substantial improvements over non-neural baselines.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Neuralization of Entity Grid:** The primary contribution is a convolutional neural network that effectively captures long-range entity transitions within the entity grid framework, moving beyond the limitations of discrete representations and fixed-length transition probabilities.
2. **End-to-End Task-Specific Learning:** The authors demonstrate the benefits of learning task-specific features directly within the neural network through end-to-end training, improving performance across all evaluation tasks.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The presented experimental results consistently show improvements over existing entity grid models across three different coherence evaluation tasks, establishing a new state-of-the-art.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Core Limitation:** The paper directly tackles a significant limitation of the established entity grid models – the inability to effectively model long-range dependencies due to the discrete representation of transitions.  The use of distributed representations and convolutional networks provides a clear and promising solution.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three diverse tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) strengthens the validity of the findings.  The choice of these tasks allows for a thorough assessment of the model’s performance in different coherence-related scenarios.  Reproducing results on a standard dataset (WSJ) is also a strong point.
3. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on all three evaluation tasks is a compelling argument for acceptance.  The reported gains (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating) are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivates the research. The connection to existing theories of coherence (Centering Theory) is well-established, and the limitations of prior work are clearly articulated.
5. **Code Release:** The release of the code is a valuable contribution, facilitating reproducibility and enabling further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Neural Architecture:** While the application of neural networks to the entity grid framework is a valuable contribution, the architectural choices (convolutional networks) are fairly standard in NLP.  The novelty lies more in *applying* these techniques to this specific problem rather than introducing fundamentally new neural network components. A more detailed discussion of why these specific architectures were chosen over alternatives would strengthen the paper.
2. **Entity-Specific Feature Incorporation:** The integration of entity-specific features feels somewhat superficial.  The authors acknowledge this by stating they only used a subset of features available in previous work.  A more comprehensive exploration of different entity-specific features and their impact on performance would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the approach of simply concatenating features could be limiting; exploring more sophisticated integration methods is warranted.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper would benefit from an ablation study to better understand the individual contributions of the different components of the model (e.g., distributed representations, convolutional layers, entity-specific features).  This would help isolate the key factors driving the performance improvements.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** While the WSJ corpus is a standard benchmark, its relatively formal writing style might not generalize well to other types of text.  Exploring performance on more diverse datasets would be valuable.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing convolutional neural networks specifically for modeling entity transitions? Were alternative architectures (e.g., recurrent neural networks) considered, and if so, why were they rejected?
2.  You mention the limitations of incorporating only a subset of available entity-specific features. Could you discuss the challenges associated with incorporating a larger number of features (e.g., computational cost, data sparsity) and how these might be addressed?
3.  Would you be willing to provide an ablation study demonstrating the impact of each key component of the model (distributed representations, convolutional layers, entity-specific features) on performance?
4.  How does the model perform on texts with a more conversational or informal style compared to the WSJ corpus? Have you considered evaluating on datasets beyond WSJ?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent. Please ensure consistent formatting throughout the paper.
*   Consider briefly discussing the computational complexity of the proposed model compared to traditional entity grid approaches.
