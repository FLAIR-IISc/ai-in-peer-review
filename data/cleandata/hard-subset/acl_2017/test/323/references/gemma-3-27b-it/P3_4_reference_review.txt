## Review for "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

**Summary:** This paper presents a neural network architecture for assessing local text coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely, the discrete representation of grammatical roles and task-agnostic feature computation – by using distributed representations and end-to-end training. They demonstrate improvements over existing non-neural models on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Neuralization of the Entity Grid:** The paper successfully adapts the entity grid representation to a neural framework, demonstrating the benefits of distributed representations for capturing entity transitions. This allows the model to consider longer-range dependencies than traditional methods.
2. **Demonstrated Empirical Improvements:** The authors provide strong empirical evidence of improvements across three established coherence evaluation benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art results. This validates the approach and suggests it is a viable alternative to existing methods.
3. **End-to-End Learning:** The end-to-end training approach allows for learning task-specific features automatically, removing the need for manual feature engineering which is a common limitation of earlier grid-based methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The consistent performance gains across multiple evaluation tasks are compelling. The use of standard benchmarks facilitates comparison and demonstrates the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of statistical significance testing adds further rigor.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models, providing a clear rationale for the proposed neural approach.
3. **Well-Explained Architecture:** The neural network architecture is described clearly and concisely, with a helpful diagram (Figure 2) illustrating the flow of information. The explanation of each layer and its purpose is easy to follow.
4. **Practical Contribution (Code Release):** The release of the code is a valuable contribution, facilitating reproducibility and enabling further research in this area.
5. **Thoughtful Consideration of Existing Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of related work, acknowledging the strengths and weaknesses of existing approaches and positioning the current work within the broader context of coherence modeling.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Neural Architecture:** While the application to the entity grid is valuable, the core neural architecture (CNN with embeddings) is relatively standard. The novelty lies more in the *application* of these techniques to this specific problem than in the architectural innovation itself. This is not necessarily a fatal flaw, but it impacts the overall significance of the contribution.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** While the paper includes results for models with and without entity-specific features, a more thorough ablation study would be beneficial. For example, it would be interesting to see the impact of different embedding sizes, convolutional filter sizes, and pooling strategies. Understanding the contribution of each component is crucial for analyzing the model’s behavior.
3. **Focus on Local Coherence:** The paper explicitly focuses on *local* coherence. While this is a well-defined problem, it’s important to acknowledge the limitations of this approach and the need for models that can capture longer-range dependencies and global discourse structure.  The discussion around this limitation is brief.
4. **Dependency on Coreference Resolution:** The entity grid inherently relies on accurate coreference resolution. The paper doesn’t discuss the impact of coreference errors on the performance of the model, and how these errors might propagate through the system.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Ablation Study:** Could you provide a more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different hyperparameters (embedding size, filter size, pooling) and architectural choices on the model's performance?
2.  **Coreference Sensitivity:**  How sensitive is your model to errors in the coreference resolution system used to construct the entity grid? Have you experimented with different coreference resolvers, and what impact did this have on performance?
3. **Long-Range Dependencies:** While your model addresses the length limitations of traditional entity grids, do you have any plans to incorporate mechanisms for capturing even longer-range dependencies and global discourse structure?  Would a recurrent or transformer-based architecture be a viable direction?
4. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide examples of cases where your model performs poorly, and analyze the reasons for these failures? What types of texts or coherence phenomena does the model struggle with?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "SIGF V.2" for statistical significance testing could be expanded with a brief explanation of what SIGF is for reviewers unfamiliar with the method.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough, but could be streamlined by grouping papers thematically rather than presenting them in chronological order.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling. While the neural architecture itself isn't groundbreaking, the effective application of neural networks to the entity grid framework, coupled with strong empirical results and code release, make this work worthy of acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and its impact. I recommend acceptance.
