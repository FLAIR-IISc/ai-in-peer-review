## Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

**Summary:** This paper proposes a neural network architecture for assessing local text coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors aim to address limitations of traditional entity grid models – their reliance on discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation – by using distributed representations and end-to-end training.  They demonstrate improvements over existing methods on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Neuralization of the Entity Grid:** The primary contribution is the successful application of a convolutional neural network to model local coherence using the entity grid representation. This allows for learning richer, distributed representations of entity transitions compared to traditional discrete approaches.
2. **End-to-End Training:**  The paper demonstrates the benefits of end-to-end training for coherence modeling, allowing the model to learn task-specific features automatically.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently achieves state-of-the-art results across three evaluation tasks, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models and effectively motivates the need for a neural approach. The problem of capturing long-range dependencies and learning task-specific features is well-defined.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence tasks using well-established datasets (WSJ and DUC’03).  The inclusion of multiple baselines, including a strong extended entity grid model, provides a good benchmark for comparison. Statistical significance tests are also performed.
3. **Well-Explained Architecture:** The neural architecture is described clearly and concisely, with a helpful diagram (Figure 2). The rationale behind design choices, such as the use of convolutional layers, is also explained well.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors have released their code, which is a significant strength and promotes reproducibility.
5. **Consistent Improvements:**  The neural model consistently outperforms the non-neural counterparts across all tasks, demonstrating a clear benefit from the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the *application* of a CNN to the entity grid is a valuable contribution, the architecture itself isn't particularly novel.  CNNs are widely used in NLP, and this work largely adapts existing techniques to a new domain. The core architectural innovation feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Feature Engineering still Present:** While end-to-end training is claimed, the inclusion of *some* entity-specific features (even a limited set of three) still involves feature engineering. The paper doesn't fully explore a purely learned feature representation. The benefit of *adding* these features, while shown empirically, isn't deeply analyzed – is it truly essential or just a performance boost?
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the model struggles (e.g., specific types of coherence violations) would provide valuable insights and directions for future work.
4. **Discussion of Related Work could be Deeper:** The related work section briefly mentions other coherence models but doesn’t thoroughly engage with the broader literature on discourse modeling, neural approaches to discourse, or related representation learning techniques.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the limitations of the discrete representation in traditional entity grids. How does the learned embedding space capture semantic relationships between grammatical roles (S, O, X, -)?  Can you provide any analysis of the learned embeddings (e.g., visualization, nearest neighbors)?
2.  While you show improvements with the inclusion of a small set of entity-specific features, have you explored a purely end-to-end approach without any pre-defined features? What was the performance in that scenario?
3.  Could you provide some examples of cases where your model fails, and discuss why you think it fails in those instances?  A detailed error analysis would be very valuable.
4.  The results on the summary coherence rating task are less convincing than the other two tasks. Could you elaborate on the challenges of applying this model to summary evaluation and potential ways to improve performance in this setting?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning, is clear and concise.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of discourse coherence modeling. While the architectural novelty is limited, the successful application of neural networks to the entity grid framework, combined with the strong empirical results and clear presentation, makes this a worthwhile contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically regarding error analysis and deeper engagement with related work.
