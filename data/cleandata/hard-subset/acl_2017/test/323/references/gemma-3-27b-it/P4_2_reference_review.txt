This paper presents a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of previous grid-based models by using distributed representations and an end-to-end trainable approach. The primary contribution is the successful application of convolutional neural networks to model entity transitions within the entity grid, allowing for the capture of longer-range dependencies and task-specific feature learning. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive evaluation across three established coherence tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. Finally, the release of the code is a valuable contribution to the research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing entity grid models across all three evaluation tasks, with statistically significant improvements (indicated by the '†' symbol). The gains of 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary coherence rating demonstrate a clear and measurable benefit of the proposed neural approach. This is particularly notable on the more challenging insertion task.
2. **Effective Use of CNNs for Coherence:**  The application of convolutional neural networks to the entity grid representation is a novel and effective approach. The authors clearly articulate the rationale for using CNNs—to capture long-range entity transitions and learn higher-level features in a location-invariant manner.  This is a conceptually sound and well-executed idea.
3. **End-to-End Training and Feature Learning:**  The end-to-end training approach, combined with distributed representations, addresses a key limitation of traditional entity grid models, which relied on handcrafted features.  This allows the model to automatically learn task-specific features, potentially leading to better generalization and performance.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper evaluates the model on three different tasks, providing a thorough assessment of its capabilities. Using established evaluation protocols and datasets allows for a fair comparison with existing approaches.  The inclusion of the summary coherence rating task, which involves human judgments, adds further weight to the evaluation.
5. **Code Release:** The release of the code facilitates reproducibility and encourages further research in this area. This is a significant benefit to the community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** While the *application* of CNNs to the entity grid is novel, the core idea of using neural networks for coherence modeling is not entirely new. Other approaches, such as Li and Hovy (2014), have already explored neural architectures for coherence assessment. The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from this existing work in terms of the underlying neural network architecture itself. The CNN is a pragmatic choice, but its innovation is incremental.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Reporting:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters on a development set, but provides limited details about the search space and the specific criteria used for selection beyond accuracy. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the rationale behind the chosen settings would strengthen the results.
3. **Limited Analysis of Entity-Specific Features:** The paper incorporates entity-specific features but uses only a small subset of those proposed by Elsner and Charniak (2011). The rationale for selecting only these features is not fully explained, and a more thorough investigation of the impact of different entity-specific features on performance would be valuable. Did the performance gains saturate with only three features?
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares the performance of Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN, it lacks a more detailed ablation study. For example, it would be informative to evaluate the contribution of the convolutional layers *without* the entity-specific features to better understand their individual impact on performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter search process? What ranges were explored for each hyperparameter, and what criteria were used to select the optimal settings?
2. Why were only three entity-specific features selected from the Elsner and Charniak (2011) work? Was there a limitation in computational resources or a specific reason why the others were excluded?
3.  How did you handle cases where the coreference resolution failed, leading to missing or incorrect entity mentions in the grid? What impact does coreference accuracy have on the model’s performance?
4.  Could you comment on the computational complexity of your approach compared to the traditional entity grid models?

*   A brief discussion of potential limitations and future directions beyond those mentioned in Section 7 would be beneficial.
