This paper proposes a neural architecture for coherence assessment, aiming to capture long-range entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features in an end-to-end trainable manner. The approach is evaluated on three tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, achieving state-of-the-art results. 

The primary contribution of this work is the application of convolutional neural networks to the entity grid representation of text for coherence modeling. While entity grids are a well-established technique, the authors effectively demonstrate how to leverage the strengths of CNNs – particularly their ability to learn hierarchical representations and handle variable-length inputs – to overcome limitations of traditional grid-based methods, specifically the curse of dimensionality and decoupling of feature extraction from the target task. The incorporation of entity-specific features within this neural framework is a solid secondary contribution, offering a principled way to integrate richer information into the coherence assessment process.  The consistent performance improvements across multiple evaluation tasks further strengthens the value of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Effective Use of CNNs for Coherence Modeling:**  The application of CNNs to entity grid data is a significant strength. This allows the model to learn complex patterns of entity transitions that are difficult to capture with traditional, discrete representations.  The use of convolutional filters naturally addresses the limitation of considering only short transitions (k ≤ 3) in previous entity grid models, as filters can learn to identify relevant patterns over longer sequences.
2. **End-to-End Training:** The end-to-end training approach is crucial. By jointly learning feature representations and the coherence assessment function, the model can adapt to the specific nuances of each task, leading to improved performance compared to systems with decoupled feature engineering.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent improvements over state-of-the-art baseline models on three different evaluation tasks provide compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Gains of 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating are substantial and demonstrate the practical impact of the work.
4. **Clear and Thorough Experimentation:** The paper provides a detailed description of the experimental setup, including the datasets used, the baseline models compared, and the hyperparameter settings. This level of detail enhances the reproducibility and credibility of the results.
5. **Code Release:** The release of the source code is a commendable practice, promoting transparency and allowing other researchers to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of CNNs and entity grids is effective, the individual components are not entirely novel. CNNs have been extensively used in NLP, and entity grids are a well-established approach to coherence modeling. The primary novelty lies in the application of CNNs to this specific problem.
2. **Dependence on Entity Resolution:** The performance of the model is inherently limited by the accuracy of the coreference resolution system used to identify entities. Errors in coreference resolution can propagate through the entity grid and negatively impact the coherence assessment. This dependency isn't fully discussed or mitigated.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The extensive hyperparameter tuning process, as evidenced by Table 2, suggests that the model's performance may be sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and robustness would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Analysis of Learned Features:** The paper describes the architecture but offers limited insight into *what* the convolutional filters are actually learning. Visualizing or analyzing the learned filters could provide a better understanding of the model's behavior and potential biases.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the performance of the coreference resolution system? Have you experimented with different coreference resolvers and analyzed the impact on coherence assessment accuracy?
2.  Can you provide any insights into what types of entity transitions the convolutional filters are learning to detect?  Are there specific patterns or combinations of grammatical roles that consistently activate certain filters?
3.  While you report improvements over existing methods, are there specific types of texts or coherence phenomena where your model still struggles? 
4.  The paper mentions experimenting with pre-training the model. Could you elaborate on the pre-training dataset and the impact of pre-training on performance, specifically for the summary coherence rating task?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-organized and comprehensive.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   Consider providing ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of individual components, such as the entity-specific features.
