This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The core contribution is the replacement of discrete representations and hand-engineered features with learned, distributed representations and convolutional layers, enabling end-to-end training. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on standard coherence evaluation tasks. A third, though less significant, contribution is the release of code for reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **State-of-the-art Results:** The paper convincingly demonstrates superior performance on three established coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The reported gains, particularly the 4%+ improvement on discrimination and 2.5%+ on insertion, are substantial and suggest a tangible improvement over existing methods.
2. **Principled Approach:** The motivation for moving to a neural network representation is sound. The limitations of discrete representations and task-agnostic feature engineering in the traditional entity grid approach are well-articulated. The proposed convolutional architecture naturally addresses these issues by learning distributed representations and allowing for end-to-end training.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on a diverse set of tasks, providing a comprehensive assessment of its capabilities. Utilizing the Wall Street Journal corpus and comparing against a range of existing methods, including the strong extended grid model from Elsner and Charniak (2011), strengthens the validity of the findings.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed architecture and experimental setup. Figure 2 provides a helpful visual overview of the neural network.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Architecture:** While the application to coherence modeling is novel, the core architectural components (convolutional layers, embedding layers, max pooling) are well-established and not particularly innovative. The paper doesn't introduce any new neural network building blocks. The architecture feels like a fairly direct application of existing techniques, rather than a substantial architectural contribution.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Reporting:** The report of hyperparameters (Table 2) feels incomplete.  What search strategy was employed? Why were those *specific* values chosen? A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance is necessary. The sensitivity of the results to these parameters is not adequately addressed.
3. **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper presents results for “Grid-CNN” and “Extended Grid-CNN.” However, a more thorough ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of the entity-specific features. Specifically, how much does each specific feature contribute to the overall performance? Were other feature combinations explored?  It’s unclear if the improvements attributed to entity features are truly significant or simply a result of increased model complexity.
4. **Overly Optimistic Claims and Minor Inconsistencies:** The claim of "state-of-the-art results" needs qualification. While the model performs well on the presented datasets, the paper doesn’t sufficiently address the limitations of these datasets or discuss potential biases. The authors state the summary coherence rating results are new, but this isn’t fully substantiated – there’s no clear comparison to all previous work on DUC’03. Also, in section 3.2, the equation for pairwise ranking loss uses a "max{0, ...}" which is a hinge loss, but the text doesn't explicitly state this, leading to ambiguity.
5. **Dataset Limitations:** The reliance on relatively small and somewhat dated datasets (e.g., DUC’03) is a concern. The generalizability of the findings to more complex and realistic text is questionable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process. What search strategy was used, and why were the final hyperparameter values chosen?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed ablation study of the entity-specific features? Specifically, how much does each feature contribute to the overall performance, and were other feature combinations explored?
3.  How do the results generalize to other datasets beyond WSJ and DUC'03? Have you considered evaluating the model on more recent and challenging datasets?
4.  Can you clarify the loss function used for training (e.g., hinge loss)?
5.  Why was the recursive neural network model by Li and Hovy (2014) excluded from the final results? Were there specific issues encountered during implementation or training?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list appears incomplete. Several citations are mentioned in the text but are missing from the bibliography.
* A discussion of computational complexity (training and inference time) would be valuable.
