## Review for "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a neural network architecture for assessing local text coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely, their discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation – by leveraging distributed representations and end-to-end training. They demonstrate improvements over existing methods on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Neuralization of the Entity Grid:** The primary contribution is a viable neural network architecture that effectively replaces the handcrafted feature engineering inherent in traditional entity grid models. This offers a more flexible and potentially more powerful approach to capturing local coherence.
2. **Demonstrated Empirical Improvements:** The paper shows consistent and statistically significant improvements across three established evaluation tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results.  This provides strong evidence that the proposed approach is effective.
3. **End-to-End Training:**  The end-to-end training approach, while not entirely novel in NLP, is a crucial aspect, allowing the model to learn task-specific features directly from the data.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Formulation & Motivation:** The introduction effectively articulates the importance of coherence modeling and the limitations of existing approaches, specifically the entity grid method. The rationale for a neural approach is well-justified.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent performance gains across multiple tasks are compelling. The improvements, particularly the 2.5% gain on insertion and >4% on summary coherence rating, are substantial and demonstrate the value of the proposed model. The statistical significance testing strengthens these claims.
3. **Well-Established Evaluation Protocol:**  The use of standard evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) and datasets allows for direct comparison with previous work and facilitates reproducibility.
4. **Principled Approach:** The use of convolutional neural networks feels like a natural fit for the grid-based representation, allowing for the capture of local patterns and long-range dependencies. The justification for design choices (wide convolutions, max pooling) is reasonable.
5. **Code Release:**  The authors have released their code, which is a significant positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application to the entity grid is valuable, the underlying neural network architecture (CNN with embeddings) is relatively standard. The paper doesn’t introduce fundamentally new neural components.  The novelty resides more in the *application* rather than architectural innovation.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** The paper mentions extensive hyperparameter tuning, but doesn’t provide detailed insights into the sensitivity of the model to these parameters. Understanding the robustness of the model to different parameter settings would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper presents results for the “Extended Grid-CNN”, but lacks detailed ablation studies exploring the contribution of each component (e.g., the impact of entity-specific features, different embedding sizes, convolutional filter sizes).  This would help understand which aspects of the model are most crucial for performance.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The WSJ dataset, while standard, may not fully represent the diversity of text genres. Evaluating the model on a broader range of datasets (e.g., news, fiction, social media) would strengthen the generalizability claims.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Ablation Studies:**  Could you provide more detailed ablation studies, specifically isolating the impact of the entity-specific features and the different convolutional parameters (filter size, number of filters) on the performance of your model?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** What is the sensitivity of your model's performance to changes in the key hyperparameters (embedding size, learning rate, dropout rate)?  Could you provide a plot or table illustrating this?
3. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide some examples of cases where your model fails, and discuss the potential reasons for these failures? What types of coherence issues does the model still struggle with?
4. **Computational Cost:** Compared to the traditional entity grid approaches, what is the computational cost (training and inference time) of your neural model? Is the increase in complexity justified by the performance gains?
5.  **Generalizability to Other Tasks:** Beyond the three evaluated tasks, do you anticipate your model being applicable to other coherence-related tasks such as text generation or machine translation?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality is generally good, but some sections could benefit from more concise explanations.
* The inclusion of related work is thorough.
* It would be helpful to include a more visual representation of the entity grid and the neural network architecture beyond Figure 2.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The neuralization of the entity grid framework is a promising approach, and the empirical results are compelling. While the architectural novelty is limited, the demonstrated improvements and the well-defined experimental setup justify acceptance. I recommend **accepting** this paper with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above.
