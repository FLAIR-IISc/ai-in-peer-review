This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The primary contribution, as presented by the authors, is the improvement in coherence assessment performance across three tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – achieved by replacing discrete representations with distributed ones and employing end-to-end training. A secondary contribution is the successful incorporation of entity-specific features into this neural framework. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent performance gains over established baselines (entity grid models) on three standard coherence evaluation tasks. The reported improvements (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4% on summary coherence rating) are not trivial and suggest a genuine benefit from the proposed neural approach. The fact that state-of-the-art results were achieved is a positive indicator.
2. **Well-Established Baseline:** The comparison against the entity grid model is appropriate, as this is a widely used and respected method in the field of discourse coherence. This allows for a meaningful assessment of the proposed method’s value.  The authors appropriately acknowledge and build upon the prior work of Barzilay and Lapata and Elsner and Charniak.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The architecture diagram (Figure 2) is helpful in understanding the model.
4. **Code Release:** The release of the code is a significant positive aspect, allowing for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Innovation:** While the results are positive, the core innovation – applying a neural network to a well-established feature representation (entity grids) – feels incremental. The benefits of distributed representations and end-to-end training are well-known in NLP, and applying them to this specific problem doesn't represent a paradigm shift.  The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate *why* a neural network is fundamentally better suited for this task than, for example, a carefully engineered feature-based model.
2. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** The architecture itself (convolutional neural network) is quite standard. The application to entity grids isn’t particularly novel in the context of recent NLP trends. The paper doesn't explore more sophisticated neural architectures (e.g., transformers, LSTMs) that might offer further improvements.
3. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** While the authors present results with and without entity-specific features, a more thorough ablation study would be valuable. For instance, it would be informative to see the performance of the model *without* end-to-end training, to isolate the benefits of each component.  Furthermore, analyzing the learned embeddings for grammatical roles could provide insight into what the model is actually capturing.
4. **Task Difficulty & Generalizability:** The gains on insertion are smaller than on discrimination, which suggests the model may struggle with more challenging aspects of coherence.  It remains unclear how well this model would generalize to other types of text or different coherence phenomena not captured in the evaluation datasets. The reliance on WSJ corpus, while standard, limits the scope of generalizability claims.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned embeddings for grammatical roles? What patterns or distinctions do they capture?
2.  What were the results of running the model *without* end-to-end training? How much of the performance gain is attributable to this aspect of the approach?
3.  How do the performance gains vary across different types of entities (e.g., named entities, pronouns, common nouns)?
4.  Have you experimented with different neural architectures (e.g., transformers, LSTMs) for this task? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to Li and Hovy (2014) and the explanation for excluding their results feels somewhat dismissive. A more thorough comparison, or a justification for why their approach is fundamentally different, would be beneficial.
*   The notation in equation (5) could be clarified. It's not immediately obvious why maximizing this objective function leads to better coherence ranking.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a reasonable improvement over existing methods. However, the incremental nature of the innovation and the lack of deeper analysis limit its overall impact. I would lean towards acceptance, but with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses raised above in their response.
