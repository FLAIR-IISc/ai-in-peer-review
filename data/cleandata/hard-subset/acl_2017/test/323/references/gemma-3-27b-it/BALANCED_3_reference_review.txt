This paper presents a neural architecture for modeling local coherence, aiming to overcome limitations of traditional entity grid models. The core idea is to use distributed representations of entity transitions and features, and to learn task-specific features through end-to-end training. The authors evaluate their approach on three tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – and report state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Neural Entity Grid Model:** The most significant contribution is the proposed neural architecture which replaces the discrete representations of traditional entity grids with continuous distributed representations, allowing for the capture of longer-range dependencies and enabling the incorporation of arbitrary entity-specific features.
2. **End-to-End Training:** The authors demonstrate the benefit of end-to-end training for learning task-specific features, as opposed to relying on pre-defined, task-agnostic features used in prior work. This is a valuable contribution that leverages the power of neural networks for feature learning.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides solid empirical evidence, consistently outperforming existing entity grid models across three different evaluation tasks, establishing a new state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models, providing a strong motivation for the proposed neural approach. The connection to Centering Theory is also well-explained.
2. **Well-Designed Architecture:** The proposed architecture, leveraging convolutional layers for modeling entity transitions, appears well-suited to the task and addresses the limitations of discrete representations. The inclusion of entity-specific features is also a sensible extension.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating the model on three distinct tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) provides a robust assessment of its generalizability and effectiveness.
4. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** Consistently achieving state-of-the-art results on all three tasks demonstrates the practical value and efficacy of the proposed method.
5. **Code Release:** Releasing the code is a significant strength, allowing for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of neural networks to this specific problem is valuable, the core architecture (convolutional layers, embeddings) is fairly standard. The novelty lies primarily in the application *to* entity grid modeling, not in a fundamentally new architectural component. This weakens the claim of significant novelty.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning on a development set but lacks sufficient detail on the search space and specific values used. A more detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process would strengthen the reproducibility and reliability of the results.
3. **Ablation Study is Limited:** While the paper compares the extended version of the model to the basic version, a more comprehensive ablation study exploring the contribution of different components (e.g., entity-specific features, different convolutional filter sizes) would provide deeper insights into the model’s behavior and the importance of each component.
4. **Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of error analysis. Identifying specific cases where the model fails and analyzing the reasons for those failures could provide valuable insights for future improvement. What types of coherence issues does the model still struggle with?
5. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons to existing grid models are present, the lack of comparison to more recent neural coherence models (beyond the brief mention of Li & Hovy, which was excluded) is a notable omission. A comparison to models based on Transformers or other modern architectures would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed method’s performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter search space and the specific values used for the final experiments?
2. Would you be willing to conduct a more detailed ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of your model (e.g., different entity-specific features, convolutional filter sizes)?
3. Can you provide some examples of cases where the model fails to accurately assess coherence, and discuss the potential reasons for these failures?
4.  Have you considered comparing your model against more recent neural coherence models based on Transformers or other modern architectures? If not, why?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
* The figures are helpful in illustrating the model architecture and the entity grid representation.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the proposed model and existing entity grid models.
* The reference list is comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The proposed neural architecture effectively addresses the limitations of traditional entity grid models and achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple evaluation tasks. While the architectural novelty is limited, the application of neural networks to this problem and the demonstration of end-to-end learning are valuable contributions. The weaknesses are primarily related to a lack of detailed analysis and comparison to stronger baselines. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding hyperparameter tuning details, ablation studies, and error analysis.
