## Review of "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

**Summary:** This paper proposes a neural network architecture for assessing local text coherence, building upon the widely-used entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely, their reliance on discrete representations and task-agnostic feature computation – by employing distributed representations and end-to-end training. They demonstrate improvements over existing methods on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Neuralization of the Entity Grid:** The primary contribution is a compelling demonstration of how to effectively translate the established entity grid formalism into a neural network architecture. This allows for the learning of richer, distributed representations of entity transitions, circumventing the limitations of discrete representations.
2. **End-to-End Training for Coherence:**  The adoption of an end-to-end training approach, combined with the neural framework, enables the model to learn task-specific features automatically, a significant advantage over manually engineered features.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The authors convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art performance across three established coherence evaluation tasks, providing a solid validation of their approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the shortcomings of existing entity grid models and provides a strong motivation for exploring neural network-based alternatives. The connection to established theories of coherence (Centering Theory) is also well-defined.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The proposed architecture – leveraging convolutional networks for feature extraction – is a logical and effective choice given the task. The justifications for design decisions (e.g., wide convolutions, max-pooling) are well-explained.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Evaluating the model on three diverse tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating) strengthens the conclusions.  The use of a standard dataset (WSJ) and comparison to established baselines is commendable.
4. **Reproducibility:**  The authors' release of their code is a significant strength, promoting reproducibility and further research.
5. **Consistent Improvements:** The gains over the baselines are consistent across all three tasks, indicating the robustness of the approach. The improvements on insertion, a more challenging task, are particularly noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of neural networks *to* the entity grid is a valuable contribution, the specific neural architecture itself (CNN with max-pooling) is not particularly novel. The novelty resides more in *how* this architecture is applied to the specific problem of coherence modeling.  The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of architectural choices and a comparison to other potential neural architectures.
2. **Entity-Specific Feature Integration is Basic:** While the authors incorporate entity-specific features, the method is relatively simple (concatenation).  Exploring more sophisticated integration methods (e.g., attention mechanisms, feature gating) could potentially yield further improvements.
3. **Ablation Studies are Limited:** While results are shown for Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN, a more comprehensive ablation study exploring the impact of individual components (e.g., embedding dimension, filter size, pooling length) would provide deeper insights into the model's behavior.
4. **Discussion of Error Analysis is Missing:** A qualitative analysis of failure cases could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the model and potential areas for future improvement.  Why does the model *fail* in certain scenarios?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Architectural Justification:**  Why was a CNN chosen over other potentially suitable architectures (e.g., RNNs, Transformers) for modeling entity transitions? Did you experiment with alternative architectures, and if so, what were the results?
2. **Entity Feature Integration:**  You mention the potential for more sophisticated integration of entity-specific features.  Have you experimented with approaches beyond simple concatenation (e.g., attention mechanisms, feature gating)? If so, could you share those results?
3. **Error Analysis:**  Could you provide examples of cases where the model performs poorly, and discuss potential reasons for these failures?  This would provide valuable insights into the model's limitations.
4. **Impact of Pre-training:**  In the summary coherence rating task, you mention pre-training on the WSJ corpus.  How significant was the performance improvement due to pre-training, and what is the trade-off between pre-training data size and performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The discussion of related work is solid, though a brief mention of recent work using transformers for discourse analysis might be relevant.
*  The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The neuralization of the entity grid framework is a well-motivated and effectively executed idea, resulting in state-of-the-art performance. While the architecture itself is not groundbreaking, the application to this specific problem, coupled with the comprehensive evaluation, makes this a worthy contribution. I recommend acceptance.
