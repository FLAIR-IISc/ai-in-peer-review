This paper presents a neural network-based approach to modeling local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The core idea is to use distributed representations and convolutional layers to overcome the limitations of discrete representations and task-agnostic feature computation inherent in traditional entity grid models. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing methods on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating.

**Contributions:**

1. **Neuralization of the Entity Grid:** The most significant contribution is the successful adaptation of the entity grid model to a neural network architecture. This allows for the learning of distributed representations of entity transitions and features, which is a conceptually sound approach to address the curse of dimensionality and limitations of discrete representations.
2. **End-to-End Training:** The authors propose an end-to-end training method, enabling the model to learn task-specific features automatically. This is a key advantage over previous approaches where feature engineering was a crucial and often manual step.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines (including state-of-the-art entity grid models) across all three evaluation tasks. The improvements, particularly on the summary coherence rating task, are substantial.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models and provides a compelling motivation for exploring neural network-based alternatives.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:** The use of convolutional neural networks is a natural fit for modeling local coherence, as it allows the model to learn patterns and dependencies between entity transitions. The architecture is well-explained and logically structured.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation on three distinct tasks provides a thorough assessment of the model’s performance. The choice of tasks is appropriate and representative of the challenges in coherence modeling.
4. **Statistical Significance:** The authors appropriately use statistical significance tests to validate their results and demonstrate that the improvements are not due to chance.
5. **Code Release:** The release of the code promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of neural networks to the entity grid framework is a valuable contribution, the specific architecture (CNN) is relatively standard and lacks substantial architectural innovation. The paper does not explore alternative neural network architectures (e.g., recurrent networks, transformers) that might be better suited for capturing long-range dependencies.
2. **Entity-Specific Feature Incorporation:** The incorporation of entity-specific features is somewhat limited. The authors only include three features, and the potential benefits of exploring a wider range of features are not fully investigated. Furthermore, the paper does not discuss how these features are selected or their relative importance.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The description of the hyperparameter tuning process is somewhat brief. While the authors mention using a development set, more details about the search space and the criteria used for selecting the optimal hyperparameters would be beneficial.
4. **Comparison to Recent Neural Coherence Models:** The related work section does not adequately address recent advancements in neural coherence modeling beyond the entity grid framework, such as those based on language models or discourse-aware pre-trained models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa). A more thorough comparison to these approaches would provide a better understanding of the model’s strengths and weaknesses.
5. **Impact of Pre-training:** While pre-training improves performance on the summary coherence rating task, the impact of different pre-training strategies or datasets is not investigated. A more detailed analysis of pre-training could reveal valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why was a CNN chosen as the primary neural network architecture? Have you experimented with other architectures like RNNs or Transformers, and if so, what were the results?
2.  What criteria were used to select the three entity-specific features used in the Extended Grid-CNN model? Have you explored other features, and if so, what was their impact on performance?
3.  Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the specific criteria used for selecting the optimal hyperparameters?
4.  How does your model compare to more recent neural coherence models based on language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa)?
5.  Did you try different pre-training datasets and strategies for the summary coherence rating task, and if so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The figures are clear and informative.
*   The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive.
*   Consider adding an ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., entity-specific features, convolutional layers).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The neuralization of the entity grid framework is a significant step forward, and the empirical results are compelling. While the architecture itself is not particularly novel, the application to this problem domain and the strong performance justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
