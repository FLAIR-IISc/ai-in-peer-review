- Proposes a neural network version of the entity grid for local text coherence, using distributed representations and end-to-end training.  
- Demonstrates empirical improvements on discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating tasks.

- Successfully neuralizes the entity grid, showing learned distributed representations outperform hand-engineered features.  
- Shows consistent, statistically significant gains and state-of-the-art results across standard coherence tasks.  
- Advocates end-to-end task-aware training for coherence modeling, improving adaptability.

- Clearly motivates the move from discrete entity grids to a neural approach with ties to coherence theory.  
- Presents strong empirical results and a convincing ablation (Extended vs Grid-CNN).  
- Uses standard evaluation tasks for comparability and sets a reproducible baseline.  
- Releases code, aiding reproducibility and future work.  
- Employs CNNs appropriately to capture local transition patterns and incorporates entity-specific features.

- The neural architecture (CNN + embeddings) is fairly standard; novelty is mainly in application rather than architecture.  
- Lacks analysis of learned representations (embeddings, filters, activations) to explain why the model works.  
- Depends on external coreference resolution, introducing error sources and limiting true end-to-end learning; impact not fully explored.  
- Missing detailed error analysis to identify coherent phenomena the model still fails to capture.

- Request examples of learned embeddings or filter weights to illustrate captured features.  
- Ask how sensitive results are to different coreference resolvers and their performance variability.  
- Request examples and discussion of cases where the model performs poorly.  
- Ask whether pretrained embeddings (word2vec/GloVe) were tried for entity roles and their impact.

- Writing is clear, paper well-structured, and references comprehensive.

- Overall recommendation: Accept, given solid empirical contribution and clear presentation despite limited architectural novelty.
