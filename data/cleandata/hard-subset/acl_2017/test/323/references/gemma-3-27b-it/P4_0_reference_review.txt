This paper proposes a neural architecture for modeling local coherence, aiming to improve upon traditional entity grid-based methods. The core idea is to use distributed representations and convolutional layers to capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion. The model is evaluated on three tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, demonstrating state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Neural Entity Grid Representation:** The primary contribution is a neural network architecture that effectively represents the entity grid, moving away from discrete representations to distributed ones. This allows for capturing longer-range dependencies and potentially more nuanced relationships between entities.
2. **End-to-End Training:** The authors introduce an end-to-end training method, enabling the model to learn task-specific features automatically, rather than relying on manually engineered features. This is a significant step towards simplifying the coherence modeling pipeline.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents consistently strong empirical results across three evaluation tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach and establishing new state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models and provides a compelling motivation for exploring neural alternatives. The problem of coherence assessment is well-defined and its importance is highlighted.
2. **Well-Designed Architecture:** The proposed architecture, combining embedding layers, convolutional layers, and pooling, is logically sound and well-suited to the task of capturing entity transitions. The use of convolutional layers is particularly effective for modeling local patterns without being constrained by fixed transition lengths.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation on three diverse tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – provides a thorough assessment of the model's capabilities. This demonstrates the generalizability of the approach beyond a single evaluation metric.
4. **Strong Baselines and Comparisons:** The paper compares the proposed model against several strong baselines, including the traditional entity grid models and a graph-based approach. The consistent improvements over these baselines provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
5. **Code Release:** The release of the code promotes reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of a convolutional neural network to the entity grid representation is a reasonable approach, the architecture itself isn’t particularly novel. Convolutional neural networks are widely used in NLP, and the specific configuration presented doesn't introduce significant architectural innovations.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters on a development set, but doesn't provide much detail on the search process or the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the findings.
3. **Feature Incorporation Limited:** While the incorporation of entity-specific features is mentioned, the paper only utilizes a limited set of such features. Exploring a wider range of potentially relevant features could further improve performance. The impact of different feature combinations is not thoroughly investigated.
4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper doesn't include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of individual components of the architecture (e.g., the embedding layer, convolutional layers, pooling layer). This makes it difficult to understand which parts of the model are most important for achieving the observed performance gains.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the types of errors the model makes and potential reasons for those errors. This could provide valuable insights for future work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings? Specifically, how did you choose the optimal values for the key hyperparameters (e.g., embedding size, filter size, dropout rate)?
2. Were any other entity-specific features considered for incorporation into the model? If so, why were they ultimately excluded?
3. Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the model's predictions, highlighting examples of both successful and unsuccessful coherence assessments?
4. What is the computational cost of training and applying the proposed model compared to the traditional entity grid approaches?
