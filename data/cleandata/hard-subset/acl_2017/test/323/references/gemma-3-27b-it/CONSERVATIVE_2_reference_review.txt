This paper presents a neural network architecture for local coherence modeling, building upon the established entity grid framework. The primary contribution is the application of convolutional neural networks to learn distributed representations of entity transitions and features, enabling end-to-end training and, according to the authors, state-of-the-art results on standard coherence evaluation tasks. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating that incorporating entity-specific features within this neural framework further improves performance. Finally, the release of code is a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over existing entity grid models on three established coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). While the gains aren't massive, they are consistent across tasks and represent a positive step forward. The reported gains of around 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4% on summary coherence rating are noteworthy given the competitive baseline.
2. **Solid Baseline Comparison:** The authors compare against a range of relevant baselines, including the original entity grid model and its extensions, as well as a more recent graph-based approach. This allows for a reasonable assessment of the proposed method’s effectiveness within the existing literature.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The architecture is illustrated effectively in Figure 2, aiding understanding. The connection to Centering Theory and existing entity grid work is also well established.
4. **Code Release:** The availability of the code promotes reproducibility and allows for further research building upon this work. This is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Advancement & Limited Novelty:** While the results are positive, the core innovation—applying CNNs to entity grid features—feels incremental. The paper largely frames itself as *neuralizing* an existing approach, rather than proposing a fundamentally new coherence modeling paradigm. The benefits of using CNNs (distributed representations, end-to-end learning) are known and have been applied in many NLP tasks. The question is whether those benefits *significantly* outweigh the complexities added.
2. **Over-Reliance on Established Tasks:** The evaluation focuses exclusively on the standard discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating tasks. While these tasks are widely used, they have known limitations. They may not fully capture the nuances of coherence in real-world text generation or understanding scenarios. A more diverse evaluation suite, perhaps including human evaluation of generated text, would be stronger.
3. **Feature Engineering Remains Important:** Despite the claim of end-to-end learning, the best-performing model still relies on carefully selected entity-specific features. This suggests that the neural network isn’t fully learning relevant features automatically and that feature engineering still plays a crucial role in performance.  The authors acknowledge this to some extent but downplay its significance. The pre-trained model also suggests the network relies on learning from a related task, rather than mastering coherence from scratch.
4. **Limited Ablation Study:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed ablation study. For instance, what is the contribution of each entity-specific feature? How sensitive is the model to different embedding dimensions or convolutional filter sizes? A more thorough analysis would provide deeper insights into the model's behavior.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned embeddings? Are there specific entity transitions or features that the network consistently identifies as important for coherence?
2. The paper mentions limitations of existing tasks. What specific limitations do you see with the evaluation tasks used, and do you have plans for evaluating the model on more diverse or realistic coherence scenarios?
3. How does the performance of the neural models compare to the original entity grid model when using the *same* entity-specific features? This would isolate the contribution of the neural architecture more clearly.
4. You state that the model shares layers during training. Does this sharing introduce any challenges or limitations?



**Additional Comments:**

The citation formatting is inconsistent. Some citations are complete, while others are abbreviated (e.g., "Bengio et al., 2003"). This should be standardized. The reference to SIGF V.2 is helpful.
