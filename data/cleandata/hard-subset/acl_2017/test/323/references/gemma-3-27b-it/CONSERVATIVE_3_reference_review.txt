This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of convolutional neural networks to learn representations of entity transitions, moving away from discrete probability calculations. A secondary contribution is the end-to-end training approach, attempting to learn task-specific features directly. The reported improvements on three established coherence evaluation tasks are presented as a key result.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Formulation & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of coherence modeling and its importance, grounding the work in existing theories (Centering Theory) and established methods (entity grid). The limitations of the entity grid approach are also well-defined.
2. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on three standard coherence evaluation tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – allowing for a direct comparison with existing methods. The consistent performance gains across these tasks are encouraging.
3. **Established Baselines:** The comparison against well-known baselines, including variations of the entity grid model, provides a necessary benchmark for assessing the proposed approach. The inclusion of the graph-based model (Guinaudeau & Strube, 2013) further strengthens the evaluation.
4. **Code Release:** The authors’ release of their code is a positive step towards reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the application of convolutional neural networks to this problem is a reasonable step, the novelty feels incremental. The core idea of replacing discrete probability calculations with learned embeddings and convolutions doesn't represent a paradigm shift. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this neural approach is fundamentally superior to optimized versions of the entity grid, beyond the reported performance gains.
2. **Limited Feature Engineering Exploration:** The Extended Grid-CNN incorporates only a *subset* of the entity-specific features used in the original Elsner & Charniak (2011) work. The authors state this was to “keep the model simple,” but this raises the question of whether the performance gains are truly attributable to the neural architecture, or simply to a less comprehensive feature set. A more thorough exploration of feature integration is needed.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper lacks ablation studies to dissect the contribution of different components of the proposed architecture. For example, how much of the performance gain comes from the convolutional layers versus the learned embeddings? This makes it difficult to understand *why* the model works.
4. **Performance Gains – Statistical Significance & Practical Impact:** While the reported gains (e.g., 4% on discrimination) are statistically significant, their *practical* impact remains unclear. Are these gains substantial enough to justify the increased complexity of the neural model? A discussion of error analysis and qualitative examples would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors your model makes, compared to the baseline models? Are there specific cases where the neural approach consistently outperforms or underperforms?
2. Why did you choose to limit the number of entity-specific features incorporated into the Extended Grid-CNN? Would including the full set of features from Elsner & Charniak (2011) further improve performance, and at what computational cost?
3. Could you perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of the embedding layer, convolutional layers, and pooling layers to the overall performance?
4. Is the improvement worth the computational cost? Can you provide some numbers on training and inference time for both the Grid-CNN and Extended Grid-CNN compared to the baseline models?

**Additional Comments:**

The reference to "Section 3.1 Incorporating Entity-Specific Features" is confusing. The section number doesn’t align with the actual structure of the paper. Please double-check the section numbering. Also, while the paper cites recent work in neural NLP, it would be helpful to connect this work more explicitly to the broader context of neural discourse modeling.
