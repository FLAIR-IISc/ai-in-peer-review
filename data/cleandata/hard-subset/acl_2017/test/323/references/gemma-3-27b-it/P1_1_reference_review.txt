## Peer Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for assessing text coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely, their reliance on discrete representations and task-decoupled feature engineering – by using distributed representations and end-to-end training. The model learns to represent entity transitions and incorporates entity-specific features.  The approach is evaluated on three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Neuralization of the Entity Grid:** The paper successfully adapts the well-established entity grid approach to a neural framework, enabling the use of continuous representations and potentially capturing longer-range dependencies than previous discrete methods.
2. **Empirical Improvement on Standard Coherence Tasks:**  The proposed model consistently outperforms existing non-neural and neural baselines on three widely used coherence evaluation benchmarks, demonstrating its practical effectiveness.
3. **Demonstration of End-to-End Learning:** The authors showcase the benefits of end-to-end training, allowing the model to learn task-specific features automatically, rather than relying on manually engineered features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The consistent and significant performance improvements across all three evaluation tasks are compelling. The reported gains (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, >4% on summary coherence) are substantial in this field, and establishing state-of-the-art performance is a significant achievement.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models and logically presents the neural approach as a solution.  The rationale for using distributed representations and end-to-end learning is well-explained.
3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The explanation of the neural architecture is clear, aided by the helpful figure.  The description of the evaluation tasks and experimental setup is thorough.
4. **Code Release:**  The release of the code is a valuable contribution, facilitating reproducibility and further research.
5. **Principled Extension of Existing Work:**  The work builds on a solid foundation (entity grid models) rather than proposing a completely novel approach, making it easier to understand and contextualize.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the application of a CNN to the entity grid is a sensible approach, the proposed architecture itself isn't particularly novel.  CNNs are widely used in NLP, and the specific configuration described (convolution, pooling) is fairly standard. The core innovation lies in *applying* this architecture to the coherence problem and demonstrating its effectiveness within the entity grid framework, rather than a breakthrough in neural network design.
2. **Entity-Specific Feature Incorporation is Basic:**  The inclusion of entity-specific features, while beneficial, feels somewhat superficial. The authors acknowledge only using three readily available features.  A more thorough exploration of diverse and potentially more informative entity-specific features would strengthen the work.
3. **Ablation Study is Missing:** A crucial missing element is an ablation study. The paper doesn’t clearly demonstrate the individual contributions of the distributed representations *vs.* the end-to-end training *vs.* the inclusion of entity-specific features.  Which of these changes has the biggest impact?  A more granular analysis would be extremely valuable.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any in-depth discussion of where the model *fails*. Understanding the types of coherence issues the model still struggles with would provide valuable insights for future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Ablation Study:** Can you provide an ablation study demonstrating the individual contribution of distributed representations, end-to-end training, and the inclusion of entity-specific features to the overall performance gains?
2. **Feature Engineering:**  Did you experiment with a broader range of entity-specific features beyond the three reported? If so, what were the results? If not, why did you limit your exploration to these specific features?
3. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide examples of cases where the model makes incorrect predictions, and discuss the potential reasons for these errors? What types of coherence phenomena are particularly challenging for the model?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the window size and the number of filters?
5. **Scalability:** What is the computational complexity of your model, and how does it scale with the length of the input document and the number of entities?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The mention of the Li & Hovy (2014) model and its disappointing results is interesting, but a more detailed explanation of *why* it performed poorly would be helpful.
*   The reference to SIGF V.2 for statistical significance testing is good practice.
*   The authors should clarify whether the WSJ corpus was pre-processed in any way (e.g., tokenization, part-of-speech tagging) before applying the entity grid.
