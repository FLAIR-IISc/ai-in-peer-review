## Review for "A Neural Local Coherence Model"

**Summary:** This paper proposes a neural network architecture for assessing local text coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely their discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation – by leveraging distributed representations and end-to-end training. The model is evaluated on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating), demonstrating state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Neuralization of Entity Grids:** The paper demonstrates a successful application of convolutional neural networks to the entity grid framework, effectively capturing long-range entity transitions. This is a significant step toward integrating neural methods with a well-established symbolic approach to coherence modeling.
2. **Empirical Improvement on Standard Tasks:** The authors achieve consistent and substantial gains across three established coherence evaluation tasks, providing compelling evidence for the effectiveness of their approach. These results are particularly noteworthy given the strong baselines (including the extended entity grid model).
3. **End-to-End Training for Coherence:** The end-to-end training methodology allows the model to learn task-specific features directly from the data, a clear advantage over traditional feature engineering approaches.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently superior performance on all three tasks is the strongest argument for acceptance. The reported gains (4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating) are substantial and demonstrate a clear benefit over existing methods.
2. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models and provides a compelling rationale for exploring neural network-based solutions. The connection to Centering Theory is well-established, and the motivation is sound.
3. **Well-Chosen Baseline Comparisons:**  The authors compare against a comprehensive set of baselines, including the widely-used extended entity grid model. This makes it easier to understand the relative strengths of the proposed approach.
4. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experimental design appears rigorous, with appropriate data splits, hyperparameter tuning (using a development set), and statistical significance testing. The release of code further strengthens the reproducibility of the results.
5. **Effective Integration of Entity-Specific Features:**  The authors demonstrate a simple but effective method for incorporating entity-specific features into their neural model, which further improves performance.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Neural Architecture:** While the application of CNNs to entity grids is effective, the architecture itself (convolutional layers, max pooling) is relatively standard. The novelty lies primarily in the *application* to this specific problem, rather than a groundbreaking new architectural component. A deeper discussion of why this specific architecture was chosen, and exploration of alternatives, would strengthen the work.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from more detailed ablation studies. For example, how much does each component of the neural network (embedding layer, convolutional layers, pooling) contribute to the overall performance? What is the impact of different filter sizes or numbers of filters? Understanding these contributions would provide deeper insight into the model's behavior.
3. **Limited Analysis of Learned Representations:** While the model achieves strong performance, the paper provides little insight into *what* the model is learning. Visualizing the learned embeddings or analyzing the learned convolutional filters could reveal valuable information about the model's understanding of coherence.
4. **Summary Coherence Rating – Small Dataset:** The summary coherence rating task uses a relatively small dataset, making the results potentially less robust. The improvements are smaller than those observed on the sentence ordering tasks.  Further investigation, or evaluation on a larger dataset, would be beneficial.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Architectural Justification:** Why was a convolutional neural network specifically chosen for this task? What other neural architectures were considered, and why were they rejected?
2. **Ablation Study Details:** Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the contribution of each component of the neural network to the overall performance? For example, what is the performance drop when removing the embedding layer or the convolutional layers?
3. **Learned Representations:** Have you performed any analysis of the learned embeddings or convolutional filters? Can you provide examples of what the model has learned to identify as coherent or incoherent patterns?
4. **Error Analysis:**  Can you provide some examples of cases where the model makes incorrect predictions?  What types of texts or coherence phenomena does the model struggle with?
5. **Computational Cost:**  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of your model compared to the existing entity grid models?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper could benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the entity grid framework itself. Are there inherent limitations to modeling coherence based solely on entity transitions?
* The reference to "pairwise input" in Figure 2 is a little unclear. It might be helpful to elaborate on how the input pairs are constructed and used during training.
* The statement about the difficulty of reproducing results with the distributed sentence model (Li & Hovy, 2014) is interesting. More detail on why it failed could be beneficial for the community.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The authors successfully demonstrate the effectiveness of neural networks for enhancing the well-established entity grid framework. While the architecture itself is not radically novel, the application to this problem is effective and the empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the weaknesses raised above through author responses and potential revisions.
