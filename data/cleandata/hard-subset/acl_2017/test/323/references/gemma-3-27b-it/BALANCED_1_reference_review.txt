This paper presents a neural architecture for modeling local coherence, aiming to improve upon traditional entity grid methods. The core idea is to leverage distributed representations and convolutional neural networks to capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion. The authors evaluate their approach on three tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Contributions:**

1. **Neural Entity Grid Representation:** The most significant contribution is the novel application of a convolutional neural network to model the entity grid. This allows for learning distributed representations of entity transitions and potentially capturing longer-range dependencies than traditional discrete representations.
2. **End-to-End Training:** The proposed end-to-end training method, leveraging pairwise ranking, is a valuable contribution. By learning task-specific features directly, the model avoids the limitations of decoupled feature engineering inherent in previous entity grid approaches.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The consistently reported improvements across three different evaluation tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models (discrete representations, task-agnostic feature computation) and effectively motivates the need for a neural approach.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:** The choice of CNNs for modeling entity transitions is well-justified, and the architecture is described in a clear and concise manner. The use of convolutional filters to capture transition patterns and max-pooling for dimensionality reduction is a sensible design.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across three diverse tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) provides a thorough assessment of the model's capabilities and generalizability. The comparison against strong baselines, including the established entity grid extensions, strengthens the results.
4. **Implementation Availability:** Releasing the code is a commendable practice that promotes reproducibility and facilitates future research.
5. **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The inclusion of Figure 2 is helpful for understanding the model architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of CNNs and entity grids is novel, the individual components (CNNs, distributed representations) are not themselves new. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how these components are adapted and specifically tailored to the coherence modeling task.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results presented depend on carefully tuned hyperparameters (Table 2).  The paper lacks discussion of how sensitive the performance is to different hyperparameter settings, and whether any particular parameters proved particularly challenging to optimize.
3. **Limited Analysis of Learned Features:** The paper demonstrates that the model *performs* well, but offers limited insight into *why* it performs well.  Analyzing the learned filters or embedding vectors could reveal valuable information about the types of entity transitions the model prioritizes. What specific patterns are being learned?
4. **Simplification in Entity-Specific Features:** While incorporating entity-specific features is mentioned, the implementation uses a limited subset (3 features) of those proposed by Elsner and Charniak (2011).  The paper doesn't explore the impact of adding more features or the potential benefits of different feature engineering strategies.
5. **Comparison to Stronger Neural Baselines:**  The comparison to Li and Hovy (2014) is briefly mentioned and dismissed due to poor performance. However, the paper does not compare to other more recent and potentially stronger neural coherence models that may have emerged since its writing.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned convolutional filters? Are there specific transition patterns that consistently activate certain filters?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters? Did you perform any ablation studies to assess the importance of different hyperparameters (e.g., embedding size, filter size)?
3. Were any attempts made to incorporate a larger set of entity-specific features as proposed by Elsner and Charniak (2011)? If so, what were the results? If not, why?
4. Given the improvements demonstrated over the existing methods, what are the computational costs associated with the proposed approach compared to the traditional entity grid models?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to Brown coherence toolkit could benefit from a more direct link or citation.
* The discussion of related work is comprehensive, but could be slightly more focused on recent advancements in neural coherence modeling.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and well-executed contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The proposed neural architecture demonstrates significant improvements over existing entity grid methods and achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple evaluation tasks. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and application to this specific problem are effective. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and could be addressed through further analysis and experimentation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions raised above.
