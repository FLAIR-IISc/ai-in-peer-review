## Peer Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

**Summary:**

This paper presents a neural network architecture for assessing local text coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely their reliance on discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation – by employing distributed representations and end-to-end training. The model utilizes convolutional layers to capture long-range entity transitions and incorporates entity-specific features. Evaluation on three standard coherence tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, consistently outperforming non-neural baselines.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Neuralization of Entity Grid Models:** The paper successfully adapts the widely-used entity grid framework to a neural architecture, allowing for continuous representations and the potential to learn more complex patterns of entity transitions. This represents a significant step toward more sophisticated coherence modeling.
2. **End-to-End Learning for Coherence:** By training the model in an end-to-end fashion, the authors demonstrate the ability to learn task-specific features automatically, removing the need for hand-engineered features and potentially improving performance across different applications.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling experimental results showing state-of-the-art performance on three established coherence evaluation tasks, indicating the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models and provides a strong motivation for exploring neural network alternatives. The connection to established theories of coherence (Centering Theory) is well-defined.
2. **Sound Technical Approach:** The use of convolutional neural networks to process the entity grid is a logical and effective choice, allowing the model to capture long-range dependencies without suffering from the curse of dimensionality. The inclusion of entity-specific features is a valuable extension.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation on three different tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – provides a thorough assessment of the model’s capabilities.  Using established datasets and comparing against strong baselines strengthens the validity of the results.
4. **Reproducibility:**  The authors state they have released their code, which is crucial for fostering research and allowing other researchers to build upon their work. This significantly increases the impact of the paper.
5. **Significant Performance Gains:** The reported improvements over existing methods (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and over 4% on summary coherence rating) are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Architecture:** While applying a CNN to the entity grid is a logical step, the architecture itself isn't fundamentally novel. CNNs are widely used in NLP, and the application to this specific task, while effective, doesn't introduce a breakthrough architectural innovation.  The core contribution lies more in the successful *application* and adaptation of existing techniques than in the development of new ones.
2. **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper presents results for a model incorporating entity-specific features, a more detailed ablation study analyzing the contribution of *each* feature individually would be beneficial. It's unclear which features are most impactful and how they interact.
3. **Insufficient Detail on Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions a development set was used for hyperparameter tuning, but doesn’t provide sufficient detail about the search space or the rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters. More transparency in this process would increase confidence in the results.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The WSJ dataset, while standard, is relatively limited in size.  The performance on more diverse and larger datasets could further validate the generalizability of the model.  The summary coherence dataset is particularly small, potentially impacting the reliability of the results on that task.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the ablation studies performed? Specifically, what was the performance impact of incorporating each entity-specific feature independently?
2.  Can you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? What was the search space for each hyperparameter, and what criteria were used to select the final values?
3.  You mention limitations with the summary coherence rating task due to the small dataset size.  Do you have any plans to evaluate your model on larger and more diverse datasets for this task?
4. The paper discusses the benefit of end-to-end training. What is the impact on performance when initializing the embedding layer with pre-trained word embeddings versus random initialization?
5. The paper mentions a failed experiment with the distributed sentence model. Could you provide more details regarding the setup of that experiment and the potential reasons for the poor performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a visual representation of the convolutional filters and what patterns they learn to capture. This would help to illustrate the model's inner workings.
*   The reference list is extensive and well-organized.
*   The writing is clear and concise, making the paper easy to understand.
