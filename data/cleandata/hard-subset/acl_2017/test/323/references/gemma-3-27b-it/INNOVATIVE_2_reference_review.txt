This paper presents a neural network approach to local coherence modeling, building upon the established entity grid framework. The core contribution is the application of convolutional neural networks to learn distributed representations of entity transitions, moving beyond the discrete representations of prior work. A secondary contribution is the end-to-end training approach, allowing the model to learn task-specific features. Finally, the demonstration of consistent improvements across three evaluation tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – represents a practical validation of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of CNNs to Discourse Coherence:** The central idea of using CNNs to model entity transitions within the entity grid framework is a genuinely novel and promising direction. The use of distributed representations and convolutional filters to capture long-range dependencies directly addresses a key limitation of traditional entity grid models. This is not merely incremental improvement, but a conceptually different approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently reports improvements over strong baselines (including the state-of-the-art extended entity grid model) across all three evaluation tasks. The gains, while not massive, are consistent and statistically significant, lending credibility to the proposed approach. The release of code further supports reproducibility and encourages future research.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-articulated, the limitations of existing methods are thoroughly discussed, and the proposed model is described in a detailed and accessible manner. Figures and tables are effectively used to illustrate key concepts and results.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The task of coherence modeling is fundamental to many NLP applications (text generation, summarization, etc.). A more effective coherence model translates directly into improvements in these downstream tasks. The fact that the model can learn task-specific features end-to-end is also a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Learned Representations:** While the paper demonstrates *that* the model works, it provides little insight into *why* it works. A more in-depth analysis of the learned convolutional filters and entity embeddings would be valuable. What kinds of transition patterns are the filters learning? Are the embeddings capturing meaningful semantic relationships?
2. **Dependence on Existing Pipeline (Entity Grid):** The model still relies on the entity grid as a pre-processing step, meaning that errors in coreference resolution or entity typing can propagate through the system. While this is a common practice, the authors do not address the potential impact of these errors on performance. Exploring end-to-end models that directly process raw text (perhaps with attention mechanisms) might be a worthwhile future direction.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The number of hyperparameters involved (embedding size, filter number, window size, pooling length, dropout rate, etc.) raises the possibility that the reported results are sensitive to specific hyperparameter settings. Although the authors performed a hyperparameter search, more discussion about the robustness of the model to different settings would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide some qualitative examples of the convolutional filters learned by your model? What kinds of entity transition patterns do they seem to capture?
2. How sensitive are your results to errors in coreference resolution and entity typing? Have you investigated the impact of using different coreference resolvers or entity taggers?
3. Could you comment on the computational cost of your model compared to the traditional entity grid approach? Is the increase in computational complexity justified by the performance gains?
4. While you pre-train on WSJ, do you see performance changes on other domains?



**Additional Comments:**

The choice of using max-pooling feels somewhat arbitrary. Exploring other pooling strategies (e.g., average pooling) might be worthwhile. A brief discussion of related work in neural discourse modeling (beyond the cited papers) would be helpful to contextualize the contribution more fully.
