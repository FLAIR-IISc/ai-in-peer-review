## Peer Review: A Neural Local Coherence Model

**Summary:**

This paper presents a neural network architecture for assessing local text coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models – discrete representations and task-decoupled feature computation – by using distributed representations of entity transitions and employing an end-to-end training approach.  The model is evaluated on three standard coherence evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) and achieves state-of-the-art results, consistently improving over non-neural baselines.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Successful Neuralization of Entity Grid Models:** The primary contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of applying a neural, specifically convolutional, architecture to the established entity grid framework. This allows for capturing longer-range dependencies and learning more nuanced feature representations than previous discrete methods.
2. **End-to-End Training for Coherence Assessment:**  The paper showcases the benefit of end-to-end training, allowing the model to learn task-specific features automatically, rather than relying on hand-engineered features.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent and statistically significant improvements over existing baselines across multiple evaluation tasks provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing entity grid models and provides a compelling motivation for exploring a neural approach.
2. **Solid Experimental Evaluation:** The thorough evaluation on three standard tasks, with comparisons to multiple baselines, provides convincing evidence of the model’s effectiveness.  The inclusion of statistical significance testing is also a plus.
3. **Well-Explained Architecture:**  The neural network architecture is described clearly and the reasoning behind the design choices (e.g., convolutional layers for transition modeling) is well-articulated. The figures are helpful in understanding the flow of information.
4. **Practical Impact & Code Release:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on commonly used coherence tasks is valuable. The release of the code further enhances the impact of the work by allowing others to build upon it.
5. **Effective Incorporation of Entity-Specific Features:** The method for incorporating entity-specific features, while relatively simple, demonstrates a clear path for enriching the model with external knowledge and achieves improved performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** While the *application* to the entity grid framework is novel, the individual components (embeddings, convolutional layers, max pooling) are standard deep learning building blocks. The architectural novelty is relatively modest.
2. **Ablation Study is Missing:** It would be beneficial to include an ablation study to better understand the contribution of each component of the neural network.  For example, what is the impact of removing the convolutional layers or the entity-specific features?
3. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses heavily on quantitative results.  A brief qualitative error analysis, highlighting examples where the model succeeds or fails, would provide valuable insights into its strengths and weaknesses.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The authors acknowledge that the summary coherence rating task suffers from limited training data. This impacts the robustness of the results on this particular task and could benefit from further investigation.  The reliance on the WSJ corpus for sentence ordering, while standard, also has limitations regarding domain diversity.
5. **Comparison to Recent Neural Coherence Models:** While the paper references Li & Hovy (2014), a more comprehensive comparison to other recent neural coherence models would strengthen the paper. The reason for excluding Li & Hovy’s results (poor performance) isn't fully convincing without further analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Ablation Study:** Could you please provide results for an ablation study, specifically examining the impact of removing the convolutional layers and/or the entity-specific features? This would help to understand the individual contribution of each component.
2.  **Error Analysis:**  Can you provide some examples of cases where the model makes incorrect predictions, along with a brief explanation of why the model might have failed in those instances?
3.  **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost (training time, memory requirements) of your model compared to the baselines?  Is the increased performance worth the additional computational burden?
4. **Li & Hovy Comparison:** Could you elaborate on the setup and potential reasons for the poor performance of the Li & Hovy (2014) model on your dataset? Were any attempts made to optimize their model or investigate the source of the issues?
5.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the hyperparameter settings (e.g., embedding size, filter size, dropout rate)? Did you observe significant performance variations with different configurations?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   It would be helpful to indicate the statistical significance of the improvements in the tables. While mentioned in the text, it’s not immediately apparent from the table itself.
*  The phrase "\Q2012\E" in the references appears to be an artifact.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling. The authors successfully demonstrate the benefits of applying a neural architecture to the entity grid framework, achieving state-of-the-art results on several standard evaluation tasks. While the architectural novelty is relatively modest, the thorough evaluation and strong empirical results justify acceptance.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the addition of an ablation study and error analysis, would further strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
