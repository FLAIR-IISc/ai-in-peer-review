This paper proposes a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the entity grid framework. The primary claimed contribution is the application of neural networks – specifically, convolutional layers – to learn distributed representations of entity transitions and features, allowing for end-to-end training and improved performance on coherence assessment tasks. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of state-of-the-art results on three standard coherence evaluation tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating. A tertiary contribution lies in the release of code for reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently achieves state-of-the-art results across all three evaluation tasks, demonstrating tangible improvements over existing entity grid models. The gains reported, while not massive, are consistent and statistically significant, suggesting a genuine advancement in performance.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional entity grid models – namely, their reliance on discrete representations and task-agnostic feature computation. The proposed neural approach directly addresses these shortcomings, offering a more flexible and learnable framework.
3. **Clear and Organized Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The explanation of the neural architecture and training procedure is relatively straightforward and easy to follow. The related work section provides a concise overview of relevant prior research.
4. **Reproducibility:** The release of code is a significant strength, facilitating verification of the results and enabling further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Neural Architecture:** While the application of convolutional neural networks to the entity grid framework is a reasonable step, the architecture itself is relatively standard. The use of convolutional layers and max-pooling is not particularly innovative within the NLP field, and the paper does not offer a compelling justification for these specific architectural choices beyond their general suitability for the task.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper presents results for a “Grid-CNN” and an “Extended Grid-CNN” model, but lacks a thorough ablation study to determine the individual contribution of each component (e.g., the entity-specific features). It is unclear how much of the performance gain is attributable to the neural network itself versus the inclusion of these features.
3. **Over-Reliance on Existing Datasets and Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation is conducted on established datasets and using standard metrics. While this allows for direct comparison with existing work, it does not explore the potential of the proposed model in more challenging or realistic scenarios. The sensitivity of the model to different data distributions or types of text is not investigated.
4. **Minor Implementation Details and Reporting Issues:** The hyperparameter tuning process isn't fully transparent. The selection of optimal parameters is mentioned but without sufficient detail regarding the search space explored or the criteria used for selection. Furthermore, the notation and clarity surrounding equation (5) could be improved – the ranking objective needs further elaboration.
5. **Questionable Justification of Pre-trained Model Performance:** The improvements shown with pre-trained models on the summary coherence task, while interesting, are presented with little justification. It is unclear why pre-training on the sentence ordering task improves performance on summary coherence.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space explored and the criteria used for selection?
2. Please present a targeted ablation study to evaluate the contribution of the entity-specific features relative to the neural network architecture itself.
3. Why does pre-training on the sentence ordering task improve performance on the summary coherence rating task? Is there a theoretical explanation or specific feature transfer occurring?
4. Could you clarify the practical implications of the "wide convolution" used and whether its use was particularly critical for the success of the model?
5. The text mentions dropout. What dropout rate was used in experiments?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is generally good, but there are a few instances of awkward phrasing and minor grammatical errors.
*   Please provide a clearer explanation of the ranking objective in Equation (5).
*   Consider exploring more sophisticated neural network architectures (e.g., attention mechanisms, recurrent networks) in future work.



**Overall Assessment:**

While this paper presents a solid and well-executed approach to modeling local coherence, the limited novelty of the proposed architecture and the lack of comprehensive ablation studies raise concerns. The strong empirical results are encouraging, but further analysis is needed to fully understand the contributions of each component and to assess the generalizability of the model. I am leaning towards **rejection** due to the incremental nature of the work and the aforementioned shortcomings, despite acknowledging the valuable empirical results and contributions to reproducibility. Addressing the weaknesses and questions raised in this review could significantly strengthen the paper.
