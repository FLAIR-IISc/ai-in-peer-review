This paper presents a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The primary contribution is the application of convolutional neural networks to learn distributed representations of entity transitions and incorporate entity-specific features in an end-to-end fashion, moving beyond the discrete representations and decoupled feature engineering of prior work. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of consistent performance gains across three standard coherence evaluation tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – achieving state-of-the-art results. Finally, the release of code is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of CNNs to Coherence:** The core idea of utilizing convolutional networks to model entity transitions within the entity grid framework is genuinely novel and promising. This allows the model to capture longer-range dependencies than traditional methods limited by the curse of dimensionality, and learn these dependencies directly from data, rather than relying on hand-engineered features.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent and substantial improvements across all three evaluation tasks, establishing a clear advantage over existing entity grid models and other baselines. Achieving state-of-the-art performance is a significant accomplishment. The gains on the insertion task, in particular, suggest a deeper understanding of coherence than simply discriminating coherent from incoherent texts.
3. **End-to-End Learning:** The end-to-end training approach is a key strength.  By learning task-specific features directly, the model avoids the limitations of decoupled feature extraction and has the potential to adapt to different coherence assessment tasks more effectively.
4. **Code Release:**  Providing publicly available code is crucial for reproducibility and facilitating further research in this area. It lowers the barrier to entry and allows the community to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the full model, it lacks a thorough ablation study to dissect the contributions of individual components. For example, how much of the performance gain is attributable to the convolutional layers versus the incorporation of entity-specific features? A more detailed analysis would help to understand the interplay between these components and guide future improvements.
2. **Superficial Discussion of Neural Architecture Choices:** The rationale behind certain architectural choices, like the specific filter sizes and pooling lengths, feels somewhat arbitrary. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of why these parameters were chosen and how they impact performance.
3. **Reliance on Existing Evaluation Tasks:**  The evaluation relies on established tasks (discrimination, insertion, summary coherence rating). While these are standard, they may not fully capture the nuances of coherence in real-world applications like text generation or dialogue systems. Exploring new, more challenging evaluation scenarios could strengthen the paper's claims.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the types of errors the model makes. Understanding *where* the model struggles would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, examining the contribution of the convolutional layers and the entity-specific features separately?
2.  What is the computational cost of the neural model compared to the traditional entity grid model?
3.  You mention pre-training on the WSJ corpus and fine-tuning. Did you explore other pre-training datasets, and did they yield different results?
4.  Could you provide examples of cases where the model fails, and discuss the potential reasons for these failures?



**Additional Comments:** The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The connection to previous work is well established. Despite the weaknesses, the novelty and strong empirical results outweigh the flaws, and I believe this paper represents a significant step forward in the field of coherence modeling. I recommend acceptance.
