This paper proposes a neural network architecture for assessing text coherence, aiming to improve upon traditional entity grid models. The core idea is to represent entity transitions and features in a distributed manner and to learn task-specific features through end-to-end training. The model is evaluated on three tasks: discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating, achieving state-of-the-art results on all.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of a convolutional neural network to model local coherence, moving beyond the discrete representations used in traditional entity grid models. The use of distributed representations allows the model to capture longer-range dependencies and potentially generalize better. A second contribution lies in the end-to-end training approach, which enables the model to learn task-specific features directly from data, rather than relying on hand-engineered features. The empirical results demonstrating improvements across multiple tasks provides evidence of the effectiveness of this approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent and significant improvements over existing entity grid models across all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The gains of up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and 4% on summary coherence rating are substantial and clearly indicate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The claim of achieving state-of-the-art results is well-supported by the experimental findings.
2. **Addressing Limitations of Entity Grids:** The authors identify key limitations of existing entity grid models (discrete representations, task-agnostic feature computation) and directly address these with their neural architecture. The use of distributed representations and end-to-end training are sensible solutions to these problems.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly explained, and the model architecture is described in a detailed and understandable manner, with the help of Figure 2. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly and concisely.
4. **Practical Contribution: Code Release:** Releasing the code alongside the paper is a significant benefit to the research community, allowing others to reproduce the results and build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Neural Network Architecture:** While the application of a convolutional neural network to this specific problem is valuable, the architecture itself is relatively standard. The novelty lies more in the application and adaptation of existing techniques to the domain of text coherence modeling, rather than a fundamentally new neural network design.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, the details are somewhat limited. It would be helpful to provide more insights into the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters and the rationale behind the chosen settings. Specifically, the choices for filter sizes and the number of filters could be elaborated on.
3. **Limited Analysis of Learned Features:** The paper focuses primarily on the performance gains achieved by the model. A more in-depth analysis of the learned features (e.g., visualizing the filters or examining the embedding space) could provide valuable insights into *why* the model works and what types of coherence patterns it is capturing.
4. **Comparison with Recent Neural Coherence Models:** The related work section mentions a neural model by Li and Hovy (2014) but dismisses its results without a detailed explanation. A more thorough comparison with other recent neural coherence models, beyond just mentioning them, would strengthen the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more details regarding the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, how did you determine the optimal filter sizes and the number of filters used in the convolutional layers?
2. Could you share any insights into the learned features? For example, can you visualize some of the convolutional filters or analyze the embedding space to understand what types of coherence patterns the model is capturing?
3. While you state that the Li & Hovy (2014) model did not perform well, could you elaborate on potential reasons for this discrepancy? Were there specific aspects of their approach that were incompatible with the dataset or task?
4. You mention the possibility of training the model specifically on the insertion task. Have you explored this, and if so, what were the results?
