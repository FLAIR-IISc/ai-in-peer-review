This paper presents a neural network-based approach to local coherence modeling, building upon the established entity grid framework. The core contribution lies in replacing the discrete representations and task-decoupled feature engineering of traditional entity grid models with distributed representations learned in an end-to-end fashion using convolutional neural networks. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of improved performance across three standard coherence evaluation tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – achieving state-of-the-art results. Finally, the release of code is a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Performance:** The reported results consistently show improvements over strong baselines, including the widely used extended entity grid model, across all three evaluation tasks. Gains of 4% in discrimination, 2.5% in insertion, and over 4% in summary coherence rating are not trivial and suggest a genuine advance.
2. **Solid Baseline Comparison:** The authors thoughtfully compare their method against a range of established baselines, including the graph-based model and various iterations of the entity grid approach. This thorough comparison strengthens the claim of improvement and provides context for the results.
3. **End-to-End Learning:** The proposed end-to-end training approach is a sensible direction, as it allows the model to learn task-specific features directly from the data, avoiding the limitations of hand-crafted features in previous models. The authors correctly identify the decoupling of feature extraction as a limitation of prior work.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. Figure 2 provides a helpful visual overview of the neural architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the application of convolutional neural networks to the entity grid framework is a reasonable approach, the novelty of this work feels incremental. Convolutional networks are now commonplace in NLP, and simply applying them to an existing framework doesn't constitute a breakthrough. The core idea of using distributed representations is also not entirely new in the context of NLP.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The paper presents results for two versions of the neural model – one without entity-specific features (Grid-CNN) and one with them (Extended Grid-CNN). However, a more thorough ablation study would be valuable. For instance, it would be insightful to investigate the contribution of different components of the network (e.g., the convolutional layers, the pooling layers) individually.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The description of hyperparameter tuning feels somewhat cursory. While the authors mention searching over a range of values, the justification for the specific choices made is not fully explained. The sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters is not adequately addressed.
4. **Data Set Limitations:** While using standard datasets is commendable, the size of the summary coherence rating dataset is relatively small, potentially limiting the statistical power of the results. This makes it harder to confidently assess the significance of the gains.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any qualitative error analysis. Understanding *where* the model succeeds and fails would provide valuable insights into its strengths and weaknesses and suggest directions for future work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the range of values explored for each parameter and the criteria used to select the final values?
2. Can you elaborate on the specific types of errors made by the model on the sentence ordering and summary coherence rating tasks? Providing examples would be helpful.
3.  You mention a failed attempt to incorporate the distributed sentence model by Li and Hovy (2014). Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered when trying to integrate this model?
4.  Given the incremental nature of the proposed approach, what future research directions do you believe hold the greatest promise for significantly advancing the field of coherence modeling?
