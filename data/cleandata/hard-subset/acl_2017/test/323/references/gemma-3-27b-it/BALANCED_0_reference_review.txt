This paper presents a neural network model for assessing text coherence, building upon the entity grid framework. The authors address limitations of traditional entity grid models by utilizing distributed representations and end-to-end training. The core contribution lies in adapting the entity grid approach to a neural architecture that can capture long-range dependencies and learn task-specific features. A secondary contribution is the strong empirical performance across three standard coherence evaluation tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – achieving state-of-the-art results. Finally, the release of the code is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core NLP Problem:** Text coherence is fundamental to natural language understanding and generation. Improving coherence modeling is thus a significant goal. This work directly tackles this problem and proposes a well-motivated approach.
2. **Principled Extension of Existing Work:** The authors build upon the established entity grid framework, a well-regarded approach to coherence modeling. By adapting this framework to a neural architecture, they leverage prior knowledge while overcoming its limitations (discrete representations, task-decoupled features). This is a sensible and effective strategy.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms existing baselines across all three evaluation tasks, and achieves state-of-the-art results. The reported gains (up to 4% on discrimination, 2.5% on insertion, and >4% on summary coherence rating) are meaningful, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, the proposed model, and the experimental setup. The figures and tables are informative and effectively convey the key findings.
5. **Code Release:** Making the code publicly available enhances reproducibility and encourages further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Neural Architecture:** The neural network architecture (convolutional layers, max-pooling) is relatively standard and lacks significant novelty. While the *application* to the entity grid framework is valuable, the architectural contributions themselves are limited. A stronger justification for the architectural choices is needed. Why convolutional layers specifically? Are other architectures less suitable and why?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning Details:** The hyperparameter tuning process is described somewhat briefly. While the authors report the optimal parameters, the search space isn’t clearly defined, and it’s not clear how confident they are that these are truly optimal. More detail on the tuning procedure would be beneficial. What was the impact of different embedding sizes? What effect did dropout have?
3. **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the authors compare the standard CNN-based model with a version incorporating entity-specific features, a more thorough ablation study would be valuable. For instance, how does the performance change when removing the convolutional layers entirely? What impact do different numbers of filters have? This would provide a deeper understanding of the contribution of each component of the model.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The choice of datasets, while standard, feels somewhat dated. WSJ is a good dataset, but more modern text genres (e.g., social media, online forums) might present different coherence challenges and reveal limitations of the model. It is not clear if the model will generalize to diverse forms of text.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing convolutional layers over other neural architectures (e.g., recurrent neural networks, transformers) for modeling entity transitions?
2.  Could you provide more detail about the hyperparameter tuning process, including the specific search space and the criteria used for selecting the optimal parameters?
3.  Do you have any insights into the types of coherence errors that your model still makes? Are there specific types of texts or coherence phenomena where the model struggles?
4.  Have you experimented with incorporating pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) into the entity embedding layer? Would this improve performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   In the introduction, the authors mention the limitations of existing entity grid models. It might be helpful to provide concrete examples of how the proposed model overcomes these limitations.
*   Consider adding a discussion of computational complexity and scalability. How does the training and inference time scale with the length of the document?
*   The abbreviation "E&C" is used without explicit definition. It should be defined the first time it's used in a table or in the main text.



Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of text coherence modeling. The strengths of the work – the principled approach, strong empirical results, and clear presentation – outweigh the weaknesses. Addressing the weaknesses identified above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
