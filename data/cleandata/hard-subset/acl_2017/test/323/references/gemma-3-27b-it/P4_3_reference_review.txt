This paper presents a neural network model for assessing text coherence, aiming to improve upon traditional entity grid-based approaches. The core idea is to leverage distributed representations and convolutional layers to capture long-range entity transitions and learn task-specific features in an end-to-end fashion. The model is evaluated on three tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – and achieves state-of-the-art results. 

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of a neural architecture, specifically convolutional neural networks, to model local coherence within the framework of entity grids. While neural networks have been applied to many NLP tasks, effectively integrating them with established coherence modeling techniques like entity grids represents a notable step. The ability to learn distributed representations of entity transitions, and to incorporate entity-specific features within this framework, is a strong point. A second contribution is the comprehensive empirical evaluation of the model across three standard coherence evaluation tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements over non-neural baselines. The reported gains, while not overwhelmingly large, are statistically significant and suggest the proposed approach is a viable alternative to existing methods. Finally, the release of the code is a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of CNNs to Coherence Modeling:** The use of convolutional neural networks to model entity transitions is a key strength. This allows the model to capture longer-range dependencies compared to traditional entity grid methods, which are limited by the curse of dimensionality when considering longer transition lengths. This is a reasonable and well-motivated approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently outperforms the baseline entity grid models across all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating). The reported improvements are statistically significant, which supports the claim that the proposed neural architecture is more effective at capturing local coherence.
3. **End-to-End Training:** The end-to-end training approach allows the model to learn task-specific features automatically, rather than relying on hand-engineered features. This is a desirable characteristic, as it reduces the need for domain expertise and allows the model to adapt to different tasks more easily.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making it easy to follow the proposed approach. The figures and tables are informative and help to illustrate the key concepts.
5. **Code Release:** The release of the code is a valuable contribution, as it allows other researchers to reproduce the results and build upon the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Neural Network Architecture:** While the *application* to coherence modeling is novel, the core neural network architecture (CNN with embeddings) is fairly standard and doesn’t present any significant architectural innovation. The paper doesn’t delve into detailed ablation studies exploring the specific contributions of different components of the network, such as the convolutional layers or the embedding layer.
2. **Incremental Improvements:** The improvements over the baseline models, while statistically significant, are relatively modest (e.g., 4% absolute gain on discrimination). This raises the question of whether the added complexity of the neural network is justified by the performance gains. More substantial improvements might be expected given the advancements in deep learning.
3. **Entity-Specific Feature Integration:** The integration of entity-specific features seems somewhat simplistic. The authors acknowledge that they only used a subset of the features from Elsner and Charniak (2011) due to computational complexity and ease of implementation. A more comprehensive integration of entity-specific features, perhaps using more sophisticated embedding techniques, could potentially lead to further improvements.
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Neural Coherence Models:** The paper does not compare itself to other recent neural approaches to coherence modeling, such as those based on transformers or recurrent neural networks. A comparison to these more advanced models would provide a better understanding of the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide an ablation study to determine the relative contribution of the convolutional layers, the embedding layer, and the entity-specific features to the overall performance of the model?
2. Why was the pre-trained model from the discrimination task only used for the summary coherence rating task? Were there any issues encountered when attempting to use it for the discrimination or insertion tasks?
3. The paper mentions limitations with including all entity-specific features due to complexity. Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with incorporating more features, and discuss potential strategies for mitigating this cost?
4. How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the filter size and the number of filters in the convolutional layers?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the entity grid representation itself. While the authors address some limitations, they don't discuss potential biases or shortcomings inherent in the grid structure.
*   The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding more recent advances in neural coherence modeling.
* The statement “We were not sure what could go wrong, therefore, we excluded it from our table of results” regarding the Li and Hovy (2014) model is not a strong justification for excluding a relevant baseline. Some attempt at debugging or further analysis would be preferred.
