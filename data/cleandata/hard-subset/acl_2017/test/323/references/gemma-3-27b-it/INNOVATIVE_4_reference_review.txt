This paper presents a neural network-based approach to modeling local coherence, building upon the established entity grid framework. The primary contribution is the application of convolutional neural networks to learn representations of entity transitions, offering a way to capture long-range dependencies and incorporate entity-specific features in an end-to-end trainable manner. A secondary contribution is demonstrating consistent performance improvements across three standard coherence evaluation tasks – discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating – achieving state-of-the-art results. Finally, the release of code is a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of CNNs to Coherence:** The core idea of using CNNs to process entity grid representations is genuinely novel and promising. Existing coherence models, particularly those based on entity grids, often struggle with the curse of dimensionality and feature engineering. This approach elegantly sidesteps these issues by learning representations directly from the data. The potential to capture longer-range dependencies is particularly appealing.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms existing methods across all three evaluation tasks. The reported gains, while not massive, are significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Achieving state-of-the-art results lends credibility to the methodology.
3. **End-to-End Trainability:** The end-to-end training paradigm is a key advantage. By learning features directly from the task, the model can adapt to the specific nuances of coherence assessment, potentially leading to more robust and generalizable performance.
4. **Code Release:** The availability of code promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area. This is a valuable contribution to the community.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The figures and tables are helpful in understanding the model architecture and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Overall Framework:** While the *application* of CNNs is novel, the underlying framework remains rooted in the entity grid model, which itself is a well-established approach. The paper doesn't fundamentally challenge the core assumptions of the entity grid framework; it refines it. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but it limits the broader impact of the work.
2. **Ablation Study Insufficient:** The paper mentions incorporating entity-specific features but doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to demonstrate the *independent* contribution of these features. It's unclear how much of the performance gain is attributable to the CNN architecture versus the incorporation of entity-specific information.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While hyperparameter tuning is mentioned, the report of optimal values in Table 2 feels a bit disconnected from the main text. A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters would be valuable. Were certain parameters particularly critical?
4. **Limited Analysis of Errors:** The paper presents quantitative results, but lacks qualitative analysis. Examining examples where the model fails could provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.
5. **Task Specificity:** The evaluation tasks, while standard, are somewhat artificial. It would be beneficial to demonstrate the model's performance on a more realistic downstream application, such as text generation or summarization.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to isolate the impact of the CNN architecture and the incorporation of entity-specific features? Specifically, how does the performance of the Grid-CNN model compare to the Extended Grid model *without* the CNN layers?
2. Could you provide examples of texts where the model makes incorrect coherence judgments, and discuss the potential reasons for these errors?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the filter size and the number of filters?
4. Do you foresee any challenges in applying this model to languages with richer morphology or more complex coreference patterns?
5. What are your plans for exploring more realistic downstream applications of this model, such as text generation or summarization?
