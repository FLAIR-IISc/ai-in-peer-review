This paper presents a neural network architecture for modeling local coherence, building upon the entity grid framework. The primary contribution is the application of convolutional neural networks to learn representations of entity transitions, moving beyond discrete probabilities and enabling the incorporation of entity-specific features in an end-to-end trainable manner. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of improvements over existing entity grid models across three standard coherence evaluation tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of using CNNs to represent and learn from entity grid transitions is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from traditional, feature-engineered approaches. This is a bold move that has the potential to unlock more nuanced understanding of coherence.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent improvements across all three evaluation tasks (discrimination, insertion, and summary coherence rating) are compelling. Achieving state-of-the-art results, even with modest gains, validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The gains, particularly on the summary coherence rating task, are noteworthy.
3. **End-to-End Learning:** The end-to-end training paradigm is a clear advantage. Decoupling feature engineering from task learning allows the model to adapt and learn features specifically relevant to the evaluation task, leading to better performance.
4. **Code Release:** The release of the code is commendable and facilitates reproducibility and further research. It encourages the community to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper demonstrates improvements with the extended grid-CNN (incorporating entity features), a more detailed ablation study is needed. Specifically, it would be beneficial to understand the relative contribution of the CNN architecture *versus* the entity-specific features. Are the gains primarily driven by the neural network’s ability to learn better representations or by the inclusion of more informative features?
2. **Task-Specific Fine-tuning:** The results suggest that fine-tuning from pre-trained models (on the discrimination task) significantly improves performance on the summary coherence rating task. This raises questions about the generalizability of the model. Does the model truly learn a universal representation of coherence, or is it simply adapting to the nuances of each specific task?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but doesn’t provide substantial detail. It's unclear how sensitive the performance is to the choice of hyperparameters, and a more thorough analysis of this aspect would be valuable.
4. **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical justification for *why* CNNs are well-suited for modeling entity grid transitions. A more insightful discussion of the connection between the CNN architecture and the underlying principles of coherence would strengthen the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, isolating the contributions of the CNN architecture and the entity-specific features?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters? Can you provide a sensitivity analysis, showing the impact of different hyperparameter settings on performance?
3. Can you elaborate on the limitations of your approach? Are there specific types of texts or coherence phenomena where the model is likely to struggle?
4.  You mention the possibility of training directly on the insertion task. Have you explored this, and if so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

* The related work section is comprehensive and well-organized.
* The writing is generally clear and concise.
* Consider adding a visualization of the learned convolutional filters to provide more insight into what the model is learning.



**Overall Assessment:** Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong submission with a novel and promising approach. The strong empirical results and the release of the code make this a valuable contribution to the field of coherence modeling. I lean towards acceptance, as the novelty and potential impact outweigh the limitations.
