**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors refine the prerequisite skills for RC, which cover the textbase and situation model, and include knowledge reasoning. They also leverage a wide range of linguistic features to evaluate readability. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and analyze the results. The study demonstrates that there is only a weak correlation between the difficulty of questions and the readability of context texts in RC datasets.

**Contributions of the Work**

1. The authors adopt two classes of evaluation metrics to analyze RC datasets, which provides a more comprehensive understanding of the datasets' characteristics.
2. The authors refine the prerequisite skills for RC, which includes knowledge reasoning, and provide a more detailed classification of skills required for RC tasks.
3. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics, which provides a valuable guide for the construction of new datasets and the development of RC systems.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the RC datasets, which highlights the characteristics of each dataset and provides insights into the development of RC systems.
2. The authors propose a comprehensive classification of prerequisite skills for RC, which can be used to evaluate the performance of RC systems.
3. The paper demonstrates the importance of considering both prerequisite skills and readability when evaluating RC datasets.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The annotation process relies on human annotators, which may introduce subjectivity and variability in the results.
2. The authors only analyze six RC datasets, which may not be representative of all RC datasets.
3. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed metrics can be used to improve the development of RC systems.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How can the proposed metrics be used to improve the development of RC systems, and what are the potential benefits of using these metrics?
2. How can the subjectivity and variability in the annotation process be addressed, and what measures can be taken to ensure the reliability of the results?
3. Can the proposed metrics be applied to other natural language processing tasks, and what are the potential applications of these metrics in other areas?
