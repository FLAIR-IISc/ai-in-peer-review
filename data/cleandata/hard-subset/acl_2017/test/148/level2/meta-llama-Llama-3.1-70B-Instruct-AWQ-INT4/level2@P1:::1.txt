**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a study on evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors refine the prerequisite skills for RC and define evaluation metrics for each class. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and analyze the results. The study highlights the characteristics of the datasets and provides insights for the construction of new datasets and the development of RC systems.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. The authors adopt two classes of evaluation metrics to analyze the quality of RC datasets, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the datasets' characteristics.
2. They refine the prerequisite skills for RC, organizing knowledge reasoning in terms of entailment phenomena and human text comprehension in psychology.
3. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with the refined metrics, making the results publicly available for future research.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the RC datasets, highlighting their strengths and weaknesses.
2. The authors' refinement of the prerequisite skills for RC is a significant contribution to the field, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the skills required for RC.
3. The study's findings on the correlation between readability and question difficulty are insightful and have implications for the development of RC systems.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The paper's focus on two specific classes of metrics (prerequisite skills and readability) might limit the generalizability of the findings to other aspects of RC.
2. The annotation process, although thorough, might be subjective and dependent on the annotators' expertise.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the findings for the development of RC systems and the construction of new datasets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the subjectivity of the annotation process in future studies?
2. Can the authors provide more insights on how the refined prerequisite skills for RC can be used to improve the development of RC systems?
3. How do the authors think the findings on the correlation between readability and question difficulty can be applied to other areas of natural language processing?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their methodology and findings. The study's contributions to the field of RC are significant, and the authors' refinement of the prerequisite skills for RC is a valuable addition to the existing literature. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the findings and the limitations of the study.
