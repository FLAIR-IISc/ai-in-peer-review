- Investigates evaluation of RC datasets beyond accuracy by applying prerequisite skills and readability metrics to six popular datasets.  
- Demonstrates a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, suggesting hard RC tasks can arise from easily readable text (e.g., MCTest).  
- Proposes a revised prerequisite-skill taxonomy grounded in textual entailment and human comprehension models, offering stronger theoretical footing.  
- Releases annotated datasets publicly, providing a useful resource for the RC community.  
- Strength: comprehensive use of quantitative readability metrics combined with a refined skill-based annotation scheme.  
- Strength: high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) and clear, well-organized methodology and results.  
- Strength: authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., inability to evaluate sentence selection competence), increasing credibility.  
- Weakness: analysis is largely descriptive and does not investigate why readability–difficulty correlation is weak or how to exploit the finding.  
- Weakness: human annotation is subjective and may not reflect machine difficulties; interactions and nuances within skill categories are underexplored.  
- Weakness: sentence-selection distance metric is rudimentary and needs more sophisticated contextual dependency analysis.  
- Question: What strategies could deliberately create RC datasets with high difficulty despite low readability?  
- Question: How were “nonsense” questions defined and how do they affect results (notably for MS MARCO)?  
- Question: Will the authors release the annotation guidelines to ensure reproducibility and wider use?  
- Additional comment: Flesch–Kincaid grade level has limitations as a sole readability measure; consider alternative metrics.  
- Additional comment: include a small table of example questions with assigned skills to illustrate annotation granularity.
