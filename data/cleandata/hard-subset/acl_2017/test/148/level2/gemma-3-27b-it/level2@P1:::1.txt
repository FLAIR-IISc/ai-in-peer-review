This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors adopt two classes of metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – and apply them to six existing datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA). They demonstrate a weak correlation between these two factors, suggesting that creating easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer datasets is possible. The main contributions are: (1) adoption and application of the two metric classes to analyze RC datasets, (2) revision of the prerequisite skills classification based on textual entailment and psychological models of comprehension, and (3) public release of the annotated datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant and Important Problem:** Understanding the characteristics of RC datasets is crucial for both evaluating system performance and designing better datasets. The paper addresses a significant gap in the field by moving beyond simple accuracy and exploring more nuanced metrics. The observation that readability doesn't necessarily correlate with question difficulty is a valuable insight.
2. **Comprehensive Metric Selection:** The authors' choice to leverage both prerequisite skills and readability metrics provides a more holistic assessment of dataset quality than relying on single metrics. The grounding of these metrics in prior work from both NLP (textual entailment) and psychology (human comprehension models) strengthens their justification. The refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy is particularly notable.
3. **Large-Scale Annotation & Public Release:** Annotating six datasets with the proposed metrics is a substantial effort, and making the annotated data publicly available is a significant contribution that will benefit the RC research community. This allows for replication, validation, and further analysis by other researchers.
4. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The results are presented clearly in tables and figures, allowing for easy comparison across datasets. The discussion of the findings is thoughtful and provides useful interpretations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Annotation Methodology Limitations:** The primary weakness lies in the inherent limitations of the annotation process. The paper acknowledges that the methodology cannot evaluate the competence of *selecting* the necessary sentences for answering questions. This is a crucial skill in RC, and its exclusion limits the scope of the analysis. The annotation focuses on *understanding* the relevant context once it is provided, rather than the entire RC process.
2. **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** While inter-annotator agreement is reported (90.1%), the nature of the prerequisite skills themselves is somewhat subjective. It's unclear how disagreements were resolved and whether the annotated skill assignments perfectly capture the cognitive processes required to answer the questions. Providing more detail about the annotation guidelines and examples would be beneficial.
3. **Weak Correlation is Not Fully Explained:** While the paper highlights the weak correlation between readability and difficulty, it doesn’t deeply investigate *why* this is the case. More analysis on the types of questions that are difficult despite being paired with readable text could yield valuable insights. For example, are these questions more reliant on commonsense reasoning or complex inference?
4. **Limited Discussion of Dataset Biases:** The analysis touches on the sourcing methods of some datasets (e.g., automated curation in MS MARCO leading to nonsense questions), but a more thorough discussion of potential dataset biases (e.g., topic distributions, question types) would strengthen the analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the annotation guidelines provided to the annotators, perhaps with a few example questions and their corresponding skill annotations?
2. How were disagreements between annotators resolved during the annotation process?
3. Beyond reporting the correlation coefficients, could you provide more qualitative examples of questions that are difficult despite having high readability scores, and discuss the specific skills required to answer them?
4. How might the findings of this study inform the development of new RC datasets or the improvement of existing ones, beyond the general recommendation of using a broader range of metrics?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is quite extensive and well-organized.
*   The use of Table 6 to detail sentence selection distances is a good addition to the results.
*   Minor grammatical edits throughout the paper could further improve readability (e.g., phrasing in the introduction and conclusion).
*   Consider explicitly mentioning the potential for cultural biases in the datasets, as text comprehension can be influenced by background knowledge.



Overall, this is a solid paper that addresses an important problem in the RC field. The contributions are valuable, and the public release of the annotated datasets is a major asset. While the limitations of the annotation methodology are a concern, the insights gained from the analysis are nonetheless significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
