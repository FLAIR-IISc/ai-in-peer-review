This paper presents an analysis of six reading comprehension datasets through the lens of prerequisite skills and readability metrics. The core idea—moving beyond simple accuracy as the sole evaluation metric—is valuable. The work claims three main contributions: adopting two classes of metrics to characterize RC datasets, revising a previous classification of prerequisite skills, and providing a publicly available annotation of six datasets.

Strengths:

1. **Addressing a Crucial Need:** The paper rightly points out the limitations of relying solely on accuracy for evaluating RC datasets and systems. A more nuanced understanding of dataset characteristics is essential for progress in the field. The investigation of prerequisite skills and readability offers a useful, albeit preliminary, step toward this goal.
2. **Methodological Rigor:** The adoption of established theories from psychology (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) to inform the definition of prerequisite skills is a strong point. This grounding in cognitive science adds credibility to the analysis. The attempt to refine the existing skill categorization, incorporating insights from textual entailment, is a sensible approach.
3. **Publicly Available Resource:** The annotation of six datasets with the proposed metrics is a valuable contribution to the community. This resource allows other researchers to replicate the analysis, validate the findings, and potentially use the annotations for building and evaluating their own systems. The detailed description of the annotation procedure is helpful for reproducibility.
4. **Insightful Observations:** The analysis reveals interesting characteristics of the datasets. For example, the finding that readability doesn't strongly correlate with question difficulty, and the identification of MCTest as an example of a relatively easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer dataset, are noteworthy observations.

Weaknesses:

1. **Weak Correlation Findings, Limited Impact:** While the paper highlights a weak correlation between readability and difficulty, this finding feels somewhat unsurprising. The paper doesn’t fully explore *why* this correlation is weak, or what implications this has for dataset design. Simply stating the correlation is weak lacks depth.
2. **Annotation Subjectivity & Limited Inter-Annotator Agreement Details:** The inter-annotator agreement is reported as 90.1%, which sounds good. However, the paper lacks detail on *how* this agreement was calculated (e.g., Cohen’s Kappa) and whether disagreements were resolved. Without this, it's difficult to assess the reliability of the annotations.
3. **Methodological Limitation: Sentence Selection:** The paper acknowledges that its methodology doesn’t evaluate the competence of *selecting* the necessary sentences to answer a question, only the understanding of the selected content. This is a significant limitation, as sentence selection is a core component of reading comprehension.
4. **Lack of System-Level Evaluation:** The analysis primarily focuses on dataset characteristics. It would be stronger if the authors had investigated how the proposed metrics correlate with the performance of existing RC systems on these datasets. This would demonstrate the practical utility of the metrics.



Questions to Authors:

1.  The paper mentions using a filtering criterion for NewsQA (is answer absent = 0, is question bad = 0, validated answers do not include bad question or none). Could you provide more details on the origin of these annotations and how they were obtained?
2.  Regarding inter-annotator agreement, could you please specify the metric used (e.g., Cohen’s Kappa) and describe the process for resolving disagreements between annotators?
3.  How might the proposed metrics be adapted to evaluate the sentence selection capabilities of RC systems, given the acknowledged limitation?
4.  Have you explored whether the identified prerequisite skills can be used to predict system performance on these datasets (e.g., using the skill distribution as features in a regression model)?

Additional Comments:
The organization is generally clear, but the related work section could be more focused on work directly related to dataset evaluation. The discussion section feels somewhat speculative; grounding the claims with more empirical evidence would be beneficial.
