This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors adopt two classes of metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – and apply them to six existing datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA). They demonstrate a weak correlation between these two factors, suggesting that creating easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer datasets is possible. The main contributions are: (1) Adoption of the two metric classes for RC dataset evaluation and demonstration of a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability; (2) Revision of the prerequisite skills classification, grounding it in textual entailment and psychological models of human comprehension; and (3) Annotation of six datasets with the defined metrics and public release of the annotated data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant and Important Problem:** The analysis of RC dataset quality is crucial for driving progress in NLU.  Simply reporting accuracy on datasets without understanding their characteristics can be misleading. This work directly addresses this issue by attempting to quantify different aspects of dataset difficulty.
2. **Comprehensive Metric Selection:** The authors’ choice to adopt both prerequisite skills and readability metrics provides a more holistic view of dataset quality than relying on a single metric.  The grounding of the skills classification in established work in textual entailment and psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) is a strength.
3. **Dataset Annotation and Public Release:**  The annotation of six widely used datasets is a significant contribution.  Making this annotated data publicly available will allow other researchers to leverage it for further analysis and potentially use it to filter or augment existing datasets, or to develop more robust evaluation protocols.  The detailed explanation of the annotation procedure, including the filtering criteria for NewsQA, enhances the value of this resource.
4. **Insightful Findings:**  The finding of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is interesting and counterintuitive.  The example of MCTest, being easy to read but challenging to answer, illustrates this point effectively. This suggests current datasets might be biased and highlights the need for datasets explicitly designed to test specific reasoning skills.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** The annotation of prerequisite skills is inherently subjective. While the paper mentions inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), it lacks details on *where* disagreements occurred and how they were resolved. A more granular analysis of agreement per skill category would be valuable.  It is also not clear to what extent annotator background or expertise influenced the annotation process.
2. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:**  The reliance on Vajjala and Meurers’ (2012) features for readability is reasonable, but potentially limiting. These metrics focus primarily on syntactic and lexical complexity.  Other factors, such as coherence, discourse structure, and background knowledge requirements, could also contribute to readability and are not captured by these features.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper demonstrates a correlation between the metrics, but does not attempt to establish a causal relationship. The authors acknowledge this, but the discussion would benefit from more speculation on the underlying reasons for the observed weak correlation. For example, is the weak correlation due to flaws in the metrics themselves, or does it reflect fundamental limitations in how RC datasets are currently constructed?
4. **Methodological Limitations – Sentence Selection:** As noted in the paper itself, the annotation focuses on identifying sentences *needed* to answer the question. This doesn’t evaluate a system's ability to *find* those sentences. A more complete evaluation would include metrics for information retrieval precision/recall as part of the annotation process.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the inter-annotator agreement, specifying which prerequisite skills showed the highest and lowest agreement? What steps were taken to resolve disagreements?
2.  The paper mentions that the annotation methodology could not evaluate "the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read." Do you plan to explore methodologies that *do* address this aspect of reading comprehension in future work?
3.  Regarding the observed weak correlation between readability and difficulty, do you have any hypotheses about why this might be the case beyond the points briefly discussed in the paper?  Are there specific characteristics of the datasets that might contribute to this finding?
4. Could you elaborate on how you filtered the questions in NewsQA? What specific criteria were used to identify and exclude "bad" questions?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The table and figure numbers seem slightly inconsistent throughout the paper.
*  The reference list could be formatted more consistently.
*  Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the study and potential avenues for future research in the conclusion.
