## Review: Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability

This paper presents a valuable investigation into evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets beyond simple accuracy. The authors address the critical need for more nuanced metrics by adopting and refining two classes – prerequisite skills and readability – and applying them to six popular RC datasets (MCTest, SQuAD, etc.). The core contribution lies in demonstrating a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, suggesting the possibility of constructing challenging RC tasks from easily readable text, exemplified by MCTest.  A second contribution is a revised categorization of prerequisite skills, grounded in textual entailment and psychological models of human comprehension, offering a more theoretically sound framework than previous work. Finally, the publicly released annotated datasets are a significant resource for the RC community.

The strengths of this work are numerous. The identification of a disconnect between readability and difficulty is insightful and has important implications for dataset creation and task design. The thorough application of both quantitative readability metrics and a refined skill-based annotation scheme provides a comprehensive analysis. The inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% indicates a reasonable level of consistency in the skill annotation process. The paper is well-motivated, clearly explains the methodology, and presents results in an organized manner. The acknowledgement of limitations, such as the inability to evaluate sentence selection competence, strengthens the work’s credibility.

However, the paper does have weaknesses. The analysis, while insightful, is largely descriptive. While a weak correlation is demonstrated, the paper doesn't delve into *why* this correlation is weak or propose specific strategies to leverage this finding for improving RC systems.  The annotation procedure, while sound, relies on human annotation, which is inherently subjective and may not perfectly reflect the challenges faced by machines. The analysis doesn’t extensively explore the nuances within each skill category or how different skills interact to contribute to difficulty. Furthermore, the distance metric for sentence selection feels somewhat rudimentary and could benefit from a more sophisticated analysis of contextual dependencies.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Beyond acknowledging the weak correlation, what potential strategies could be employed to deliberately create RC datasets with high difficulty despite low readability scores? 
2.  Could you elaborate on the criteria used to determine “nonsense” questions and how this impacts the observed results, particularly for datasets like MS MARCO?
3.  Do you plan to release the annotation guidelines used for assigning prerequisite skills to ensure reproducibility and facilitate further research using this annotation scheme?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of using Flesch-Kincaid grade level as a sole measure of readability. Consider mentioning alternative readability metrics. Also, a small table showing example questions with their assigned skills would be helpful for readers to grasp the granularity of the annotation.
