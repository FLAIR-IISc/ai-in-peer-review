

This paper proposes two complementary classes of metrics for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors refine and apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA), analyzing their characteristics and the relationship between these two dimensions. The paper makes several contributions: a refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC, application of these metrics to multiple datasets, and empirical demonstration of the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a significant gap in RC research by moving beyond superficial metrics (e.g., question types) to provide a more nuanced evaluation framework. The authors correctly identify that current RC datasets lack meaningful quality metrics that could guide system development.

2. The refinement of prerequisite skills represents a meaningful contribution. By organizing knowledge reasoning skills in terms of textual entailment and human text comprehension, the authors provide a theoretically grounded classification that improves upon previous work. The detailed description of the 13 skills (particularly the distinction between bridging and elaboration inferences) demonstrates thoughtful theoretical grounding.

3. The empirical analysis reveals a valuable insight: readability of context does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty. The authors provide compelling evidence through their annotation results and statistical analysis (Table 5, Figures 3-4), showing that datasets like MCTest demonstrate high question difficulty despite low readability. This finding has important implications for dataset construction.

4. The paper's methodological approach is rigorous. The inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) provides confidence in the annotation quality, and the authors transparently document their sampling methodology and limitations.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not sufficiently justify why their specific classification of 13 prerequisite skills is comprehensive or superior to alternative classifications. While they reference prior work, they don't provide evidence that their classification captures all relevant skills or that their modifications address specific shortcomings in previous approaches.

2. The annotation procedure has several ambiguities that could affect the reliability of the results. The paper identifies "nonsense" questions as a category but doesn't clearly define what constitutes a "nonsense" question in RC. This ambiguity affects the interpretation of results, particularly for datasets like MS MARCO that showed high nonsense rates.

3. The paper's claim that "the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is overstated. The paper demonstrates a weak correlation (Pearson coefficients from 0.025 to 0.416), but the authors don't sufficiently analyze why this correlation is weak or what factors might actually influence question difficulty beyond the metrics they propose.

4. The paper does not adequately address how the proposed metrics could be practically applied to improve RC systems. While the authors briefly mention "curriculum learning" in the discussion, they don't provide concrete examples of how these metrics could guide system development or dataset construction.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper states that the classification of prerequisite skills was revised "in terms of textual entailment and human text comprehension." What specific evidence from these domains led to the specific modifications to the original classification?

2. How should "nonsense" questions be defined in RC, and what criteria could be used to distinguish them from questions that require unusual skills or knowledge?

3. The paper mentions that "our methodology was used to evaluate the understanding of contextual entailments in a broader sense for RC." How might this limitation be addressed to evaluate the full range of RC skills, including the competence of selecting necessary sentences from context?

4. The authors state that "it is possible to create an RC dataset that is easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer." What specific dataset construction techniques could be used to achieve this, based on the insights from their analysis?
