

Summary and Contributions: This paper proposes two complementary classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills (measuring cognitive demands of answering questions) and readability (measuring linguistic complexity of text). The primary contribution is a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills, incorporating insights from textual entailment research and psychology, which the authors apply to six RC datasets. The secondary contribution is the empirical analysis demonstrating only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability, showing that it's possible to create datasets that are easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer (e.g., MCTest). The tertiary contribution is the publicly available annotation of six RC datasets using their metrics.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a critical gap in RC research: most datasets are evaluated with superficial metrics (question types, answer types), while the quality of the underlying text and question difficulty is not systematically assessed. The authors provide a much-needed framework for more nuanced dataset evaluation that goes beyond simple accuracy.

2. The refinement of prerequisite skills taxonomy is well-justified and theoretically grounded. The authors thoughtfully build on prior work while incorporating insights from textual entailment research and psychology, creating a more comprehensive and theoretically sound classification than previous approaches.

3. The empirical analysis is thorough and well-executed. The authors annotate six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA), analyze the results systematically, and draw meaningful conclusions about the relationship between readability and question difficulty. The finding that MCTest is easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer is particularly insightful and challenges common assumptions about RC datasets.

4. The paper has clear practical implications for RC system development. The authors suggest a curriculum learning approach to RC development based on their metrics, which could guide the step-by-step development of more robust systems by progressing from easy-to-read/easy-to-answer datasets to more challenging ones.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not sufficiently address how these metrics could be integrated into system evaluation. While the authors discuss using the metrics for dataset construction, they don't explain how a system's performance could be evaluated against these metrics (e.g., which metrics are most important for a given system). This limits the practical utility of the framework.

2. The annotation procedure lacks sufficient detail on how disagreements between annotators were resolved. With an inter-annotator agreement of 90.1%, it's unclear how the remaining 9.9% was handled, which affects the reliability of the results. The authors should clarify their disagreement resolution process.

3. The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. They acknowledge their methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting necessary sentences" but don't discuss how this limitation might affect their findings or what alternative approaches might be needed to address this gap.

4. The claim that "it is possible to create an RC dataset that is easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer" is supported by the MCTest example, but the paper doesn't provide a clear methodology for how to create such datasets, which limits the practical utility of this finding for dataset creators.

5. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how their metrics might be applied to new datasets or extended to other NLP tasks beyond RC, which would strengthen the generalizability of their contribution.

Questions to Authors:
1. How could your metrics be integrated into the evaluation of RC systems, rather than just the evaluation of datasets? What specific metrics would be most valuable for system developers?

2. The paper identifies MCTest as an example of an easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer dataset. What specific design choices in MCTest contributed to this characteristic, and how could dataset creators systematically incorporate these choices into new datasets?

3. How might your metrics be extended to handle datasets with different text genres or question types, such as those involving more complex discourse structures or specialized domains?

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the RC community by providing a more nuanced framework for evaluating datasets. The refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills and the empirical analysis of the readability-question difficulty relationship are particularly strong. With some additional clarification on the practical implementation of the metrics and their limitations, this work could have significant impact on both dataset construction and system development for reading comprehension.
