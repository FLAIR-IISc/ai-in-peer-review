

Summary: This paper proposes two classes of metrics for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills (which measure the cognitive demands of answering questions) and readability (which measures the linguistic complexity of the text). The authors refine a prior classification of prerequisite skills, apply both metrics to six existing RC datasets, and analyze the relationship between them. The main contributions are: (1) a refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC grounded in psychological theory, (2) an empirical analysis showing weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, and (3) publicly available annotations of six RC datasets.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a critical gap in RC research - the lack of robust metrics for evaluating dataset quality beyond simple accuracy. The authors' focus on "prerequisite skills" as a measure of question difficulty is well-motivated and grounded in psychological theories of text comprehension, providing a more nuanced understanding of RC challenges than current superficial metrics.

2. The refined classification of prerequisite skills (particularly the reorganization of knowledge reasoning into bridging/elaboration) is a significant improvement over prior work. The authors thoughtfully incorporate insights from textual entailment research and psychological theories of human inference, creating a more comprehensive and theoretically sound framework.

3. The empirical analysis across six datasets provides valuable insights about the relationship between readability and question difficulty. The finding that MCTest (easy to read, difficult to answer) exists demonstrates that these two dimensions are separable - a crucial observation for dataset construction and system development.

4. The paper's methodology is clear and well-executed. The annotation procedure was carefully designed with inter-annotator agreement checks, and the analysis of readability metrics is appropriate for the task.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper overstates its main finding by claiming "readability does not directly affect question difficulty" based on weak correlations (0.025-0.416). These correlations, while not strong, are not zero, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explore what "directly affect" means in this context. The evidence doesn't support the strong claim made in the abstract and conclusion.

2. The methodology has a critical limitation: it doesn't evaluate the competence of selecting relevant sentences to answer questions, which is a core component of RC. As acknowledged in Section 6, the authors explicitly state their method "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read," yet this is a fundamental aspect of RC that the metrics are supposed to address.

3. The exclusion of several important datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) without thorough justification weakens the analysis. The paper dismisses CNN/Daily Mail as "anonymized and contains some errors" - but this is a common characteristic of many datasets and doesn't inherently make it unsuitable for analysis. The exclusion of LAMBADA due to "text formatting" is questionable given its prominence in RC research.

4. The paper lacks sufficient discussion about how the metrics could practically guide RC system development. While they mention curriculum learning, they don't provide concrete examples of how to use the metrics to design better training regimes or identify specific weaknesses in current systems.

5. The annotation of "nonsense" questions is problematic without a clear definition of what constitutes a solvable question. The authors note disagreements about this, but don't provide a principled way to resolve these disagreements, potentially affecting the reliability of their annotations.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would you define a "solvable" RC question in the context of your metrics? What specific criteria would you use to distinguish solvable questions from nonsense questions?

2. Given the limitation in evaluating sentence selection competence, how could your metrics be extended to incorporate this aspect of RC?

3. Could you provide more details on why CNN/Daily Mail was deemed unsuitable for annotation, especially given its prominence in RC research? How might including this dataset change your conclusions?
