- Summary: Proposes two RC dataset metrics—prerequisite skills and readability—refines a skills taxonomy, applies both to six datasets, finds weak correlation between readability and difficulty, and releases annotations.

- Contribution: Refines prerequisite-skills classification for RC grounded in psychological theory.

- Contribution: Empirical analysis showing weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

- Contribution: Public release of annotations for six RC datasets.

- Strength: Addresses gap in RC evaluation by proposing cognitively grounded "prerequisite skills" beyond accuracy.

- Strength: Improved taxonomy (reorganizing knowledge reasoning into bridging/elaboration) informed by entailment and psychological theories.

- Strength: Cross-dataset empirical analysis demonstrates separability of readability and difficulty (e.g., MCTest).

- Strength: Clear, well-executed methodology with careful annotation procedures and inter-annotator agreement checks.

- Weakness: Overstates claim that "readability does not directly affect question difficulty" despite nonzero weak correlations and lack of clarity on "directly affect."

- Weakness: Fails to evaluate sentence-selection competence, a core RC component the metrics aim to address.

- Weakness: Excludes prominent datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) without sufficient justification, weakening the analysis.

- Weakness: Lacks concrete guidance on how metrics would practically inform RC system development or curriculum design.

- Weakness: Annotation of "nonsense" questions is problematic due to no principled definition of solvability and annotator disagreement.

- Question: How do you define a "solvable" RC question and what criteria distinguish solvable from nonsense questions?

- Question: How could the proposed metrics be extended to evaluate the sentence-selection competence aspect of RC?

- Question: Why was CNN/Daily Mail deemed unsuitable for annotation, and how might including it affect your conclusions?
