Strengths:
- Proposes a nuanced evaluation framework for RC datasets that goes beyond superficial metrics like question types.
- Refines prerequisite skills into 13 theoretically grounded categories, distinguishing entailment and comprehension (e.g., bridging vs elaboration).
- Empirically demonstrates weak correlation between context readability and question difficulty, with examples like MCTest being hard despite low readability.
- Uses a rigorous methodology with high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) and transparent sampling and limitations.

Weaknesses:
- Does not sufficiently justify that the 13-skill classification is comprehensive or superior to alternative taxonomies.
- Annotation procedure is ambiguous, especially the unclear definition of "nonsense" questions, harming result reliability (notably for MS MARCO).
- Overstates the claim that readability does not affect difficulty without deeper analysis of factors behind the weak correlations observed.
- Fails to provide concrete guidance or examples for how the proposed metrics can be practically applied to improve RC systems or dataset construction.

Questions to authors:
- What specific evidence from textual entailment and human comprehension motivated the specific modifications to the original skill classification?
- How should "nonsense" questions be precisely defined and operationally distinguished from questions requiring unusual skills?
- How can the methodology be extended to evaluate the full range of RC skills, such as selecting necessary sentences from context?
- What concrete dataset construction techniques would produce easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer RC datasets based on your analysis?
