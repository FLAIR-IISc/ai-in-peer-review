

This paper proposes a novel framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two complementary metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors refine Sugawara et al.'s classification of prerequisite skills, organizing knowledge reasoning according to textual entailment and human text comprehension principles, and apply these metrics to six RC datasets (MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA, and QA4MRE).

The main contributions are: (1) a comprehensive framework for analyzing RC datasets beyond superficial question types, (2) a refined classification of prerequisite skills based on psychological and NLP research, and (3) publicly available annotations of six RC datasets using these metrics.

Strengths: The paper addresses a critical gap in RC research by providing meaningful metrics for dataset evaluation. The methodology is well-structured with strong inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) and clear metrics. The finding that readability and question difficulty show only weak correlation (Pearson's r=0.025-0.416) is significant, demonstrating that easy-to-read texts can contain difficult questions (e.g., MCTest). The paper also provides practical guidance for dataset construction, suggesting a curriculum learning approach for system development.

Weaknesses: The paper doesn't sufficiently elaborate on how these metrics could directly improve RC systems beyond the general curriculum learning suggestion. The annotation guidelines for distinguishing between bridging and elaboration skills are somewhat vague, potentially affecting reproducibility. The limitation regarding their inability to evaluate sentence selection competence is acknowledged but not sufficiently addressed in the implications for system development.

The paper is well-written, properly contextualized within relevant literature, and provides valuable insights for the field. The publicly available annotations will be a useful resource for future work. However, the authors should clarify how their metrics can be operationalized to guide system development more concretely, and provide more detailed annotation guidelines to enhance reproducibility.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to RC dataset evaluation, though it would benefit from deeper discussion of practical implementation for system development and more detailed annotation protocols. The findings about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty are particularly compelling and warrant further exploration.
