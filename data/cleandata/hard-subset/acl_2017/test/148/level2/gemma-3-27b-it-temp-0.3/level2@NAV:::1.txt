## Review: Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability

This paper presents a valuable analysis of six popular reading comprehension (RC) datasets through the lens of two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors aim to move beyond simple accuracy measurements and provide a more nuanced understanding of dataset characteristics and their potential impact on RC system development. The core contribution lies in the application and refinement of existing psychological models of text comprehension to the evaluation of RC datasets, and the public release of the annotated data. A secondary contribution is the reorganization of prerequisite skills based on textual entailment and human text comprehension theories.

The strengths of this work are significant. The motivation for a more detailed dataset evaluation is well-articulated, and the chosen metrics are grounded in established theories. The annotation process appears sound, with a reported high inter-annotator agreement. The analysis reveals interesting insights, notably the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, and the varying skill demands across datasets (e.g., MCTest requiring more causal reasoning despite being easier to read). The publicly available annotated datasets are a valuable resource for the RC community.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The methodology focuses solely on annotating sentences *needed* for answering, neglecting the crucial aspect of identifying relevant information within a larger context – a key skill for RC systems. This limits the scope of the evaluation. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, its impact on the conclusions isn't fully explored. The analysis, while insightful, is largely descriptive. The paper would benefit from exploring how these metrics correlate with the performance of existing RC models, providing a more direct link between dataset characteristics and system behavior. Furthermore, the discussion of "nonsense" questions feels somewhat underdeveloped; a more rigorous definition and analysis would be beneficial.

Finally, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of potential biases introduced by the annotators and the specific annotation guidelines. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How might the methodology be extended to evaluate a system's ability to *identify* relevant sentences within a larger context, rather than simply assessing the skills required once those sentences are provided?
2.  Have you investigated the correlation between the proposed metrics and the performance of any existing RC models on these datasets? If so, what were the findings?
3.  Could you elaborate on the criteria used to identify "nonsense" questions and discuss any potential subjectivity in this process?
