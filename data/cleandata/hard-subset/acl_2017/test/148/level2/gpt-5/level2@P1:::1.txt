Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a framework to evaluate reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two complementary axes: (a) prerequisite skills required to answer each question, grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology (bridging vs. elaboration), and (b) readability metrics that capture lexical and syntactic difficulty. The authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with multi-label prerequisite skills and compute readability on sentences deemed necessary to answer each question. Key findings include a weak correlation between readability and the number of required skills, demonstrating that datasets can be easy to read yet difficult to answer (e.g., MCTest).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, especially a clearer organization of knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration, integrating insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology.
2) A comparative annotation study of six RC datasets with these skills plus readability features, yielding dataset-level profiles and the empirical finding of weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.
3) Public release of annotations (as claimed), supporting future dataset construction and system analysis.

Strengths
- Clear, theoretically grounded skill taxonomy: The reorganization of “knowledge reasoning” into bridging and elaboration, linked to retrieved vs. generated inferences and to entailment phenomena, is well motivated and practical. It advances prior work by offering a more principled view that should help both analysis and dataset design.
- Multi-dataset, cross-genre comparative analysis: Annotating six widely used datasets (with different collection methodologies and genres) provides useful, actionable insights. Examples include QA4MRE being broadly hardest, MS MARCO easiest, WDW’s high ellipsis due to its construction, and MCTest’s narrative nature producing multi-sentence reasoning.
- Empirical decoupling of readability and difficulty: The weak correlations (and illustrative cases like SQuAD vs. MCTest) are valuable for the community, countering common assumptions that “hard text” implies “hard QA,” and informing curriculum/transfer learning strategies for system development.
- Methodological transparency and reliability: The study describes annotation procedures, includes selection of supporting sentences, reports inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), and outlines the readability feature set. The intention to release annotations increases impact and reproducibility.
- Practical guidance for dataset and system design: The discussion connects findings to concrete recommendations (e.g., staging systems from easy-to-read/easy-to-answer to harder datasets; considering narrative vs. expository genres).

Weaknesses
- Limited sample size and statistical analysis: Annotating 100 questions per dataset provides useful signals but limits statistical power. The reported correlations are described as weak, but there is little detail on significance tests, confidence intervals, or corrections for multiple comparisons. Per-dataset analyses could benefit from more rigorous statistics and larger samples.
- Subjectivity and potential biases in annotation: Annotators saw the answer and selected “necessary sentences,” which may bias both the skill labels and the readability subset. The “nonsense” label is also subjective, and filtering decisions differ across datasets (e.g., NewsQA), potentially confounding cross-dataset comparisons. More detail on the annotation protocol, adjudication, and per-skill agreement would strengthen credibility.
- Assumption that “number of skills = difficulty”: The core difficulty measure treats skills as additive and equally weighted. In practice, certain skills (e.g., multi-hop bridging, elaboration invoking external knowledge) may be more difficult than others, and interactions between skills may be non-linear. No validation is provided linking “number of skills” to human difficulty or system error rates.
- Readability scope and selection: Readability is computed on the subset of sentences chosen as “necessary,” not the entire passage, which sidelines a major aspect of RC difficulty: locating evidence in long or noisy contexts. Moreover, the focus on a limited set of features (plus Flesch–Kincaid for correlation plots) may miss discourse-level properties known to affect comprehension. The relationship between sentence distance and difficulty is only briefly explored.
- Dataset selection and generality: Excluding some influential datasets (e.g., CNN/DailyMail) constrains generality. The rationale is understandable, but it would help to discuss how the proposed framework might adapt to such datasets (e.g., handling anonymization, noise) and to other formats (multiple-choice vs. span extraction vs. cloze).

Questions to authors
- Can you provide fuller statistics on correlation analyses (coefficients, p-values, CIs) and clarify whether you controlled for dataset effects (e.g., mixed-effects models)? Are the weak correlations consistent across all readability metrics in Table 5, or mainly driven by Flesch–Kincaid?
- How was inter-annotator agreement computed for multi-label skill assignments? Please report per-skill agreement (e.g., F1/Krippendorff’s alpha) and agreement on “nonsense” vs. “solvable.”
- How were annotators trained to distinguish bridging vs. elaboration vs. coreference, given their conceptual overlap? Can you share the guidelines and decision rules with examples?
- Do you plan to validate the “number of skills” as a difficulty proxy via human studies (e.g., response time/accuracy) or by correlating with system error rates? Have you tried weighting skills by estimated difficulty?
- Will you release the code to compute readability features and the sentence selection annotations? Are there plans to annotate more questions to increase statistical power?

Additional comments
- Consider complementing “skills count” with measures of reasoning depth/graph structure (e.g., number of hops, cross-sentence dependency length) and with whole-passage readability and discourse features.
- Reporting per-dataset, per-skill confusion matrices and typical failure modes would help future system analyses.
- Exploring automatic prediction of prerequisite skills from question–context pairs could enable scalable dataset profiling without manual annotation.
