Summary and contributions:
- The paper proposes two complementary classes of metrics to characterize RC datasets: (a) prerequisite skills needed to answer questions (a refined taxonomy grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology) and (b) readability features (largely following Vajjala & Meurers). The authors annotate six RC datasets (100 questions each) with these metrics and analyze relationships between skill requirements, readability, and dataset properties.
- Key empirical finding: there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (proxied by the number of prerequisite skills) and readability (e.g., Flesch–Kincaid and related features), suggesting it is possible to build datasets that are easy to read yet hard to answer.
- The paper releases the annotations (stated) and offers qualitative insights per dataset that may inform dataset construction and system development.

Strengths:
- Cross-dataset perspective with a unifying analytic lens. Applying a consistent annotation scheme across six well-known datasets yields informative comparisons (e.g., MCTest’s narrative genre driving higher causal/coreferential demands; SQuAD being hard to read but relatively easy to answer).
- Thoughtful refinement of “prerequisite skills.” The taxonomy integrates insights from textual entailment (bridging vs. elaboration) and cognitive psychology (retrieved vs. generated inferences) and resolves ambiguities in prior work (e.g., clearer treatment of ellipsis, apposition, idioms).
- Empirical evidence against a common assumption. The weak correlation between readability and skill demand is useful for both dataset creators and system developers (e.g., curriculum or transfer strategies that disentangle reading complexity from inferential difficulty).
- Concrete, actionable analyses. The per-skill frequency distributions, counts of required sentences, and sentence-distance analyses provide practical signals about dataset design choices (e.g., avoiding overly local questions).
- Annotation protocol aims for comparability across heterogeneous task formulations (extractive, multiple-choice, etc.) by focusing on entailment between question–answer hypotheses and selected sentences.

Weaknesses:
- Validation of “difficulty” is limited. Using the number of annotated skills as a proxy for question difficulty lacks external validation. The paper does not test whether skill counts correlate with human difficulty (e.g., crowd accuracy/response time) or with model performance. Without such validation, the central conclusion may be weakened.
- Inconsistencies and reporting gaps. The text states that QA4MRE averages 4.6 skills per question, while Table 3 shows 3.25. Inter-annotator agreement is reported as 90.1% but without a clear metric (percent agreement vs. Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa) or per-skill agreement; only 62 questions were sampled. Statistical analyses (e.g., correlations 0.025–0.416) lack p-values/confidence intervals and controls for multiple comparisons or confounds (genre, passage length).
- Annotation design may bias results. Annotators saw the gold answer and selected relevant sentences; this does not assess the difficulty of sentence selection or answer span identification, and may inflate agreement on which skills are “required.” The classification (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration, ellipsis) can be subtle; the paper does not describe a detailed codebook, training, or adjudication procedures, which affects reproducibility.
- Readability operationalization is narrow. Features are computed only on annotator-selected sentences (not full contexts), potentially biasing readability downward for datasets where answers are typically local. Readability metrics are mostly lexical/syntactic; discourse-level measures (e.g., entity grid, coherence relations) known to affect comprehension are not used. The suitability of AWL across heterogeneous genres (children’s narratives vs. news vs. Wikipedia) is questionable.
- Comparability across datasets is confounded by filtering and sourcing differences. For example, NewsQA was filtered using meta labels, while other datasets were not; WDW and MS MARCO include “nonsense” artifacts by construction. The exclusion of widely used datasets (e.g., CNN/DM) reduces external impact, and the small sample size (100 questions per dataset) may not capture dataset variability.

Questions to authors:
- Please clarify the discrepancy between the reported average number of skills for QA4MRE (4.6 in text) and Table 3 (3.25). Which is correct?
- What agreement metric was used for the 90.1% IAA? Can you report per-skill kappa scores and agreement on sentence selection vs. skill labels?
- Is the annotation guideline/codebook available? How were borderline cases (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration; ellipsis vs. coreference) resolved?
- Are the reported correlations statistically significant? Please provide p-values/CI and control for genre and passage length (e.g., partial correlations or mixed-effects models).
- Do skill counts correlate with human difficulty or system performance? Any preliminary evidence using existing human or model accuracy on these dev splits?

Additional comments and suggestions:
- Provide external validation of the skill-count difficulty proxy: correlate with human response time/accuracy or with baseline/model performance stratified by skill counts.
- Consider weighting skills by difficulty (e.g., learned from human performance) instead of a flat count; or analyze co-occurrence patterns to identify particularly challenging combinations.
- Expand readability to include discourse/coherence features (e.g., entity grid, RST relations) and report metrics over full passages as well as answer-relevant sentences.
- Normalize sentence-distance by passage length and report distributional statistics; this would better capture cross-dataset retrieval demands.
- Standardize handling of “nonsense” across datasets and report its annotation agreement; clarify its impact on analyses.
- Increase transparency and reproducibility: release annotations, code, and sampling scripts; include per-dataset sampling diagnostics (topic, paragraph length).
- If space permits, include small-scale system analyses demonstrating how the proposed metrics predict error patterns, to strengthen the practical relevance.

Overall, the paper addresses an important gap and offers a useful framework and initial evidence. With clearer reporting, stronger validation of the difficulty proxy, and more rigorous statistics, the contribution would be substantially strengthened.
