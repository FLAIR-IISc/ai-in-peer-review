- Proposes two complementary evaluation metrics for RC datasets: a refined prerequisite-skills taxonomy and readability features to assess question difficulty and text ease.
- Annotated 100 questions from six datasets and report weak correlations between readability and required skills, arguing datasets can be easy-to-read yet hard-to-answer.
- Provides annotations, summary statistics, and qualitative insights intended to guide dataset design and system evaluation.

Contributions:
- Empirical cross-dataset characterization showing weak readability–difficulty correlation across six RC datasets.
- A theoretically grounded, revised taxonomy of prerequisite skills with an associated annotation protocol and high reported agreement.
- Public release of annotations and auxiliary measures to support curriculum-style dataset construction and targeted evaluation.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation addressing a gap beyond accuracy and superficial categories in RC evaluation.
- Consistent cross-dataset protocol enabling meaningful comparisons and actionable observations (e.g., MCTest profile).
- Grounded taxonomy linking skills to textual entailment and cognitive psychology (bridging vs. elaboration).
- Practical value: findings and released artifacts useful for dataset design, diagnostics, and curriculum learning.
- Reported annotation agreement (90.1%) suggests a workable coding scheme.

Weaknesses:
- Limited statistical power: 100 questions per dataset and correlations reported without confidence intervals, effect sizes, or multiple-comparison controls.
- Reliability reporting underspecified: percent agreement on 62 items is insufficient for multi-label judgments; kappa/alpha per skill not provided.
- Scope excludes retrieval and answer-selection skills since annotators saw answers and selected evidence sentences.
- Construct validity untested: using “number of skills” as a proxy for difficulty is plausible but not validated against human difficulty or model errors.
- Readability analysis narrow: relies on FK and top-10 features without exploring alternative measures or controlling for passage length/genre.

Questions for authors:
- Please report per-skill inter-annotator reliability (e.g., Krippendorff’s alpha) and confidence intervals for correlations.
- Do trends hold with larger samples, different splits, and when controlling for passage length and genre?
- Is there evidence that “number of skills” predicts human difficulty or model error rates?
- Will you release detailed guidelines and code for feature extraction to ensure reproducibility?
- How was “nonsense” operationalized, and how sensitive are results to that labeling?
