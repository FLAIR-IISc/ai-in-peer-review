Summary
The paper proposes two complementary classes of metrics to evaluate reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (i) prerequisite skills (a refined taxonomy grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology) to approximate question difficulty, and (ii) readability features (following Vajjala & Meurers) to quantify text ease. Annotating 100 questions from six datasets, the authors find only weak correlations between readability and required skills, arguing that datasets can be easy-to-read yet difficult-to-answer. Annotations, summary statistics, and qualitative insights are offered to guide dataset design and system evaluation.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical characterization across six RC datasets showing weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, challenging common assumptions about dataset difficulty.
- A revised, theoretically motivated taxonomy of prerequisite skills (bridging/elaboration distinctions, meta-knowledge) and an associated annotation protocol with high observed agreement.
- Public release of annotations and auxiliary measures that can seed curriculum-style dataset construction and targeted evaluation.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and framing: the study addresses a real gap in RC evaluation beyond accuracy and superficial categories.
- Cross-dataset analysis: consistent protocol applied to six prominent datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MS MARCO, NewsQA) enables meaningful comparisons and actionable takeaways (e.g., MCTest’s easy-to-read yet hard-to-answer profile).
- Grounded taxonomy: the skill set is well anchored in textual entailment phenomena and cognitive psychology (bridging vs. elaboration), improving on prior ad-hoc categorizations.
- Practical value: the findings and released annotations can inform dataset design, system diagnostics, and curriculum learning pipelines.
- Annotation quality: reported 90.1% agreement indicates a workable and learnable coding scheme.

Weaknesses
- Limited statistical power and reporting: 100 questions per dataset and correlations reported without CIs, effect sizes, or multiple-comparison controls make some conclusions tentative.
- Agreement metrics are underspecified: percent agreement on 62 items is insufficient for multi-label judgments; kappa/alpha per skill would strengthen reliability claims.
- Scope limits: annotators saw answers and selected evidence sentences, excluding retrieval and answer selection skills; conclusions pertain to entailment understanding only.
- Construct validity: “number of skills” as a proxy for difficulty is plausible but unvalidated against human difficulty or system performance; no predictive validation is shown.
- Readability–skill analysis leans on FK and top-10 features; robustness to alternative readability measures and controls for passage length/domain is not fully explored.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report per-skill inter-annotator reliability (e.g., Krippendorff’s alpha) and CIs for correlations?
- Do the trends persist with larger samples or different splits, and when controlling for passage length and genre?
- Is there evidence that “number of skills” predicts human difficulty or model error rates?
- Will you release detailed guidelines and code for feature extraction to ensure reproducibility?
- How exactly was “nonsense” operationalized, and how sensitive are results to that labeling?
