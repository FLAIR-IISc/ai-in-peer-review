Summary and primary contributions
- This paper proposes a framework to characterize the quality of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two complementary classes of metrics: (a) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills required to answer questions (grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology), and (b) readability metrics (drawing on Vajjala and Meurers, 2012). Six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) are annotated and analyzed.
- The main contributions, in order of perceived strength:
  1) An empirical, cross-dataset analysis with released annotations that reveals dataset-specific skill profiles and shows a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty (as proxied by the number of required skills).
  2) A reorganized skill taxonomy, especially for knowledge reasoning, distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration and integrating insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology.
  3) Practical observations for dataset construction and system development (e.g., curriculum-style progression, the impact of corpus genre, and pitfalls of automated question sourcing).

Strengths
- Empirical characterization across diverse datasets: The study covers six widely used RC datasets with a consistent annotation protocol (annotators select needed sentences and skill labels for each question). Reporting per-dataset trends—e.g., high ellipsis in Who-did-What due to decoupled question/context sources; more “nonsense” in MS MARCO and Who-did-What; MCTest requiring multi-sentence reasoning—provides actionable insight for both dataset creators and system developers.
- The refined skill taxonomy is principled: The reorganization of “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs. elaboration, with links to Kintsch (1993) and McNamara & Magliano (2009) and to entailment phenomena (Dagan et al., 2013; LoBue & Yates, 2011), improves theoretical grounding relative to prior RC skill lists. The taxonomy balances coverage with operational annotability.
- Methodological clarity and inter-annotator agreement: The workflow (joint view of context, question, answer; sentence selection; multi-label skill assignment) is clearly described, and an overall agreement of 90.1% on a sample is encouraging for feasibility of the annotation scheme.
- Central finding with practical implications: The weak correlations (r ≈ 0.03–0.42) between readability and the number of required skills challenge the common assumption that “hard-to-read” texts imply “hard-to-answer” questions, and highlight opportunities to build challenging, yet accessible, datasets (e.g., MCTest).
- Resource value: Public release of annotations and per-dataset profiles is likely to be useful for dataset design (targeting specific skills) and system analysis (capability diagnostics), and aligns with the community’s need for more interpretable evaluation beyond superficial categories.

Weaknesses
- Validation of “difficulty” proxy: The assumption that the number of skills correlates with question difficulty is plausible but under-validated here. No human difficulty judgments (e.g., accuracy, response time) or correlations with system performance are reported. This limits the strength of claims tying skill counts to difficulty. Even small-scale validation with human annotators or baseline models would strengthen the link.
- Agreement metrics and reliability details: The reported 90.1% agreement on 62 questions lacks detail on the measure (percent agreement vs. Cohen’s kappa vs. Krippendorff’s alpha), unit of analysis (per-skill label vs. per-question set), and per-skill reliability. Categories such as bridging vs. elaboration are known to be subtle; per-label confusion matrices or κ would help establish robustness.
- Readability analysis choices: Readability features are computed only on sentences selected as necessary, which may bias grade-level estimates and attenuate variability across datasets. In addition, multiple correlation tests are reported without statistical significance or correction for multiple comparisons. The analysis would benefit from (a) complementary discourse-level measures (e.g., Pitler & Nenkova, 2008) when possible, (b) per-dataset and pooled significance tests with confidence intervals, and (c) sensitivity analyses (e.g., using full passage vs. selected sentences).
- Sampling and comparability: Dataset-specific filtering choices (e.g., NewsQA excluding “bad” and “no answer” questions; Who-did-What’s easy-question exclusion) may affect comparability of “nonsense” rates and skill distributions. A discussion of how these sampling differences may bias cross-dataset comparisons would improve interpretability.
- Scope limitation of the methodology: By showing the answer and having annotators select needed sentences, the framework measures entailment-oriented understanding but not competencies critical for many RC setups, such as evidence retrieval or answer span selection. While acknowledged, this limits how directly findings translate to end-to-end system behavior.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal/clarification)
- Agreement and reliability: What is the exact agreement metric for the 90.1% figure? Please report per-skill κ or α and a confusion matrix, especially for bridging vs. elaboration vs. coreference, and for multi-sentence vs. single-sentence skills.
- Difficulty validation: Are there correlations between the number of required skills and human difficulty (accuracy/response time) or with performance of baseline RC systems on the same items? If not available, could a small-scale validation be provided?
- Readability statistics: Were Pearson correlations tested for significance, and were multiple comparisons controlled? How sensitive are conclusions to computing readability on entire contexts rather than selected sentences?
- Annotation protocol: Could the annotation guidelines be released, along with adjudication procedures and time-per-question estimates? How were disagreements resolved?
- Resource availability: Where will the annotations be hosted, and under what license? Are code and scripts for readability computations available?

Suggestions for improvement
- Essential: (1) Clarify and strengthen reliability reporting (metric, per-skill agreement, confusion). (2) Provide at least a small validation that skill counts correlate with difficulty (human or system-based), or temper claims accordingly. (3) Report statistical significance and confidence intervals for correlations; discuss multiple testing and sampling biases across datasets.
- Optional but valuable: (1) Add sensitivity analyses for readability (selected vs. full text; inclusion of discourse features where feasible). (2) Provide more positive/negative examples for difficult distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration) to improve reproducibility. (3) Explore coupling the skill annotations with error analyses of one or two baseline systems to illustrate diagnostic value.

Overall, the work addresses an important gap in how the community assesses RC datasets. With clearer reliability/validity evidence and tightened statistical analysis, the contribution would be strong and practically useful.
