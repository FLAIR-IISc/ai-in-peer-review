- Proposes two metric classes for RC datasets: prerequisite skills (refined taxonomy) and readability features, applied to six datasets (100 questions each).
- Finds weak correlation between question difficulty (skill count) and readability, suggesting readable but hard datasets are possible.
- Releases annotations and offers qualitative per-dataset insights to inform dataset design and systems.

Strengths:
- Cross-dataset, unified annotation enables informative comparisons across six well-known RC datasets.
- Thoughtful refinement of prerequisite-skill taxonomy integrating textual entailment and cognitive psychology.
- Provides empirical evidence challenging the assumption that readability predicts inferential difficulty.
- Offers concrete, actionable analyses (per-skill frequencies, sentence counts, sentence-distance) useful for dataset design.
- Annotation protocol aims for comparability across diverse QA formats by focusing on entailment with selected sentences.

Weaknesses:
- Uses raw skill counts as a difficulty proxy without external validation against human or model performance.
- Reporting inconsistencies (e.g., QA4MRE skill average mismatch) and unclear IAA metric/details; limited sample for agreement.
- Annotation setup (annotators saw gold answers and selected sentences) may bias results and inflate agreement; codebook/training not described.
- Readability measures computed only on selected sentences and focus on lexical/syntactic features, omitting discourse/coherence metrics.
- Cross-dataset comparability confounded by differing filtering/sourcing and exclusion of some popular datasets; small sample size per dataset may miss variability.

Questions for authors:
- Which QA4MRE skill average is correct (4.6 vs. 3.25)?
- What exact IAA metric was used for the reported 90.1% and can per-skill kappas and sentence-selection agreement be provided?
- Is the annotation guideline/codebook available and how were borderline cases adjudicated?
- Are reported correlations statistically significant with p-values/CIs and controls for genre/passage length (e.g., partial correlations or mixed models)?
- Do skill counts correlate with human difficulty or model performance on these splits?

Suggestions:
- Validate the skill-count difficulty proxy against human response time/accuracy or model errors.
- Consider weighting skills by difficulty or analyzing co-occurrence patterns to find challenging skill combinations.
- Extend readability features to discourse/coherence metrics and compute metrics over full passages as well as answer sentences.
- Normalize sentence-distance by passage length and report distributional statistics for better cross-dataset comparisons.
- Standardize handling and reporting of “nonsense” artifacts across datasets and report agreement on them.
- Improve transparency: release annotations, code, sampling scripts, and per-dataset sampling diagnostics.
- If possible, include small-scale system analyses showing how the metrics predict error patterns to demonstrate practical relevance.
