- Paper proposes a framework to characterize RC datasets using a refined skill taxonomy and readability metrics, and annotates six datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA).
- Main contributions: cross-dataset empirical analysis with released annotations showing dataset-specific skill profiles and weak readability–difficulty correlation.
- Main contribution: reorganized, theoretically grounded skill taxonomy distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration and linking to entailment/cognitive psychology.
- Main contribution: practical observations for dataset construction and system development (curriculum progression, genre effects, pitfalls of automated sourcing).
- Strength: empirical coverage across six diverse datasets with a consistent annotation protocol and actionable per-dataset trends (e.g., ellipsis in Who-did-What; multi-sentence reasoning in MCTest).
- Strength: principled, operationally annotable skill taxonomy that improves grounding of “knowledge reasoning” categories.
- Strength: methodological clarity and reported overall annotator agreement of 90.1%, supporting feasibility of the scheme.
- Strength: central empirical finding that readability correlates weakly with required skills, challenging assumptions that hard-to-read implies hard-to-answer.
- Strength: release of annotations and per-dataset profiles provides a useful resource for dataset design and system diagnostics.
- Weakness: proxying difficulty by number of skills is under-validated; no human difficulty judgments or system performance correlations reported.
- Weakness: agreement reporting lacks detail (metric type, unit of analysis, per-skill reliability, confusion matrices), undermining claims about annotation robustness.
- Weakness: readability analysis may be biased by using only selected sentences, lacks discourse-level measures, and reports correlations without significance testing or multiple-comparison correction.
- Weakness: dataset filtering and sampling choices vary across corpora and may bias cross-dataset comparability of skill distributions and “nonsense” rates.
- Weakness: methodology measures entailment-oriented understanding (answer shown, sentences selected), limiting applicability to retrieval/span-selection RC setups.
- Question: report the exact 90.1% agreement metric and per-skill κ/α plus confusion matrices for subtle categories (bridging vs. elaboration vs. coreference).
- Question: are there correlations between skill counts and human difficulty (accuracy/response time) or baseline system performance; can a small validation be provided?
- Question: were Pearson correlations tested for significance and corrected for multiple comparisons; how sensitive are results to using full contexts vs. selected sentences?
- Question: will annotation guidelines, adjudication procedures, and time-per-question be released; how were disagreements resolved?
- Question: where will annotations be hosted, under what license, and are code/scripts for readability computations available?
- Essential improvements requested: clarify and strengthen reliability reporting; provide at least small validation of skill-counts vs. difficulty or temper claims; report statistical significance/confidence intervals and discuss sampling biases.
- Optional improvements: add sensitivity analyses for readability (selected vs. full text, discourse features), more examples for difficult distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration), and couple annotations with baseline system error analyses.
- Overall: addresses an important gap with practical value, but needs stronger reliability/validity evidence and tightened statistical analysis to substantiate claims.
