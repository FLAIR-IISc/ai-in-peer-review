- Framework evaluating RC datasets on two axes: question difficulty via prerequisite skills and text difficulty via readability metrics.
- Refined a 13-skill taxonomy grounded in psychology and textual entailment.
- Annotated 100 questions from each of six datasets (600 total) and released the annotations.
- Main empirical finding: readability and question difficulty are only weakly correlated, so datasets can be easy-to-read but hard-to-answer and vice versa.
- Secondary contribution: the structured taxonomy itself as an analysis tool and the public annotation resource.

- Tackles an important problem by disentangling reading difficulty from reasoning difficulty in RC evaluation.
- Uses a principled methodology grounded in the C-I model and established readability metrics, applied across six diverse datasets.
- Public release of 600 annotated questions is a significant community resource for fine-grained analysis and targeted system development.
- Provides actionable guidance for dataset selection and curriculum-learning strategies for RC systems.

- The assumption that more prerequisite skills imply greater difficulty is unvalidated; no correlation shown with model performance.
- Skill definitions can be subjective and overlapping (e.g., Bridging vs Elaboration); annotation guidelines and disagreement resolution are under-described.
- Sampling only 100 questions per dataset risks unrepresentative analysis and sampling bias, especially for large datasets like SQuAD.
- The paper underdevelops evidence-finding competence (selecting relevant sentences), focusing mostly on post-evidence reasoning.

- Have you validated that model performance degrades as the annotated number of required skills increases?
- Can you provide challenging annotation examples and detailed guidelines that clarify the Bridging vs Elaboration distinction?
