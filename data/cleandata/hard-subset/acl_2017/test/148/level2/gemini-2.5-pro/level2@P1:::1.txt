**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a framework for evaluating and analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the difficulty of reading the context text (readability) and the difficulty of answering the questions (prerequisite skills). The authors refine a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills required for comprehension (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging, elaboration), drawing from prior NLP work and psychological theories of text comprehension. They also employ a set of 10 established readability metrics to quantify text complexity. Using this framework, the authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (including MCTest, SQuAD, and MS MARCO). The primary finding of their analysis is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and the number of skills required to answer a question. This suggests that datasets can be easy to read but hard to answer (e.g., MCTest) or hard to read but easy to answer (e.g., SQuAD), providing a more nuanced view of dataset difficulty than is currently available.

**Contributions of this work (as I see them)**

1.  **A large-scale, multi-faceted analysis of six prominent RC datasets.** The core contribution is the systematic annotation and comparative analysis of these datasets using a unified framework. This work provides valuable, concrete insights into the characteristics and challenges of each dataset, moving beyond surface-level statistics. The public release of these annotations is a significant resource for the community.
2.  **An important empirical finding that decouples text readability from question complexity.** The paper demonstrates that the difficulty of the underlying text and the reasoning required by the questions are largely independent dimensions. This is a crucial insight for researchers designing new datasets and for practitioners selecting appropriate datasets for model development and evaluation.
3.  **A refined and well-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for reading comprehension.** While building on previous work, the authors have organized and elaborated on a set of skills grounded in both NLP and psychological literature, providing a useful vocabulary for discussing the reasoning capabilities tested by RC tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a timely and important problem.** As the number of RC datasets grows, the community urgently needs more sophisticated tools to understand their properties, compare their challenges, and diagnose model failures. This paper makes a strong contribution toward this goal by proposing a principled evaluation methodology that goes beyond simple accuracy or question-type analysis.
2.  **Comprehensive and insightful dataset analysis.** The study covers a diverse set of six datasets, and the analysis yields compelling characterizations. For example, identifying MCTest as "easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer" and SQuAD as "hard-to-read but easy-to-answer" provides an intuitive and useful summary for researchers. The discussion of how dataset creation methods (e.g., automated generation in Who-did-What) influence the required skills is also very insightful.
3.  **Valuable public resource.** The annotated dataset of 600 questions across six benchmarks is a valuable contribution in its own right. It can enable future work in areas such as error analysis, curriculum learning for RC models, and the creation of targeted evaluation suites that test specific reasoning skills.
4.  **The proposed framework is well-motivated.** The two classes of metrics are grounded in established theories of human text comprehension from psychology (e.g., Kintsch's C-I model). This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the analysis and increases the credibility of the proposed skill taxonomy.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The key assumption that question difficulty is the sum of required skills.** The paper's analysis hinges on the assumption that the number of required skills is a direct proxy for question difficulty. This is a simplification, as some skills are likely much more challenging for current models than others. A question requiring one instance of complex `Logical reasoning` might be harder than a question requiring `Coreference resolution`, `Punctuation`, and `Causal relation`. The paper does not provide evidence to validate this linear-sum assumption of difficulty.
2.  **Potential subjectivity and ambiguity in the skill annotation process.** While a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is reported, the definitions of the 13 skills have some potential for overlap (e.g., the distinction between `Bridging` and `Elaboration` seems to depend on a fine-grained distinction between "lexical" and "commonsense" knowledge). The provided IAA metric is also not fully specified (e.g., is it an exact match on the set of labels, or a per-label accuracy?), which makes it difficult to fully assess the reliability of the annotations. A more standard metric like Fleiss' Kappa would be more informative.
3.  **Limited sample size for large datasets.** The analysis is based on a random sample of 100 questions per dataset. While this may be sufficient for smaller datasets, for massive datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO, a sample of this size may not capture the full diversity of phenomena present. A discussion of the statistical confidence of the conclusions based on this sample size would strengthen the paper's claims.
4.  **The analysis of multi-sentence reasoning could be deeper.** The paper briefly analyzes the number and distance of sentences required to answer a question (Table 6). This is an interesting direction, but the current analysis is brief. For instance, the distance metric is coarse, and the results are not integrated into the main analysis correlating skills with readability. This feels like a missed opportunity to connect the skill-based analysis with the information-gathering challenge.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify how the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement was calculated? Was it an exact match on the set of labels assigned to a question, or a per-instance agreement averaged across all labels? Given the multi-label nature of the task, a chance-corrected coefficient like Fleiss' Kappa might provide a clearer picture of annotation reliability.
2.  Your primary measure of question difficulty is the count of required skills. Have you considered alternative weighting schemes? For example, one could hypothesize that skills like `Logical reasoning` or `Elaboration` are inherently more difficult than `Punctuation`. How might such a weighting affect your conclusion about the correlation with readability?
3.  The discussion section proposes using these metrics for curriculum learning. This would require classifying millions of examples from large datasets automatically. Do you have any thoughts on how your manual annotation framework could be scaled up, perhaps by training a classifier to predict the required skills for a given question?
