**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a two-part evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets, analyzing them through the lenses of "prerequisite skills" needed to answer questions and text "readability." The authors apply this framework to six prominent RC datasets, including SQuAD and MCTest, through manual annotation. The primary contribution of this work is the resulting large-scale comparative analysis, which reveals key characteristics of these datasets and offers valuable insights for the community. The most significant finding is the demonstrated weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty. A secondary contribution is the refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills, which is grounded in psychology and textual entailment literature, providing a more structured method for analyzing question complexity. The public release of these annotations is also a valuable resource.

**Strengths**

1.  **Important and Novel Analysis:** The paper addresses a critical need for deeper, qualitative evaluation of RC datasets beyond simple accuracy metrics. The central finding—that easy-to-read texts can contain difficult questions (e.g., MCTest) and vice-versa (e.g., SQuAD)—is a compelling and important insight that challenges common assumptions in the field. This has direct implications for how we design and select datasets for model training and evaluation.

2.  **Well-Grounded and Comprehensive Framework:** The methodology is well-motivated, building its two-pronged approach on established theories of human comprehension. The revised "prerequisite skills" taxonomy is a clear improvement over prior work, offering a more granular and principled classification. Applying this unified framework across six diverse datasets provides a much-needed, standardized comparison of the RC landscape.

3.  **Valuable Community Resource:** The authors' decision to release the annotations for all six datasets is a significant contribution. This resource will enable future research in areas such as targeted model evaluation, error analysis, and the development of curriculum learning strategies for RC systems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Sample Size:** The analysis relies on annotating only 100 questions per dataset. For large and diverse corpora like SQuAD or MS MARCO, this small sample may not be fully representative, potentially limiting the statistical robustness of the conclusions. While practical, this limitation could affect the generalizability of the reported skill distributions and dataset characteristics.

2.  **Inherent Subjectivity of Skill Annotation:** While the taxonomy is well-defined, the task of assigning prerequisite skills remains subjective. For instance, the distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" can be subtle. Although a high inter-annotator agreement is reported, a more detailed discussion of disagreement cases or ambiguous scenarios would strengthen the paper's claims about the framework's reliability.

3.  **Partial Evaluation of the RC Task:** The paper acknowledges that its methodology primarily evaluates the inference stage of RC and does not assess a model's ability to locate relevant information within a long document. While the sentence distance analysis in Section 6 is a good first step, this is a major component of modern RC tasks that the primary analysis largely sidesteps.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you comment on the statistical significance of your findings, particularly the weak correlation between readability and skill count, given the 100-question sample size? Have you performed any validation to ensure the sampled questions are representative of the larger datasets?

2.  The distinction between "Bridging" (grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (general/commonsense knowledge) seems crucial but potentially ambiguous. Could you provide a few challenging examples from your annotation process that highlight how your annotators consistently differentiated between these two skills?
