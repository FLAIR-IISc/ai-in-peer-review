**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the difficulty of answering questions, measured by a set of "prerequisite skills," and the difficulty of reading the text, measured by established "readability" metrics. The authors refine a previous taxonomy of prerequisite skills, grounding it in theories from psychology and textual entailment. They apply this framework to annotate and analyze 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets (including QA4MRE, MCTest, and SQuAD). The primary contribution is the empirical finding that text readability and question difficulty (as measured by the number of required skills) are only weakly correlated. This leads to the important insight that it is possible to create datasets that are easy to read but hard to answer, and vice versa. A secondary contribution is the refined 13-skill taxonomy itself, which provides a more structured way to analyze the reasoning required by RC tasks. Finally, the public release of the annotations on these 600 questions is a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Critical and Timely Problem:** As the field produces larger and larger RC datasets, there is a pressing need for more nuanced evaluation methods beyond simple accuracy scores. This paper tackles the crucial task of characterizing and comparing the intrinsic properties of these datasets. The core research question—disentangling reading difficulty from reasoning difficulty—is fundamental to understanding what our models are actually learning and how to build better ones. The findings provide a much-needed sanity check against the assumption that complex language (e.g., Wikipedia articles) automatically implies complex reasoning tasks.

2.  **Principled and Comprehensive Methodology:** The authors ground their two-axis evaluation framework in well-established theories from psychology (the C-I model) and NLP (readability metrics). The refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy is thoughtful, drawing on concepts from textual entailment and human inference to create categories like "Bridging" and "Elaboration." Applying this framework across six diverse datasets allows for a broad and comparative analysis, leading to robust and interesting conclusions about the unique character of each dataset (e.g., SQuAD being difficult to read but simple in reasoning, while MCTest is the opposite).

3.  **Significant Community Resource:** The authors' decision to release their annotations for 600 questions across six key datasets is a major strength. This resource will enable other researchers to perform fine-grained error analyses of their models, investigate model performance on specific reasoning skills, and potentially develop systems that are explicitly trained to handle certain skills. This contribution significantly enhances the paper's long-term impact.

4.  **Actionable Insights for Dataset and System Development:** The paper's conclusions are not merely descriptive; they offer clear guidance for future work. The characterization of each dataset helps researchers select appropriate corpora for their specific goals. Furthermore, the discussion on using these metrics for curriculum learning—starting with easy-to-read, easy-to-answer tasks and progressing to more difficult quadrants—presents a concrete and promising path for building more robust RC systems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The "Number of Skills" as a Proxy for Difficulty is an Unverified Assumption:** The paper's central analysis rests on the assumption that a question requiring more skills is inherently more difficult for a machine to answer. While this is intuitive, it is never empirically validated within the paper. The analysis would be far more compelling if it demonstrated a correlation between the number of annotated skills for a question and the performance of state-of-the-art RC systems on that question. Without this connection, the "difficulty" axis in the analysis remains a measure of human-perceived complexity, which may not perfectly align with machine difficulty.

2.  **Subjectivity and Potential Ambiguity in Skill Definitions:** The annotation of prerequisite skills is an inherently subjective task. While a high inter-annotator agreement is reported (90.1%), some skill definitions appear to have fuzzy boundaries. For instance, the distinction between "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge) can be very subtle in practice. A more detailed discussion of the annotation guidelines, including how edge cases and disagreements were resolved, would strengthen the reproducibility and reliability of the proposed skill taxonomy.

3.  **Limited Sample Size and Potential for Sampling Bias:** The analysis is based on a sample of 100 questions per dataset. For very large and diverse datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO, this sample size may not be fully representative of the entire distribution of questions, which could limit the generality of the conclusions. For example, the SQuAD sampling strategy of taking only one question per paragraph might miss variations in question complexity within paragraphs. While precedent for this sample size exists, a discussion of the statistical confidence of the findings would be beneficial.

4.  **Superficial Treatment of Evidence-Finding Competence:** The authors correctly acknowledge that their methodology does not evaluate the crucial competence of selecting the necessary sentences from a long context. The brief analysis of sentence number and distance in the discussion section (Table 6) is a welcome addition but feels underdeveloped. Since identifying the evidence span is a primary challenge in many modern RC tasks, this limitation means the paper's analysis of "difficulty" is focused almost exclusively on the reasoning required *after* the evidence has been found.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your core argument relies on the number of prerequisite skills being a proxy for question difficulty. Have you considered validating this assumption by analyzing the performance of one or more existing RC models on your annotated questions? Showing that models perform worse as the number of required skills increases would provide strong empirical grounding for your claims.

2.  The distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" seems crucial to your taxonomy of reasoning skills, but it also appears to be one of the most subjective. Could you provide a few challenging examples from your annotations that illustrate this distinction and elaborate on the guidelines given to annotators to differentiate them consistently?
