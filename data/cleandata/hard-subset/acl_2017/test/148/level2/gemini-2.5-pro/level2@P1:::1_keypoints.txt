- Paper proposes a framework to evaluate RC datasets along text readability and question prerequisite skills.  
- Authors refine a 13-skill taxonomy for comprehension and use 10 readability metrics.  
- They manually annotate 100 questions from six RC datasets and release the annotations.  
- Main finding: weak correlation between text readability and number of skills required to answer questions.  

- Contribution: large-scale, unified analysis of six prominent RC datasets with public annotations.  
- Contribution: empirical finding that text readability and question reasoning complexity are largely independent.  
- Contribution: a refined, literature-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite comprehension skills.  

- Strength: tackles a timely need for deeper RC dataset analysis beyond accuracy and simple question types.  
- Strength: comprehensive, insightful dataset characterizations (e.g., MCTest vs. SQuAD).  
- Strength: provides a valuable public resource of 600 annotated questions for future work.  
- Strength: framework is theoretically motivated by psychological models of comprehension.  

- Weakness: assumes question difficulty equals sum of required skills without validating that linear assumption.  
- Weakness: potential subjectivity/overlap in skill labels and unclear details on how IAA (90.1%) was computed.  
- Weakness: sample size of 100 per dataset may be insufficient to represent very large datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO.  
- Weakness: superficial analysis of multi-sentence reasoning (coarse distance metric and limited integration with other analyses).  

- Question: clarify how the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement was calculated and report a chance-corrected metric.  
- Question: have you considered weighted difficulty schemes for skills (e.g., Logical reasoning harder than Punctuation) and their effect on conclusions?  
- Question: how could the manual annotation framework be scaled (e.g., automated classifiers) for curriculum learning on large datasets?
