**Summary of the paper and its main contributions**

This paper introduces a principled methodology for evaluating and characterizing reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The proposed framework is built on two classes of metrics: (1) a refined taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills required to answer questions, which serves as a proxy for question difficulty, and (2) a set of 10 established readability metrics, which measure the complexity of the context text. The work applies this framework to six prominent RC datasets, performing a manual annotation of 100 questions from each dataset to identify the required skills. The analysis reveals the distinct characteristics of each dataset and, most notably, demonstrates that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty.

The main contributions are:
1.  A comprehensive empirical analysis of six RC datasets, providing valuable insights into their comparative strengths and weaknesses. The central finding—that readability and question difficulty are not strongly correlated—is an important observation for the community, suggesting that creating challenging questions does not require complex source texts.
2.  The release of a new resource: manual annotations of prerequisite skills for 600 questions across six datasets. This resource can facilitate more nuanced model evaluation and future dataset analysis.
3.  A refined, 13-category taxonomy of prerequisite skills for reading comprehension, grounded in prior work in both NLP (textual entailment) and psychology (models of human text comprehension).

**Strengths**

1.  The work addresses a crucial and timely problem. As the field develops ever-larger RC datasets, methods for qualitatively understanding their properties and the reasoning challenges they pose are essential for measuring true progress in natural language understanding. This paper provides a concrete and well-motivated framework for this purpose.
2.  The analysis is thorough and insightful. By applying the same methodology to six diverse datasets (ranging from narratives for children like MCTest to technical documents in QA4MRE), the paper offers a valuable comparative perspective. The visualization in Figure 3, which plots datasets in a 2D space of readability versus skill count, effectively summarizes the key finding and highlights the unique profile of each dataset.
3.  The proposed taxonomy of prerequisite skills is well-grounded in established literature from psychology and textual entailment. This theoretical foundation lends credibility to the classification, making it less ad-hoc than some prior taxonomies and more likely to be stable and reusable by other researchers.
4.  The public release of the skill annotations for 600 questions is a commendable contribution. This resource enables reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this analysis, for instance, by developing models that target specific skills or by performing more fine-grained error analysis.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The analysis is based on a relatively small sample of 100 questions per dataset. While this may provide a reasonable snapshot, conclusions about massive datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO drawn from such a small sample may not be fully generalizable. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion acknowledging this limitation and the potential for sampling bias to affect the results. This is an essential point of clarification for interpreting the work's conclusions.
2.  The paper defines question difficulty solely as the number of required prerequisite skills. This is a reasonable simplification for an initial study, but it is a simplification nonetheless, as some skills may be inherently more challenging for current systems than others. For example, a question requiring `Mathematical reasoning` might be harder for a standard pre-trained language model than a question requiring both `Coreference resolution` and `Clause relation`. Acknowledging this simplification in the discussion and suggesting alternative or weighted measures of difficulty as future work would add valuable nuance.
3.  While a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is reported, crucial details about its calculation are missing. The reliability of the annotations is fundamental to the paper's claims. Providing more information—such as the specific agreement metric used (e.g., Cohen's Kappa, Fleiss' Kappa), how it was calculated (e.g., on a per-skill, per-question basis), and the process for resolving disagreements—is essential for readers to fully assess the validity of the annotation data.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could the paper provide more details on the calculation of the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement? Specifically, what agreement metric was used, and on what unit of annotation was it computed (e.g., was it a chance-corrected measure like Kappa, calculated on the presence/absence of each of the 13 skills for each question)?
2.  Considering the analysis is based on a 100-question sample from each dataset, could the paper elaborate on the potential impact of this sample size on the generalizability of the dataset characterizations, particularly for the larger corpora like SQuAD and MS MARCO? For instance, how were the samples drawn to ensure they were representative?
