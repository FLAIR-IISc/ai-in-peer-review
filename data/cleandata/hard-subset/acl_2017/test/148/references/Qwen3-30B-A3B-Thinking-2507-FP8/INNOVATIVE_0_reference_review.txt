

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets based on two dimensions: (1) prerequisite skills required to answer questions, and (2) readability of the text. The authors refine a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills by drawing on psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), and apply readability metrics from NLP research. They annotate six major RC datasets with these metrics, revealing that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel Framework for RC Dataset Evaluation**: The paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to evaluating RC datasets that moves beyond superficial metrics (like question types) to examine the cognitive skills required to answer questions and the readability of the text. This is a significant conceptual contribution that addresses a genuine problem in the field.

2. **Theoretically Grounded Taxonomy of Prerequisite Skills**: The authors build on prior work (Sugawara et al., 2017) but substantially refine the taxonomy by drawing on psychological research about human text comprehension. Their classification of knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration inferences is particularly insightful and well-justified.

3. **Empirical Analysis of RC Datasets**: By applying their framework to six major RC datasets, the authors reveal important patterns (e.g., MCTest is easy to read but difficult to answer), providing valuable insights that were previously unavailable.

## Strengths

1. **Bold Conceptual Shift**: The paper fundamentally shifts how we think about evaluating RC datasets. Instead of focusing on accuracy metrics, it proposes evaluating what cognitive skills the dataset actually tests. This is a genuinely novel and bold approach that could reshape the field's understanding of RC datasets.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The authors ground their work in psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), which gives their metrics a solid theoretical basis. This is a significant strength compared to many NLP papers that lack such grounding.

3. **Empirical Validation of Framework**: The authors don't just propose a theoretical framework—they apply it to six major RC datasets and find meaningful patterns. The finding that MCTest is easy to read but hard to answer is particularly compelling and demonstrates the utility of their framework.

4. **Addresses a Real Problem**: The paper identifies a genuine problem in the field—current RC datasets are evaluated with superficial metrics that don't help understand what systems are actually learning. This is a problem that has been noted by others (Chen et al., 2016), but the authors provide a concrete solution.

5. **Potential for Significant Impact**: This work has the potential to change how RC datasets are designed and evaluated. It provides a framework for understanding what cognitive skills are being tested, which could lead to more targeted system development and better datasets.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Analysis**: The paper only analyzes six RC datasets. While this is sufficient for their analysis, it's a relatively small number compared to the many RC datasets that have been proposed. A more comprehensive analysis would strengthen the generalizability of their findings.

2. **No New RC System or Algorithm**: As a purely evaluation-focused paper, it doesn't propose a new RC system or algorithm. While this is acceptable for an evaluation-focused paper, it may be seen as less impactful than a paper that combines evaluation with a new model.

3. **Minor Methodological Limitations**: The authors acknowledge that their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences for answering questions. While this is a limitation, it's not a major flaw for the paper's primary contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you envision this framework being integrated into the process of designing new RC datasets? What specific guidance would you give to dataset designers based on your analysis?

2. The paper notes that MS MARCO was "relatively easy" in terms of prerequisite skills but contained many nonsense questions. How would you address this issue in future dataset design?

3. The authors state that "RC datasets that are difficult to read are not necessarily difficult to answer." How might this insight influence the development of RC systems? Could systems be designed to handle specific types of cognitive skills more effectively?

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by proposing a novel framework for evaluating RC datasets that moves beyond superficial metrics. The theoretical grounding in psychological research is strong, and the empirical analysis provides valuable insights that were previously unavailable. While the paper is purely about evaluation metrics rather than a new system, the novelty and boldness of the conceptual framework outweigh this limitation for an ACL reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection.

The paper's contribution is substantial enough that it could change how researchers think about RC datasets. The weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability (e.g., MCTest being easy to read but hard to answer) is a particularly valuable insight that could guide future dataset design.

I believe this paper would be a significant contribution to the field of reading comprehension and could potentially influence how future RC datasets are designed and evaluated. The conceptual novelty and the empirical validation of the framework make this a strong candidate for acceptance at ACL.
