

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of Paper and Contributions

This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors refine Sugawara et al.'s (2017) classification of prerequisite skills, organizing knowledge reasoning based on textual entailment and human text comprehension, and apply readability metrics from Vajjala and Meurers (2012). They annotate six RC datasets with these metrics and claim to demonstrate a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Annotation of six RC datasets** with the proposed metrics (this is the most concrete contribution)
2. **Refinement of prerequisite skills classification** (though the novelty is limited)
3. **Demonstration of weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability** (though the evidence is insufficient)

## Strengths

1. **Clear motivation for the study**: The paper effectively identifies a gap in current RC evaluation methodology, noting that most datasets are evaluated only with superficial categories like question types.

2. **Systematic approach to metrics**: The authors' attempt to ground their metrics in psychological theories (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) is commendable and provides theoretical grounding.

3. **Practical contribution of dataset annotations**: The authors make their annotations publicly available, which is valuable for the community.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Critical Inconsistency in Core Claim (Major Flaw)
The paper states: "QA4MRE had the highest score; on average, each question required 4.6 skills" (Section 5.1(ii)). However, Table 3 clearly shows the average number of required skills for QA4MRE is 3.25, not 4.6. This is a fundamental inconsistency that undermines the credibility of the paper. The authors cannot claim to have found an average of 4.6 skills when their own table shows 3.25. This suggests either a serious error in data collection or a failure to carefully proofread the manuscript.

### 2. Lack of Clear Definition for "Nonsense" Questions (Critical Flaw)
The paper repeatedly uses the term "nonsense" to describe unsolvable questions, but provides no clear definition of what constitutes a nonsense question. In Section 4.1, they state: "The annotators were allowed to select nonsense for an unsolvable question to distinguish it from a solvable question that required no skills." This is logically confusing - if a question is unsolvable, it should be marked as nonsense, but the phrasing suggests they're using "nonsense" as a separate category from "unsolvable." Without a clear, objective definition of what makes a question "nonsense," the annotation process lacks methodological rigor, and the results are questionable.

### 3. Missing Statistical Evidence for Core Claim (Major Flaw)
The paper's main contribution is the claim that "there is only a weak correlation between the difficulty of questions and the readability of context texts in RC datasets." However, they reference "Table 5" which presents Pearson's correlation coefficients, but this table is not included in the paper. Without the actual correlation values, readers cannot verify their claim. The paper states there are "weak correlations from 0.025 to 0.416," but provides no evidence for this range. This is a significant omission that prevents evaluation of their key claim.

### 4. Inconsistent Methodology for Skill Annotation (Major Flaw)
The paper claims to have refined Sugawara et al.'s (2017) classification of ten reading comprehension skills into thirteen, but fails to clearly explain how this refinement differs from the original. The paper states "Skills that have been modified/elaborated from the original definition are appended with an asterisk (∗), and new skills unique to this study are appended with a dagger (†)," but then provides no clear comparison between the original and revised classifications. Without this, it's impossible to evaluate the significance of their "refinement."

### 5. Fundamental Limitation Not Addressed (Significant Flaw)
The paper acknowledges a major limitation in Section 1: "our methodology could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read and answer candidates from the context." This is a critical flaw because reading comprehension inherently requires identifying relevant context passages. The paper fails to address how this limitation affects their entire evaluation framework. If their metrics cannot assess the most fundamental aspect of RC (identifying relevant text), their entire approach is questionable.

## Questions to Authors

1. Please clarify the discrepancy between the stated average number of skills (4.6) and the value in Table 3 (3.25) for QA4MRE.

2. Please provide a clear, objective definition of what constitutes a "nonsense" question in your annotation process.

3. Please provide the actual correlation coefficients (Table 5) to support your claim about weak correlation between question difficulty and readability.

4. Please explain in detail how your refined classification of prerequisite skills differs from Sugawara et al.'s (2017) original classification, with specific examples.

5. How does the fundamental limitation of not evaluating "competence of selecting necessary sentences" affect your overall conclusions about the relationship between question difficulty and readability?

## Overall Assessment

This paper has a clear motivation and some potentially useful annotations, but it suffers from multiple critical flaws that prevent it from being accepted. The fundamental inconsistency in the core claim (4.6 vs. 3.25 average skills), the lack of clear definition for "nonsense" questions, and the missing statistical evidence for the main claim are serious issues that undermine the paper's credibility.

While the annotation of six datasets is a valuable contribution, the methodological flaws and inconsistencies prevent the results from being trusted. The paper's contribution is largely incremental rather than transformative, and the authors fail to demonstrate how their metrics can be used to improve RC systems or dataset construction in a meaningful way.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial methodological flaws and inconsistencies that compromise the paper's credibility and contribution. The issues identified are not minor formatting or presentation errors but rather fundamental problems with the paper's data, methodology, and claims.

## Recommendation

Reject (Due to fundamental methodological flaws and inconsistencies that undermine the paper's core claims)
