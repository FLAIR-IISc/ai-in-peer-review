

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" and "readability." The authors refine a previous classification of prerequisite skills (Sugawara et al., 2017) and combine it with readability metrics (Vajjala and Meurers, 2012) to analyze six existing RC datasets. Their main claim is that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability. They annotate the datasets with their metrics and make the results publicly available.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Annotation of six RC datasets with a refined classification of prerequisite skills and readability metrics, providing a useful resource for the community.
2. Demonstration of a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability in RC datasets.
3. Revision of the previous classification of prerequisite skills for RC, organizing knowledge reasoning in terms of entailment phenomena and human text comprehension.

## Strengths
1. **Identifies a genuine problem in the field**: The paper correctly identifies that current RC datasets lack detailed metrics beyond superficial categories like question types and answer types. The observation that "questions easy for humans often turn out to be difficult for systems" is well-founded and recognized in the literature.

2. **Methodologically sound annotation process**: The authors report an inter-annotator agreement of 90.1%, which is strong for a task involving subjective judgment of comprehension skills. Their annotation procedure is well-described and transparent.

3. **Useful dataset analysis**: The paper provides valuable insights into the characteristics of six major RC datasets. For instance, their finding that MCTest is easy to read but difficult to answer (contrary to common assumptions) is an important observation that could inform dataset construction.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty**: The paper's main contribution is a refinement of previously proposed metrics rather than a truly novel approach. The authors themselves admit they're "reorganizing" the classification from Sugawara et al. (2017), which was based on only two datasets. This is not a significant methodological advancement, especially given the paper's claim that previous work "lacks a strong theoretical background" - they don't provide evidence that their reorganization is theoretically superior.

2. **No evidence of practical utility**: The authors claim their metrics can guide "the step-by-step construction of better RC datasets" and "improve RC systems" through "curriculum learning." However, they provide no empirical evidence to support this claim. The paper is purely descriptive and analytical, with no experimental validation of how these metrics would actually improve system development.

3. **Weak justification for the metrics**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that their refined classification of prerequisite skills is more useful or accurate than existing methods. They claim to "revised" the previous classification, but don't demonstrate how this revision improves upon the original. The paper is essentially descriptive without showing why their particular categorization is superior.

4. **No comparison to established baselines**: The paper makes claims about the "quality" of RC datasets but doesn't compare their metrics to existing evaluation methods. Without showing how their metrics provide better insights than current approaches, the contribution is hard to justify.

5. **The main finding is not particularly surprising**: The observation that question difficulty doesn't necessarily correlate with text readability is not novel. As the authors themselves note, "questions easy for humans often turn out to be difficult for systems" - this is a well-known phenomenon in NLP that doesn't require a new metric framework to observe.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide evidence that your refined classification of prerequisite skills is superior to Sugawara et al.'s original classification? What specific improvements does your approach offer, and how do you measure this improvement?

2. The paper claims their metrics "will help researchers to develop a method for the step-by-step construction of better RC datasets." Can you provide at least one concrete example of how these metrics would be used in practice to improve dataset construction or system development?

3. The paper acknowledges that their methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read." How might this limitation affect the interpretation of their results, and what steps would you take to address this in future work?

4. The authors state that "our methodology could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read and answer candidates from the context." How do they reconcile this limitation with their claim that their metrics provide "qualitative features of RC datasets"?

## Overall Recommendation
Reject

The paper identifies a problem worth addressing but fails to provide a genuinely novel or useful contribution. The metrics proposed are a refinement of existing work without sufficient evidence of superiority, and the paper lacks empirical validation of the practical utility of these metrics. The main finding is not particularly novel, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that their approach would actually improve RC system development. As a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods and rigorous validation, I find the paper's contributions insufficiently justified for publication at ACL.
