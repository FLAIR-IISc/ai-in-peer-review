

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two orthogonal classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors argue that current RC datasets are often evaluated with superficial metrics (e.g., question types, answer types) that fail to capture the true complexity of the tasks. They refine the prerequisite skills classification from prior work using insights from textual entailment research and human text comprehension psychology. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these metrics and analyze the results, finding only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by required skills) and text readability.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
1. A refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC, organized based on textual entailment phenomena and human text comprehension research (rather than the provisional categorization in prior work).
2. Empirical demonstration that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by required prerequisite skills) and text readability across six RC datasets.
3. A publicly available annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics, providing a valuable resource for the community.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical foundation and meaningful metrics**: The paper establishes a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed metrics by drawing on established work in psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (textual entailment). The authors successfully refine the prerequisite skills classification to be more comprehensive and theoretically grounded than previous work, addressing the limitations of Sugawara et al. (2017). The metrics are well-motivated and clearly defined.

2. **Important empirical insights**: The analysis reveals a significant disconnect between text readability and question difficulty, which challenges common assumptions in the field. For example, MCTest has easy-to-read narrative texts but requires complex reasoning skills, while SQuAD has difficult-to-read Wikipedia articles but questions that are relatively easy to answer. This finding (Figures 3-4, Table 5) is well-supported by data and has important implications for dataset construction and system development.

3. **High-quality annotation and community resource**: The authors conducted a thorough annotation of six RC datasets with strong inter-annotator agreement (90.1%). Making the results publicly available provides a valuable resource for the community to understand the qualitative features of these datasets. The analysis of which datasets are more challenging (e.g., QA4MRE being the most difficult, MS MARCO the easiest) offers practical guidance for researchers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of dataset analysis**: The authors only analyze six RC datasets, which may not be representative of the broader landscape. They exclude several major datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) without providing strong justification. For example, CNN/Daily Mail is excluded because "it is anonymized and contains some errors" (Chen et al., 2016), but Chen et al. already identified these issues. Including this dataset (and explaining how their metrics would handle such issues) would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.

2. **Methodological limitation**: The authors acknowledge that their methodology "could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read and answer candidates from the context" (Section 1). This is a significant limitation because a key aspect of RC is finding the relevant parts of the text. The paper does not sufficiently discuss how this limitation affects their main conclusions or how it might be addressed in future work.

3. **Lack of practical demonstration**: While the authors suggest using their metrics to guide RC system development (e.g., curriculum learning in Section 6), they do not provide evidence or examples of how these metrics would be used in practice. Without demonstrating how the metrics can actually guide system development, the contribution remains descriptive rather than prescriptive.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors excluded CNN/Daily Mail because it "is anonymized and contains some errors" (Section 4.2). However, Chen et al. (2016) already identified these issues. Given that CNN/Daily Mail is one of the largest RC datasets, why didn't the authors include it in their analysis and discuss how their metrics would handle such issues?

2. The authors state that their methodology "could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read" (Section 1). How would you propose to extend your metrics to evaluate this aspect of RC? Is this something you plan to address in future work?

3. In Table 2, NewsQA has 1.0 instances of "meta-knowledge" (which appears to be a typo for 0.0), but the authors claim NewsQA is advantageous because it provides "meta information on the reliability of the questions." How does this meta information relate to the "meta-knowledge" skill in your classification? Why does NewsQA have 1.0 instances of "meta-knowledge" in Table 2 if it's supposed to be about question reliability?

## Recommendation

The paper presents a theoretically grounded and empirically supported methodology for analyzing RC datasets along meaningful dimensions. The findings about the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability are significant and well-supported. The publicly available annotations provide a valuable resource for the community. While the scope of the analysis could be expanded and the methodological limitations addressed, the core contribution is strong and valuable for the field. I recommend acceptance.
