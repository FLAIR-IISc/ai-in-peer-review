

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two complementary metrics: "prerequisite skills" (based on human text comprehension research) and "readability" (based on linguistic features). The authors refine Sugawara et al.'s (2017) classification of prerequisite skills and apply their metrics to annotate six existing RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA). Their key finding is that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability, challenging the assumption that easier-to-read texts produce easier questions.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Systematic analysis of RC datasets using complementary metrics**: The authors provide a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets beyond simple accuracy, demonstrating that question difficulty and text readability are largely independent dimensions.

2. **Refined classification of prerequisite skills**: The authors reorganize knowledge reasoning skills based on textual entailment and human inference types (bridging vs. elaboration), creating a more comprehensive and theoretically grounded framework.

3. **Publicly available annotated datasets**: The authors make their annotation results for six major RC datasets publicly available, creating a valuable resource for the community.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Contribution to Dataset Evaluation**: This paper addresses a critical gap in the RC field - the lack of metrics to evaluate dataset quality beyond simple accuracy. The authors demonstrate that readability of text does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty (e.g., MCTest has easy-to-read narrative text but difficult questions), which is a key insight that challenges common assumptions in dataset construction. This finding has direct implications for how future RC datasets should be designed.

2. **Rigorous Methodology and Analysis**: The paper is methodologically sound with clear definitions of metrics, a well-documented annotation procedure (including 90.1% inter-annotator agreement), and thorough statistical analysis. The authors provide detailed tables (2-6) and visualizations (Figures 3-4) that clearly present their findings. Their analysis of the correlation between prerequisite skills and readability using Pearson's coefficients (Table 5) provides strong empirical evidence for their main claim.

3. **Practical Value for the Community**: The authors explicitly discuss how their framework can guide the development of better RC systems through curriculum learning, making the paper highly relevant for both dataset construction and system development. The public release of their annotated datasets creates an immediately useful resource for the community, as they state: "Our annotation results will help researchers to develop a method for the step-by-step construction of better RC datasets and a method to improve RC systems."

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Nature of the Prerequisite Skills Classification**: While the authors refine Sugawara et al.'s (2017) work, the contribution appears somewhat incremental rather than transformative. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why their specific reorganization of the 13 skills is fundamentally better than the original classification. The authors state they "reorganized the category of knowledge reasoning in terms of textual entailment and human text comprehension," but they don't provide clear evidence that this reorganization leads to meaningful improvements in understanding RC tasks compared to prior work.

2. **Limited Scope of Dataset Analysis**: The authors only analyze six RC datasets, excluding several prominent ones (like CNN/Daily Mail) based on their own criteria. While they justify this by stating these datasets "did not seem to be suitable for annotation," the exclusion of major datasets weakens the generalizability of their findings. A more comprehensive analysis across a wider range of RC datasets would have strengthened their claims about the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability.

3. **Lack of Discussion on the Relationship Between Metrics and System Performance**: The paper focuses on dataset evaluation but doesn't explore how these metrics relate to system performance. The authors don't investigate whether systems that perform well on datasets with high prerequisite skills requirements also perform well on other metrics, or whether their metrics could help identify specific weaknesses in RC systems. This limitation reduces the paper's impact for system developers.

4. **Insufficient Treatment of "Nonsense" Questions**: The paper identifies that some datasets (like MS MARCO) contain high rates of "nonsense" questions, but the discussion of this issue is limited. The authors acknowledge they "did not have a clear definition of what a 'solvable' question is," which is a significant methodological limitation. This problem is particularly relevant for automated dataset curation (e.g., MS MARCO's web snippets and search queries), yet the paper doesn't explore how their framework could help address this issue.

5. **Limited Discussion of the "Competence of Selecting Necessary Sentences" Limitation**: The authors acknowledge in Section 1 that their methodology "could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read and answer candidates from the context." While they briefly analyze sentence distance (Section 6, Table 6), they don't fully explore how this limitation affects the interpretation of their results or what it means for the practical application of their metrics.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you address the issue of "nonsense" questions in RC datasets, given that your framework doesn't have a clear definition for "solvable" questions? Could your metrics help identify and filter such questions?

2. The paper states that "our methodology was used to evaluate the understanding of contextual entailments in a broader sense for RC." How do you reconcile this with the fact that your metrics don't capture the competence of selecting necessary sentences? Could this limitation be overcome with additional metrics?

3. The authors claim their framework can guide "curriculum learning" for RC systems. How would you specifically apply this framework to create a curriculum of RC datasets with increasing difficulty, and what evidence do you have that this approach would lead to better system performance?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a systematic framework for evaluating RC datasets and demonstrating a key insight about the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability. The methodology is sound, the analysis is thorough, and the publicly available annotated datasets will be a useful resource for the community. However, the contribution is somewhat incremental in the classification of prerequisite skills, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the scope of their dataset analysis and the relationship between their metrics and system performance.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important gap in the field and provides a useful framework for dataset evaluation, but the authors should strengthen their analysis of the limitations and broaden their discussion of the implications for system development.

**Score: 7 (Strong Accept)**
