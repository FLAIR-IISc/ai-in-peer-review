- Paper proposes evaluating RC datasets with two metric classes: prerequisite skills (13-skill taxonomy) and readability, annotating six major RC datasets and finding weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability.

- Contributions: theoretically grounded prerequisite-skill taxonomy, systematic analysis of six RC datasets on question difficulty and readability, and public release of annotated metrics.

- Strength: Strong theoretical foundation by grounding metrics in cognitive science and refining prior taxonomies (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration).

- Strength: Practical insights for dataset construction, e.g., MCTest is easy-to-read but hard-to-answer, challenging assumptions linking text difficulty to question difficulty.

- Strength: Comprehensive annotation framework with 13 skills, careful category decisions, and high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%).

- Weakness: Lacks concrete connection to system development—no clear examples of how metrics improve model training, curriculum learning, or error analysis.

- Weakness: Limited annotation scope—excludes prominent datasets like CNN/Daily Mail and LAMBADA, reducing generalizability.

- Weakness: Unclear definition and inconsistent annotation of "nonsense" / "no answer" questions, undermining reliability for datasets containing such items.

- Weakness: No validation against actual system performance to show metrics predict or relate to model behavior on specific skills.

- Weakness: Insufficient treatment of sentence selection competence; brief/ superficial analysis of sentence distance doesn't address core selection challenges.

- Question: How do metrics capture the relationship between text support and question difficulty, i.e., why high-readability texts can yield difficult questions?

- Question: For MCTest, is difficulty driven by comprehension skills rather than text complexity given its child-level texts?

- Question: How do MS MARCO’s “nonsense” questions relate to its relatively low prerequisite-skill scores—should nonsense imply fewer required skills?

- Question: Clarify why Who-did-What shows high ellipsis given its automatic generation and potential missing context.

- Question: How could the framework be extended to evaluate sentence selection competence and what additional metrics would be needed?

- Overall recommendation: Weak Accept — valuable theoretical and annotation contributions but needs clearer “nonsense” definitions, stronger system-connection examples, and extended methodology for sentence selection.
