

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors argue that current RC datasets are evaluated using superficial categories (e.g., question types) that fail to capture the true complexity of RC tasks. They refine the prerequisite skills classification from previous work, organizing it based on textual entailment and human text comprehension research, and develop readability metrics based on linguistic features. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and demonstrate that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. A refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC, organized based on textual entailment phenomena and human text comprehension research (bridging vs. elaboration inferences), which provides a more theoretically grounded and comprehensive framework than previous approaches.

2. An empirical analysis demonstrating that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by the number of required prerequisite skills) and text readability across six RC datasets, with specific examples (like MCTest being easy to read but difficult to answer) illustrating this key finding.

3. The annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics and the public release of these results, which provides valuable insights for future dataset construction and system development.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful gap in RC evaluation**: The paper correctly identifies a significant problem in the field—the lack of meaningful evaluation metrics beyond superficial categories like question types. The examples in Figure 1 effectively illustrate how text readability does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty, which is a genuine issue in current RC research. This is a well-motivated contribution that addresses a real need in the field.

2. **Theoretical grounding of prerequisite skills classification**: The refinement of prerequisite skills is well-justified and builds upon existing work in both NLP (textual entailment) and psychology (human text comprehension). The authors provide clear explanations for how they improved upon previous classifications (e.g., Sugawara et al., 2017), particularly by organizing knowledge reasoning in terms of bridging vs. elaboration inferences. This represents a solid theoretical contribution to the field.

3. **Clear empirical findings with practical implications**: The analysis of six RC datasets reveals the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, which has significant implications for dataset construction and system development. The specific examples provided (e.g., MCTest being easy to read but difficult to answer) effectively illustrate this finding. The discussion of using these metrics for curriculum learning in system development is a valuable practical contribution that could guide future research.

4. **Well-defined methodology and high inter-annotator agreement**: The annotation procedure is clearly described, and the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement demonstrates the reliability of their approach. The authors also provide a thorough explanation of how they handled different dataset types and why they excluded certain datasets from their analysis.

5. **Public availability of annotation results**: Making the annotation results publicly available for six RC datasets provides a valuable resource for the community, enabling further research and replication of their findings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Significant limitation in methodology**: The authors acknowledge that their methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read and answer candidates from the context." This is a fundamental limitation for an RC evaluation framework, as the ability to locate relevant information is a core component of RC. The paper does not adequately address how to overcome this limitation or provide a path forward for future work to address it.

2. **Lack of comparison with existing evaluation frameworks**: The paper does not sufficiently compare their metrics with existing evaluation frameworks used in RC research. It would strengthen the paper to demonstrate how their metrics provide more nuanced insights than current approaches, rather than just stating that current categories are superficial.

3. **Limited analysis of practical application**: While the paper mentions using the metrics for curriculum learning, it lacks concrete examples of how researchers could use these metrics to improve RC systems or construct better datasets. The practical utility of their framework would be significantly enhanced with specific examples.

4. **Insufficient justification for dataset selection**: The paper excludes several RC datasets (e.g., CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) but does not provide a comprehensive justification for why these datasets were excluded. The paper would be stronger with a clearer explanation of why these datasets were not suitable and whether the patterns observed would hold across a broader set of RC datasets.

5. **No analysis of correlation with system performance**: The paper analyzes the relationship between prerequisite skills and readability but does not examine how these metrics correlate with actual system performance. Demonstrating how their metrics relate to current RC system capabilities would significantly strengthen the practical relevance of their framework.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper acknowledges that the methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting relevant sentences. How would you propose addressing this limitation in future work, and what would be the next steps to develop a more comprehensive evaluation framework?

2. Can you provide specific examples of how researchers could use your metrics to improve RC systems or construct better datasets, beyond the general discussion of curriculum learning? For instance, how might a researcher use these metrics to identify weaknesses in a current system?

3. How do your metrics compare to existing evaluation frameworks used in RC research (e.g., those used in the papers you cite)? Can you provide specific examples of how your metrics provide more nuanced insights than current approaches?

4. Why did you choose to annotate only six datasets? Would the patterns you observed (weak correlation between prerequisite skills and readability) hold across a broader set of RC datasets, including those you excluded from your analysis?

5. Have you analyzed how your metrics correlate with actual system performance on these datasets? If so, what insights did this analysis provide about the relationship between the metrics and system capabilities?
