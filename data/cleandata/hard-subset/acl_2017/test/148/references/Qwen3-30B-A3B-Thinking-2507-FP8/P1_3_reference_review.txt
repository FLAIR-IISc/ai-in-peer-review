

# Peer Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a systematic framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" and "readability." The authors argue that current RC datasets lack meaningful evaluation metrics beyond superficial categories like question types, making it difficult to accurately assess system performance. Their main contributions are:

1. A refined taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills for RC, grounded in psychological theories of text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, 2009; Kintsch, 1993), which moves beyond previous superficial categorizations.
2. A comprehensive annotation of six major RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) using these metrics, making the results publicly available.
3. Empirical evidence that there is only a weak correlation between the difficulty of RC questions (as measured by required prerequisite skills) and the readability of the context texts.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a critical gap in RC evaluation**: The paper effectively identifies a significant problem in the field—current RC datasets are evaluated using superficial metrics, making it difficult to understand what specific capabilities systems actually possess. The authors' framework provides a meaningful way to analyze RC datasets' qualitative features, which is sorely needed in the field. Their findings that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated (Table 5, Figures 3-4) directly challenge common assumptions in RC research.

2. **Theoretical grounding of prerequisite skills**: The authors' refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy is well-justified and theoretically sound. By drawing from psychological research on text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment (Dagan et al.), they create a more comprehensive and less ad-hoc classification than previous work (Sugawara et al., 2017). The distinction between bridging and elaboration inferences, for example, is particularly well-motivated and useful for understanding RC challenges.

3. **Valuable public resource**: The large-scale annotation of six major RC datasets with these metrics represents a significant contribution to the field. Making these results publicly available provides a valuable resource for future dataset construction and system development, as the authors note. The annotation procedure was robust (90.1% inter-annotator agreement), and the dataset specifications (Table 1) provide important context for the results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited practical utility for system development**: While the paper discusses how the metrics could guide system development (Section 6), it fails to provide concrete examples or evidence of how researchers could actually apply these metrics to improve RC systems. The "curriculum learning" analogy is too vague to be practically useful. Without clear guidance on how to use these metrics to build better systems, the contribution's practical value is diminished.

2. **Methodological limitations**: The authors explicitly acknowledge that their methodology "could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read and answer candidates from the context" (Section 1). This is a fundamental limitation for RC tasks, where information location is often as challenging as comprehension. The paper doesn't address how this limitation affects their conclusions or how future work could overcome it.

3. **Insufficient analysis of the "nonsense" question issue**: The paper identifies "nonsense" questions as a problem in some datasets (e.g., MS MARCO), but doesn't propose a clear solution for identifying or handling such questions. The authors mention that NewsQA has meta-information about question quality, but they don't discuss how this could be systematically incorporated into future dataset construction. This is a significant gap given that Chen et al. (2016) already highlighted this issue.

4. **Weak correlation analysis**: While the paper demonstrates a weak correlation between prerequisite skills and readability, it doesn't deeply analyze *why* this correlation is weak or what this implies for RC research. The authors could have provided more insight into how this finding should change the way researchers approach dataset construction or system evaluation.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you specifically use your metrics to guide the development of an RC system, beyond the vague curriculum learning analogy? Could you provide a concrete example of how a researcher might use these metrics to improve their system?

2. Given that your methodology cannot evaluate the "competence of selecting necessary sentences," how would you propose to extend this framework to address this critical aspect of RC tasks? Would you incorporate metrics related to information retrieval or attention mechanisms?

3. The paper notes that MS MARCO had a high rate of "nonsense" questions (Table 2), but doesn't discuss how to prevent this in future datasets. How would you recommend dataset creators ensure that questions are "solvable" without relying on meta-information like NewsQA provides?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets. The refinement of prerequisite skills and the large-scale annotation of six datasets are significant achievements. However, the paper's practical utility is limited by its failure to provide concrete guidance on how these metrics can be used to improve RC systems, and by the methodological limitation that excludes a critical aspect of RC tasks. With revisions addressing these limitations, this paper would make an important contribution to the field. As it stands, I recommend borderline acceptance with the understanding that the authors will address these limitations in their revision.
