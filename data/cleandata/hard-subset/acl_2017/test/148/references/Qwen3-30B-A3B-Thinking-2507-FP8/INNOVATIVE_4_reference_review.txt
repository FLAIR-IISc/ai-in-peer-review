

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper introduces two novel classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" (revised from prior work using psychological insights) and "readability" (based on linguistic features). The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and demonstrate that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability. This challenges the common assumption that readability correlates with question difficulty, which has significant implications for how RC datasets are constructed and evaluated.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **The core insight that readability does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty**: This is a fundamental, non-obvious contribution that challenges a long-standing assumption in the field. The authors provide compelling empirical evidence through their analysis of six datasets, showing that RC systems can face difficult questions from easy-to-read texts (like MCTest) and easy questions from hard-to-read texts (like SQuAD).

2. **The reorganization of prerequisite skills using psychological and linguistic insights**: The authors build upon Sugawara et al. but ground their classification in Kintsch's and McNamara & Magliano's theories of text comprehension, creating a more theoretically robust framework than previous work.

3. **The creation of a new annotated dataset with these metrics**: The authors make their annotation results publicly available, providing a valuable resource for the RC community that can be used for future research and dataset construction.

## Strengths
1. **Bold, novel insight with broad implications**: The paper's central finding—that readability and question difficulty don't correlate—represents a significant conceptual shift in how we evaluate RC datasets. This insight has profound implications for dataset construction (e.g., MCTest demonstrates that narrative texts can create difficult questions) and system development (e.g., systems could be trained on progressively harder datasets with varying readability). This is the strongest contribution and justifies the paper's novelty.

2. **Strong theoretical grounding**: Unlike many NLP papers that propose metrics without theoretical foundation, the authors anchor their metrics in established psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). This provides credibility and helps move the field beyond superficial metrics.

3. **Practical utility for dataset construction**: The paper doesn't just present metrics; it provides a clear methodology for evaluating RC datasets and suggests how these metrics can be used in practice (e.g., through curriculum learning). This immediately useful contribution makes the paper valuable to the community.

4. **Comprehensive comparative analysis**: By analyzing six different RC datasets, the authors demonstrate the applicability of their metrics across a range of RC tasks, from technical documents (QA4MRE) to children's narratives (MCTest). This breadth strengthens their argument.

5. **Addresses a real problem in the field**: The paper correctly identifies a critical gap in current RC evaluation—datasets are evaluated only with superficial metrics (question types, answer types) that don't capture the true difficulty of the tasks. The authors provide a meaningful solution to this problem.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited scope of dataset analysis**: The paper only analyzes six RC datasets, which, while important, may not be representative of the broader landscape of RC datasets. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide evidence that their findings would generalize to other datasets.

2. **Methodological limitation in annotation**: The authors explicitly state that their methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting necessary sentences" (the ability to find relevant text), which is a key component of RC. This is a significant limitation for an evaluation metric, as it means they're only evaluating one part of the RC process.

3. **Lack of system evaluation**: While the paper suggests how these metrics could be used in system development (e.g., curriculum learning), it doesn't provide empirical evidence that using these metrics leads to better systems. There's no demonstration that systems trained with these metrics perform better.

4. **Not entirely novel metrics**: While the combination and refinement of metrics is novel, the individual metrics (readability features, some prerequisite skills) are based on previous work. The novelty is in the integration and application, not the metrics themselves.

5. **Limited discussion of practical implementation**: The paper describes the annotation procedure but doesn't provide enough guidance on how researchers could practically implement these metrics in their own work, which would increase the paper's utility.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would you respond to the criticism that the weak correlation (0.025-0.416) might not be meaningful for practical RC system development, especially since the paper's main contribution is to demonstrate this weak correlation?

2. Given that your methodology cannot evaluate sentence selection competence, what would you propose as a way to address this limitation in future work? Could you integrate this with the current metrics?

3. The paper suggests using these metrics for curriculum learning, but doesn't provide concrete examples. Could you elaborate on how this would work in practice, with specific examples of dataset construction and system training?

## Recommendation
**Accept**

This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to the field of reading comprehension by challenging a fundamental assumption about RC dataset evaluation. The core insight that readability does not correlate with question difficulty is bold, novel, and well-supported by empirical evidence. While the methodology has limitations (as acknowledged by the authors), the novelty and conceptual importance of the paper outweigh these concerns. The theoretical grounding in psychology and the practical utility for dataset construction make this a valuable contribution to the field. As an ACL reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper represents a significant step forward in how we evaluate and construct RC datasets.
