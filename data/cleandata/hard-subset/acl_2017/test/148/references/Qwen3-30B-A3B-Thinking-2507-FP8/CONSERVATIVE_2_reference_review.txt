

# ACL Review

## Summary
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" and "readability." The authors refine an existing classification of prerequisite skills based on psychological research (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and linguistic features for readability (Vajjala & Meurers). They annotate six RC datasets with these metrics to analyze the relationship between question difficulty and text readability. The key finding is that there is only a weak correlation between these two aspects, challenging the assumption that harder-to-read texts would have harder questions.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Annotation of six RC datasets with refined metrics**: The authors provide a detailed, publicly available annotation of six RC datasets using their proposed metrics, which is a valuable resource for the community.
2. **Empirical demonstration of weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability**: The paper presents evidence that the difficulty of RC questions (measured by prerequisite skills required) does not strongly correlate with text readability, which is an important finding for dataset construction.
3. **Refinement of prerequisite skills classification**: The authors improve upon previous work by organizing knowledge reasoning skills based on psychological and NLP research, creating a more systematic framework for RC analysis.

## Strengths
1. **High-quality dataset annotations**: The annotation process was conducted by three annotators with high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), and the authors made the annotation results publicly available. This is a significant contribution to the field as it creates a new resource for researchers.

2. **Theoretical grounding**: The metrics are well-grounded in psychological research (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP research (Dagan, LoBue & Yates), providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed metrics.

3. **Important empirical finding**: The demonstration of a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability is a valuable empirical contribution that challenges common assumptions in the RC field. This finding has clear implications for dataset construction and evaluation.

4. **Clear methodology**: The paper provides a detailed description of the annotation procedure, including how they handled multiple-choice questions and nonsense questions. The authors also acknowledge limitations of their approach (e.g., inability to evaluate competence of selecting necessary sentences).

5. **Practical implications for system development**: The paper suggests a curriculum learning approach for RC system development based on these metrics, which could guide future work in a practical way.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of empirical validation against established baselines**: The paper is purely descriptive and analytical. It doesn't present any new methods or models that can be evaluated against established baselines. The authors claim their metrics can guide dataset construction and system development, but they provide no evidence that using these metrics actually leads to better systems. This is a critical weakness for an ACL paper, which should demonstrate methodological contributions with empirical validation.

2. **Limited scope of datasets analyzed**: The authors only annotate six RC datasets. They exclude several well-known datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) without providing sufficient justification. The results might not be generalizable to the broader RC landscape, limiting the paper's impact.

3. **Theoretical claims without sufficient evidence**: The paper claims their refined classification of prerequisite skills is "comprehensive but not overly specific," but doesn't provide evidence that it's better than previous classifications for predicting system performance. The paper doesn't compare their classification with existing ones in terms of utility for RC tasks.

4. **Lack of connection to actual system performance**: The paper analyzes datasets but doesn't show how these metrics correlate with actual system performance. For example, they don't demonstrate that systems performing well on datasets requiring more prerequisite skills are better at those specific skills. Without this, the practical utility of their metrics remains unclear.

5. **Overstated practical utility**: The paper suggests that their metrics can guide the "step-by-step construction of better RC datasets" and "a method to improve RC systems," but provides no evidence that these claims are valid. This is a significant overstatement for a paper that doesn't present any empirical validation.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you plan to validate that your metrics actually lead to better RC systems? Have you tested whether systems trained on datasets annotated with your metrics perform better on the target tasks?

2. Why did you exclude well-known RC datasets like CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, and LAMBADA from your analysis? What makes the six datasets you chose representative of the broader RC landscape?

3. How do your metrics differ from existing approaches to RC dataset analysis (e.g., question type analysis, answer type analysis)? What specific advantages do they offer over these established methods?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents an interesting descriptive analysis of RC datasets using metrics grounded in psychological research. The dataset annotations are valuable and well-executed, and the finding of a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability is significant. However, as a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's claims about practical utility unsupported by empirical evidence.

The paper lacks the methodological rigor expected at ACL, as it doesn't present any new methods that can be validated against established baselines. The authors make strong claims about how their metrics can guide dataset construction and system development, but provide no evidence to support these claims. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could demonstrate how their metrics lead to better RC systems through empirical validation.

While the dataset annotations are a valuable contribution, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its broader claims about improving RC systems. As a result, I recommend rejection for lack of methodological contribution and insufficient empirical validation. The paper would be more suitable for a venue focused on dataset creation rather than methodological innovation.

**Recommendation: Reject**
