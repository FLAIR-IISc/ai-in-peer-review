

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two new evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" and "readability." The authors claim these metrics can help analyze both the quality of RC datasets and the performance of RC systems. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and report a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability. The paper builds on previous work by Sugawara et al. (2017) and draws on psychological theories of text comprehension.

## Main Contributions (As I See Them)
1. **Annotation of six RC datasets with a detailed skill classification** (though the classification itself is not novel)
2. **Demonstration of a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability** (a descriptive finding, not a methodological contribution)
3. **Organization of knowledge reasoning skills in terms of entailment phenomena and human text comprehension** (a reorganization of existing concepts, not a new theoretical framework)

## Strengths

1. **Significant annotation effort**: The paper presents a substantial amount of annotation work across six datasets with good inter-annotator agreement (90.1%). This is a valuable resource for the community, and the authors have made the results publicly available.

2. **Important problem identification**: The paper correctly identifies a real problem in the RC field: the lack of detailed metrics beyond superficial categories like question types. The authors demonstrate that readability of text does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty, which is a useful insight.

3. **Practical framework for dataset construction**: The paper provides a useful framework for evaluating RC datasets, which could help researchers design better datasets. The suggestion to use these metrics for curriculum learning (Section 6) is a practical application with potential value.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: The core contribution is not novel. The authors claim to "revise" previous classifications of prerequisite skills, but they are simply reorganizing existing concepts from psychology literature (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). The paper fails to demonstrate how their revised classification is substantially better or more rigorous than previous work. The "new" skills (like Bridging and Elaboration) are directly derived from existing psychological literature without new theoretical insights.

2. **No validation against RC system performance**: The paper makes a strong claim about the weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability, but crucially lacks validation against actual RC system performance. The authors don't show how these metrics would help improve RC systems or how they correlate with system performance. Without this, the metrics remain purely descriptive rather than useful for system development.

3. **Insufficient evidence for claimed contributions**: The paper claims their metrics "will help researchers to develop a method for the step-by-step construction of better RC datasets," but provides no evidence that this is actually the case. The paper doesn't demonstrate how these metrics would be used in practice to improve system performance or dataset quality. The discussion section is largely speculative without empirical grounding.

4. **Failure to address limitations**: The authors acknowledge their methodology "could not evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences," but they don't discuss how this limitation affects the validity of their metrics for evaluating RC systems. They also don't compare their metrics with established RC evaluation metrics or demonstrate how their metrics would outperform existing approaches.

5. **Overstated claims about contribution**: The paper overstates the novelty of its contributions. The title claims "Prerequisite Skills and Readability" as evaluation metrics, but these are not new metrics in the sense of being novel evaluation methodologies. The paper presents a descriptive analysis rather than a methodological advancement that would help the community build better RC systems.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your metrics specifically improve upon existing RC evaluation metrics (e.g., SQuAD leaderboard metrics) in terms of guiding system development? Please provide concrete examples where these metrics would lead to different system development decisions compared to current metrics.

2. The paper claims to have "revised the previous classification of prerequisite skills," but doesn't provide evidence that this revision is superior. How did you validate that your classification better reflects actual RC difficulty compared to previous classifications?

3. You state that "RC datasets that are difficult to read are not necessary difficult to answer." What evidence do you have that this insight would help RC researchers build better systems? Without showing how this insight leads to improved system performance, it remains a descriptive finding rather than a useful contribution.

## Final Recommendation

Reject

This paper presents a descriptive analysis of RC datasets using metrics derived from psychological literature, but fails to demonstrate how these metrics would meaningfully advance the field of RC. The core methodology lacks novelty, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these metrics would actually help researchers build better RC systems. As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and rigorous validation of new claims, I find the paper's contributions overstated and lacking in the evidence required for acceptance at ACL. The annotation effort is valuable, but it's not enough to compensate for the lack of methodological novelty and failure to validate the utility of the proposed metrics against system performance.
