

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" (refined from previous work) and "readability." The authors argue that current RC datasets lack meaningful metrics beyond superficial categories like question type, making it difficult to assess system performance accurately. They refine a taxonomy of prerequisite skills based on psychological research (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment, and apply it to annotate six existing RC datasets. Their key finding is that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability, challenging the assumption that easier-to-read texts lead to easier RC tasks.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Theoretical refinement of prerequisite skills taxonomy**: The paper provides a more comprehensive and theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, drawing on psychological research on text comprehension and textual entailment. This moves beyond ad-hoc categorizations to a taxonomy with clear theoretical foundations.

2. **Systematic annotation of six RC datasets**: The authors provide the first systematic analysis of multiple RC datasets using these refined metrics, revealing important characteristics and patterns across datasets that have previously gone unnoticed.

3. **Key insight on the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty**: The finding that text readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated (with Pearson coefficients ranging from 0.025 to 0.416) is a significant contribution that challenges common assumptions in RC dataset design and evaluation.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical grounding**: The paper is exceptionally well-grounded in psychological research on text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed metrics. The authors clearly explain how their taxonomy relates to established concepts like "retrieved vs. generated" inferences and "bridging vs. elaboration" inferences. This theoretical foundation elevates the paper beyond ad-hoc categorizations and provides a principled approach to RC evaluation.

2. **Comprehensive and systematic analysis**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of six different RC datasets using the proposed metrics. The annotation procedure (with 90.1% inter-annotator agreement) is well-described, and the results are presented with clear tables and figures. The analysis of both the frequency of skills required and their correlation with readability metrics provides valuable insights that will help researchers understand the characteristics of different RC datasets.

3. **Practical implications for dataset construction and system development**: The paper offers concrete suggestions for how the metrics can be used to guide the development of better RC systems through curriculum learning. The authors propose a step-by-step development approach where systems are first trained on easy-to-read and easy-to-answer datasets, then progress to more challenging ones. This practical guidance is valuable for the RC community and has the potential to improve system development practices.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of empirical validation for system improvement**: While the paper discusses how the metrics could be used for system development, it provides no empirical evidence that using these metrics actually leads to better RC systems. The claim that "RC systems can be built to solve an easy-to-read and easy-to-answer dataset, then an easy-to-read and difficult-to-answer dataset, and finally a difficult-to-read and difficult-to-answer dataset" is theoretical but not validated. Without demonstrating that this approach improves system performance, this contribution remains speculative.

2. **Limited scope of dataset analysis**: The paper only analyzes six datasets, which is reasonable for a first study, but the exclusion of some major datasets (like CNN/Daily Mail) due to quality concerns may bias the analysis. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why these six datasets are representative of the broader landscape of RC datasets. Additionally, the authors don't provide a clear method for determining which datasets to include in such analyses, which limits the generalizability of their findings.

3. **Ambiguity in "nonsense" question classification**: The paper identifies that some datasets have a high rate of "nonsense" questions (e.g., MS MARCO and Who-did-What), but it doesn't provide a clear methodology for distinguishing "nonsense" from "solvable" questions. The authors note that "there were questions for which there were disagreements regarding the decision of nonsense," which raises concerns about the reliability of their analysis. Without a clear definition and consistent method for identifying nonsense questions, this aspect of the analysis is problematic.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you validate that the proposed metrics lead to better RC systems in practice? Could you provide a case study or a small-scale experiment showing how using these metrics for curriculum learning improves system performance?

2. The paper acknowledges that their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences to answer questions. How would you extend the methodology to address this limitation, which is a critical aspect of RC?

3. The paper states that "text readability does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" but doesn't provide a strong theoretical explanation for why this is the case. Could you elaborate on the psychological or linguistic reasons behind this weak correlation, especially given that readability metrics are designed for human readers?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the RC field by providing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets. The refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills is well-justified and could become a standard for dataset analysis. The key finding about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is significant and will likely influence future RC dataset design.

However, the lack of empirical validation for the practical implications of the proposed metrics is a major limitation. While the theoretical insights are strong, the paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating how these metrics can actually improve RC systems.

**Recommendation: Accept with Minor Revisions**

The paper is strong in its theoretical contributions and systematic analysis, but needs to address the lack of empirical validation for the proposed system development approach and clarify the methodology for handling "nonsense" questions. With these revisions, the paper would make a significant contribution to the RC field.
