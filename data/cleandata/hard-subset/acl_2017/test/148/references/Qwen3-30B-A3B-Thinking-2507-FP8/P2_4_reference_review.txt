

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets by introducing two complementary evaluation metrics: "prerequisite skills" (measuring the cognitive skills required to answer questions) and "readability" (measuring text complexity). The authors refine a previous classification of prerequisite skills using insights from psychological theories of text comprehension and textual entailment research. They annotate six major RC datasets with these metrics and demonstrate a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, challenging the assumption that difficult texts necessarily lead to difficult questions.

## Main Contributions
1. A theoretically grounded classification of prerequisite skills for RC, organized around textual entailment and human text comprehension research
2. Systematic analysis of six RC datasets showing weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability
3. Publicly available annotations of these datasets for future research

## Strengths

### 1. Theoretical Foundation and Conceptual Clarity
The paper's most significant strength is its strong theoretical foundation. The authors build upon established psychological frameworks (Kintsch's Construction-Integration model and McNamara & Magliano's text comprehension theory) and textual entailment research to develop a comprehensive yet practical classification of prerequisite skills. This is a major improvement over previous work that used ad-hoc or weakly justified categorizations (e.g., Sugawara et al., 2017). The distinction between bridging and elaboration inferences is particularly well-justified and useful for understanding different types of reasoning required in RC.

### 2. Empirical Demonstration of Key Insight
The paper makes a crucial empirical contribution by demonstrating that the difficulty of questions and the readability of text are largely independent dimensions of RC datasets. The authors show that MCTest (with easy-to-read narrative text) requires complex reasoning skills, while SQuAD (with difficult-to-read Wikipedia text) is relatively easy to answer. This challenges the common assumption that difficult texts automatically lead to difficult questions and has significant implications for dataset design and system evaluation. Their quantitative analysis (Figures 3-4, Table 5) clearly supports this claim.

### 3. Practical Value and Openness
The authors make their annotations publicly available, which is a substantial contribution to the field. This resource will enable future researchers to evaluate new RC datasets against these metrics and to develop systems that target specific types of reasoning skills. The paper's practical guidance on using these metrics for curriculum learning (Section 6) provides a concrete path for applying these insights to system development.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Limited Dataset Coverage and Potential Selection Bias
The authors exclude several prominent datasets from their analysis (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA), citing reasons such as "anonymized content" or "not suitable for human reading." While they provide justification, this exclusion limits the generalizability of their findings. Notably, CNN/Daily Mail is one of the largest RC datasets and has known quality issues (Chen et al., 2016), so excluding it may bias their conclusions about dataset quality. A more comprehensive analysis would have included these datasets to strengthen their argument about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

### 2. Ambiguity in Skill Definitions
While the authors provide detailed definitions for most prerequisite skills, some remain ambiguous or potentially overlapping. "Meta-knowledge" (skill 11) is particularly vague—it seems to capture a range of knowledge types that could be incorporated into other skills like "Elaboration." Similarly, the distinction between "Schematic clause relation" and "Punctuation" is not entirely clear. These ambiguities could lead to inconsistencies in annotation across different researchers.

### 3. Insufficient Discussion of Practical Implementation
The paper identifies the potential for using their metrics in curriculum learning (Section 6), but fails to provide concrete examples or detailed guidance on how to implement this. For instance, how would one systematically select datasets at each "difficulty level" along the two dimensions? How would this differ from existing curriculum learning approaches? Without more specific guidance, the practical utility of their framework remains somewhat theoretical.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper notes that "QA4MRE had the highest scores for frequencies among the datasets" (Table 2), but it's unclear why this is the case. Could you elaborate on what specific characteristics of QA4MRE's technical documents lead to this result?

2. The exclusion of CNN/Daily Mail is justified by the authors' concern about its anonymized content, but Chen et al. (2016) identified quality issues (25% unsolvable questions) that are directly relevant to the paper's focus. How might including CNN/Daily Mail have affected your conclusions about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty?

3. In Table 3, the average number of required skills for MS MARCO is 1.19, yet Table 2 shows 14% of questions were marked as "nonsense." How do you reconcile these numbers, and what does this suggest about the relationship between question quality and skill requirements?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets that addresses a critical gap in the field. The weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability is a significant insight with clear implications for dataset construction and system evaluation. While the paper has some limitations in dataset coverage and skill definition clarity, these do not undermine the core contribution. The publicly available annotations are particularly valuable for the community.

I recommend acceptance. The paper provides a much-needed framework for understanding the qualitative features of RC datasets, and its findings will guide future dataset construction and system development in the field. The theoretical grounding, empirical analysis, and practical openness make this a strong contribution to the field of reading comprehension evaluation.
