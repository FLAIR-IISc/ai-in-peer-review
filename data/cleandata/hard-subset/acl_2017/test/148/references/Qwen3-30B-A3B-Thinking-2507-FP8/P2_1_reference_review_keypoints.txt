- Summary: Proposes a framework of "prerequisite skills" and "readability" metrics to evaluate RC datasets and finds weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.  
- Contribution 1: Refines a theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills drawing on psychological research and textual entailment.  
- Contribution 2: Systematically annotates and analyzes six RC datasets using the refined metrics.  
- Contribution 3: Shows weak correlation between readability and question difficulty (Pearson 0.025–0.416).  
- Strength 1: Strong theoretical grounding in text comprehension literature (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano).  
- Strength 2: Comprehensive, systematic analysis with clear tables/figures and high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%).  
- Strength 3: Practical implications suggested for dataset construction and curriculum-based system development.  
- Weakness 1: No empirical validation that using these metrics improves RC system performance—claims are speculative.  
- Weakness 2: Limited dataset scope (only six datasets) and exclusion of major datasets may reduce generalizability.  
- Weakness 3: Ambiguity and lack of clear methodology for classifying "nonsense" vs. solvable questions, with annotator disagreements.  
- Question 1: How would you empirically validate that these metrics improve RC systems (e.g., via a case study or experiment)?  
- Question 2: How can the methodology be extended to evaluate sentence-selection competence, a key RC component?  
- Question 3: Can you provide a stronger theoretical explanation for why readability metrics weakly correlate with question difficulty?  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—solid theoretical and analytical contribution but needs empirical validation and clearer nonsense-question methodology.
