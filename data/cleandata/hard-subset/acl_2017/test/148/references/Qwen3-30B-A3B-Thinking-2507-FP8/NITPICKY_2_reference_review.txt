

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of Paper
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" and "readability." The authors claim these metrics can help evaluate the quality of RC datasets and guide the development of better RC systems. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics to analyze the relationship between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The authors introduce a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, building on previous work by Sugawara et al.
2. They annotate six RC datasets with these metrics and make the results publicly available.
3. They demonstrate a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability.

## Strengths

1. **Identifying a meaningful gap**: The paper correctly identifies an important gap in RC evaluation - the lack of metrics that can distinguish between question difficulty and text readability. The examples in Figure 1 effectively illustrate this problem, showing how readability of text doesn't necessarily correlate with question difficulty.

2. **Prerequisite skills taxonomy**: The authors' refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy (Section 3.1) is a valuable contribution that builds on previous work in a more theoretically grounded way than Sugawara et al. (2017). The distinction between bridging and elaboration inferences is particularly well-motivated by psychological research.

3. **Publicly available dataset annotations**: Making the annotation results publicly available is a significant contribution to the field that will be useful for future research.

## Weaknesses

1. **Fundamental methodological contradiction**: The paper claims their methodology evaluates "the understanding of contextual entailments in a broader sense for RC" (Section 1), but then explicitly states "our methodology could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read and answer candidates from the context" (Section 1). This is a serious contradiction - if the methodology doesn't evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences, how can it claim to evaluate "understanding of contextual entailments"? This undermines the entire premise of the paper.

2. **Poorly justified dataset exclusions**: The paper excludes several datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) from annotation with weak justifications:
   - CNN/Daily Mail: "anonymized and contains some errors" (Chen et al., 2016) - but the paper doesn't provide evidence that these errors prevent meaningful annotation.
   - CBTest: "for language modeling tasks rather than RC task" - without providing evidence or justification.
   - LAMBADA: "texts are formatted for machine reading, and all tokens are lowercased" - this is a questionable justification as proper nouns aren't always necessary for inference.

3. **Statistical deficiencies in correlation analysis**: The paper reports Pearson's correlation coefficients between prerequisite skills and readability metrics (Table 5), but fails to provide p-values or statistical significance testing. A correlation of 0.416 could be statistically significant with a large enough sample size, making the claim of "weak correlation" potentially misleading without statistical context.

4. **Formatting and presentation inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous formatting issues that undermine its credibility:
   - Table 2 and Table 3 percentages don't add up correctly (e.g., Table 2 for QA4MRE sums to 295.0 instead of 100%).
   - Inconsistent terminology ("prerequisite skills" vs "skills," "knowledge reasoning" vs "knowledge reasoning skills").
   - Figure 2 references a Wikipedia page (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_Word_List) as if it were a formal citation.
   - Inconsistent citation style (some years in parentheses, others outside).
   - The paper states "inter-annotator agreement was 90.1% for 62 randomly sampled questions" but doesn't specify what metric was used (Cohen's kappa, Fleiss' kappa, etc.).

5. **Overstated contributions**: The paper claims "our annotation results will help researchers to develop a method for the step-by-step construction of better RC datasets and a method to improve RC systems." This is a significant claim that isn't adequately supported by the limited analysis provided (only six datasets, no evaluation of how the metrics would actually guide system development).

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify how your methodology evaluates "understanding of contextual entailments" when you explicitly state it cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences? This contradiction is fundamental to your approach.

2. For the excluded datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA), can you provide specific evidence that the quality issues or task formulation prevented meaningful annotation, rather than simply excluding them because they didn't fit your preconceived expectations?

3. Why didn't you provide statistical significance for your correlation analysis (Table 5)? How many questions were used to calculate each correlation coefficient, and what were the p-values?

4. In Table 2, the percentages for "Skills" and "Nonsense" don't sum to 100%. For example, for QA4MRE: 11.0+4.0+32.0+15.0+7.0+1.0+26.0+13.0+69.0+60.0+1.0+52.0+34.0+10.0 = 285.0. How should these values be interpreted?

5. The paper states that "our methodology could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read" but then in Section 6, Table 6 reports "average number and distance of sentences that need to be read." How does this analysis align with your methodology description?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection.

While the paper identifies a meaningful problem (the lack of metrics to distinguish between question difficulty and text readability), the fundamental methodological contradiction, statistical deficiencies, and numerous formatting inconsistencies significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The authors' claims about the value of their work are overstated relative to the actual analysis provided. The paper would require major revisions to address these critical issues before it could be considered for publication.
