

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel conceptual framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets through two complementary classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" (a refined classification of skills needed to answer questions) and "readability" (metrics for text complexity). The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics, revealing that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by the number of prerequisite skills required) and text readability. This challenges the common assumption that more readable texts would produce more difficult questions.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A fundamentally new conceptual framework for evaluating RC datasets that moves beyond superficial question-type categorizations to analyze the actual cognitive demands of questions and text complexity.
2. A systematic refinement of prerequisite skills classification, grounded in textual entailment research and human text comprehension psychology, which provides a more theoretically sound basis than previous work.
3. Empirical evidence demonstrating the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, revealing a significant gap in how RC datasets have been traditionally evaluated and designed.

## Strengths
1. **High Novelty and Conceptual Boldness**: This paper introduces a genuinely new perspective on evaluating RC datasets. The authors don't just propose another metric—they challenge the foundational assumption that readability correlates with question difficulty, which has been implicitly accepted in the field for years. This conceptual shift is exactly the kind of bold approach I value as a reviewer.

2. **Significant Insight with Practical Implications**: The finding that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated (with MCTest as a prime example—easy-to-read children's stories with difficult questions) is highly significant. It directly addresses a critical problem in the field: current RC datasets are often evaluated based on superficial categories, making it difficult to assess system capabilities accurately. This insight will reshape how researchers design and evaluate future RC datasets.

3. **Cross-Disciplinary Inspiration**: The paper successfully bridges psychology research (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) with NLP, demonstrating how insights from human text comprehension can inform better evaluation metrics for RC systems. This interdisciplinary approach is rare and valuable in our field.

4. **Actionable Resource for the Community**: The authors make their annotated datasets publicly available, providing a valuable resource for researchers to develop better RC systems and datasets. This practical contribution makes the paper immediately useful to the community.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope of Annotation**: While the six datasets annotated provide valuable insights, the scope is somewhat limited. The authors acknowledge this, noting they excluded datasets like CNN/Daily Mail due to "anonymization and errors," but the generalizability of their findings to the broader RC landscape could be strengthened with more comprehensive annotation.

2. **Methodological Subjectivity**: The annotation process relies on human judgment, which introduces subjectivity. While the inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is reasonable, the paper doesn't provide sufficient detail on how disagreements were resolved or how the subjectivity might affect the metrics' reliability.

3. **Limited Demonstration of System Impact**: While the paper describes how these metrics could be used to guide system development (e.g., curriculum learning), it doesn't provide concrete examples of how these metrics would directly improve RC systems. The connection between the evaluation framework and actual system improvements needs more elaboration.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you plan to address the limitation of only annotating six datasets? Could you provide a roadmap for scaling this annotation approach to cover more RC datasets, including those not currently well-annotated?

2. The paper mentions "nonsense" questions in some datasets (like MS MARCO). How do you plan to distinguish between genuinely nonsensical questions and questions that simply require very complex reasoning that might be beyond current systems?

3. The paper states that the methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read." How could this limitation be addressed in future work, perhaps through a combined metric that includes both the comprehension of contextual entailments and the selection of relevant sentences?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to the RC field by challenging a fundamental assumption and introducing a novel framework for dataset evaluation. The insight that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated is both surprising and highly important for the field. While the methodology has some limitations, the novelty and conceptual significance of the work far outweigh these concerns.

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper offers a fresh perspective on how we evaluate RC datasets, which is a critical gap in the field. The authors have made a clear and impactful contribution to our understanding of what makes RC questions difficult, and their framework provides a valuable foundation for future research on dataset design and system development. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that advances the field in meaningful ways.
