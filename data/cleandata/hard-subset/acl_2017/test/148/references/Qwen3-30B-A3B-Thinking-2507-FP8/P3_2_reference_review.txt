

# Peer Review for ACL

## Summary
This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two complementary classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" (based on human text comprehension research) and "readability" (based on linguistic features). The authors refine and extend previous work on prerequisite skills, create a comprehensive taxonomy of 13 skills, and annotate six major RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these metrics. Their key finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the difficulty of questions (measured by prerequisite skills) and the readability of the text, which challenges common assumptions in the field.

## Main Contributions
1. A theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, refined using insights from cognitive psychology and textual entailment research
2. A systematic analysis of six major RC datasets along two dimensions (question difficulty and text readability)
3. The public release of annotated dataset metrics, providing a valuable resource for the RC community

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The authors have made a significant contribution by grounding their metrics in established cognitive science research (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) rather than relying solely on ad-hoc classification. Their refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy, particularly the distinction between bridging and elaboration inferences, is well-justified and addresses previous limitations in Sugawara et al.'s work. This theoretical grounding is a major strength that elevates the work beyond typical NLP evaluation metrics.

2. **Practical insights for dataset construction**: The paper provides concrete evidence that current RC datasets vary significantly along the two dimensions they identify. The finding that MCTest is "easy to read but difficult to answer" is particularly valuable, as it challenges the common assumption that text difficulty directly correlates with question difficulty. This insight can guide future dataset construction efforts to better target specific comprehension skills.

3. **Comprehensive annotation framework**: The authors' careful refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy (13 skills total) demonstrates significant effort to create a nuanced, comprehensive, and theoretically sound framework. Their decision to exclude ambiguous categories (like "scheme" in figures of speech) and merge related concepts (e.g., understanding of constructions into idioms) shows thoughtful methodology. The inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% further validates their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of connection to system development**: While the authors claim their metrics can guide system development, they fail to provide concrete examples of how these metrics would be used in practice. The brief mention of "curriculum learning" is not developed into a meaningful methodological contribution. Without demonstrating how these metrics would specifically improve system development (e.g., through targeted model training or error analysis), the practical utility of the work remains unclear.

2. **Methodological limitations in annotation scope**: The exclusion of major datasets like CNN/Daily Mail (despite its popularity) and LAMBADA (despite its relevance to RC) significantly limits the generalizability of their findings. The authors provide reasons for exclusion, but this creates a gap in understanding whether their framework would apply to datasets with different characteristics. The paper would be substantially stronger if they could address how their metrics would apply to these excluded datasets.

3. **Unclear definition of "nonsense" questions**: The paper acknowledges that annotators disagreed about what constitutes a "nonsense" question (e.g., questions solvable with external knowledge without context), but does not provide a clear definition or resolution. This is a critical flaw because the "nonsense" category appears in multiple datasets (MS MARCO, Who-did-What), and without a clear criterion, the reliability of their annotations is questionable. The authors' admission that their methodology "failed to specify" "no answer" questions further undermines confidence in their framework.

4. **No validation against system performance**: The paper analyzes the datasets themselves but does not connect these metrics to actual system performance. For example, they don't show whether models that perform well on datasets with high prerequisite skills scores demonstrate better performance on the specific skills. Without this connection, it's difficult to assess whether the metrics are actually useful for evaluating RC systems, rather than just describing dataset properties.

5. **Insufficient treatment of the sentence selection challenge**: The authors acknowledge their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences to answer questions, which is a core component of RC. Their brief analysis of sentence distance (Table 6) is superficial and doesn't address the complexity of this selection process. This limitation is significant because many RC systems fail precisely on this task.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the difficulty of their questions." However, for a given text, the difficulty of a question should depend on how well the text supports the question. Could you elaborate on this relationship and how your metrics capture it? For instance, why would a text with high readability (e.g., simple vocabulary) still require difficult questions?

2. The authors claim that "MCTest is a good example of an RC dataset that is easy to read but difficult to answer." However, MCTest is designed for children, so the text difficulty is intentionally low. How do you reconcile this with the fact that the dataset is meant to be at a child's reading level? Is the difficulty primarily due to the complexity of the comprehension skills required, rather than the text difficulty?

3. The paper mentions that MS MARCO is "relatively easy" in terms of prerequisite skills but contains "nonsense questions." How do you explain the relationship between these two properties? If questions are nonsense (not answerable from context), shouldn't they require fewer prerequisite skills, not more?

4. In Table 2, Who-did-What has the highest score for ellipsis (16.0%). The authors attribute this to the dataset's automatic generation method. However, if questions are automatically generated from articles not used as context, wouldn't this lead to more ellipsis (as context is missing), rather than less? Could you clarify this relationship?

5. The authors state that their methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read." Given that this is a core challenge in RC, how would you extend your framework to address this limitation, and what additional metrics would be needed?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets. The refinement of prerequisite skills taxonomy and the empirical analysis of six major datasets are significant strengths. However, the paper's practical utility is limited by the lack of connection to system development and the methodological limitations in annotation scope and definition of "nonsense" questions.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept**

The paper's theoretical contribution and careful annotation framework are strong enough to warrant acceptance, but the limitations in practical application and methodology need to be addressed in revisions. The authors should clarify the "nonsense" definition, provide concrete examples of how their metrics would guide system development, and address the limitations in their methodology regarding sentence selection. With these revisions, the paper would make a much stronger contribution to the field.
