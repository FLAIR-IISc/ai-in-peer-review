- Proposes a framework with two complementary metrics—prerequisite skills and readability—to evaluate RC datasets, annotates six datasets, and finds weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.
- Refines prerequisite-skill taxonomy grounded in text comprehension and textual entailment theories.
- Systematically annotates six major RC datasets with the new metrics to reveal dataset characteristics.
- Provides empirical evidence that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated, challenging common dataset-design assumptions.
- Addresses a critical evaluation gap by moving beyond superficial dataset metrics (question/answer types) to cognitive demands.
- Establishes a strong theoretical foundation by integrating psychology and entailment research, clarifying inference distinctions like bridging vs. elaboration.
- Presents valuable dataset-level empirical findings (e.g., MCTest vs. SQuAD contrasts) that highlight design issues.
- Methodologically cannot evaluate sentence-selection competence, a key RC capability, and only offers limited analysis of sentence distance.
- Fails to provide a systematic method for identifying or filtering high rates of nonsense/unsolvable questions in some datasets.
- Lacks concrete, actionable guidance for using the metrics to drive system development (e.g., curriculum design specifics).
- Annotation process has subjectivity concerns—90.1% agreement but unresolved disagreements on "nonsense" labels and unclear resolution thresholds.
- Asks authors to extend the framework to evaluate sentence-selection competence, recommend handling datasets lacking question-quality meta-data, and give concrete examples of how metrics guide model training.
- Overall: a strong theoretical contribution with valuable annotations and findings, but practical impact limited by methodological and guidance shortcomings; recommendation: Weak Accept.
