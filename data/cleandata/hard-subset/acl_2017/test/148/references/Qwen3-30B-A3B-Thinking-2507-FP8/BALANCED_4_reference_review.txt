

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets based on two classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" (which measure the cognitive skills required to answer questions) and "readability" (which measures the linguistic complexity of the text). The authors refine the prerequisite skills classification from previous work, organizing it around textual entailment and human text comprehension theories. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and analyze the relationship between the two classes of metrics.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A refined classification of prerequisite skills for reading comprehension, organized based on textual entailment and human text comprehension theories, which provides a more theoretically grounded framework than previous work.
2. A systematic annotation of six RC datasets using these metrics, making the results publicly available and enabling comparative analysis of RC datasets.
3. The key empirical finding that there is only a weak correlation between the difficulty of questions (measured by prerequisite skills) and the readability of context texts in RC datasets.

## Strengths
1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper's theoretical grounding in psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (textual entailment research) is a significant strength. The authors effectively leverage insights from multiple disciplines to develop their framework, providing a more rigorous basis than previous work that often lacked theoretical justification. The connection to psychological theories of text comprehension is particularly compelling.

2. **Systematic and reliable annotation**: The paper describes a clear annotation methodology with high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%). The approach of having annotators select sentences needed for answering questions and then annotate them with prerequisite skills is well-justified and provides a reliable basis for their analysis. The detailed specification of the 13 prerequisite skills (with clear distinctions between bridging and elaboration inferences) represents a significant contribution to the field.

3. **Important empirical finding**: The key finding that there's a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability is highly significant. This challenges a common assumption in RC research and has important implications for dataset construction and system evaluation. The examples provided (e.g., MCTest being easy to read but difficult to answer) make this finding concrete and impactful. The analysis of why this weak correlation exists (e.g., MCTest's narrative format) adds valuable depth to the discussion.

4. **Practical utility for dataset construction**: The paper provides clear guidance for how the metrics can be used in practice, including a discussion of using them in a curriculum learning framework for RC system development. The "step-by-step construction" approach for developing better RC datasets is particularly valuable and could guide future dataset creation efforts.

5. **Publicly available data**: The authors plan to make their annotation results publicly available, which will be a valuable resource for the community and enable future research. This commitment to reproducibility is commendable.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited demonstration of practical utility**: While the paper discusses how the metrics could be used for dataset construction and system development, it doesn't provide concrete evidence of how these metrics would actually improve RC systems. There's no analysis of how models perform on different subsets of the data based on these metrics, which would strengthen the argument for their utility. For example, it would be valuable to see if models perform better or worse on questions requiring specific skills.

2. **Lack of comparison to existing metrics**: The paper doesn't adequately compare the proposed metrics to existing ones (e.g., question types, answer types) in terms of their usefulness for dataset analysis or system development. A comparison would help establish the added value of the proposed metrics. For instance, how do these metrics complement or improve upon existing categorizations?

3. **Insufficient discussion of methodological limitations**: While the paper acknowledges limitations in their methodology (e.g., inability to evaluate "competence of selecting necessary sentences"), it doesn't thoroughly explore how these limitations affect their results or what they mean for the validity of their metrics. The paper states this limitation but doesn't discuss how it might impact the interpretation of their findings or how future work could address it.

4. **Incomplete justification for dataset selection**: The paper excludes some datasets (like CNN/Daily Mail) but doesn't provide a strong theoretical justification for why these datasets were excluded, which might have affected the representativeness of their analysis. The exclusion of CNN/Daily Mail, a widely used RC dataset, is particularly problematic without clear justification. The authors state it contains errors, but they don't quantify how these errors might affect their analysis.

5. **Lack of concrete examples**: While the paper describes the metrics and provides some examples (like Figure 1), it could benefit from more concrete examples of how the metrics would be used to analyze specific questions or datasets. For instance, a detailed walkthrough of a single question from one dataset showing how the metrics would be applied would make the framework more tangible.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states that "our methodology could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read and answer candidates from the context." How would you propose to address this limitation in future work, and what would be the impact on your current analysis?

2. The paper claims a weak correlation between prerequisite skills and readability metrics (Pearson's coefficients ranging from 0.025 to 0.416), but these correlations are quite low. Could you elaborate on why these correlations are so low and what this means for RC dataset design? How might this inform the creation of more balanced datasets?

3. The paper excludes CNN/Daily Mail and CBTest from analysis. Could you provide more justification for why these datasets were excluded, especially given that CNN/Daily Mail is a widely used RC dataset? How might the exclusion of these datasets affect the generalizability of your findings?

4. The paper mentions that "QA4MRE had the highest scores for frequencies among the datasets" but doesn't provide a clear explanation for why this is the case. Could you elaborate on the characteristics of QA4MRE that lead to this result, and how this might inform future dataset construction?

## Additional Comments
- The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of how the proposed metrics relate to existing metrics in the field, and what specific advantages they offer over current approaches.
- The discussion of "nonsense" questions (which is a significant portion of some datasets) could be expanded to discuss how this impacts the reliability of RC evaluation and how future datasets might better address this issue.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed metrics, particularly regarding their applicability to different types of RC tasks (e.g., multiple-choice vs. open-ended questions).

## Recommendation
I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets, with a significant empirical finding that challenges common assumptions in the field. The methodology is sound, the annotation is reliable, and the potential impact on dataset construction and system development is substantial.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses, particularly in not demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed metrics through concrete examples or model performance analysis. The lack of comparison to existing metrics also weakens the argument for the added value of this work.

The authors should address the questions above in their response, particularly regarding the practical utility of their metrics and the justification for excluding certain datasets. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
