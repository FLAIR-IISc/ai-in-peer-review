

# Review for ACL Submission

## Summary
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors refine Sugawara et al.'s (2017) classification of prerequisite skills, organizing knowledge reasoning based on textual entailment phenomena and human text comprehension research. They also adopt readability metrics from Vajjala and Meurers (2012). The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and analyze the relationship between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. A refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC, organized in terms of entailment phenomena and human text comprehension.
2. Demonstration that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability in RC datasets.
3. Annotation of six RC datasets with these metrics, making the results publicly available.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical foundation for evaluation metrics**: The paper provides a strong theoretical basis for their evaluation framework, drawing on established psychological research (McNamara & Magliano, 2009; Kintsch, 1993) and textual entailment literature (Dagan et al., 2013; LoBue & Yates, 2011). This careful grounding in prior research gives credibility to their metric selection and classification.

2. **Significant empirical finding on weak correlation**: The key finding that question difficulty and text readability show only weak correlation (with Pearson's coefficients ranging from 0.025 to 0.416) is well-documented with thorough analysis across six datasets. This challenges the common assumption that difficult-to-read texts necessarily contain difficult questions, and vice versa, providing valuable insights for dataset construction.

3. **Practical resource for the community**: The authors make their annotation results publicly available, which is a significant contribution to the RC community. The specific observations about each dataset (e.g., MCTest being easy to read but difficult to answer) provide actionable insights for dataset designers and system developers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in metric classification**: The authors claim to revise previous work (Sugawara et al., 2017), but the changes to the prerequisite skills classification appear largely terminological or minor reorganization rather than substantial theoretical advancement. The core skills remain largely unchanged, and the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how their revised classification provides significantly better insights than previous approaches.

2. **Lack of connection between metrics and system performance**: While the paper analyzes the datasets themselves, it fails to connect these metrics to how RC systems actually perform on them. Without showing how the metrics relate to system performance (e.g., do systems that perform well on datasets with high prerequisite skills scores also perform well on other metrics?), the practical value of these metrics for system development remains unproven.

3. **Insufficient justification for dataset exclusions**: The paper excludes several RC datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) with relatively brief justifications. For example, they exclude CNN/Daily Mail because it's "anonymized and contains some errors" without providing specific evidence of how these errors affect the annotation process. Similarly, they exclude CBTest as being "for language modeling tasks rather than RC task" without explaining why it wouldn't be suitable for RC evaluation. This exclusion of potentially relevant datasets weakens the generalizability of their findings.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to have revised the previous classification of prerequisite skills, but the changes appear to be mostly terminological. Could the authors provide specific examples of how their revised classification provides new insights compared to Sugawara et al. (2017)?

2. The paper analyzes the relationship between question difficulty and text readability but doesn't analyze how these metrics correlate with actual system performance. Could the authors provide evidence showing how their metrics relate to the performance of existing RC systems?

3. The paper excludes CBTest as being "for language modeling tasks rather than RC task." Could the authors clarify why they believe CBTest wouldn't be suitable for RC evaluation, given that it's commonly used in RC benchmarking?

4. The authors note that their methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read to answer questions." How might this limitation affect the interpretation of their results, and what would be needed to address this limitation in future work?
