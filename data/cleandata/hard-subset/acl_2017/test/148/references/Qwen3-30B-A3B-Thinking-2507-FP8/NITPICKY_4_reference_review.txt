

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" (refining existing categories of reading comprehension skills based on psychological theories) and "readability" (using linguistic features to assess text complexity). The authors annotate six RC datasets with these metrics to analyze the relationship between question difficulty and text readability. The key claim is that there is only a weak correlation between the difficulty of questions and the readability of context texts.

## Main Contributions
1. A refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC based on textual entailment and human text comprehension theories.
2. A methodological framework for evaluating RC datasets using both prerequisite skills and readability metrics.
3. Annotation of six existing RC datasets with these metrics, making the results publicly available.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a genuine gap in the RC field: the lack of metrics beyond simple accuracy to evaluate dataset quality and system performance.
2. The theoretical grounding in psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) provides a solid foundation for the proposed metrics.
3. The annotation of six RC datasets is a useful contribution that could help the community develop better RC systems.

## Weaknesses (Major Issues)

### 1. Inconsistent and Ambiguous Definition of "Nonsense" Questions
The paper states in Section 4.1 that "The annotators were allowed to select nonsense for an unsolvable question to distinguish it from a solvable question that required no skills." However, this definition is inconsistent with the paper's own findings. In Section 5(i), they report "nonsense" scores for MS MARCO (14.0%) and Who-did-What (27.0%), claiming this is due to "automated curation." Yet, they excluded NewsQA from their analysis because it "includes annotations of meta information whether or not a question makes sense (is question bad)" - suggesting they had a clear definition of nonsense questions for NewsQA. This inconsistency undermines the reliability of their annotation procedure. The paper fails to provide a clear definition or guidelines for when to mark a question as "nonsense," making their results questionable.

### 2. Inconsistent Use of Readability Metrics
Section 3.2 states they used "the classification of linguistic features proposed by Vajjala and Meurers (2012)" for readability metrics (Figure 2 lists 10 metrics). However, in Sections 5(iii) and Figures 3-4, they only report Flesch-Kincaid grade levels (FK) as the readability metric. This is a major inconsistency - they claim to use multiple metrics but only report one. The paper fails to explain why they chose to report only one metric or how the other metrics were evaluated. This undermines their claim about the relationship between question difficulty and readability.

### 3. Inconsistent Results Reporting (Table 3)
Table 3 reports "Frequencies (%) of the number of required prerequisite skills for the RC datasets." However, the percentages for most datasets do not sum to 100%. For example, for QA4MRE, the percentages (18.0, 13.0, 20.0, 14.0, 13.0, 10.0, 1.0, 1.0, 3.0) sum to 93.0%, not 100%. This is a significant mathematical error that casts doubt on the reliability of their results and analysis.

### 4. Inadequate Justification for Dataset Selection
The paper excludes several datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) for specific reasons, but the justifications are inconsistent. They exclude CNN/Daily Mail because it "contains some errors" (citing Chen et al., 2016), but they include MS MARCO which they later report has a high "nonsense" rate (14.0%). If MS MARCO has "nonsense" questions, why was it included but CNN/Daily Mail excluded? The paper fails to provide a consistent criterion for dataset inclusion/exclusion.

### 5. Ambiguous Definitions of Prerequisite Skills
Several skills have ambiguous definitions that contradict the literature or lack justification:
- "Object tracking*": The paper claims it's a "renamed version of list/enumeration" but doesn't explain why this renaming is necessary or how it differs from other skills.
- "Mathematical reasoning*": The paper states they "merged statistical and quantitative reasoning with mathematical reasoning" without explaining why this merging is appropriate, as these are typically distinct concepts.
- "Punctuation*": The paper claims they "excluded scheme in figures of speech as ambiguous" but provides no examples or justification for this exclusion.

## Questions to Authors

1. How did you define "nonsense" questions, and why did you not apply the same definition to all datasets (including those you excluded)?
2. Why did you only report Flesch-Kincaid grade levels as your readability metric when you claimed to use multiple metrics (Figure 2)?
3. Why do the percentages in Table 3 not sum to 100% for most datasets, and how does this affect your analysis?
4. What specific criteria did you use to determine whether a dataset was "suitable for human reading and annotation" (as for LAMBADA), and why was this criteria not applied consistently to other datasets?
5. How did your "prerequisite skills" classification differ from Sugawara et al. (2017) in a way that justifies the reorganization of skills?

## Final Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in the RC field, the methodological flaws are severe and undermine the paper's contributions. The inconsistent definition of "nonsense" questions, the failure to properly report their claimed readability metrics, and the mathematical error in Table 3 represent fundamental flaws that cannot be easily addressed in a revision. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these issues are substantial enough to outweigh the paper's potential contributions. The authors would need to completely revise their methodology and re-analyze their data to address these fundamental inconsistencies.
