

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses a critical gap in reading comprehension (RC) research by proposing two complementary evaluation metrics: "prerequisite skills" to measure the cognitive demands of answering questions, and "readability" to measure the difficulty of reading context texts. The authors refine a previous taxonomy of prerequisite skills using insights from textual entailment and human text comprehension research, then systematically annotate six major RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) with these metrics. Their key finding is that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (as measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability, challenging the common assumption that easier-to-read texts lead to easier questions.

## Main Contributions
1. A refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC tasks, organized based on textual entailment and human text comprehension research, which is more comprehensive and theoretically grounded than previous work.
2. Systematic annotation of six major RC datasets with these metrics, providing a valuable comparative resource for the community.
3. The key insight that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (as measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability, which has important implications for RC dataset construction and system development.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation for the prerequisite skills taxonomy**: The authors provide a compelling and well-justified refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy. They explicitly address limitations of previous work (e.g., Sugawara et al.'s provisional categorization) and ground their taxonomy in established research from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (Dagan, LoBue & Yates). The taxonomy includes 13 skills with clear definitions, examples, and distinctions (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration inferences), making it more comprehensive and theoretically sound than previous approaches. This represents a significant methodological contribution to the field.

2. **Systematic annotation of six major RC datasets**: The authors provide a rigorous and well-documented annotation process with strong inter-annotator agreement (90.1%). The annotation methodology is clearly described, with a focus on consistency across datasets (selecting sentences needed for answering and annotating skills under the same condition). The resulting analysis (Tables 2-5) provides concrete, actionable insights about the characteristics of these datasets, such as the high frequency of bridging and elaboration skills in QA4MRE, the high frequency of ellipsis in Who-did-What, and the weak correlation between question difficulty and readability. This annotated dataset is a valuable resource for the community.

3. **Key insight about the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability**: The paper's most significant contribution is demonstrating that there is only a weak correlation between the number of prerequisite skills required to answer a question and the readability of the text (Flesch-Kincaid grade level). This challenges a common assumption in RC research and has important implications for dataset construction. The authors provide compelling evidence through multiple analyses (Figures 3-4, Table 5), showing examples like MCTest (easy to read but difficult questions) and SQuAD (difficult to read but relatively easy questions). This insight helps explain why some datasets like MCTest may be challenging despite having child-level text.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of empirical validation for the metrics' utility**: While the paper provides a detailed description of the metrics and their application, it does not demonstrate how these metrics would actually improve RC system development or dataset construction. The authors claim the metrics can be used for "curriculum learning" and "transfer learning" but provide no empirical evidence that using these metrics would lead to better systems or datasets. This limits the practical impact of the work.

2. **Limited scope of datasets analyzed**: The authors exclude several important RC datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) without fully justifying their exclusion or demonstrating that these exclusions significantly impact their conclusions. For example, they exclude CNN/Daily Mail because "it did not seem to be suitable for annotation," but this is not sufficiently justified. The analysis is limited to a small subset of available RC datasets, which may not be representative of the broader landscape.

3. **Potential subjectivity in annotation decisions**: While the inter-annotator agreement is strong (90.1%), the paper does not sufficiently address potential subjectivity in the annotation process. The authors note disagreements about whether certain questions are "nonsense" or solvable without context, but they do not provide a detailed analysis of these disagreements or how they were resolved. The taxonomy of prerequisite skills could also be subject to interpretation, especially for complex questions requiring multiple skills. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of these annotation challenges.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you validate that the prerequisite skills taxonomy actually correlates with human difficulty in answering questions? The paper claims the taxonomy reflects cognitive demands, but doesn't provide evidence that human performance aligns with the skill counts.

2. The paper mentions that their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences to answer questions. How would you extend this work to include that dimension of the RC task, and how might this affect your analysis?

3. The authors state that MCTest is easy to read but difficult to answer, which they attribute to the narrative genre. What specific aspects of narrative texts make them more cognitively demanding for RC tasks, and how might this inform future dataset construction?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills and systematically analyzing six major RC datasets with these metrics. The key insight about the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability is significant and well-supported by the analysis. While the paper has limitations in scope and lacks empirical validation of the metrics' utility, its methodological contribution and insightful analysis make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The annotated datasets and analysis provide a valuable resource for the community.
