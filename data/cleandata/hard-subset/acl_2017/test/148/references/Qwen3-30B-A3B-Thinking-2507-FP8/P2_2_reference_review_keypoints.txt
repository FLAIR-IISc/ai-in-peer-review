- Proposes two evaluation metric classes for RC: prerequisite skills (cognitive skills for answering) and readability (text difficulty).  
- Shows weak correlation between question difficulty (prerequisite skills) and text readability across six RC datasets.  
- Refines prerequisite-skills taxonomy grounded in psychological and linguistic theories.  
- Provides publicly available annotations of six major RC datasets using the proposed metrics.  

- Contribution: challenges assumption that harder-to-read texts yield harder questions and offers metrics to better characterize RC datasets.  
- Contribution: presents a theoretically grounded, more robust prerequisite-skills taxonomy.  
- Contribution: releases annotated datasets as a practical resource for the RC community.  

- Strength: clear problem statement with concrete examples demonstrating disconnect between readability and question difficulty.  
- Strength: metrics are theoretically grounded in established cognitive and linguistic work.  
- Strength: offers practical guidance for dataset construction and system development (curriculum-learning analogy).  

- Weakness: insufficient demonstration that the revised taxonomy is significantly better than prior work (lack of detailed comparisons).  
- Weakness: limited dataset coverage—excluded major datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA), reducing generalizability.  
- Weakness: ambiguity and lack of clear framework for annotating “nonsense” questions, affecting annotation reliability.  
- Weakness: no empirical connection shown between the proposed metrics and actual system performance.  

- Q: Provide a detailed comparison showing how the revised taxonomy improves over prior taxonomies (e.g., Sugawara et al., 2017).  
- Q: How does excluding CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, and LAMBADA affect conclusions, and what would inclusion likely change?  
- Q: What criteria or systematic approach do the authors use (or propose) to identify “nonsense” questions?  
- Q: Can the authors show preliminary analyses linking these metrics to system performance to demonstrate practical utility?  

- Overall: well-written, theoretically grounded, and practically useful work that should be accepted if authors address novelty demonstration, dataset coverage, nonsense-question criteria, and metric-to-performance connections.
