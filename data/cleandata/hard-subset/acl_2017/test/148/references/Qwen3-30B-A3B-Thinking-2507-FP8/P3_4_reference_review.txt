

# Peer Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets through two complementary metrics: "prerequisite skills" (capturing cognitive demands of answering questions) and "readability" (assessing linguistic complexity of texts). The authors refine a previous classification of prerequisite skills using insights from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment research, then annotate six major RC datasets with these metrics. Their key finding is a weak correlation between readability and prerequisite skills, challenging the assumption that easier-to-read texts yield easier questions.

## Main Contributions
1. A theoretically grounded refinement of prerequisite skills for RC, organized around textual entailment and human text comprehension theories
2. Systematic annotation of six RC datasets with these metrics, revealing significant dataset characteristics
3. Empirical evidence of weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, with important implications for dataset design

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a critical gap in RC evaluation**: The paper correctly identifies that current RC datasets are evaluated with superficial metrics (question types, answer types), failing to capture the actual cognitive challenges. This is a significant contribution to the field, as dataset quality assessment has been overlooked in favor of system performance.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The authors' grounding in psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment (Dagan, LoBue & Yates) is well-justified and adds depth to the field. Their reorganization of prerequisite skills (particularly the distinction between bridging and elaboration inferences) is theoretically sound and addresses limitations in prior work.

3. **Valuable empirical findings**: The weak correlation between readability and question difficulty (ranging from 0.025 to 0.416) is a significant empirical contribution. The examples of MCTest (easy to read but difficult to answer) and SQuAD (difficult to read but relatively easy to answer) provide concrete evidence that challenges common assumptions in dataset design.

## Weaknesses

1. **Methodological limitation in evaluating sentence selection competence**: As acknowledged by the authors in Section 6, their methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read to answer questions." This is a critical limitation since RC systems must first locate relevant text segments before understanding them. The paper provides only a brief analysis of sentence distance (Table 6), but doesn't integrate this into the core evaluation framework.

2. **Insufficient handling of nonsense questions**: While the authors note that MS MARCO and Who-did-What have high rates of nonsense questions, they don't provide a systematic methodology for identifying or filtering these. This is a major issue for dataset quality, as Chen et al. (2016) already highlighted that ~25% of CNN/Daily Mail questions are unsolvable. The paper's approach to "nonsense" questions (simply labeling them) is inadequate for practical dataset curation.

3. **Lack of concrete guidance for system development**: The paper suggests using their metrics for "curriculum learning" but provides no specific examples of how these metrics could guide system development. Without concrete examples of how to design intermediate datasets based on their metrics, this contribution remains theoretical rather than practical.

4. **Subjectivity in annotation process**: The paper mentions inter-annotator agreement was 90.1%, but acknowledges significant disagreements about "nonsense" questions. The authors don't detail how they resolved these disagreements or provide a clear threshold for determining when a question is "nonsense." This subjectivity undermines the reliability of their annotations.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you extend your methodology to evaluate the "sentence selection competence" of RC systems, which is a critical component of the RC task not addressed in your current framework?

2. The paper notes that NewsQA has meta-information about question quality (is question bad), but other datasets don't. How would you recommend handling datasets without such meta-information, particularly for improving dataset quality during curation?

3. Can you provide specific examples of how your metrics could guide the development of RC systems, beyond the general suggestion of "curriculum learning"? For instance, what would be the first dataset a system should train on according to your framework?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the RC field by providing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating dataset quality beyond simple accuracy. The weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is an important finding that should influence future dataset design. However, the methodology's limitation in evaluating sentence selection competence and the lack of concrete guidance for system development represent significant weaknesses. The paper would benefit from addressing these limitations in future work.

The annotation results are valuable and could serve as a useful reference for researchers, but the practical utility of the framework is limited by the methodological constraints noted above. With improvements to address these limitations, this work could become a significant contribution to the field of RC evaluation. As it stands, it's a strong theoretical contribution that would benefit from more practical implementation guidance.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution but has significant methodological limitations that reduce its practical impact.)
