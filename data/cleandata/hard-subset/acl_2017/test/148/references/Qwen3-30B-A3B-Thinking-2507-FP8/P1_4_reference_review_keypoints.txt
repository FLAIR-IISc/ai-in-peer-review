- Proposes two evaluation metrics for RC datasets: prerequisite skills and readability, refining Sugawara et al.’s skills into a 13-item taxonomy.
- Provides theoretical grounding linking text comprehension psychology and textual entailment to the prerequisite-skill taxonomy.
- Annotates six major RC datasets with the proposed metrics and publicly releases the annotations.
- Empirically shows weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, challenging common assumptions.
- Addresses a critical gap in RC evaluation by moving beyond superficial categories and demonstrating mismatches between text complexity and question difficulty.
- Theoretically well-founded taxonomy improves on prior provisional categorizations.
- Public dataset annotations are a valuable resource for targeted RC research and benchmarking.
- Lacks concrete examples or methodology showing how the metrics would guide practical system development or curriculum learning.
- Insufficient justification and operational distinction for some skills (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration) and unclear mapping to system capabilities.
- Correlation analysis between skills and readability lacks assessment of statistical significance and practical meaning.
- Methodological limitations—such as inability to evaluate sentence-selection competence—are acknowledged but not fully addressed regarding impact on conclusions.
- Asks authors for a concrete example of using the metrics to diagnose and improve an RC system.
- Requests clarification on distinguishing datasets that are “easy” due to low skill requirements from those with poor question quality.
- Seeks elaboration on how the finding that readability does not determine question difficulty should inform future dataset design.
- Overall: valuable, theoretically grounded contribution with useful public resources; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen practical implications and taxonomy justification.
