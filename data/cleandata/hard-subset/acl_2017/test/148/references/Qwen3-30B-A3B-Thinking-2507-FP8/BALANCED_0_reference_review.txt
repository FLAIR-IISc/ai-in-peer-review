

# Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets by introducing two complementary classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" and "readability." The authors argue that existing RC datasets are typically evaluated only with superficial categories (e.g., question types), which fails to capture the true complexity of the tasks. To address this, they refine the classification of prerequisite skills for RC tasks, organizing knowledge reasoning in terms of textual entailment and human text comprehension theories from psychology. They also propose a set of readability metrics based on linguistic features.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. **A theoretically grounded refinement of prerequisite skills for RC tasks:** The authors provide a comprehensive and well-motivated reorganization of the prerequisite skills needed for RC, drawing on insights from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment research (Dagan, LoBue & Yates). This is a significant improvement over previous work (Sugawara et al., 2017) which had a provisional categorization of knowledge reasoning.

2. **Systematic annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics:** The authors annotate six major RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) using their refined metrics, providing valuable insights into the characteristics of these datasets.

3. **Empirical demonstration of the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability:** The authors show through their annotation that the difficulty of questions (measured by the number of required prerequisite skills) does not strongly correlate with the readability of the context texts, challenging the common assumption that harder-to-read texts necessarily lead to harder questions.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical grounding and refinement of prerequisite skills:** The authors' reorganization of prerequisite skills is a significant contribution. They draw on established theories from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment research (Dagan, LoBue & Yates) to create a more comprehensive and theoretically sound classification. This is a major improvement over previous work (Sugawara et al., 2017) which had a provisional categorization of knowledge reasoning. The authors clearly explain how they've incorporated insights from these fields, making the classification more robust and applicable to a wider range of RC tasks.

2. **Valuable empirical analysis of multiple RC datasets:** The systematic annotation of six major RC datasets provides important insights into the characteristics of these datasets. The authors demonstrate that different datasets vary significantly in terms of the required prerequisite skills and readability, which has important implications for both dataset construction and system development. For instance, their finding that MCTest (a dataset written for children) requires more prerequisite skills than SQuAD (written for adults) challenges common assumptions about the relationship between text difficulty and question difficulty.

3. **Clear demonstration of the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability:** The authors provide strong empirical evidence that the readability of context texts (measured by Flesch-Kincaid grade level) does not strongly correlate with the difficulty of the questions (measured by the number of required prerequisite skills). This is a significant finding that challenges the common assumption that harder-to-read texts necessarily lead to harder questions. Their analysis of three datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD) in Figure 4 particularly illustrates this point well.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of dataset analysis:** The authors only analyze six RC datasets, which is a small subset of the many RC datasets available. This limits the generalizability of their findings. For example, they exclude CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, and LAMBADA for various reasons, but it's unclear whether these exclusions significantly impact their main conclusions. A more comprehensive analysis would strengthen their claims about the relationship between prerequisite skills and readability across the broader landscape of RC datasets.

2. **Lack of clear connection between the metrics and system performance:** While the authors propose these metrics as a way to evaluate RC datasets, they don't provide a clear demonstration of how these metrics could be used to guide system development or evaluation. The discussion in Section 6 about "How to utilize the two classes of metrics for system development" is somewhat vague and doesn't provide concrete examples of how a system developer would use these metrics to improve their model. This weakens the practical utility of their work.

3. **Ambiguity in the definition of "nonsense" questions:** The authors mention that they had disagreements about identifying "nonsense" questions (those that can be solved without reading the context), but they don't provide a clear definition or criteria for what constitutes a "nonsense" question. This is a significant issue because it affects the quality of their annotation, particularly for datasets like MS MARCO where they found a high percentage of "nonsense" questions. Without a clear definition, it's difficult to assess the reliability of their annotation.

4. **Limited discussion of the limitations of their methodology:** The authors acknowledge that their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences to answer questions (Section 1), but they don't fully explore the implications of this limitation. They briefly mention this in Section 6, but a more thorough discussion of how this limitation affects their analysis and what could be done to address it would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors state that they excluded CNN/Daily Mail because it "contains some errors" (Chen et al., 2016) and is "anonymized." However, Chen et al. (2016) showed that 25% of questions in CNN/Daily Mail are unsolvable or nonsense. How do the authors justify excluding a dataset that they themselves identify as having quality issues, when their metric is intended to help identify such quality issues?

2. The authors mention that their annotation procedure allowed multiple labeling, but they don't provide details about how they resolved disagreements between annotators. Specifically, for the "nonsense" questions, they mention disagreements, but don't explain how they ultimately decided which questions were "nonsense" and which were not. Could the authors provide more details about their inter-annotator agreement process and how they handled disagreements?

3. In Table 3, the authors report the average number of required prerequisite skills per question. However, they don't explain why QA4MRE has a higher average (3.25) than the other datasets (which range from 1.19 to 2.43). Is this simply because QA4MRE is a more complex dataset, or are there other factors at play? Could the authors provide more context about the nature of QA4MRE that explains this higher average?

4. The authors state that "Our methodology could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read and answer candidates from the context." This seems like a significant limitation. How do they think this limitation could be addressed in future work, and what would be the implications for their metric if this limitation were overcome?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written paper that makes a theoretically sound contribution to the evaluation of RC datasets. The refinement of the prerequisite skills classification is particularly strong, and the empirical analysis of six major RC datasets provides valuable insights. However, the limited scope of the dataset analysis and the lack of clear connection between the metrics and practical system development weaken the paper's impact.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly regarding the limited dataset scope, the definition of "nonsense" questions, and the practical utility of the metrics for system development.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a more nuanced framework for evaluating RC datasets, and with the suggested revisions, it has the potential to be a useful resource for the community.
