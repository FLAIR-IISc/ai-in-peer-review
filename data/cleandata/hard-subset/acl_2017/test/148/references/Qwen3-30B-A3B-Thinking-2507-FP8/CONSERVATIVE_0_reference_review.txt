

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" (a refined classification of cognitive skills required to answer RC questions) and "readability" (linguistic complexity metrics). The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and analyze the relationship between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability. Their main finding is a weak correlation between these two dimensions, with examples like MCTest (easy to read but difficult to answer) and SQuAD (difficult to read but relatively easy to answer).

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Annotation of six RC datasets with proposed metrics**: The authors provide a valuable resource by annotating six major RC datasets with their metrics, making the results publicly available.
2. **Demonstration of weak correlation between readability and question difficulty**: The paper clearly shows that readability of text does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty, an important insight for dataset construction.
3. **Refinement of prerequisite skills classification**: The authors make minor improvements to a previous classification of prerequisite skills, grounding it more firmly in psychological theories.

## Strengths
1. **Practical Resource**: The annotated datasets provide a valuable resource for the RC community. Researchers can use these annotations to better understand the characteristics of existing datasets and potentially guide the construction of new ones.

2. **Clear Demonstration of Key Insight**: The paper successfully demonstrates that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated, which is an important finding for the field. The examples provided (MCTest vs. SQuAD) effectively illustrate this point.

3. **Connection to Psychological Theories**: The authors make a reasonable effort to ground their metrics in established psychological theories (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), which adds credibility to their approach and shows they've done their homework on the theoretical foundations.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Novelty and Insufficient Validation**: The core idea of using multiple metrics to evaluate RC datasets is not novel—many papers have already proposed various metrics for RC evaluation (question type, answer type, linguistic complexity). The paper fails to demonstrate how their metrics provide significant new insights beyond existing approaches. Crucially, the authors do not validate their metrics against actual RC systems—they don't show how these metrics correlate with system performance. Without this validation, it's unclear how useful these metrics are for system development.

2. **Incremental Contribution**: The refinements to the prerequisite skills classification are minor rather than transformative. The authors claim to "revise" the previous classification, but they don't provide evidence that their revision is significantly better. The paper doesn't compare the predictive power of their new classification versus the old one, making it difficult to assess the value of their revision.

3. **Methodological Limitations**: The authors explicitly acknowledge significant limitations in their methodology, including:
   - Their approach cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences needed to answer questions (a critical aspect of RC)
   - They have difficulty handling "nonsense" questions and questions with "none of the above" answers
   - They exclude several major datasets (e.g., CNN/Daily Mail) without strong justification

4. **Insufficient Scope**: The analysis is limited to only six datasets, which is insufficient to draw broad conclusions about RC datasets as a whole. The exclusion of major datasets like CNN/Daily Mail and CBTest weakens the generalizability of their findings.

5. **Lack of Practical Guidance**: The paper makes vague claims about using their metrics for system development ("build to solve easy-to-read and easy-to-answer dataset..."), but provides no concrete examples or guidance on how to implement this approach. The connection to curriculum learning is mentioned but not substantiated.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do your metrics correlate with actual RC system performance? Have you tested this correlation on any existing RC systems, and if so, what were the results?

2. Why did you choose to exclude CNN/Daily Mail (Hermann et al., 2015) from your analysis, given it's one of the largest RC datasets? What would the results have been if you had included it?

3. Can you provide evidence that your revised classification of prerequisite skills is superior to the previous classification? How does it improve the predictive power of the metrics compared to the original Sugawara et al. (2017) classification?

4. How do you plan to address the limitation of your methodology that cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences? Is there a way to extend your approach to include this critical aspect of RC?

5. The paper mentions "curriculum learning" but doesn't provide concrete examples of how to use your metrics to create such a curriculum. Can you provide specific examples of how your metrics could be used in a practical system development pipeline?

## Overall Recommendation
I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper. While the annotation of six RC datasets is a useful resource, the paper fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or utility beyond existing approaches. The core contribution is an incremental refinement of an existing classification rather than a significant methodological advance. Most critically, the paper lacks validation against actual RC systems, which is essential for demonstrating the practical value of the proposed metrics. The methodological limitations acknowledged by the authors further undermine the utility of their approach for evaluating RC systems. Without evidence that these metrics correlate with system performance or provide new insights beyond what's already known, the paper does not meet ACL's standards for methodological rigor and contribution.
