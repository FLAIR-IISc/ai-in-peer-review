- Paper proposes two evaluation metric classes for RC: refined "prerequisite skills" and "readability," and annotates six RC datasets, finding weak correlation between skill difficulty and readability.
- Contribution: reorganizes and refines prerequisite skills drawing on textual entailment and comprehension psychology.
- Contribution: provides the first systematic annotation of multiple RC datasets using these metrics.
- Contribution: shows a weak correlation between question difficulty (prerequisite skills) and text readability, challenging common assumptions.
- Strength: addresses a meaningful gap by moving beyond superficial RC evaluation metrics and accuracy.
- Strength: strong theoretical grounding using cognitive psychology and NLP literature; improves on prior skill taxonomies.
- Strength: annotations yield practical insights (e.g., MCTest is easy to read but hard to answer; MS MARCO has many nonsense questions).
- Weakness: lacks concrete, prescriptive guidance on how to use metrics for constructing better RC datasets.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of why correlations between skills and readability are weak.
- Weakness: annotation methodology has limitations (handling of "nonsense" questions and not evaluating sentence-selection competence) that affect validity.
- Weakness: limited discussion of broader implications or applicability to other NLP tasks and dataset types.
- Question: how should researchers concretely use these metrics to guide dataset construction?
- Question: what factors could explain the weak relationship between prerequisite skills and readability beyond the observed correlations?
- Question: how does not evaluating sentence-selection competence affect the "number of required skills" metric and how could this be integrated in future work?
- Overall: valuable, theoretically grounded, and practically useful descriptive analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address methodology and prescriptive guidance.
