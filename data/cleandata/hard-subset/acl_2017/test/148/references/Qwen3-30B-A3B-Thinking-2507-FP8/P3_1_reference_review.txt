

# Peer Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a dual-metric framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on "prerequisite skills" (the cognitive demands of answering questions) and "readability" (the linguistic complexity of text). The authors refine a taxonomy of prerequisite skills based on psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP research on textual entailment (Dagan et al., LoBue & Yates). They annotate six RC datasets with these metrics and find a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability.

As I see them, the paper's most significant contributions are:

1. **A theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills** that goes beyond superficial question-type classifications and provides a more nuanced understanding of RC task difficulty.
2. **Empirical analysis of six major RC datasets** using these metrics, providing valuable insights into the characteristics of current RC benchmarks.
3. **The key insight that question difficulty and text readability are only weakly correlated**, challenging assumptions in the field about how to evaluate RC datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper is exceptionally well-grounded in psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch's Construction-Integration model, McNamara & Magliano's distinctions between bridging/elaboration inferences) and NLP research on textual entailment. This provides a robust basis for their metric development, which is a significant improvement over prior work that often lacked theoretical grounding.

2. **Valuable empirical dataset analysis**: The annotation of six major RC datasets with these metrics is a substantial contribution to the field. The authors provide detailed statistics on the frequency of different prerequisite skills across datasets, which reveals important insights about dataset characteristics (e.g., QA4MRE requiring the most skills, MCTest having high causal reasoning requirements).

3. **Important practical insight**: The finding that there's only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability is a crucial contribution that challenges common assumptions in RC research. This insight has direct implications for dataset design and system development, as demonstrated in their discussion of curriculum learning approaches.

4. **Clear methodological transparency**: The paper provides detailed descriptions of their annotation procedure, inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), and dataset selection criteria, which enhances the reproducibility of their work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of connection to actual system performance**: The paper focuses on dataset evaluation but doesn't demonstrate how these metrics could be used to improve RC systems. The discussion of "how to utilize the two classes of metrics for system development" is vague and lacks concrete examples or empirical evidence showing that systems trained on datasets with specific metric profiles perform better. The authors claim this could be used for "curriculum learning," but provide no evidence that such an approach would actually improve performance.

2. **Arbitrary exclusion of major datasets**: The authors exclude several important RC datasets without sufficient justification. For example, they exclude CBTest because "it is for language modeling tasks rather than RC task," but this seems like a questionable distinction (CBTest is explicitly designed for RC). They also exclude LAMBADA because "all tokens are lowercased, which seemingly prevents inferences based on proper nouns," but this is a methodological choice rather than a fundamental limitation. This selective dataset coverage limits the generalizability of their findings.

3. **Methodological limitations in annotation**: The authors acknowledge that their methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read," which is a critical aspect of RC. Their brief analysis of sentence distance (Section 6) is insufficient to address this limitation. For example, they note that "SQuAD and MS MARCO showed lower scores [in sentence distance] because most of their questions seem only to require reading a single sentence," but this doesn't account for the fact that SQuAD questions might require reading a single sentence but that sentence might be embedded in a longer context.

4. **Theoretical inconsistencies in taxonomy**: While the authors claim to build on Kintsch and McNamara & Magliano, there are some inconsistencies in how they apply these theories. For example, the distinction between bridging and elaboration seems somewhat arbitrary, and the "meta-knowledge" skill appears to be a subset of elaboration rather than a distinct category. The paper would benefit from a clearer theoretical justification for their specific taxonomic choices.

5. **Limited evaluation of metric validity**: The paper doesn't provide evidence that their metrics actually correlate with human difficulty or system performance. They show correlation with readability metrics, but not with how well systems perform on these datasets. Without this, it's unclear whether their metrics capture the actual cognitive demands of RC tasks.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the difficulty of their questions," but the data in Figure 3 shows that SQuAD is difficult to read but relatively easy to answer (compared to other datasets), while QA4MRE is difficult to read and difficult to answer. How do you reconcile this with your claim that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated? Could it be that readability is only weakly correlated with question difficulty when considering datasets as a whole, but more strongly correlated when considering individual questions?

2. You state that "MCTest is a good example of an RC dataset that is easy to read but difficult to answer." However, MCTest was designed for children, so it's by construction easy to read. How would you adjust your metrics to account for the fact that "easy to read" for children might not correspond to "easy to read" for adults or RC systems?

3. The paper mentions that "MS MARCO was relatively easy dataset in terms of prerequisite skills" but also contained many nonsense questions. How do you think the presence of nonsense questions affects the interpretation of your metrics, and how would you modify your annotation procedure to account for this?

4. You claim to have "revised the previous classification of prerequisite skills," but the taxonomy presented in Section 3.1 appears to be largely a reorganization of Sugawara et al.'s work rather than a fundamental revision. What are the specific theoretical or empirical justifications for the changes you made to the taxonomy?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with major revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets and presenting empirical data on the characteristics of major RC benchmarks. The insight about the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability is particularly significant.

However, the paper has substantial weaknesses that need to be addressed, particularly regarding the connection to actual RC system performance, the exclusion of major datasets, and the methodological limitations in their annotation approach. The authors should strengthen their theoretical justification, provide more concrete examples of how these metrics could be used in system development, and address the limitations they acknowledge in their methodology.

The paper has the potential to be a highly valuable contribution to the field if these weaknesses are addressed, but as currently written, it falls short of the expected standard for ACL.

## Additional Comments

The authors should consider expanding their discussion of how these metrics could be used in practice for dataset creation and system development. For example, they could provide more concrete examples of how a researcher might use these metrics to design a curriculum for RC system development.

The exclusion of CBTest seems particularly problematic, as it's a major RC dataset that has been widely used in the field. The authors should reconsider this exclusion or provide a more compelling justification.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their metrics relate to existing RC evaluation metrics (e.g., F1 score, EM) and how they complement rather than replace these standard metrics.
