

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" (measuring the cognitive skills required to answer questions) and "readability" (measuring the difficulty of reading the text). The authors argue that current RC datasets are evaluated using superficial metrics (e.g., question types, answer types) that don't capture the true difficulty of tasks. They demonstrate through analysis of six RC datasets that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability. The paper provides a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills based on psychological theories of text comprehension and presents annotated datasets for public use.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A comprehensive analysis demonstrating the weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability, challenging the assumption that harder-to-read texts lead to harder questions.
2. A theoretically grounded refinement of prerequisite skills taxonomy for RC, organized based on linguistic and psychological theories of text comprehension, which provides a more robust framework than previous work.
3. A publicly available annotation of six major RC datasets with the proposed metrics, creating a valuable resource for the RC community.

## Strengths

1. **Strong problem statement and compelling motivation**: The paper effectively identifies a genuine gap in the RC field through clear examples (Figure 1), demonstrating that text readability doesn't necessarily correlate with question difficulty. The authors provide concrete evidence of this disconnect, making a convincing case for their proposed metrics.

2. **Theoretical foundation and rigor**: The metrics are not arbitrary but grounded in established psychological theories (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and linguistic analyses (Dagan et al.). This theoretical grounding provides credibility and a solid foundation for future work, distinguishing the paper from ad-hoc metric proposals.

3. **Practical utility for dataset construction and system development**: The paper goes beyond mere analysis to provide concrete guidance for the RC community. The discussion of "how to utilize the two classes of metrics for system development" and the curriculum learning analogy are particularly valuable for practitioners. The authors demonstrate how these metrics can inform step-by-step system development, which is a significant practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear novelty demonstration**: While the metrics are well-organized, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how their revision of the prerequisite skills taxonomy is significantly better than previous work. The authors claim to have "revised" previous classifications but don't provide a detailed comparison showing specific improvements or how their taxonomy addresses limitations of prior work. This weakens the paper's contribution claim.

2. **Limited dataset coverage**: The authors explicitly state they did not annotate several important RC datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) due to various reasons (anonymization, focus on language modeling, formatting issues). This significantly limits the generalizability of their findings, as these datasets are widely used in the field. A more comprehensive analysis would have strengthened their claims.

3. **Ambiguity in "nonsense" question annotation**: The paper acknowledges difficulties in determining what constitutes a "nonsense" question but doesn't provide a clear framework for resolving these ambiguities. This is a significant methodological weakness that affects the reliability of their annotations and the validity of their findings.

4. **Lack of connection to system performance**: While the paper discusses how the metrics could be used for system development, it doesn't actually show how these metrics correlate with actual system performance. Without this connection, it's difficult to assess how practically useful these metrics would be for improving RC systems.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to "revised" previous classifications of prerequisite skills, but doesn't clearly demonstrate how this revision is superior. Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison between their taxonomy and previous taxonomies (e.g., Sugawara et al., 2017), highlighting specific improvements and addressing the limitations they claim to have resolved?

2. The exclusion of several major datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) due to various reasons limits the generalizability of the findings. How do the authors think the exclusion of these datasets affects their conclusions, and what would they expect the results to look like if they had included these datasets?

3. The paper acknowledges ambiguity in determining "nonsense" questions but doesn't provide a clear definition or framework. Could the authors share their criteria for identifying nonsense questions, or propose a more systematic approach to this challenging annotation task?

4. The paper discusses the theoretical utility of their metrics but doesn't show how they correlate with actual system performance. Could the authors provide preliminary analysis or examples showing how these metrics could guide the development of better RC systems?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with a clear problem statement, strong theoretical grounding, and significant practical utility for the RC community. The main contribution is the comprehensive analysis demonstrating the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, supported by a detailed annotation of six RC datasets. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a clearer demonstration of novelty, a more comprehensive dataset coverage, and a stronger connection to system performance.

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the RC field and should be accepted, though the authors should address the weaknesses noted above in any revision. The publicly available dataset annotations will be particularly useful for future work in the field.
