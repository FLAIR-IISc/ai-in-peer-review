

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" and "readability." The authors argue that current RC datasets are evaluated only with superficial categories (e.g., question types) and that there's a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability. They refine the classification of prerequisite skills based on psychology research and textual entailment, and annotate six RC datasets with these metrics.

My assessment of the main contributions (ordered by strength):

1. **Refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills**: The authors develop a theoretically grounded classification of 13 prerequisite skills for RC tasks, building on psychology research (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment studies (Dagan et al., LoBue & Yates). This addresses a clear gap in previous work (Sugawara et al., 2017) that had a "provisional" classification with "weak theoretical background."

2. **Systematic analysis of multiple RC datasets**: The paper provides a comparative analysis of six RC datasets using the same metrics, demonstrating the weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability (measured by Flesch-Kincaid grade level), which challenges common assumptions about RC dataset design.

3. **Publicly available dataset annotations**: The authors make their annotations publicly available, creating a valuable resource for the community to guide future RC dataset construction and system development.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills**: The authors make a significant contribution by building a more comprehensive and theoretically justified classification of prerequisite skills for RC. They draw on established psychological frameworks (Kintsch's C-I model, McNamara & Magliano's distinctions) and textual entailment research to refine the classification. This is a clear improvement over previous work, which had a "provisional" classification with "weak theoretical background." The distinction between bridging (grammatical/lexical knowledge) and elaboration (general knowledge) inferences is particularly well-motivated and useful.

2. **Systematic comparative analysis of RC datasets**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of six RC datasets using the same metrics, allowing for meaningful cross-dataset comparison. The key finding that there is "only a weak correlation between the difficulty of questions and the readability of context texts" (Table 5, Figures 3-4) is a significant contribution that challenges common assumptions in the field. This analysis demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed metrics.

3. **Practical resource for the community**: The authors make their dataset annotations publicly available, which is a substantial contribution to the field. The analysis provides concrete insights into how different datasets vary in terms of required skills and readability, which can directly inform future dataset construction and system development. The discussion of how to use these metrics for "curriculum learning" in system development is particularly valuable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear justification for the 13-skill taxonomy**: While the authors claim to have refined the taxonomy, they don't provide sufficient evidence for why these 13 specific skills are necessary and sufficient. Some skills seem to overlap conceptually (e.g., "Causal relation" and "Spatiotemporal relation" both involve understanding relationships between entities/events). The paper doesn't demonstrate how this taxonomy would be applied in practice or why it's superior to simpler classifications. The authors acknowledge this is "not constructed to be dependent on RC models," but this makes it less useful for the specific purpose of RC evaluation.

2. **Limited evaluation of annotation methodology**: The paper mentions an inter-annotator agreement of 90.1%, but doesn't provide details on how disagreements were resolved or whether the annotation process was robust. The authors note significant issues with "nonsense" questions (e.g., questions that can be solved without context), but they don't provide a clear definition of what constitutes a "solvable" question in RC. This is a fundamental issue that undermines the reliability of their evaluation.

3. **Lack of connection to RC system development**: While the paper mentions "How to utilize the two classes of metrics for system development," it doesn't provide concrete examples of how these metrics could guide actual system development. The discussion of "curriculum learning" is theoretical but lacks empirical evidence or specific examples of how the metrics would be applied in practice. The paper focuses on dataset evaluation but doesn't demonstrate how these metrics would improve RC system performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors state that "our methodology was used to evaluate the understanding of contextual entailments in a broader sense for RC." How does this differ from existing work on textual entailment or semantic textual similarity, and how does it specifically address the unique challenges of RC tasks?

2. The paper notes that "MS MARCO and Who-did-What received relatively high scores for nonsense questions" due to automated curation. How would you define a "nonsense" question in an RC context, and what specific criteria would you propose for filtering such questions from future datasets?

3. The authors acknowledge that "the methodology failed to specify" "no answer" questions in QA4MRE. How would you extend your taxonomy to handle such questions, and what implications does this have for the evaluation of RC systems that must handle "no answer" cases?
