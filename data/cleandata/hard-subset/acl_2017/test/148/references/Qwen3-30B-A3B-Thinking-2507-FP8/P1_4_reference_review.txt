

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two complementary evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills (measuring the cognitive skills required to answer questions) and readability (measuring the textual complexity of documents). The authors refine Sugawara et al.'s (2017) classification of prerequisite skills using insights from textual entailment research and human text comprehension psychology, resulting in a revised taxonomy of 13 skills. They annotate six major RC datasets with these metrics and analyze the relationship between the two classes of metrics.

The main contributions of this work, ordered by strength, are:

1. A theoretically grounded classification of prerequisite skills for RC that bridges psychological research on text comprehension with NLP evaluation needs.
2. Empirical evidence demonstrating the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, challenging the assumption that more difficult text automatically means more difficult questions.
3. A publicly available annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics, providing a valuable resource for future RC research.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a critical gap in RC evaluation**: The paper correctly identifies that current RC datasets lack meaningful metrics beyond superficial categories (e.g., question types), making it difficult to accurately assess system performance. The authors effectively demonstrate this issue through their Figure 1 examples, showing that readability and question difficulty are not necessarily correlated. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it challenges a fundamental assumption in RC research.

2. **Theoretical grounding**: The refinement of prerequisite skills based on psychological models (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara & Magliano, 2009) and textual entailment research (Dagan et al., 2013) provides a solid theoretical foundation for the metrics. This is a major improvement over previous work that had "provisional" categorizations with "weak theoretical background" (as the authors correctly note).

3. **Valuable public resource**: The annotation of six major RC datasets with the proposed metrics creates a valuable resource for the community. This dataset can serve as a benchmark for future work on RC system development and dataset construction, enabling more targeted improvements in specific skill areas rather than relying solely on overall accuracy metrics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited practical utility for system development**: While the authors mention curriculum learning in their discussion, they fail to provide concrete examples of how these metrics would be used in practice to guide system development. The paper claims these metrics can "help researchers to develop a method for the step-by-step construction of better RC datasets," but provides no specific methodology or examples of how to apply this in practice. This limits the paper's immediate impact on the field.

2. **Insufficient justification for skill taxonomy**: The paper introduces 13 prerequisite skills but lacks sufficient justification for why these specific skills were chosen over alternatives. For example, the distinction between "bridging" and "elaboration" is not clearly explained in terms of how they differ operationally in RC tasks. The paper also doesn't demonstrate how these skills map to actual RC system capabilities, making it difficult to see how this taxonomy would be used to diagnose system failures.

3. **Limited analysis of correlation significance**: The paper shows weak correlations (0.025-0.416) between prerequisite skills and readability metrics but doesn't adequately analyze whether these correlations are statistically significant or practically meaningful. The paper's main conclusion that "there is not necessarily a strong correlation" is somewhat tautological given the weak correlation values presented, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these metrics would lead to better RC systems.

4. **Methodological limitations not fully addressed**: The authors acknowledge that their methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting necessary sentences" (Section 6), which is a critical aspect of RC. However, they don't sufficiently address how this limitation affects the validity of their conclusions or how future work might overcome it. This is a significant limitation given that most RC systems must first identify relevant context before answering questions.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you specifically use these metrics to guide the development of an RC system? Could you provide a concrete example of how a system developer would use this framework to identify and address specific weaknesses in their model?

2. The paper mentions that "MS MARCO was a relatively easy dataset in terms of prerequisite skills" but contained "nonsense questions." How would you distinguish between a dataset being "easy" due to low skill requirements versus being "easy" due to poor question quality, and how would you ensure that future datasets avoid such issues?

3. The authors state that "the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the difficulty of their questions." Could you elaborate on how this finding might inform the design of future RC datasets, particularly in terms of balancing text complexity with question difficulty?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the RC field by providing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets beyond superficial metrics. The weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is an important empirical finding that challenges assumptions in the field. The publicly available dataset annotation is particularly valuable for future research.

However, the paper's practical utility for system development is limited, and the justification for the specific skill taxonomy could be strengthened. With minor revisions addressing these concerns, this paper would make a strong contribution to ACL.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the practical implications and clarify the skill taxonomy justification. The paper addresses an important gap in the field and provides a valuable resource for future RC research.
