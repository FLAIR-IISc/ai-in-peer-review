- Paper proposes two RC evaluation metrics: "prerequisite skills" (question difficulty) and "readability" (text difficulty), and annotates six RC datasets.  
- Authors find a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, challenging the assumption that easier text yields easier questions.  
- Contribution: systematic annotation framework grounded in cognitive psychology (McNamara & Magliano, 2009).  
- Contribution: empirical analysis showing low Pearson correlations (0.025–0.416) between readability and question difficulty, with dataset-specific examples (e.g., MCTest).  
- Contribution: publicly released dataset annotations intended to aid dataset construction and system development.  
- Strength: addresses a gap in RC evaluation by moving beyond superficial question-type categories toward theoretically grounded metrics.  
- Strength: provides valuable, counterintuitive empirical findings that impact dataset design and interpretation.  
- Strength: proposes a curriculum learning approach based on the metrics to guide stepwise system development.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—metric revisions are minor and mostly build on prior work (Sugawara et al.).  
- Weakness: major methodological limitation—metrics do not evaluate sentence-selection/document retrieval competence, a core RC skill.  
- Weakness: questionable dataset selection—excludes major datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) with weak justifications.  
- Weakness: no evidence that proposed metrics correlate with actual system performance on datasets.  
- Weakness: potential misuse of human readability metrics without validating their relevance to machine comprehension.  
- Question: how do the metrics address the competence of selecting necessary sentences for answering questions?  
- Question: why assume human readability metrics (for L2 learners) reflect RC system difficulty, and what evidence supports this?  
- Question: how would the methodology handle "nonsense" questions and define a formal criterion for a "solvable" RC question?  
- Overall: useful and systematic dataset annotations with a significant empirical finding, but methodological flaws, limited novelty, and missing system-validation weaken the paper; substantial revisions needed.
