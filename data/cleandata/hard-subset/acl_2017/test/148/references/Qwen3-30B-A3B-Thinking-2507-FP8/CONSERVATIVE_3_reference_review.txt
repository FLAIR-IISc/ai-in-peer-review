

# ACL Review

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" (a taxonomy of cognitive skills required to answer questions) and "readability" (linguistic complexity metrics). The authors claim that these metrics can help analyze RC dataset quality and guide system development. They annotate six RC datasets with these metrics and find that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Creation of an annotated dataset with six RC datasets labeled with their proposed metrics (a useful resource for the community)
2. Empirical demonstration that there is a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability (a useful observation for RC researchers)
3. Refinement of a taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, organized using insights from textual entailment and psychology

## Strengths

1. **Valuable Annotated Resource:** The paper provides a useful resource in the form of six RC datasets annotated with the authors' metrics. This is a concrete contribution that will be helpful for future RC research. The annotation process is well-described and the inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is respectable.

2. **Clear Empirical Finding:** The paper makes a clear, empirically supported observation that the readability of text does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty in RC datasets. This is an important insight for dataset creators and system developers, as it challenges assumptions about what makes RC tasks difficult.

3. **Methodological Clarity:** The authors provide a clear description of their metrics, annotation procedure, and analysis methods. The paper is well-organized and the methodology is reproducible, which is a significant strength.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Approach:** The paper's core contribution is a reorganization of existing concepts from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (Vajjala & Meurers) rather than a fundamentally new approach. The authors claim to have "revised" previous classifications but don't provide sufficient evidence that their taxonomy is significantly better or more useful than existing ones. This is a major weakness given the reviewer's preference for established methods.

2. **No Validation Against System Performance:** The paper claims that their metrics will help guide RC system development, but crucially, they don't demonstrate that systems trained or evaluated using these metrics actually perform better. Without showing that the metrics correlate with system performance, the metrics remain purely descriptive rather than prescriptive. This is a critical omission for a paper claiming to help "develop better RC systems."

3. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The paper doesn't compare their metrics against established evaluation metrics for RC datasets. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether their metrics provide additional value beyond what's already known or used in the field. The paper doesn't show how their metrics improve upon current evaluation practices.

4. **Limited Scope of Validation:** The authors only annotate six datasets, which is a small sample size for making broad claims about RC datasets. They don't demonstrate how their metrics generalize across different types of RC tasks or datasets. The exclusion of many popular RC datasets (like CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest) for arbitrary reasons further limits the scope.

5. **Theoretical Weakness in Taxonomy:** The authors claim their taxonomy is "comprehensive but not overly specific," but they don't provide a rigorous justification for why their specific categorization is better than alternatives. They cite previous work as having "weak theoretical background," but don't provide evidence for this claim or show how their approach overcomes it.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their metrics will help guide RC system development. Have you tested whether systems that perform well on datasets with low prerequisite skills also perform better on overall RC tasks? Without this validation, how can you claim the metrics are useful for system development?

2. You state that "the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the difficulty of their questions." Can you provide evidence that this finding isn't simply a result of your specific metric choices? For example, would different readability metrics yield different correlations?

3. Your taxonomy of prerequisite skills seems to be a reorganization of existing concepts rather than a fundamentally new contribution. Could you provide a more thorough comparison with previous taxonomies (like Sugawara et al.) to demonstrate the added value of your approach?

4. You excluded several major RC datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest) from your analysis. What specific criteria did you use to exclude them, and how do you know these datasets wouldn't challenge your main finding about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty?

## Final Recommendation
The paper presents a useful annotated dataset that could be valuable for the community, but the core methodological contribution is not sufficiently novel or validated. The lack of evidence connecting the proposed metrics to actual system performance is a critical weakness that undermines the paper's main claims. While the empirical observation about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is interesting, the paper doesn't provide enough evidence that this observation is meaningful for system development. Given the reviewer's preference for established methods and skepticism toward unproven approaches, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.

**Decision: Reject**
