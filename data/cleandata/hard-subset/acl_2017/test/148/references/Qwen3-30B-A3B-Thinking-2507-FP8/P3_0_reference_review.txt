

# Peer Review for ACL

## Summary

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two complementary dimensions: "prerequisite skills" (the cognitive skills required to answer questions) and "readability" (the difficulty of the text). The authors refine a previous classification of prerequisite skills by grounding it in psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment research. They annotate six major RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these metrics, finding that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (as measured by required prerequisite skills) and text readability. The authors argue that this challenges the assumption that more difficult-to-read texts necessarily lead to more difficult questions.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Theoretical refinement of prerequisite skills classification**: The authors significantly improve upon previous work by grounding their classification of prerequisite skills in psychological theories of text comprehension and textual entailment research, moving beyond superficial categorizations.

2. **Empirical analysis of six major RC datasets**: The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of six widely used RC datasets across two dimensions, revealing important insights about the relationship between question difficulty and text readability.

3. **Publicly available annotation of RC datasets**: The authors make their annotations publicly available, creating a valuable resource for the community that can guide future dataset construction and system development.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper's most significant strength is its well-grounded theoretical foundation. The authors successfully connect their classification of prerequisite skills to psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment research (Dagan et al.). This is a substantial improvement over previous work that lacked theoretical backing for their skill classifications. The authors' reorganization of knowledge reasoning into bridging and elaboration inferences, based on established psychological distinctions, is particularly insightful.

2. **Significant empirical finding**: The key finding that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (as measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability is highly significant. This challenges a common assumption in the field and provides important insights for dataset creators and system developers. The examples they provide (MCTest being easy to read but difficult to answer, SQuAD being difficult to read but relatively easy to answer) are compelling illustrations of this finding.

3. **Practical utility for dataset construction**: The paper provides a clear methodology for evaluating RC datasets that can directly guide researchers in constructing better datasets. The authors explicitly discuss how these metrics can be used in a curriculum learning approach for system development, which is a practical application that would benefit the community. The public availability of their annotations further enhances the practical utility of this work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of connection to actual system performance**: A major limitation is that the paper focuses exclusively on dataset evaluation without connecting these metrics to the performance of actual RC systems. The authors don't analyze how current state-of-the-art RC systems perform on datasets with different combinations of prerequisite skills and readability. Without this connection, it's unclear how their metrics would directly inform system development. For instance, do systems that perform well on datasets with high bridging inference requirements also perform well on other datasets with similar requirements?

2. **Methodological limitations in annotation process**: The authors acknowledge that their methodology cannot evaluate the "competence of selecting necessary sentences to answer questions," which is a fundamental challenge in RC. This is a significant limitation because identifying relevant text segments is a critical component of RC systems. While they provide a brief analysis of sentence distance in Appendix B, this is not integrated into the main analysis, leaving a gap in their evaluation framework.

3. **Ambiguous "nonsense" category**: The paper identifies "nonsense" questions as a category, but it's unclear how the authors determined which questions were nonsense. The annotation process for this category seems subjective, and the paper doesn't provide clear criteria for what constitutes a "nonsense" question. For example, the paper states that "MS MARCO and Who-did-What received relatively high scores" for nonsense questions, but without clear criteria, it's difficult to assess the validity of this finding.

4. **Limited discussion of practical implementation**: While the authors mention using their metrics for curriculum learning, they don't provide concrete examples of how this would work in practice. The discussion of corpus genre and question types is somewhat superficial and doesn't provide actionable insights for dataset creators. A more detailed discussion of how to apply these metrics to guide dataset construction would strengthen the paper.

5. **Lack of validation against human performance**: The paper doesn't establish how well their metrics correlate with human performance on the tasks. Without this validation, it's unclear whether their metrics truly capture the cognitive difficulty of the tasks as humans experience them. The authors could have included a small-scale human evaluation to validate their metrics.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you plan to connect your metrics to actual system performance? Would you consider running experiments to see if systems that perform well on datasets with certain skill requirements also perform well on other datasets with similar requirements?

2. The authors state that "the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the difficulty of their questions." Can you provide specific examples from your dataset analysis where this is clearly demonstrated? For instance, can you identify a dataset that has high readability but requires many prerequisite skills, and vice versa?

3. The paper acknowledges that their methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read." How do you think this limitation affects your overall conclusions about the relationship between prerequisite skills and readability? Could you suggest ways to address this limitation in future work?

4. For the "nonsense" category, what specific criteria did you use to determine if a question was nonsense? How did you handle cases where different annotators disagreed on whether a question was nonsense?

5. How do you envision these metrics being used by researchers who are creating new RC datasets? Could you provide a step-by-step guide for dataset creators on how to apply your framework to ensure their datasets have the desired properties in terms of prerequisite skills and readability?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets. The refinement of prerequisite skills classification and the empirical analysis of six major datasets are significant strengths. However, the lack of connection to actual system performance and the methodological limitations in the annotation process are substantial weaknesses that need to be addressed. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address these weaknesses, particularly by providing a clearer connection between the metrics and system performance, and by addressing the limitations in the annotation methodology.
