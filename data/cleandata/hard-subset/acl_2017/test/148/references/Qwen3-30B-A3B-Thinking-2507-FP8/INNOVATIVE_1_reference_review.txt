

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets based on two complementary dimensions: "prerequisite skills" (which the authors refine from prior work) and "readability" metrics. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics, analyze their relationships, and find that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (as measured by required prerequisite skills) and text readability. The paper makes these annotations publicly available as a resource for the community.

## Primary Contributions
1. A refined theoretical foundation for evaluating RC datasets based on psychological models of text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch).
2. A systematic empirical analysis across six RC datasets revealing the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability.
3. The annotated datasets, which provide a valuable resource for future RC research.

## Strengths

### 1. Identifies a critical gap in RC evaluation
The paper correctly identifies a fundamental problem in the field: RC datasets are currently evaluated with superficial metrics (question types, answer types) rather than meaningful metrics that capture the actual cognitive demands of the tasks. This is a significant gap that has been overlooked in much of the RC literature. The authors' approach to grounding evaluation metrics in psychological research is a welcome step toward more principled evaluation.

### 2. Provides a valuable resource for the community
The annotated datasets (with their prerequisite skills and readability metrics) represent a concrete contribution that will be useful for researchers developing new RC systems and datasets. This is particularly valuable given the current trend toward larger, more complex RC datasets where understanding the specific cognitive demands is increasingly important.

### 3. Challenges an important assumption in the field
The finding that MCTest is easy to read but difficult to answer (Figure 3) is particularly noteworthy. This directly challenges the common assumption that easier-to-read texts will produce easier-to-answer questions, which has implications for how we design and evaluate RC systems. This insight could guide future dataset construction efforts.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Incremental novelty with limited conceptual innovation
While the authors refine the prerequisite skills classification from Sugawara et al. (2017), the changes are largely incremental (e.g., renaming "list/enumeration" to "object tracking," merging some skills). The core framework is not fundamentally new, and the paper doesn't provide a truly bold new direction for the field. The main novelty is in applying these metrics to multiple datasets rather than in the metrics themselves.

### 2. Limited prescriptive value for RC system development
The paper is largely descriptive rather than prescriptive. While it identifies patterns in existing datasets, it doesn't provide clear guidance for how to build better RC systems or create better datasets. The discussion of "how to utilize the two classes of metrics for system development" (Section 6) is vague and doesn't offer concrete methods or algorithms that would enable researchers to build better systems.

### 3. Methodological limitations in the annotation process
The paper doesn't adequately address how they handled annotation disagreements or ensured inter-annotator reliability beyond stating the overall agreement rate (90.1%). The annotation procedure for "nonsense" questions is particularly problematic, as the authors note disagreements about what constitutes a "solvable" question. This raises questions about the robustness of their findings.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to have "revised the previous classification of prerequisite skills for RC" - can you provide specific examples where your classification leads to fundamentally different insights compared to Sugawara et al. (2017), rather than just minor refinements?

2. How would you apply your framework to guide the construction of a new RC dataset that is specifically designed to be challenging in both readability and question difficulty? The paper discusses this in Section 6 but doesn't provide concrete methodology.

3. The paper states that "MS MARCO was a relatively easy dataset in terms of prerequisite skills" but contains many "nonsense" questions. How would you reconcile this apparent contradiction when designing a new dataset? What specific metrics would you use to ensure both quality and appropriate difficulty?

## Overall Assessment

This paper addresses an important gap in RC evaluation and provides a useful resource for the community. However, the novelty is limited to refinement rather than fundamental innovation. While the findings about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty are interesting, they don't represent a significant enough conceptual shift to outweigh the incremental nature of the work.

Given my review criteria that prioritize novel ideas over methodological perfection, I'm somewhat conflicted. The paper's contribution is valuable but not sufficiently bold to warrant acceptance. The insights are useful but not transformative for the field. The paper would be better positioned as a data paper (with the annotated datasets) rather than as a full research paper.

**Decision: Reject** 

While the paper has merit as a resource for the community, the lack of significant conceptual novelty and the incremental nature of the contributions fall short of what would be expected for an ACL publication. The paper doesn't introduce a new direction or fundamentally change how we think about RC evaluation.
