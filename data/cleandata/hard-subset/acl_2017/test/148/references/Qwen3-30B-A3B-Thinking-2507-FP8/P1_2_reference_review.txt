

# ACL Peer Review

## Summary

This paper proposes a systematic framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two orthogonal dimensions: "prerequisite skills" (the cognitive skills required to answer questions) and "readability" (the linguistic complexity of the text). The authors argue that current RC datasets are evaluated using superficial metrics (e.g., question types), and that there's a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability. They refine the classification of prerequisite skills based on psychological research, define readability metrics based on linguistic features, and annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics. Their analysis reveals that there's only a weak correlation between the two dimensions, suggesting that RC datasets can be difficult to answer even with easy-to-read text.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **A systematic framework for evaluating RC datasets**: The paper provides a principled approach to evaluating RC datasets using two orthogonal dimensions (prerequisite skills and readability) that addresses a significant gap in the field. Previous work has primarily used superficial metrics, but this framework offers a much-needed nuanced understanding of dataset quality.

2. **Refined classification of prerequisite skills**: The authors significantly improve upon previous work by reorganizing the classification of knowledge reasoning skills using insights from textual entailment and psychology. This addresses a key weakness in prior work that had a provisional categorization with weak theoretical grounding.

3. **Large-scale annotation of RC datasets**: The paper provides a publicly available annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics, which will serve as a valuable resource for the community to understand dataset characteristics and guide future dataset construction.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a significant gap in the field**: The paper identifies and addresses a critical gap in how RC datasets are evaluated. Current evaluation practices rely on superficial metrics (e.g., question types), but the authors demonstrate that these don't capture the true difficulty of the tasks. Their framework provides a much-needed way to understand the qualitative characteristics of RC datasets.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The authors ground their work in psychological research on human text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, 2009; Kintsch, 1993), which provides a solid foundation for their metrics. This is a significant improvement over previous work that lacked a strong theoretical basis.

3. **Comprehensive and well-executed annotation**: The annotation procedure is well-described, with inter-annotator agreement of 90.1%. The authors' decision to focus on sentences needed to answer questions rather than the entire context is a practical and reasonable approach. The analysis of six datasets provides a rich comparative perspective.

4. **Valuable insights about dataset characteristics**: The paper reveals important insights about RC datasets, such as the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, and the fact that some datasets (like MCTest) are easy to read but difficult to answer. These insights will be valuable for dataset construction and system development.

5. **Practical utility for the community**: The authors make their annotation results publicly available, which will enable future research on RC dataset construction and system development. The framework they propose can be used to guide curriculum learning approaches for RC systems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear methodology for determining "nonsense" questions**: The paper identifies that some datasets contain nonsense questions (e.g., MS MARCO and Who-did-What), but the methodology for determining which questions are "nonsense" is not clearly defined. The authors mention that they allowed annotators to select "nonsense" for unsolvable questions, but they don't provide clear criteria for this judgment. This is a significant weakness because the reliability of their findings depends on consistent identification of nonsense questions.

2. **Limited discussion of practical implications for system development**: While the paper suggests that their framework could guide curriculum learning for RC systems, it doesn't provide concrete examples of how exactly this would work. The discussion in Section 6 is somewhat vague about how the metrics would be used in practice to develop better RC systems.

3. **No analysis of how metrics correlate with actual system performance**: The paper focuses on the characteristics of the datasets themselves, but doesn't analyze how these metrics correlate with actual system performance. For example, do systems that perform well on datasets with high prerequisite skills also perform well on datasets with high readability? This would strengthen the argument for the usefulness of their metrics.

4. **Lack of comparison with other evaluation frameworks**: The paper doesn't compare their framework with other existing evaluation frameworks for RC datasets. It would be valuable to see how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods.

5. **Unclear handling of multiple-choice questions**: The paper mentions that for multiple-choice questions, they showed all candidates and labeled the correct one with an asterisk, but it doesn't discuss how this affects the annotation of prerequisite skills. For example, does the presence of multiple choices change the skills required to answer the question? This is an important consideration for the validity of their metrics.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide more details on how the annotators determined which questions were "nonsense"? What specific criteria did they use, and how did they resolve disagreements on this point?

2. The paper mentions that their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences to answer questions. How do you think this limitation could be addressed in future work, and how might it affect the interpretation of your results?

3. Could you provide more specific examples of how the proposed metrics could be used in a curriculum learning approach for RC system development? What would be the concrete steps for using these metrics to guide the training process?

4. How do you think the metrics would perform when applied to datasets that use different question-answer formats (e.g., open-ended vs. multiple-choice)? The paper mentions that they handled multiple-choice questions by showing all candidates, but it's not clear how this affects the skill annotation.

5. The paper identifies that NewsQA has no nonsense questions because it includes meta information about question quality. How do you think this meta information was generated, and how reliable is it compared to the annotator judgments in the other datasets?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a principled framework for evaluating RC datasets. The theoretical foundation is strong, the annotation is well-executed, and the insights about dataset characteristics are significant. While there are some weaknesses in the methodology and analysis, particularly regarding the determination of "nonsense" questions and the lack of correlation with system performance, these can be addressed in revision. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
