

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" (to assess question difficulty) and "readability" (to assess text difficulty). The authors claim to have annotated six RC datasets with these metrics to analyze the relationship between question difficulty and text readability. They argue that there is only a weak correlation between these two aspects, which has important implications for dataset construction and system development.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A revised classification of prerequisite skills for RC tasks, organized in terms of textual entailment and human text comprehension
2. A set of readability metrics for evaluating RC datasets
3. Annotation of six RC datasets using these metrics, with results made publicly available

## Strengths
1. **Identification of a meaningful gap**: The paper correctly identifies that current RC datasets lack detailed evaluation metrics beyond superficial categories (question types, answer types), and that readability does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty.

2. **Theoretical grounding**: The authors correctly ground their work in psychological theories of text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, 2009; Kintsch, 1993), which provides a solid foundation for their metric development.

3. **Practical contribution**: The public availability of their annotation results is a valuable contribution that could help future researchers in dataset construction.

## Weaknesses

1. **Fundamental misunderstanding of percentage representation (Critical flaw)**:
   - The authors present Table 2 as "Frequencies (%)" but the numbers don't sum to 100% (e.g., QA4MRE sums to 275.0 instead of 100.0). This indicates a fundamental misunderstanding of how percentages should be reported.
   - This is a severe methodological flaw that undermines the credibility of all quantitative results in the paper. The numbers presented appear to be raw counts, not percentages, but the table is labeled as "Frequencies (%)". This error would be immediately noticed by any reviewer or reader.

2. **Inconsistent dataset coverage and analysis**:
   - The paper claims to have annotated six datasets, but in Section 4.2 they state they excluded CNN/Daily Mail and CBTest. However, their results (Table 2) include six datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA), which contradicts their exclusion statement.
   - The paper states they "randomly selected 100 questions from each dataset" but doesn't provide clear evidence that all six datasets were indeed analyzed with 100 questions each (Table 3 shows averages that don't align with the stated 100 questions per dataset).

3. **Lack of methodological clarity**:
   - The paper claims 90.1% inter-annotator agreement but provides no details on how this was calculated or what the annotation guidelines were.
   - The paper doesn't explain how they determined "nonsense" questions (Table 2 shows "Nonsense" as a category), which is a critical aspect of their analysis.
   - The paper states that "we did not construct this classification to be dependent on RC models," but then uses this classification to analyze RC datasets, creating a circular reasoning problem.

4. **Weak statistical analysis**:
   - The paper claims to have found "weak correlations" (0.025 to 0.416) but doesn't provide the actual correlation coefficients or statistical significance testing. This is a major weakness in their main claim.
   - The paper claims "MCTest is a good example of an RC dataset that is easy to read but difficult to answer," but doesn't provide clear evidence for why MCTest is "difficult to answer" beyond high scores in certain skills, without comparing to other datasets.

5. **Technical and formatting inconsistencies**:
   - Inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "Sugawara et al. (2017)" vs. "Sugawara and Aizawa (2016)").
   - The reference list contains formatting errors (e.g., "Aluisio et al\.,? 2010, Aluisio et al\., ").
   - The paper contains grammatical errors (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look at one sentence" should be "the question is easy to solve by simply looking at one sentence").
   - The paper references "Figure 1" but doesn't provide the figure.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you clarify the discrepancy between your claim of annotating six datasets and the fact that you excluded CNN/Daily Mail and CBTest in Section 4.2? Which six datasets did you actually annotate?

2. In Table 2, you present "Frequencies (%)" but the numbers don't sum to 100%. Could you clarify whether these are raw counts or percentages, and provide the correct percentage values?

3. Could you provide the actual Pearson correlation coefficients (not just the range 0.025 to 0.416) for the relationship between prerequisite skills and readability metrics, along with statistical significance testing?

4. How did you define "nonsense" questions, and what was the process for determining whether a question was nonsense or solvable?

5. Could you provide more details about the inter-annotator agreement calculation (90.1%) and the annotation guidelines?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

This paper has significant methodological flaws that undermine its core claims. The fundamental error in Table 2 (reporting raw counts as percentages) is a critical flaw that invalidates the quantitative results presented. The paper also contains multiple inconsistencies in dataset coverage, methodological details, and statistical analysis that suggest a lack of careful review and preparation.

While the paper identifies a meaningful gap in RC dataset evaluation, the execution is flawed to the point that the results cannot be trusted. The authors' claims about the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability are not properly supported by their data, and the paper's technical errors and inconsistencies prevent it from being considered for publication at ACL.

The paper's main contribution (the revised classification of prerequisite skills) is potentially valuable, but the presentation and analysis are so flawed that this contribution cannot be properly evaluated or utilized by the community. The public availability of the annotation results is a positive aspect, but it's not sufficient to overcome the paper's fundamental methodological shortcomings.
