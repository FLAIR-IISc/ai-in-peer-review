- Summary: Proposes two evaluation metrics for RC — prerequisite skills and readability — and annotates six datasets to show question difficulty and text readability are weakly correlated.

- Contribution 1: Provides a theoretically grounded classification of prerequisite skills based on psychological and textual entailment research.

- Contribution 2: Systematically analyzes six RC datasets and finds a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability.

- Contribution 3: Publishes annotations of these datasets for community use.

- Strength 1: Strong theoretical foundation using established cognitive models and clear distinction between bridging and elaboration inferences.

- Strength 2: Empirical evidence challenging the assumption that harder texts yield harder questions, illustrated with dataset comparisons (e.g., MCTest vs. SQuAD).

- Strength 3: Practical value through public annotations and guidance for curriculum learning.

- Weakness 1: Limited dataset coverage and possible selection bias from excluding prominent datasets (e.g., CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA).

- Weakness 2: Ambiguities and potential overlaps in some skill definitions (e.g., "meta-knowledge," "schematic clause relation," "punctuation") risking annotation inconsistency.

- Weakness 3: Insufficient practical guidance on implementing curriculum learning with the proposed metrics.

- Question 1: Why did QA4MRE show the highest frequency scores — what characteristics of its technical documents explain this?

- Question 2: How would including CNN/Daily Mail (with known quality issues) affect conclusions about the readability–difficulty correlation?

- Question 3: How do you reconcile MS MARCO's low average required skills with 14% of questions marked "nonsense" — what does this imply about question quality vs. skill requirements?

- Overall recommendation: Recommend acceptance — valuable, theoretically grounded framework with strong empirical insight despite some limitations.
