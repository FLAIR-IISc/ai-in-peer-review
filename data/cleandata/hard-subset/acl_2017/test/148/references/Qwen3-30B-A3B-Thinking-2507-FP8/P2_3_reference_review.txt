

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes two complementary classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" (refined from prior work) and "readability." The authors argue that existing RC datasets lack meaningful metrics beyond superficial categories like question types, making it difficult to assess system performance and dataset quality. They systematically annotate six RC datasets using their metrics, finding only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by required prerequisite skills) and text readability.

## Main Contributions
1. **Systematic reorganization of prerequisite skills for RC**, incorporating insights from textual entailment and human text comprehension psychology to create a more comprehensive classification.
2. **First systematic annotation of multiple RC datasets using these metrics**, revealing important characteristics of existing datasets that have been overlooked.
3. **Demonstration of weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability**, challenging the assumption that harder-to-read texts necessarily contain harder questions.

## Strengths

**1. Addressing a meaningful gap in RC evaluation:** The paper correctly identifies a critical problem in RC research: the lack of meaningful metrics beyond simple accuracy and superficial categories. The authors effectively demonstrate how current datasets fail to provide a nuanced understanding of what makes RC questions challenging, which is essential for developing better systems and datasets.

**2. Strong theoretical grounding:** The paper builds on solid foundations from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (textual entailment research), providing a theoretically sound basis for their metrics. Their reorganization of prerequisite skills (e.g., distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration inferences) represents a significant improvement over prior work by Sugawara et al. (2017), which was limited to two datasets and had a weak theoretical basis.

**3. Practical value of the annotation results:** The authors' annotation of six major RC datasets provides concrete, actionable insights. For example, their finding that MCTest (written for children) is difficult to answer despite being easy to read challenges common assumptions and has clear implications for dataset construction. The results also highlight datasets with problematic characteristics (e.g., MS MARCO's high nonsense question rate), which is valuable for researchers.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited practical guidance for dataset construction:** While the paper identifies important characteristics of existing datasets, it doesn't provide clear methodology for how to use these metrics to *construct* better datasets. The discussion in Section 6 mentions "curriculum learning" as a potential application but doesn't provide concrete steps for how to apply the metrics to dataset design. Without this, the paper remains descriptive rather than prescriptive.

**2. Insufficient analysis of the correlation findings:** The paper reports weak correlations (0.025-0.416) between prerequisite skills and readability metrics but doesn't deeply explore *why* this correlation is weak. For instance, they don't discuss whether this is due to the nature of RC tasks, limitations in their metric definitions, or characteristics of the datasets themselves. A deeper analysis would strengthen their main finding.

**3. Methodological limitations in annotation:** The paper acknowledges limitations in their methodology, particularly regarding "nonsense" questions and the inability to evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences. These limitations significantly impact the validity of their findings, yet they don't sufficiently address how these limitations affect their conclusions. For example, they note that some "nonsense" questions might be solvable with external knowledge, but they don't provide a clear definition of what constitutes a "solvable" question in RC.

**4. Limited discussion of broader implications:** The paper focuses narrowly on RC datasets but doesn't explore how these metrics might apply to other NLP tasks (e.g., summarization, dialogue systems) or how they might evolve with new dataset types (e.g., multilingual, multimodal). This limits the paper's broader impact.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you recommend researchers use these metrics to specifically guide the construction of new RC datasets? What concrete steps would you suggest for dataset creators?

2. The paper reports weak correlations between prerequisite skills and readability. Could you elaborate on the factors that might explain this weak relationship, beyond simply noting that it exists?

3. The annotation methodology doesn't evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences. How might this limitation affect the interpretation of the "number of required skills" metric, and what would be a viable way to incorporate this aspect into future work?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by systematically analyzing RC datasets using two complementary classes of metrics that are theoretically grounded and practically useful. The reorganization of prerequisite skills is particularly well-executed and represents an important improvement over prior work. The annotation results provide significant insights into the characteristics of existing RC datasets that will be valuable for the community.

However, the paper's impact would be significantly strengthened by addressing the methodological limitations and providing more concrete guidance for how these metrics can be used to improve dataset construction and system development. While the findings are important, the paper currently serves more as a descriptive analysis than as a prescriptive framework for future work.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful problem in the RC research community. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, it would be a strong contribution to ACL. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Rating: Strong Accept**
