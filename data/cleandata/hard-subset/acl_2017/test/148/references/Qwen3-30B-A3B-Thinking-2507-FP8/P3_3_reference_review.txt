

# Peer Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes two evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" (to measure question difficulty) and "readability" (to measure text difficulty). The authors annotate six RC datasets with these metrics and find a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, challenging the assumption that easier-to-read texts necessarily lead to easier questions.

## Primary Contributions
1. **Systematic annotation framework** for RC datasets based on cognitive psychology principles (McNamara & Magliano, 2009), moving beyond superficial question-type categorization.
2. **Empirical analysis** demonstrating that question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability are weakly correlated, with concrete examples (e.g., MCTest being easy to read but difficult to answer).
3. **Publicly available dataset annotations** providing researchers with a valuable resource for dataset construction and system development.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a critical gap in RC evaluation**: The paper correctly identifies that current RC datasets lack meaningful metrics beyond superficial categorization. The authors' focus on cognitive psychology foundations (textbase/situation model) provides a theoretically grounded approach to evaluating RC difficulty that goes beyond surface-level analysis.

2. **Valuable empirical findings**: The demonstration that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated (Pearson coefficients 0.025-0.416) is significant and counterintuitive. This finding has important implications for dataset construction, as it shows that RC difficulty cannot be reduced to text complexity alone—MCTest serves as a strong example where narrative texts are easy to read but require complex reasoning.

3. **Practical utility for system development**: The paper proposes a curriculum learning approach for RC systems based on their metrics, suggesting a stepwise development path (easy-to-read/easy-to-answer → easy-to-read/difficult-to-answer → difficult-to-read/difficult-to-answer). This provides a concrete, actionable framework for researchers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in metric refinement**: The authors claim to "revise" Sugawara et al.'s classification, but their changes are mostly minor (e.g., merging mathematical and statistical reasoning, slight rewording of categories). The new "Meta-knowledge" and "Ellipsis" categories don't significantly advance prior work. The most significant contribution is the application of existing metrics to multiple datasets, not the metrics themselves.

2. **Major methodology limitation**: The authors explicitly state their method cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read" (Section 1), which is a core RC skill. Their analysis only covers understanding of contextual entailments, not the document retrieval aspect of RC. This limitation is not adequately addressed, and it's unclear how their metrics would help develop systems that need to select relevant sentences.

3. **Questionable dataset selection**: The exclusion of major datasets like CNN/Daily Mail (due to "anonymization" and errors) and CBTest (labeled as "for language modeling") is problematic. These datasets would have provided valuable comparative data, especially given Chen et al.'s (2016) critique of CNN/Daily Mail's quality. The exclusion of LAMBADA due to "lowercase tokens" is particularly weak justification.

4. **Lack of system performance correlation**: While the paper claims their metrics can guide system development, it provides no evidence that their metrics correlate with actual system performance. The paper analyzes datasets but doesn't evaluate how these metrics relate to model performance on the datasets, which is a critical gap.

5. **Misunderstanding of readability metrics**: The authors use readability metrics designed for human readers (Vajjala & Meurers, 2012) without addressing whether these metrics correlate with machine comprehension difficulty. This is a fundamental methodological flaw, as their entire analysis rests on the assumption that human readability metrics are relevant for RC systems.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would your metrics specifically help address the "competence of selecting necessary sentences" limitation, given that this is a core RC skill?
2. Why should human readability metrics (designed for second-language learners) be assumed to correlate with RC system difficulty, and what evidence supports this assumption?
3. How would your methodology handle the "nonsense" questions you identified in some datasets, and what criteria would you use to define a "solvable" question in RC?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a systematic framework for evaluating RC datasets beyond superficial metrics. The empirical finding about the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability is significant and should inform future dataset construction. However, the methodological limitations, particularly regarding the inability to evaluate sentence selection and the questionable use of human readability metrics, significantly weaken the paper's claims. The novelty is marginal, as the metrics themselves aren't substantially new, though their systematic application to multiple datasets is useful. With substantial revisions addressing these limitations, this paper could make a meaningful contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper's main value lies in the publicly available dataset annotations, which will be useful for future research.
