

# Peer Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" and "readability." The authors argue that current RC datasets are often evaluated with superficial metrics (e.g., question types, answer types), and they propose a more nuanced framework for analyzing dataset quality and difficulty. They refine a previous classification of prerequisite skills (from 10 to 13 skills) based on insights from textual entailment research and psychology (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara and Magliano, 2009), and use readability metrics from Vajjala and Meurers (2012). They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and find that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by required prerequisite skills) and text readability.

## Main Contributions
1. **Systematic annotation of six RC datasets using refined prerequisite skills metrics**, providing valuable insights into the characteristics of these datasets.
2. **Demonstration of the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability**, challenging common assumptions about how RC tasks should be designed.
3. **Revision of the prerequisite skills classification** grounded in psychology and textual entailment research, providing a more comprehensive framework for analyzing RC.

## Strengths
1. **Addresses a significant gap in RC research**: The paper correctly identifies that current RC datasets are often evaluated with superficial metrics, and the authors provide a much-needed framework for more nuanced analysis of dataset quality. This is an important contribution to the field, as it helps researchers better understand what makes RC tasks difficult and how to design better datasets.

2. **Strong theoretical grounding**: The authors' revision of the prerequisite skills classification is well-grounded in established work in psychology (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara and Magliano, 2009) and textual entailment (Dagan et al., 2013; LoBue and Yates, 2011). This theoretical foundation gives their framework credibility and makes it more likely to be adopted by the community.

3. **Valuable empirical insights**: The analysis of six different RC datasets provides concrete evidence that there's only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by required prerequisite skills) and text readability. This finding is particularly significant because it challenges the common assumption that difficult text automatically leads to difficult questions, which has implications for dataset construction.

4. **Practical utility for dataset construction**: The paper's discussion of how to use these metrics for curriculum learning in RC system development is a strong practical contribution. The authors provide a clear roadmap for step-by-step system development based on dataset properties, which could guide future work in the field.

5. **Publicly available annotation results**: The authors make their annotation results publicly available, which is a valuable resource for the community that can help guide future dataset construction and system development.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient justification for skill classification changes**: The paper mentions revising the classification from 10 to 13 skills but doesn't provide sufficient justification for the specific changes made. For example, the authors mention merging "statistical and quantitative reasoning with mathematical reasoning" but don't clearly explain why this was necessary or how it improves upon the previous classification. The paper states that previous categorizations were "provisional with a weak theoretical background," but doesn't adequately demonstrate why their revision is theoretically superior.

2. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper claims to find a "weak correlation" between question difficulty and readability, but the correlation coefficients reported (0.025 to 0.416) are quite low. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these correlations, which weakens the strength of their main finding. Without significance testing, it's difficult to determine if the observed correlations are meaningful or just noise.

3. **Insufficient description of annotation methodology**: The paper states that inter-annotator agreement was 90.1%, but doesn't provide details about how disagreements were resolved or how annotators were trained. This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess the reliability of their annotations. The paper also doesn't explain why they chose to use three annotators specifically, or how they ensured consistency across annotators.

4. **Limited discussion of related work**: The paper doesn't adequately compare their metric framework to other existing frameworks for RC dataset evaluation. It would have been helpful to see how their approach differs from or improves upon previous work in this area, such as the work by Chen et al. (2016) on dataset quality.

5. **Underdeveloped discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges that their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences to answer questions, but doesn't adequately discuss how this limitation affects their results or what additional work would be needed to address it. This is a significant limitation, especially since sentence selection is a crucial component of RC systems.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims a "weak correlation" between prerequisite skills and readability, but the correlation coefficients reported are quite low (0.025 to 0.416). Could the authors provide statistical significance testing for these correlations to strengthen their claim?

2. In their revision of the prerequisite skills, they merged several skills (e.g., statistical and quantitative reasoning with mathematical reasoning). Could the authors provide more detailed justification for these specific changes, especially since they claim the previous categorization was "provisional with a weak theoretical background"?

3. The paper mentions that their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences to answer questions. How might this limitation affect the interpretation of their results, and what additional work would be needed to address this limitation?

4. The paper doesn't adequately compare their metric framework to other existing frameworks for RC dataset evaluation. Could the authors provide a more thorough comparison with previous work in this area?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a more nuanced framework for evaluating RC datasets. The theoretical grounding is strong, and the empirical analysis provides important insights about the relationship between question difficulty and text readability. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing statistical significance testing for their correlation claims and by better justifying their revisions to the prerequisite skills classification. 

With these improvements, this paper could be a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid paper with clear value but some important limitations that need to be addressed. I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors will address these weaknesses in their revision.
