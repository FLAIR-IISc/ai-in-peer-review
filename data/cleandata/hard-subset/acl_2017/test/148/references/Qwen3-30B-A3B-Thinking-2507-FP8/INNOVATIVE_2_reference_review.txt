

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets by introducing two complementary classes of metrics: (1) "prerequisite skills" that capture the cognitive demands of answering questions (refining prior work with psychological foundations), and (2) "readability" metrics that assess the linguistic complexity of text. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and demonstrate that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability. This challenges the assumption that harder-to-read texts automatically lead to more difficult RC questions.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **A novel framework for RC dataset evaluation** that systematically integrates psychological insights about text comprehension into NLP evaluation, providing a more nuanced understanding of what makes RC tasks challenging.
2. **A refined classification of prerequisite skills** that is theoretically grounded in cognitive psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), moving beyond previous ad-hoc categorizations.
3. **The significant empirical insight** that question difficulty and text readability are weakly correlated in existing RC datasets, with important implications for dataset construction.

## Strengths

### 1. Novel conceptual framework for RC evaluation
The paper's most significant contribution is its integration of psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch's C-I model, McNamara & Magliano's distinctions) into NLP evaluation. This is a genuinely novel approach that hasn't been systematically applied to RC evaluation before. By framing RC evaluation through the lens of human cognitive processes, the paper offers a fresh perspective that could fundamentally change how we design and evaluate RC tasks. This conceptual novelty outweighs the fact that the individual metrics (readability features, some skill categories) are built on prior work.

### 2. Important empirical insight with practical implications
The key finding that question difficulty and text readability are weakly correlated is both novel and practically significant. The examples in Figure 1 (SQuAD vs. MCTest) perfectly illustrate this insight. This challenges a common assumption in the field and has clear implications for dataset construction: we can create difficult questions from easy-to-read texts (as with MCTest) or easy questions from hard-to-read texts (as with SQuAD). This insight is valuable for researchers building new datasets and systems, as it suggests that dataset difficulty can be controlled independently through question design.

### 3. Practical utility and community contribution
The authors provide a refined skill classification with clear definitions, demonstrate its application across six major RC datasets, and make their annotation results publicly available. This creates a valuable resource for the community. The methodology is designed to be general and applicable to many RC architectures, not tied to specific models, which increases its potential impact. The paper also provides concrete guidance for dataset construction (e.g., "attention should be paid to the genre of corpus used to construct a dataset").

## Weaknesses

### 1. Limited novelty of core components
While the *integration* of these metrics is novel, the individual components (readability metrics, some skill categories) are largely built on prior work (Sugawara et al., Vajjala & Meurers). The paper doesn't introduce fundamentally new theoretical concepts, but rather synthesizes and applies existing ones. This is a significant weakness for a conference that values bold new directions. The contribution is more about systematic application than about introducing a groundbreaking new idea.

### 2. Methodological limitations that undermine the framework's completeness
The paper explicitly acknowledges a major limitation: "our methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read to answer questions." This is a critical flaw for an RC evaluation framework, as the ability to identify relevant text is a core component of RC. The paper doesn't adequately address how this limitation affects the utility of their framework, which undermines the claim that it provides a comprehensive evaluation approach.

### 3. Theoretical grounding is insufficiently developed
While the paper cites psychological research (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), it doesn't deeply engage with the limitations of applying these theories to machine comprehension. For example, it doesn't address how human comprehension processes differ from machine processing, which is crucial for an RC evaluation framework. The connection between psychological concepts and practical RC evaluation remains somewhat superficial.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you plan to address the limitation that your framework cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read to answer questions"? Could this limitation be overcome by incorporating additional metrics or modifying the annotation procedure?

2. Given that you acknowledge the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, how might this insight change the design of future RC datasets? Could you provide specific examples of how dataset creators might use your framework to construct datasets with controlled difficulty profiles?

3. The paper mentions that "MS MARCO was a relatively easy dataset in terms of prerequisite skills" but contained "nonsense questions." How might your framework be extended to automatically detect such nonsense questions, and how would this impact the evaluation of RC systems?

## Overall Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by introducing a novel framework for RC dataset evaluation that integrates psychological insights into NLP. While the individual components aren't entirely new, their systematic application to RC evaluation represents a meaningful step forward. The key insight about the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability is significant and practically useful for dataset construction.

The paper is not groundbreaking in the sense of introducing a completely new theoretical framework or model, but it is a bold step toward more nuanced RC evaluation. Given the reviewer's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper meets ACL's standards for acceptance. The practical utility of the framework and the publicly available annotation results further strengthen its contribution to the field.

The methodological limitations noted are important but not fatal to the paper's core contribution. The authors have clearly articulated the scope and limitations of their work, and the framework still provides valuable insights for the community.
