

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets based on two classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" (which capture the cognitive skills required to answer questions) and "readability" (which captures the difficulty of the text itself). The authors refine Sugawara et al.'s (2017) classification of prerequisite skills by grounding it in psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara & Magliano, 2009) and textual entailment research (Dagan et al., 2013; LoBue & Yates, 2011). They also adopt readability metrics from Vajjala and Meurers (2012). The authors annotate six major RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these metrics and analyze the results to show that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (as measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills for reading comprehension:** The authors provide a theoretically grounded, comprehensive classification of cognitive skills required for RC, significantly improving upon previous work by addressing its limitations (limited scope, weak theoretical grounding).
2. **Systematic annotation of six RC datasets with the refined metrics:** The authors provide a substantial, publicly available annotation effort across six major RC datasets, creating a valuable resource for the community.
3. **Empirical analysis showing weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability:** The paper demonstrates through empirical analysis that the readability of text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of questions, challenging a common assumption in the field.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation for the prerequisite skills taxonomy:** The authors have made a significant contribution by grounding their taxonomy in established psychological theories (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara & Magliano, 2009) and textual entailment research (Dagan et al., 2013; LoBue & Yates, 2011). The distinction between bridging and elaboration inferences is particularly well-justified, addressing a key limitation in previous work that simply categorized "knowledge reasoning" as a single skill. This refined taxonomy provides a more nuanced understanding of what makes RC questions difficult.

2. **Substantial and well-executed dataset annotation effort:** The authors performed a large-scale annotation of six major RC datasets with high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%). The annotation procedure is well-described, and the public availability of the results makes this a valuable resource for the community. The analysis across multiple datasets provides meaningful comparative insights that go beyond previous single-dataset analyses.

3. **Important empirical insight about the disconnect between question difficulty and text readability:** The paper's main finding—that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (as measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability—challenges a common assumption in the field and has significant implications for RC dataset construction. The examples provided (particularly the MCTest dataset) effectively illustrate this point, showing that easy-to-read texts can contain difficult questions requiring multiple cognitive skills.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of evaluation showing how metrics correlate with actual system performance:** The paper focuses on evaluating datasets but fails to demonstrate how these metrics correlate with the performance of actual RC systems. Without evidence that the metrics predict system performance or can guide system development, it's unclear how useful they would be for practitioners. The discussion in Section 6 about using the metrics for system development is too high-level and lacks supporting evidence.

2. **Insufficient criteria for identifying "nonsense" questions:** The paper identifies that some datasets contain "nonsense" questions (e.g., MS MARCO and Who-did-What), but the methodology for determining whether a question is "nonsense" is not well-defined. The authors note disagreements among annotators on this point, but provide no clear criteria for making this judgment. This is a significant limitation as it affects the quality of the dataset analysis and the interpretation of results.

3. **Limited discussion of how the annotation methodology's limitations affect the results:** The authors acknowledge their methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting necessary sentences that need to be read" (Section 1, Section 6), but they don't fully explore how this limitation affects their results. For example, they briefly mention sentence distance (Table 6), but don't integrate this into their main analysis. The paper would be stronger if it discussed how this limitation might impact the interpretation of the "number of required skills" metric.

4. **Lack of comparison to other dataset analysis frameworks:** The paper doesn't compare its proposed metrics to other frameworks for analyzing RC datasets (e.g., the analysis of question types or answer types mentioned in the introduction). This makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed framework is truly novel or just another way of categorizing the same information. A comparison to existing frameworks would strengthen the paper's contribution claim.

5. **Limited discussion of how to practically implement the metrics for system development:** While Section 6 discusses using the metrics for system development, the discussion is quite high-level and lacks concrete examples or evidence. The authors suggest a "curriculum learning" approach but don't provide evidence that this would actually improve system performance or explain how to implement it.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences. How would you modify your annotation procedure to better capture this aspect of RC, which seems important for real-world applications?

2. The paper states that "RC datasets that are difficult to read are not necessarily difficult to answer." However, it's not clear how "difficult to read" is defined for the purpose of this paper. The readability metrics are based on linguistic features, but how do these metrics correlate with human difficulty of reading? How would you expect these metrics to correlate with system difficulty?

3. The paper identifies "nonsense" questions in some datasets. What criteria would you use to define a "nonsense" question in an RC dataset, and how would you handle such questions in a training or evaluation setting?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection** for this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution through its refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills and the substantial annotation effort across six datasets. However, the lack of evaluation showing how these metrics correlate with actual system performance is a significant weakness that limits the paper's practical impact. The paper's main contribution (the refined taxonomy) is strong, but the paper fails to sufficiently demonstrate how this taxonomy can be used to improve RC systems, which is the ultimate goal of the work.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add an analysis showing how the metrics correlate with system performance:** The authors should collect or obtain performance data for multiple RC systems on the annotated datasets and analyze how the metrics correlate with system performance. This would provide strong evidence for the utility of the metrics.

2. **Refine the criteria for identifying "nonsense" questions:** The authors should develop and document clear criteria for determining whether a question is "nonsense" and apply these consistently across all datasets.

3. **Expand the discussion of methodology limitations:** The authors should more thoroughly discuss how the limitations of their annotation methodology (particularly regarding sentence selection) affect their results and analysis.

4. **Compare to existing dataset analysis frameworks:** The authors should explicitly compare their framework to other existing approaches for analyzing RC datasets to better establish the novelty and value of their contribution.

5. **Provide concrete examples of how to use the metrics for system development:** The authors should provide specific, actionable examples of how the metrics could be used to guide the development of RC systems, rather than just discussing high-level concepts.

This is a promising paper with significant potential, but it requires substantial revision to strengthen the connection between the proposed metrics and practical system development. With these improvements, it could make a valuable contribution to the field.
