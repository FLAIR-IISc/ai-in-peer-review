

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" (refined from Sugawara et al., 2017) and "readability." The authors annotate six RC datasets with these metrics to analyze the relationship between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability. The claimed contributions are: (1) adopting two classes of evaluation metrics to demonstrate weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability; (2) revising the classification of prerequisite skills based on textual entailment and human text comprehension; and (3) annotating six RC datasets with these metrics and making results publicly available.

## Strengths

1. **Important Problem Addressed**: The paper tackles a significant gap in RC research - the lack of fine-grained evaluation metrics beyond simple accuracy. The authors correctly identify that current RC datasets are evaluated with superficial categories (e.g., question types) rather than meaningful metrics that capture the cognitive processes involved in comprehension.

2. **Psychological Foundation**: The paper appropriately grounds its metrics in psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara and Magliano, 2009), which provides a solid theoretical basis for the metrics. This is a strong point that elevates the work beyond typical NLP evaluation approaches.

3. **Empirical Annotation**: The annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics provides valuable empirical data for the community. The authors have made the results publicly available, which is a significant contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Data Presentation Errors**: Tables 2 and 3 are labeled as "Frequencies (%)" but present absolute counts instead of percentages. For example, Table 2 shows "11.0" for "Object tracking" in QA4MRE, but this is not a percentage. This is a fundamental error in data presentation that undermines the paper's credibility. The numbers in Table 2 don't sum to 100% for any dataset (e.g., QA4MRE: 11.0+4.0+32.0+...+34.0 = 159.0), making the table completely misleading.

2. **Ambiguous Definition of "Nonsense"**: The paper states "the annotators were allowed to select nonsense for an unsolvable question," but fails to provide a clear operational definition of what constitutes a "nonsense" question. This is a critical ambiguity that affects the reliability of the annotation process. For instance, in Table 2, the "Nonsense" row shows percentages that don't align with the interpretation in the text (e.g., "MS MARCO and Who-did-What received relatively high scores" for nonsense).

3. **Inconsistent Terminology and Symbol Usage**: The paper uses asterisks (∗) and daggers (†) to denote modified and new skills, but this notation is inconsistently applied and not clearly explained. For example, "Object tracking*" is described as a renamed version of "list/enumeration," but the original classification's "list/enumeration" skill isn't clearly defined in the paper. The inconsistent use of symbols undermines the paper's precision.

4. **Lack of Methodological Details**: The paper states "the inter-annotator agreement was 90.1% for 62 randomly sampled questions" but doesn't specify the metric used (e.g., Cohen's Kappa, Fleiss' Kappa) or how disagreements were resolved. This is a significant methodological omission for a paper that claims to have a rigorous annotation process.

5. **Inconsistency in Skill Classification**: The paper claims to have "revised" the classification of prerequisite skills, but fails to provide evidence that this revision is superior to the original. For example, "Schematic clause relation" and "Punctuation" skills have overlapping descriptions, and it's unclear why they're separate categories.

## Questions to Authors

1. How did you calculate the inter-annotator agreement (Cohen's Kappa, Fleiss' Kappa, etc.)? What was the baseline agreement?

2. Tables 2 and 3 are labeled as "Frequencies (%)" but show absolute counts. Please clarify whether this was a formatting error and provide corrected tables with percentages.

3. Please provide a clear, operational definition of "nonsense" questions that was used during the annotation process. How did you ensure consistency in this definition across annotators?

4. Why is "Schematic clause relation" a separate skill from "Punctuation" when both involve understanding sentence structure? Please clarify the distinction between these two skills.

5. The paper claims to have "revised the previous classification of prerequisite skills," but doesn't provide evidence that this revision improves reliability or validity. Please provide evidence that your new classification is more reliable or valid than the previous one.

## Final Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper addresses an important problem in reading comprehension research and has a theoretically grounded approach, the numerous methodological and presentation flaws are severe enough to warrant rejection. The critical data presentation errors in Tables 2 and 3 (showing absolute counts instead of percentages) fundamentally undermine the paper's credibility. The ambiguous definition of "nonsense" questions, inconsistent terminology, and lack of methodological details further weaken the paper.

These are not minor issues - they are fundamental to the paper's evaluation methodology and results. Given the paper's claim to be meticulous in its approach, these errors are particularly disappointing and inconsistent with the standards expected for ACL publication. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmitting to a venue like ACL.
