

# Peer Review for ACL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets by introducing two complementary classes of metrics: prerequisite skills (for evaluating the difficulty of answering questions) and readability (for evaluating the difficulty of the text). The authors refine a previous classification of prerequisite skills using insights from psychology and NLP, and apply these metrics to annotate six existing RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA). Their key finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the readability of the text and the difficulty of the questions, challenging common assumptions in RC research.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **A theoretically grounded refinement of prerequisite skills classification** for RC, which is more comprehensive and better justified than previous work.
2. **Empirical analysis demonstrating the weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty** across multiple RC datasets.
3. **A practical framework for step-by-step RC system development** based on the proposed metrics, which could guide researchers in dataset construction and system evaluation.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical foundation of the prerequisite skills classification**: The authors have made a significant contribution by grounding their classification of prerequisite skills in established psychological theories (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP work on textual entailment (Dagan, LoBue & Yates). This is a major improvement over previous work that often had weak theoretical foundations. The 13 refined skills (with clear distinctions between bridging and elaboration inferences) provide a much more comprehensive and nuanced understanding of the cognitive processes required for RC than previous classifications.

2. **Compelling empirical finding on readability vs. question difficulty**: The paper's most significant contribution is demonstrating that readability and question difficulty are only weakly correlated (with correlation coefficients ranging from 0.025 to 0.416). This challenges a common assumption in RC research that difficult-to-read texts automatically lead to difficult questions. The analysis across six diverse datasets (including examples like MCTest, which is easy to read but difficult to answer) provides strong evidence for this claim. This insight has important implications for both dataset construction and system evaluation.

3. **Practical utility for RC system development**: The authors propose a concrete methodology for building RC systems in a stepwise manner (from easy-to-read/easy-to-answer to difficult-to-read/difficult-to-answer datasets), which is directly applicable to real-world system development. This approach resembles curriculum learning but is specifically tailored to RC, offering a valuable practical contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **The metrics do not capture the full complexity of RC**: The authors explicitly acknowledge that their methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read" (section 1 and 6), which is a fundamental aspect of reading comprehension. This is a critical limitation because in real RC tasks, identifying the relevant context is often as challenging as understanding the text. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing how this limitation could be overcome or how the metrics could be extended to include this aspect.

2. **Limited scope of datasets analyzed**: The authors exclude several important RC datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) for various reasons, but they don't sufficiently justify why these exclusions don't affect the generalizability of their findings. The paper would be more compelling if it could analyze a more representative sample of RC datasets, including those that have been identified as problematic (like CNN/Daily Mail, which Chen et al. (2016) showed has significant quality issues).

3. **Lack of comparison with other evaluation frameworks**: The paper does not compare its proposed metrics with other approaches to RC evaluation (e.g., the work by Chen et al. on dataset quality or the various metrics used in the QA4MRE challenge). This makes it difficult to assess the novelty and relative value of the proposed metrics. A comparison would help establish how the authors' framework improves upon or complements existing approaches.

4. **Insufficient discussion of practical implementation**: While the authors mention using the metrics for step-by-step system development (section 6), they don't provide concrete examples of how researchers could actually implement this approach. The paper is more focused on analysis than on practical application, which limits its immediate utility for the community.

5. **Limited discussion of the "nonsense question" issue**: The paper identifies that some datasets (like MS MARCO and Who-did-What) contain a significant number of nonsense questions, but it doesn't deeply explore the implications of this for RC research. This is a critical issue for dataset quality, yet the discussion remains somewhat superficial.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper acknowledges that the methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting relevant sentences for answering questions. How do you plan to address this limitation in future work, and how would you incorporate this aspect into your evaluation framework?

2. You state that "the context texts of MCTest dataset are easy to read, but the difficulty of the questions is comparable to that of the other datasets." How do you reconcile this with MCTest's design as a dataset for children's reading level? Is the difficulty of the questions related to the narrative genre, and if so, what implications does this have for creating more effective RC datasets?

3. The paper mentions that "MS MARCO was a relatively easy dataset in terms of prerequisite skills" but also "contained nonsense questions." How do you suggest researchers should handle datasets with a high proportion of nonsense questions when using your metrics for system development?

4. The paper identifies NewsQA as advantageous because it provides meta information on question reliability. How do you think the field could standardize this kind of meta information across datasets to improve overall dataset quality?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and helpful figures/tables that effectively present the analysis. The authors have made their annotations publicly available, which is a valuable contribution to the community. The theoretical grounding of the prerequisite skills classification is particularly strong and represents a significant advance over previous work in this area.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their metrics could be integrated into existing RC evaluation pipelines and how they might complement other evaluation approaches. A case study showing how a specific RC system could be improved using their metrics would also strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that provides valuable theoretical grounding and empirical insights for the RC community. While there are some limitations, the work addresses an important gap in the field and provides a framework that could guide future research in RC dataset construction and system evaluation.
