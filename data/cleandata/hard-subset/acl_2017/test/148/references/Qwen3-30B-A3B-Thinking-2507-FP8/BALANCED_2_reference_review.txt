

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a two-class framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets, consisting of "prerequisite skills" (to assess the difficulty of answering questions) and "readability" (to assess the difficulty of reading the text). The authors refine the prerequisite skills classification based on psychological theories of text comprehension and apply it to six major RC datasets. They find that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability, which has important implications for dataset construction and system development.

The main contributions of the paper are:
1. A novel two-class framework for analyzing RC datasets (prerequisite skills and readability) that reveals the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability.
2. A refined classification of prerequisite skills grounded in psychological theories of text comprehension, with a more systematic organization of knowledge reasoning skills.
3. A large-scale annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics, making the results publicly available for the community.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical foundation and systematic refinement of prerequisite skills**: The paper makes a significant contribution by grounding the classification of prerequisite skills in psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). The authors provide a clear justification for their reorganization of skills (e.g., distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration inferences), which addresses limitations in previous work that had "provisional categorization with weak theoretical background." This systematic approach to classifying skills is much more rigorous than the superficial question-type classifications commonly used in RC research.

2. **Practical value of the annotated datasets**: The authors provide a valuable resource by annotating six major RC datasets with their metrics. The results reveal important insights about dataset characteristics that have not been previously documented in the literature. For example, the finding that MCTest (a narrative dataset for children) is easy to read but difficult to answer challenges the assumption that text readability directly correlates with question difficulty. This is a significant contribution to the field as it provides a more nuanced understanding of dataset properties.

3. **Clear demonstration of the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability**: The paper provides compelling evidence through multiple analyses (Tables 2-5, Figures 3-4) that the readability of context texts does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions. This is an important insight that has practical implications for dataset construction and system development. The authors effectively use examples from Figure 1 to illustrate this point, showing that a simple question from a complex text (SQuAD) can be easier than a complex question from a simple text (MCTest).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of empirical validation of the metrics' usefulness**: While the paper provides a detailed framework for evaluating RC datasets, it does not demonstrate how these metrics can actually be used to improve RC systems or datasets. The authors mention "curriculum learning" as a potential application, but fail to provide concrete examples of how the metrics would guide system development. Without evidence that these metrics correlate with human performance or system performance, it's difficult to assess their practical value for the community.

2. **Significant methodological limitation in the annotation approach**: The authors explicitly state that their methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read to answer questions," which is a fundamental aspect of RC. This is a major limitation because finding relevant text passages is a core challenge in RC. While they briefly analyze sentence distance (Section 6), this is not integrated into their main framework. This limitation significantly reduces the applicability of their metrics to real-world RC systems.

3. **Insufficient comparison with existing work**: The paper acknowledges that Sugawara et al. (2017) analyzed only two datasets and had a "provisional categorization," but it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of their classification with the previous work to demonstrate the specific improvements. A more thorough comparison would strengthen their claim of a refined classification.

4. **Lack of discussion on practical implementation**: The paper doesn't address how the proposed metrics could be implemented in practice. For example, how would a dataset creator use these metrics to construct better datasets? How would a system developer use them to evaluate their models? The discussion section mentions "curriculum learning" but doesn't provide concrete examples of how the metrics would be used in this context.

5. **Inadequate exploration of the "nonsense" questions**: The authors note that some datasets contain "nonsense" questions (e.g., MS MARCO has 14% nonsense questions), but they don't discuss the implications of this finding or how it relates to their framework. This is particularly important given that Chen et al. (2016) previously identified similar issues with dataset quality.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you validate that your metrics actually correlate with human performance or system performance? Without this validation, it's unclear whether the metrics have practical value for the community.

2. The paper acknowledges that the methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting necessary sentences," which is a major limitation. How do you plan to address this limitation in future work, and how would you integrate this aspect into your framework?

3. The paper identifies MCTest as having easy-to-read but hard-to-answer questions. Could you provide specific examples of questions from MCTest that require multiple prerequisite skills, and explain why they are challenging in the context of the dataset?

4. The authors state that "RC datasets that are difficult to read are not necessary difficult to answer." What are the theoretical reasons behind this observation, and how does it relate to the psychological theories of text comprehension that the paper is grounded in?

5. The paper mentions that NewsQA is advantageous because it provides meta information about question reliability. How could this information be integrated into your framework to improve dataset construction and system evaluation?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets that addresses an important gap in the literature. The refinement of prerequisite skills is well-justified, and the annotation of six major datasets provides a valuable resource for the community. However, the paper would benefit significantly from more concrete demonstrations of how these metrics can be used to improve RC systems or datasets, as well as addressing the methodological limitation regarding sentence selection.

While the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a more nuanced understanding of RC dataset properties, the lack of empirical validation and practical implementation guidance limits its immediate impact. With the suggested revisions, particularly addressing the methodological limitations and providing concrete examples of how the metrics could be used, this paper could make a strong contribution to the ACL community.

**Decision**: Weak Accept (with major revisions required)

**Justification**: The paper presents a novel and well-justified framework for evaluating RC datasets, which addresses an important gap in the literature. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the annotated datasets provide a valuable resource. However, the lack of empirical validation of the metrics' usefulness and the significant methodological limitation regarding sentence selection need to be addressed. With revisions to demonstrate practical utility and address these limitations, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field.
