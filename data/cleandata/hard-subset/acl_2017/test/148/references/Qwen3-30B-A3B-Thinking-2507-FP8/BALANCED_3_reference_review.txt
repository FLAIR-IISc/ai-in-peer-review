

# ACL Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors argue that existing RC datasets are typically evaluated only with superficial metrics (e.g., question types), making it difficult to assess system performance meaningfully. The paper's main contributions are:

1. A refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC, grounded in psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara and Magliano, 2009) and textual entailment (Dagan et al., 2013; LoBue and Yates, 2011), reorganized from 10 to 13 skills with clear theoretical justification.

2. An analysis of six major RC datasets using these metrics, demonstrating that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by required prerequisite skills) and text readability.

3. The public release of annotated datasets, which will help researchers design better RC datasets and develop more targeted RC systems.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Gap Addressed**: The paper identifies a critical gap in RC evaluation - the lack of meaningful metrics beyond superficial categories like question types. The examples in Figure 1 effectively illustrate the disconnect between text readability and question difficulty, providing strong motivation for the work. This is a genuine contribution to the field that has been overlooked in much of the RC literature.

2. **Theoretical Foundation and Rigorous Skill Reorganization**: The authors' reorganization of prerequisite skills is a substantial improvement over previous work (Sugawara et al., 2017). By grounding the classification in psychological research and textual entailment theory, they provide a more comprehensive and theoretically sound framework. The distinction between bridging (grammatical/lexical knowledge) and elaboration (general commonsense knowledge) is particularly well-justified and useful for understanding RC challenges.

3. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis**: The annotation of six major RC datasets with the proposed metrics provides valuable insights into dataset characteristics. The key finding that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated (Figures 3-4, Table 5) is significant and challenges common assumptions in the field. The analysis of MCTest as an example of an easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer dataset is particularly compelling.

4. **Practical Utility**: The paper offers concrete suggestions for how the metrics could be used in practice, such as developing RC systems through a curriculum learning approach (starting with easier datasets and progressing to more difficult ones). This bridges the gap between theoretical analysis and practical application, making the work immediately useful for researchers.

5. **Rigorous Annotation Process**: The authors report high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), indicating that their metrics are well-defined and the annotation procedure is robust. The detailed description of the annotation process (Section 4.1) and dataset selection criteria (Appendix A) adds credibility to their work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of System Evaluation**: The paper claims to evaluate "both the quality of datasets and the performance of systems," but the system performance evaluation is entirely absent. The metrics are applied only to datasets, not to system outputs. Without demonstrating how these metrics would be used to evaluate system performance, the primary goal of RC evaluation remains unaddressed. The authors should clarify how these metrics would be integrated into system evaluation pipelines.

2. **Limited Dataset Scope**: The authors exclude several major RC datasets (CNN/Daily Mail, CBTest, LAMBADA) based on subjective criteria (e.g., "anonymized," "for language modeling tasks"). This selection bias limits the generalizability of their findings. The exclusion of CNN/Daily Mail, which has been widely studied, is particularly problematic given Chen et al. (2016) already identified quality issues with this dataset.

3. **Ambiguous Definition of "Nonsense" Questions**: The paper notes significant disagreement about which questions are "nonsense" (e.g., MS MARCO had high nonsense scores), but provides no clear, objective criterion for this classification. This undermines the reliability of the results, as the interpretation of "nonsense" questions varies between annotators and affects the analysis.

4. **Readability Metrics' Relevance for RC Systems**: The authors acknowledge that readability metrics "do not necessarily correlate with those for RC systems," yet they don't provide evidence that these metrics are relevant for RC systems. Without demonstrating that readability metrics predict system difficulty, the entire readability analysis lacks justification for RC tasks.

5. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper makes strong claims about weak correlations (Table 5), but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these correlations. Without p-values or confidence intervals, it's unclear whether the observed correlations are meaningful or due to chance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to evaluate "both the quality of datasets and the performance of systems," but the system performance evaluation is not conducted. Could you clarify how these metrics would be used to evaluate system performance, and provide a concrete example of how you would apply them to a system's outputs?

2. You excluded CNN/Daily Mail based on it being "anonymized and contains some errors." Given that many datasets have quality issues, could you provide more justification for excluding this dataset specifically, and discuss how this exclusion might affect the generalizability of your findings?

3. The paper mentions disagreements about "nonsense" questions but doesn't define what constitutes a "nonsense" question. Could you provide a clear, objective criterion for determining whether a question is nonsense, and explain how you would handle ambiguous cases in future work?

4. You state that readability metrics "do not necessarily correlate with those for RC systems." Could you provide evidence or examples showing that your readability metrics are relevant for RC systems, rather than just for human readability?

5. Your methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read," yet you include sentence distance analysis (Table 6). How does this analysis relate to your stated limitation, and what are the implications for your overall framework?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper addresses a genuine gap in RC evaluation with a theoretically grounded approach that could significantly benefit the field. The reorganization of prerequisite skills is well-justified and represents a meaningful improvement over previous work. The dataset analysis provides valuable insights, particularly about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

However, the lack of system evaluation and the exclusion of major datasets based on subjective criteria significantly weaken the paper's contribution. The ambiguity around "nonsense" questions and the lack of statistical significance testing for key claims also undermine the paper's impact.

**Decision**: **Weak Reject**

The paper has several important strengths that suggest it could be a valuable contribution, but the weaknesses are substantial enough to prevent acceptance at ACL. With significant revisions addressing the weaknesses (particularly adding system evaluation, providing a clear criterion for "nonsense" questions, and justifying dataset selection), this work could make a strong contribution to the field.

## Suggestions for Revision

1. Conduct a system evaluation using the proposed metrics to demonstrate how they can be applied to assess RC system performance.

2. Justify the exclusion of major RC datasets more thoroughly, and consider including at least one excluded dataset to demonstrate the robustness of your framework.

3. Develop and provide a clear, objective criterion for identifying "nonsense" questions, with examples to illustrate the boundary cases.

4. Provide statistical significance testing for the correlation claims in Table 5.

5. Discuss the relevance of readability metrics specifically for RC systems, not just human readability, with evidence or examples.

6. Address the tension between your stated methodology limitations (cannot evaluate sentence selection competence) and the sentence distance analysis presented in Table 6.
