This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors adopt two classes of metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – to analyze six existing datasets and annotate them accordingly. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating a weak correlation between these two factors, suggesting that difficult questions can be posed within easy-to-read texts and vice-versa. A secondary contribution involves a revised categorization of prerequisite skills, drawing on textual entailment and psychological models of human text comprehension. Finally, the authors publicly release the annotated datasets, providing a valuable resource for the RC research community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Important Investigation:** The central question explored – the relationship between question difficulty and text readability – is a crucial one for advancing the field of RC. Existing evaluations often focus solely on accuracy, overlooking the nuanced characteristics of datasets and the specific challenges they pose. Identifying this weak correlation is a significant insight, highlighting the need for more sophisticated dataset construction and evaluation.
2. **Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The adoption of two distinct classes of metrics (prerequisite skills and readability) provides a more holistic assessment of RC datasets than traditional approaches. The grounding of these metrics in established theories from both NLP (textual entailment) and psychology (human text comprehension) strengthens their theoretical foundation and interpretability. The refinement of the prerequisite skills, particularly the organization of knowledge reasoning, is a valuable contribution.
3. **Publicly Available Annotated Data:** The release of the annotated datasets is a significant practical contribution. This resource will enable researchers to conduct further analyses, develop more targeted evaluation methods, and design better RC datasets. The annotation procedure, while having limitations (discussed in weaknesses), provides a valuable starting point for future work.
4. **Detailed Analysis and Discussion:** The paper presents a thorough analysis of the annotated datasets, with clear presentation of results in tables and figures. The discussion section effectively interprets these results and outlines implications for dataset construction and system development, including suggestions for curriculum learning and the importance of genre considerations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Annotation Limitations & Subjectivity:** The annotation process, while reasonably well-defined, is inherently subjective and relies on annotator judgment. The paper acknowledges inter-annotator agreement of 90.1%, but the source of disagreement and its impact on the results aren’t fully explored. More granular analysis of annotation discrepancies would strengthen the validity of the findings. Specifically, the reliance on annotators to determine *necessary* skills is challenging and potentially prone to bias.
2. **Methodological Limitation: Sentence Selection Not Evaluated:** The authors explicitly acknowledge that their methodology doesn't evaluate the ability to identify relevant sentences within the context. This is a critical limitation, as sentence selection is a core component of many RC systems. The analysis focuses solely on understanding *given* context, rather than the process of *finding* it.
3. **Weak Correlation Doesn’t Imply Independence:** While the paper correctly identifies a *weak* correlation between readability and difficulty, it’s important to avoid implying complete independence. Statistical significance of the observed correlations isn’t fully explored. Reporting p-values or confidence intervals would help clarify the strength of the relationship. The discussion could benefit from a more nuanced consideration of potential confounding factors.
4. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** The chosen readability metrics are primarily focused on surface-level linguistic features. While these are relevant, they don’t capture all aspects of text complexity, such as discourse structure or conceptual density. Incorporating more sophisticated measures of text complexity could provide a richer understanding of the relationship between readability and RC difficulty.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the annotation process, including examples of questions where annotators disagreed and how those disagreements were resolved?
2. Given the acknowledged limitation of not evaluating sentence selection, do you foresee any modifications to your methodology to incorporate this critical aspect of RC?
3. What statistical tests were used to determine the significance of the observed correlations between readability metrics and prerequisite skills? Please provide p-values or confidence intervals.
4. How might the findings of this study inform the design of a new RC dataset specifically aimed at addressing the identified gap between readability and question difficulty?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work on dataset bias and fairness in RC.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the characteristics of each annotated dataset (e.g., genre, domain, source).
*   The appendix provides useful details regarding sampling and distance calculations.
