This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets, proposing two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors annotate six existing datasets with these metrics, making the results publicly available. 

The primary contribution of this work is the comprehensive annotation of six RC datasets with both prerequisite skill requirements and readability metrics. This annotated data provides a valuable resource for researchers aiming to better understand the challenges inherent in existing RC datasets and to inform the development of more nuanced evaluation methodologies. While the idea of combining these two aspects isn’t novel, the scale and systematic application to multiple established datasets are significant. 

A secondary, but important, contribution is the refinement of the prerequisite skills classification, drawing on insights from textual entailment and human text comprehension research (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). This provides a more theoretically grounded framework compared to previous work (Sugawara et al., 2017). The reorganization of skills into categories like bridging and elaboration is a positive step towards a more fine-grained analysis of question difficulty.

A third contribution is the demonstration, through analysis of the annotated datasets, that there is a weak correlation between question difficulty (as measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability. This finding highlights the limitations of relying solely on readability metrics to assess the difficulty of RC tasks and reinforces the need for more sophisticated evaluation approaches.



**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Annotated Dataset:** The annotated datasets are a significant contribution to the RC community. They offer a rich source of information for analyzing existing datasets and developing new, more targeted evaluation metrics.  The public availability of this resource will facilitate further research in this area.
2. **Theoretical Grounding:** The revision of prerequisite skills based on research in textual entailment and psychology provides a stronger theoretical foundation for analyzing question difficulty. The connection to established theories in human comprehension lends credibility to the proposed classification scheme. Specifically, anchoring the skills in Kintsch's inferences (retrieved vs generated) and McNamara & Magliano’s bridging vs. elaboration is well-justified.
3. **Empirical Evidence of Weak Correlation:** The finding that readability and question difficulty are not strongly correlated is insightful. This challenges the assumption that simpler text automatically leads to easier questions and underscores the importance of considering the cognitive skills required to answer a question. The results, presented in Table 2 and 3, effectively illustrate these differences across datasets.
4. **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough analysis of six popular RC datasets.  The detailed discussion of each dataset’s characteristics and the differences observed in the annotation results are well-presented.
5. **Clear Methodology:** The annotation procedure is clearly described, including details about annotator guidelines and inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), lending confidence to the reliability of the annotation.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Metrics:** While the application of these metrics to RC is valuable, the individual metrics themselves (readability features, prerequisite skills) are not fundamentally new. The novelty lies in their *combination* and application to a broader set of datasets. The paper could benefit from more explicitly acknowledging this.
2. **Annotation Scope & Limitations:** The annotation focuses solely on *required* skills, not the *sufficiency* of those skills. A question might require skill A, but whether mastering skill A *guarantees* a correct answer isn't assessed.  Furthermore, the methodology, as acknowledged by the authors, does not evaluate the competence of selecting relevant sentences – a crucial aspect of RC.
3. **Correlation Analysis Depth:** The correlation analysis (Table 5) is somewhat superficial. Simply reporting Pearson’s correlation coefficients doesn’t provide a deep understanding of *how* the metrics relate. Investigating specific skills that contribute most to the weak correlation, or exploring non-linear relationships, could offer more valuable insights.
4. **Lack of System-Level Impact:** The paper primarily focuses on dataset analysis. While it suggests potential applications for system development, it doesn't demonstrate how the proposed metrics can be used to *improve* RC systems directly.  A small-scale experiment applying these metrics to guide system training or evaluation would strengthen the contribution.
5. **Discussion of 'Nonsense' Questions:** The discussion around "nonsense" questions is a bit underdeveloped. While identifying these is important, the paper doesn’t delve into the implications for dataset quality control or potential strategies for automatically detecting such questions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The annotation methodology focuses on skills *required* to answer the question. Could you elaborate on the challenges of annotating the *sufficiency* of those skills, and whether that's a direction for future work?
2.  You acknowledge the limitation that the annotation doesn't assess sentence selection competence.  Are there plans to incorporate sentence selection evaluation into a future iteration of this work?  If so, what methodology would you consider?
3.  Given the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, are there other dataset characteristics (e.g., domain specificity, question phrasing) that you suspect might be more strongly correlated with prerequisite skill requirements?
4.  Could you provide specific examples of questions in the datasets where the readability score is high, but the prerequisite skill count is also high, illustrating the disconnect you observed?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is quite extensive, which is positive.
*   The figures are generally clear and helpful, but consider adding error bars to represent variability in the results.
*   Appendix A and B are well-written and provide useful details about the methodology.



Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. The annotated datasets are a significant resource, and the analysis provides important insights into the challenges of evaluating RC systems. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
