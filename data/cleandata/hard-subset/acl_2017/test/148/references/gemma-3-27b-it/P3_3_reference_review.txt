## Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the important issue of evaluating reading comprehension (RC) systems beyond simple accuracy. The authors argue that existing RC datasets lack nuanced categorization, hindering a clear understanding of system capabilities. They propose leveraging two classes of evaluation metrics – "prerequisite skills" and "readability" – inspired by psychological models of text comprehension. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and present an analysis revealing a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Comprehensive Framework for RC Dataset Evaluation:** The paper presents a valuable framework for analyzing RC datasets, moving beyond superficial categories like question type. The combination of prerequisite skills and readability offers a more holistic view of dataset characteristics.
2. **Refined Categorization of Prerequisite Skills:** The authors’ revision of Sugawara et al.’s skills taxonomy, drawing on insights from textual entailment and psychology, is a significant improvement. The incorporation of distinctions like bridging vs. elaboration offers a more theoretically grounded approach.
3. **Publicly Available Annotated Datasets:** The annotation of six widely used RC datasets is a valuable resource for the research community. This allows for standardized evaluation and comparison of systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement:** The paper effectively highlights the limitations of current RC evaluation practices and clearly articulates the need for more nuanced metrics. The example in Figure 1 is particularly effective in illustrating the disconnect between readability and difficulty.
2. **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** Grounding the work in established psychological models of text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) adds credibility and provides a strong rationale for the chosen metrics.  The connection to textual entailment research further strengthens the theoretical basis.
3. **Thorough Annotation Process:** The detailed description of the annotation procedure, including the allowance for multiple labeling and the attempt to standardize annotation conditions across datasets, demonstrates a commitment to data quality.  The reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% is reassuring.
4. **Interesting and Potentially Important Findings:** The observation of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a key contribution. This suggests that simply increasing text complexity isn't necessarily a path to building more challenging RC tasks and opens avenues for targeted dataset creation.
5. **Practical Resource:** The publicly available annotated datasets will be a valuable asset for researchers developing and evaluating RC systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the annotation of six datasets is commendable, the focus on only 100 questions per dataset feels limiting, particularly given the size of some datasets (e.g., SQuAD). This raises questions about the generalizability of the findings.  The sampling method, while described, could potentially introduce bias.
2. **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** Despite the effort to standardize annotation, the classification of "prerequisite skills" inherently involves a degree of subjectivity. While the inter-annotator agreement is good, it doesn’t eliminate the possibility of nuanced differences in interpretation that could impact the results. A more detailed error analysis of disagreements would be beneficial.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper demonstrates a correlation between readability and prerequisite skills, but does not establish causation. It’s unclear whether question difficulty *causes* the need for specific skills, or vice-versa, or if both are driven by other underlying factors.
4. **Readability Metrics Limitations:** The authors acknowledge that the chosen readability metrics may not perfectly correlate with difficulty *for systems*. These metrics are designed for human readability.  Exploring metrics more directly related to model performance (e.g., attention patterns, activation analysis) could be a fruitful avenue for future work.
5. **Lack of System-Level Analysis:** The paper focuses solely on analyzing the datasets themselves. A compelling next step would be to evaluate how well these metrics predict the performance of existing RC systems. Do systems struggle with questions requiring specific skills, regardless of readability?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the sampling method for questions: Could you provide more detail on the criteria used to select the 100 questions from each dataset? Were there any efforts to ensure representativeness (e.g., stratified sampling based on question type)?
2.  You mention disagreements during annotation. Could you provide more details about the nature of these disagreements and how they were resolved? Were there specific skills that proved particularly challenging to annotate consistently?
3.  Do you have preliminary plans to use these metrics to predict the performance of existing RC models? If so, could you share any insights into how these metrics might be used for model debugging or improvement?
4.  You mention that the readability metrics were chosen because of their success with human readability. Do you foresee investigating other metrics that might be better suited to correlate with machine performance?
5.  Given the subjectivity of the skill annotations, did you consider quantifying the certainty or confidence of the annotators in their judgments?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is reasonably comprehensive.
*   The writing is clear and well-organized.
*   The inclusion of Appendix A and B is helpful for transparency.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. While some weaknesses remain, the paper presents a compelling framework for analyzing RC datasets and provides a useful resource for the research community.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and concerns raised above. The potential impact of this work on the development of more robust and nuanced RC systems is significant.
