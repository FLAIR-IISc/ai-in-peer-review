This paper proposes a methodology for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets based on two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors argue that current datasets lack sufficient categorization beyond superficial features, hindering accurate assessment of systems and dataset quality. They refine existing skill classifications and annotate six datasets, revealing a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the detailed annotation of six RC datasets with a refined set of prerequisite skills. This annotated data *could* be a valuable resource for the community, allowing for more nuanced analysis of dataset characteristics. A secondary contribution is the attempt to formalize and categorize the skills required for RC, drawing from psychology and linguistics. The observation regarding the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, while not entirely novel, is supported by the presented data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Detailed Annotation:** The effort to annotate six datasets with the defined prerequisite skills is commendable. This provides a substantial, potentially useful resource for researchers interested in dataset analysis and curriculum learning. The inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% suggests reasonable consistency in the annotation process.
2. **Refined Skill Categorization:** The authors' attempt to reorganize and refine the prerequisite skills, grounding them in theories from textual entailment and human text comprehension, is a positive step. The incorporation of concepts like bridging and elaboration offers a more granular understanding of the cognitive demands of RC.
3. **Empirical Evidence of Weak Correlation:** The analysis clearly demonstrates a lack of strong correlation between readability and the complexity of skills required to answer questions. This finding underscores the need for more sophisticated dataset evaluation metrics beyond simple readability scores.
4. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a reasonably thorough overview of existing RC datasets and relevant research from both NLP and psychology.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Limitations - Sentence Selection Bias:** The annotation process *only* considered the sentences needed to answer the question. This fundamentally biases the analysis. It does not evaluate the system’s ability to *identify* relevant sentences – a critical component of RC. The authors acknowledge this, but its impact is significant. The entire evaluation hinges on a pre-selected context, invalidating assessment of information retrieval aspects.
2. **Granularity of Skills:** While the skill categorization is more refined than previous work, the definitions remain somewhat subjective and potentially overlapping. The distinction between certain skills (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration) may be difficult to consistently apply, even with trained annotators. This ambiguity introduces noise into the analysis.
3. **Lack of System-Level Evaluation:** The paper focuses solely on dataset characteristics. It doesn't demonstrate how these metrics can be *used* to improve RC system development or how they correlate with system performance. Analyzing system performance on these annotated datasets would significantly strengthen the work.
4. **Presentation and Redundancy:** The paper is verbose and repetitive. Certain points are reiterated multiple times, and the writing style could be more concise. Several figures and tables are unnecessarily detailed, obscuring key findings. For example, Table 2, while presenting frequencies, lacks clear interpretation or highlights.
5. **Minor Formatting Issues:** Numerous inconsistencies exist in formatting. Figure 2's text is smaller than the surrounding text. There are also inconsistencies in citation style.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How would the findings change if the annotation process included evaluating a system’s ability to *select* the necessary sentences from the context, rather than pre-selecting them?  Can you comment on the impact of this bias on your conclusions?
2.  What measures were taken to ensure consistency in the application of the prerequisite skill categories among the annotators? Please provide specific examples of how ambiguous cases were resolved.
3.  Do you have any preliminary results correlating these metrics with the performance of existing RC systems on the annotated datasets? If not, what are the plans to explore such correlations?
4.  Can you elaborate on the limitations of using readability metrics developed for human comprehension to assess the difficulty of RC tasks for machines?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are not consistently formatted.
*   The discussion section feels somewhat disconnected from the results. A more focused analysis tying the findings back to the broader goals of RC research is needed.
*   The appendix provides details about the sampling method, which is helpful. However, a more detailed description of the annotation guidelines would be beneficial.



Overall, while the paper presents a potentially valuable resource (the annotated datasets) and raises important points about dataset evaluation, significant methodological limitations and presentation issues detract from its overall impact. The work would benefit substantially from a system-level evaluation and a more focused analysis of the annotation process and its implications. I lean towards rejection due to the fundamental limitations of the methodology and the overly verbose presentation.
