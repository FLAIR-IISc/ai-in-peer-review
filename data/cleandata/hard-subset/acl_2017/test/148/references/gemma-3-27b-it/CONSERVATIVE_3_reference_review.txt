This paper explores the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets, proposing a framework based on “prerequisite skills” and “readability” metrics. The authors annotate six datasets with these metrics and present an analysis of the results.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the *annotation of six existing RC datasets* with a relatively comprehensive set of features related to both question difficulty (prerequisite skills) and text complexity (readability). This provides a valuable resource for the community, allowing for more nuanced analysis of dataset characteristics. A secondary contribution is the *refinement of existing prerequisite skill categorizations*, grounding them in cognitive science literature on text comprehension. However, this refinement feels incremental. Finally, the *observation that readability and question difficulty are only weakly correlated* is a potentially useful finding, though not entirely surprising.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Dataset Annotation:** The annotated datasets are a significant practical contribution. Having these features readily available will facilitate more informed dataset selection and analysis for researchers developing and evaluating RC systems. The scale of the annotation (100 questions per dataset) is sufficient for initial insights.
2. **Grounded Approach to Skill Categorization:** Anchoring the prerequisite skill categories in established theories of human text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) provides a stronger theoretical foundation than previous work, as acknowledged by the authors. This is a step towards a more principled understanding of what makes an RC task difficult.
3. **Clear Presentation of Metrics:** The paper provides a clear and well-organized description of the chosen readability metrics and the refined prerequisite skills. The use of existing, established readability measures is sensible.
4. **Identified Dataset Characteristics:** The analysis reveals interesting dataset-specific trends. The observation that MCTest requires more multi-sentence reasoning than other datasets, despite being more readable, is insightful. The identification of potential issues in automatically generated datasets like MS MARCO is also valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Method:** The core idea – assessing RC difficulty through a combination of textual features and cognitive skill requirements – is not entirely new. The refinement of the skill categorization, while welcome, doesn't represent a substantial methodological breakthrough. The paper builds *on* existing work rather than presenting a truly innovative approach.
2. **Limited Impact of Analysis:** While the observation of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is interesting, the paper doesn't fully leverage this finding. The implications for RC system design or dataset creation are not explored in sufficient depth. What does this weak correlation *mean* for how we should build better systems or datasets?
3. **Annotation Subjectivity:** The annotation process itself introduces potential subjectivity. While inter-annotator agreement is reported, the paper lacks detail on how disagreements were resolved. The reliance on human annotation is a limitation, and exploring automated approaches to skill identification would strengthen the work. The 'nonsense' category feels particularly prone to subjective interpretation.
4. **Insufficient System-Level Evaluation:** The paper focuses solely on dataset analysis. It would be significantly stronger if the authors had used these metrics to analyze the *performance* of existing RC systems on the annotated datasets. For instance, does performance correlate with the number of prerequisite skills required for a question?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How were disagreements between annotators resolved? Please provide more details on the annotation protocol and quality control measures.
2. Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty? How might this finding inform the design of RC datasets or training strategies for RC systems?
3. Have you explored any automated methods for identifying the prerequisite skills required for a question, and if not, why?
4.  The paper mentions that the methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences. How significant do you believe this limitation is, and could future work address this shortcoming?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are well-chosen and relevant. The writing is generally clear and concise. Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of using Flesch-Kincaid grade level as a proxy for text difficulty, as it is a relatively simplistic measure.
