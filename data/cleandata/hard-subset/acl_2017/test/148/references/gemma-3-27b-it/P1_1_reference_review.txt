## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a more nuanced evaluation of reading comprehension (RC) datasets beyond simple accuracy. The authors argue that current datasets lack fine-grained categorization of difficulty, focusing solely on superficial features like question type. They adopt two classes of metrics – “prerequisite skills” (building upon Sugawara et al., 2017) and “readability” (based on Vajjala & Meurers, 2012) – to analyze six existing RC datasets. The core contribution is an annotation of these datasets with the defined metrics, aiming to reveal the relationship between question difficulty and text readability. The analysis reveals a weak correlation between these two aspects, demonstrating that difficult questions can arise from easy-to-read text, and vice versa.

**Main Contributions (as perceived by reviewer):**

1. **Dataset Annotation & Public Release:** The primary contribution is the annotated dataset. Providing a resource with richer metadata (prerequisite skills and readability scores) for six established RC datasets is valuable to the community. This allows for more targeted evaluation of RC systems and dataset construction.
2. **Refined Skill Categorization:** The reorganization of “prerequisite skills,” grounding it in textual entailment and psychological models of comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), provides a more theoretically sound framework than prior work. This is a step towards a more interpretable and actionable understanding of RC difficulty.
3. **Highlighting Disconnect Between Readability & Difficulty:** The empirical demonstration of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a notable finding. It challenges the intuitive assumption that more complex text automatically leads to harder questions, prompting a rethinking of dataset design and system evaluation.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of current RC dataset evaluation and provides a compelling rationale for a more granular approach. The initial example in Figure 1 effectively illustrates the disconnect between readability and question difficulty.
2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** Grounding the “prerequisite skills” in established theories from both NLP (textual entailment) and psychology (human comprehension models) lends credibility to the framework. This moves beyond simply listing skills and offers a theoretical basis for their categorization.
3. **Comprehensive Metric Selection:**  The adoption of a wide range of readability metrics from Vajjala & Meurers (2012) provides a solid foundation for assessing textual complexity.  Combining this with the refined prerequisite skills offers a holistic view of RC difficulty.
4. **Practical Value through Public Release:** The publicly available annotated datasets will be a valuable resource for the research community, enabling more informed dataset design, targeted system evaluation, and potentially curriculum learning strategies.
5. **Detailed Methodology:** The annotation procedure is described clearly, including details about inter-annotator agreement. This increases confidence in the reliability of the annotations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:**  While the annotation of six datasets is a good starting point, it's not representative of *all* RC datasets. The generalizability of the findings to datasets with different characteristics (e.g., domain, task format) is unclear.
2. **Annotation Limitations (as acknowledged by authors):** The methodology *specifically* does not evaluate a system's ability to *find* the relevant information within the text. It only assesses the skills needed *once* the necessary sentences are identified. This is a crucial limitation that should be prominently acknowledged.  
3. **Weak Correlation Doesn't Imply Causation:** The paper demonstrates a weak correlation, but doesn't delve into *why* this correlation is weak.  Further investigation could explore the factors that contribute to difficult questions in easy-to-read texts (e.g., subtle reasoning, implicit knowledge).
4. **Readability Metrics & RC Performance:** The paper acknowledges that readability metrics designed for humans may not directly correlate with difficulty for RC systems. However, the analysis doesn’t extensively explore potential alternative or system-specific readability measures.  More discussion about the mismatch would be valuable.
5. **Statistical Significance:** While inter-annotator agreement is reported, the paper lacks statistical analysis of the results. Demonstrating statistical significance for the observed trends (e.g., differences in skill distribution across datasets) would strengthen the conclusions.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding the annotation limitations:**  How could the methodology be extended to *also* evaluate a system’s ability to identify relevant information in the context?  What challenges would be associated with such an extension?
2. **Causation of Weak Correlation:** What are your hypotheses about *why* the correlation between readability and question difficulty is weak?  Do you anticipate certain types of questions (e.g., those requiring commonsense reasoning) being particularly prone to this disconnect?
3. **Alternative Readability Metrics:**  Have you considered investigating readability metrics specifically designed for machine comprehension models, or creating new metrics tailored to the challenges faced by these systems?
4. **Statistical Analysis:** Can you provide statistical analysis of the annotation results (e.g., ANOVA tests to compare skill distributions across datasets) to support the observed trends?
5. **Impact of Dataset Genre:**  You mention the importance of corpus genre. How would you advise researchers to balance the use of different genres (e.g., news articles vs. narratives) when constructing RC datasets?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   Minor: Consider adding a table summarizing the different RC datasets annotated, including their source, size, and general characteristics.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension by providing a more nuanced understanding of dataset difficulty and offering a publicly available resource for future research. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work – particularly the refined skill categorization and the dataset annotation – outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
