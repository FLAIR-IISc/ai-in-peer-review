This paper presents a valuable investigation into the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors rightly point out the limitations of relying solely on superficial categories like question types and propose adopting metrics from prerequisite skills and readability to provide a more nuanced evaluation of both datasets and systems. The core idea of disentangling these two aspects of difficulty is sound and addresses a significant gap in the current RC evaluation landscape.

**Contributions:**

1. **Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The adoption and refinement of prerequisite skills (building on Sugawara et al., 2017) and leveraging readability metrics (Vajjala & Meurers, 2012) provide a robust framework for analyzing RC datasets beyond simple accuracy. This is the strongest contribution, offering a potentially powerful tool for dataset designers and researchers.
2. **Dataset Analysis & Insights:** The annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics and the subsequent analysis reveal interesting trends and correlations (or lack thereof) between question difficulty and text readability. The finding that these factors aren't strongly correlated is noteworthy and challenges assumptions within the field.
3. **Publicly Available Annotations:**  Making the annotated datasets publicly available is a valuable contribution to the RC community, enabling further research and facilitating comparisons across different systems and datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the need for more fine-grained evaluation metrics in RC, citing limitations of existing datasets and the difficulties in accurately assessing system performance.
2. **Solid Theoretical Foundation:**  Grounding the prerequisite skills in theories from both NLP (textual entailment) and psychology (human text comprehension – Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) strengthens the validity and interpretability of the proposed metrics. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of relevant literature.
3. **Thorough Methodology:** The annotation procedure is described in detail, and the inter-annotator agreement is reported, providing confidence in the reliability of the annotations.  The attempts to control for annotation biases (e.g., filtering in NewsQA) are commendable.
4. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The results are presented in a clear and organized manner, using tables and figures to effectively illustrate the key findings. The discussion of the observations for each dataset is insightful.
5. **Practical Implications:** The paper concludes with practical implications for both dataset construction and system development, suggesting how the metrics can be used to guide the development process and create more challenging and informative benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While annotating six datasets is a good start, the analysis is still limited in scope.  Extending the annotation to a larger and more diverse set of datasets would strengthen the generalizability of the findings. The choice of datasets, while standard, leans heavily towards English datasets, potentially limiting broader applicability.
2. **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** Despite the detailed guidelines, the annotation of prerequisite skills inevitably involves some level of subjectivity. While the reported inter-annotator agreement is good, it's important to acknowledge the potential for discrepancies and their impact on the results.  A more detailed error analysis of annotation disagreements could be valuable.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper demonstrates a lack of strong correlation between readability and question difficulty, but it doesn't explore *why* this is the case. A more in-depth analysis of the types of questions that are difficult despite easy-to-read text could provide valuable insights.  For example, are these questions more reliant on commonsense reasoning or complex inference?
4. **Methodological Limitation – Sentence Selection:** As acknowledged by the authors, the methodology only evaluates understanding of contextual entailments *given* the relevant sentences. It doesn't assess the system's ability to *identify* those relevant sentences within the broader context, a crucial component of RC. This is a significant limitation that should be explicitly highlighted.
5. **Readability Metric Limitations:** The use of Flesch-Kincaid grade level as a proxy for readability is a simplification. This metric focuses primarily on sentence length and word complexity and may not capture other important aspects of readability, such as coherence and logical flow.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific challenges annotators faced when labeling the prerequisite skills? Were there particular skills that proved more difficult to consistently annotate, and if so, why?
2.  You acknowledge the limitation that your methodology doesn't evaluate sentence selection. Do you have any plans to address this limitation in future work, perhaps by incorporating metrics that assess relevance scoring or attention mechanisms?
3.  The paper highlights the lack of strong correlation between readability and difficulty. Do you have any hypotheses about *why* this is the case? What types of questions seem to defy this expectation?
4.  Given the subjectivity inherent in skill annotation, how sensitive are your results to different annotator interpretations? Have you explored the impact of varying annotation guidelines or using multiple annotators per question?




**Additional Comments:**

*  Consider including a brief discussion of the potential biases in the datasets themselves.
* The appendix provides useful details about the annotation process and the calculation of sentence distance.
* The references are comprehensive and well-formatted.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. The proposed metric framework and the insightful analysis of existing datasets provide a solid foundation for future research. While some limitations remain, the authors acknowledge them and offer avenues for improvement. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
