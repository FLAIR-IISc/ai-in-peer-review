This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors propose adopting two classes of evaluation metrics—prerequisite skills and readability—to analyze the qualitative features of existing datasets and system performance. They annotate six datasets with these metrics and present initial findings, suggesting a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the detailed annotation of six RC datasets with a refined set of prerequisite skills and readability metrics. This annotated data itself is valuable for the community, offering a resource for more nuanced dataset analysis and system evaluation. A secondary contribution is the attempt to formalize and categorize the skills required for RC, building upon previous work and incorporating insights from cognitive psychology and textual entailment research. The observation of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, while not entirely novel, is supported by the annotations and contributes to a more informed understanding of current datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Annotated Resource:** The annotated datasets are a significant asset. Providing this resource to the community allows for more rigorous and informed analysis of existing RC tasks and facilitates the development of targeted evaluation methodologies. The availability of the annotation is explicitly stated and promised, which is crucial.
2. **Comprehensive Skill Categorization:** The authors demonstrate a strong engagement with relevant literature from both NLP and cognitive psychology. The refinement of Sugawara et al.'s prerequisite skills, incorporating insights from textual entailment and human comprehension models, is a well-motivated and thorough effort. The attempt to create a more theoretically grounded categorization of skills is commendable.
3. **Detailed Methodology:** The paper meticulously details the annotation procedure and dataset specifications. The clear explanation of the process, including how disagreements were handled (implicitly, through high inter-annotator agreement) and the rationale behind dataset selection, enhances the reproducibility and credibility of the findings.
4. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The tabular presentation of prerequisite skill frequencies and readability metrics is effective. The inclusion of the correlation analysis, although showing weak correlations, is important for providing a complete picture of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** The central idea – that readability doesn't directly translate to question difficulty – is not groundbreaking. This has been observed anecdotally and hinted at in prior work (as the authors acknowledge). The paper's novelty primarily lies in the detailed empirical support for this observation through the annotation effort.
2. **Annotation Scope & Potential Bias:** While the inter-annotator agreement is high, the annotation process appears to focus *solely* on the skills needed to identify the answer *given* the context. It doesn't evaluate a system's ability to select relevant sentences from the document – a crucial step in many RC tasks. This limitation is acknowledged by the authors, but it significantly constrains the applicability of the metrics. Furthermore, the choice of 100 questions per dataset, while pragmatic, may not be representative of the dataset as a whole.
3. **Weak Correlation Interpretation:** The authors emphasize the weak correlation between readability and prerequisite skills. However, the reported correlation coefficients are relatively low, suggesting only a marginal relationship, or, at best, a complex interplay of factors. The discussion could benefit from acknowledging the potential influence of confounding variables and the limitations of Pearson correlation for capturing non-linear relationships.
4. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** There are several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of bolding, minor grammatical errors) that detract from the professionalism of the paper. While not critical, these issues suggest a lack of meticulous attention to detail. The figure captions are sometimes vague and require more context.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You acknowledge the limitation that your annotation doesn't evaluate sentence selection.  Could you elaborate on how this limitation impacts the interpretation of your results and the potential utility of your metrics for evaluating end-to-end RC systems?
2.  The number of prerequisite skills ranges considerably across datasets.  What steps were taken to ensure consistency in the application of these skills during annotation, and how might differences in annotation style contribute to these variations?
3.  The paper mentions filtering the NewsQA dataset for "bad" questions.  What specific criteria were used to identify these questions, and how might this filtering bias the results?
4.  Given the weak correlations observed, do you believe the two classes of metrics—prerequisite skills and readability—are truly independent, or are they potentially measuring overlapping aspects of question difficulty?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are not formatted consistently.
*   The organization of Section 3 could be improved.  The flow between the general introduction to the two classes of metrics and the detailed description of each metric is somewhat disjointed.
*   Please provide a link to the annotated datasets upon publication.
