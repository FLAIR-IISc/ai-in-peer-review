## Peer Review for "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the critical need for more nuanced evaluation of Reading Comprehension (RC) systems beyond simple accuracy. The authors propose augmenting standard RC evaluation with metrics capturing two key dimensions: *prerequisite skills* required to answer a question, and the *readability* of the context text. They refine existing skill categorizations based on insights from psychology and textual entailment, annotate six popular RC datasets with these metrics, and analyze the relationship between question difficulty and text readability.  The primary finding is a weak correlation between these two factors, suggesting that current datasets often contain either easily-read but challenging questions, or vice versa.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The paper provides a well-motivated and relatively comprehensive framework for analyzing the *qualitative* properties of RC datasets, going beyond superficial categorization (question type, answer type). The combination of prerequisite skill analysis and readability assessment offers a valuable lens for understanding dataset strengths and weaknesses.
2. **Refined Skill Categorization:** The reorganization of prerequisite skills, informed by research in textual entailment and cognitive psychology, represents a genuine improvement over previous attempts. The distinctions between bridging and elaboration, and the inclusion of skills like ellipsis, are particularly valuable.
3. **Publicly Available Annotations:**  The annotation of six datasets with these metrics is a significant contribution, providing a resource for researchers to develop more targeted and informative evaluations of RC systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Problem Definition:** The paper convincingly argues that current RC evaluation is insufficient. The examples in Figure 1 effectively illustrate the disconnect between text readability and question difficulty.
2. **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the metric classes in McNamara & Magliano’s work on human text comprehension, and the integration of insights from textual entailment and psychology, provide a strong theoretical basis for the proposed approach.
3. **Thorough Methodology:** The annotation procedure is clearly described, and the inter-annotator agreement reported (90.1%) suggests good reliability.  The explicit acknowledgement of the methodology’s limitations (e.g., not evaluating sentence selection) is commendable.
4. **Interesting & Actionable Findings:** The observation of a weak correlation between readability and prerequisite skills is a key takeaway. It highlights the need for dataset design to intentionally address both factors, rather than relying on the assumption that complex text automatically leads to challenging questions.
5. **Practical Resource:** The publicly available annotations will be immensely useful for the RC community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** While the chosen readability metrics are well-established, relying solely on linguistic features feels somewhat limited.  Factors like domain-specific knowledge, logical complexity, or the presence of ambiguity are not directly captured.  A more holistic assessment of text difficulty might be beneficial.
2. **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:**  Despite the high inter-annotator agreement, the categorization of prerequisite skills inevitably involves some degree of subjectivity.  Providing more detailed annotation guidelines or examples could further improve consistency.  There is a risk of annotator bias influencing skill assignment.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper establishes a correlation between readability and prerequisite skills, but does not investigate *why* this correlation is weak.  Further analysis could explore whether certain types of questions systematically violate the expected relationship, or whether specific dataset construction methods contribute to the observed pattern.
4. **Focus on Existing Datasets:** While analyzing existing datasets is valuable, the paper doesn't offer concrete guidance on *how* to design new datasets that address the identified shortcomings. A discussion of specific strategies for creating datasets with varying levels of difficulty and skill requirements would strengthen the contribution.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Annotation Guidelines:** Could you share more details about the annotation guidelines provided to the annotators? Specifically, how did you address potential ambiguity in the skill definitions, and how did you ensure consistency in applying those definitions across different datasets?
2. **Readability Expansion:**  Have you considered incorporating additional readability metrics beyond those proposed by Vajjala and Meurers (2012)? If so, why were those particular metrics selected, and what are the limitations of this choice?  What about metrics tailored towards reasoning complexity, beyond surface linguistic features?
3. **Causality Investigation:** Did you perform any analysis to investigate *why* the correlation between readability and prerequisite skills is weak?  For example, did you identify specific types of questions or datasets where this disconnect is most pronounced?
4. **Dataset Design Recommendations:**  Based on your findings, what specific recommendations would you offer to researchers designing new RC datasets to ensure a more balanced and informative evaluation?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The use of tables and figures effectively presents the results.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential biases in the existing RC datasets that might influence the observed results.



Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of Reading Comprehension. The proposed framework, refined skill categorization, and publicly available annotations represent a significant step towards more nuanced and informative evaluation of RC systems. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the impact of this work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
