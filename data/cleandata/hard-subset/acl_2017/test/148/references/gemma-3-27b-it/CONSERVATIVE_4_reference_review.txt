This paper attempts to provide a more nuanced evaluation of reading comprehension (RC) datasets by introducing two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors annotate six existing datasets with these metrics and analyze the results, demonstrating a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the annotated datasets themselves, offering a valuable resource for the RC community. A secondary contribution lies in the refinement of prerequisite skill categorization, drawing on insights from textual entailment and psychology. Finally, the observation of a weak correlation, while not entirely surprising, serves as a useful empirical confirmation of an intuitive idea.

**Strengths:**

1. **Resource Creation:** The annotated datasets are the most significant aspect of this work. Providing these annotations—mapping prerequisite skills to questions—will be highly beneficial for researchers aiming to develop more targeted and effective RC systems. The public availability of these datasets is a strong positive.
2. **Systematic Approach:** The paper presents a reasonably systematic approach to evaluating RC datasets. While the concepts aren’t entirely novel, the authors attempt to combine and refine existing frameworks (McNamara & Magliano, Sugawara et al.) into a more comprehensive evaluation strategy.
3. **Empirical Validation of Intuition:** The finding that readability doesn't strongly correlate with question difficulty reinforces a common-sense notion. Quantifying this observation with data provides a useful benchmark and justifies further investigation into the factors *actually* driving question complexity.
4. **Clear Presentation of Metrics:** The descriptions of both the prerequisite skills and readability metrics are relatively clear and well-organized, making it easier to understand the evaluation framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Ideas:** The underlying concepts – assessing comprehension through prerequisite skills and considering text readability – are not groundbreaking. The paper relies heavily on existing work in psychology and NLP, and the “refinement” of the skill categorization feels incremental rather than transformative.
2. **Annotation Subjectivity:** The annotation process, relying on three annotators, is susceptible to subjectivity. While inter-annotator agreement is reported (90.1%), the paper lacks a detailed discussion of disagreement resolution. The potential impact of annotator bias on the results isn’t adequately addressed.  Specifically, the instructions to annotators are not provided in the appendix, making it difficult to assess annotation quality.
3. **Limited Impact of Analysis:** The primary result – a weak correlation – doesn't offer a clear path forward for improving RC systems or dataset creation. Simply knowing readability isn’t a good predictor doesn’t, in itself, solve the problem of evaluating or building better datasets. Further analysis is needed to identify *what* does drive question difficulty.
4. **Methodological Limitations:** The paper explicitly acknowledges a limitation: the annotation focuses on contextual entailment and doesn’t evaluate the system’s ability to *find* relevant information within the text. This is a significant caveat, as information retrieval is a crucial component of many RC systems.
5. **Weak Statistical Analysis:** The paper mentions Pearson’s correlation coefficients but doesn't provide sufficient context or significance testing. Reporting p-values would strengthen the statistical validity of the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide the detailed annotation instructions given to the annotators (perhaps as supplementary material)? This is crucial for understanding the consistency and reliability of the annotations.
2. What measures were taken to resolve disagreements between annotators? Can you provide examples of questions where disagreements occurred and how they were addressed?
3. Beyond simply observing a weak correlation, what specific recommendations do you have for dataset creators or system developers based on your analysis? What other metrics or factors should be considered?
4.  You acknowledge the limitation regarding sentence selection. Do you envision a future annotation scheme that *does* evaluate this competency? If so, how would it be designed?
5. Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the statistical significance of the observed correlations?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's organization is generally sound, and the writing is clear. However, the introduction feels somewhat repetitive. The related work section is adequate but could benefit from a more critical assessment of existing RC datasets. The presentation of results (Tables 2, 3, and 5) could be improved with more descriptive captions and clearer formatting.
