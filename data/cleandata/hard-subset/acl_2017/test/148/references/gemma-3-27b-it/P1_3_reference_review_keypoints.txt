- Paper studies relationship between question difficulty and text readability in RC datasets and argues for richer evaluation than accuracy alone.
- Proposes a combined evaluation framework using prerequisite skills and readability metrics to better characterize RC datasets and system performance.
- Refines and reorganizes prerequisite skill taxonomy, grounding it in textual entailment and psychological models of comprehension.
- Annotates six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics and releases the annotations publicly.
- Finding: weak correlation between readability measures and question difficulty across datasets.
- Strength: addresses a critical gap by moving beyond simple accuracy to explain why systems succeed or fail.
- Strength: strong theoretical grounding in Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano and textual entailment literature.
- Strength: practical contribution through publicly released annotated datasets for empirical validation.
- Strength: paper is clear, well-structured, and well-motivated.
- Weakness: weak correlations limit immediate practical guidance for dataset creation or system design.
- Weakness: annotation subjectivity concerns remain despite 90.1% agreement; more detail on disagreements and kappa would help.
- Weakness: readability metrics are limited to Vajjala & Meurers' features and miss semantic/algorithmic complexity measures.
- Weakness: focuses on dataset analysis but misses an opportunity to evaluate system performance wrt identified skills.
- Questions: request details on annotation disagreements, skills hard to annotate, and disagreement resolution.
- Questions: ask whether more complex interactions between readability features and skills were explored beyond Pearson correlations.
- Questions: ask about plans to analyze existing RC systems using the annotated datasets and possible methodologies.
- Questions: request approximate annotation time per question to gauge cost and scalability.
- Additional: suggest deeper review of automated readability methods, justification for dataset choices, and clearer definition of the "nonsense" category.
- Overall: solid, valuable contribution with potential impact; addressing noted weaknesses would strengthen the work.
