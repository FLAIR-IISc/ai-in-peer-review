## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the relationship between the difficulty of answering questions and the readability of the source text in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors argue that current RC datasets often lack nuanced evaluation, relying heavily on superficial categories. They propose a framework based on two classes of metrics – “prerequisite skills” (drawing from cognitive psychology and textual entailment) and “readability” – and apply these metrics to annotate six existing RC datasets.  The core finding is a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, suggesting that complex questions can be formulated from simple texts, and vice versa.

**Main Contributions (as claimed by authors, ordered):**

1.  Adoption of two classes of evaluation metrics (prerequisite skills and readability) to qualitatively analyze RC datasets.
2.  Revision of the prerequisite skills classification, organizing knowledge reasoning based on entailment and psychological models of human text comprehension.
3.  Annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics and public release of the results.

**Contributions (as I see them, ordered):**

1.  **The annotated datasets are a valuable resource.** The authors have performed a substantial annotation effort, which will be useful to the RC community for more fine-grained analysis of existing datasets and potentially for creating more targeted training data.
2.  **Highlighting the disconnect between readability and question difficulty is important.**  The empirical findings, demonstrating a weak correlation, challenge the common assumption that more readable texts necessarily lead to easier questions and vice-versa. This is a key insight for dataset construction.
3.  **The attempt to ground RC evaluation in cognitive science is commendable.**  The effort to link skills to theories of human comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) provides a more theoretically grounded approach to evaluating RC systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Methodology:** The annotation process, while subjective, appears well-defined, and the inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% suggests a reasonable level of consistency. The explicit definition of the skills and readability metrics provides a clear framework for the annotation.
2. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** Applying the metrics to six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) provides a broad and insightful comparative analysis of their characteristics. The tables summarizing the results are informative and well-presented.
3. **Theoretical Grounding:**  Connecting the work to established theories in cognitive psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) provides a strong rationale for the chosen metrics and enhances the interpretability of the findings. The refinement of prerequisite skills based on textual entailment and human inference is a solid contribution.
4. **Practical Implications:** The findings have direct implications for the design of future RC datasets.  The authors advocate for a more nuanced approach to dataset construction, considering not only readability but also the cognitive skills required to answer questions.
5. **Publicly Available Resource:**  The planned public release of the annotated datasets will significantly benefit the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The study focuses solely on identifying *which* skills are required, but doesn’t assess *how well* systems utilize those skills.  This is a crucial next step for truly understanding system performance.  The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it's significant.
2. **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** While the inter-annotator agreement is good, the categorization of prerequisite skills is inherently subjective. Different annotators might reasonably disagree on which skills are required to answer a given question. A deeper analysis of the disagreements, and the potential sources of subjectivity, would be valuable.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:** While the authors demonstrate a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, they do not establish a causal relationship. It is possible that other factors, not measured in this study, are driving the observed patterns.
4. **Granularity of Readability Metrics:** The readability metrics employed (Vajjala & Meurers, 2012) are primarily focused on surface-level linguistic features. More sophisticated measures of text complexity, such as semantic coherence and discourse structure, might provide a more comprehensive assessment.
5. **Methodological Limitation in Sentence Selection**: The methodology only evaluates understanding of contextual entailments, as it relies on annotators *selecting* the relevant sentences.  This misses the crucial skill of *identifying* the relevant information within a longer text, which is a core component of RC.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the difficulty of categorizing questions with "no answer." Could you elaborate on how this limitation might affect the generalizability of your findings? Do you envision a revised annotation scheme to handle such questions more effectively?
2.  Given the subjectivity inherent in skill annotation, could you provide more details about the annotation guidelines and the process for resolving disagreements between annotators? Were specific examples provided to ensure consistency in application of the skill definitions?
3.  The study focuses on skills *required* to answer a question.  Do you believe the *absence* of certain skills in a dataset could also be a valuable metric (e.g., a dataset lacking questions requiring causal reasoning)?
4.  You acknowledge that your methodology does not assess the ability to *find* relevant sentences. Do you have any thoughts on how to integrate this aspect into a more comprehensive evaluation framework?
5.  The statistical analysis focuses on Pearson correlation. Would other correlation measures, such as Spearman's rank correlation, be more appropriate given the potentially non-linear relationship between readability and skill requirements?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
*   The inclusion of the annotation results as a publicly available resource is a significant strength.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. While there are some limitations, the insightful analysis of existing datasets and the development of a more theoretically grounded evaluation framework are significant advancements. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The publicly available dataset will be a valuable resource for the RC community.
