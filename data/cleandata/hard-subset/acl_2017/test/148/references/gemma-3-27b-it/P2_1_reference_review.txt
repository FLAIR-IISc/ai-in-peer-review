## Review for ACL Submission: Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the critical problem of evaluating reading comprehension (RC) systems beyond simple accuracy. The authors argue that existing RC datasets lack sufficient granularity in their evaluation metrics, focusing primarily on superficial question/answer types. They propose adopting two classes of metrics – “prerequisite skills” (cognitive skills needed to answer) and “readability” – based on work in psychology and NLP. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and present preliminary analysis demonstrating a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A nuanced framework for RC dataset evaluation:**  The paper provides a valuable shift in perspective, advocating for a more detailed analysis of *why* RC systems succeed or fail, moving beyond simply *how often*.  The adoption of prerequisite skills and readability as orthogonal evaluation axes is a key strength.
2. **A refined categorization of prerequisite skills:** The authors build upon prior work, integrating insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology to propose a more theoretically grounded categorization of skills required for RC. This is a solid contribution to understanding the cognitive demands of RC tasks.
3. **A publicly available annotated dataset:**  The annotation of six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics is a significant practical contribution, providing a resource for future research and system development.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a crucial gap in RC evaluation:** Current RC metrics largely ignore the qualitative aspects of dataset difficulty and the cognitive skills required. This paper directly addresses this gap, providing a framework for more informative evaluation.
2. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The grounding of the proposed metrics in established theories from cognitive psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (textual entailment) adds credibility and provides a strong rationale for the chosen categories.
3. **Comprehensive annotation effort:** Annotating six datasets is a substantial undertaking and provides a good foundation for empirical analysis.  The publicly available dataset will be beneficial to the research community.
4. **Clear presentation and organization:**  The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The inclusion of examples (Figure 1) helps illustrate the key concepts.
5. **Interesting findings regarding weak correlation:** The observation that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated is a key insight. It highlights that creating challenging RC tasks doesn't necessarily require complex language, and simpler text can still test deeper cognitive abilities.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of the Analysis:** While the annotation is valuable, the presented analysis is relatively descriptive.  Beyond reporting frequencies and weak correlations, the paper lacks a deeper statistical analysis that could reveal more nuanced relationships between the metrics. For example, could the prerequisite skill distribution *predict* system performance?
2. **Annotation Reliability:** Although the inter-annotator agreement is reported (90.1%), details on the annotation process are somewhat limited. More information about the training of annotators and the resolution of disagreements would strengthen the findings.  The subjectivity inherent in identifying prerequisite skills is a concern.
3. **Methodology limitations acknowledged by authors:** The authors correctly point out that their method only evaluates "understanding of contextual entailments" and doesn’t assess the ability to *find* relevant information within the text. This is a significant limitation and needs to be prominently acknowledged in the discussion.
4. **Potential for metric overlap:** Some of the prerequisite skills appear closely related (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration), potentially leading to redundancy and making interpretation difficult. Further refinement and justification of these categories would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Statistical Analysis:**  Could you provide a more detailed statistical analysis of the relationship between the prerequisite skills, readability metrics, and the performance of existing RC systems on these datasets?
2. **Annotation Details:** Please elaborate on the annotation process, including the training materials provided to annotators and the specific procedures used to resolve disagreements.
3. **Skill Distinction:**  How did you address potential overlap between the prerequisite skills (e.g., bridging and elaboration) during annotation? Were annotators provided with clear guidelines for distinguishing between these categories?
4. **Future Directions:** How do you envision these metrics being used to *actively design* better RC datasets, rather than simply evaluating existing ones? Could you provide specific examples of how to create datasets that target specific skills or readability levels?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-curated and demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature.
*   The discussion section could be expanded to explore the implications of the findings for the development of more robust and human-like RC systems.
*   Exploring the connection between the difficulty levels identified by these metrics and the types of errors made by different RC models would be a valuable addition.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. The paper presents a compelling argument for more nuanced evaluation of RC datasets and provides a useful framework and annotated resources for future research. While some aspects of the analysis could be strengthened, the identified weaknesses do not detract from the overall significance of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and concerns raised above.
