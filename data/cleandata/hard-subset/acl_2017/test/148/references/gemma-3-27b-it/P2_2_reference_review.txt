## Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:** This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets based on two classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" (the cognitive abilities needed to answer a question) and "readability" (the linguistic complexity of the text). The authors refine existing skill taxonomies and apply these metrics to annotate six popular RC datasets, releasing the annotated data for public use. They find a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, suggesting current datasets don’t adequately capture the nuances of RC challenges.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A comprehensive and theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC:**  The paper moves beyond simple question/answer type categorizations and provides a more detailed framework, drawing on psychology and textual entailment literature, for understanding the cognitive demands of RC questions. This is the strongest contribution.
2. **Empirical analysis of existing RC datasets:** The annotation and subsequent analysis of six datasets offer valuable insights into their characteristics.  The finding of a weak correlation between readability and difficulty is notable and highlights the need for more nuanced dataset construction.
3. **Publicly available annotated datasets:**  Releasing the annotated data is a practical contribution that will facilitate future research in RC dataset analysis and system development.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the prerequisite skill taxonomy in established cognitive science theories (Kintsch’s C-I model, McNamara & Magliano) gives the work credibility and provides a principled basis for the chosen metrics. This is a significant improvement over ad-hoc categorizations.
2. **Comprehensive Metric Selection:** The paper considers a broad range of skills beyond simple factual recall, including logical reasoning, causal relations, and bridging inferences.  The inclusion of readability metrics adds another layer of analysis.
3. **Practical Value of the Annotated Datasets:** The annotated datasets are a valuable resource for the RC community. They can be used to train models to predict question difficulty, to evaluate dataset quality, or to create more targeted training sets.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results. The organization of the different components is logical and easy to follow.
5. **Important Observation about Dataset Quality:** The finding that readability doesn’t strongly correlate with difficulty is crucial. It underlines that simply using complex text doesn’t automatically create challenging RC tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Annotation Subjectivity:** While inter-annotator agreement is reported (90.1%), the inherent subjectivity of assigning prerequisite skills is a concern. The paper doesn’t provide sufficient detail about the annotation process to fully assess the potential impact of annotator bias.  It’s unclear *how* disagreements were resolved.
2. **Limited Scope of "Difficulty":** The paper equates question difficulty with the *number* of required skills. This is a reasonable starting point, but doesn’t capture the *type* or *complexity* of those skills.  Some skills are inherently harder than others. A question requiring a single complex inference might be more difficult than one requiring multiple simple ones.
3. **Methodological Limitation – Sentence Selection:** As the authors acknowledge, the methodology only evaluates understanding of contextual entailments *given* a set of relevant sentences. It doesn't assess the system’s ability to *identify* those relevant sentences – a crucial aspect of RC.
4. **Weak Correlation Analysis:** The analysis of correlation between readability and prerequisite skills relies on Pearson’s correlation, which may not be the most appropriate measure for these types of variables. Exploring other statistical methods could reveal more nuanced relationships. The interpretation of "weak" correlation could also be made more specific.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Annotation Details:**  Please provide more detail about the annotation process, including specific guidelines given to annotators, examples of how disagreements were resolved, and the background/expertise of the annotators. Were the annotators NLP experts, or non-experts?
2.  **Skill Weighting:**  Did you consider assigning weights to different prerequisite skills to reflect their relative difficulty? If not, why?  Would this be a viable extension to your work?
3.  **Sentence Selection Evaluation:**  Given the acknowledged limitation regarding sentence selection, do you have plans to extend this work to include an evaluation of that aspect of RC? Perhaps by comparing system performance on datasets where relevant sentences are pre-highlighted versus those where they are not?
4. **Alternative Correlation Measures:** Did you explore alternative measures of correlation beyond Pearson’s, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper references Appendix A and B but those sections weren’t included in the submitted version. Please ensure these are provided.
*   Consider discussing the potential implications of these findings for dataset creation strategies.  What concrete recommendations can be made to developers looking to build more challenging and informative RC datasets?



**Overall Assessment:** This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. The theoretical grounding, comprehensive analysis, and publicly available resources make it a strong submission. While there are some weaknesses, they do not fundamentally detract from the significance of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions above.
