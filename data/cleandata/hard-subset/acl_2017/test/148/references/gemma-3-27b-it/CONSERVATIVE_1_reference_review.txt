This paper explores the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets, proposing a framework based on “prerequisite skills” and “readability” metrics. The authors annotate six existing datasets with these metrics, aiming to provide a more nuanced evaluation of dataset quality and system performance.

The primary contribution of this work is the application of a formalized, psychologically-grounded framework – borrowing from McNamara & Magliano – to analyze existing RC datasets beyond simple accuracy metrics. The refinement of prerequisite skills, drawing on textual entailment and cognitive psychology, is a reasonable extension of prior work (Sugawara et al.). The annotation of six datasets and public release of the data is also a positive contribution, providing a resource for future research. However, the contribution is incremental, building upon existing work rather than presenting a fundamentally new approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Methodological Rigor (relative to the field):**  The paper demonstrates a clear methodology, grounding its analysis in established theories of human text comprehension. The attempt to move beyond superficial dataset categorization (question type) is commendable. The annotation process, though limited to three annotators, appears carefully considered with attention to inter-annotator agreement.
2. **Comprehensive Metric Selection:** The adoption of both prerequisite skills *and* readability metrics represents a more holistic approach to dataset evaluation than is often seen in RC research. Leveraging the existing work of Vajjala and Meurers (2012) on readability is sensible.
3. **Dataset Analysis and Observations:** The findings – weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, varying skill requirements across datasets – are insightful.  The observation that MCTest is relatively difficult despite its readability is a particularly valuable point. The identified differences between datasets, like the high nonsense rate in MS MARCO, are useful for the community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Approach:** The core idea – that RC requires more than just surface-level text processing – is not new.  The paper largely *applies* existing frameworks (McNamara & Magliano, Sugawara et al.) rather than proposing a groundbreaking new technique. While refinement is valuable, the foundational concepts are well-established.
2. **Limited Empirical Validation:** The analysis is primarily descriptive. While the authors identify trends, they do not demonstrate how this analysis *improves* RC system development. There’s no evidence presented showing that systems performing well on datasets characterized by specific skill profiles generalize better, or that targeting skill gaps leads to performance improvements. Simply characterizing datasets doesn’t inherently advance the field; demonstrating utility is crucial.
3. **Annotation Limitations & Scope:**  The annotation is limited to identifying skills *required* to answer a question, but doesn't assess a system's ability to *identify* the necessary information within the text. The authors acknowledge this. Furthermore, the relatively small sample size (100 questions per dataset) raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.  The dataset selection is also somewhat arbitrary.
4. **Weak Correlation Findings – Understated Significance:** The finding of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is presented as a key result, yet the paper doesn’t sufficiently explore *why* this correlation is weak.  Is it a flaw in the readability metrics? A limitation of the prerequisite skill categorization? A fundamental property of RC tasks? This requires further investigation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide examples of how the annotated data (prerequisite skills) could be used to guide the training or architecture design of an RC system? Specifically, how might a system be adapted to better perform on datasets requiring high levels of “bridging” or “elaboration”?
2.  You acknowledge the limitation that your methodology doesn't evaluate the competence of selecting relevant sentences. Do you foresee a natural extension of this work to incorporate such a capability, and if so, what challenges would that entail?
3.  Given the weak correlations observed, do you believe the current readability metrics are adequate for capturing the complexity relevant to RC tasks, or would you suggest exploring alternative or additional features?
4.  You mention the issues with automatically created datasets like MS MARCO. Have you explored methods to automatically identify and filter out "nonsense" questions during dataset creation, potentially using the proposed prerequisite skill framework?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The referencing is comprehensive.  While the work is valuable, its impact is somewhat limited by the lack of a clear connection to system-level improvements or novel methodological contributions. The paper is more of a descriptive analysis than a breakthrough advance.
