## Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets beyond simple accuracy. The authors advocate for considering two classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" (the cognitive abilities needed to answer a question) and "readability" (the linguistic complexity of the text). They refine existing skill categorizations based on psychology and textual entailment literature, annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics, and present an analysis revealing a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The most significant contribution is the articulation of a more nuanced evaluation framework for RC datasets, moving beyond superficial categorization and incorporating cognitive skill requirements. This offers a potentially valuable lens for dataset analysis and construction.
2. **Refined Skill Categorization:** The revision of the prerequisite skill categorization, particularly grounding it in textual entailment and cognitive psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), provides a more theoretically sound basis for assessment.
3. **Annotated Datasets:** Publicly releasing the annotated datasets is a valuable contribution to the community, enabling researchers to investigate the characteristics of existing resources and potentially use the annotations for model training or evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical need:** The paper tackles a vital problem in the RC field: the lack of rigorous methods for evaluating dataset quality. Relying solely on accuracy can be misleading, as demonstrated by the identified issues in datasets like CNN/Daily Mail.
2. **Strong theoretical grounding:** The authors effectively leverage insights from cognitive psychology (understanding human comprehension) and computational linguistics (textual entailment) to inform their metric development. This grounding lends credibility to their approach.
3. **Detailed methodology:** The annotation procedure is well-described, and the decision to allow multiple labeling for prerequisite skills seems sensible. The attempt to quantify sentence distance is a good effort to capture context complexity.
4. **Insightful findings:**  The observation of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a significant finding. It highlights the limitations of solely focusing on text complexity when evaluating or building RC systems and datasets. The dataset-specific observations are also helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the annotation is valuable, the analysis primarily focuses on *descriptive* statistics (frequencies of skills).  A more compelling evaluation would involve investigating how these metrics correlate with the performance of various RC *systems* across the datasets.  Do systems struggle with questions requiring specific skills, or at certain readability levels?
2. **Annotation Reliability Concerns:**  While inter-annotator agreement is reported (90.1%), the paper doesn't detail *where* disagreements occurred. Understanding the nature of these disagreements would be crucial for assessing the reliability and validity of the annotations.  Are there specific skills that are consistently difficult to categorize?
3. **Subjectivity in Skill Assignment:** The categorization of "prerequisite skills" inherently involves a degree of subjectivity. The paper doesn’t fully address how annotators were trained to consistently apply these skills, or how potential biases were mitigated.
4. **Methodological Limitation – Sentence Selection:** As the authors themselves acknowledge, the annotation only focuses on identifying the *required* sentences, not evaluating the system’s ability to *find* them. This limits the scope of the evaluation to understanding, given the context, rather than the complete RC process.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **System Performance Correlation:** Have you investigated how the proposed metrics correlate with the performance of existing RC systems (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) on the annotated datasets?  Can these metrics predict which datasets or questions will be more challenging for different types of systems?
2. **Annotation Disagreement Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the inter-annotator disagreements? Which skills showed the highest levels of disagreement, and what were the primary reasons for these discrepancies?
3. **Annotation Training & Guidelines:** Please elaborate on the training provided to the annotators and the detailed guidelines they followed when assigning prerequisite skills. Were examples provided, and how was consistency enforced?
4. **Impact of Annotation Focus:**  Recognizing the limitation of focusing only on sentence *selection*, do you have any plans to extend this work to incorporate evaluation of the sentence *retrieval* component of RC?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more visually compelling presentation of the data, perhaps using scatter plots or heatmaps to illustrate the correlations between metrics.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of applying these metrics, as this may be a barrier to adoption for some researchers.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper that addresses an important challenge in the RC field. The proposed framework and annotated datasets have the potential to significantly advance our understanding of dataset quality and guide the development of more robust RC systems. While there are weaknesses regarding the scope of evaluation and annotation reliability, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on addressing the questions raised above.
