## Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable investigation into the multifaceted nature of reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors rightly point out the limitations of relying solely on accuracy as an evaluation metric and propose a framework based on two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. While the methodology has some limitations, the detailed analysis of several datasets and the publicly released annotations represent a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary of the Paper:** The paper addresses the issue of accurately evaluating RC systems and datasets. It argues that simple accuracy metrics are insufficient, as question difficulty isn’t solely determined by text readability. The authors propose evaluating datasets based on two criteria: (1) prerequisite skills needed to answer a question, and (2) the readability of the text. They annotate six existing datasets with these metrics and analyze the results, finding a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** The detailed annotation of six widely used RC datasets with both prerequisite skills and readability metrics provides a valuable resource for the community. This analysis reveals nuanced characteristics of each dataset, highlighting their strengths and weaknesses.
2. **Refined Framework for Prerequisite Skills:** The reorganization of prerequisite skills, incorporating insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology, is a thoughtful improvement over prior work.  The categorization (bridging, elaboration, etc.) provides a more theoretically grounded approach to assessing question difficulty.
3. **Highlighting the Disconnect between Readability and Difficulty:** The paper effectively demonstrates, through empirical analysis, that higher readability does not necessarily equate to easier questions in RC datasets. This challenges a common assumption and encourages a more nuanced approach to dataset design and evaluation.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the need for more sophisticated evaluation metrics beyond simple accuracy in RC. The examples provided in the introduction effectively illustrate the issue.
2. **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the methodology in established theories of human text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) lends credibility to the approach. Leveraging insights from textual entailment also strengthens the analysis.
3. **Rigorous Methodology:** The annotation process, while manual, appears well-defined, and the reported inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) suggests a reasonable level of reliability. The inclusion of detailed descriptions of the annotation procedure and dataset specifications enhances reproducibility.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:** The commitment to making the annotated datasets publicly available is a significant contribution that will benefit the research community. This fosters further research and allows for validation of the findings.
5. **Thoughtful Discussion of Limitations:**  The authors openly acknowledge the limitations of their methodology (e.g., inability to evaluate sentence selection competence) and discuss potential avenues for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** Despite the inter-annotator agreement, the assignment of prerequisite skills is inherently subjective.  It would be beneficial to explore methods for quantifying the uncertainty associated with these annotations (e.g., reporting the distribution of annotations for each question).
2. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** While the chosen readability metrics are well-established, they may not fully capture the nuances of text difficulty relevant to *reading comprehension* specifically. The metrics focus heavily on surface features and may not account for factors like domain knowledge or discourse structure.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper demonstrates a weak *correlation* between readability and prerequisite skills.  However, it doesn’t investigate the *causal* relationship between these factors. It is possible that other variables contribute to both readability and question difficulty.
4. **Annotation Focused on Entailment:** The authors explicitly state that their methodology evaluates "understanding of contextual entailments in a broader sense for RC". While valid, it's a narrower scope than fully evaluating RC and this should be more prominently acknowledged. It doesn't assess *why* a system might fail to select the relevant text, just whether it can reason about the provided context.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Annotation Uncertainty:**  Could you provide more details on the distribution of annotations for each prerequisite skill?  Were there specific skills that exhibited significantly lower agreement?
2. **Readability Metric Justification:** Could you elaborate on why the specific set of readability metrics chosen was considered most relevant for assessing RC difficulty?  Did you consider including other metrics related to discourse coherence or domain specificity?
3. **Future Directions:**  You mention limitations regarding the evaluation of sentence selection competence.  Are there potential methods for incorporating this aspect into your framework, perhaps through a separate annotation stage?
4. **Entailment Focus:**  Given that your methodology primarily focuses on contextual entailment, how do you see it complementing other RC evaluation techniques that assess different aspects of comprehension (e.g., knowledge integration, inference generation)?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures (e.g., Figure 3 & 4) are helpful for visualizing the relationships between the metrics.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough and contextualizes the contributions of this paper effectively.
*   The writing is clear and concise, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.



In conclusion, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension by providing a nuanced analysis of existing datasets and proposing a more comprehensive evaluation framework. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
