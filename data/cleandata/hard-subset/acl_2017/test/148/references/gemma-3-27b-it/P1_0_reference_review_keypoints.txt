- Paper studies the link between question difficulty and source-text readability in RC datasets, proposing "prerequisite skills" and "readability" metrics and annotating six datasets; finds weak correlation between readability and difficulty.

- Contribution: Adopts two metric classes (prerequisite skills, readability) for qualitative RC dataset analysis.

- Contribution: Revises prerequisite skills taxonomy, grounding it in textual entailment and cognitive theories of comprehension.

- Contribution: Annotates six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) and plans to publicly release the annotations.

- Reviewer: The annotated datasets are a valuable, substantial resource for fine-grained RC analysis and dataset design.

- Reviewer: Empirical finding that readability weakly correlates with question difficulty challenges common assumptions in dataset construction.

- Reviewer: Grounding the evaluation in cognitive science is a commendable theoretical advance that improves interpretability.

- Strength: Annotation methodology is well-defined with high inter-annotator agreement (~90.1%), showing reasonable consistency.

- Strength: Broad, comparative analysis across six diverse RC datasets with clear, informative result tables.

- Strength: Theoretical grounding in cognitive psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) strengthens metric rationale and skill refinement.

- Strength: Practical implications for dataset design, advocating consideration of cognitive skills beyond surface readability.

- Strength: Public release of annotated datasets is beneficial for the community.

- Weakness: Scope limited to identifying required skills without evaluating whether systems can actually use those skills.

- Weakness: Skill annotation is inherently subjective; deeper analysis of disagreements and sources of subjectivity is needed.

- Weakness: Demonstrates correlation but does not establish causation; unmeasured factors might drive observed patterns.

- Weakness: Readability metrics used are surface-level; more advanced measures (semantic coherence, discourse structure) could improve assessment.

- Weakness: Methodology depends on annotators selecting relevant sentences, missing the crucial skill of identifying relevant information in longer texts.

- Question: How does categorizing "no answer" questions affect generalizability, and can the annotation scheme be revised to handle them better?

- Question: Can the authors provide more details on annotation guidelines, disagreement resolution, and example-driven consistency checks?

- Question: Would measuring the absence of certain skills (e.g., lack of causal reasoning) be a useful dataset-level metric?

- Question: How might the evaluation integrate the ability to find relevant sentences, not just understand them, into a comprehensive framework?

- Question: Would alternative correlation measures (e.g., Spearman) be more appropriate given possible non-linear relationships?

- Additional: Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and references are comprehensive and up-to-date.

- Overall assessment: Valuable contribution with significant insights and useful resources; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
