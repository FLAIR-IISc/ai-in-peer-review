- Paper proposes a nuanced RC evaluation framework using "prerequisite skills" and "readability" beyond simple accuracy.
- Refines skill categorizations by integrating psychological and NLP theories (textual entailment, comprehension models).
- Publishes annotations of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics for community use.
- Presents empirical analysis showing weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.
- Strong motivation and clear problem definition illustrating limits of current metrics.
- Solid theoretical grounding in cognitive and NLP literature.
- Well-defined annotation methodology with reported inter-annotator agreement.
- Practical value and replicability via publicly available annotated datasets and detailed methodology.
- Insightful finding that readability alone is a poor predictor of RC difficulty.
- Readability metrics used are standard and may miss RC-relevant textual complexity.
- Analysis depends on annotation quality; more review of disagreement cases and annotator influence is needed.
- Study shows correlation but not causation; other unexamined factors may drive results.
- Lacks system-level evaluation demonstrating how metrics diagnose real RC system failures.
- Requests clarification on annotation challenges and which skills caused disagreement.
- Asks whether and how sentence-selection competence could be incorporated in future work.
- Seeks concrete examples of integrating these metrics into RC system development and improvement.
- Asks about potential confounding factors affecting observed relationships among skills, readability, and difficulty.
- Writing is clear but some sections (e.g., readability metric descriptions) could be streamlined.
- Reference list is comprehensive and relevant.
- Overall recommendation: solid contribution; accept with minor revisions.
