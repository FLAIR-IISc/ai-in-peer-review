This paper presents a valuable, though preliminary, analysis of reading comprehension datasets through the lens of prerequisite skills and readability. The core contribution is the adoption and refinement of a framework from psychology (McNamara & Magliano, 2009) to *characterize* existing RC datasets, revealing a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty.  A secondary, but also significant, contribution is the publicly released annotation of six datasets with these metrics, providing a resource for future work. The reorganization of prerequisite skills, aligning them with textual entailment and human comprehension models, is a positive step, even if not a breakthrough in itself.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Framing:** The paper's most significant strength is its framing of RC dataset evaluation. Moving beyond simple accuracy and question types to consider the cognitive demands (prerequisite skills) and linguistic complexity (readability) offers a promising new direction. This is a genuinely insightful perspective that moves the field toward more nuanced dataset understanding.
2. **Practical Dataset Analysis:** The annotation of six popular RC datasets is a concrete and useful contribution.  The resulting data, even if preliminary, provides a valuable starting point for researchers seeking to understand the characteristics of different datasets and design targeted training strategies. The identified variations (e.g., MCTest being challenging despite readability) are immediately insightful.
3. **Psychologically Grounded Approach:**  The grounding of the methodology in cognitive psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) adds credibility and suggests a potentially more robust approach to evaluating RC tasks than purely linguistic analyses.  Connecting NLP tasks to underlying cognitive processes is always a strength.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly presents its methodology and findings. The organization around the two metric classes (prerequisite skills, readability) is logical and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Validation & Scope:** While the analysis is insightful, it is largely descriptive. There is no demonstration of how these metrics *improve* RC system development or evaluation. The paper states its aim is to help construct better datasets and systems, but doesn’t illustrate *how*. A small-scale experiment showing a correlation between performance on questions categorized by these metrics and system capabilities would be incredibly valuable.
2. **Annotation Subjectivity:**  The reliance on annotator judgments for prerequisite skills introduces subjectivity. While inter-annotator agreement is reported, the potential for differing interpretations of skills (particularly the more abstract ones like “bridging” or “elaboration”) could impact the results. A more detailed analysis of disagreements, and exploration of ways to reduce subjectivity, is needed.
3. **Weak Correlation, Limited Explanation:** The finding of a weak correlation between readability and difficulty is interesting, but the paper doesn’t deeply explore *why* this is the case. It hints at datasets like MCTest but doesn't fully investigate the linguistic features or question design that might contribute to this discrepancy.  More analysis of specific question/passage examples is warranted.
4. **Methodological Limitations:** The statement that the methodology cannot evaluate the “competence of looking for sentences” is a significant limitation. Reading comprehension, at its core, *is* about identifying relevant information. Ignoring this aspect limits the scope of the evaluation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide examples of questions from each dataset that highlight significant discrepancies between readability and the number of prerequisite skills required? A deeper dive into specific examples would be highly informative.
2.  What steps were taken to mitigate subjectivity in the annotation process?  Were annotators provided with detailed guidelines and examples for each prerequisite skill? Were disagreements resolved through discussion and consensus?
3.  Do you have preliminary ideas about how these metrics could be used to *actively* improve RC systems (e.g., curriculum learning, targeted data augmentation)?
4.  Have you considered incorporating metrics related to the *search* process (e.g., sentence selection accuracy) into your evaluation framework?

*   The paper’s title could be more evocative; "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension" is somewhat generic.



**Overall:** This paper represents a promising first step towards a more nuanced understanding of reading comprehension datasets. While the work is currently descriptive and lacks rigorous validation, the novel framing, practical contribution of annotated datasets, and grounding in cognitive psychology are compelling. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors acknowledging the limitations and outlining future directions for exploring the *impact* of these metrics on RC system development. The boldness of the approach and the potential for future impact outweigh the current methodological shortcomings.
