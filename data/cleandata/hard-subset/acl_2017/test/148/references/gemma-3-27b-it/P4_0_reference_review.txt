This paper proposes a methodology for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets based on two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The core idea is to move beyond simple accuracy and analyze *how* difficult a question is, and whether that difficulty correlates with the complexity of the text it’s based on. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and present an analysis of the results, finding a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the attempt to provide a more nuanced characterization of RC datasets. While the individual metrics themselves aren’t novel (they draw heavily from established work in psychology and NLP), the combination and application of these metrics to analyze existing datasets is a valuable contribution. The annotation of six datasets and public release of the results is also a significant undertaking, providing a resource for the RC community. A secondary contribution is the refinement of prerequisite skills for RC, organizing them in terms of textual entailment and human text comprehension, offering a theoretically grounded framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial issue in the RC field: the limitations of relying solely on accuracy as a measure of progress. The introduction clearly articulates the need for more fine-grained evaluation metrics.
2. **Comprehensive Methodology:** The adoption of two distinct classes of metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – provides a holistic approach to dataset analysis. Leveraging existing frameworks from psychology (McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (Vajjala & Meurers) lends credibility to the chosen metrics.
3. **Resource Creation:** The annotation of six commonly used RC datasets is a substantial contribution. Making these annotations publicly available will facilitate further research and enable more informed comparisons between datasets.
4. **Interesting Findings:** The observation of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is noteworthy and challenges the intuitive assumption that more complex texts inherently lead to more difficult questions. This finding prompts a deeper consideration of what factors *do* contribute to question difficulty.
5. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The tables and figures effectively summarize the annotation results and allow for easy comparison between datasets. The discussion of each dataset’s characteristics based on the metrics is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Metrics:** The core metrics used—readability scores and prerequisite skills—are not new. The paper’s novelty resides in the *application* and combination of these metrics, not their invention. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion acknowledging this.
2. **Annotation Subjectivity:** While inter-annotator agreement is reported (90.1%), the inherent subjectivity in identifying prerequisite skills could introduce bias into the results. A more detailed discussion of the annotation process, including examples of disagreements and how they were resolved, would strengthen the study.
3. **Methodological Limitation – Sentence Selection:** The methodology only focuses on *understanding* contextual entailments, given a set of relevant sentences. It does not evaluate the system’s ability to *identify* those relevant sentences. This is a significant limitation, as sentence selection is a crucial component of many RC systems.
4. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper identifies a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, but doesn't explore potential causal relationships. Further analysis could investigate whether specific readability features are associated with particular types of prerequisite skills, or vice versa.
5. **Impact of Dataset Construction:** The influence of dataset creation methods (e.g., automated curation vs. human-authored) on the observed results isn’t fully explored. A more in-depth discussion of this factor could provide valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions the annotation process allowed for multiple labeling of prerequisite skills. Could you provide more detail on how these multiple labels were handled during analysis (e.g., were they combined, averaged, or considered individually)?
2.  The annotation process was designed to be “equivalent” across datasets, focusing on contextual entailment. Given the known differences in task formulation across these datasets, could you elaborate on how this equivalence was ensured, and whether any adjustments were made to the annotation guidelines for specific datasets?
3.  The paper highlights the limitation that the methodology doesn’t evaluate sentence selection competence. Do you envision extending this work to incorporate an evaluation of sentence selection, and if so, what approach would you take?
4.  Could you provide more detail on how the “nonsense” questions were identified and handled during the annotation process? What criteria were used to classify a question as nonsense?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are generally well-chosen and relevant.
*   The writing is clear and concise.
*   Appendix A and B are helpful in providing details of the annotation and analysis process.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential applications of these metrics beyond dataset evaluation, such as for curriculum learning or adaptive testing.
