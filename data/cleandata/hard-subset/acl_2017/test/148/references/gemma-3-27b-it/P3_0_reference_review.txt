## Peer Review for "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors argue that existing datasets lack sufficient granularity in their evaluation metrics, relying primarily on superficial categories. They propose adopting two classes of metrics – prerequisite skills (building on previous work and informed by psychological models of comprehension) and readability – to provide a more nuanced understanding of dataset characteristics and system performance. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and present initial findings, demonstrating a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The paper offers a well-motivated and relatively comprehensive framework for evaluating RC datasets beyond simple accuracy, combining considerations of cognitive skill requirements and textual complexity. This framework is a valuable contribution for the community.
2. **Refined Skill Categorization:** The reorganization of prerequisite skills, drawing on textual entailment and cognitive psychology, represents a thoughtful refinement of existing categorizations and provides a more theoretically grounded approach.
3. **Annotated Datasets:** The publicly available annotations of six popular RC datasets represent a significant resource for researchers, enabling more targeted analysis of dataset characteristics and system strengths/weaknesses.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of current RC dataset evaluation methods and convincingly argues for the need for more nuanced metrics. The initial examples effectively illustrate the disconnect between text readability and question difficulty.
2. **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the framework in cognitive psychology (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) lends credibility and theoretical depth to the proposed metrics.  This connection helps move beyond purely surface-level features.
3. **Detailed Methodology:** The annotation procedure is clearly described, and the authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., not evaluating sentence selection). The careful consideration given to inter-annotator agreement is also commendable.
4. **Interesting Initial Findings:** The observed weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a significant finding, suggesting that dataset quality cannot be assessed solely through readability metrics. This highlights the importance of considering the cognitive demands of the questions.
5. **Practical Resource:**  The annotated datasets are a valuable contribution to the RC research community, enabling more detailed analysis of existing resources and serving as a basis for future dataset creation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Analysis:** While the annotation is a valuable resource, the analysis presented is largely descriptive. The paper lacks a deeper exploration of *why* the observed weak correlations exist.  For example, are certain types of questions consistently difficult regardless of readability? Is there a correlation between specific prerequisite skills and dataset performance?
2. **Subjectivity of Skill Annotation:** Despite the clear guidelines, annotating prerequisite skills inherently involves a degree of subjectivity. The paper doesn’t fully address the potential impact of annotator bias on the results.  A more detailed analysis of inter-annotator agreement on *individual skills* would be beneficial.
3. **Readability Metrics as Proxies:** The paper acknowledges that readability metrics don’t perfectly correlate with cognitive difficulty for RC systems.  This raises a concern about the validity of using these metrics as a primary measure of "reading difficulty."  Exploring more directly relevant metrics (e.g., features that capture syntactic ambiguity or semantic complexity) might be more insightful.
4. **Lack of System-Level Evaluation:** The paper focuses primarily on dataset characteristics.  It would be strengthened by presenting results of applying these metrics to evaluate the performance of existing RC systems across different datasets.  This would demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed framework.
5. **Minor: Limited Discussion of Related Work:** While a related work section exists, it feels somewhat cursory. A more thorough discussion of relevant work on question difficulty, cognitive modeling of RC, and dataset bias would enrich the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Inter-Annotator Agreement Details:** Could you provide more detailed statistics on inter-annotator agreement, broken down by individual prerequisite skills? Were there any specific skills where agreement was particularly low, and if so, what steps were taken to address this?
2. **Deeper Analysis of Results:**  Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of the relationship between the identified prerequisite skills and dataset performance? Are certain skills consistently required for difficult questions, regardless of readability?
3. **System-Level Evaluation Plans:** Do you have plans to apply these metrics to evaluate the performance of existing RC systems? If so, could you share any preliminary findings?
4. **Limitations of Readability Metrics:** You acknowledge that readability metrics are proxies for cognitive difficulty.  Have you considered exploring alternative metrics that might be more directly relevant to the cognitive demands of RC?
5. **Future Work:** How do you envision this framework being used to guide the *creation* of new RC datasets that are more challenging and better aligned with human cognitive processes?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The figures and tables are helpful and informative.
*  The inclusion of a public dataset is a significant strength.
*  Consider adding a discussion of potential biases in the datasets themselves that might contribute to the observed results.



Overall, this is a strong paper with valuable contributions to the field of reading comprehension. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further enhance the paper's impact and usefulness to the research community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
