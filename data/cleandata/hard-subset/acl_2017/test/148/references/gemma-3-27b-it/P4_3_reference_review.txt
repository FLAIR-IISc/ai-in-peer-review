This paper proposes an analysis of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics, aiming to demonstrate a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, and to provide a more nuanced understanding of dataset characteristics. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a formalized framework for analyzing RC datasets beyond simple accuracy, incorporating both cognitive skills required for answering questions and the linguistic complexity of the text; (2) a refined categorization of prerequisite skills based on insights from textual entailment and psychology, building upon previous work by Sugawara et al.; and (3) a publicly available annotated dataset, providing a valuable resource for researchers.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Valuable Analytical Framework:** The core idea of analyzing RC datasets through the lens of prerequisite skills *and* readability is strong.  Existing evaluation often focuses solely on accuracy, overlooking the specific cognitive demands and linguistic features contributing to difficulty. This framework provides a more granular and insightful view of dataset quality and system performance. The explicit connection to cognitive models of comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) lends theoretical grounding.
2. **Refined Prerequisite Skill Categorization:** The authors' revision of the prerequisite skills, particularly the integration of textual entailment and psychological frameworks, is a significant improvement over previous categorizations. Moving beyond a simplistic listing of skills to a more theoretically informed organization (bridging vs. elaboration, retrieved vs. generated) enhances the framework’s analytical power and potential for guiding system development.
3. **Publicly Available Annotation:** The annotation of six datasets is a valuable contribution to the RC research community. Providing this resource allows others to validate the findings, conduct further analysis, and build upon the work.  This significantly increases the impact of the study.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly motivates the research. The initial examples (Figure 1) effectively illustrate the disconnect between text readability and question difficulty, establishing the need for the proposed analysis. The authors effectively tie their work to existing literature and identify gaps in current RC evaluation practices.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Weak Correlation Findings Need Further Investigation:** While the observation of a weak correlation between prerequisite skills and readability is interesting, the paper lacks a deeper exploration of *why* this correlation is weak. Is it inherent to the task, a limitation of the metrics, or a characteristic of the datasets themselves?  More in-depth analysis – perhaps examining specific skill types and readability features – could provide more actionable insights. The use of Pearson correlation alone feels somewhat limited.
2. **Annotation Methodology Limitations:** The methodology relies on annotators identifying “needed” sentences.  This inherently focuses on *entailment* as stated, but doesn't evaluate a system's ability to *find* relevant information. This is a crucial distinction.  Moreover, the potential for annotator bias (e.g., subjective interpretation of skill requirements) isn't fully addressed.  Inter-annotator agreement is reported, but further details on disagreements and resolution would be valuable.
3. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** The reliance on a fixed set of readability metrics (Vajjala & Meurers, 2012) feels somewhat restrictive. These metrics focus largely on surface-level linguistic features.  Exploring more sophisticated measures of text complexity, such as coherence relations or discourse structure, could provide a more comprehensive assessment of readability and its relation to question difficulty.
4. **Potential Confounding Factors Not Addressed:** The paper doesn't fully address potential confounding factors that could influence the results. For example, the topic of the text or the type of question (e.g., who, what, why) may play a significant role in difficulty, independent of prerequisite skills and readability. Controlling for these factors could strengthen the analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper states that the annotation methodology evaluates “understanding of contextual entailments in a broader sense”. Could you elaborate on how this differs from standard textual entailment tasks, and how your annotation approach specifically captures aspects of RC beyond entailment?
2. Given the weak correlation observed, what specific hypotheses do you have about the reasons for this disconnect between prerequisite skills and readability? What further analysis could be conducted to investigate these hypotheses?
3. Could you provide more details on the annotator training process and the guidelines used to ensure consistency in skill annotation?  Were there specific instances where annotators disagreed, and how were those disagreements resolved?
4.  How might the proposed framework be extended to evaluate the *generation* of RC datasets, rather than simply analyzing existing ones? Could it be used to guide the creation of datasets with specific skill profiles?
