## Peer Review for "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the crucial problem of evaluating reading comprehension (RC) systems beyond simple accuracy. The authors argue that current RC datasets lack sufficient granularity in their categorization, focusing primarily on superficial aspects like question type. They propose a framework for evaluating datasets and system performance based on two classes of metrics: “prerequisite skills” required to answer questions and the “readability” of the context.  They refine existing skill categorization based on psychological and linguistic theories, annotate six RC datasets with these metrics, and present initial findings regarding the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The paper's most significant contribution is the articulation of a more nuanced framework for RC evaluation, moving beyond simplistic accuracy metrics to consider both the cognitive skills required for comprehension and the textual characteristics of the context. This provides a valuable lens for analyzing RC datasets and system performance.
2. **Refined Skill Categorization:**  The revision of prerequisite skills, grounding it in theories of textual entailment and human text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), is a valuable contribution.  The move away from purely task-specific categorization towards psychologically informed skills offers a more generalizable framework.
3. **Annotated Datasets:** The annotation of six widely used RC datasets with these metrics is a significant undertaking and provides a useful resource for the research community.  Making this annotation publicly available will facilitate further research and development in the field.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of current RC evaluation practices and provides a strong motivation for exploring more nuanced metrics. The examples in Figure 1 effectively illustrate the disconnect between readability and question difficulty.
2. **Strong Theoretical Grounding:**  The authors appropriately leverage insights from cognitive psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and linguistics to inform their metric definitions.  This elevates the work beyond a purely empirical exercise.
3. **Rigorous Methodology:**  The annotation procedure appears well-defined, and the inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) suggests a reasonable level of reliability.  The consideration of potential biases in the annotation process (e.g., filtering in NewsQA) is commendable.
4. **Insightful Results:** The finding of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is important and challenges the assumption that more complex text inherently leads to more challenging RC tasks. The dataset-specific analyses offer valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each dataset.
5. **Public Availability of Data:** Making the annotated datasets publicly available is a huge benefit to the community and encourages further exploration of the proposed metrics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the annotation of six datasets is valuable, the analysis focuses primarily on *descriptive* statistics of the metrics. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more *analytical* investigation—for example, correlating the metrics with system performance on those same datasets. Without this, it’s hard to assess the practical utility of the metrics in diagnosing system weaknesses.
2. **Annotation Limitations:** The methodology explicitly acknowledges that it doesn’t evaluate sentence selection competence, a key aspect of RC.  This limitation should be more prominently discussed, as it shapes the conclusions drawn from the annotation.  Furthermore, the reliance on annotators to identify “prerequisite skills” is inherently subjective. Exploring alternative methods for skill identification (e.g., using NLP tools) could enhance the objectivity of the framework.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:**  The paper identifies correlations but doesn’t delve into potential causal relationships. For example, *why* is there a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty? Exploring underlying mechanisms could yield deeper insights.
4. **Readability Metric Selection:** While leveraging Vajjala & Meurers (2012) is sensible, the paper doesn’t justify the choice of those *specific* metrics beyond their general relevance to readability.  A more detailed discussion of the theoretical basis for each selected metric would be beneficial.
5. **Overly Broad Skill Definitions**: Some of the skill definitions (particularly within "Commonsense Reasoning") feel quite broad.  While a balance between granularity and practicality is important, the breadth could introduce ambiguity in annotation and limit the precision of the analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **System Performance Correlation:** Have you analyzed the correlation between the metrics you've defined and the performance of existing RC systems on the annotated datasets? If so, what insights did this analysis reveal?  If not, are you planning to conduct such an analysis?
2. **Sentence Selection Evaluation:**  Recognizing the limitation of not evaluating sentence selection, do you have any thoughts on how this aspect could be incorporated into a more comprehensive evaluation framework? Could techniques like attention mechanisms be leveraged to assess which sentences a system attends to?
3. **Skill Disambiguation:**  How did you address potential ambiguity during the annotation process, particularly with the broader skill definitions within “Commonsense Reasoning?” What training was provided to the annotators to ensure consistency in applying these labels?
4. **Metric Weighting:**  Do you envision a scenario where different prerequisite skills could be weighted based on their importance or difficulty? If so, how might such weighting be determined?
5. **Automated Metric Calculation:**  Could some of the prerequisite skill annotations be automated using existing NLP tools (e.g., coreference resolution tools, causal relation extraction)? What challenges would need to be addressed to achieve reliable automation?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections (e.g., the description of readability metrics) could benefit from more detailed explanation.
*  The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the typical performance of RC systems on each dataset, to provide context for interpreting the metric values.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. The proposed framework for evaluating RC datasets and systems is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and supported by empirical evidence. While some limitations remain, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and the public release of the annotated datasets will be a significant asset to the research community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section.
