This paper presents a valuable, if somewhat exploratory, analysis of existing reading comprehension datasets through the lens of prerequisite skills and readability. The central contribution is the adoption and refinement of a framework – drawing from psychology and textual entailment – to *characterize* rather than *solve* RC difficulties. The authors annotate six datasets with these metrics, providing a resource for future work and demonstrating a weak, but present, correlation between readability and the cognitive skills required for answering questions. A second, smaller contribution is the reorganization of prerequisite skills, specifically in the area of knowledge reasoning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Framing:** The paper’s strength lies in shifting the focus from purely performance-based evaluation of RC systems to a more nuanced understanding of *what* makes a question difficult. Framing RC difficulty in terms of prerequisite skills and readability, inspired by cognitive science, is a bold and welcome move. This encourages a move beyond simply chasing higher accuracy and towards building systems that can demonstrably handle different kinds of reasoning.
2. **Resource Creation:** The annotated datasets are a valuable contribution. Making these annotations publicly available will allow researchers to analyze existing datasets with a new set of tools and potentially identify areas for dataset improvement or targeted system development.
3. **Insightful Observations:** The findings, particularly the weak correlation between readability and difficulty, are insightful. This challenges the intuitive assumption that more complex text automatically leads to harder questions.  The observation that datasets like MCTest can be difficult despite being written for children is particularly strong.
4. **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of relevant work in psychology (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) and textual entailment, providing a strong foundation for their chosen metrics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited System Impact:** While the analysis is insightful, the paper doesn’t demonstrate how these metrics can be *used* to improve RC systems. The discussion of curriculum learning is a start, but a more concrete proposal for system development guided by these metrics would strengthen the work. It’s primarily a characterization, not a prescription.
2. **Annotation Subjectivity:**  The annotation process, while described, is inherently subjective. The inter-annotator agreement is good (90.1%), but the paper doesn’t delve into the types of disagreements encountered or how they were resolved.  Further analysis of annotation discrepancies could reveal insights into the ambiguity of the skill categorization.
3. **Methodological Limitations:** The authors acknowledge that their methodology doesn’t evaluate the sentence selection process – a critical component of RC. This limits the scope of the analysis. Focusing *only* on the skills needed *given* the selected context misses a crucial aspect of human (and potentially AI) reading comprehension.
4. **Statistical Significance:** While correlation coefficients are reported, the paper lacks robust statistical testing to determine the significance of the observed relationships.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide examples of annotation disagreements and how they were resolved? What types of questions/skills were most prone to disagreement?
2. How could the framework be extended to evaluate the sentence selection process itself? (e.g., adding a skill related to relevance assessment)
3. Do you envision these metrics being used directly as loss functions for training RC models, or primarily as diagnostic tools for dataset analysis? If the former, can you elaborate on how this might be implemented?
4. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the Flesch-Kincaid grade level was chosen as the primary measure of readability? Did you consider other readability metrics and, if so, why were they not included?



**Additional Comments:**

* Table 6 is helpful but a bit buried in the discussion. It could be highlighted more prominently.
* The related work section is adequate but could be slightly more focused on recent advancements in RC evaluation.



Overall, this is a promising paper that introduces a novel perspective on RC evaluation. While the methodological limitations and lack of direct system impact prevent a highly enthusiastic recommendation, the insights gained and the publicly available annotations are valuable contributions. The boldness of the framing and the potential for future work outweigh the weaknesses, leading me to recommend **Acceptance**.
