This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC), proposing two classes of evaluation metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – to analyze existing datasets. The authors annotate six datasets with these metrics and present a descriptive analysis of the results.

The primary contribution of this work is a structured annotation of six existing RC datasets using a combination of established cognitive science principles (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) and NLP techniques (Vajjala & Meurers). The resulting annotated datasets, if made publicly available as claimed, represent a valuable resource for the RC community. A secondary, less compelling, contribution is the reorganization of prerequisite skills based on textual entailment and human text comprehension, although this largely represents a refinement of existing work (Sugawara et al.). Finally, the descriptive analysis, while not groundbreaking, confirms an intuitive observation – a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Resource Creation:** The annotated datasets are the strongest aspect of this work. The systematic application of the proposed metrics to multiple, established datasets offers a consistent framework for future research. The claim of public availability is crucial; if fulfilled, this will significantly benefit the community.
2. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** Grounding the evaluation metrics in established cognitive science (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) provides a more principled approach than purely task-specific evaluations. This is a positive step towards understanding *why* certain questions are difficult, rather than just *that* they are difficult.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The annotation procedure is reasonably well-defined, including mention of inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), lending credibility to the results.  The consideration of multiple prerequisite skills per question is also a strength.
4. **Confirmation of Intuition:** The finding of a weak correlation between readability and difficulty, while not novel, is a useful empirical confirmation that supports the need for more nuanced evaluation methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core ideas – using cognitive principles to analyze RC tasks – are not entirely new.  The refinement of prerequisite skills, while helpful, is incremental. The paper relies heavily on existing literature and does not present a substantially novel algorithmic or theoretical breakthrough.
2. **Descriptive Analysis Lacks Depth:** The paper largely presents descriptive statistics. While the results are interesting, there’s a lack of in-depth statistical analysis (beyond correlation coefficients) to explore more complex relationships between the metrics. For example, exploring interactions between different prerequisite skills would be beneficial.
3. **Annotation Limitations:**  The methodology, as acknowledged by the authors, doesn't evaluate the *selection* of relevant sentences, only the understanding of those sentences once they are provided. This limits the scope of the evaluation. Furthermore, the annotation process might be subjective, and a more detailed error analysis would be valuable.
4. **Weak Justification for Dataset Selection:** The rationale for choosing the specific six datasets isn't particularly strong. While commonly used, a more systematic selection process (e.g., representing different RC paradigms or difficulty levels) would have been preferable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more detail on the inter-annotator agreement beyond the overall percentage? Specifically, what was the agreement *per skill*? Were there specific skills where agreement was low, and if so, how were disagreements resolved?
2.  You mention that the methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences. Do you have plans to address this limitation in future work, perhaps by incorporating a separate evaluation component for sentence selection?
3.  You state the annotated datasets will be publicly available. Can you provide a specific timeline and repository details (e.g., GitHub link) for access?
4.  The correlation coefficients in Table 5 are generally weak.  Do you have any hypotheses about why this is the case?  Could the metrics themselves be refined to better capture the difficulty of RC tasks?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 2 could be improved for readability (e.g., using a more consistent decimal format).
*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly recent advancements in RC evaluation beyond simple accuracy.
*  The use of "nonsense" as a category requires more justification. Is this simply unsolvable, or does it indicate a flaw in the question itself?



Overall, this paper presents a valuable, albeit incremental, contribution to the field of reading comprehension. The annotated datasets represent a significant resource, and the grounding in cognitive science principles is commendable. However, the lack of substantial novelty and the descriptive nature of the analysis limit its overall impact. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the public release of the annotated datasets and a response to the questions raised above.
