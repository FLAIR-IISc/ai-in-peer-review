## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets beyond simple accuracy, focusing on two classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" (the cognitive abilities needed to answer a question) and "readability" (the linguistic complexity of the text). The authors refine existing skill categorizations based on psychology and textual entailment research and annotate six RC datasets with these metrics. The key finding is a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, suggesting that many RC datasets contain questions solvable with minimal reading effort or, conversely, require complex reasoning from easily readable text.

**Main Contributions (as perceived by reviewer):**

1. **A comprehensive framework for RC dataset evaluation:** The paper provides a valuable, multi-faceted approach to assessing the *quality* of RC datasets, going beyond simple performance metrics.  The combination of prerequisite skills and readability offers a nuanced view of what a dataset is actually testing.
2. **Refined categorization of prerequisite skills:** The reorganization of knowledge reasoning skills, grounded in cognitive science and textual entailment, offers a theoretically stronger foundation for analyzing the cognitive demands of RC tasks than previous work.
3. **Annotated Datasets:** The public release of the annotated datasets is a valuable resource for the RC community, enabling researchers to analyze existing datasets and design better ones.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical gap in RC research:**  Current RC evaluation heavily relies on accuracy scores, which offer limited insight into *why* systems succeed or fail. This paper directly tackles this problem by proposing a more granular analysis.
2. **Strong theoretical grounding:** The authors effectively draw upon research in psychology (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) and NLP (textual entailment) to justify their choice of metrics and refine existing skill categorizations. This adds credibility and depth to the work.
3. **Thorough annotation process:** The annotation methodology appears well-defined, with clear guidelines and inter-annotator agreement reported, enhancing the reliability of the results. The inclusion of nonsense question identification is a particularly important contribution.
4. **Clear and insightful results:** The finding of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a significant observation with important implications for dataset design. The dataset-specific analyses provide valuable insights into the characteristics of existing resources.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited scope of “difficulty” assessment:**  While the paper effectively measures the complexity of *understanding* the context, it doesn't address the difficulty of *finding* the relevant information within the context.  The authors acknowledge this limitation but it's a crucial aspect of RC that remains unaddressed by their metrics.
2. **Annotation granularity & subjectivity:**  Assigning prerequisite skills is inherently subjective. While inter-annotator agreement is reported, the nuances of skill categorization could still introduce variability.  Further analysis of disagreement would be beneficial.
3. **Correlation doesn't equal causation:** The paper demonstrates a weak correlation, but doesn’t explore *why* this correlation is weak.  Further investigation into the underlying factors driving this relationship would strengthen the conclusions. For example, are certain question types consistently independent of readability?
4. **Limited analysis of system performance *using* the metrics:** The paper focuses on dataset analysis. The real power of these metrics will be demonstrated by showing how they correlate with system performance (e.g., can they predict which systems will perform well on which datasets?).




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Addressing information retrieval difficulty:**  How might the framework be extended to incorporate metrics related to the difficulty of *locating* the relevant information needed to answer a question? Could sentence position or highlighting requirements be used as proxies?
2. **Annotation Disagreement:** Can you provide more details about the types of disagreements encountered during annotation? Were some skills consistently more difficult to categorize than others?
3. **Causality of weak correlation:** Do you have any hypotheses as to why the correlation between readability and prerequisite skills is weak? Are there specific dataset characteristics or question types that contribute to this?
4. **Future work with system performance:** Do you have plans to use these metrics to analyze the performance of existing RC systems? If so, could you describe the anticipated experimental setup?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work in dataset bias and the limitations of current evaluation metrics.
* A visual representation of the prerequisite skill hierarchy (e.g., a tree diagram) could enhance clarity.
* Consider providing the annotation data publicly alongside the code used for calculating readability metrics.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of Reading Comprehension. The proposed framework offers a much-needed approach to evaluating the quality of RC datasets and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.  While some limitations exist, they are clearly acknowledged by the authors. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions outlined above.
