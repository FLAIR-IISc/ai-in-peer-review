## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the critical need for more nuanced evaluation of Reading Comprehension (RC) systems beyond simple accuracy. The authors argue that existing RC datasets lack sufficient categorization to understand *why* a system succeeds or fails. They propose a framework leveraging two classes of metrics – "prerequisite skills" (building on prior work and psychological models of comprehension) and "readability" – to analyze both the datasets themselves and, potentially, system performance. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and present initial findings, demonstrating a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The paper presents a well-motivated and structured framework for evaluating RC datasets, moving beyond superficial categories. The integration of cognitive psychology principles (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) into the definition of “prerequisite skills” is a valuable contribution.
2. **Annotated Datasets:** The authors provide a publicly available annotation of six popular RC datasets, which is a significant resource for the community. This allows for direct comparison of datasets along the proposed metrics and facilitates more targeted system development.
3. **Empirical Demonstration of Dataset Characteristics:** The analysis reveals important characteristics of existing datasets, particularly the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty. This finding underscores the limitations of relying solely on surface-level features of the text.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of current RC evaluation practices and provides a compelling justification for a more granular approach. The examples in the introduction effectively illustrate the disconnect between readability and comprehension difficulty.
2. **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the "prerequisite skills" metric in established psychological models of text comprehension lends credibility and theoretical depth to the proposed framework.  The connection to textual entailment research is also well-justified.
3. **Thorough Methodology:**  The annotation process is clearly described, including the consideration of inter-annotator agreement. The selection of datasets is also reasonable, covering a range of complexities and creation methods.
4. **Valuable Resource Creation:** The annotated datasets are a practical and impactful contribution, providing the research community with a tool for more informed dataset analysis and system development.
5. **Thoughtful Discussion:** The discussion section raises important considerations for future research, including the role of corpus genre and the need for more sophisticated question types.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Analysis:** While the annotation itself is valuable, the analysis primarily focuses on descriptive statistics.  The paper doesn't delve deeply into *how* these metrics correlate with system performance on these datasets (beyond stating a general weak correlation). Exploring correlations with different model architectures or specific failure cases would strengthen the impact.
2. **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:**  The annotation of "prerequisite skills" inherently involves subjective judgment. While the authors report good inter-annotator agreement, the potential for bias and ambiguity in skill categorization remains a concern.  Providing more detailed annotation guidelines or examples would mitigate this.
3. **Readability Metrics – Limited Scope:** The chosen readability metrics, while established, represent only a subset of potential linguistic features. Exploring other features (e.g., discourse structure, argument complexity) could provide a more comprehensive picture of text difficulty.
4. **Annotation Limitations - Sentence Selection:**  The annotation focuses on the sentences *required* to answer the question, but doesn’t evaluate the system’s ability to *identify* those sentences. As the authors acknowledge, this limits the scope to understanding contextual entailment *given* the correct context.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **System Performance Correlation:** Could you elaborate on any preliminary investigations correlating the annotated metrics (prerequisite skills, readability) with the performance of existing RC systems on these datasets?  Even preliminary results would provide valuable insights.
2. **Annotation Refinement:** What steps were taken to ensure consistency and mitigate subjectivity during the annotation process?  Can you provide examples of ambiguous cases and how they were resolved?
3. **Future Work –  Expanding Readability Metrics:** Do you plan to incorporate additional readability metrics (e.g., discourse-level features) in future work? If so, which ones and why?
4. **Annotation Tool & Data Availability:**  Can you provide information on the availability of the annotation tool used and any plans for making the annotated data more readily accessible (e.g., a dedicated website)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 is helpful in illustrating the motivation for the work.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. The proposed framework, annotated datasets, and initial findings provide a strong foundation for more nuanced evaluation of RC systems and datasets. While some areas could benefit from further investigation, the work is well-motivated, methodologically sound, and has the potential to significantly impact the development of more robust and intelligent RC systems. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weakness section and the questions to authors.
