This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC), proposing a framework based on “prerequisite skills” and “readability” metrics to analyze existing datasets. The authors annotate six datasets with these metrics and present preliminary findings.

The primary contribution, and the most compelling aspect of this work, is the explicit framing of RC dataset analysis through the lens of cognitive skills – specifically, breaking down question difficulty into required “prerequisite skills” derived from psychology and textual entailment research. This is a *bold* move, shifting the focus from simply achieving high accuracy to *understanding what* makes a question hard. While the specific skill categorization isn't groundbreaking in itself, the attempt to ground RC evaluation in a more theoretically-motivated framework is valuable. A second, supporting contribution is the public annotation of six popular RC datasets with these metrics, providing a resource for future research. Finally, the demonstration of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a noteworthy empirical observation, challenging a common intuitive assumption.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Framing:** The core strength lies in reframing RC dataset evaluation. Most work focuses on *building* better models; this paper focuses on *understanding* the datasets themselves, and specifically the cognitive demands they place on a system.  This is a crucial, often overlooked, aspect of progress in RC.
2. **Cognitively-Inspired Approach:**  Drawing upon work in psychology (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) and textual entailment provides a solid foundation, moving beyond ad-hoc categorization schemes. The attempt to tie RC difficulty to specific cognitive skills is a significant step toward more interpretable evaluation.
3. **Resource Creation:** The annotated datasets are a valuable contribution.  Researchers can now use these annotations to develop targeted training datasets, analyze model performance on specific skills, and diagnose weaknesses in existing systems.
4. **Counterintuitive Finding:** The finding that readability doesn't strongly correlate with question difficulty is interesting and potentially important. It suggests that simply increasing text complexity isn’t necessarily a good strategy for creating challenging RC tasks.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Annotation Subjectivity:** While inter-annotator agreement is reported, the inherent subjectivity in assigning prerequisite skills is a concern.  The paper doesn’t delve deeply into resolving disagreements, and the potential impact of annotator bias isn’t fully addressed.  A more detailed analysis of annotation discrepancies would be beneficial.
2. **Limited Validation of Skill Categorization:** The paper acknowledges the provisional nature of the skill categorization. While drawing from existing literature is helpful, a more rigorous validation of this categorization—perhaps through correlation with human performance or cognitive modeling—would strengthen the work.
3. **Methodological Limitations:** The annotation methodology only considers skills *required to answer* the question, not skills required to *find* the relevant information in the text.  This limits the scope of the analysis. A system might struggle not because it can't *understand* the text once it's located, but because it can't *find* the text in the first place.
4. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The observed weak correlation between readability and difficulty doesn't establish causation. It’s possible that other factors (e.g., question phrasing, background knowledge requirements) are more strongly correlated with difficulty.
5. **Limited Exploration of Dataset Differences:** While descriptive statistics are presented, the paper doesn’t fully explore *why* different datasets exhibit different patterns of skill usage. A more in-depth analysis of the characteristics of each dataset could provide valuable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more detail on the annotation process? Specifically, how were disagreements between annotators resolved? Were there any guidelines for handling ambiguous cases?
2.  What steps could be taken to validate the prerequisite skill categorization? Would it be possible to correlate the skill assignments with human performance data on the same datasets?
3. Do you plan to extend the methodology to also consider the skills required to locate relevant information within the text, not just to answer the question given the text?
4.  What are the most surprising or unexpected findings from the dataset analysis? Were there any datasets that exhibited particularly unusual patterns of skill usage?



**Additional Comments:**

The table formatting is a little awkward in places. Consider using a more consistent style for presenting the results. While the connection to psychology is a strength, explicitly framing the work as a step toward building more *human-like* RC systems could strengthen the motivation.
