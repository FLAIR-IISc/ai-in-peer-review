## Peer Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors argue that current RC evaluation relies too heavily on simple accuracy and lacks nuanced metrics. They propose adopting two classes of evaluation metrics – prerequisite skills (building on prior work and informed by psychology and textual entailment) and readability – to better characterize datasets and system performance. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and present initial findings suggesting a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The most significant contribution is the proposal of a combined framework utilizing prerequisite skills and readability metrics for RC dataset evaluation. This moves beyond simple accuracy and offers a more granular understanding of dataset characteristics.
2. **Refined Prerequisite Skill Taxonomy:** The reorganization and expansion of existing prerequisite skills, particularly grounding them in theories of textual entailment and human comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), is a valuable refinement. This provides a more theoretically motivated and potentially robust classification scheme.
3. **Annotated Datasets:** The creation and public release of annotated datasets is a useful resource for the RC community, enabling more informed analysis and comparison of systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Gap:** The paper rightly identifies a weakness in current RC evaluation: the reliance on simplistic metrics that don't reveal *why* a system succeeds or fails.  The proposed framework directly addresses this by providing a richer characterization of both questions and texts.
2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** Grounding the prerequisite skills in established psychological models of comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and linguistic theories (textual entailment) lends credibility and theoretical depth to the work. This isn’t just an ad-hoc set of skills; it’s informed by decades of research on how humans understand text.
3. **Practical Contribution with Annotations:**  The annotation of six datasets is a significant practical contribution.  It provides a concrete resource for researchers and allows for empirical validation of the proposed metrics. The decision to make these datasets publicly available further enhances their impact.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The motivation is clear, the methodology is explained adequately, and the results are presented in a straightforward manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Weak Correlation – Limited Practical Impact (Currently):** While the finding of a weak correlation between readability and difficulty is interesting, it doesn’t yet translate into clear guidance for dataset creation or system development. The paper acknowledges this, but more work is needed to determine *which* prerequisite skills are most strongly correlated with difficulty and how those skills can be targeted during system design.  The current findings are more descriptive than prescriptive.
2. **Annotation Subjectivity & Inter-Annotator Agreement:** While a 90.1% inter-annotator agreement is good, the potential for subjectivity in identifying prerequisite skills remains a concern.  The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of annotation disagreements and how they were resolved, and potentially a kappa statistic.
3. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** Relying solely on Vajjala & Meurers' linguistic features for readability feels somewhat limited. While a good starting point, other readability measures (e.g., those focused on semantic complexity) could provide a more comprehensive assessment. The paper concedes that these metrics don't necessarily correlate with system performance, suggesting a need for more nuanced measures of *algorithmic* readability.
4. **Methodology Focus – Missed Opportunity for System Evaluation:** The paper explicitly focuses on dataset *analysis* rather than system performance evaluation.  A compelling follow-up would be to use the annotated datasets to analyze the performance of existing RC systems with respect to the identified prerequisite skills. This could reveal which skills current systems struggle with and guide future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Annotation Disagreements:** Could you provide more detail on the types of disagreements encountered during annotation, and how these were resolved? Were any skills consistently more difficult to annotate than others?
2. **Correlation Analysis:** Beyond the Pearson correlations presented in Table 5, did you investigate more complex relationships between readability metrics and prerequisite skill counts?  For example, did you explore interactions between specific readability features and specific skills?
3. **System Performance Analysis:** Do you have any plans to use the annotated datasets to analyze the performance of existing RC systems? If so, could you share preliminary thoughts on how this analysis might be conducted?
4. **Annotation Time:** Approximately how much time did each annotator spend annotating a single question? This would provide some insight into the cost and scalability of the annotation process.



**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of related work on *automated* readability assessment, particularly approaches that go beyond surface-level linguistic features.
*  The choice of datasets is reasonable, but a brief justification for their selection (beyond simply being "existing RC datasets") would be helpful.
* The paper uses "nonsense" as a category. It might be beneficial to define this more explicitly (e.g., "unsolvable due to ambiguity, missing information, or logical inconsistency").




Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. The proposed framework and annotated datasets have the potential to significantly advance our understanding of dataset quality and guide the development of more robust and capable RC systems. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
