This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors propose analyzing datasets through two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability, building on prior work in psychology and NLP. They annotate six existing datasets with these metrics and present an analysis of the results, demonstrating a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty. 

The primary contribution of this work is the comprehensive application and refinement of existing psychological and NLP-based metrics to the analysis of RC datasets. While the individual metrics themselves aren’t novel, the combination, the adaptation of prerequisite skills categorization based on textual entailment and human comprehension, and the large-scale annotation across multiple datasets represent a valuable resource and insightful analysis for the RC community. A second contribution is the highlighting of the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, underscoring the need for more nuanced evaluation of RC systems and datasets than simply focusing on text complexity. Finally, the publicly released annotated datasets provide a useful benchmark for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive and Methodologically Sound:** The paper adopts a well-defined framework, grounded in both psychological theories of comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) and NLP techniques for readability assessment. The combination of these approaches provides a rigorous basis for the analysis.
2. **Large-Scale Annotation:** Annotating six diverse datasets with the proposed metrics is a significant undertaking and provides a valuable resource for the research community. The inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% lends confidence to the quality of the annotation.
3. **Insightful Findings:** The finding of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a crucial observation. It challenges the intuitive assumption that easier-to-read texts necessarily lead to easier questions and suggests that question construction plays a significant role in overall difficulty. This pushes the field to consider more complex factors when designing and evaluating RC datasets.
4. **Clear Presentation of Metrics:** The paper clearly defines and explains each metric within the two classes, along with justification for their selection. The tables detailing the metrics and their values for each dataset are well-organized and informative.
5. **Practical Implications:** The analysis provides valuable guidance for dataset construction and system development. The suggestion of a curriculum learning approach based on these metrics is a practical and promising direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Annotation:** While annotating six datasets is commendable, the selection of 100 questions *per* dataset may not be fully representative of the entire dataset’s characteristics. A stratified sampling approach might have yielded more robust results.
2. **Annotation Methodology Limitations:** The authors acknowledge that their methodology doesn’t evaluate the competence of *sentence selection* – a critical skill in RC. This is a significant limitation, as the annotation assumes the correct sentences are already provided.  The focus is solely on the difficulty *given* the context, not the ability to find the relevant context.
3. **Weak Correlation Interpretation:** While the finding of a weak correlation is significant, the paper doesn’t thoroughly explore *why* this correlation is weak. More in-depth analysis, such as identifying specific question types or skills that contribute to difficulty independent of readability, could strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Readability Metrics Choice:**  The reliance on Vajjala and Meurers (2012) for readability metrics is reasonable, but the paper doesn't justify *why* these specific metrics were chosen over others. A brief discussion of alternative metrics and their potential relevance would be helpful.
5. **Lack of System-Based Validation:** The analysis focuses solely on dataset characteristics. It would be beneficial to correlate these metrics with the performance of existing RC systems on the annotated datasets to demonstrate their predictive power.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How was the selection of 100 questions from each dataset determined? Was it truly random, or was there any bias in the selection process?
2.  Given the acknowledged limitation regarding sentence selection, are there plans to extend the annotation to include this aspect, perhaps through a separate annotation task or by leveraging existing attention mechanisms from RC models?
3.  Could you provide more examples of questions that are high in prerequisite skills but low in readability, and vice versa?  This would help illustrate the disjunction you've identified.
4.  Were any attempts made to calculate the statistical significance of the observed weak correlations? 
5.  How would you propose to incorporate these metrics into an automated RC dataset evaluation pipeline?



**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of related work is adequate but could be more focused on recent advancements in RC dataset evaluation.
* The formatting of Table 2 could be improved for readability (e.g., using bold font for significant values).
*  Appendix A and B are well-written and provide necessary details regarding the methodology.
