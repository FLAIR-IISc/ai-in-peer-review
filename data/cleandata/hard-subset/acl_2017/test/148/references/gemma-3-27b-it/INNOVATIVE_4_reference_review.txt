This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC), proposing a framework utilizing “prerequisite skills” and “readability” metrics to analyze existing datasets. The primary contribution is a nuanced characterization of several RC datasets, revealing a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty – a counterintuitive but important observation. A secondary contribution is the refinement of prerequisite skill categorization, drawing on insights from textual entailment and psychology. Finally, the authors provide a publicly available annotation of six datasets using their framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Important Observation:** The core finding – that readability doesn’t strongly correlate with question difficulty – is genuinely insightful. This challenges the intuitive assumption that harder-to-read texts automatically equate to more challenging RC tasks. It highlights the importance of question *design* and the cognitive skills required beyond simply decoding the text. This is a significant contribution to understanding what RC datasets actually measure.
2. **Interdisciplinary Approach:** The paper successfully integrates concepts from NLP (textual entailment), psychology (human text comprehension models), and education (readability metrics). This breadth of perspective strengthens the analysis and provides a more holistic framework for evaluating RC datasets. The grounding in established psychological models (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) adds credibility.
3. **Practical Value through Dataset Analysis:** The annotation and analysis of six popular RC datasets provide a valuable resource for the community. The detailed breakdown of prerequisite skills needed for each dataset allows researchers to target specific skills in system development and to identify datasets suited for particular evaluation purposes.
4. **Well-Motivated Refinement of Prerequisite Skills:** The revision of Sugawara et al.’s categorization, informed by textual entailment and cognitive models, represents a clear improvement in defining and classifying the cognitive demands of RC questions. The distinction between bridging and elaboration, in particular, is a useful addition.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Annotation Subjectivity & Limited Inter-Annotator Agreement Details:** While 90.1% agreement is reported, the paper lacks details regarding *where* disagreements occurred. Understanding the types of annotation discrepancies is crucial. Were disagreements concentrated on specific skills, datasets, or question types? This is important to assess the reliability of the annotations.
2. **Methodological Limitations in Sentence Selection:** The authors explicitly acknowledge that their methodology doesn’t evaluate the system’s ability to *find* the relevant sentences, only its ability to *understand* them once provided. This is a significant limitation; a core skill in RC is identifying the necessary information within the context.
3. **Correlational Analysis, Not Causation:** The paper demonstrates correlations, but doesn't establish causal relationships. It's possible that other factors beyond readability and prerequisite skills influence question difficulty. Further investigation into these confounding variables would be beneficial.
4. **Readability Metrics – Limited Scope:** The choice of readability metrics is somewhat limited. While Vajjala & Meurers’ selection is reasonable, a wider range of metrics, potentially including those specifically designed for assessing cognitive load, could provide a more comprehensive picture.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more detail regarding the types of disagreements encountered during the annotation process? Specifically, which skills or datasets exhibited the highest levels of disagreement, and what were the main reasons for these discrepancies?
2.  Do you have any plans to extend this framework to incorporate an evaluation of the system's ability to *select* the relevant sentences from the context, in addition to understanding them?
3.  Were any attempts made to control for other potentially confounding variables that might influence question difficulty, beyond readability and prerequisite skills? If so, what were they?
4.  Given the acknowledgement that the current metrics don't fully capture the difficulty for systems finding information, would you consider the "prerequisite skills" a better proxy for *system* difficulty versus human difficulty?



**Additional Comments:**

The formatting is generally good, and the paper is clearly written. The related work section is adequate but could benefit from including more recent studies on RC dataset analysis. Table 4 regarding readability metrics seems incomplete as it lacks values for some metrics. Table 6 is interesting and could be further explored. While the lack of complete validation is a concern, the novelty of the insights and the practical value of the annotated datasets outweigh these limitations. I lean towards acceptance.
