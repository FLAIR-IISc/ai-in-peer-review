This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC), proposing a framework based on “prerequisite skills” and “readability” metrics. The authors annotate six RC datasets with these metrics, aiming to provide a more nuanced evaluation of both datasets and RC systems. 

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of a psychologically-grounded framework (McNamara & Magliano, 2009) to the analysis of RC datasets. While the individual components (readability metrics, prerequisite skills) are not novel, their combined use and application to multiple datasets provide a potentially valuable lens for understanding dataset characteristics and informing system development. A secondary contribution is the publicly available annotated datasets, which could serve as a useful resource for future research. Finally, the refinement of the prerequisite skills categorization, incorporating insights from textual entailment and psychology, represents a modest advancement over prior work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Psychological Grounding:** The connection to McNamara and Magliano’s work adds a theoretical depth often missing in RC dataset analysis. Framing the evaluation in terms of human comprehension processes is a strong point.
2. **Comprehensive Metric Suite:** The adoption of both prerequisite skills *and* readability metrics provides a more holistic view of dataset difficulty than relying solely on accuracy or question type.
3. **Dataset Annotation & Public Release:** The annotation of six datasets, and making them publicly available, is a significant practical contribution. This allows the community to reproduce the results and build upon this work.
4. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The tables summarizing the skill frequencies and readability metrics are clear and well-organized, enabling easy comparison between datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Weak Correlation Findings – Understated Implications:** The paper consistently finds only weak correlations between prerequisite skills and readability. While acknowledged, the implications of this finding aren’t fully explored. The authors state it implies that easy-to-read texts *can* have difficult questions, but don’t delve into *why* this is the case – what specific skills are driving the difficulty independent of readability? This limits the actionable insights derived from the analysis.
2. **Annotation Reliability Concerns:** The inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is acceptable, but a more detailed breakdown of agreement *per skill* would be valuable. Were certain skills consistently more difficult to annotate? Disagreement rates for specific skills might reveal ambiguities in the skill definitions or challenges in their application.
3. **Methodological Limitation – Sentence Selection:** The authors explicitly acknowledge their methodology doesn’t evaluate sentence selection competence, focusing solely on understanding *given* context. This is a significant limitation. A robust RC system must *first* identify relevant sentences, and this step isn’t assessed. The metric only assesses comprehension *after* relevant information is provided.
4. **Superficial Analysis of Nonsense Questions:** The identification of “nonsense” questions is valuable, but the analysis lacks depth. Simply reporting the frequency of nonsense questions isn’t sufficient. What characteristics distinguish these questions? Are they related to specific datasets or question types?
5. **Minor Formatting/Presentation Issues:** There are numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of bolding, varying indentation, occasional grammatical errors). These detract from the paper's overall polish.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the weak correlation between readability and prerequisite skills, can you elaborate on *what specific skills* are most frequently required in questions that are difficult despite being posed over easy-to-read texts? Provide examples from the annotated datasets.
2.  Please provide a more granular analysis of inter-annotator agreement, reporting agreement rates *for each individual prerequisite skill*. Were there specific skills where annotators consistently disagreed?
3.  Do you plan to extend your methodology to evaluate the sentence selection component of RC systems? If not, can you discuss the implications of excluding this critical step from your analysis?
4.  Can you provide examples of “nonsense” questions from the datasets and explain why they were categorized as such? What characteristics consistently identify these questions?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 6 is not referenced in the main text.
*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough literature review of existing work on RC dataset analysis.
*   Consider clarifying the distinction between "textual entailment" and "knowledge reasoning" – their relationship is not entirely clear.



Overall, while the paper presents a valuable framework and a useful resource, the weak correlations and methodological limitations, combined with the numerous minor flaws, prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a deeper analysis of the disconnect between readability and difficulty, is required.
