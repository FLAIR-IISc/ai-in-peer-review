This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC), proposing two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and present an analysis of the results, claiming a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, a revised categorization of prerequisite skills, and the public release of the annotated datasets.

The primary contribution is the detailed annotation of six RC datasets with a refined set of prerequisite skills and readability metrics. This provides a valuable resource for the RC community, enabling more nuanced analysis of dataset characteristics. A secondary contribution is the identification of a weak correlation between question difficulty (as measured by required skills) and text readability, challenging the assumption that more complex texts necessarily lead to more difficult questions. Finally, the refinement of the prerequisite skill categorization, drawing on insights from textual entailment and psychology, offers a more theoretically grounded framework than prior work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Annotation:** The extensive annotation of six datasets is a significant undertaking and provides a valuable resource for researchers. The detailed skill breakdown offers a more granular view of question difficulty than simple accuracy scores.
2. **Theoretical Grounding:** The grounding of the prerequisite skills in theories of textual entailment and human text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) is a strength. This moves beyond purely empirical categorization and provides a more robust framework.
3. **Highlighting Dataset Nuances:** The analysis reveals interesting differences between datasets (e.g., MCTest’s readability vs. difficulty, the issues with automatically generated questions in MS MARCO). This nuanced understanding of existing datasets is crucial for informed dataset development.
4. **Clear Methodology:** The annotation procedure is reasonably well-defined, and the attempt to control for annotator bias (through guidelines and inter-annotator agreement) is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Weak Correlation – Insufficient Exploration:** While the paper *claims* a weak correlation between readability and difficulty, the analysis feels superficial. Correlation coefficients are reported (Table 5), but there's limited discussion of *why* this correlation is weak. The figures (3 & 4) are difficult to interpret and don’t clearly demonstrate the claimed relationship.  A more thorough statistical analysis and visualization are needed.
2. **Annotation Limitations – Sentence Selection:** The methodology explicitly *excludes* evaluating the competence of sentence selection. This is a significant limitation. A critical skill in RC is identifying relevant information within a context. Ignoring this aspect drastically narrows the scope of the evaluation. The statement that the annotation focuses on “understanding of contextual entailments” is a misleading attempt to reframe this limitation.
3. **Prerequisite Skill Granularity & Overlap:** Some of the prerequisite skills feel overly granular and potentially overlapping. For example, the distinction between “Bridging” and “Elaboration” may be subtle and difficult for annotators to consistently apply. A more streamlined categorization might be beneficial. The definitions also remain somewhat vague.
4. **Readability Metrics – Limited Scope:** Relying solely on Vajjala & Meurers’ (2012) features feels restrictive. Other readability metrics, particularly those focusing on discourse coherence, could provide additional insights. The authors acknowledge this, but don’t offer a justification for their choice.
5. **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper suffers from repetitive phrasing and a slightly convoluted writing style. Several sections could benefit from more concise language and clearer organization. The repeated referencing of previous work (Sugawara et al.) feels excessive.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Beyond simply stating a weak correlation, can you provide a more in-depth analysis of *why* readability and question difficulty are not strongly correlated? What specific aspects of the datasets or question types contribute to this discrepancy?
2.  You acknowledge that your methodology doesn’t evaluate sentence selection. How does this limitation affect the generalizability of your findings? Do you believe sentence selection is a crucial component of RC, and if so, how could it be incorporated into future evaluations?
3.  Can you provide examples of questions that were challenging for annotators to categorize based on the prerequisite skills (particularly Bridging vs. Elaboration)? What steps were taken to address disagreements among annotators?
4.  Why were Vajjala & Meurers' (2012) readability metrics chosen over other potentially relevant features, such as measures of discourse coherence?
5.  Could you provide a more visually compelling representation of the relationship (or lack thereof) between readability and question difficulty, perhaps using scatter plots or other visualizations that highlight the distribution of data points?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive but could benefit from better formatting.
*   Appendix A and B are helpful, but could be more integrated into the main text.
*   The discussion section feels somewhat speculative and lacks concrete recommendations for dataset construction or system development beyond the general suggestion of “curriculum learning”.



Overall, while the paper presents a valuable resource and raises important questions about RC evaluation, the analysis feels incomplete and the conclusions are not fully supported by the evidence. The limitations in the methodology and the superficial exploration of the results prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. I recommend **rejection** at this time, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version.
