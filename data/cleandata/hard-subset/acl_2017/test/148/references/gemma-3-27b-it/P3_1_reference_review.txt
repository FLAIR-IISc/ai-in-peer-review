## Peer Review for "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper addresses the limitations of current evaluation metrics for Reading Comprehension (RC) systems, arguing that simple accuracy is insufficient. The authors propose a more nuanced approach by adopting two classes of metrics – “prerequisite skills” and “readability” – to analyze both datasets and system performance. They refine existing skill categorization based on psychology and textual entailment research, annotate six RC datasets with these metrics, and present an analysis demonstrating a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty. 

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The primary contribution is the proposal of a structured framework for evaluating RC datasets beyond simple accuracy, incorporating both cognitive skill requirements and textual complexity. This provides a valuable lens for understanding *why* systems succeed or fail.
2. **Refined Skill Categorization:**  The reorganization of prerequisite skills, grounding it in established theories of human text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), is a solid advancement over previous work (Sugawara et al.). The move towards entailment-based reasoning is well-justified.
3. **Annotated Datasets:** The public release of the annotated datasets is a significant contribution to the community, providing a resource for more in-depth analysis of existing benchmarks and for developing more targeted training data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the work in cognitive psychology and textual entailment provides a robust framework for the proposed metrics. The clear connection to existing theories (Kintsch’s inferences, McNamara & Magliano’s comprehension aspects) elevates the work beyond simple feature engineering.
2. **Addresses a Critical Gap:** The paper identifies a legitimate weakness in the field – the lack of nuanced evaluation beyond accuracy – and offers a practical approach to address it.  The observation that readability doesn't necessarily correlate with difficulty is insightful and important.
3. **Thorough Methodology:** The annotation process is clearly described, and the inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) suggests good reliability. The selection of datasets covers a reasonable range of RC tasks.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the concepts and methodology accessible. The tables summarizing the results are clear and informative.
5. **Valuable Dataset Resource:** Providing the annotated datasets to the research community is a significant contribution that will facilitate further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Skill Evaluation:** While the refined skill categorization is a strength, the annotation methodology only focuses on *required* skills *given* a correct answer.  It doesn't assess whether a system *incorrectly* identifies required skills, or whether the skill categorization itself is flawed for a given question. This limits the diagnostic power of the metrics.
2. **Readability Metrics' Relevance:** The paper acknowledges that the readability metrics are designed for human readability, not machine comprehension.  While a useful baseline, their correlation (or lack thereof) with prerequisite skills may not be particularly informative about the challenges faced by RC *systems*.  The paper could benefit from exploring more NLP-focused readability measures.
3. **Lack of System-Level Analysis:**  The paper primarily analyzes datasets. It would be significantly stronger if it included even a preliminary analysis of how existing RC systems perform on these annotated datasets, potentially identifying areas where systems struggle based on the identified skill requirements.
4. **Annotation Burden & Potential Bias:**  The annotation task is complex and potentially subjective.  While inter-annotator agreement is good, the potential for annotator bias in identifying required skills remains a concern. A more detailed discussion of annotator training and disagreement resolution would be beneficial.
5. **Distance Metric Simplification:** The calculation of sentence distance seems overly simplistic.  Syntactic relationships between sentences might be more important than simple linear distance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Skill Evaluation Depth:**  How would the methodology need to be adapted to assess *why* a system fails on a question – i.e., does it misidentify the necessary skills, or struggle with those skills even when correctly identified?
2. **NLP-Focused Readability:**  Did you consider incorporating more NLP-driven readability metrics (e.g., based on parsing complexity, dependency relations) that might be more aligned with the challenges faced by RC systems?  If not, why?
3. **System Performance Analysis:** Do you have any preliminary results applying these metrics to analyze the performance of existing RC systems on the annotated datasets?  If so, can you share them?
4. **Annotator Bias Mitigation:** What specific steps were taken to minimize annotator bias during the training and annotation process? Were any disagreement resolution mechanisms in place?
5. **Distance Metric Justification:** Why was simple linear sentence distance chosen as the metric for assessing context span? Could more sophisticated metrics (e.g., based on syntactic parsing) improve the analysis?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Appendix A and B are helpful for understanding the methodological details.
*   The Related Work section could be expanded to include more recent work on explainable AI for RC, which is relevant to the concept of identifying prerequisite skills.
*   Consider discussing the limitations of using datasets originally designed for different purposes (e.g., QA4MRE) within this new framework.



Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of Reading Comprehension. The proposed metric framework is well-motivated and potentially impactful. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly through a more in-depth analysis of system performance, would significantly strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
