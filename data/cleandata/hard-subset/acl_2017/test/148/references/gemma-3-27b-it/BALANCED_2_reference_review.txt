This paper investigates evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on the relationship between question difficulty and text readability. The authors adopt two classes of metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – and apply them to annotate six existing RC datasets, making the annotated data publicly available. 

The primary contribution of this work is the comprehensive annotation of six RC datasets with a refined set of prerequisite skills and readability metrics. This annotated resource is valuable for the RC community, enabling more nuanced analysis of dataset quality and system performance than simple accuracy scores allow. The reorganization of prerequisite skills, aligning them with textual entailment and psychological models of human comprehension, is a solid secondary contribution. Finally, demonstrating a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability is a notable, though perhaps unsurprising, finding.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Annotated Resource:** The annotated datasets represent a significant contribution. Providing the RC community with richer metadata beyond question type and answer type is crucial for targeted improvement of both datasets and models. The annotation procedure is clearly described, and the inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) suggests reasonable reliability.
2. **Refined Prerequisite Skills:** The authors’ effort to refine the existing categorization of prerequisite skills, drawing upon research in textual entailment and psychology, is commendable.  The distinctions between bridging and elaboration, and the inclusion of ellipsis and meta-knowledge, offer a more theoretically grounded and potentially more useful framework for analyzing question difficulty. This is a stronger approach than simply adopting the previous categorization without critical evaluation.
3. **Insightful Correlation Analysis:** The finding that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability is important. It challenges the intuitive assumption that more complex text automatically leads to more difficult questions, and highlights the need for more sophisticated metrics and dataset design. Presenting the data visually (Figures 3 and 4) strengthens this point.
4. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed analysis of each of the six datasets, highlighting their strengths and weaknesses based on the proposed metrics. This provides valuable guidance to researchers selecting datasets for specific tasks or evaluating the capabilities of their models.
5. **Clear Presentation of Methodology:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology used for annotation and analysis. The inclusion of an appendix detailing the sampling methods and distance calculations further enhances transparency.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Readability Metrics:** While the adoption of readability metrics from Vajjala and Meurers (2012) is reasonable, these metrics are not novel to the field. The paper doesn't significantly advance the state-of-the-art in readability assessment itself, primarily leveraging existing measures.
2. **Annotation Limitations: Sentence Selection:** The methodology focuses on annotating *sentences needed to answer the question* without evaluating the *process* of selecting those sentences. As the authors acknowledge, this limits the assessment of a system's ability to identify relevant context. A more holistic evaluation would involve annotating the rationale behind the selection of specific sentences.
3. **Subjectivity in Prerequisite Skill Annotation:** Despite a reasonable inter-annotator agreement, the annotation of prerequisite skills inherently involves some degree of subjectivity. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how disagreements were resolved, and a deeper analysis of the sources of potential bias in the annotation process. Providing examples of annotated questions could also improve clarity.
4. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The analysis demonstrates a correlation, but does not establish a causal relationship between prerequisite skills and readability. Further investigation is needed to understand whether improving readability metrics directly translates to improvements in RC performance.
5. **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on descriptive statistics and correlation analysis. It would be beneficial to explore potential applications of the annotated data, such as training models to predict question difficulty or generating more challenging questions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a few examples of questions from each dataset along with their annotations (prerequisite skills and readability metrics)? This would help clarify the annotation process and the application of the proposed metrics.
2. How were disagreements between annotators resolved during the annotation process? Was there a majority-rules approach, or were disagreements adjudicated by a senior annotator?
3.  You mention the limitation that your methodology doesn't evaluate the selection of relevant sentences. Have you considered ways to address this limitation in future work, such as incorporating attention weights or rationale annotation?
4. The paper focuses on a relatively small sample size (100 questions per dataset). How confident are you that the observed trends would generalize to the entire dataset?
5.  How could this annotated dataset be used to create new, more robust reading comprehension benchmarks? What properties would such benchmarks ideally have?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work on question difficulty and dataset quality in reading comprehension.
*   Consider including a table summarizing the characteristics of each dataset (e.g., source, size, genre) in addition to the annotation results.
*   The figures could be improved by adding axis labels and clearer legends.



Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the RC community. The annotated datasets are a significant resource, and the analysis provides valuable insights into the characteristics of existing benchmarks. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the weaknesses raised in the review and responding to the questions posed to the authors.
