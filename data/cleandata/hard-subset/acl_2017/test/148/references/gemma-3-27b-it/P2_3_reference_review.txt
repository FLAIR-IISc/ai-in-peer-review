## Review for ACL Submission: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the need for more nuanced evaluation of Reading Comprehension (RC) systems and datasets. The authors argue that current metrics (primarily accuracy) are insufficient, and propose a framework utilizing two classes of evaluation: "prerequisite skills" (the cognitive abilities needed to answer a question) and "readability" (the linguistic complexity of the text). They refine existing skill categorizations, annotate six RC datasets with these metrics, and present an analysis showing a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A curated and formalized framework for analyzing RC datasets:** The paper synthesizes concepts from psychology and NLP to propose a structured way to evaluate RC datasets beyond simple accuracy. The refined categorization of prerequisite skills, drawing on textual entailment and cognitive models of comprehension, is a valuable contribution.
2. **A publicly available annotated dataset:**  The annotation of six established RC datasets with the proposed metrics is a significant practical contribution. This will provide a resource for researchers to more deeply analyze dataset characteristics and build more targeted systems.
3. **Empirical evidence supporting the disconnect between readability and question difficulty:** The analysis confirms the authors’ hypothesis that readability alone is not a good indicator of RC task difficulty, highlighting the need for more holistic evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing RC evaluation methods and convincingly argues for a more comprehensive approach. The examples in the introduction effectively illustrate the point that readability doesn’t guarantee difficulty.
2. **Solid Theoretical Grounding:** The authors ground their work in relevant theories from psychology (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch’s C-I model) and NLP (textual entailment). This lends credibility to the proposed framework and provides a strong justification for the chosen metrics.
3. **Comprehensive Methodology:** The annotation process is well-defined, and the authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., inability to evaluate sentence selection competence). The inclusion of inter-annotator agreement is positive.
4. **Practical Value & Replicability:** The publicly available annotated datasets are a key strength, enabling other researchers to build upon this work. The detailed description of the methodology facilitates replication.
5. **Insightful Findings:** The empirical results, particularly the weak correlation between readability and difficulty, provide valuable insights into the challenges of RC and the need for more sophisticated dataset design.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** While the authors leverage existing readability metrics, these are fairly standard and may not capture all aspects of textual complexity relevant to RC.  More specialized or RC-focused readability measures could be explored.
2. **Annotation Dependence:** The quality of the analysis is inherently dependent on the quality of the annotations. While inter-annotator agreement is reported, a deeper dive into disagreement cases and potential biases would be beneficial. It's unclear how challenging the annotation task was and whether annotator expertise influenced the results.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper demonstrates a *correlation* between the metrics, but doesn’t establish *causation*.  It’s possible other factors not considered in this study are driving the observed trends. 
4. **Lack of System-Level Evaluation:** The paper focuses primarily on dataset analysis. While this is valuable, it would be strengthened by demonstrating how these metrics can be used to diagnose problems with existing RC *systems*.  For example, are systems consistently struggling with questions requiring specific prerequisite skills?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the challenges encountered during the annotation process? Were there specific skills that proved particularly difficult for annotators to agree on, and why?
2.  The paper mentions the methodology cannot evaluate sentence selection competence.  Do you envision a future extension of this work incorporating such an evaluation?  If so, how would you approach it?
3.  How do you see these metrics being integrated into the development cycle of an RC system?  Can you provide a concrete example of how these metrics could guide system improvements?
4.  You acknowledge the correlation doesn’t imply causation.  What are some potential confounding factors that could be influencing the observed relationships between prerequisite skills, readability, and question difficulty?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, though some sections (e.g., the detailed description of the readability metrics) could benefit from further streamlining.
*   The reference list is extensive and relevant.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of Reading Comprehension. The proposed framework, annotated datasets, and empirical findings provide valuable insights for both researchers designing RC datasets and those developing RC systems. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them, and I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and concerns outlined above.
