This paper proposes an analysis of reading comprehension datasets through the lens of “prerequisite skills” and “readability,” aiming to clarify the relationship between question difficulty and text complexity. The authors refine existing skill categorizations and annotate six datasets with these metrics, making the annotated datasets publicly available. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the annotation effort and resulting dataset characteristics; the refined skill categorization feels incremental. A secondary contribution is a demonstration – though a relatively weak one, as detailed below – that question difficulty and text readability are not strongly correlated in existing datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Annotation:** The creation and public release of annotated datasets are valuable resources for the RC community. Providing detailed breakdowns of the skills required to answer questions offers a more granular understanding of dataset characteristics than simple accuracy scores. This allows for more targeted dataset creation and system development.
2. **Focus on Dataset Quality:** The paper rightly highlights the importance of evaluating not just system performance, but also the quality of the datasets themselves. Identifying potential issues like “nonsense” questions (though the methodology struggles with this, see weaknesses) and weak correlations between readability and difficulty is a necessary step toward building more robust RC benchmarks.
3. **Connection to Cognitive Science:** Grounding the analysis in theories of human text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) provides a theoretical framework, even if the application feels somewhat loose. Linking NLP tasks to cognitive models is generally a positive direction.
4. **Comprehensive Metric Suite:** The adoption of both prerequisite skills *and* readability metrics provides a more holistic view of dataset properties than focusing on either in isolation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Weak Correlation Findings:** The central claim – that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated – is not particularly surprising, and the evidence presented is relatively weak.  The correlations reported in Table 5 are often quite low, and the visualizations (Figures 3 & 4) are somewhat ambiguous. While *a* correlation isn’t found, the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate *why* this is the case, nor does it provide particularly actionable insights beyond “datasets are complex.”
2. **Methodological Limitations of Skill Annotation:** The annotation process, while described, lacks crucial details.  Inter-annotator agreement is reported, but not broken down *by skill*. Agreement on "bridging" might be vastly different than on “mathematical reasoning.” More importantly, the methodology relies on annotators identifying the *necessary* skills *after* knowing the question and answer. This doesn't assess a system's ability to *determine* which skills are relevant during the reading process.  It's a post-hoc analysis, not a simulation of how an RC system would approach the problem.
3. **Incremental Skill Refinement:** While the refinement of Sugawara et al.’s skills is presented as a contribution, many of the changes (e.g., merging “statistical and quantitative reasoning” into “mathematical reasoning”) seem minor and lack strong justification.  The grounding in entailment theory is not fully leveraged.
4. **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on descriptive statistics of the annotated datasets. There is little attempt to *use* these metrics to, for example, predict system performance or identify specific dataset weaknesses that could be addressed through targeted curation.
5. **"Nonsense" Question Handling:** The identification of "nonsense" questions is important, but the methodology seems to struggle with it, as acknowledged by the authors. This casts doubt on the overall reliability of the annotation process.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the inter-annotator agreement, specifically for each of the prerequisite skills?
2. How would you address the limitation that the annotation process assesses *required* skills after question/answer knowledge, rather than simulating a system’s skill *selection* during reading?  Would a different annotation paradigm be more informative?
3. Could you provide examples of questions from each dataset that exhibit particularly strong or weak correlations between readability and required skills?  Illustrative examples would help to clarify the observed patterns.
4. Beyond descriptive statistics, what specific analyses have you performed *using* these metrics to gain further insights into the datasets or to inform system development?
