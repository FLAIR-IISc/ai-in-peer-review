This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors propose utilizing two classes of evaluation metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – to analyze six existing RC datasets. They annotate these datasets with these metrics and present the resulting analysis, arguing that a weak correlation exists between question difficulty and text readability. The primary contributions are the adoption and refinement of these two evaluation metric classes, the annotation of six datasets, and the demonstration of a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Metric Framework:** The adoption of both prerequisite skills and readability metrics offers a more nuanced evaluation of RC datasets than relying solely on accuracy. This dual approach allows for a deeper understanding of *why* a system succeeds or fails, moving beyond a simple performance score. The refinement of prerequisite skills, grounding it in textual entailment and psychological models of comprehension, is a notable strength.
2. **Large-Scale Annotation Effort:** Annotating six existing datasets with the proposed metrics is a significant undertaking. This provides a valuable resource for the research community, enabling further investigation and comparison of datasets. Making the annotated data publicly available (as stated in the paper) is commendable and significantly increases the impact of the work.
3. **Insightful Analysis & Counterintuitive Findings:** The key finding – a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability – is provocative and challenges common assumptions about RC tasks. The observation that datasets like MCTest present difficult questions with easy-to-read text is particularly interesting and suggests that current dataset design might prioritize surface-level complexity over deeper cognitive demands. This finding warrants further research and could influence the development of more challenging and representative datasets.
4. **Clear Methodology and Justification:** The paper clearly outlines the annotation procedure and provides a rationale for the chosen metrics and their refinement. The connection to established theories in psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) strengthens the theoretical grounding of the work and provides a clear justification for the chosen approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Annotation Subjectivity & Reliability:** While the paper reports an inter-annotator agreement of 90.1%, the inherent subjectivity of identifying "prerequisite skills" remains a concern. The potential for annotator bias, even with guidelines, could influence the results. A more detailed discussion of the annotation process, including examples of disagreements and how they were resolved, would strengthen the confidence in the findings.
2. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** The chosen readability metrics, while comprehensive according to Vajjala and Meurers (2012), are primarily focused on surface-level linguistic features. They do not capture more nuanced aspects of text complexity, such as conceptual density or coherence. This limitation might contribute to the weak correlation observed with prerequisite skills.
3. **Methodological Limitation: Sentence Selection:** The methodology focuses on annotating the *sentences required to answer the question* but does not evaluate the system’s (or human’s) ability to *identify* those sentences. This is a crucial aspect of reading comprehension and a significant limitation of the study. The authors acknowledge this but the scope is still somewhat restricted.
4. **Correlation vs. Causation:** While the paper demonstrates a weak correlation, it doesn't establish a causal relationship between readability and difficulty. Other factors, such as question phrasing or the required depth of reasoning, could also play a significant role. The discussion section could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of these potential confounding factors.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the annotation guidelines and examples of how disagreements between annotators were resolved?
2. Were any attempts made to incorporate more sophisticated measures of text complexity beyond the linguistic features used in Vajjala and Meurers (2012)? If not, could you discuss the potential impact of this limitation on the results?
3. Given the acknowledged limitation of not evaluating sentence selection, do you envision a future extension of this work that would address this aspect of reading comprehension?
4. Could you elaborate on potential confounding factors that might explain the weak correlation between readability and difficulty, beyond those mentioned in the discussion?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a visual representation of the distribution of prerequisite skills across the different datasets.
*   Table 6 could be included in the main text instead of the appendix.
*   Consider a brief discussion on how these metrics could be used to automatically generate or augment RC datasets.
