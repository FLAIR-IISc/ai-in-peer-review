This paper explores the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC), proposing a framework for evaluating datasets based on “prerequisite skills” and “readability” metrics. The authors annotate six existing datasets with these metrics, aiming to provide a more nuanced understanding of RC challenges than simple accuracy scores allow.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the *attempt* to move beyond superficial evaluation of RC datasets. The paper highlights a critical, and often overlooked, point: that readability doesn’t necessarily equate to difficulty in reading *comprehension*. The authors’ effort to categorize and quantify the skills required to answer questions is a valuable step toward a more granular understanding of what constitutes a challenging RC task. The annotation of six datasets, while not massively scaled, provides a useful resource for the community. A secondary, though still notable, contribution is the refinement of prerequisite skills based on insights from textual entailment and psychology—a theoretically informed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Framing:** The core idea of dissecting RC difficulty into prerequisite skills and readability is genuinely novel. Most RC work focuses on model architecture or training data scale, not on a fundamental analysis of *what makes a question hard*. This framing opens up avenues for targeted dataset construction and curriculum learning strategies, as the authors themselves suggest.
2. **Theoretical Grounding:** Anchoring the skill categorization in established theories from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (textual entailment) adds credibility and provides a strong foundation for future work. It's refreshing to see a less purely empirical approach to a heavily empirically driven field.
3. **Resource Creation:** Providing the annotated datasets is a significant practical benefit. While the scale is limited to 100 questions per dataset, it offers a starting point for researchers to explore these metrics and validate their own approaches.
4. **Clear Identification of a Problem:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of existing RC datasets, particularly their reliance on superficial categories and the potential for ill-formed questions. This sets the stage for the proposed evaluation framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Empirical Validation:** The correlation analysis between prerequisite skills and readability is weak. While the authors acknowledge this, it undermines the central premise of the paper – that these two aspects are meaningfully related. More robust statistical analysis and potentially the inclusion of human performance data are needed to strengthen this claim.
2. **Annotation Subjectivity:** The inherent subjectivity in annotating “prerequisite skills” is not adequately addressed. The inter-annotator agreement is reasonable (90.1%), but the paper lacks details on how disagreements were resolved and whether there were systematic biases in the annotation process. This impacts the reliability of the dataset.
3. **Methodological Limitations:** The methodology *only* assesses understanding of contextual entailments, explicitly admitting it cannot evaluate the ability to find relevant sentences. This is a significant constraint and limits the scope of the evaluation. Focusing solely on entailment doesn't capture the full complexity of RC.
4. **Lack of System-Level Evaluation:** The paper focuses entirely on dataset analysis. It would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating how these metrics can be used to *improve* RC systems—e.g., by training on datasets stratified by skill level or by incorporating skill prediction into model training.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You state the annotation methodology could not evaluate sentence selection competence. Could you elaborate on potential methods to incorporate evaluation of this ability into the annotation process?
2.  The correlation analysis revealed weak correlations. Were any alternative statistical tests considered to investigate the relationship between prerequisite skills and readability (e.g., non-parametric tests given the potentially non-normal distribution of the data)?
3.  Can you provide more details on the annotation process, specifically how disagreements between annotators were resolved, and were there any specific annotation guidelines given to address potential subjectivity?
4.  Do you have any preliminary results on how these metrics might correlate with human performance on the same datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly structured.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   Table 6's title and description could be more clearly written. The terms "number" and "distance" are not immediately obvious in their meaning within the context.



Despite the methodological weaknesses and limited validation, I lean towards **acceptance**. The novelty of the framing, theoretical grounding, and potential for future research outweigh the current shortcomings. This paper tackles an important problem in the RC field and provides a valuable, albeit preliminary, framework for more nuanced evaluation. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses raised in the review during the rebuttal period.
