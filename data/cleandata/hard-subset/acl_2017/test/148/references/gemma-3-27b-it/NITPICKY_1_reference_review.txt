## Review

This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC), proposing two classes of evaluation metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – and applying them to six existing datasets. The authors aim to provide a more nuanced evaluation of RC datasets beyond simple accuracy, and to contribute to the development of better datasets and systems.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the attempt to systematically analyze existing RC datasets through a more granular lens than typically employed, incorporating linguistic features and cognitive skills. The annotation of six datasets, while not groundbreaking in itself, provides a valuable resource for the community. A secondary contribution lies in the refinement of the prerequisite skills framework, drawing upon insights from textual entailment and psychology. However, this refinement feels incremental rather than transformative. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Detailed Analysis & Resource Creation:** The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of six RC datasets based on the proposed metrics. The resulting annotated datasets are a useful resource for researchers interested in more fine-grained evaluation. The tables presenting the skill frequencies and readability metrics are clear and informative.
2. **Interdisciplinary Approach:**  The authors effectively integrate concepts from NLP (textual entailment, readability metrics) and psychology (human text comprehension models) to inform their methodology. This demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the underlying cognitive processes involved in RC.
3. **Highlighting Dataset Weaknesses:** The analysis effectively reveals inconsistencies and potential flaws within existing datasets, particularly regarding the correlation between readability and question difficulty, and the presence of "nonsense" questions. This is a valuable contribution toward improving dataset quality.
4. **Clear Methodology:** The annotation procedure is well-defined, although limited by its reliance on human annotators. The authors acknowledge the potential limitations of this approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Weak Correlation & Limited Insight:** Despite the detailed analysis, the core finding – a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty – isn't particularly surprising. The paper doesn’t offer strong *explanations* for this disconnect, beyond stating it exists. This weakens the overall impact of the research.  The discussion feels descriptive rather than analytical.
2. **Prerequisite Skill Refinement – Incremental Advance:** The reorganization of prerequisite skills, while sensible, feels like a relatively minor refinement. The justification for some categorizations remains somewhat weak (e.g., separating bridging and elaboration).  The claim of a “comprehensive” classification is debatable.
3. **Methodological Limitations – Sentence Selection:** The methodology *specifically excludes* evaluating the ability to identify relevant sentences within a document. This is a crucial aspect of reading comprehension and a significant limitation that severely restricts the scope of the analysis. The authors acknowledge this, but it fundamentally undermines the practical applicability of the evaluation metrics.  The stated focus on "contextual entailments" feels narrow in the broader context of RC.
4. **Lack of System-Level Evaluation:**  The paper focuses solely on dataset analysis. There's no discussion of how these metrics could be used to *improve* RC systems directly.  Applying the metrics to evaluate different system architectures would strengthen the work significantly.
5. **Formatting/Presentation Issues:**  Several minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper detract from its overall polish (e.g., inconsistent use of bolding, varying levels of detail in figure captions, and unnecessary abbreviations).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the limited correlation between readability and question difficulty, what specific factors *beyond* those captured by your metrics might explain why easy-to-read texts can pose challenging questions?
2.  How would you address the limitation of not evaluating sentence selection competence? Could the annotation process be modified to incorporate this aspect?
3.  Can you provide more concrete examples of how these metrics could be used to guide the development of new RC systems or to diagnose the weaknesses of existing ones?
4.  The paper repeatedly emphasizes the difficulty of defining “solvable” or “reasonable” questions.  How did you address disagreements among annotators on this point, and what impact did these disagreements have on your results?
5.  You mention the intention of making the analysis available. Is this available publicly now, and if so, where can it be accessed?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is quite extensive, but would benefit from formatting consistency.
*   Figures 3 and 4 are somewhat redundant. Consider streamlining the visualization.
*   The discussion of existing RC datasets in Section 2.1 feels rather cursory. A more critical assessment of their strengths and weaknesses would be beneficial.
*   The use of “*” and “†” symbols to indicate modifications/additions to the prerequisite skills is somewhat distracting. A clearer labeling scheme could be adopted.



**Overall Assessment:**

While this paper presents a well-intentioned and thorough analysis of RC datasets, its impact is limited by the weak correlations observed, the methodological constraints, and the lack of system-level evaluation. The annotated datasets are a valuable resource, but the paper doesn’t offer sufficiently novel insights to warrant strong acceptance. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version. The work has potential, but currently falls short of the standards for publication in ACL.
