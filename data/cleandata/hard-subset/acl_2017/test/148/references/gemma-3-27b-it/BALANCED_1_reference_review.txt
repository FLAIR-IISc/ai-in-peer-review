This paper investigates the relationship between the difficulty of answering questions and the readability of context in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors adopt two classes of evaluation metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – and apply them to six existing RC datasets, annotating them to analyze dataset quality and system performance. The core contribution lies in the annotation and subsequent analysis revealing a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, suggesting that challenging questions can be crafted from easily readable text. They also refine existing prerequisite skill categorization based on textual entailment and human text comprehension theories.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Valuable Dataset Analysis:** The annotation of six established RC datasets with both prerequisite skill and readability metrics is a significant contribution. This provides a richer understanding of these datasets than simply reporting accuracy scores. The resulting data is a valuable resource for the RC research community.
2. **Refined Prerequisite Skill Categorization:** The reorganization of prerequisite skills, drawing from textual entailment research and psychological models of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), is a strong point. This moves beyond a simplistic skill list toward a more theoretically grounded framework for analyzing question difficulty.
3. **Insightful Findings Regarding Correlation:** The finding of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a key insight. This challenges the assumption that more readable text automatically translates to easier questions, and highlights the importance of focusing on the cognitive skills required to answer questions.
4. **Publicly Available Annotation:** The commitment to making the annotation data publicly available enhances the impact of this work and facilitates further research by others. This reproducibility is highly commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of "Difficulty" Measurement:** The study focuses on *cognitive* difficulty, as assessed by prerequisite skills, but doesn’t fully account for other factors impacting difficulty like ambiguity, world knowledge requirements *not* captured by the defined skills, or stylistic complexities beyond readability metrics. The chosen metrics might not comprehensively capture all aspects of what makes a question challenging.
2. **Annotation Methodology Concerns:** The annotation process relies on annotators identifying sentences *needed* for answering. This inherently evaluates comprehension *after* sentence selection – a skill not directly related to the core goal of assessing question/text difficulty. The methodology, as acknowledged by the authors, doesn't evaluate the competence of selecting relevant sentences. This potentially biases the results towards assessing understanding of already-identified information rather than the initial search process.
3. **Readability Metric Limitations:** The reliance solely on linguistic features for readability (Vajjala & Meurers, 2012) feels somewhat limited. While these features are useful, they don’t capture higher-level aspects of text complexity like logical structure, argument strength, or conceptual density, all of which can influence comprehension difficulty.  The paper acknowledges this limitation.
4. **Lack of System-Level Evaluation:** While the dataset analysis is strong, the paper lacks a systematic evaluation of how these metrics correlate with the performance of existing RC systems. Showing, for example, that systems struggle more on questions requiring specific skills (as identified by the annotation) would strengthen the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the sentence selection annotation step, could you elaborate on the instructions given to the annotators to ensure consistency in identifying "necessary" sentences? Were any specific guidelines provided for ambiguous cases where multiple sentences might be relevant?
2.  The paper acknowledges that the methodology doesn't assess sentence selection competence. Have you considered alternative annotation approaches that would explicitly measure this ability, perhaps by asking annotators to *rank* sentences by relevance?
3.  Could you provide more examples of questions in the datasets that demonstrate the observed disconnect between readability and difficulty? This would help illustrate the key finding more concretely.
4. How might these metrics be used to *automatically* generate challenging RC questions from easy-to-read text, thus providing a method to augment existing datasets and evaluate system robustness?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-cited and relevant.
*   Table 6 (sentence distance) is interesting but could be further explored with statistical significance testing.
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of potential biases in the annotated datasets (e.g., topic distribution, writing style).
*  Consider including a brief discussion of future work incorporating these metrics into automatic dataset generation or adversarial training techniques.



Overall, this is a solid and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of reading comprehension. While the methodology has some limitations, the findings are compelling and the publicly available annotation data will be a significant resource for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
