This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors adopt two classes of evaluation metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – to analyze six existing RC datasets and make the annotated data publicly available. They identify a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability, suggesting that challenging questions can be crafted even from easily readable text.

The primary contribution of this work is the comprehensive annotation of six RC datasets using a refined set of prerequisite skills and readability metrics. This annotated data provides a valuable resource for researchers seeking to understand the characteristics of existing datasets and to develop more targeted training materials and evaluation protocols. The secondary contribution is the reorganization of prerequisite skills based on textual entailment and human text comprehension, providing a more theoretically grounded framework than previous work. Finally, the analysis demonstrating a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a noteworthy observation, highlighting the need for more nuanced evaluation of RC systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Annotated Resource:** The creation and public release of the annotated datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) is a significant contribution. This allows other researchers to leverage this work for dataset analysis, model training, and evaluation without the costly and time-consuming effort of annotation.
2. **Refined Prerequisite Skills Framework:**  The authors’ revision of the prerequisite skills classification, informed by research in textual entailment and psychology, offers a more principled and robust framework than prior approaches.  Connecting the skills to established theories (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) provides a stronger foundation for understanding the cognitive demands of RC.
3. **Insightful Correlation Analysis:** The finding of a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is important. This challenges the simplistic notion that “harder to read” automatically equates to “harder to answer” and underscores the complexity of RC. This insight motivates further research into the specific skills and knowledge required for effective RC.
4. **Thorough Dataset Analysis:** The paper offers a comprehensive comparative analysis of the six datasets, utilizing both prerequisite skills and readability metrics, providing valuable insights into their respective strengths and weaknesses. The detailed tables summarizing the results are clearly presented and facilitate comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** The reliance on a specific set of readability metrics (Vajjala & Meurers, 2012) may not fully capture the nuances of text complexity relevant to RC.  While these metrics are a good starting point, exploring a broader range of linguistic features could provide a more comprehensive assessment of readability.
2. **Annotation Methodology Concerns:** The annotation process, while described, lacks detail regarding inter-annotator agreement *beyond* the reported 90.1%. A more granular breakdown of agreement across specific skills would strengthen confidence in the reliability of the annotations.  Furthermore, the potential for annotator bias (e.g., interpreting “prerequisite skills” differently) isn't sufficiently addressed.
3. **Lack of System-Level Evaluation:** The paper focuses solely on *dataset* analysis, without demonstrating how these metrics could be used to *improve* RC system performance.  A pilot study applying these metrics to guide system development or evaluation would significantly strengthen the practical impact of this work.  The suggestion in the discussion section is insufficient.
4. **Distance Metric’s Interpretation:** The explanation of the “distance” metric for sentence selection is somewhat unclear. It's not immediately obvious how this metric provides meaningful insight beyond simply counting the number of sentences required. A stronger justification for its inclusion and a more detailed analysis of its correlation with other metrics would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the inter-annotator agreement, specifically showing agreement rates for individual prerequisite skills?
2.  Did you consider incorporating other readability metrics, such as those focusing on discourse structure or semantic complexity, beyond the set proposed by Vajjala & Meurers (2012)? If not, why?
3.  Do you have any plans to explore how these metrics can be used to dynamically adjust the difficulty of RC tasks during training or evaluation?
4.  The "nonsense" classification appears highly subjective.  Could you provide more specific guidelines used by annotators to identify nonsensical questions?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are formatted correctly and comprehensive.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The connection between the annotation methodology and the goal of evaluating “understanding of contextual entailments” could be further clarified.
