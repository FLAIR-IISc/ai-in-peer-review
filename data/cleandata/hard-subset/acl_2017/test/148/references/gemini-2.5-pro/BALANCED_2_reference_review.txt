**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces a dual-axis evaluation framework for Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets, aiming to separately analyze the difficulty of understanding the text (readability) and the difficulty of reasoning to find the answer (prerequisite skills). The authors refine an existing skill taxonomy by grounding it in psychology and textual entailment literature, and use it alongside standard readability metrics to annotate and analyze six prominent RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA). The central finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the number of skills required to answer a question about it. The resulting annotations are made publicly available.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **A comprehensive, large-scale qualitative analysis of RC datasets:** The core contribution is the manual annotation and systematic analysis of six diverse datasets. This provides the community with a valuable resource and a clear, comparative characterization of these popular benchmarks, moving beyond superficial statistics like question type frequencies.
2.  **Empirical validation of a key hypothesis:** The work provides strong empirical evidence for the claim that question difficulty (proxied by the number of required skills) is only weakly correlated with text readability. This is an important and non-obvious insight for dataset creators and system developers, challenging the simple assumption that harder texts lead to harder tasks.
3.  **A refined and well-grounded skill taxonomy:** The paper presents a thoughtful revision of a previous skill taxonomy, particularly in the area of knowledge reasoning, by incorporating established concepts from psychology (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration). This provides a more robust and theoretically-sound framework for future qualitative analysis of RC tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and clearly articulated problem:** The paper opens with a compelling example (SQuAD vs. MCTest) that perfectly illustrates the central problem: the disconnect between text readability and question complexity. The motivation to develop a more nuanced methodology for understanding and evaluating RC datasets is strong, timely, and well-supported throughout the paper.
2.  **Solid theoretical grounding:** The proposed framework is not ad-hoc. The "prerequisite skills" are grounded in prior NLP work and are thoughtfully refined using established theories from psychology (Kintsch's Construction-Integration model, McNamara & Magliano's work on comprehension) and textual entailment research. This theoretical foundation lends significant credibility and depth to the proposed taxonomy.
3.  **Thorough and insightful empirical analysis:** The authors analyze six diverse datasets, providing a broad and valuable view of the RC landscape. The analysis is multi-faceted, examining skill frequencies, skill counts, readability, and the correlation between them. The per-dataset summaries in Section 5 are particularly insightful and useful for researchers choosing a dataset for a specific purpose. The public release of the detailed annotations is a commendable contribution to the community.
4.  **Constructive and forward-looking discussion:** The paper concludes with a strong discussion section that goes beyond simply summarizing results. It offers concrete suggestions on how the framework can guide system development (e.g., via curriculum learning), raises important considerations for future dataset design (corpus genre, global coherence questions), and honestly acknowledges the limitations of the annotation methodology (e.g., sentence selection, "no answer" questions).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The assumption that "# skills = difficulty" is a significant simplification:** The paper's primary proxy for question difficulty is the raw count of required prerequisite skills. While a reasonable first-order approximation, this assumes all skills are equally difficult for a model to perform. Intuitively, `Mathematical reasoning` or `Elaboration` (requiring external knowledge) might be far more challenging for current systems than `Punctuation` or `Causal relation` (often signaled by explicit markers). The analysis could be strengthened by acknowledging this limitation more explicitly or perhaps discussing how one might weigh different skills in a difficulty score.
2.  **Limited detail on the annotation process and disagreements:** The paper reports a high overall inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), which is encouraging. However, a breakdown of this agreement by skill category would be highly valuable. It is likely that agreement was much higher for objective skills like `Punctuation` than for more subjective ones like `Elaboration` or `Bridging`. Providing this breakdown would give readers a better sense of the robustness of each skill's definition and would be crucial for other researchers seeking to apply or extend this annotation scheme.
3.  **Potential for confounding variables in the correlation analysis:** The analysis in Section 5 convincingly shows a weak correlation between skill count and readability when data from all six datasets are aggregated. However, this may mask more nuanced trends. The datasets vary widely in genre (narratives, news, web documents) and construction methodology. It is possible that a stronger correlation exists *within* a specific genre (e.g., for news articles) but is washed out when combined with others. A more stratified analysis, controlling for genre or source, could provide additional insights.
4.  **The analysis of sentence selection feels disconnected:** The analysis of the number and distance of required sentences in Section 6 is an interesting addition that addresses a key limitation of the primary annotation scheme. However, it feels somewhat ancillary to the main analysis. This contribution could have been more integrated. For instance, does a higher sentence distance correlate with the requirement of specific skills like `Coreference resolution` or `Spatiotemporal relation`? Connecting the sentence-level analysis back to the skill-level analysis would create a more unified picture of task complexity.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your primary metric for question difficulty is the number of required skills. Could you comment on the validity of treating all skills as equally contributing to difficulty? Did you consider any weighting scheme, perhaps based on annotator uncertainty or preliminary model performance, to create a more nuanced difficulty score?
2.  Could you provide a breakdown of the inter-annotator agreement by skill? This information would be very helpful for understanding which of the defined skills are most robust and which may require further refinement.
3.  The analysis of sentence distance in Section 6 is insightful. Did you investigate whether the need to integrate information across distant sentences correlates with the requirement of specific skills (e.g., a higher distance making coreference or temporal reasoning more likely to be necessary)?
