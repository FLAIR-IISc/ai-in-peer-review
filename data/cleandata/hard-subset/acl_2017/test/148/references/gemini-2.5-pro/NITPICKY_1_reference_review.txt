### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the difficulty of answering questions, quantified by a taxonomy of "prerequisite skills," and the difficulty of reading the text, measured by standard readability metrics. The authors refine a pre-existing skill taxonomy, ground it in psychology and textual entailment literature, and use it to annotate 100 question-answer pairs from each of six prominent RC datasets. Their primary finding is that text readability and the number of required skills (a proxy for question difficulty) are only weakly correlated. They suggest this framework can guide the development of RC systems in a curriculum learning-style approach.

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:
1.  The manual annotation of six RC datasets with a detailed skill taxonomy, providing a valuable resource for analyzing the challenges posed by each dataset. The empirical analysis comparing these datasets is the paper's strongest contribution.
2.  The refinement and formalization of a skill taxonomy for RC, drawing justification from established concepts in psychology and textual entailment.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper addresses an important and often overlooked distinction between the complexity of a text (readability) and the complexity of the reasoning required to answer a question about it. The introductory examples from SQuAD and MCTest effectively illustrate this dichotomy and motivate the need for a more nuanced evaluation of RC datasets.

2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Study:** The analysis spans six diverse and widely-used RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA). This breadth lends significant weight to the findings and allows for a valuable comparative analysis of the challenges inherent in each. The resulting annotations, which the authors intend to release, represent a useful resource for the community.

3.  **Grounded Theoretical Framework:** The proposed two-axis evaluation is well-grounded in prior work. The prerequisite skills are an extension of previous work by the same authors but are more rigorously justified by connecting them to theories of human comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and entailment phenomena (Dagan et al.). Similarly, the readability metrics are adopted from established NLP research.

### Weaknesses

Despite the merits of the core idea, the paper is marred by a significant number of methodological ambiguities, analytical inconsistencies, and presentational flaws that undermine the credibility of the results.

1.  **Critical Lack of Methodological Detail and Rigor:** The annotation process, which is the foundation of the entire empirical contribution, is insufficiently described and justified.
    *   **Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA):** The paper reports an IAA of "90.1%" but fails to specify the metric used. Simple percentage agreement is a notoriously poor metric as it does not account for chance agreement. For a multi-label task like this, a chance-corrected metric such as Fleiss' Kappa or Krippendorff's Alpha is required. Without this information, the high agreement score is difficult to interpret and may be misleading.
    *   **Subjectivity of Skills:** The distinction between certain skills remains fuzzy. For instance, the line between "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge) is highly subjective. A clear decision rubric or discussion of how annotators handled ambiguous cases is missing.
    *   **Sentence Distance Calculation:** The definition for calculating the distance between required sentences (Appendix B) is ambiguous. For more than two sentences, the distance is the "sum of distances of any two sentences." This is not a well-defined operation. Does this mean the distance between the first and last required sentence? The sum of all pairwise distances? This ambiguity makes the results reported in Table 6 unreliable.

2.  **Inconsistencies and Superficiality in Analysis:** The analysis contains at least one major numerical error and generally lacks depth.
    *   **Incorrect Average Skill Calculation:** In Section 5(ii), the text states that for QA4MRE, "on average, each question required 4.6 skills." However, a direct calculation from the frequencies presented in Table 3 yields an average of 3.25 skills ((1\*13 + 2\*13 + 3\*20 + ... + 10\*3)/100 = 3.25). This is a substantial error that calls into question the care with which the entire analysis was conducted.
    *   **Oversimplified Correlation Analysis:** The paper introduces ten different readability metrics in Figure 2 but the main visual analysis (Figures 3 and 4) relies solely on the Flesch-Kincaid grade level as a single proxy for readability. While Pearson coefficients are provided for all metrics in Table 5, there is no discussion of why some features (e.g., ModVar) correlate more strongly than others, or whether different types of skills correlate differently with readability. The analysis stops at the high-level observation of a weak correlation.

3.  **Poor Formatting and Presentation:** The paper exhibits a lack of care in its preparation, most notably in the reference section.
    *   **Bibliography Errors:** The reference section is replete with formatting errors, including stray characters (e.g., "Aluisio et al\.,? 2010"), unconverted BibTeX macros (e.g., "\Q2016\E"), and an incomplete, malformed entry for NewsQA at the very end. Such sloppiness is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference and reflects poorly on the overall quality of the work.
    *   **Vague Statements:** Footnotes and text contain weak phrasing like "we will make available the analysis... as much as possible" (Footnote 3), which does not inspire confidence. The description of why some datasets were excluded is also somewhat cursory.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Please specify the exact inter-annotator agreement metric used to obtain the 90.1% figure. If it was simple agreement, please also report a chance-corrected coefficient like Fleiss' Kappa.
2.  Please address the discrepancy regarding the average number of skills for QA4MRE, which is stated as 4.6 in the text but calculates to 3.25 from Table 3. Which value is correct, and how did this error occur?
3.  Could you provide a precise, unambiguous formula for the sentence distance calculation described in Appendix B?
4.  Given the potential for subjectivity, could you elaborate on the guidelines provided to annotators to distinguish between "Bridging" and "Elaboration"? Please provide an example of a difficult case and explain how it was resolved.
