### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes and applies a two-dimensional framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on "prerequisite skills" (the complexity of reasoning required to answer a question) and "readability" (the linguistic complexity of the context text). The authors refine a pre-existing typology of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding the classification in theories from textual entailment and cognitive psychology. They manually annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these skills. In parallel, they compute a suite of standard readability metrics for the sentences required to answer each question. The central finding of their analysis is that there is only a weak correlation between the number of required skills and the readability of the text. This suggests that these are distinct axes of difficulty. The work provides a detailed comparative analysis of the six datasets and the authors make their annotations publicly available as a resource for the community.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A comprehensive, multi-dataset empirical analysis that demonstrates the decoupling of question complexity and text readability in RC tasks.** The primary contribution is the application of a systematic framework across six different datasets, leading to the key insight that difficult-to-read text does not imply difficult-to-answer questions, and vice-versa. The paper provides concrete evidence (e.g., SQuAD has high readability but low skill requirements, while MCTest is the opposite), offering a valuable new perspective for understanding the challenges posed by existing benchmarks.
2.  **A refined and theoretically grounded typology of prerequisite skills for RC.** The work revises a previous skill classification, notably by restructuring knowledge reasoning into categories like "Bridging" and "Elaboration" based on established concepts from psychology and linguistics. This provides the community with a more structured and robust tool for fine-grained analysis of the inferential steps required by RC questions.
3.  **The public release of detailed annotations for six major RC datasets.** This is a valuable resource that can enable other researchers to conduct more nuanced error analyses of their models, diagnose specific skill-based weaknesses, and potentially guide the development of more challenging and balanced datasets.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Motivation and Clear Framing:** The paper addresses a well-recognized need in the RC community: moving beyond simple accuracy scores to a more nuanced understanding of task difficulty. The central hypothesis—that textual complexity and reasoning complexity are not strongly correlated—is clearly articulated and effectively illustrated with examples in the introduction. This provides a solid foundation for the subsequent investigation.
2.  **Methodologically Sound Annotation and Analysis:** The study is well-designed. The skill typology is grounded in prior literature, lending it credibility. The annotation process reports a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), which suggests the definitions are sufficiently clear and replicable. The analysis is systematic and thorough, presenting multiple views of the data: frequencies of individual skills (Table 2), distribution of the number of skills per question (Table 3), readability scores (Table 4), and finally, a direct analysis of the correlation between the two dimensions (Figures 3 & 4, Table 5). This structured approach provides strong empirical support for the paper's conclusions.
3.  **Significant and Actionable Insights:** The paper’s main finding has important implications for the field. It cautions against using text complexity as a proxy for task difficulty. The discussion section offers concrete suggestions on how these metrics can be used to guide future research, such as creating a curriculum learning path for models (from easy-read/easy-answer to easy-read/hard-answer tasks) or informing the design of better-balanced datasets. The qualitative characterizations of each dataset are a useful guide for practitioners.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Scale of Annotation:** The analysis is based on a sample of 100 questions per dataset. For large-scale datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO, which contain over 100,000 questions, this sample size may not be fully representative of the overall distribution of skills and readability. While the sampling appears to be random, conclusions about the entire datasets are extrapolations that could be affected by sampling bias. The generalizability of the findings from this sample to the full datasets remains a potential concern.
2.  **Potential Subjectivity in Skill Definitions:** While the reported inter-annotator agreement is high, the distinction between some skills remains inherently subjective. For example, the boundary between "Bridging" (inferences using grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences using general/commonsense knowledge) can be ambiguous. The line between rich lexical semantics and world knowledge is not always sharp, and different annotators might draw it in different places despite training. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of such ambiguous cases and the specific heuristics used to resolve them.
3.  **Preliminary Analysis of Supporting Sentence Selection:** The authors rightly acknowledge that their core annotation methodology bypasses the "search" component of RC (i.e., identifying the relevant sentences). The analysis of the number and distance of required sentences in Section 6 is a useful addition but feels underdeveloped and disconnected from the main skill-based analysis. The method for calculating "distance" for more than two sentences is ambiguously described in Appendix B ("the sum of distances of any two sentences"). A more integrated analysis exploring how the search difficulty relates to the required prerequisite skills would have provided a more complete picture of task difficulty.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" is crucial for your analysis of knowledge-based reasoning. Could you provide a challenging example from your annotation where this distinction was difficult to make, and explain the specific guidance or rule that led to the final decision? For instance, how would you classify an inference that relies on knowing that a "dagger" is a type of "weapon"?
2.  Given that your analysis is based on a 100-question sample for each dataset, how confident are you that the observed distributions and the weak correlation finding would generalize to the full datasets? Did you perform any post-hoc analysis to check if your sample's distribution of, for instance, question types (e.g., what, who, how) matches the distribution in the complete development set?
3.  The calculation for sentence distance in Appendix B is unclear for cases requiring more than two sentences. The text states it is "the sum of distances of any two sentences." Could you please clarify this? For three required sentences at indices {s1, s2, s3}, is the distance (s2 - s1) + (s3 - s2), or the maximum span (s3 - s1), or another calculation?
