### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for evaluating and analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The framework aims to disentangle the difficulty of reading the source text from the complexity of answering the question. It achieves this by proposing two sets of metrics: (1) a refined set of 13 "prerequisite skills" grounded in psychology and textual entailment research, which quantify the reasoning and comprehension steps needed to answer a question; and (2) a set of 10 standard readability metrics to measure the lexical and syntactic complexity of the text. The authors apply this framework by manually annotating 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets and performing a comparative analysis.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Principled Annotation Framework:** The key contribution is the development and application of a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills. Unlike simple question-type labels, this framework is grounded in established theories of human text comprehension and textual entailment, lending it significant theoretical weight and making it a more robust tool for analysis.
2.  **A Multi-Dataset Empirical Analysis:** The paper presents a valuable empirical study of six popular RC benchmarks (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA). This analysis provides the community with a detailed, qualitative characterization of what these datasets actually test, and the authors' commitment to releasing the annotations creates a valuable resource for future research.
3.  **A Key Empirical Finding:** The central finding—that the readability of a text has only a weak correlation with the difficulty of answering questions about it—is an important and non-obvious insight. This challenges a common implicit assumption in the field and provides a more nuanced understanding of dataset difficulty, with clear implications for both dataset creation and model development.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The methodology is well-motivated and stands on a solid theoretical foundation. By drawing from psychological models of comprehension (e.g., Kintsch's C-I model) and established NLP concepts (e.g., textual entailment phenomena), the authors create a "prerequisite skills" framework that is more principled and interpretable than many previous ad-hoc dataset analyses. This makes the resulting classification of datasets more credible and insightful.

2.  **Valuable and Illuminating Analysis:** The comparative analysis across six datasets yields genuinely useful insights. For example, the characterization of SQuAD as having high readability difficulty but low question difficulty (requiring an average of only 1.28 skills) versus MCTest having low readability difficulty but moderate question difficulty (1.56 skills) provides a clear, quantitative basis for understanding the different challenges these datasets pose. The breakdown in Table 2, showing MCTest's focus on coreference and causal relations (typical of narratives) versus QA4MRE's need for bridging and elaboration (typical of technical text), is extremely informative for researchers selecting benchmarks or diagnosing model failures.

3.  **Significant Practical Implications:** The paper's findings and proposed framework have clear utility for the RC community. The central conclusion encourages dataset creators to think beyond text complexity and focus on designing questions that require multi-step reasoning. The framework itself provides a concrete tool for this. Furthermore, the discussion of using these metrics to guide curriculum learning for RC models (e.g., easy-read/easy-answer → easy-read/hard-answer → hard-read/hard-answer) is a compelling and practical application of the work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Strong, Unvalidated Assumption about Difficulty:** The analysis hinges on the core assumption that "the more skills that are required to answer a question, the more difficult the question is." While intuitive, this is a significant simplification. It treats all skills as equally difficult, which is unlikely to be true; a question requiring complex `Logical reasoning` might be far harder for a model than one requiring `Coreference resolution` and `Schematic clause relation`. The paper does not provide any external validation for this assumption, for instance, by showing that the skill count correlates with the error rates of state-of-the-art systems or with human processing time. This makes the "difficulty" axis in Figures 3 and 4 a plausible but unproven proxy.

2.  **Potential Subjectivity in Annotation:** The distinction between some skill categories, particularly `Bridging` (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and `Elaboration` (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge), appears difficult to operationalize and prone to subjective judgment. While a high overall inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is reported, this single figure could mask lower agreement on these more ambiguous categories. Without a per-skill breakdown of agreement or a more detailed account of the annotation guidelines used to resolve such ambiguities, the reliability of these specific skill annotations is difficult to fully assess.

3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The analysis deliberately excludes a critical component of the RC task: locating the relevant information within a potentially long document. The methodology involves annotators first selecting the necessary sentences and then analyzing them. This sidesteps the "search" challenge, which varies dramatically across datasets (e.g., SQuAD answers are contiguous spans, while QA4MRE might require synthesizing information from distant sentences). As shown in Table 6, datasets like QA4MRE have a much larger average sentence distance, suggesting a higher search cost that is not captured by the prerequisite skill count. This omission means the paper's characterization of "difficulty" is incomplete.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the crucial distinction between `Bridging` and `Elaboration`, could you provide more detail on your annotation guidelines? How did annotators consistently differentiate between an inference based on "lexical knowledge" (e.g., hypernymy) and one based on "general knowledge"? Could you also provide a per-skill breakdown of the inter-annotator agreement to help clarify the reliability of the more subjective categories?

2.  The claim that the number of skills is a good proxy for question difficulty is central to your work. Have you considered validating this by correlating your skill counts for the annotated questions with empirical measures of difficulty, such as the accuracy of various published models on those specific questions? Such an analysis would significantly strengthen your central claim.

3.  You acknowledge that your methodology does not evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences. How do you believe incorporating this "search" aspect of difficulty would alter your findings? For instance, do you think the relatively low skill count for SQuAD is balanced by a high search difficulty (due to long articles), or is the search problem trivialized by the dataset's span-based nature, making it truly an "easy" dataset overall?
