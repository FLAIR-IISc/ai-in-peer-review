**Summary of the paper:**
This paper proposes a new framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the complexity of the questions (measured by the number of "prerequisite skills" required) and the difficulty of the source text (measured by standard "readability" metrics). The authors refine a previous taxonomy of prerequisite skills, grounding it in cognitive psychology and textual entailment research. They then manually annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets with this skill taxonomy and analyze the texts with readability metrics. The central finding is that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability. This suggests that datasets like SQuAD can have complex, high-grade-level text but require relatively few reasoning skills to answer questions, while datasets like MCTest can use simple, child-friendly text to pose questions that demand more complex reasoning. The authors argue this framework provides a more nuanced understanding of RC difficulty and can guide future dataset creation and system development.

**Contributions:**
1.  The primary contribution is the novel conceptual framework that decouples question difficulty from text readability. This provides a multi-dimensional and more insightful view of what makes an RC task challenging, moving the field beyond monolithic accuracy scores or superficial question-type categories.
2.  A valuable secondary contribution is the creation and public release of annotations for six major RC datasets. This resource enables researchers to perform more fine-grained analyses of both datasets and model performance.
3.  The paper also contributes a refined and theoretically-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, improving upon previous work by incorporating concepts from human text comprehension and textual entailment.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Clarity:** The core idea of evaluating RC along the two axes of prerequisite skills and readability is both simple and profound. It challenges the implicit assumption in the community that difficult text naturally leads to difficult questions. This reframing is a significant conceptual leap that opens up new avenues for research in dataset design, curriculum learning for models, and error analysis. The paper's argument that we should be able to create challenging tasks from simple text (as MCTest does) is a bold and important direction for the field.
2.  **Strong Grounding in Cognitive Science:** The authors don't invent their framework in a vacuum. By basing their metrics on established models of human text comprehension (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), they add significant credibility and theoretical weight to their proposed taxonomy. This connection to a related scientific field is a major strength and distinguishes this work from more ad-hoc dataset analyses.
3.  **Insightful and Impactful Analysis:** The analysis provides immediate, clear, and actionable insights into the landscape of existing RC datasets. The visualization in Figure 3, showing SQuAD as "hard-to-read, easy-to-answer" and MCTest as "easy-to-read, hard-to-answer," is extremely illuminating. These findings have direct implications for how researchers should select datasets for specific research goals and how they should interpret their models' performance. The work provides a much-needed critical lens through which to view the barrage of new RC datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Annotation Scale:** The analysis is based on a sample of 100 questions per dataset. While this is sufficient for a proof-of-concept and to reveal broad trends (following precedents like Chen et al., 2016), it is a small fraction of the full datasets. Conclusions about the overall characteristics of massive datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO based on this sample size should be considered indicative rather than definitive. However, given the novelty of the framework itself, this is an acceptable limitation for an initial study.
2.  **Inherent Subjectivity in Skill Taxonomy:** While the authors have made a commendable effort to ground their skill taxonomy, the definitions (e.g., the distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" based on whether knowledge is "grammatical/lexical" vs. "general/commonsense") remain somewhat subjective. This could pose challenges for reproducibility and consistent application by other research groups. The high inter-annotator agreement is encouraging, but the fuzziness of some category boundaries is a minor weakness.
3.  **The "Sentence Selection" Problem is Sidestepped:** The paper's methodology requires annotators to first select the sentences necessary to answer the question, and then annotate the skills required to reason over them. This explicitly excludes the difficulty of *finding* the relevant information in a long context, which is a major challenge in RC. The authors acknowledge this and provide a brief, post-hoc analysis in Table 6, but this feels disconnected from the main framework. The proposed two-dimensional view of difficulty is thus incomplete, as it misses this crucial "search" or "localization" dimension.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Your framework compellingly separates question complexity from text readability. However, you acknowledge that it does not account for the difficulty of *locating* the necessary information. Could you elaborate on how a third axis, perhaps related to the "sentence distance" you briefly analyze, could be integrated into your framework? Would this "localization difficulty" be independent of the other two axes?
2.  The refined skill taxonomy is a key part of your contribution. If another research group wanted to apply your framework to a new dataset, what are the most ambiguous distinctions they would face (e.g., Bridging vs. Elaboration), and what guidance would you provide to ensure high-quality, consistent annotation?
