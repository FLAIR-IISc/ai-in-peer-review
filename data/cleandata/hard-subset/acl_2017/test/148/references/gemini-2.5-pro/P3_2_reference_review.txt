**Summary & Contributions**
This paper proposes a framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the complexity of the reasoning required to answer a question ("prerequisite skills") and the linguistic complexity of the source text ("readability"). The authors refine a pre-existing taxonomy of prerequisite skills by grounding it in theories from psychology and textual entailment. They then manually annotate 100 question-answer pairs from each of six popular RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these skills. Their key finding is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and the number of prerequisite skills required, suggesting that these are two largely independent dimensions of difficulty. The analysis provides a comparative characterization of these datasets, highlighting their differing strengths and weaknesses.

From my perspective, the primary contributions are:
1.  **A large-scale, comparative analysis of six major RC datasets using a unified annotation framework.** This provides the community with valuable, fine-grained insights into the challenges posed by these benchmarks, moving beyond simple accuracy or superficial question-type categories. The finding that readability and question difficulty are decoupled is a particularly important takeaway.
2.  **A refined and well-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC.** By drawing on established concepts from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara) and textual entailment research, the authors develop a more robust and principled classification than prior work.
3.  **The release of the annotations as a public resource.** This will enable other researchers to conduct further analyses, diagnose model performance, and potentially guide the creation of more challenging and balanced datasets.

**Strengths**
1.  **Important and Timely Research Question:** The rapid proliferation of RC datasets and models has created an urgent need for better evaluation and diagnostic tools. This work directly addresses the crucial question of *what* these datasets are actually testing. By disentangling text complexity from reasoning complexity, the paper provides a much-needed analytical lens that can help guide future research toward more robust language understanding.
2.  **Strong Methodological Grounding:** The authors root their framework in established cognitive science and NLP literature. The choice to base the two evaluation classes on McNamara and Magliano's model of text comprehension and to refine the skill definitions using concepts like "bridging" vs. "elaboration" gives the taxonomy strong theoretical credibility. This is a significant improvement over ad-hoc categorization schemes.
3.  **Comprehensive Empirical Analysis:** The annotation effort across six diverse datasets is substantial and allows for insightful comparisons. The results are presented clearly (e.g., Figure 3 effectively visualizes the central claim), and the per-dataset summaries in Section 5 offer a concise and useful characterization of the current RC landscape (e.g., SQuAD being "difficult to read but easy to answer" vs. MCTest being "easy to read but difficult to answer").
4.  **Honest Appraisal of Limitations:** The authors are commendably transparent about the limitations of their methodology, particularly that it cannot evaluate the "search" or information-finding aspect of RC since annotators are tasked with identifying the necessary sentences first. The brief analysis of sentence distance in Section 6 is a thoughtful, if preliminary, attempt to address this.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Potential Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** The central analysis rests on the manual annotation of skills. While the reported inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is high, this is an overall figure. The distinction between certain skills, particularly `Bridging` (using "grammatical/lexical knowledge") and `Elaboration` (using "general/commonsense knowledge"), can be extremely fine-grained and subjective. The boundary between lexical knowledge (e.g., hypernymy) and world knowledge is notoriously blurry. A more detailed breakdown of agreement on these ambiguous categories would strengthen the paper's claims. Without it, one might worry that different annotator groups could produce different distributions.
2.  **Oversimplification of "Question Difficulty":** The paper uses the raw count of required skills as a proxy for the difficulty of answering a question. This assumes that all skills are equally difficult to master, which is a strong and likely incorrect assumption. For example, resolving a simple pronoun (`Coreference resolution`) is arguably an easier task for modern models than performing multi-step `Logical reasoning` or leveraging external `Elaboration`. A more nuanced model of difficulty, perhaps weighting skills differently, could lead to different conclusions about the datasets and the correlation with readability.
3.  **The "Entailment" Framing of RC:** By instructing annotators to first select the necessary sentences, the study frames the core RC challenge as one of local entailment rather than a broader process of search, aggregation, and inference over a full document. This methodological choice significantly impacts the interpretation of the results. For datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO, where a key challenge is locating the correct answer span within a potentially long context, this framework may systematically underestimate their difficulty. The analysis in Table 6 is a good start, but it doesn't fully capture the complexity of the search problem.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Regarding the potential subjectivity of skill definitions: Could you provide a confusion matrix or per-skill agreement scores, especially for `Bridging` vs. `Elaboration`? This would help clarify how consistently these fine-grained distinctions were applied.
2.  The paper's conclusion that SQuAD questions are "easy to answer" seems to hinge on the fact that they require few skills (average of 1.28) once the evidence sentence is located. However, state-of-the-art models before the advent of large pretrained models still struggled to achieve high performance. How do you reconcile your "easy-to-answer" characterization with the historical difficulty of the SQuAD task? Does this suggest that the "search" component, which your methodology excludes, is a primary driver of difficulty in that dataset?
3.  You propose using these metrics for curriculum learning. Given that your difficulty metric (number of skills) treats all skills equally, how would you design a curriculum? Would it be based purely on the number of skills, or would you introduce skills in a specific order (e.g., `Coreference` before `Elaboration`)?
