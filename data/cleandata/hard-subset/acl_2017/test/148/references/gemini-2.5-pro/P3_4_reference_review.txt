### Summary of the paper
This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets and systems, focusing on the distinct difficulties of "answering questions" and "reading text." The authors argue that current evaluation is superficial and that text readability does not necessarily correlate with question complexity. To address this, they propose a methodology based on (1) a refined set of 13 prerequisite skills required to answer questions, grounded in psychology and textual entailment research, and (2) a suite of standard readability metrics. They apply this framework to annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets. Their central empirical finding is that the number of required skills (a proxy for question difficulty) and text readability are only weakly correlated. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the characteristics of each dataset and makes the annotations publicly available as a resource for the community.

### Contributions
1.  **A large-scale, multi-dataset annotation and analysis that provides a novel, empirically-grounded characterization of the RC landscape.** The primary contribution is the meticulous annotation of six major datasets using a unified framework. This effort produces a valuable public resource and, more importantly, yields the significant insight that question difficulty and text readability are largely orthogonal dimensions. This finding challenges implicit assumptions in the field and provides a more nuanced way to compare datasets and measure progress.
2.  **A refined and theoretically-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC.** While building on prior work, the authors make a notable contribution by reorganizing the reasoning skills. They decompose "commonsense" into more precise categories like *Bridging* (lexical/grammatical inference), *Elaboration* (world knowledge inference), and *Ellipsis*, drawing clear motivation from well-established models of human comprehension and textual entailment. This provides a more robust and principled classification scheme for analyzing inference in RC.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses a critical and timely research question.** The field of RC has seen rapid progress in model performance, but there is a growing consensus that simple accuracy metrics are insufficient for understanding what these models are truly capable of. This paper tackles the crucial need for more fine-grained analysis, providing a concrete framework and data to move the conversation forward.
2.  **Strong conceptual grounding and clear motivation.** The decision to separate question difficulty from text difficulty is well-motivated by cognitive psychology research (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). This theoretical underpinning lends significant credibility to the proposed two-dimensional evaluation space, elevating it from an ad-hoc collection of metrics to a principled framework.
3.  **Thorough and insightful empirical analysis.** The authors apply their framework to a diverse set of six datasets, revealing distinct profiles for each. The visualization in Figure 3 is particularly effective, clearly illustrating that datasets occupy different regions of the readability-vs-difficulty space (e.g., SQuAD being difficult-to-read but easy-to-answer). These dataset-specific characterizations are valuable for researchers selecting benchmarks or diagnosing model failures.
4.  **Creates a valuable public resource.** The release of these detailed annotations across multiple datasets is a significant service to the community. This resource can be used for a wide range of future studies, including error analysis, curriculum learning for RC models, and the targeted creation of more challenging datasets.

### Weaknesses
1.  **The "number of skills = difficulty" assumption is an oversimplification.** The paper's core assumption is that question difficulty is proportional to the number of prerequisite skills required. While intuitive, this is a simplification. A question requiring a single, complex analogy might be significantly harder for a model than a question requiring three simple steps (e.g., two coreference resolutions and a causal link). The paper does not discuss or attempt to validate this crucial assumption, which could affect the interpretation of the results in Table 3 and Figure 3.
2.  **Potential for subjectivity in skill annotation, especially for inference types.** The distinction between *Bridging* (inference from lexical/grammatical knowledge) and *Elaboration* (inference from world knowledge) is a key refinement but is also inherently subjective. For instance, understanding that "the team won the pennant" implies they played baseball could be classified as either. While the high inter-annotator agreement is encouraging, the paper would be stronger if it provided more detailed annotation guidelines or discussed difficult cases to demonstrate the robustness of these distinctions.
3.  **The Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA) metric is insufficient.** The paper reports a raw agreement of 90.1%. While high, this metric can be misleading for multi-label annotation tasks where the "not present" label is dominant. A chance-corrected coefficient, such as Fleiss' Kappa, is standard practice and would provide a much more reliable estimate of the quality and reproducibility of the annotation scheme, especially for the less frequent but more interesting skills.
4.  **The analysis of sentence selection is underdeveloped.** The authors rightly note that their primary annotation does not evaluate the competence of *finding* the necessary sentences. The brief analysis added in Section 6 is a welcome addition but feels preliminary. The sentence distance metric is not clearly defined in the main text (Appendix B is brief) and does not seem to be normalized by document length, making comparisons across datasets with different document sizes difficult to interpret.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the exact method used to calculate the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement? Was it a simple label-wise accuracy across all skills and questions? Given the potential for class imbalance (many skills being absent for a given question), could you provide a chance-corrected agreement score like Fleiss' Kappa to give a clearer picture of the annotation reliability?
2.  The distinction between *Bridging* and *Elaboration* is a cornerstone of your refined skill set. Could you provide a few examples from your annotation that were challenging to categorize and explain the reasoning or specific guideline that led to the final decision?
3.  Regarding the sentence distance metric in Section 6 and Appendix B: for questions requiring more than two sentences, the calculation is described as "the sum of distances of any two sentences." This is ambiguous. Does it mean the sum of all pairwise distances, or the distance between the two most distant sentences? Furthermore, does this metric account for document length? A distance of 10 sentences has a very different meaning in a 20-sentence context versus a 200-sentence context.
