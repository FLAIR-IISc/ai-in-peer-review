**Summary & Contributions**

This paper introduces a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the complexity of the reasoning required to answer questions (measured by "prerequisite skills") and the difficulty of reading the source text (measured by "readability metrics"). The authors refine a pre-existing taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding it in cognitive psychology and textual entailment research. They use this framework to manually annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA). The primary empirical finding is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and the number of skills required to answer a question. This leads to a more nuanced characterization of datasets, such as SQuAD being "hard to read but easy to answer" and MCTest being "easy to read but hard to answer."

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Valuable Annotated Resource:** The most significant contribution is the creation and public release of fine-grained annotations for six prominent RC datasets. This resource enables researchers to perform detailed analyses of both datasets and model performance, moving beyond simple accuracy scores.
2.  **A Well-Motivated Evaluation Framework:** The paper provides a principled, two-dimensional framework for characterizing RC tasks. By explicitly separating text difficulty from question difficulty and grounding the "prerequisite skills" taxonomy in established cognitive science and NLP literature, the authors provide a more structured and insightful vocabulary for discussing RC challenges.
3.  **Actionable Empirical Insights into RC Datasets:** The analysis yields compelling and non-obvious characterizations of popular datasets. The finding that readability and question complexity are not strongly correlated is an important result that challenges simplistic assumptions and can guide future dataset creation and the application of curriculum learning strategies for model development.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The methodology is well-founded. Basing the two classes of metrics on McNamara and Magliano's model of text comprehension and refining the skill categories using concepts from Kintsch (psychology) and Dagan et al. (textual entailment) lends significant credibility to the framework. This is a marked improvement over ad-hoc categorization schemes.
2.  **High-Impact Analysis and Discussion:** The paper's core finding—the decoupling of readability and question complexity—is important and clearly demonstrated. The resulting characterizations of datasets are insightful (e.g., SQuAD's high readability score but low average skill count). Furthermore, the discussion in Section 6, which suggests using these metrics for curriculum learning and highlights the importance of corpus genre (narrative vs. expository), provides concrete guidance for future RC research.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The definitions of the 13 prerequisite skills are clearly laid out, and the analysis is presented through accessible tables and figures. The authors' commitment to releasing their annotations is a major strength that will benefit the community. The reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% suggests the annotation scheme is robust.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Simplifying Assumption of Difficulty:** The paper's primary measure for question difficulty is the *number* of required skills. This is a strong simplifying assumption. A question requiring a single, complex "Logical Reasoning" step may be significantly harder for current models than a question requiring three simpler skills like "Coreference Resolution," "Clause Relation," and "Punctuation." This assumption is stated but never validated, and it potentially flattens the nuanced difficulty landscape the paper aims to reveal.
2.  **Limited Scale of Annotation:** While annotating 600 questions is a significant effort, sampling only 100 questions from massive datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO raises concerns about representativeness. The paper does not provide an analysis of how the distribution of phenomena in their sample compares to the full dataset, making it difficult to assess how generalizable their specific quantitative findings (e.g., average skills per question) are.
3.  **Lack of Connection to Model Performance:** The paper's title and introduction motivate the work as a way to "clarify what a system achieves" and "analyze... the performance of systems." However, the entire analysis is focused on the datasets themselves. The paper would have been much stronger if it had included even a brief analysis of how existing models perform across these skill categories. For example, showing that a state-of-the-art model fails disproportionately on questions requiring "Elaboration" or "Bridging" would have powerfully demonstrated the utility of this framework for system analysis, not just dataset analysis.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the assumption that the number of skills equates to difficulty: Have you considered that different skills may have different intrinsic difficulties? Could you comment on the possibility of a weighted scheme for calculating question difficulty, and how one might learn or define such weights?
2.  Regarding the annotation scale: Can you provide any evidence that your 100-question samples are representative of the larger datasets, for instance by comparing the distribution of question types or answer lengths in your sample to those of the full development sets?
3.  The distinction between "Bridging" (lexical/grammatical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (general/commonsense knowledge) seems potentially ambiguous. Could you provide an example of a difficult case you encountered during annotation and explain the guideline that helped annotators make a consistent choice?
