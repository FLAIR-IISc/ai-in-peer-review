### **Review**

#### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on the distinction between the difficulty of answering questions and the difficulty of reading the context text. The authors propose two sets of metrics: (1) a refined taxonomy of 13 "prerequisite skills" needed to answer a question (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging, causal reasoning), which serves as a proxy for question difficulty; and (2) a set of established "readability" metrics, which captures text complexity. The core of the work is an extensive manual annotation effort, applying the prerequisite skills taxonomy to 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets. The analysis of these annotations reveals the distinct characteristics of each dataset and, most importantly, demonstrates that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty (number of skills) and text readability.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive annotation study and comparative analysis of six major RC datasets.** The paper provides a valuable, fine-grained characterization of what skills are required by datasets like SQuAD, MCTest, and MS MARCO. The public release of these annotations is a significant resource for the community, enabling more targeted model development and error analysis.
2.  **An insightful and practical framework for evaluating RC challenges.** By decoupling question complexity from text complexity, the paper provides a more nuanced lens for understanding RC datasets than is typically available. This framework helps explain phenomena like why a dataset with simple text (MCTest) can still pose a significant challenge, and it offers a clear methodology for designing or selecting datasets for specific research goals (e.g., curriculum learning).
3.  **A refined, psychologically-grounded skill taxonomy for RC.** Building on previous work, the authors present a more structured and theoretically-motivated classification of skills, particularly for knowledge reasoning (e.g., Bridging vs. Elaboration), drawing from literature in psychology and textual entailment. This provides a solid foundation for future work in RC skill analysis.

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Timely Problem:** The paper addresses a critical need in the field. As models achieve near-human performance on many leaderboards, it becomes crucial to understand *what* is actually being measured. The paper argues convincingly that superficial metrics like accuracy or simple question-type categorization are insufficient. The introductory example contrasting an "easy" SQuAD question in a difficult text with a "hard" MCTest question in a simple text perfectly illustrates the problem and motivates the proposed framework. This work encourages a move toward more qualitative and insightful evaluation.
2.  **Solid Empirical Methodology and Execution:** The annotation of 600 questions across six diverse datasets is a substantial undertaking. The reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% suggests that the proposed skill taxonomy, while complex, is reliable and can be consistently applied. The analysis is thorough, systematically comparing the datasets across multiple dimensions: frequency of individual skills, average number of required skills, readability scores, and their correlation. The resulting characterizations (e.g., SQuAD is "difficult to read, easy to answer"; QA4MRE is "difficult to read, difficult to answer") are clear and well-supported by the data presented.
3.  **Actionable Insights and Future Directions:** The paper's findings are not merely descriptive; they provide practical guidance. For researchers, the analysis can inform the choice of dataset for testing a specific capability (e.g., use MCTest for multi-sentence reasoning, use Who-did-What for ellipsis). For dataset creators, it offers a blueprint for how to construct datasets that target specific skills or occupy a different space in the readability/difficulty plane. The discussion on using this framework for curriculum learning is particularly compelling and points to a promising direction for future system development.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Simplification of "Question Difficulty":** The paper's primary metric for question difficulty is the raw count of required prerequisite skills. This assumes that all skills are equally difficult to implement or execute, which is a strong simplification. For instance, performing multi-step "Mathematical Reasoning" may be fundamentally harder for current models than resolving a simple pronominal "Coreference". A single complex "Logical Reasoning" step could be more challenging than three separate, simpler skills. This unweighted sum could potentially mask the true difficulty of certain questions and may weaken the correlation analysis.
2.  **Potential Subjectivity in Skill Definitions:** While the high inter-annotator agreement is reassuring, the conceptual boundaries between some skills, particularly the newly defined knowledge-based ones, could be clearer. The distinction between "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge) feels fuzzy. For example, resolving an inference based on hyponymy (e.g., "a beagle is a dog") could be seen as either lexical knowledge (from a resource like WordNet) or general world knowledge. Providing more examples of borderline cases in an appendix would strengthen the paper's claim of a robust and clear taxonomy.
3.  **Limited Scale and Representativeness of Annotation:** While annotating 600 questions is a significant effort, sampling 100 questions may not be sufficient to draw definitive conclusions about massive datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO. The authors' findings could be sensitive to sampling bias. For example, the conclusion that SQuAD questions are relatively easy to answer could be skewed if the random sample happened to under-represent the more complex, multi-hop questions that exist in the full dataset. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this limitation more directly or discussed the sampling strategy in more detail (e.g., whether it was stratified to cover different phenomena).
4.  **The "Search" Component of RC is Underexplored:** The authors correctly state that their methodology evaluates contextual entailment rather than the ability to locate relevant information. This is a major aspect of RC. The brief analysis of sentence distance in the discussion section is a welcome addition but feels cursory. Given that locating evidence is a key challenge, especially in long-document RC, this limitation should be highlighted more prominently as a key scope boundary of the current work.

#### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration," could you provide guidance on how you handled cases that lie in the gray area between lexical and commonsense knowledge? For instance, how would your framework classify the knowledge that "Paris" is the capital of "France"? Is this general knowledge (Elaboration) or a fact that can be found in a structured lexical resource (closer to Bridging)?
2.  Your definition of question difficulty relies on a simple count of skills. Did you consider or experiment with a weighted scheme, where some skills might contribute more to the overall difficulty? Do you believe such a scheme would significantly change your findings, particularly the weak correlation observed between difficulty and readability?
3.  Given that you sampled 100 questions from each dataset, how might your sampling strategy affect the generality of your conclusions? For instance, for MS MARCO, did you sample from questions with varying numbers of candidate passages, and for SQuAD, did you ensure a mix of questions from the beginning, middle, and end of articles? This information would help assess how representative your findings are.
