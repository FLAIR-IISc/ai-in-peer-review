### Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a new framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the complexity of reasoning required to answer questions (termed "prerequisite skills") and the linguistic complexity of the source text ("readability"). The authors refine a previous taxonomy of prerequisite skills, grounding it more firmly in theories of human comprehension from psychology and phenomena from textual entailment research. They then annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with this framework. The central empirical finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the number of skills required to answer questions about it.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A significant empirical analysis across six diverse RC datasets** that provides a novel, nuanced characterization of their respective challenges. The key finding—that readability and question difficulty are largely decoupled—is an important corrective to simplistic assumptions about dataset difficulty and has strong implications for future dataset design. The public release of these annotations is a valuable resource for the community.
2.  **A well-motivated and refined taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills** for RC. By synthesizing prior work in NLP with foundational theories from psychology (e.g., Kintsch's C-I model, bridging vs. elaboration), the authors create a more principled and comprehensive vocabulary for describing the reasoning challenges in RC tasks than previous ad-hoc categorizations.

### Strengths
1.  **Important and Counter-intuitive Main Finding:** The paper's central conclusion—that text readability does not strongly predict question difficulty—is well-supported by the evidence presented (Figure 3, Table 5) and is of high importance to the field. It compellingly shows that datasets like SQuAD, despite being sourced from complex Wikipedia text, can contain questions that are relatively simple in terms of reasoning, while datasets like MCTest, with simple children's stories, can pose more complex inferential challenges. This provides a crucial lens for both selecting datasets for model evaluation and for designing new, more challenging benchmarks.

2.  **Principled and Comprehensive Framework:** The choice to analyze datasets along the two axes of skills and readability is intuitive and powerful. The prerequisite skills taxonomy is a particular strength, moving beyond simple question-type labels. Grounding the categories, especially the crucial distinction between `Bridging` (lexical/grammatical inference) and `Elaboration` (world knowledge inference), in established psychological and linguistic theory lends the framework significant credibility and makes it more robust than prior attempts.

3.  **Broad and Diverse Dataset Analysis:** The selection of six datasets is excellent, covering a wide range of genres (technical documents, narratives, news, web snippets), creation methodologies (expert-authored, crowdsourced, automatically generated), and task formats. This breadth makes the paper's conclusions far more generalizable and impactful than if they had focused on only one or two datasets. The specific insights provided for each dataset (e.g., the high rate of `ellipsis` and `bridging` in Who-did-What due to its generation process) are very illuminating.

4.  **High Annotation Quality and Future Utility:** The reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% is very strong, indicating that the skill definitions are clear and the framework is reliable. The authors also provide thoughtful discussion on how this work can be used, for instance, in guiding curriculum learning for RC models, which makes the contribution practical and forward-looking.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Incomplete Model of Reading Comprehension:** The annotation methodology explicitly sidesteps a critical component of many RC tasks: locating the relevant information within a larger context. By asking annotators to first select the sentences needed to answer a question, the framework evaluates only the "comprehension-given-context" part of the task, not the "search" part. For datasets with long contexts like SQuAD, QA4MRE, or MS MARCO, identifying the correct evidence sentence(s) is a non-trivial part of the challenge. The brief analysis in Table 6 is a good start but feels like an afterthought; this limitation significantly narrows the scope of the "difficulty" being measured.

2.  **Oversimplification in Aggregating Skills:** The primary metric for question difficulty is the simple count of required skills. This implicitly assumes that all 13 skills are of equal difficulty, which is unlikely to be true. For instance, `Mathematical reasoning` or multi-step `Elaboration` is likely far more challenging for current models than identifying `Punctuation` cues or a simple `Causal relation` marked by "because". A more weighted or nuanced aggregation of skills could provide a more accurate picture of difficulty.

3.  **Potentially Limited Sample Size:** While the authors justify the sample of 100 questions per dataset by citing precedent (Chen et al., 2016), this number may be too small to draw robust statistical conclusions for large and heterogeneous datasets like SQuAD (100k+ questions) or MS MARCO (1M+ questions). While the trends observed are compelling, they might be influenced by sampling bias. A larger-scale annotation would further strengthen the paper's claims.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The distinction between `Bridging` and `Elaboration` is central to your revised taxonomy. However, the line between lexical knowledge (e.g., hypernymy) and world knowledge can be blurry. Could you provide a few challenging examples from your annotation process where this distinction was difficult to make, and explain the guidelines or principles you used to resolve them?

2.  Your primary difficulty metric is the unweighted sum of required skills. Did you consider or experiment with any weighting schemes? For example, one could weight skills based on how frequently models fail on questions requiring them, or based on annotator judgments of their intrinsic complexity. Do you have any intuition on which skills are "harder" than others?

3.  Given the limitation that your framework does not evaluate the "search" aspect of RC, how might you extend it to incorporate this dimension? Could metrics like the "sentence distance" you calculated, perhaps normalized by document length, be integrated into a more holistic difficulty score?
