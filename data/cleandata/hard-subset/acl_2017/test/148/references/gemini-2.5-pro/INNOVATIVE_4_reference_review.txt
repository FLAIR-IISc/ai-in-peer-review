### Summary of the Paper
This paper argues that the current evaluation of reading comprehension (RC) systems, which relies heavily on accuracy and superficial question categories, is insufficient for understanding model capabilities and dataset characteristics. To address this, the authors propose a novel, two-dimensional evaluation framework. The first dimension, "prerequisite skills," defines the cognitive and linguistic abilities required to answer a question by refining a previous taxonomy into 13 distinct skills. The second dimension, "readability," uses standard NLP metrics to measure the lexical and syntactic complexity of the source text. The authors apply this framework by manually annotating 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets. Their central finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the difficulty of reading a text (readability) and the difficulty of answering a question about it (number of required skills). This framework provides a new lens for analyzing datasets, revealing, for example, that SQuAD contains difficult-to-read texts but relatively simple questions, while MCTest contains easy-to-read texts but more complex questions.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel Conceptual Framework for RC Evaluation:** The primary contribution is the introduction of a new paradigm for analyzing and evaluating RC datasets and systems. By decomposing difficulty into two distinct axes—the skills needed to reason about the text ("prerequisite skills") and the complexity of the text itself ("readability")—the work offers a much more nuanced and insightful perspective than singular accuracy metrics. This is a bold and important conceptual shift for the field.
2.  **A Publicly Available Annotated Resource:** The paper provides annotations for six major RC datasets according to the proposed framework. This resource is a significant contribution in itself, enabling other researchers to verify the findings, conduct their own meta-analyses, and potentially use the annotations for developing more targeted models.
3.  **A Refined Taxonomy of Prerequisite Skills:** The authors revise and expand a previous classification of RC skills, grounding it more thoroughly in theories from cognitive psychology and research on textual entailment. This provides a more principled and robust foundation for this type of qualitative analysis.

### Strengths
1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** This paper presents a refreshingly novel and critically important idea. In a field often dominated by incremental gains on benchmark leaderboards, this work takes a necessary step back to question what we are actually measuring. The proposed framework has the potential to fundamentally change how we design, analyze, and compare RC datasets, moving the community toward a more principled, skill-based approach to model development. This is precisely the kind of foundational, thought-provoking work that drives a field forward.
2.  **Insightful and Counter-intuitive Findings:** The central finding—that readability and question complexity are weakly correlated—is a powerful result that challenges common assumptions. The concrete examples and analysis (e.g., contrasting SQuAD and MCTest) compellingly illustrate the utility of the framework. These insights help explain why certain models excel on some datasets but fail on others, and provide a clear path toward creating more balanced and challenging benchmarks in the future.
3.  **Practical Implications for Model and Dataset Development:** The authors' discussion of how this framework can be used to guide system development, for instance through curriculum learning, is highly compelling. The idea of training models progressively on datasets with different skill/readability profiles is a tangible and promising research direction enabled by this work. This makes the paper not just an analytical exercise, but a launchpad for future innovation.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Scale of Annotation:** The analysis relies on a sample of 100 questions per dataset. While this is sufficient to introduce the framework and demonstrate its potential, it may not be fully representative of the multi-thousand-question datasets being analyzed. Definitive claims about the overall composition of SQuAD or MS MARCO would require a more extensive annotation effort. However, for a paper introducing a novel concept, this scale is an acceptable starting point.
2.  **Inherent Subjectivity of the Skill Taxonomy:** While the authors have done a commendable job grounding their skill taxonomy in prior literature, any such classification will have fuzzy boundaries and elements of subjectivity. For example, the distinction between "Bridging" (lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (commonsense knowledge) can be ambiguous. While the high inter-annotator agreement is reassuring, the challenge of creating a fully objective and reproducible taxonomy for these complex cognitive phenomena remains.
3.  **Incomplete Capture of the "Search" Problem:** The authors correctly note that their methodology focuses on the reasoning required once the relevant sentences are identified, and does not directly measure the difficulty of *locating* those sentences within a larger context. The brief analysis of sentence distance is a nice addition, but this search and localization aspect is a major part of the RC challenge that is not fully integrated into the core framework.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The proposed framework is presented as a powerful tool for dataset *analysis*. Could you elaborate on its potential for guiding dataset *creation*? For instance, could this taxonomy be used in a crowdsourcing interface to explicitly ask workers to create questions that target specific, underrepresented skills (e.g., more "Logical reasoning" or "Analogy" questions)?
2.  The discussion notes the difficulty of annotating "none of the above" questions. How might the framework be extended to more explicitly handle phenomena like negation, contradiction, or questions that are unanswerable from the text? Would this require a new skill, or a different dimension of analysis altogether?
