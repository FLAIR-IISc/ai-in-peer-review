### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the complexity of the reasoning required to answer questions ("prerequisite skills") and the linguistic difficulty of the context text ("readability"). The authors refine a pre-existing taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding it in theories from cognitive psychology and textual entailment. They apply this framework by manually annotating 100 questions from six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) and analyzing them with 10 readability metrics. The central finding is that readability and question difficulty (measured by the number of required skills) are only weakly correlated. The work provides a detailed comparative analysis of the datasets, highlighting their unique characteristics.

The main contributions of this work, in order of significance, are:
1.  **A comprehensive, multi-dataset qualitative analysis:** The most valuable contribution is the annotation and comparative analysis of six varied RC datasets using a unified framework. This provides the community with crucial insights into the specific challenges posed by each benchmark (e.g., SQuAD has high readability demands but requires simpler reasoning, while MCTest is the opposite). The public release of these annotations is a significant resource.
2.  **An empirically-backed decoupling of readability and question difficulty:** The paper provides strong evidence for an important clarification: text from complex sources (like Wikipedia) does not automatically yield challenging comprehension tasks. This insight challenges common assumptions and can guide more deliberate dataset creation.
3.  **A refined and theoretically-grounded skill taxonomy for RC:** The authors improve upon previous work by creating a more principled vocabulary for discussing reasoning in RC. Grounding skills like "Bridging" and "Elaboration" in established cognitive and linguistic concepts makes the taxonomy more robust and interpretable.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Critical and Timely Problem:** As models approach saturation on many RC leaderboards, the community urgently needs better tools to understand what is actually being tested. This paper moves the evaluation beyond simple accuracy scores, providing a well-motivated methodology for fine-grained analysis of both datasets and, by extension, the systems trained on them.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The credibility of the "prerequisite skills" taxonomy is significantly enhanced by its connection to established theories in cognitive psychology (e.g., Kintsch's C-I model) and NLP (textual entailment). This foundation makes the framework more principled and less ad-hoc than similar efforts.
3.  **Broad and Insightful Empirical Analysis:** The study's strength lies in its application across six diverse datasets, which vary in source text (e.g., news, children's stories), question style, and creation methodology. This breadth allows for a rich comparative analysis and demonstrates the general applicability of the framework. The specific observations for each dataset (e.g., QA4MRE is difficult on both axes, MCTest is a good example of easy-to-read but hard-to-answer) are clear and valuable.
4.  **Actionable Implications for Future Research:** The paper concludes with concrete suggestions for how this framework can be used to guide future work, such as developing curricula for model training, intentionally designing datasets with specific properties (e.g., focusing on narrative phenomena), and creating more diverse question types.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Question Difficulty is Oversimplified:** The primary metric for question difficulty is the raw count of required skills. This assumes that all skills are equally difficult for a model to master, which is a strong and likely incorrect assumption. For example, a question requiring multi-hop `Coreference resolution` is likely very different in difficulty from one requiring `Elaboration` (commonsense knowledge). The analysis would be more convincing with a more nuanced model of difficulty that considers the intrinsic challenge of each skill.
2.  **Potential for Subjectivity and Limited Scale in Annotation:** The analysis relies on manual annotation of a relatively small sample (100 questions) from each dataset. While the reported inter-annotator agreement is high, the initial step of selecting "sentences that need to be read" is inherently subjective and could be a significant source of variation. An incorrect or incomplete selection of evidence sentences would fundamentally alter the subsequent skill annotation. Furthermore, 100 samples may not be fully representative of the diversity within massive datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO.
3.  **Analysis is of Datasets, Not Systems:** The paper's title and framing suggest the metrics are for evaluating "systems," but the study does not include any analysis of actual model performance. The work provides an excellent characterization of the datasets' intrinsic properties, but it stops short of demonstrating how these properties correlate with the successes and failures of existing RC models. Connecting the skill requirements to an error analysis of a state-of-the-art model would have substantially strengthened the claim that these metrics are useful for system analysis.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The metric for question difficulty—the number of required skills—treats all skills as equal. Did you consider that different skills might contribute differently to the overall difficulty? For example, is a question requiring `Elaboration` and `Coreference` truly comparable in difficulty to one requiring `Causal relation` and `Spatiotemporal relation`? Could you comment on the potential impact of this simplifying assumption on your conclusions?
2.  The annotation process begins with identifying the necessary evidence sentences. Could you elaborate on the specific instructions and training provided to annotators for this critical first step? How did you handle disagreements or ensure consistency, especially for questions where a broader understanding of the context is implicitly required?
3.  Your analysis identifies high rates of "nonsense" questions in certain datasets. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown or definition of what constitutes a "nonsense" question in your annotation? For example, does it only mean the answer is not in the text, or does it also cover ill-formed questions or questions that are unanswerable even with external world knowledge?
