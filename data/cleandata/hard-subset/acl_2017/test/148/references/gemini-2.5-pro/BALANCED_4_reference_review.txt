### **Review**

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets, aiming to decouple the difficulty of reading a text from the difficulty of answering a question about it. The authors propose a set of metrics based on (1) **Prerequisite Skills**, a refined taxonomy of 13 cognitive skills (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging inference) required to answer a question, and (2) **Readability**, a set of 10 linguistic features measuring textual complexity. The authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) with the prerequisite skills and analyze them alongside their readability scores. The primary finding is that there is only a weak correlation between a text's readability and the number of skills required to answer questions about it, suggesting that text complexity and question complexity are largely independent dimensions of difficulty in RC.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A large-scale, systematic analysis of six RC datasets using a principled, two-dimensional framework.** This work provides the community with a much-needed qualitative lens to understand and compare popular benchmarks beyond simple accuracy scores. The core finding—that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated—is a significant, empirically-grounded insight. The public release of the annotations is a valuable resource for future research.
2.  **A refined and well-grounded taxonomy of "prerequisite skills" for RC.** Building on prior work, the authors develop a more comprehensive classification of skills, drawing upon theories from cognitive psychology and textual entailment. This provides a robust and theoretically sound tool for analyzing the reasoning requirements of RC tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Important and Timely Research Direction:** The paper addresses a crucial question for the field: what makes an RC task truly difficult? By moving beyond surface-level metrics and deconstructing difficulty into textual complexity versus reasoning complexity, the work provides a more nuanced perspective on dataset design and model evaluation. The conclusion that easily readable texts can still pose difficult questions (e.g., MCTest) is an important finding that should inform the creation of future datasets.
2.  **Principled and Theoretically Grounded Framework:** The proposed evaluation framework is not ad-hoc; it is firmly rooted in established theories from cognitive psychology (e.g., the Construction-Integration model) and NLP (textual entailment). Grounding the "prerequisite skills" in concepts like bridging vs. elaboration inferences lends significant credibility to the taxonomy and the subsequent analysis. This principled approach is a major strength.
3.  **Thorough and Illuminating Empirical Analysis:** The application of the framework to six diverse datasets is comprehensive and insightful. The paper effectively uses tables and visualizations (e.g., Table 2, Figure 3) to reveal the distinct "fingerprints" of each dataset, such as SQuAD being difficult to read but relatively simple in its reasoning demands, or WDW requiring more inferential reasoning. This comparative analysis is highly valuable for researchers choosing datasets for specific research goals.
4.  **Constructive Implications for the Field:** The authors provide a thoughtful discussion on how this framework can guide future research. The suggestion to use these metrics to create a curriculum for training more robust RC models is compelling. Likewise, the call to focus on different text genres (narratives) and question types (testing global coherence) provides concrete and actionable directions for the community.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Potential Oversimplification of Question Difficulty:** The paper's primary metric for question difficulty is the raw count of required prerequisite skills. This assumes that all skills are equally difficult for a model to master, which is unlikely to be true. For instance, `Mathematical reasoning` might be intrinsically harder than resolving a simple `Punctuation` ambiguity. A question requiring a single complex skill (e.g., `Elaboration`) could be more challenging than one requiring three simpler skills (e.g., `Coreference`, `Clause relation`, `Punctuation`). This simplification could affect the conclusions, such as the claim that SQuAD is "easy to answer" simply because it requires a low number of skills on average.
2.  **Questions Regarding Annotation Reliability and Scalability:** While an inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% is reported, this figure seems exceptionally high for a complex multi-labeling task with 13 overlapping categories. The paper lacks crucial details on the agreement calculation (e.g., using a chance-corrected metric like Krippendorff's Alpha) and the annotation process itself (e.g., annotator training, resolving disagreements). Furthermore, the manual annotation process is labor-intensive, which limits the framework's scalability for analyzing entire datasets or for use in dynamic evaluation.
3.  **Analysis is Limited to Datasets, Not Systems:** The paper proposes "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension," but the analysis is confined to the datasets themselves and does not extend to evaluating any RC systems. A more compelling validation of the framework would involve demonstrating how it explains the performance and failure modes of existing models. For example, showing that a state-of-the-art model's accuracy drops significantly on questions annotated with a specific skill (e.g., `Bridging`) would provide direct evidence of the framework's utility for system analysis. Without this link, the connection to system evaluation remains speculative.
4.  **Small Sample Size for Annotation:** The analysis is based on a sample of 100 questions per dataset. While justified by citing prior work, this represents a very small fraction of large-scale datasets like SQuAD (~100k questions) and MS MARCO (~1M questions). It is possible that this small sample may not be fully representative of the entire dataset, potentially limiting the generalizability of the reported distributions and characteristics.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify how the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement was calculated? Was a chance-corrected metric such as Cohen's Kappa or Krippendorff's Alpha used? Given the complexity of the 13-skill annotation scheme, providing more detail on the methodology would strengthen confidence in the reliability of the annotations.
2.  Your central proxy for question difficulty is the number of required skills. Have you considered weighting skills by their perceived difficulty or analyzing the impact of specific skill combinations? How might this affect your characterization of datasets like SQuAD as "easy to answer"?
3.  While the paper focuses on characterizing datasets, have you conducted any preliminary analysis correlating your skill-based metrics with the performance of actual RC models? For instance, do models struggle more with questions that you have identified as requiring a higher number of skills, or are there particular skills that prove to be bottlenecks for current architectures? Such an analysis would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about its utility for system evaluation.
