### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the complexity of the reasoning required to answer questions, and the readability of the context text. The authors propose a taxonomy of 13 "prerequisite skills" (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging, elaboration) grounded in psychology and textual entailment research, and use 10 established linguistic features to measure text readability. They manually annotate 100 questions from each of six prominent RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these skills. The central finding of their analysis is that there is only a weak correlation between the number of required skills (question difficulty) and text readability, suggesting these are largely independent dimensions of RC task difficulty.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A large-scale, comparative analysis of six RC datasets using a unified, fine-grained annotation scheme.** This work provides a valuable, data-driven characterization of popular benchmarks, offering insights into their relative strengths and weaknesses (e.g., SQuAD has high readability but low reasoning complexity, while MCTest requires more complex reasoning from simpler texts). The public release of these annotations is a significant resource for the community.
2.  **A refined and theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for reading comprehension.** By drawing upon established theories in psychology (e.g., Kintsch's construction-integration model) and textual entailment, the authors develop a more principled and interpretable classification of reasoning phenomena than purely ad-hoc lists, particularly in their decomposition of "commonsense reasoning" into distinct skills like Bridging and Elaboration.
3.  **An empirical demonstration that text readability and question complexity are largely independent dimensions.** This is a key insight that challenges the implicit assumption that difficult texts lead to difficult questions. It provides a clear motivation for the principled design of future datasets that can populate the full two-dimensional space of difficulty.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Problem and Clear Motivation:** The paper addresses the critical need for a more nuanced evaluation of RC systems and datasets beyond simple accuracy scores. It convincingly argues that understanding *what* a dataset tests is crucial for targeted model development and robust progress. The introductory examples (Figure 1) effectively illustrate how text readability and question difficulty can diverge, motivating the entire study.
2.  **Methodologically Sound Taxonomy:** The proposed taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills is a key strength. Instead of creating an arbitrary list, the authors ground their classification in prior work from both psychology (distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration inferences) and NLP (textual entailment phenomena). This theoretical foundation lends credibility and robustness to their framework, making it more than a simple annotation project. The revision and expansion of a previous skill set (Sugawara et al., 2017) represents a clear step forward.
3.  **Comprehensive Empirical Analysis and Resource:** The annotation and analysis of six diverse RC datasets is a substantial undertaking. The results presented (Tables 2-5, Figures 3-4) offer a clear comparative view of the landscape of RC benchmarks. The findings are insightful and allow researchers to better select datasets for specific research goals. By making the annotations publicly available, the authors provide a valuable resource that can enable further research into fine-grained model analysis and dataset construction.
4.  **Actionable Insights for the Field:** The discussion section provides thoughtful directions for how this work can be utilized. Suggestions for using the metrics to guide curriculum learning, to consider the impact of corpus genre (narrative vs. expository), and to inspire new question types that test global coherence are valuable and forward-looking. This elevates the paper from a purely descriptive analysis to a work that offers a concrete roadmap for future RC research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Subjectivity and Unclear Reliability of Annotation:** The paper's core analysis rests on manual annotation. While a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is reported, it is based on a small sample (62 questions) and the exact agreement metric used (e.g., simple agreement vs. a chance-corrected metric like Cohen's Kappa) is not specified. The distinction between certain skills, such as "Bridging" (lexical/grammatical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (general/commonsense knowledge), appears highly subjective and could be difficult for annotators to apply consistently. Without more detail on the agreement metric and the resolution process for disagreements, the robustness of the annotations is difficult to fully assess.
2.  **Oversimplification of Question Difficulty:** The paper operationalizes question difficulty as a simple count of the number of required skills. This is a significant simplification, as it implicitly assumes all skills are equally difficult for a machine to master. For instance, a system might find "Coreference Resolution" far easier than "Logical Reasoning". Furthermore, the difficulty may lie not in the number of skills, but in their complex interaction. The analysis would be stronger if it acknowledged this limitation or explored alternative conceptualizations of difficulty, such as weighting skills or analyzing skill combinations.
3.  **Exclusion of Evidence Retrieval from the Framework:** The authors explicitly state that their methodology does not evaluate the crucial competence of selecting the necessary sentences from a larger context. Their analysis is confined to a pre-selected set of sentences. While they provide a brief, separate analysis of sentence distance (Table 6), this aspect is not integrated into their primary framework. This makes the framework an evaluation of sentence-level entailment given the correct evidence, rather than an evaluation of the full end-to-end RC task, which is a major limitation for datasets with long contexts like SQuAD or MS MARCO.
4.  **Static Analysis of Datasets:** The evaluation framework provides a static analysis of the datasets' intrinsic properties. However, the difficulty of a skill is not absolute; it is relative to the current state-of-the-art models. A skill that is difficult today may be considered "solved" by future architectures. The paper provides the necessary foundation for analyzing model-specific weaknesses, but does not include any such analysis, which would have made the contribution more impactful by connecting the dataset properties to the behavior of actual systems.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the specific inter-annotator agreement metric used to arrive at the 90.1% figure? Was it a chance-corrected score like Fleiss' Kappa, or simple percentage agreement? Given the potential for subjectivity, what were the most common sources of disagreement between annotators (e.g., distinguishing Bridging from Elaboration), and what was the protocol for resolving them?
2.  Your primary metric for question difficulty is the number of required skills. Did you consider alternative metrics, such as weighting skills by a presumed difficulty or analyzing the impact of specific skill combinations? Does your annotation data lend itself to such an analysis?
3.  You acknowledge that your framework does not evaluate the evidence retrieval component of RC. Do you have thoughts on how your skill-based taxonomy could be extended to incorporate this crucial step, perhaps by adding skills related to information seeking or identifying relevant text spans within a long document?
