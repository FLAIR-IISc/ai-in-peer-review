### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a framework for the qualitative analysis of reading comprehension (RC) datasets, arguing that evaluation should move beyond simple accuracy to consider two distinct dimensions of difficulty: the complexity of reasoning required to answer a question ("prerequisite skills") and the linguistic complexity of the source text ("readability"). The authors refine a prior typology of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding it in research from psychology and textual entailment. They apply this framework by annotating 100 question-answer pairs from six prominent RC datasets and calculating ten standard readability metrics for the corresponding context sentences. Their primary finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the number of required skills and text readability, demonstrating that these are largely independent axes of difficulty.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive, multi-dataset qualitative analysis and annotation resource.** The core contribution is the systematic annotation of six diverse RC datasets using a unified, principled framework. This analysis provides valuable, concrete insights into the specific challenges posed by each dataset (e.g., the prevalence of multi-sentence reasoning in MCTest vs. the lexical complexity of SQuAD) and offers a useful resource for researchers selecting datasets or diagnosing model failures.
2.  **Empirical evidence for decoupling text readability from question complexity.** While intuitive, the idea that an easy-to-read text can pose a difficult reasoning challenge (and vice-versa) is an important one for the community to internalize. This paper provides clear, quantitative evidence for this phenomenon across multiple datasets, highlighting the limitations of current evaluation practices and motivating the need for more nuanced analysis.
3.  **A refined and theoretically-grounded typology of prerequisite skills for RC.** The authors improve upon previous work by reorganizing the skill set, particularly for knowledge-based inference (e.g., distinguishing Bridging vs. Elaboration), and explicitly connecting these categories to established concepts in cognitive psychology and textual entailment. This provides a more robust vocabulary for discussing RC challenges.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses an Important and Overlooked Problem:** The paper tackles a critical issue in NLP evaluation. As models achieve near-human performance on leaderboards, it becomes increasingly important to understand *what* they are learning and where their weaknesses lie. This work provides a methodology to move beyond monolithic accuracy scores and analyze the specific capabilities required by different datasets, which is crucial for building more robust and generalizable RC systems.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The proposed framework is well-motivated and not ad-hoc. The primary distinction between question complexity and text readability is rooted in influential models of human text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, 2009). The refinement of the prerequisite skills, especially the knowledge reasoning categories, draws from established work in psychology (Kintsch, 1993) and textual entailment (Dagan et al., 2013), lending significant credibility to the classification scheme.
3.  **Broad and Insightful Cross-Dataset Analysis:** The study's inclusion of six diverse datasets (spanning different genres, creation methodologies, and question styles) is a major strength. The comparative analysis (e.g., Figures 3 and 4) effectively illustrates the unique character of each dataset on the two proposed axes of difficulty. This "cartography" of the RC landscape is highly valuable for researchers, offering a clear guide to which dataset to use for testing specific abilities.
4.  **Value as a Public Resource:** The release of the annotations is a welcome contribution. This data can enable future meta-analyses, help other researchers diagnose their models' specific skill gaps without replicating the annotation effort, and potentially serve as training data for models that predict question difficulty or reasoning types.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The "Number of Skills" as a Proxy for Difficulty:** A central assumption of the analysis is that question difficulty is directly proportional to the number of required prerequisite skills. This is a significant simplification. Not all skills are equally difficult for current models to master (e.g., coreference resolution may be easier than commonsense elaboration), and certain combinations of skills might introduce emergent complexity. The analysis treats all skills as interchangeable, additive units of difficulty, which may not accurately reflect the true computational or cognitive challenge.
2.  **Limited Scale and Potential for Sampling Bias:** The analysis relies on only 100 questions per dataset. For massive datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO, this is a very small fraction of the data. While the authors cite precedent for this sample size, it raises concerns about statistical robustness and whether the sample is truly representative of the entire dataset. Conclusions drawn about the distribution of rare skills or fine-grained differences between datasets should be interpreted with caution.
3.  **Potential Subjectivity in Annotation:** While the overall inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is high, this single figure can mask significant disagreement on specific, more subjective categories. The distinction between "Bridging" (using grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (using general/commonsense knowledge) is particularly critical to the paper's claims about knowledge reasoning but is notoriously difficult to draw consistently. The paper would be stronger if it provided a per-skill breakdown of agreement and discussed challenging edge cases.
4.  **Lack of Direct Connection to System Performance:** The paper is motivated by the need to better evaluate systems, but the analysis is confined to the datasets themselves. While the discussion suggests how the framework *could* be used for system development (e.g., curriculum learning), the work would be far more impactful if it included even a small-scale analysis connecting model performance (e.g., for a standard baseline) to the required skills. For example, showing that a model's error rate is disproportionately high on questions requiring "Elaboration" would directly validate the utility of this framework for system analysis.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The assumption that difficulty scales linearly with the number of skills seems like a strong simplification. Have you considered alternative models of difficulty, for example by weighting skills based on their intrinsic complexity or analyzing if certain combinations of skills are disproportionately challenging for systems?
2.  The distinction between Bridging and Elaboration is central to your refined typology but can be subjective. Could you please provide the per-skill inter-annotator agreement rates? A discussion of specific examples where annotators disagreed on this distinction would be very helpful for understanding the nuances and limitations of the annotation process.
3.  Given the small sample size (100 questions) relative to the size of datasets like SQuAD, how confident are you that the observed skill distributions are representative of the datasets as a whole? Did you perform any analysis to check for sampling variability (e.g., by annotating a second, non-overlapping sample)?
