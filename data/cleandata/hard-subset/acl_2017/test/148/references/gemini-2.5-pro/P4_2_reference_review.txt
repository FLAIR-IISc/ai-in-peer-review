### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes and applies a two-part evaluation framework to analyze and compare six reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The framework aims to disentangle the difficulty of reading the context text from the difficulty of answering the question. The first part of the framework, "prerequisite skills," is a refined taxonomy of 13 skills (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging, elaboration) required to answer a question, where the number of required skills serves as a proxy for question difficulty. The second part, "readability," uses ten established linguistic features (e.g., sentence length, syntactic complexity) to measure the difficulty of reading the text. The authors manually annotated 100 questions from each of the six datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) with the prerequisite skills. The analysis reveals the distribution of skills in each dataset and shows only a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, providing a more nuanced understanding of the challenges posed by these popular benchmarks. The annotations are made publicly available.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Large-Scale Comparative Analysis of RC Datasets:** The primary contribution is the detailed, multi-faceted analysis of six prominent RC datasets using a unified, theoretically-grounded framework. This analysis provides the community with significant insights into the specific linguistic and reasoning challenges posed by each dataset, moving beyond simple accuracy leaderboards. The public release of the detailed skill annotations is a valuable resource that enables deeper error analysis and future meta-studies.
2.  **A Refined Taxonomy of Prerequisite Skills for RC:** The work introduces a refined and structured classification of 13 prerequisite skills needed for reading comprehension. This taxonomy builds upon prior work but improves it by grounding the categories for knowledge reasoning (e.g., Bridging, Elaboration) in established concepts from psychology and textual entailment research, providing a more robust tool for qualitative analysis.
3.  **Empirical Evidence for the Decoupling of Readability and Question Difficulty:** The paper provides concrete, quantitative evidence across multiple datasets that the difficulty of reading a text (readability) is not strongly correlated with the difficulty of the reasoning required to answer a question about it. This finding objectively validates a commonly held intuition and highlights the importance of considering both aspects when creating and evaluating RC systems and datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Contribution to Dataset Understanding and Characterization:** The paper provides a much-needed qualitative characterization of popular RC datasets. By analyzing skill distributions and readability, it offers a clear picture of what makes each dataset unique (e.g., SQuAD texts are difficult to read, but questions require fewer skills; MCTest texts are simple, but questions require non-trivial reasoning). This is highly valuable for researchers selecting datasets for specific phenomena, designing new models tailored to certain reasoning types, or aiming to create more balanced and challenging benchmarks in the future.
2.  **Sound and Well-Grounded Methodology:** The two-pronged evaluation framework is well-motivated, drawing on established theories from psychology (McNamara and Magliano, Kintsch) and NLP (Vajjala and Meurers). The refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy, particularly the more granular breakdown of knowledge-based reasoning, adds rigor to the analysis. The annotation process appears methodologically sound, and the high reported inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) suggests that the skill categories, while complex, are sufficiently well-defined for reliable application.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation and High Value as a Community Resource:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It effectively presents a large amount of analytical data through well-designed tables and figures, making the results easy to interpret. By making the skill annotations publicly available, the authors provide a tangible resource that can be immediately used by the community for a variety of purposes, including model error analysis, curriculum learning experiments, and targeted dataset augmentation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on a Simplified, Unweighted Model of Question Difficulty:** The core analysis assumes that question difficulty is directly proportional to the number of required prerequisite skills. This is a significant simplification, as it treats all skills as equally difficult for a model to perform and ignores the potentially complex interactions between them. For example, a question requiring only "Logical Reasoning" might be substantially more difficult for current systems than one requiring "Coreference Resolution," "Clause Relation," and "Punctuation" combined. This assumption could affect the validity of the correlation analysis and the resulting conclusions about dataset difficulty.
2.  **Subjectivity and Lack of Scalability in the Annotation Method:** The paper's analysis is fundamentally based on manual annotation. While inter-annotator agreement is high, the task of identifying which of the 13 skills are "required" remains inherently subjective, particularly for nuanced skills like "Bridging" versus "Elaboration." More critically, this manual approach does not scale. It serves as a tool for analyzing static datasets but cannot be used as a dynamic evaluation metric for new models or large-scale test sets, which limits its practical utility for ongoing system development. The paper does not propose a path toward automating this analysis.
3.  **Potentially Mismatched Readability Metrics for Machine Readers:** The readability metrics are drawn from literature on human second-language acquisition. While this is appropriate for testing the hypothesis about human-centric readability, these metrics may not accurately reflect text complexity for modern transformer-based RC models. For such models, lexical and shallow syntactic features (e.g., average syllables per word, sentence length) are likely less challenging than phenomena like long-range dependencies, syntactic ambiguity, or subtle discourse relations, which are not directly measured by the chosen metrics. This potential mismatch between human- and machine-perceived text difficulty could temper the paper's conclusions.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your measure of question difficulty is the unweighted count of required skills. Have you considered that different skills might pose different levels of challenge to current RC models? How might your correlation analysis and conclusions change if you were to apply weights to the skills based on their intrinsic difficulty (e.g., giving a higher weight to "Logical Reasoning" than to "Punctuation")?
2.  The manual annotation of prerequisite skills is a core component of your work but is not scalable for automatic evaluation. Do you foresee a path to automating the prediction of required skills for a given question-context pair? Could a model be trained on your annotations to create an automatic, multi-faceted evaluation tool?
3.  The readability metrics used are designed to measure difficulty for human readers. What types of text complexity metrics do you believe would be more effective for evaluating the difficulty of a passage from the perspective of a large pre-trained language model, and how might that change your analysis of the relationship between text and question difficulty?
