**Summary of the paper:**
This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on (1) the "prerequisite skills" needed to answer a question and (2) the "readability" of the source text. The authors refine an existing taxonomy of skills, drawing on theories from psychology and textual entailment. They apply this framework to annotate 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets: QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA. The analysis reveals characteristics of each dataset and demonstrates a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by the number of required skills) and text readability, suggesting these are largely independent dimensions of difficulty.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**
1.  The primary contribution is the creation and public release of a resource: annotations of six diverse RC datasets using a detailed skill taxonomy. This resource enables more nuanced, qualitative analysis of both datasets and the systems trained on them.
2.  A secondary contribution is the refined, principled taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills. By grounding the classification in established work from psychology and textual entailment, the authors provide a more structured framework than previous versions.
3.  The paper provides a valuable empirical analysis of the current RC dataset landscape, quantitatively confirming the intuition that text readability does not necessarily correlate with question complexity.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Valuable Community Resource:** The annotated datasets are a significant and tangible contribution. They provide a much-needed tool for researchers to move beyond simple accuracy metrics. This enables fine-grained error analysis, aids in the selection of appropriate datasets for targeted research (e.g., focusing on multi-sentence reasoning), and can inform the development of more robust models, perhaps through curriculum learning as the authors suggest.
2.  **Well-Motivated Framework:** The proposed two-axis evaluation (skills vs. readability) is intuitive and well-grounded in established psychological models of text comprehension (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). This principled approach provides a more holistic view of RC task difficulty than prior analyses that focus only on superficial question types. The effort to reorganize the skill taxonomy based on theories of inference and entailment is commendable.
3.  **Broad and Insightful Analysis:** The analysis spans six important and varied RC datasets, offering a clear comparative overview. The results in Tables 2-6 effectively highlight the unique profiles of each dataset (e.g., QA4MRE's overall complexity, SQuAD's high readability paired with low skill requirements, MCTest's narrative-driven reasoning). This cross-dataset comparison is extremely useful for understanding the biases and challenges present in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Ambiguity and Lack of Rigor in Annotation Methodology:** The validity of the paper's core contribution—the annotations—is undermined by a lack of methodological detail.
    *   The inter-annotator agreement is reported as 90.1% but without specifying the metric used. For a multi-label classification task, simple percentage agreement is insufficient and can be misleadingly high. A more appropriate metric like Fleiss' Kappa or a label-wise F1-score is necessary to properly assess reliability.
    *   The distinction between key skills is blurry. The line between `Bridging` (lexical/grammatical knowledge) and `Elaboration` (general/commonsense knowledge) is highly subjective and context-dependent. The paper offers no detailed annotation guidelines or illustrative examples to show how annotators consistently made this distinction, which is critical for the framework's credibility.
    *   There is a significant numerical discrepancy that calls the analysis into question. In Section 5(ii), the text states QA4MRE requires "on average, each question required 4.6 skills." However, Table 3 lists the average for QA4MRE as 3.25. This is a substantial contradiction that was not caught in proofreading.

2.  **Insufficient Justification for Analytical Choices:**
    *   While the authors compute ten different readability metrics (Figure 2), the core correlation analysis in Figures 3 and 4 relies solely on the Flesch–Kincaid grade level without justification. Table 5 shows that correlation coefficients vary across different metrics. Relying on a single, unexplained choice of metric may oversimplify or misrepresent the relationship between readability and skill requirements. The authors acknowledge this in a footnote ("we ideally have to consult various features") but do not address the limitation in their main analysis.
    *   The method for calculating "sentence distance" in Appendix B is ill-defined: "If a question required more than two sentences to be read, its distance was the sum of distances of any two sentences." This is ambiguous. Is it the sum of distances between all pairs of sentences? The maximum distance between any two? This lack of precision makes the results in Table 6 difficult to interpret or replicate.

3.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of minor but unprofessional errors, suggesting a lack of careful preparation.
    *   The reference section is exceptionally poorly formatted. Many entries contain extraneous LaTeX-like commands (`\.,?`, `\Q`, `\E`), inconsistent formatting, and duplicated author/year information (e.g., "Aluisio et al\.,? 2010, Aluisio et al\.,"). The final reference entry for NewsQA is completely broken.
    *   Superfluous footnotes are used (e.g., footnote 1).
    *   Awkward phrasing is present throughout (e.g., "we are working on attempting" in Section 2.2).
    *   The title of Section 3.1 refers to thirteen skills, but the text defines twelve skills plus four sub-categories for "commonsense reasoning", which can be jointly required. This is slightly confusing.
    While individually minor, the high frequency of such flaws detracts from the paper's professionalism and raises concerns about the level of care applied to the research itself.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Could you please specify the exact metric used to calculate the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement?
2.  Please resolve the discrepancy regarding the average number of skills for QA4MRE: is it 4.6 (as stated in the text) or 3.25 (as listed in Table 3)? Could you clarify how the average in Table 3 was calculated (e.g., was it normalized by the number of non-`nonsense` questions)?
3.  The distinction between `Bridging` and `Elaboration` seems difficult to operationalize. Could you provide 1-2 concrete examples from your annotation data that clearly illustrate how your annotators distinguished between these two skills?
4.  Could you provide a precise, unambiguous formula for the "sentence distance" calculation described in Appendix B?
