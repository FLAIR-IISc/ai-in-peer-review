### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets, aiming to move beyond simple accuracy metrics. The framework analyzes datasets along two axes: 1) the difficulty of answering questions, operationalized as the number of "prerequisite skills" required, and 2) the difficulty of reading the context text, measured by established readability metrics. The authors refine a pre-existing taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding it in cognitive psychology and textual entailment research. They manually annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) with these skills and analyze the readability of the corresponding evidence passages. The central finding is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, demonstrating that these are distinct challenges.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A valuable public resource and empirical analysis:** The authors provide a detailed, multi-dataset annotation of reasoning skills required for RC. The resulting analysis, which empirically demonstrates and quantifies the decoupling of text readability from question complexity, is a significant and insightful finding for the community. It provides concrete evidence for a widely held intuition (e.g., SQuAD texts are complex, but many questions are simple).
2.  **A principled, refined taxonomy of RC skills:** The paper presents a well-structured classification of 13 prerequisite skills. By grounding the revision of a prior taxonomy in established theories from cognitive psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (textual entailment), the authors provide a more robust and credible framework for qualitative analysis than previous ad-hoc attempts.
3.  **A systematic characterization of popular RC datasets:** The application of the framework to six datasets offers a clear comparative analysis, highlighting their unique characteristics (e.g., MCTest's narrative focus, QA4MRE's overall complexity, WDW's reliance on bridging inferences). This serves as a valuable guide for researchers selecting datasets for specific research goals.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Critical and Timely Problem:** At the time of this work's likely publication, the RC field was heavily focused on leaderboard performance on datasets like SQuAD. This paper provides a much-needed critical perspective, offering tools to dissect *why* a system succeeds or fails and to understand the intrinsic properties of the datasets themselves. It moves the conversation from "how accurate?" to "what can the model actually do?". The central finding—that readability and reasoning complexity are not strongly correlated—is fundamental to building more robust and generalizable RC systems.

2.  **Well-Grounded and Principled Methodology:** The authors do not simply invent a set of skills. Their decision to refine a previous taxonomy by drawing on influential models of human comprehension (e.g., the Construction-Integration model, bridging vs. elaboration inferences) and established phenomena in textual entailment research lends significant weight and credibility to their framework. This grounding makes the taxonomy more likely to be stable and interpretable.

3.  **Thorough and Illuminating Analysis:** The annotation effort across six diverse datasets is commendable and allows for a compelling comparative study. The visualizations (esp. Figure 3) and correlation analysis (Table 5) are effective in communicating the paper's main takeaway. The per-dataset breakdown provides actionable insights for practitioners; for instance, a researcher working on commonsense inference would learn that QA4MRE, WDW, and NewsQA are richer sources of `Elaboration` challenges than SQuAD or MCTest.

4.  **Actionable Implications for Future Research:** The discussion section effectively translates the paper's findings into concrete suggestions for the field. The proposed use of this framework for curriculum learning—starting with easy-to-read/easy-to-answer tasks and progressing to harder quadrants—is a compelling idea for structured model development. The discussion on corpus genre and question types also points towards important future directions for dataset creation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inherent Subjectivity and Limited Scale of Annotation:** The paper's core evidence rests on manual annotation of prerequisite skills. While an inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% is strong, this is calculated on a small subset (62 questions), and the definitions for skills like `Bridging` vs. `Elaboration` can be fuzzy at the boundaries. It is unclear if this level of agreement would hold with different annotators or across a larger sample. More critically, the analysis is based on only 100 questions per dataset, a very small fraction of the full datasets. While sufficient for illustrating trends, this limits the statistical certainty of the conclusions and may not capture the full diversity of phenomena within each corpus.

2.  **Oversimplification of "Difficulty":** The paper uses the *count* of required skills as a direct proxy for question difficulty. This assumes all skills are equally difficult to implement in a system and that their difficulty is additive. This is likely not true. A question requiring complex `Logical reasoning` might be far harder than one requiring `Coreference resolution` and `Punctuation` understanding. Furthermore, the interaction between skills is ignored. Certain combinations of skills might pose a disproportionately larger challenge. The analysis would be strengthened by a more nuanced model of difficulty.

3.  **Exclusion of the Information Retrieval Challenge:** The authors explicitly state that their methodology evaluates understanding *after* the relevant sentences have been identified. While the analysis of sentence distance in Section 6 is a welcome addition, it is brief. For many datasets (e.g., MS MARCO, QA4MRE), a primary challenge is locating the relevant information within a much larger context. By focusing only on the reasoning within pre-selected sentences, the framework overlooks a major component of the overall task difficulty. This makes the claim that MS MARCO is "the easiest" dataset potentially misleading, as its retrieval difficulty may be high even if its local inference is simple.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the skill taxonomy and annotation: The distinction between `Bridging` (lexical/grammatical knowledge) and `Elaboration` (general/commonsense knowledge) seems crucial but potentially subjective. Could you provide examples of difficult borderline cases you encountered during annotation and explain the heuristics or rules you developed to resolve them?

2.  Regarding the difficulty metric: Your analysis treats the number of skills as a linear proxy for difficulty. Have you considered that some skills might be inherently harder than others? For instance, would it be possible to weight the skills based on current system performance (e.g., skills that most systems fail on get a higher weight) to create a more nuanced difficulty score?

3.  Regarding the scope of evaluation: You acknowledge that your framework does not assess the difficulty of identifying the necessary sentences. How do you think this retrieval component interacts with the reasoning skills? For example, is it possible that in datasets with long contexts like QA4MRE, the high number of required skills is correlated with the high sentence distance, suggesting that complex reasoning is often required to connect disparate pieces of information?
