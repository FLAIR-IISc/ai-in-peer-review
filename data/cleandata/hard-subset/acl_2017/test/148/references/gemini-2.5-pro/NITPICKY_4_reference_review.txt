### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the difficulty of answering questions and the difficulty of reading the text. The former is measured by counting the number of "prerequisite skills" required to answer a question, based on a refined taxonomy of 13 skills. The latter is measured using a set of 10 standard readability metrics. The authors annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with this framework. Their primary finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the number of required skills and text readability, suggesting that these are two distinct dimensions of difficulty in RC tasks. The annotated data is made publicly available.

### Contributions
1.  **A New Annotated Resource:** The primary contribution is the public release of annotations for 600 question-answer pairs across six different RC datasets. These annotations detail the prerequisite skills required for each question and the specific sentences needed for answering, providing a valuable resource for fine-grained analysis of RC datasets and models.
2.  **Empirical Analysis of RC Datasets:** The paper provides a systematic analysis that empirically decouples question difficulty from text readability. The finding that datasets with high readability (e.g., SQuAD) can have relatively simple questions, while datasets with low readability (e.g., MCTest) can require more complex reasoning, is an important insight for the community.

### Strengths
1.  **Important Research Direction:** The work addresses a crucial and often overlooked aspect of RC evaluation. Moving beyond simple accuracy scores to understand the underlying skills tested and the inherent difficulty of the text is a necessary step for the field's progress. The paper's framing of this problem is clear and well-motivated.
2.  **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** The study's inclusion of six distinct datasets, ranging from expert-authored (QA4MRE) to crowdsourced (SQuAD, NewsQA) and automatically generated (Who-did-What), strengthens the generality of its findings. The comparative analysis in Section 5 provides useful characterizations of these popular benchmarks.
3.  **Well-Grounded Skill Taxonomy:** The proposed set of prerequisite skills is not ad-hoc but is grounded in previous work in NLP (Sugawara et al., 2017) and refined with concepts from psychology (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara and Magliano, 2009) and textual entailment research. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the annotation scheme.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Oversimplified Core Assumption:** The paper's central claim rests on the assumption that "the number of required skills corresponds to the difficulty of a question." This is a significant oversimplification that is stated but never validated. It is highly unlikely that all 13 skills present an equal challenge to a system. For instance, resolving a simple pronoun (`Coreference resolution`) is likely far easier than performing `Logical reasoning` or `Elaboration` with world knowledge. Without weighting the skills by their intrinsic difficulty, the simple count is a coarse and potentially misleading proxy for question difficulty, which weakens the paper's primary conclusion.
2.  **Methodological Ambiguity and Inconsistencies:** The experimental methodology lacks rigor in several key areas:
    *   **Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA):** The paper reports a 90.1% IAA but fails to specify the metric used. Simple percentage agreement is not a suitable metric for a multi-label annotation task, as it does not account for chance agreement. Standard metrics like Fleiss' Kappa or Krippendorff's Alpha should have been used and reported. Furthermore, it is unclear if this agreement score covers the skill labeling, the crucial step of selecting sentences, or both.
    *   **Application of Readability Metrics:** Readability metrics were applied only to the sentences selected by annotators as necessary for answering. This is an unorthodox approach. Readability is typically understood as a property of the entire text a reader is presented with. Applying these metrics to a small, potentially disconnected subset of sentences may not accurately reflect the "difficulty of reading the context" and could confound the results.
    *   **Contradiction in Scope:** The introduction states, "our methodology could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read." However, Section 6 presents an analysis of the number and distance of required sentences, directly contradicting the earlier statement and delving into the very competence it claimed to ignore.
3.  **Lack of Attention to Detail and Presentation Errors:** The paper suffers from a notable number of errors and inconsistencies that undermine its credibility.
    *   **Data Discrepancy:** In Section 5, paragraph (ii), the average number of skills for QA4MRE is stated to be "4.6". However, Table 3 lists this value as "3.25". This is a major factual inconsistency in the reporting of the core results.
    *   **Poor Formatting:** The reference list is exceptionally poorly formatted. It contains inconsistent citation styles, duplicate entries (Trischler et al., 2016), strange formatting artifacts (e.g., `et al\.,?`, `\Q2016\E`), and is difficult to parse. This suggests a lack of care in the paper's preparation. The figures are low-resolution, with labels (e.g., "FK grade level" in Figure 3) being cut off.
    *   **Missing Footnote:** Figure 2 refers to "AWL" and directs the reader to footnote 4, but there is no footnote 4 in the document.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please justify the core assumption that question difficulty is a linear function of the *number* of skills required? How sensitive are your conclusions about the weak correlation between question and text difficulty to this assumption?
2.  Please clarify the Inter-Annotator Agreement calculation. What specific metric was used to arrive at 90.1%? Does this figure represent agreement on the multi-label skill annotation, the sentence selection, or both?
3.  There is a significant discrepancy in the reported average number of skills for QA4MRE (4.6 in the text vs. 3.25 in Table 3). Which value is correct, and how did this error occur?
4.  What was the rationale for calculating readability metrics only on the subset of sentences deemed necessary for answering, rather than on the full context passages? How might this choice impact the interpretation of the paper's findings regarding text difficulty?
