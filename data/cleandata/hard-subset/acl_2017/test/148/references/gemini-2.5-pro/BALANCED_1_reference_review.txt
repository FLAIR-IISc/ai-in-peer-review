### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the difficulty of answering questions and the readability of the source text. For question difficulty, the authors propose a refined typology of 13 "prerequisite skills" required for inference, building upon prior work by grounding the categories in theories from psychology and textual entailment. For text difficulty, they employ a set of 10 standard readability metrics. The authors apply this framework to manually annotate 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA). The central empirical finding is that question difficulty (number of required skills) and text readability are only weakly correlated. The work provides a nuanced characterization of these datasets and makes the resulting annotations publicly available.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive, multi-dataset annotation and comparative analysis.** This is the paper's primary contribution. By applying a single, consistent annotation scheme to six diverse and widely-used datasets, the authors provide invaluable insights into their relative strengths and weaknesses. The analysis reveals nuanced properties (e.g., SQuAD has high readability difficulty but low question difficulty, while MCTest is the inverse), which are crucial for researchers selecting datasets or diagnosing model performance. The public release of these annotations is a significant resource for the community.
2.  **The empirical demonstration that question difficulty and text readability are largely decoupled.** This core finding challenges the implicit assumption that complex texts automatically lead to complex reasoning tasks. It provides a more sophisticated understanding of what makes an RC task difficult and offers a concrete methodology for future dataset design, encouraging creators to focus on crafting challenging questions independently of text complexity.
3.  **A refined and theoretically-grounded typology of prerequisite skills for RC.** The authors improve upon previous ad-hoc skill classifications by explicitly linking their revised categories, particularly for knowledge reasoning (e.g., Bridging, Elaboration), to established concepts from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment. This adds a layer of scientific rigor and makes the framework more robust and principled.

### Strengths

1.  **High-quality, insightful analysis of popular datasets.** This work moves beyond simple leaderboard metrics to provide a much-needed qualitative characterization of what RC datasets actually test. The cross-dataset comparison is particularly illuminating, clearly positioning each dataset within the two-dimensional space of text vs. question difficulty (Figure 3). This analysis helps explain why models that perform well on one dataset may struggle on another and provides a valuable guide for practitioners looking to test specific capabilities of their systems.
2.  **Well-motivated and thoughtfully designed methodology.** The two-axis evaluation framework is intuitive and effectively disentangles two different sources of difficulty in RC. The revision of the prerequisite skills taxonomy is a clear improvement over prior work, grounding the abstract categories in established literature. This theoretical underpinning gives the annotation scheme more credibility and makes it less arbitrary than previous attempts.
3.  **Significant practical value and contribution to the community.** The public release of the annotations for six major datasets is a substantial contribution in its own right. This resource will enable researchers to perform fine-grained error analysis, better understand the phenomena their models succeed or fail on, and potentially develop more targeted models or curriculum learning strategies, as suggested by the authors. The paper serves as an excellent example of meta-analysis that can help guide the field toward more deliberate progress.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited scale of analysis and potential for sampling bias.** The analysis relies on annotating only 100 questions per dataset. For large-scale datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO, this represents a very small fraction of the data, raising concerns about how representative the sample is. While the authors state this is consistent with prior work, the strength of the paper's claims about the overall character of each dataset rests heavily on this small sample. A more thorough discussion of the sampling strategy's attempt to ensure representativeness, or an analysis of the sensitivity of the results to different random samples, would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.
2.  **Potential for subjectivity in the prerequisite skill annotation.** While an inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% is reported, this single number obscures potential nuances. The paper does not specify how this was calculated (e.g., simple agreement vs. a chance-corrected metric like Cohen's Kappa) or if agreement varied across skills. The distinction between certain skills, such as "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge), appears particularly subtle and prone to subjective interpretation. A more detailed breakdown of the agreement and clearer definitions with distinguishing examples in an appendix would increase confidence in the reliability of the annotations.
3.  **The analysis of sentence selection is underdeveloped.** The authors correctly identify that their primary methodology does not evaluate the crucial competence of selecting the necessary evidence sentences from a larger context. They include a brief, preliminary analysis of this aspect in Section 6 (Table 6), measuring the number and distance of required sentences. However, this analysis feels disconnected from the main framework. A key opportunity is missed to connect this aspect to the prerequisite skills. For instance, do questions requiring more skills also tend to require more, or more distant, sentences? Are skills like "Coreference resolution" or "Bridging" more common when multiple sentences are needed? Integrating this analysis more deeply would have provided a more complete picture of task difficulty.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide more detail on the inter-annotator agreement calculation? Was the 90.1% a simple agreement rate? Was it calculated per-question (i.e., any mismatch in the set of skill labels for a question counts as a disagreement) or per-skill-per-question? Did you compute chance-corrected agreement scores like Fleiss' Kappa, which might be more appropriate for multi-label annotation with multiple annotators?
2.  The distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" is central to your revised taxonomy but seems difficult to operationalize. Could you provide a few concrete, contrasting examples from your annotations that highlight the boundary between these two skills? How were annotators instructed to handle ambiguous cases where an inference could plausibly be supported by either lexical relations or world knowledge?
3.  Regarding the sampling of 100 questions: did you conduct any post-hoc analysis to verify that your sample was representative of the full dataset? For example, does the distribution of question types (e.g., who/what/where) or answer types in your sample for SQuAD or MS MARCO align with the known distributions in the full development sets? This would help alleviate concerns about sampling bias.
