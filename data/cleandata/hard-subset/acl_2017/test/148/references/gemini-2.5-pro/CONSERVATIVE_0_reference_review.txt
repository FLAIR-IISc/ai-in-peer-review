### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the difficulty of answering questions, measured by a taxonomy of "prerequisite skills," and the difficulty of reading the text, measured by standard readability metrics. The authors refine an existing set of 13 prerequisite skills, grounded in theories of human text comprehension. They manually annotate 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) with these skills and analyze the datasets based on this framework.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comparative analysis and annotated resource for six RC datasets.** The primary contribution is the creation and release of manual annotations detailing the cognitive skills required for a sample of questions from these datasets. The resulting analysis provides a useful characterization of the challenges posed by each benchmark.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of the disconnect between text readability and question complexity.** The paper provides quantitative evidence that text complexity (e.g., Flesch-Kincaid grade level) has only a weak correlation with the inferential complexity of the questions, a valuable insight for future dataset creation.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a critical need for more nuanced evaluation in RC.** The field has largely relied on overall accuracy scores, which can obscure the specific capabilities and limitations of models. This work pushes for a more fine-grained analysis by attempting to categorize the types of reasoning required, which is a commendable and important direction for advancing RC research.
2.  **The proposed framework is grounded in established cognitive science literature.** The authors base their two-pronged approach (skills vs. readability) and the refinement of their skill taxonomy on well-regarded models of human comprehension (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). This provides a solid theoretical underpinning that is often lacking in ad-hoc dataset analyses.
3.  **The cross-dataset analysis yields valuable insights.** The results presented in Section 5 offer a clear comparative overview of several key benchmarks. For example, the finding that SQuAD contains complex text but requires a relatively low number of skills per question, while MCTest uses simpler text for more complex questions, quantifies a widely held intuition and provides a useful guide for researchers selecting datasets for specific purposes.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The proposed skill taxonomy lacks rigorous validation.** The central analytical tool of this paper is the 13-skill taxonomy. While it is a refinement of previous work, its validity is not sufficiently established. The definitions for several skills are abstract, and the distinction between them can be subtle (e.g., `Bridging` vs. `Elaboration`). The paper reports high inter-annotator agreement on applying the labels, but this does not validate the taxonomy itself. There is no external evidence to show that this specific categorization is optimal or that the skills are truly distinct cognitive components. The entire analysis rests on the assumption that this taxonomy is a sound and reliable instrument, an assumption that is not proven within the paper.
2.  **The core assumption linking skill count to difficulty is not tested.** The paper operates on the assumption that "the more skills that are required to answer a question, the more difficult the question is." This is a critical hypothesis that is never actually tested against system performance. A much stronger paper would have correlated the annotated number of skills for each question with the actual performance of established RC models. Without this validation, the "average number of required skills" is an unproven proxy for difficulty, significantly weakening the paper's conclusions.
3.  **The analysis is based on a very small sample size.** The conclusions are drawn from annotating only 100 questions per dataset. For large-scale datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO, this represents a tiny fraction of the whole. While manual annotation is expensive, claims about the overall characteristics of these datasets are not well-supported by such a limited sample. The representativeness of this sample is not discussed or justified, making it difficult to trust that the reported distributions (e.g., Table 2, Table 3) reflect the datasets at large.
4.  **The practical utility of the framework is not demonstrated.** The paper proposes a methodology for dataset analysis but does not show how it can be used to gain new insights into RC *systems*. The discussion in Section 6 about using the metrics for curriculum learning or model development is purely speculative. A key opportunity was missed to apply the framework to analyze the error patterns of an existing state-of-the-art model, which would have demonstrated the diagnostic power of the proposed skills and provided a compelling use case. As it stands, the contribution is a descriptive exercise with unproven applicability.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your central claim is that the number of required skills serves as a proxy for question difficulty. Have you attempted to validate this assumption by correlating the number of annotated skills per question with the success/failure of one or more established RC models on those same questions? Such an analysis would be crucial for establishing the validity of your primary metric.
2.  The distinction between `Bridging` (inferences using lexical/grammatical knowledge) and `Elaboration` (inferences using general/commonsense knowledge) appears highly subjective and potentially difficult to distinguish reliably in practice. Could you elaborate on the annotation guidelines for this distinction and provide examples that were challenging for annotators? How can we be confident that this distinction is consistent and meaningful?
3.  Given the small sample size of 100 questions per dataset, how robust are your conclusions about the overall characteristics of these large and diverse datasets? Could you comment on the variance of your metrics and how confident you are that another random sample of 100 questions would yield a similar distribution of required skills?
