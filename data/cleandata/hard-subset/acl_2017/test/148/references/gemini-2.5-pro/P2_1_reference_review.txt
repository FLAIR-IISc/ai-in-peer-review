### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets, moving beyond superficial metrics like question type or overall accuracy. The proposed dimensions are: 1) **Prerequisite Skills**, a refined taxonomy of 13 cognitive and linguistic skills required to answer a question, and 2) **Readability**, a set of 10 established metrics measuring the lexical and syntactic complexity of the context text. The authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets with these prerequisite skills and analyze them alongside their readability scores. The central finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the difficulty of its questions (as proxied by the number of required skills). This demonstrates that easy-to-read texts can pose difficult questions, and vice-versa.

In my view, the main contributions are, in order of significance:

1.  **Creation and release of a valuable analytical resource:** The paper provides a fine-grained, multi-dataset annotation of the skills required for reading comprehension. This resource enables researchers to perform targeted error analysis, select datasets based on specific linguistic phenomena, and better understand the challenges posed by existing benchmarks.
2.  **An insightful analysis and characterization of popular RC datasets:** The work provides a clear, evidence-backed characterization of six datasets, highlighting their distinct profiles. For instance, it compellingly shows that SQuAD contains complex text but relatively simple questions, while MCTest presents the opposite challenge. This analysis provides actionable insights for the community.
3.  **A well-grounded conceptual framework for RC evaluation:** The paper synthesizes prior work in NLP and psychology to propose a refined taxonomy of "prerequisite skills." Grounding the framework in established theories like Kintsch's C-I model and research on textual entailment lends it significant credibility.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Critical Need in the Field:** The paper tackles the crucial and timely problem of moving beyond simplistic accuracy metrics to a deeper understanding of what RC datasets actually test. As models achieve near-human performance on some leaderboards, this kind of qualitative analysis is essential for identifying remaining challenges and guiding future progress. The work provides a concrete methodology for this deeper evaluation.
2.  **High-Value Annotated Resource:** The primary strength of this work is the annotated dataset itself. By applying a consistent annotation scheme across six diverse datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, etc.), the authors have created a resource that facilitates direct, cross-dataset comparison. This is a significant contribution that will likely be used by other researchers for model evaluation and dataset design.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The authors do not invent their evaluation metrics in a vacuum. The prerequisite skills are a thoughtful refinement of previous work, explicitly justified by foundational theories of human text comprehension from psychology (e.g., McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) and established phenomena in textual entailment research. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for their taxonomy.
4.  **Clear and Impactful Findings:** The central conclusion—that readability and question difficulty are not strongly correlated—is simple but powerful. It is convincingly demonstrated through clear visualizations (Figures 3 and 4) and quantitative analysis (Table 5). This finding challenges the implicit assumption that harder texts automatically lead to harder tasks and provides a clear rationale for why a multi-dimensional evaluation is necessary.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Central Assumption is Not Validated:** The entire quantitative analysis of question difficulty rests on the assumption that "the number of required skills corresponds to the difficulty of a question." While intuitive, this is a strong claim that is never validated. It is plausible that some skills (e.g., `Logical Reasoning`) are inherently more difficult for current models than others (e.g., `Coreference Resolution`), or that combinations of skills have non-linear effects on difficulty. Without correlating the number of skills with actual system performance (e.g., accuracy of a state-of-the-art model on the annotated questions), this remains a proxy of unverified quality, which weakens the claims about dataset difficulty.
2.  **Limited Sample Size:** The analysis is based on only 100 questions from each dataset. For massive datasets like SQuAD (~100k questions) and MS MARCO (~100k questions), this represents a tiny fraction (0.1%). While the authors cite precedent for this sample size, the statistical power of the conclusions about the overall character of these large and diverse datasets is limited. The claims would be substantially stronger if based on a larger or more stratified sample.
3.  **Potential Subjectivity in Annotation:** While a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is reported, the definitions for some skills remain subtle and potentially subjective. For example, the distinction between `Bridging` ("inferences between two facts supported by grammatical and lexical knowledge") and `Elaboration` ("inference using known facts, general knowledge") can be blurry. A word's meaning (lexical knowledge) is often a form of general knowledge. More details on the agreement metric used (e.g., Cohen's Kappa vs. raw percentage) and a discussion of systematic disagreements would help build confidence in the reliability of the annotations, which are the foundation of the entire study.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more detail on the inter-annotator agreement calculation? Specifically, what metric was used (e.g., Krippendorff's alpha, Fleiss' kappa), and was it computed on a per-skill, per-question basis? Given the subtle distinctions between skills like `Bridging` and `Elaboration`, could you discuss the most common sources of disagreement among annotators and how these cases were adjudicated?
2.  The analysis of question difficulty relies on the proxy that more skills equals a harder question. Have you considered validating this assumption? For example, did you analyze the performance of a representative RC model on your annotated 600 questions to see if there is a strong negative correlation between model accuracy and the number of required skills? Such an analysis would significantly strengthen your core claims.
3.  The methodology intentionally excludes evaluating the "competence of selecting necessary sentences," which is a core challenge in many RC tasks. Your brief analysis in Table 6 is interesting. How do you envision your framework being integrated with methods that *do* evaluate this retrieval/search component to provide a more holistic picture of a system's RC capabilities?
