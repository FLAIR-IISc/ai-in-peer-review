**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the complexity of the reasoning required to answer questions, and the readability of the context passages. To measure question complexity, the authors propose a refined taxonomy of 13 "prerequisite skills" (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging inferences, logical reasoning) that are needed to connect the context to the answer. To measure text complexity, they employ 10 established readability metrics based on lexical and syntactic features. The authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six prominent RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) using this framework. The central finding of their analysis is a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the number of prerequisite skills required by a question, suggesting that these two dimensions of difficulty are largely independent. The paper provides a detailed comparative analysis of the six datasets and releases the annotations as a public resource.

**Contributions**

1.  **A comprehensive, multi-dataset empirical analysis that demonstrates the decoupling of textual complexity and question complexity in RC.** The primary contribution is the systematic study across six diverse datasets providing strong evidence that text readability is not a reliable proxy for question difficulty. This finding challenges superficial assumptions about what makes an RC task hard and has significant implications for the design of future datasets and evaluation methodologies.
2.  **A refined annotation schema and a publicly available resource for nuanced dataset analysis.** The paper presents an improved taxonomy of prerequisite skills, grounded in theories from psychology and textual entailment, which offers a more granular tool for analysis than simple question-type categories. The public release of annotations for six datasets using this schema is a valuable resource that enables more fine-grained analysis of both datasets and model capabilities.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant and Timely Investigation:** The paper addresses a crucial need in RC research for a deeper understanding of what existing datasets measure. By empirically demonstrating the weak correlation between text readability and question reasoning complexity, the work provides a valuable, data-driven perspective that can guide more principled dataset creation and system evaluation, such as the curriculum learning paradigm suggested by the authors.
2.  **Sound and Well-Grounded Methodology:** The proposed evaluation framework is built on solid foundations. The prerequisite skill taxonomy is a thoughtful refinement of previous work, informed by established theories in psychology (e.g., Kintsch) and NLP (e.g., textual entailment). The readability metrics are adopted from well-cited prior work. The annotation process is clearly described, uses three annotators, and reports a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), lending credibility to the resulting analysis.
3.  **Broad Empirical Scope and Clear Presentation:** The study's analysis spans six diverse and widely used RC datasets, making its findings more generalizable and providing a valuable characterization of the RC landscape. The results are presented clearly through tables and figures that directly support the paper's main claims, such as the distinct clustering of datasets in Figure 3 based on the two proposed difficulty axes.
4.  **Contribution of a Reusable Public Resource:** The authors' commitment to making their annotations publicly available is a significant strength. This resource allows other researchers to replicate the findings, conduct their own fine-grained error analyses of model performance (e.g., by correlating model errors with specific required skills), and build upon this work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Potential Subjectivity in the Skill Taxonomy:** While the skill taxonomy is well-motivated, the distinction between certain skills can be subtle and potentially subjective. For example, the boundary between "Bridging" (defined as using grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (using general/commonsense knowledge) appears blurry. An inference that relies on understanding event parts (listed under Bridging) could easily be classified as requiring commonsense knowledge (Elaboration). The paper would be strengthened by providing more detailed annotation guidelines or an analysis of inter-annotator disagreements on specific skill categories.
2.  **Oversimplification of Question Difficulty:** The paper operationalizes question difficulty primarily as the *number* of required skills. This is a reasonable first-order approximation but assumes that all skills contribute equally and additively to the overall difficulty. It is plausible that a question requiring a single, complex "Logical reasoning" step is more difficult than a question requiring two simpler skills like "Punctuation" and "Coreference resolution". The analysis does not account for the intrinsic difficulty of individual skills or potential interactions between them.
3.  **Analysis is Static and Lacks Model Correlation:** The paper provides an insightful analysis of the datasets themselves but does not connect this analysis to the performance of actual RC models. While the discussion section speculates on how the framework could be used for system development, the paper would be more impactful if it included even a preliminary analysis showing that a state-of-the-art model's performance correlates negatively with the number of required skills, or that certain skills are particularly challenging for current models. This would directly validate the utility of the proposed metrics for model analysis.
4.  **Limited Annotation Sample Size:** Annotating 100 questions per dataset is a substantial effort and follows precedent from similar analysis papers. However, for very large datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO, this represents a small fraction of the data. There is a risk that the random sample may not be fully representative of the distribution of phenomena in the entire dataset. Acknowledging this limitation and perhaps discussing the diversity of the sampled questions would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" appears to be a key, yet potentially ambiguous, aspect of your taxonomy. Could you provide a few challenging examples from your annotation process that illustrate the boundary between these two skills and elaborate on the specific guidelines annotators used to distinguish them?
2.  Your primary metric for question difficulty is the count of required skills. Have you considered alternative formulations, such as weighting skills by their perceived difficulty or analyzing the impact of specific skill combinations versus a simple sum? For instance, is there evidence that questions requiring "Elaboration" are empirically harder for existing systems than those requiring "Coreference resolution," independent of the total skill count?
3.  You acknowledge that your methodology does not evaluate a model's ability to locate the necessary sentences. How do you believe this unmeasured "search" difficulty interacts with the two dimensions you analyze? For instance, it's plausible that documents with low readability (e.g., long sentences, complex syntax) also make the evidence localization task more difficult, which could be a confounding factor.
