### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on the distinction between the difficulty of reading a text and the difficulty of answering questions about it. The authors propose two classes of metrics: (1) **Prerequisite Skills**, a refined set of 13 cognitive skills (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging, elaboration) required to answer a question, grounded in psychology and textual entailment research; and (2) **Readability**, a set of 10 standard linguistic metrics measuring lexical and syntactic complexity. The authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these metrics. The central finding is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, demonstrating that easy-to-read texts can pose complex questions and vice-versa.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A comprehensive, multi-dataset analysis of RC benchmarks.** The systematic annotation and comparison of six diverse datasets using a unified framework is a significant empirical contribution. It provides the community with a much-needed qualitative characterization of these datasets, moving beyond superficial metrics like question type. The public release of these annotations is a valuable resource.
2.  **The key empirical finding that question difficulty and text readability are largely independent.** This insight challenges a common implicit assumption in the field and has important implications for both dataset creation and model evaluation, highlighting the need to control for these two dimensions of difficulty separately.
3.  **A refined and well-grounded typology of "prerequisite skills" for RC.** By drawing upon established theories from psychology and linguistics, the authors present a more principled and detailed classification of reasoning skills than in prior work, providing a useful vocabulary for analyzing RC tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Framing:** The paper is motivated by a clear and important problem: the lack of nuanced evaluation metrics for RC datasets. The introductory example comparing an easy question from a difficult SQuAD passage with a difficult question from an easy MCTest passage effectively frames the core thesis that readability and question difficulty are not the same.
2.  **Principled and Well-Grounded Methodology:** The choice to separate analysis into "prerequisite skills" and "readability" is intuitive and powerful. The authors ground their skill typology in well-established theories of human text comprehension (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment, which adds significant credibility and substance to their framework compared to ad-hoc categorizations.
3.  **Valuable Empirical Analysis and Insights:** The annotation of six different datasets is a substantial effort that yields valuable insights. The analysis presented in Tables 2, 3, and 4, and Figures 3 and 4 provides a clear "profile" for each dataset. For example, identifying SQuAD as "hard to read, easy to answer" and MCTest as "easy to read, hard to answer" is a useful characterization that can guide researchers in selecting appropriate benchmarks for testing specific capabilities.
4.  **High Potential for Impact:** This work has strong potential to influence future research. The proposed framework can guide the creation of more targeted and challenging RC datasets (e.g., by focusing on specific skills or combinations of readability/difficulty). Furthermore, the discussion on using this analysis for curriculum learning in model development is compelling and offers a concrete path for building more robust RC systems. The released annotations will be a valuable asset for fine-grained error analysis.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The "Number of Skills" as a Coarse Proxy for Difficulty:** The paper's primary measure of question difficulty is a simple count of the required prerequisite skills. This assumes that all skills contribute equally to the difficulty, which is unlikely to be true. For instance, `Elaboration` (requiring external commonsense knowledge) is arguably more challenging for current models than `Punctuation`. This simplification could lead to a skewed perception of dataset difficulty. The analysis would be stronger if it acknowledged this limitation or explored a more nuanced difficulty metric.
2.  **Potential for Sampling Bias and Scalability Issues:** The analysis is based on a random sample of only 100 questions per dataset. While this is a reasonable starting point for a manual annotation study, it may not be fully representative of large and diverse datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO. For instance, the SQuAD sampling (one question per paragraph) could miss variations in difficulty within articles. The authors should discuss the potential limitations of this sample size more directly. The manual nature of the annotation also makes it difficult to apply this analysis at scale to new or full datasets.
3.  **Ambiguity in Skill Definitions:** Some of the skill definitions, while well-intentioned, may be ambiguous in practice, potentially affecting annotation consistency. The distinction between `Bridging` (inferences from "grammatical and lexical knowledge") and `Elaboration` (inferences from "general knowledge") is particularly fuzzy. Many linguistic phenomena, like understanding thematic roles or noun-compounds, could fall into either category depending on the annotator's interpretation. Providing more boundary-case examples would strengthen the clarity and reproducibility of the annotation scheme.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your main measure of question difficulty is the unweighted sum of required skills. Have you considered that certain skills might be inherently more difficult than others? Could you comment on the validity of this simplification, and would you expect the paper's main conclusions to change if a weighted difficulty metric (e.g., based on model error rates per skill) were used instead?
2.  The analysis relies on a 100-question sample from each dataset. How confident are you that these samples are representative of the full datasets, particularly for large corpora like SQuAD and MS MARCO where question difficulty might vary significantly across different domains or articles?
3.  The distinction between `Bridging` and `Elaboration` seems critical but potentially subjective. Could you provide a few more examples that clearly illustrate the boundary between these two skills, especially for cases where lexical knowledge (e.g., hypernymy) and commonsense knowledge seem to overlap? This would help readers better understand the annotation process.
