### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the "prerequisite skills" needed to answer a question and the "readability" of the source text. The authors refine an existing taxonomy of prerequisite skills, drawing on theories from psychology and textual entailment, and apply established readability metrics to the relevant sentences for a given question. The core of the work is an annotation effort where the authors manually label 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets with these skills. The analysis of these annotations leads to the main finding: there is only a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the complexity of the questions asked about it (as measured by the number of required skills).

From my perspective, the contributions of this work are, in order of significance:

1.  **A valuable annotated dataset:** The primary contribution is the public release of annotations for 600 questions across six diverse RC datasets. This resource will allow for more nuanced error analysis and a better understanding of the challenges posed by these benchmarks, which is a service to the community.
2.  **An empirical confirmation of a key insight:** The paper provides quantitative evidence for the decoupling of text readability and question difficulty. While perhaps intuitive, demonstrating this across multiple datasets is a useful finding that underscores the need for more sophisticated question design in RC.
3.  **A refined taxonomy of skills:** The paper presents an updated taxonomy of skills required for RC, attempting to ground categories like knowledge reasoning in established literature.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Research Direction:** The paper addresses a critical need in the field. Moving beyond simple accuracy scores to a more fine-grained, qualitative evaluation of RC systems and datasets is essential for making meaningful progress. The motivation to understand *what* is being tested is very well-founded.
2.  **Grounded in Established Theory:** The authors make a commendable effort to ground their two-dimensional framework in prior work, citing psychological models of text comprehension (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) for the skills taxonomy and established NLP work (Vajjala & Meurers) for readability. This provides a solid conceptual foundation for the proposed metrics.
3.  **Creation of a Useful Public Resource:** The most significant strength is the annotation effort and the decision to make the results publicly available. This dataset, while modest in size, provides a concrete tool for researchers to analyze their models' performance on different reasoning types or to compare the composition of various benchmarks. This is a solid, empirical contribution that will have value regardless of the other aspects of the paper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unvalidated Assumption of "Difficulty":** The entire analysis hinges on the core assumption that the difficulty of a question is directly proportional to the number of required skills. This is a significant oversimplification that is stated but never validated. It is highly unlikely that all skills present equal difficulty for current models. For instance, complex `Logical Reasoning` might be far more challenging than simple `Coreference Resolution`. By treating all skills as equal and simply summing them, the paper's primary measure of question difficulty is coarse and potentially misleading. This untested assumption weakens the conclusions drawn from the correlation analysis.
2.  **Incremental and Unproven Skill Taxonomy:** The authors claim to "revise" and "reorganize" a prerequisite skill taxonomy from prior work (Sugawara et al., 2017). However, the novelty here seems limited. More critically, the new taxonomy itself is not rigorously validated. The distinctions between the newly proposed knowledge-based skills—particularly "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge)—appear highly subjective. The reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% is an aggregate figure over a small sample and does not provide insight into the reliability of annotating these more nuanced and potentially overlapping categories. Without a more thorough validation of the taxonomy's completeness, coherence, and annotator reliability at a per-skill level, its utility as a stable evaluation metric is questionable.
3.  **Lack of Connection to System Performance:** The paper's title and introduction frame the work as providing "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension" for both datasets and systems. However, the analysis is restricted entirely to the datasets themselves. The utility of these metrics for system evaluation is only discussed speculatively. The paper would be substantially stronger if it included even a basic analysis of how existing, standard RC models perform on questions requiring different skills or a different number of skills. Does model accuracy actually decrease as the number of skills increases? Are models particularly deficient in `Elaboration` or `Analogy`? Without this link, the practical value of the proposed framework for system development remains an unproven assertion.
4.  **Limited Sample Size:** The analysis is based on only 100 questions per dataset. While the authors cite precedent for this sample size, for massive datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO, this represents a tiny fraction of the data. Conclusions drawn about the overall characteristics of these datasets should be treated with caution, as the sample may not be fully representative of the entire distribution of questions and skills.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a breakdown of the inter-annotator agreement for each individual skill, especially for the newly proposed and potentially subjective categories like `Bridging` and `Elaboration`? This is crucial for assessing whether these distinctions can be reliably applied by others.
2.  The "number of skills" is used as a proxy for question difficulty. Have you considered any alternative approaches, such as weighting skills based on their perceived complexity or analyzing empirical difficulty (e.g., average accuracy of baseline models)? Why should a simple, unweighted sum be considered a methodologically sound proxy for difficulty?
3.  Given that the metrics are proposed for system evaluation, could you demonstrate their utility with a brief analysis of a standard RC model (e.g., a BERT-based model)? For instance, showing how the model's accuracy varies with the number of skills, or identifying which specific skills are most challenging for the model, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about the practical value of your framework.
