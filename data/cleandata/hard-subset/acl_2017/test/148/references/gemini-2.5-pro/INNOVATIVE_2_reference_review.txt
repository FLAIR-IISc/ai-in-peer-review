**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel, two-dimensional framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets, arguing that we must decouple the difficulty of reading a text from the difficulty of answering a question about it. The proposed framework consists of two classes of metrics: (1) a set of 13 "prerequisite skills" (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging, elaboration) required to answer a question, and (2) standard "readability" metrics that measure the lexical and syntactic complexity of the text. The authors apply this framework by manually annotating 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets. Their central finding is that there is only a weak correlation between readability and the number of required skills, challenging the implicit assumption that textually complex documents lead to more difficult RC tasks.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Conceptual Framework for RC Evaluation:** The primary contribution is the proposal of a new way to understand and characterize RC tasks. By separating text difficulty (readability) from reasoning difficulty (prerequisite skills), the paper provides a much more nuanced and insightful lens for analysis than simple accuracy scores. This is a significant conceptual leap for the field.
2.  **Valuable Annotation Resource and Empirical Analysis:** The authors have created and are releasing a valuable new resource: annotations of reasoning skills across six major datasets. The empirical analysis based on this resource provides concrete, and at times surprising, characterizations of these datasets (e.g., SQuAD is hard to read but easy to answer; MCTest is the opposite).
3.  **Grounding NLP Evaluation in Cognitive Science:** The work does an excellent job of connecting the evaluation of machine comprehension to decades of research on human text comprehension from psychology (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), giving the proposed skill taxonomy a strong theoretical foundation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Potential Impact:** This paper is a breath of fresh air. Instead of presenting another incremental improvement on a specific leaderboard, it steps back and fundamentally questions what we are measuring. The idea of decoupling text complexity from reasoning complexity is a bold and important one. This work has the potential to shift how the community designs datasets, analyzes model performance, and structures research problems (e.g., via curriculum learning, as suggested). It provides the vocabulary and methodology to move beyond leaderboards and towards genuine diagnostic evaluation.
2.  **Illuminating and Actionable Insights:** The analysis yields fascinating results that are immediately useful. The finding that SQuAD, a staple of modern RC, consists of difficult-to-read text but requires relatively few reasoning skills on average is a crucial insight. In contrast, a dataset like MCTest, written for children, is shown to require more complex reasoning. This provides researchers with a clear, data-driven guide for selecting appropriate datasets to test specific model capabilities.
3.  **Well-Motivated and Principled Approach:** The taxonomy of prerequisite skills is not ad-hoc but is thoughtfully derived from previous work and grounded in well-established models of human comprehension from psychology. This principled foundation makes the framework more credible and likely to be enduring than a purely data-driven or task-specific categorization would be.

**Weaknesses**

While my overall assessment is highly positive, I note some limitations that are understandable for a work introducing such a novel direction. I view these as opportunities for future research rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Exclusion of Evidence-Finding Competence:** The annotation methodology requires annotators to first select the necessary sentences and then annotate the skills required to reason over them. This explicitly factors out the crucial challenge of *locating* the relevant information within a potentially long document. The authors acknowledge this and provide a brief analysis of sentence distance, but a more integrated view of difficulty would also account for the search component of the RC task. This is a reasonable scoping decision for a first paper on this topic, but it's an important piece of the puzzle that remains to be addressed.
2.  **Inherent Subjectivity of Skill Annotation:** The definitions for some skills, particularly the distinction between "Bridging" (lexical/grammatical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (commonsense/general knowledge), are subtle and may be difficult for annotators to distinguish consistently. While the reported inter-annotator agreement is strong (90.1%), the subjective nature of the task means the resulting annotations should be interpreted as a strong, but not absolute, signal. For a foundational work like this, this level of rigor is acceptable.
3.  **Limited Sample Size for Annotation:** The analysis is based on 100 questions per dataset. This is sufficient to establish the paper's main thesis and reveal clear, high-level trends. However, a larger-scale annotation effort would be needed to make more fine-grained statistical claims or to analyze rarer phenomena within each dataset.

**Questions to Authors**

The work is very clearly presented, and I have no major questions that would alter my recommendation. I am curious about the future direction of this compelling research:

*   Beyond manual annotation, do you envision a path toward automatically predicting the prerequisite skills for a given question-context pair? Such a model could enable analysis at the scale of entire datasets and could potentially be used to automatically generate new datasets balanced for specific skill distributions.
