### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the relationship between two distinct types of difficulty in Reading Comprehension (RC) tasks: the difficulty of the text itself (readability) and the difficulty of the reasoning required to answer a question (prerequisite skills). To this end, the authors propose a two-part evaluation framework. The first part is a refined taxonomy of 13 "prerequisite skills" required for answering RC questions, building upon prior work by grounding it in psychological theories of comprehension. The second part adopts ten established readability metrics. The authors apply this framework to six existing RC datasets by manually annotating 100 questions from each with the required skills and calculating readability scores for the relevant text passages. Their primary finding is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and the number of prerequisite skills, suggesting that easy-to-read texts can still pose difficult questions.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A valuable new resource:** The annotation of six diverse RC datasets with a detailed skill taxonomy. The public release of these annotations is a significant contribution that will enable more fine-grained analysis of RC systems and datasets by the community.
2.  **An important empirical finding:** The paper provides strong quantitative evidence for the often-assumed but rarely-demonstrated decoupling of text readability and question complexity in RC. This finding has implications for future dataset creation and system development strategies, such as curriculum learning.
3.  **A refined skill taxonomy:** The authors synthesize concepts from NLP and psychology to refine a previous skill taxonomy for RC, particularly by elaborating on different types of knowledge and inference (e.g., Bridging, Elaboration).

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a timely and important problem:** As the field moves beyond simple accuracy metrics, there is a clear need for more nuanced evaluation frameworks. This paper tackles the crucial but under-explored question of how different facets of difficulty interact in RC, providing a methodology to disentangle text complexity from reasoning complexity.
2.  **Principled, theory-grounded methodology:** The decision to ground the prerequisite skill taxonomy in established psychological models of text comprehension (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) lends theoretical validity to the proposed framework. This contrasts favorably with more ad-hoc categorizations seen in other works.
3.  **Comprehensive cross-dataset analysis:** The study's scope, covering six different RC datasets with varying sources and formats (e.g., technical documents, narratives, news), provides a broad and valuable perspective on the current landscape of RC challenges. The comparative analysis highlights unique characteristics of each dataset.

### **Weaknesses**

My recommendation is primarily based on a significant number of minor-to-moderate methodological and presentational issues that, in aggregate, undermine confidence in the work's polish and rigor.

1.  **Lack of methodological detail and clarity:** Several key procedures are underspecified.
    *   **Annotation Process:** The paper states three annotators were used and reports 90.1% agreement, but provides no further details. What was the background of the annotators? What training was provided? More importantly, how were disagreements resolved? Was a consensus reached for the final annotation, or was it a majority vote? A simple percentage agreement can be misleadingly high; a chance-corrected metric like Cohen's Kappa should have been reported.
    *   **Misleading Statistics:** The calculation of the average number of skills in Table 3 appears to include "Nonsense" questions (which require zero skills) in the denominator (e.g., for QA4MRE, the sum of skills is 325, and 325/100 = 3.25). This deflates the average difficulty for solvable questions, presenting a skewed view. The average should be calculated only over the set of solvable questions.
    *   **Ambiguous Metrics:** The definition for "sentence distance" in Appendix B is highly ambiguous. For questions requiring more than two sentences, the distance is "the sum of distances of any two sentences." This could be interpreted in multiple ways (e.g., sum of all pairwise distances, distance between the furthest two sentences). A precise formula or a clear example is needed.

2.  **Biased sampling undermines a key comparison:** The authors state in Appendix A that for NewsQA, they "randomly chose questions that satisfied... is question bad = 0." They then report a very low "Nonsense" rate for NewsQA (1%) and contrast it with datasets like Who-did-What (27%). This is a flawed comparison. By pre-filtering questions identified as "bad," the analysis guarantees a low nonsense rate for NewsQA. This experimental choice invalidates the conclusion that NewsQA is inherently of higher quality in this regard than other datasets. While acknowledged briefly in the text, the severity of this flaw is understated.

3.  **Sloppy presentation and formatting:** The paper is marred by numerous formatting errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading. The reference section is particularly problematic, containing clear BibTeX compilation errors such as `Aluisio et al\.,? 2010, Aluisio et al\.,`, `Clark.,? 1975, Clark.,`, and `Paperno et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Paperno et al\.,`. Such errors appear repeatedly throughout the bibliography. This lack of attention to detail in the final manuscript is concerning.

4.  **Incremental novelty of the skill taxonomy:** The paper claims to "revise" and "reorganize" the skill taxonomy from Sugawara et al. (2017). However, several of the changes are simple renamings ("Object tracking*") or merges ("Mathematical reasoning*"). The primary novelty lies in subdividing commonsense reasoning. While this is a useful step, the distinction between the new categories, particularly "Bridging" (grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (general/commonsense knowledge), can be highly subjective. The paper does not discuss the challenges in making this distinction during annotation or the specific guidelines used to ensure consistency.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the calculation of the "Ave." number of skills in Table 3? Was the denominator the total number of questions sampled (100), including those labeled "Nonsense"? If so, could you provide the average calculated only over the solvable questions, as this seems to be a more direct measure of the difficulty of the actual tasks?
2.  Given that you filtered out questions flagged as "bad" from NewsQA before annotation, how can a fair comparison of "Nonsense" rates be made with other datasets that were not pre-filtered in this way? Does this not artificially advantage NewsQA in your analysis?
3.  Could you provide a precise formula or a concrete example for the "sentence distance" calculation in Appendix B, specifically for a case requiring three or more non-contiguous sentences?
4.  The distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" appears subtle. What specific guidelines or rules of thumb were provided to annotators to reliably distinguish between these two types of inference, especially in ambiguous cases?
