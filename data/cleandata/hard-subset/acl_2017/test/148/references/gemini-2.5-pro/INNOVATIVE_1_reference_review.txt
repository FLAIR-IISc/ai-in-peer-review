### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a novel, two-dimensional framework for the evaluation and analysis of Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors argue that RC difficulty is composed of two distinct axes: the difficulty of reading the text (readability) and the difficulty of answering the question (prerequisite skills). They propose a refined taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, grounded in psychology and linguistics, to quantify question difficulty. They then annotate six prominent RC datasets using this framework and analyze them, revealing that readability and question difficulty are only weakly correlated. The key finding is that a text's linguistic complexity is not a reliable indicator of the reasoning complexity required to answer questions about it.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel Conceptual Framework for RC Evaluation:** The primary contribution is the proposal to decouple text readability from question-answering complexity. This provides a new, much-needed lens for analyzing datasets and systems, moving beyond simple accuracy scores or superficial question-type categories. This conceptual shift is significant and has the potential to guide future research in a more principled direction.
2.  **A New Resource for the Community:** The authors provide detailed annotations of six diverse RC datasets according to their proposed framework. This meta-dataset is a valuable resource that enables more fine-grained analysis of existing models and can inform the creation of better, more targeted datasets in the future.
3.  **A Principled Taxonomy of Reasoning Skills:** The paper refines a previous classification of "prerequisite skills" by grounding it in established theories from psychology (e.g., Kintsch's model of comprehension) and textual entailment. This provides a more rigorous theoretical foundation for analyzing the reasoning capabilities required by RC tasks.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Boldness:** The paper’s greatest strength is its bold conceptual contribution. By explicitly separating the challenges of parsing complex language from the challenges of performing complex reasoning, the authors provide a powerful new way to think about the RC task. This challenges the implicit assumption in the community that linguistically complex sources (like Wikipedia) automatically yield complex reasoning tasks. This is a refreshing and important perspective that could fundamentally alter how we design and evaluate RC datasets.
2.  **Insightful and Counter-intuitive Findings:** The central finding—that readability and the number of required skills are not strongly correlated—is impactful. The demonstration that a dataset like MCTest can be easy to read but require a comparable number of reasoning skills to more "difficult" datasets is a crucial insight. This work provides the vocabulary and empirical evidence to articulate why datasets like SQuAD, despite their complex text, might be "easy to answer."
3.  **Strong Interdisciplinary Grounding:** The authors' effort to base their skill taxonomy on foundational work in psychology and linguistics is commendable. This brings a welcome level of theoretical rigor to the often ad-hoc process of creating task classifications in NLP. It connects the empirical work back to the cognitive processes it aims to model, which strengthens the entire enterprise.
4.  **Practical Utility for Future Research:** The released annotations and the proposed framework have clear practical benefits. They can be used for curriculum learning (as the authors suggest), for targeted error analysis of existing models (e.g., does a model fail on questions requiring "Elaboration" but succeed on "Bridging"?), and for guiding the creation of new datasets that explicitly control for both readability and skill complexity.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Inherent Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** The annotation of prerequisite skills, while principled, is inherently subjective. The distinction between categories like "Bridging" and "Elaboration," for instance, can be fuzzy and depend on an annotator's own knowledge base. While the reported inter-annotator agreement is high, it was calculated on a small subset of the data. This methodological limitation introduces some noise into the analysis. However, for a work introducing a fundamentally new approach, this is an acceptable imperfection. The value of the conceptual framework itself outweighs the initial noise in its application.
2.  **Limited Definition of "Readability" for Machines:** The paper relies on standard, human-centric readability metrics (e.g., Flesch-Kincaid, sentence length). It is an open question whether these metrics accurately capture what makes a text difficult for a modern neural network. Factors like long-range dependencies, syntactic ambiguity, or the presence of distracting information might be more relevant "readability" measures for models. The paper acknowledges this limitation, and while it's a valid point, exploring this would be a significant research project in its own right. As a starting point, the use of established metrics is reasonable.
3.  **Analysis is Primarily Descriptive:** The paper does an excellent job of analyzing and describing existing datasets through its new lens. However, it stops short of being prescriptive. While it opens the door for ideas like curriculum learning, it does not propose a concrete methodology for how to *create* a new dataset that is well-balanced across the two axes. This is less a flaw and more of a pointer towards exciting future work that this paper enables.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Your framework is an excellent tool for analysis. Have you considered using it for validation by correlating your two axes with the actual performance of state-of-the-art models on these datasets? For instance, does model performance drop more significantly as the number of required skills increases, compared to how it drops with increasing text readability? This could provide strong evidence that your "prerequisite skills" axis truly captures a dimension of difficulty for current models.
2.  The distinction between "Bridging" (lexical/grammatical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (commonsense knowledge) is crucial but seems difficult to operationalize. Could you provide a few examples from your annotation process where this distinction was ambiguous, and walk through the heuristic or reasoning your annotators used to make a final decision?
3.  You rightly point out that your methodology does not evaluate the skill of *finding* the necessary sentences in a long context. Do you have any initial thoughts on how a third axis, perhaps "Information Localization Difficulty," could be integrated into your framework to create an even more comprehensive model of RC difficulty?
