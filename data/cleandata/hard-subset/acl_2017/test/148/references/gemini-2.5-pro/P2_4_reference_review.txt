### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on the "prerequisite skills" needed to answer questions and the "readability" of the context passages. The authors refine a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding them in cognitive psychology and textual entailment research. They also adopt a set of standard readability metrics. The core of the work is an annotation effort where 100 questions from each of six prominent RC datasets are labeled with these skills, and the corresponding texts are analyzed for readability. The main finding is that question difficulty (proxied by the number of required skills) and text readability are only weakly correlated, suggesting they are independent dimensions of challenge in RC.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **A valuable diagnostic resource:** The primary contribution is the creation and public release of annotations detailing the prerequisite cognitive skills required for six major RC datasets. This resource enables a more nuanced analysis of dataset biases and the specific capabilities of RC models, moving beyond simple accuracy scores.
2.  **An important empirical finding with clear implications:** The paper provides strong evidence that text readability and question complexity are largely decoupled. This insight is crucial for the community, informing better dataset design and suggesting that curriculum learning strategies for RC systems should consider both axes of difficulty independently.
3.  **A well-motivated and refined skill taxonomy:** The paper presents a thoughtful revision of a previous skill taxonomy, grounding it in established theories from psychology and NLP. This provides a more stable and credible foundation for future qualitative analyses of RC tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Critical Need for Nuanced Evaluation:** The field of RC is often driven by leaderboard performance based on a single accuracy metric. This work pushes in a much-needed direction: understanding *why* certain questions are hard and *what* skills are being tested. By providing a framework and a resource to analyze datasets along the dimensions of linguistic complexity and reasoning complexity, the paper facilitates a deeper understanding of both our datasets and our models.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The proposed taxonomy of prerequisite skills is not ad-hoc. The authors make a concerted effort to connect their categories to influential models of human comprehension (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and established phenomena in NLP (e.g., textual entailment categories from Dagan et al.). This theoretical grounding lends significant credibility and stability to their framework, making it more likely to be adopted and built upon.
3.  **Substantial Annotation Effort and Insightful Analysis:** The annotation of six diverse datasets is a significant undertaking that provides immediate value. The resulting analysis (Tables 2-5, Figures 3-4) offers a clear and compelling comparative view. For instance, the characterization of SQuAD as having high readability but low skill requirements, and MCTest as the opposite, quantifies a widely held intuition and provides a useful lens for selecting appropriate benchmarks for specific research goals.
4.  **Clear and Actionable Implications for Future Research:** The paper concludes with a thoughtful discussion of how these metrics can be used to guide future work. The suggestion to use this 2D space for curriculum learning—starting with easy-to-read, easy-to-answer tasks and progressing systematically—is a concrete and valuable proposal for developing more robust RC systems. The discussion on corpus genre and question types further highlights the practical utility of their findings for dataset creators.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The "Number of Skills" as a Proxy for Difficulty is a Simplification:** The central assumption that question difficulty is directly proportional to the number of required skills is a strong one. This linear model treats all skills as equally difficult and ignores potential interactions between them. For instance, a question requiring complex "Logical Reasoning" might be significantly harder than one requiring "Coreference Resolution" and "Punctuation" combined. This assumption needs further validation, for example, by correlating the skill count with actual model performance or human error rates on the annotated questions.
2.  **Limited Annotation Scale and Ambiguity in IAA Reporting:** While analyzing 100 questions per dataset provides a useful snapshot, it may not be fully representative of the diversity within these large-scale benchmarks. More critically, the inter-annotator agreement (IAA) is reported as a single 90.1% figure. This is likely simple percentage agreement, which can be misleadingly high. A chance-corrected metric like Krippendorff's Alpha would be more rigorous. Furthermore, a per-skill breakdown of IAA is essential, as some categories (e.g., the distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration") are inherently more subjective and likely to have lower agreement than others (e.g., "Mathematical Reasoning").
3.  **Subjectivity in Skill Definitions:** Despite the strong theoretical grounding, the practical application of some skill definitions remains subjective. The boundary between "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge) is notoriously fuzzy. While the authors define the distinction, the paper would be stronger if it included examples of difficult cases and a more detailed discussion of how consistency was enforced during annotation. This subjectivity could pose a challenge for others seeking to apply or extend this framework.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the calculation of the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement? Was this a chance-corrected metric (e.g., Kappa, Alpha) or simple percentage agreement? Could you also provide a breakdown of the agreement scores for each of the 13 prerequisite skills to identify which were the most and least reliable to annotate?
2.  Your core claim relies on the number of skills as a proxy for question difficulty. Have you performed any analysis to validate this assumption? For example, did you find a negative correlation between the number of required skills for a question and the performance of existing state-of-the-art RC models on that question?
3.  The distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" seems particularly subtle. Could you provide a few examples from your annotation that illustrate this boundary, especially a minimal pair where one is clearly "Bridging" and the other is "Elaboration"? How were annotators guided to handle ambiguous cases?
