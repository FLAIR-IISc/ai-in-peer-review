### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a two-dimensional framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on 1) the prerequisite skills required to answer questions and 2) the readability of the context passages. The authors refine a pre-existing taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding it in cognitive psychology and textual entailment research. They then manually annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these skills. Readability is measured using a set of 10 established linguistic features. The central finding of their analysis is that the difficulty of questions (proxied by the number of required skills) and the readability of the text are only weakly correlated. The paper concludes with a discussion on how this framework can guide future dataset creation and system development.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A comprehensive, comparative analysis of six major RC datasets using a unified, principled framework.** This is the primary contribution. The analysis yields valuable insights into the characteristics of these datasets (e.g., SQuAD texts are difficult to read, but its questions are relatively simple; MCTest texts are easy to read, but its questions can require complex reasoning). The finding of a weak correlation between readability and question complexity is a significant and non-obvious result for the community.
2.  **The refinement and application of a skill-based taxonomy for RC.** The authors improve upon previous work by grounding their skill definitions in well-established theories from psychology and NLP. This provides a more robust and principled vocabulary for discussing the challenges posed by different RC questions.
3.  **The public release of the detailed annotations.** This provides a valuable resource for other researchers to conduct fine-grained error analyses of their models or to build new, more targeted RC datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Timely Problem:** The paper addresses a critical need in the RC community. As we move beyond simply chasing state-of-the-art accuracy on leaderboards, it is crucial to understand *what* skills our models are learning and what kinds of challenges are present in our evaluation benchmarks. This work provides a concrete methodology and a valuable set of initial findings to facilitate this deeper level of analysis. The observation that text readability does not equate to question difficulty is an important one that challenges common assumptions.

2.  **Principled and Well-Grounded Framework:** The choice of the two evaluation axes—prerequisite skills and readability—is intuitive and effectively captures two distinct aspects of difficulty in RC. Crucially, this framework is not ad-hoc; it is grounded in established models of human text comprehension from cognitive psychology (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). This theoretical underpinning lends credibility and substance to the proposed metrics and subsequent analysis. The revisions to the skill taxonomy, drawing from research on textual entailment, are also a clear improvement.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Empirical Analysis:** The annotation and analysis of six different datasets is a substantial effort. The results are presented clearly and effectively, particularly in Tables 2-4 and the visualizations in Figures 3-4, which powerfully illustrate the different profiles of each dataset. The discussion in Section 6 provides concrete, actionable suggestions for how these findings can be used, for example, to guide curriculum learning strategies for model development or to inform the design of future datasets that target specific phenomena (e.g., narrative understanding).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Over-simplification of "Difficulty":** The paper's quantitative analysis hinges on the assumption that question difficulty is directly proportional to the number of required skills. While this is a reasonable starting point, it is a simplification. Not all skills are created equal; for example, `Logical reasoning` or `Elaboration` using world knowledge is likely far more difficult for current models to master than `Coreference resolution`. A question requiring one complex skill might be harder than another requiring three simple ones. This nuance is lost when difficulty is measured simply by summing the count of required skills.

2.  **Limited Scale and Generalizability of Annotations:** The analysis is based on a sample of 100 questions per dataset. While the authors justify this by citing prior work, for massive datasets like SQuAD (100k+ questions) or MS MARCO, this is a very small fraction of the data. It is unclear if this sample is truly representative of the full dataset's diversity and complexity. For instance, the findings about SQuAD being "easy to answer" might not hold across the entire dataset, especially on more challenging subsets. The generalizability of the conclusions would be stronger if supported by either a larger sample or an analysis showing the sample's representativeness.

3.  **Lack of Direct Connection to System Performance:** The paper is primarily an analysis of datasets and motivates its framework as being useful for analyzing systems. However, the work stops short of demonstrating this utility. The paper would have been significantly strengthened by including an analysis of how a standard RC model's performance correlates with the annotated skills. For instance, showing that a model's accuracy drops significantly as the number of required skills increases, or that it struggles with specific skills like `Elaboration`, would provide direct evidence for the framework's value in error analysis and would more strongly tie the two parts of the motivation together.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the assumption that the number of skills equates to difficulty: Have you considered weighting the skills differently? For instance, one could hypothesize that skills requiring external knowledge (`Elaboration`) are "harder" than those relying on intra-textual cues (`Coreference resolution`). How might a weighted sum of skills, perhaps informed by preliminary model performance or human judgment, change your overall analysis and the relative ranking of dataset difficulty?

2.  Could you elaborate on the sampling strategy and its potential impact on the conclusions? For very large and potentially heterogeneous datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO, is there a risk that the 100-question sample might not capture the full range of phenomena, potentially skewing the results (e.g., under-representing more complex, multi-hop questions)?

3.  The paper argues that the framework can be used for system development. To make this argument more concrete, would it be possible to provide a small-scale analysis of an existing RC model's performance on your annotated questions? Even a simple breakdown of a baseline model's accuracy versus the number of required skills, or its performance on questions requiring `Bridging` vs. those that do not, would be a powerful demonstration of your framework's practical utility for the community.
