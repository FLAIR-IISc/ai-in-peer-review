### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the complexity of the question, measured by a taxonomy of 13 "prerequisite skills," and the complexity of the text, measured by standard readability metrics. The authors refine a previous skill taxonomy, ground it in psychology and textual entailment literature, and use it to manually annotate 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets. Their analysis reveals the distinct characteristics of each dataset and concludes that there is only a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the difficulty of the questions posed about it.

As I see them, the contributions are:

1.  **A Publicly Released Annotated Resource:** The primary contribution is the set of annotations for 600 questions across six RC datasets. This resource, detailing the prerequisite skills required for each question, is valuable for the community and can enable more fine-grained analyses of both datasets and RC models.
2.  **A Comparative Analysis of RC Datasets:** The paper provides a systematic, comparative analysis that highlights the qualitative differences between various RC datasets (e.g., the narrative focus of MCTest vs. the factual nature of SQuAD). This characterization is useful for researchers selecting datasets for specific tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Valuable Resource Creation:** The most significant strength is the creation and public release of the skill annotations. Such resources are essential for moving beyond simple accuracy metrics and understanding what our models are truly capable of. This dataset could spur future work on model error analysis and targeted dataset creation.
2.  **Systematic and Grounded Framework:** The paper proposes a well-motivated, two-dimensional framework for analysis. Grounding the "prerequisite skills" taxonomy in established work from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment lends it credibility. This is a commendable effort to bring a more principled approach to dataset analysis.
3.  **Broad Empirical Scope:** The study covers six different and widely-used RC datasets, which vary in their domain, construction methodology, and question format. This breadth allows for a meaningful comparative analysis and makes the findings more generalizable across the RC landscape of its time.
4.  **Actionable Insights for Dataset Curation:** The analysis provides concrete takeaways for dataset creators. For instance, the finding that MCTest contains difficult questions despite having easy-to-read text serves as a good example of how to decouple these two forms of difficulty, potentially leading to better-designed and more challenging RC tasks.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Inconsistencies and Lack of Clarity:** The paper contains a major, unexplained contradiction that severely undermines its credibility. Section 5(ii) states, "QA4MRE had the highest score; on average, each question required 4.6 skills." However, Table 3 reports the average for QA4MRE as 3.25. Such a large discrepancy on a key result is unacceptable. Furthermore, the methodology for calculating "sentence distance" in Appendix B is hopelessly vague ("the distance was the sum of distances of any two sentences"), making the results in Table 6 uninterpretable. The calculation of the averages in Table 3 is also opaque; it is unclear if they are averaged over all 100 questions or only the subset deemed solvable.
2.  **Insufficient Methodological Rigor:** The annotation process is not described with sufficient rigor. The reported 90.1% inter-annotator agreement is not specified as a chance-corrected metric (e.g., Fleiss' Kappa), making it difficult to assess its true strength. More critically, the paper fails to report agreement on the initial, fundamental step of *selecting the supporting sentences*. Since all readability and distance calculations are based on this selection, its reliability is paramount. Without this information, the entire readability analysis rests on an unvalidated foundation. The sample size of 100 questions per dataset is also small, limiting the statistical power of the conclusions drawn about entire datasets.
3.  **Superficiality of Core Finding:** The central conclusion—that text readability and question difficulty are only weakly correlated—is interesting but hardly surprising. The analysis does not extend this finding in a particularly impactful way. For example, the authors do not use their rich annotations to perform a fine-grained error analysis of existing RC models to demonstrate *why* certain skills are harder than others. The discussion about curriculum learning is speculative and not supported by any experiments. The work stops at dataset characterization, missing a clear opportunity to demonstrate the utility of its own framework for model development.
4.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper is marred by a significant number of formatting and typographical errors, particularly in the References section. Multiple entries contain stray characters (e.g., `?`, `\Q2016\E`), are incomplete, or are formatted inconsistently. A reference to "Footnote 4" is made in the caption of Figure 2, but no such footnote exists. This lack of attention to detail is concerning and detracts from the paper's professionalism.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Please address the critical discrepancy regarding the average number of skills for QA4MRE, which is reported as 4.6 in the main text and 3.25 in Table 3. Which value is correct, and what is the precise formula used for its calculation?
2.  Could you provide an unambiguous definition for the calculation of "sentence distance" in Appendix B? As currently written, the method for questions requiring more than two sentences is undefined.
3.  What was the inter-annotator agreement (preferably using a chance-corrected metric) for the task of selecting the supporting sentences for each question? This is a crucial step that precedes and influences all subsequent readability and distance analyses.
