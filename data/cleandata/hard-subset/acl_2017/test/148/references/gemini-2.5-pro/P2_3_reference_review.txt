### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets, aiming to disentangle the difficulty of reading a text from the difficulty of answering a question about it. The authors propose two sets of metrics: (1) **Readability**, based on established linguistic features, to measure text complexity; and (2) **Prerequisite Skills**, a refined set of 13 reasoning types required to answer a question, grounded in cognitive psychology and textual entailment research. The authors apply this framework by manually annotating 100 questions from each of six prominent RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA). The primary finding is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and the number of skills required, suggesting these are two independent axes of difficulty.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive, multi-dimensional analysis and characterization of six popular RC datasets.** The paper's core strength is its detailed annotation and analysis, which provides the community with a clear, comparative view of what these datasets actually test. For example, it empirically demonstrates that SQuAD has high text readability but low question complexity, whereas MCTest is the inverse. This is an invaluable insight for practitioners selecting datasets for specific research goals.
2.  **A revised and well-grounded typology of "prerequisite skills" for RC.** The authors build upon previous work to create a more robust and principled skill set. By grounding categories like "Bridging" and "Elaboration" in established theories from psychology (e.g., Kintsch) and NLP (textual entailment), the framework feels less ad-hoc and more scientifically sound than simple question-type categorizations.
3.  **The release of a valuable annotation resource.** The annotated dataset, covering 600 questions across six benchmarks, is a significant contribution in itself. It will enable other researchers to conduct fine-grained error analysis, develop more targeted models, and design better-calibrated datasets in the future.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Timely Problem:** The paper addresses a critical issue in modern NLP. As models begin to saturate leaderboards, the community urgently needs more sophisticated evaluation methods that go beyond simple accuracy. This work provides a concrete methodology for analyzing what skills datasets test and what capabilities models possess, which is essential for making meaningful progress.
2.  **Principled and Intuitive Framework:** The two-dimensional framework of "Prerequisite Skills" vs. "Readability" is both intuitive and powerful. It provides a clear lens for understanding dataset design. The skill typology is carefully constructed, refining previous work and connecting it to foundational research, which gives the classification strong credibility.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation and Insightful Analysis:** The annotation of six diverse datasets is a substantial effort. The results are presented clearly and offer compelling insights. The visualization in Figure 3, plotting average skills against readability, is particularly effective at summarizing the distinct profiles of each dataset. The paper successfully uses this analysis to provide a nuanced critique and characterization of each benchmark.
4.  **Actionable Implications for the Field:** The discussion section effectively translates the empirical findings into practical recommendations for both dataset creators and system developers. Suggestions like focusing on narrative texts to elicit different reasoning skills or using the metrics to guide curriculum learning are concrete and valuable for the community.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scale of Annotation:** While analyzing six datasets is commendable, the annotation of only 100 questions per dataset is a relatively small sample. This raises concerns about how representative the findings are for the full datasets, some of which contain over 100,000 questions. Strong claims, such as the 27% "nonsense" rate for Who-did-What, are based on a small number of instances and may be susceptible to sampling bias. The generalizability of the conclusions about each dataset is therefore limited.
2.  **Subjectivity and Potential Ambiguity in Skill Annotation:** The distinction between certain skills, particularly "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge), can be highly subjective. While a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is reported, the paper does not specify the metric used (e.g., Krippendorff's alpha, which corrects for chance) or provide details on the annotation guidelines and resolution of disagreements. A more rigorous treatment of annotation reliability and a discussion of difficult edge cases would strengthen the paper's claims.
3.  **Oversimplification of "Difficulty":** The paper's analysis largely rests on the assumption that "the more skills that are required... the more difficult the question is." This is a reasonable heuristic but may not hold universally. A question requiring a single, complex instance of "Logical Reasoning" could be far more difficult for current systems than a question requiring three simpler skills like "Coreference Resolution," "Punctuation," and "Causal Relation." The framework does not account for the intrinsic difficulty of each skill, which could impact the validity of the correlation analysis.
4.  **Lack of Connection to System Performance:** The paper is titled "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension" and is motivated by the need to analyze both datasets and systems. However, the analysis is almost exclusively focused on the datasets. There is no attempt to correlate the skill requirements or readability metrics with the actual performance of state-of-the-art RC models. Connecting the proposed framework to model behavior (e.g., showing that models struggle more with questions requiring "Elaboration" or a higher number of skills) would have made the contribution significantly more impactful and directly validated the utility of the metrics.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide more detail on the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement calculation? Was a chance-corrected metric like Krippendorff's alpha or Fleiss' kappa used? It would also be helpful to see examples of disagreements, especially for potentially ambiguous categories like Bridging vs. Elaboration, and understand your resolution process.
2.  Your core assumption is that the number of skills correlates with question difficulty. Have you considered validating this assumption, for example, by correlating the skill count with human response times or, more importantly, with error rates from existing RC models? Do you believe all skills contribute equally to difficulty?
3.  The sample size of 100 questions per dataset is a potential limitation. How did you ensure that the samples were representative, especially for large and diverse datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO? Have you considered reporting confidence intervals for your main findings (e.g., the frequency of skills in Table 2) to reflect the uncertainty from sampling?
