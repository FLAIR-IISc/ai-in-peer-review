**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper introduces a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two primary axes: the "prerequisite skills" required to answer a question and the "readability" of the associated context text. The authors refine a pre-existing taxonomy of prerequisite skills, grounding it more firmly in theories of human text comprehension from psychology and research on textual entailment. This framework is then used to manually annotate 100 questions from each of six prominent RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA). The analysis of these annotations reveals distinct characteristics of each dataset and demonstrates that there is only a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the complexity of the questions asked about it, where complexity is measured by the number of required skills. The authors make their annotations publicly available.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive, comparative analysis of six RC datasets using a unified annotation framework.** This empirical study provides valuable insights into the differing challenges posed by popular benchmarks. The results quantify properties of these datasets (e.g., SQuAD texts are difficult to read but questions are relatively simple; MCTest texts are simple but questions require more complex reasoning), offering an important resource for researchers choosing datasets for evaluation or designing new ones.
2.  **A refined, theory-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for reading comprehension.** Building on prior work, the authors introduce a more structured and nuanced classification, particularly for knowledge-based reasoning, by drawing parallels to concepts like bridging and elaboration inferences from cognitive psychology. This provides a more principled tool for analyzing the reasoning capabilities required by RC tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and principled framework.** The paper effectively motivates the need for a deeper analysis of RC datasets beyond superficial metrics like question type. The proposed two-axis evaluation (prerequisite skills vs. readability) is grounded in established cognitive psychology literature (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), lending theoretical soundness to the approach. The reorganization of skills, especially the distinction between bridging and elaboration, is clear and well-justified.

2.  **Thorough and insightful empirical analysis.** The core strength of the paper lies in its application of the framework to six diverse datasets. The results presented in Tables 2, 3, and 4, and visualized in Figure 3, provide a clear, quantitative comparison that highlights the unique profile of each dataset. This analysis is directly useful to the community, helping researchers understand what skills their models are being tested on and guiding the selection of appropriate datasets for specific research goals (e.g., a researcher focused on multi-sentence reasoning might prefer MCTest over SQuAD).

3.  **Significant finding on the decoupling of readability and question complexity.** The paper provides strong empirical evidence for the claim that text readability is not a reliable proxy for question difficulty. The finding that an easy-to-read corpus like MCTest can contain questions requiring a comparable or greater number of reasoning skills than a corpus with high lexical and syntactic complexity like SQuAD is an important observation for the field. This has direct implications for dataset creation and for curriculum learning approaches to training RC models.

4.  **Public release of annotations.** The commitment to making the annotation results publicly available is a significant strength. This resource enables other researchers to verify the findings, conduct new analyses, or use the skill-annotated data to train and evaluate models in a more fine-grained manner, increasing the paper's potential impact.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Potential for subjectivity in the annotation scheme.** While the authors report a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), the definitions for several key skills remain open to subjective interpretation. The distinction between "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge) is particularly fine-grained and could be difficult for annotators to apply consistently. Without a more detailed report on the agreement metric used (e.g., Cohen's Kappa, which adjusts for chance agreement) and an analysis of common disagreements, the robustness of the annotation process is difficult to fully assess.

2.  **Operationalization of "difficulty" is simplistic.** The paper's primary measure for question difficulty is a simple count of the number of prerequisite skills required. This assumes that all skills are of equal difficulty. This is a strong, unverified assumption. It is plausible that a single instance of "Elaboration" requiring external world knowledge is significantly more challenging for current models than a combination of "Coreference resolution" and "Punctuation". This simplification limits the depth of the difficulty analysis.

3.  **Small sample size per dataset.** The analysis is based on a random sample of 100 questions from each dataset. While this may be sufficient for identifying broad trends, it is a very small fraction of large-scale datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO. It is not demonstrated that this sample is representative of the full dataset's distribution of challenges, which may introduce sampling bias and limit the generalizability of the conclusions drawn for each specific dataset.

4.  **Readability metrics are human-centric.** The paper correctly notes that the chosen readability metrics were developed to measure difficulty for human readers and may not correlate with difficulty for machine learning models. This is a significant caveat. For modern transformer-based models, factors like long-range dependencies, paraphrasing, or lexical diversity might be better indicators of text difficulty than sentence length or syllable counts. The analysis would be strengthened by a discussion of, or comparison with, metrics more tailored to machine processing.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement: Could you clarify how this was calculated? Is it simple accuracy on a per-skill, per-question basis? Could you also report a chance-corrected agreement score like Cohen's Kappa to provide a more robust measure of reliability?

2.  The paper defines question difficulty as the number of required skills. Did you consider alternative ways to model difficulty, for instance, by assigning different weights to skills based on a presumed hierarchy (e.g., knowledge-based skills being "harder" than syntactic ones)? How might this change your conclusions?

3.  The distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" appears crucial yet potentially ambiguous. Could you provide a few examples from your annotation process that were difficult to categorize and explain the reasoning that led to the final decision? This would help clarify the boundary between these two important skills.
