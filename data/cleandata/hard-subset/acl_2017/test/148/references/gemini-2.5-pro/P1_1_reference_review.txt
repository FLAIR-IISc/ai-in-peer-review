### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a framework for analyzing Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the complexity of the reasoning required to answer a question ("prerequisite skills") and the linguistic complexity of the source text ("readability"). The authors refine a prior typology of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding it in theories from psychology and textual entailment. They then conduct a manual annotation study, labeling 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets with these skills. The primary finding from their analysis is that readability and question complexity (measured by the number of required skills) are only weakly correlated. This suggests they are independent dimensions of difficulty, challenging the implicit assumption that linguistically complex texts automatically lead to more challenging RC tasks.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive, multi-dataset annotation and analysis.** The core of the paper is the manual annotation of six diverse RC datasets using a single, unified framework. This enables a direct, qualitative comparison that reveals unique characteristics of each dataset (e.g., SQuAD having high readability but low skill requirements vs. MCTest having low readability but more complex reasoning). The public release of these annotations is a valuable resource for the community.
2.  **An empirical demonstration that text readability and question complexity are largely independent dimensions of difficulty.** The paper provides concrete evidence across multiple datasets that difficult-to-read text does not imply difficult-to-answer questions, and vice-versa. This is an important insight for the field, encouraging a more nuanced approach to dataset creation and model evaluation.
3.  **A refined and well-grounded typology of prerequisite skills for RC.** The authors improve upon prior work by reorganizing the skill set, especially for knowledge reasoning, and justifying the categories with established theories from psychology (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration). This provides a more principled and useful vocabulary for discussing the reasoning capabilities tested by RC tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Critical and Nuanced Research Question:** The paper tackles the crucial question of what makes an RC dataset "difficult." By systematically disentangling text complexity from reasoning complexity, it moves the conversation beyond simple accuracy metrics. This provides the community with a much-needed analytical tool to understand dataset biases, assess the true capabilities of models, and guide the creation of more challenging and balanced benchmarks.
2.  **Principled and Well-Motivated Methodology:** The framework is built on a solid foundation. The refinement of the "prerequisite skills" by drawing on established cognitive models of human comprehension (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) adds significant credibility and theoretical grounding to the typology. Similarly, leveraging well-studied readability metrics from prior NLP work is a sound and effective choice for quantifying text difficulty.
3.  **Broad and Insightful Comparative Analysis:** The analysis spans six diverse and widely-used datasets, offering a valuable comparative overview of the RC landscape. The findings are clearly presented and compelling; for example, the scatter plots in Figures 3 and 4 effectively visualize the main conclusion. The per-dataset breakdown provides actionable insights for researchers (e.g., identifying MCTest as a good benchmark for multi-sentence reasoning on simple text, or highlighting the high rate of "nonsense" questions in MS MARCO).
4.  **Valuable Resource for the Community:** The public release of the annotations for 600 question-context pairs is a tangible and useful contribution. This resource can enable a wide range of future work, including meta-analyses of model errors, targeted training of models on specific skills, and principled curriculum learning strategies.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scale of Annotation:** The analysis and conclusions for each dataset are based on a sample of only 100 questions. While this is a significant manual annotation effort, it is a very small fraction of datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO, which contain over 100,000 questions. This raises concerns about how representative the reported skill distributions and readability scores are for the full datasets. The paper draws strong conclusions about the overall character of each dataset, which may not be fully supported by such a small sample.
2.  **Potential Subjectivity and Underspecified Reliability of Skill Annotation:** The annotation of prerequisite skills is inherently subjective. The distinction between categories like `Bridging` (lexical/grammatical knowledge) and `Elaboration` (commonsense knowledge) can be blurry. While the paper reports a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), crucial details are missing. It is unclear if this is a simple percentage agreement or a chance-corrected metric like Cohen's/Fleiss' Kappa, the latter being standard for such tasks. A more detailed discussion of the annotation guidelines, difficult cases, and the resolution process would strengthen the validity of this core methodology.
3.  **Oversimplification of "Difficulty" as the Number of Skills:** A central assumption of the analysis is that question difficulty directly correlates with the number of required skills. This is a plausible but unproven simplification. It is likely that skills are not equally difficult; for example, a question requiring complex `Logical Reasoning` may be harder for current systems than one requiring `Coreference Resolution` and `Causal Relation`. This simplification could impact the validity of the difficulty rankings. The analysis would be more robust if it acknowledged this limitation or explored alternative weighting schemes.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide more detail on the inter-annotator agreement calculation? Specifically, was the 90.1% figure a chance-corrected metric (e.g., Fleiss' Kappa)? And was agreement calculated on the presence/absence of each of the 13 skills individually, or on the exact match of the entire set of skill labels for a given question?
2.  Your analysis equates question difficulty with the raw count of required skills. Did you consider that different skills might have different intrinsic difficulties for models or humans? How might weighting the skills (perhaps informed by existing model performance on questions requiring single skills) change your findings about the relative difficulty of the datasets?
3.  Given the small sample size of 100 questions per dataset, how confident are you that your findings generalize to the full datasets? Have you considered performing any statistical analysis, such as bootstrapping, to estimate confidence intervals for the reported averages (e.g., average number of skills in Table 3) to better quantify the uncertainty introduced by sampling?
