### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the difficulty of answering questions and the readability of the source text. To measure question difficulty, the authors refine a pre-existing taxonomy of 13 "prerequisite skills" (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging inference) grounded in psychology and textual entailment research. To measure text difficulty, they adopt 10 established readability metrics. The authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) with the required skills. Their analysis reveals the distribution of these skills across datasets and demonstrates a weak correlation between the number of required skills (their proxy for question difficulty) and text readability. The primary output is the annotated dataset, which is made publicly available.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Multi-Dataset Annotation Resource:** The most significant contribution is the creation and public release of annotations for 600 RC questions across six diverse datasets. This resource provides a fine-grained mapping of questions to a detailed set of cognitive and linguistic skills, which could be valuable for detailed error analysis of existing RC models.
2.  **Empirical Analysis of Dataset Characteristics:** The paper provides a quantitative analysis that characterizes and contrasts existing RC datasets. The finding that text readability is a poor predictor of question complexity is a useful, if not entirely surprising, empirical result that highlights the need for more nuanced evaluation.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses a Clear Need:** The work is well-motivated. The field of RC has moved towards large-scale datasets where performance is often measured by a single accuracy score. This paper correctly argues for a more detailed, qualitative analysis to understand what skills our models are truly acquiring and where datasets have shortcomings.
2.  **Broad Empirical Scope:** The analysis covers six different RC datasets that vary in their source material (e.g., Wikipedia, news, children's stories) and construction methodology. This breadth lends more weight to the paper's conclusions about the general properties of RC tasks and makes the resulting annotated resource more broadly useful.
3.  **Grounded in Prior Research:** The authors attempt to ground their taxonomy of skills in established concepts from psychology (Kintsch, 1988; McNamara & Magliano, 2009) and NLP (textual entailment). This provides a theoretical basis for their framework, which is preferable to a purely ad-hoc categorization.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Crucial Lack of External Validation:** The paper's central premise rests on a critical, unproven assumption: "we assume that the number of required skills corresponds to the difficulty of a question." This is the core metric for question difficulty, yet it is never validated against actual model performance. The authors do not show that questions annotated with more skills are, in fact, harder for state-of-the-art RC systems to answer. Without this correlation, the proposed difficulty metric is merely a hypothesis, and the conclusions drawn from it are speculative. This is a significant methodological omission that undermines the paper's primary claims about measuring question difficulty.
2.  **Subjectivity and Ambiguity of the Skill Taxonomy:** The proposed 13 skills, while based on prior work, contain significant potential for ambiguity and subjective interpretation during annotation. The distinction between "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge) is notoriously fuzzy and context-dependent. While an overall inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% is reported, this raw percentage can be misleading for a multi-label task with many categories. A more rigorous metric like Cohen's Kappa and a per-category agreement analysis would be necessary to establish the reliability of this complex annotation schema.
3.  **Limited Novelty of the Framework:** The paper frames its contribution as adopting and organizing evaluation metrics. The "prerequisite skills" are an explicit refinement of a previous work by some of the same authors (Sugawara et al., 2017), and the readability metrics are directly taken from an established source (Vajjala and Meurers, 2012). While the synthesis and application to new datasets is a contribution, the conceptual novelty of the framework itself is limited.
4.  **Descriptive rather than Demonstrative Utility:** The paper provides a descriptive "what" (these datasets require these skills) but fails to demonstrate the "so what." The discussion section suggests potential applications like curriculum learning for RC systems, but this is entirely speculative. The paper would be much stronger if it included even a small-scale experiment showing that this analysis leads to tangible benefits, for example, by demonstrating that a model's performance on a specific skill (e.g., "Logical reasoning") can be predicted by its proportion in the training data, or that training on a "curriculum" informed by this analysis improves final performance.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The central claim of the paper is that the number of required skills is a proxy for question difficulty. Have you performed any analysis to correlate the number of skills you annotated for a question with the performance of existing, published RC models on that same question? If not, how can you be confident that your metric is a valid measure of difficulty for current systems?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of your inter-annotator agreement? Specifically, which skills had the lowest agreement, and was a more robust metric like Cohen's Kappa calculated? The distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" seems particularly challenging.
3.  The "Nonsense" label is assigned to a significant portion of questions in some datasets (e.g., 27% in WDW, 14% in MARCO). What were your guidelines for distinguishing a "Nonsense" question from a solvable question that simply requires "Elaboration" using external world knowledge not present in the text? This distinction seems critical for dataset quality assessment.
