### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a two-dimensional framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on the distinction between the difficulty of reading a text (readability) and the complexity of answering a question about it (prerequisite skills). The authors refine a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding them in theories from cognitive psychology and textual entailment. They apply this framework by manually annotating 100 questions from each of six major RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA). The central finding is that readability and question complexity are only weakly correlated, meaning texts that are easy to read can pose difficult questions, and vice versa.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:
1.  **A valuable new annotated resource:** The manual annotation of 600 questions across six diverse RC datasets provides a unique resource for the community. This enables fine-grained analysis of dataset characteristics and model performance far beyond simple accuracy metrics.
2.  **An important empirical finding:** The paper provides strong evidence that text readability is a poor proxy for task difficulty in RC. This insight is crucial for both dataset creators and practitioners, challenging common assumptions and highlighting the need for more nuanced evaluation.
3.  **A refined skill taxonomy:** The proposed set of 13 prerequisite skills is a principled and useful refinement of previous work, particularly in its categorization of knowledge reasoning skills (Bridging, Elaboration), which are grounded in established cognitive theories.

### Strengths

1.  **Timely and impactful research direction:** The RC field is often driven by leaderboard performance on aggregate metrics. This work provides a much-needed methodology for a more qualitative and diagnostic evaluation. By breaking down the RC task into constituent skills and separating it from text complexity, the paper helps pave the way for a more scientific understanding of what current models can and cannot do, which is essential for building more robust and generalizable systems.
2.  **Well-grounded framework:** The proposed evaluation framework is intuitive and well-motivated. Grounding the distinction between prerequisite skills and readability in established models of human comprehension (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) lends theoretical weight to the approach. This connection to cognitive science makes the work more principled than a purely ad-hoc categorization.
3.  **Broad and insightful empirical analysis:** The study's analysis across six diverse datasets (from technical documents to children's stories, from expert-authored to crowdsourced questions) provides a comprehensive snapshot of the RC landscape. The resulting characterizations are insightful (e.g., SQuAD is "hard to read but easy to answer," MCTest is "easy to read but difficult to answer"). These findings offer practical guidance to researchers on selecting appropriate datasets for specific research goals.
4.  **Publicly available data:** The authors' commitment to releasing their annotations is a significant strength. This allows for the replication of their results and, more importantly, empowers other researchers to perform fine-grained error analysis on their own models, test hypotheses about specific reasoning skills, or build models that explicitly target these skills.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unvalidated assumption of difficulty:** The paper's primary measure of question difficulty is the *number* of required prerequisite skills. This is a strong and unverified assumption. A question requiring a single, complex logical inference may be significantly harder for current models than one requiring three simpler skills like coreference resolution, punctuation understanding, and spatiotemporal relations. The paper lacks an external validation of this central assumption, for instance, by showing a correlation between the skill count and the error rates of state-of-the-art models or human response times. Without this, "number of skills" is merely a measure of multiplicity, not necessarily difficulty.
2.  **Limited scale and potential sampling bias:** The analysis relies on a sample of 100 questions per dataset. While sufficient for initial analysis, this is a very small fraction of large-scale datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO, and the conclusions might not generalize to the full datasets. The paper reports a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%), but this is calculated on an even smaller subset (62 questions in total), and the metric used is not specified. Given the inherent subjectivity in classifying skills like "Bridging" vs. "Elaboration", a more detailed discussion of the annotation process and potential limitations would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Exclusion of the "search" aspect of RC:** The annotation methodology requires annotators to first select the sentences needed to answer the question. As the authors acknowledge, this deliberately ignores the crucial challenge of identifying relevant information within a larger context, which is a primary difficulty in many modern RC tasks. While this choice allows for a focused analysis of inference, it provides an incomplete picture of overall task difficulty, particularly for datasets with long contexts like SQuAD. The analysis of sentence distance in the discussion feels like a brief add-on rather than an integral part of the evaluation framework.
4.  **Coarseness of some skill definitions:** Some skill categories appear overly broad, limiting their analytical power. For example, `Bridging` is required in a very high percentage of questions across most datasets (e.g., 69% in QA4MRE, 59% in Who-did-What). This suggests it may be a catch-all category for various lexical and grammatical inferences. Breaking this down into more specific sub-skills (e.g., synonymy/hypernymy, paraphrasing, thematic role inference) could yield more granular insights.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the inter-annotator agreement of 90.1%: Could you clarify the specific metric used for this calculation (e.g., pairwise agreement, Fleiss' Kappa)? Given the multi-label annotation task, a chance-corrected metric like Kappa would be more informative.
2.  The core assumption that question difficulty is proportional to the number of required skills is central to your claims. Have you considered validating this assumption empirically? For example, one could analyze the performance of a state-of-the-art RC model on the annotated questions and check if there is a positive correlation between the number of skills you assigned and the model's error rate.
3.  The `Bridging` skill has a very high frequency, suggesting its definition might be too broad. Do you believe this category is sufficiently descriptive, or did you consider sub-dividing it into more specific types of lexical/grammatical inference during your annotation process? A finer-grained analysis of this skill could be particularly valuable.
