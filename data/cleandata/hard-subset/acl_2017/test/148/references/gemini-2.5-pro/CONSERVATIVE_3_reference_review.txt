**Summary of the paper**
This paper proposes a framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the difficulty of answering questions and the readability of the source text. To measure question difficulty, the authors propose a taxonomy of 13 "prerequisite skills" (e.g., Coreference resolution, Bridging, Elaboration), refining a previous classification. To measure text difficulty, they adopt 10 established readability metrics. The authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets with their skill taxonomy. The primary finding from their analysis is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and the number of skills required to answer a question, suggesting these are two distinct dimensions of difficulty in RC tasks.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the manual annotation of 600 questions across six diverse RC datasets using a detailed skill-based taxonomy. The public release of these annotations provides a valuable resource for researchers looking to perform fine-grained analysis of model performance or to better understand the composition of existing benchmarks.
2.  The paper presents a compelling empirical finding that text readability (e.g., Flesch-Kincaid grade level) does not strongly correlate with question complexity (as measured by the authors' framework). This is a useful insight that challenges the intuitive assumption that syntactically complex texts automatically lead to more difficult reasoning tasks.

**Strengths**
1.  **Valuable Dataset Analysis and Resource:** The paper's core strength lies in its systematic annotation and analysis of multiple RC datasets. By applying a consistent framework across six benchmarks, the authors provide a useful comparative overview (Tables 2-4) that highlights their distinct characteristics. For instance, the analysis showing SQuAD has high readability scores but a low average number of required skills aligns with the community's understanding that many SQuAD questions are single-sentence lookups. The release of these annotations is a commendable contribution.
2.  **Grounded in Established Theories:** The authors ground their two-pronged approach in established work from psychology (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (Vajjala & Meurers for readability). This provides a solid theoretical foundation for their investigation, particularly for the well-justified choice of readability metrics.
3.  **Important High-Level Insight:** The central conclusion—that question difficulty and text readability are largely orthogonal—is an important one for the field. It provides quantitative evidence for a phenomenon that many researchers may have suspected and has direct implications for dataset design. It suggests, for example, that creating truly challenging RC tasks is more about designing complex questions than simply sourcing text from complex domains.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **The Proposed Skill Taxonomy Lacks Rigorous Validation:** The central methodological innovation of this paper is the 13-skill taxonomy. While based on prior work, its refinement appears ad-hoc and lacks validation. The definitions for several skills are subjective, which raises concerns about the reliability and reproducibility of the annotation process. For instance, the distinction between "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge) is notoriously difficult to draw consistently. A more rigorous process for developing and validating the taxonomy (e.g., through multiple rounds of refinement with annotators, or by demonstrating its superiority over the original taxonomy) is needed before it can be considered an established method. The reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% on a small subset (62 questions) is insufficient without specifying the metric used (e.g., a chance-corrected score like Krippendorff's alpha is necessary) and the process for achieving it.
2.  **The "Number of Skills = Difficulty" Assumption is Unproven and Oversimplified:** A fundamental assumption underpinning the entire analysis is that the difficulty of a question is directly proportional to the number of prerequisite skills it requires. This is a strong claim that is never empirically verified. It seems highly unlikely that all 13 skills contribute equally to the cognitive load or computational complexity of a question. For example, is a question requiring "Punctuation" and "Coreference resolution" truly as difficult as one requiring "Mathematical reasoning" and "Logical reasoning"? Without evidence correlating the skill count with an external measure of difficulty—such as human response time, human error rates, or the error rates of state-of-the-art models—the claims about the relative difficulty of datasets (e.g., QA4MRE being the most difficult) rest on an unproven premise.
3.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is based on a small sample of 100 questions per dataset, which may not be representative for large-scale corpora like SQuAD or MS MARCO. Furthermore, the authors explicitly state their methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting the necessary sentences from a larger context. This is a significant omission, as the information retrieval aspect is a core challenge in many modern RC tasks. By only analyzing pre-selected evidence sentences, the study provides a limited view of the overall task difficulty. The analysis in Table 6 feels like an afterthought and does not fully address this limitation.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The distinction between several skills, particularly "Bridging" and "Elaboration," seems highly subjective. Could you elaborate on the guidelines and training provided to annotators to ensure these distinctions were made reliably? More importantly, what chance-corrected inter-annotator agreement metric (e.g., Krippendorff's alpha) was used to calculate the 90.1% score? A simple percentage agreement can be misleadingly high.
2.  Your main analysis of question difficulty relies on the core assumption that difficulty is a linear sum of the number of skills. What evidence can you provide to support this assumption? Have you considered correlating your skill counts with external measures of difficulty, such as the performance of established RC models on these specific questions, to validate that a higher skill count indeed corresponds to a harder question?
3.  Given that you only annotated 100 questions per dataset, how can you be sure your findings (e.g., the distribution of skills in Table 2) are representative of the full datasets, some of which contain over 100,000 questions? A statistical significance analysis would strengthen your claims.
