### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the "prerequisite skills" required to answer questions and the "readability" of the context passages. The authors refine a previous taxonomy of prerequisite skills, grounding it in cognitive psychology and textual entailment literature, resulting in 13 distinct skills. They also adopt a standard set of 10 readability metrics. The main contribution is the application of this framework to annotate and analyze 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA). The key empirical finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the complexity of the questions asked about it, challenging the implicit assumption that difficult texts lead to difficult questions.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A large-scale, multi-dataset analysis that empirically demonstrates the decoupling of text readability and question complexity.** This is a significant finding for the community, providing a more nuanced understanding of what makes an RC task difficult and highlighting that some datasets with complex source texts (e.g., SQuAD) may contain relatively simple reasoning tasks.
2.  **A refined and well-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills.** By drawing from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara) and NLP research on textual entailment, the authors create a more principled classification than previous work. The distinction between `Bridging` (lexical/grammatical inference) and `Elaboration` (world knowledge) is particularly valuable.
3.  **The public release of the annotations.** This dataset of annotations across six benchmarks is a valuable resource for future research, enabling fine-grained error analysis, targeted model development, and more informed dataset creation.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Clear Central Thesis:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introductory example comparing a SQuAD question to an MCTest question (Figure 1) perfectly illustrates the core problem: readability is not a proxy for question difficulty. This clear, compelling argument is carried through the entire paper and is strongly supported by the final results (Figures 3 & 4, Table 5).
2.  **Principled Methodological Framework:** The decision to ground the two evaluation classes ("prerequisite skills" and "readability") in the well-established C-I model from psychology is a major strength. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for the analysis, elevating it from an ad-hoc categorization to a principled framework. This strengthens the credibility and potential longevity of the proposed metrics.
3.  **Thorough and Insightful Empirical Analysis:** The annotation of six diverse datasets is a substantial effort. The results are presented clearly and offer valuable insights into the character of each dataset. For example, the analysis confirms intuitions that SQuAD questions often require minimal reasoning despite the difficult source text, while showing that MCTest, despite its simple language, requires a richer mix of skills like coreference and causal reasoning. The supplementary analysis of required sentence distance (Table 6) is a nice addition that further characterizes the datasets.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** This work provides a practical toolkit and a conceptual lens for both dataset creators and system developers. It offers a clear path toward building more challenging and balanced datasets by consciously manipulating both readability and skill requirements. The idea of using this framework for curriculum learning is a compelling future direction.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Reporting of Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA):** The paper reports a high raw agreement of 90.1%. However, for a multi-label annotation task where the "not required" category is likely dominant for most skills, raw agreement can be an inflated and misleading metric. A chance-corrected coefficient such as Fleiss' Kappa or Krippendorff's Alpha is the standard and necessary metric to properly assess the reliability of the annotation scheme. Without it, it is difficult to be fully confident in the robustness of the labels and, by extension, the analysis built upon them.
2.  **Oversimplification of "Difficulty":** The study's primary measure of question difficulty is the raw count of required prerequisite skills. This assumes all skills are equally difficult for a system to master, which is unlikely to be true. For example, `Elaboration` (requiring external world knowledge) is arguably a much harder challenge for current models than `Punctuation` or simple `Coreference resolution`. A more nuanced discussion of this assumption is warranted. The analysis could be strengthened by exploring a weighted sum of skills, even if the weights were heuristically determined.
3.  **Lack of Connection to Actual System Performance:** The paper is framed as providing "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension" and is motivated by the need to better assess systems. However, the analysis remains entirely focused on the datasets themselves. The paper would be significantly more impactful if it correlated the presence of certain skills (or the number of skills) with the performance of existing state-of-the-art models. For instance, demonstrating that models disproportionately fail on questions requiring `Logical reasoning` or `Elaboration` would provide direct validation for the utility of this skill taxonomy in explaining and diagnosing system failures.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a chance-corrected inter-annotator agreement score (e.g., Fleiss' Kappa) for your annotation task? This would provide a much stronger basis for assessing the reliability and reproducibility of your proposed skill taxonomy.
2.  The study equates question difficulty with the number of required skills. Have you considered that some skills might be intrinsically harder than others? Could you comment on how a potential weighting of skills (e.g., based on frequency in pre-training data or observed model error rates) might change your analysis of dataset difficulty?
3.  The analysis of required sentence distance in Table 6 is very interesting. Did you observe any correlation between the number/distance of required sentences and the number/type of required prerequisite skills? For example, do questions requiring multiple, distant sentences also tend to require more complex skills like `Bridging` or `Elaboration`?
