### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, two-dimensional framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets. Instead of relying on superficial metrics like question type, the authors propose analyzing datasets along two axes: (1) the **prerequisite skills** required to answer a question, representing the cognitive difficulty of the reasoning task, and (2) the **readability** of the context, representing the linguistic complexity of the text. The authors refine a previous taxonomy of prerequisite skills, grounding it in cognitive psychology and textual entailment research. They then manually annotate 100 questions from each of six major RC datasets using this framework and analyze the results, revealing a key finding: the readability of a text and the difficulty of the questions about it are only weakly correlated.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A New Conceptual Framework for RC Evaluation:** The primary contribution is the bold proposal to decouple the evaluation of question difficulty from text readability. This is a significant and insightful shift in perspective that challenges implicit assumptions in the field and provides a more nuanced way to understand the challenges posed by different RC tasks.
2.  **A Principled Taxonomy of Prerequisite Skills:** The paper develops a comprehensive, 13-skill taxonomy for RC. By grounding this classification in established theories from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (textual entailment), the authors move beyond ad-hoc categorizations to create a more robust and theoretically sound tool for analysis.
3.  **An Empirical Analysis of Major RC Datasets:** The application of this framework to six datasets provides the community with a valuable new lens through which to compare these resources. The resulting analysis yields non-obvious insights (e.g., SQuAD is hard to read but easy to answer; MCTest is easy to read but requires complex reasoning) that have direct implications for future dataset creation and system development. The public release of these annotations is a valuable resource in itself.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** This paper presents a genuinely new idea. The central thesis—that we must distinguish between the difficulty of reading a text and the difficulty of reasoning about it—is simple, elegant, and powerful. It provides the field with a much-needed vocabulary and framework for discussing dataset design and model capabilities in a more sophisticated way. This work moves the conversation from "which dataset is hardest?" to "in what ways is this dataset hard?". This is exactly the kind of conceptual leap our community should be encouraging.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The authors do an excellent job of connecting their proposed framework to foundational work in cognitive psychology and discourse comprehension. By explicitly building on models like Kintsch's Construction-Integration model and distinctions like bridging vs. elaboration, they lend significant credibility and depth to their taxonomy of skills. This is not just an arbitrary list of phenomena; it's a principled attempt to operationalize theories of human comprehension for the evaluation of machine comprehension.
3.  **Impactful and Actionable Findings:** The analysis is not merely descriptive; it produces clear, surprising, and useful results. The demonstration that readability and skill-based difficulty are not strongly correlated is a major finding. This insight explains why a model trained on Wikipedia (SQuAD) might struggle with a children's story (MCTest). The authors' discussion on using this framework for curriculum learning is particularly compelling, suggesting a concrete path forward for building more robust and generalizable RC systems by systematically exposing them to different quadrants of the readability-difficulty space. The qualitative characterization of each of the six datasets is a significant service to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Question Difficulty is Oversimplified as a "Skill Count":** The paper's primary methodological assumption is that the difficulty of a question is proportional to the *number* of prerequisite skills required. This is a significant simplification. It implicitly treats all skills as equally difficult, which is unlikely to be true. A question requiring a single, complex "Logical Reasoning" step may be far more difficult than a question requiring "Coreference Resolution," "Punctuation," and "Clause Relation." This simplification, while pragmatic for the analysis, potentially masks important nuances in what makes a question truly challenging.
2.  **Analysis Excludes the "Search" Aspect of Reading Comprehension:** The annotation methodology requires annotators to first identify the necessary sentences and then annotate the skills required to reason over them. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but its importance should be emphasized. A huge part of the RC challenge, especially in long-context datasets, is identifying the relevant snippets of information. By isolating the post-retrieval reasoning process, the analysis provides an incomplete picture of overall task difficulty. For example, SQuAD's low average skill count might be offset by the difficulty of locating the single correct sentence in a long paragraph, a challenge this analysis does not capture.
3.  **Potential Ambiguity in Skill Definitions:** While the taxonomy is well-grounded, the practical boundaries between certain skills can be fuzzy. The distinction between "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge) is a classic example of a continuum rather than a sharp dichotomy. While the reported inter-annotator agreement is high, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of these ambiguous cases and the specific guidelines used to resolve them, which would improve the reusability of the proposed framework.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The core of your difficulty metric is the number of skills required. Could you elaborate on the validity of this assumption? Have you considered alternative metrics, such as a weighted sum where skills are assigned different difficulty scores (perhaps derived empirically from model performance or human response times)? How might such a change affect your conclusions about the relative difficulty of the datasets?
2.  Your analysis focuses on the "entailment" aspect of RC, given the relevant sentences. How do you see your framework being extended or combined with other metrics to also account for the "search" or "information retrieval" component of the task, which you acknowledge is not covered? For instance, how would your characterization of SQuAD (low skill requirement) change if you could quantify the difficulty of locating the answer span?
