**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the difficulty of answering questions and the difficulty of reading the text. For question difficulty, the authors refine a previous taxonomy of "prerequisite skills" into a set of 13 skills grounded in psychology and textual entailment literature. For text difficulty, they employ 10 established readability metrics. The authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six major RC datasets with the required prerequisite skills. Their analysis reveals that the number of skills required to answer a question (a proxy for question difficulty) has only a weak correlation with the readability of the source text. The paper concludes with a discussion of the characteristics of each dataset and suggests potential uses for this framework in system development, such as curriculum learning.

**Contributions**

1.  **A Publicly Released Dataset Annotation:** The primary contribution is the manual annotation of 600 questions across six prominent RC datasets using a detailed, 13-category skill taxonomy. This resource could be valuable for the community to perform more fine-grained analyses of RC models and datasets.
2.  **Empirical Analysis of Dataset Characteristics:** The paper provides a systematic, comparative analysis of six RC datasets. The key finding—that text readability and question complexity are not strongly correlated—is an important, quantified insight for the field.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper tackles the crucial need for deeper evaluation metrics in RC beyond simple accuracy or superficial question types. Decomposing task difficulty into text-level and reasoning-level challenges is a sensible and important direction for understanding model capabilities and dataset biases.
2.  **Grounded in Established Theory:** The authors make a commendable effort to ground their skill taxonomy in established theories from cognitive psychology (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and prior NLP work on textual entailment. Similarly, the use of well-established readability metrics from Vajjala and Meurers (2012) for the second axis of analysis is methodologically sound.
3.  **Broad Empirical Scope:** The analysis covers six diverse and widely used RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MS MARCO, NewsQA). This breadth provides a valuable cross-dataset comparison and helps generalize the findings about the landscape of current RC challenges.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Rigor and Subjectivity of the Skill Framework:** The central contribution of the revised "prerequisite skills" taxonomy is not sufficiently validated. The definitions of several skills appear ambiguous and potentially overlapping, raising concerns about the reliability and reproducibility of the annotation process. For instance, the distinction between "Bridging" (inferences from grammatical/lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (inferences from general/commonsense knowledge) is notoriously difficult to operationalize and is likely a source of annotator disagreement. The reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% on a small sample (62 questions) is not sufficiently detailed (e.g., simple agreement vs. a chance-corrected metric like Kappa) to instill confidence in the robustness of this new framework.
2.  **Incomplete Model of Task Difficulty:** The methodology explicitly sidesteps a core component of reading comprehension: locating the relevant information within a larger context. By instructing annotators to first select the sentences needed for answering, the analysis ignores the search and retrieval aspect of the task. This is a significant simplification, as a question requiring synthesis of information from distant sentences (as noted for QA4MRE) is arguably much harder than one whose answer is in a single, adjacent sentence (as noted for SQuAD), even if the number of "skills" applied to the selected text is the same. The brief analysis of sentence distance in the discussion feels disconnected from the main difficulty metric.
3.  **Oversimplified Difficulty Metric:** The paper operationalizes question difficulty as a simple, unweighted sum of the number of required skills. This assumes that each skill contributes equally to the difficulty of a question, which is a highly questionable premise. For example, it is unlikely that understanding "Punctuation" presents the same level of challenge as performing "Logical Reasoning" or "Elaboration". This simplification limits the credibility of the subsequent correlation analysis and the claims about relative dataset difficulty.
4.  **Lack of Demonstrated Utility:** The authors suggest their framework could be used for guiding system development, such as in curriculum learning. However, this remains pure speculation. The paper does not provide any empirical evidence that these metrics are useful for predicting model performance, identifying specific model weaknesses, or creating a more effective training curriculum. Without a demonstration of its utility on a downstream task, the proposed framework is a purely observational tool whose practical value is unproven.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The distinction between "Bridging" and "Elaboration" seems highly subjective. Could you provide concrete examples from your annotation guidelines that help annotators make a consistent distinction? More broadly, could you provide more details about the annotation guidelines and the protocol for resolving disagreements?
2.  Regarding the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement: Is this a simple percentage agreement? Given the multi-label nature of the task (a question can require multiple skills), how was this metric calculated? Have you considered reporting a chance-corrected metric such as Fleiss' Kappa?
3.  Your analysis of question difficulty is based on a simple count of required skills. Have you explored alternative models of difficulty, such as a weighted sum where weights are learned or assigned based on perceived complexity? How might this change your findings on the relative difficulty of datasets?
4.  The first step of your annotation was to select the necessary sentences. This is a critical and potentially subjective step. Did you measure inter-annotator agreement on this sentence selection subtask? How does variation in sentence selection affect the subsequent skill annotation and the overall analysis of difficulty?
