Summary
This paper proposes a framework to evaluate reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: (i) prerequisite skills needed to answer a question and (ii) readability of the context. The authors refine an existing skill taxonomy (organizing knowledge reasoning via bridging vs. elaboration, adding ellipsis and meta-knowledge, etc.) and adopt readability features from Vajjala and Meurers. They annotate 100 questions each from six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with required skills and the subset of sentences needed to answer, then compute readability on those sentences. Key findings: weak correlation between readability and the number of skills required; dataset-specific profiles (e.g., SQuAD hard to read but relatively easy to answer; MCTest easy to read but requires multi-sentence reasoning). They release the annotations.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A reorganized, theory-informed taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC (notably clarifying knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration; adding ellipsis and meta-knowledge), with an annotation protocol applied uniformly across diverse datasets.
2) A comparative dataset analysis showing only weak correlation between question difficulty (operationalized as the number of required skills) and readability of evidence sentences, providing actionable insights for dataset design.
3) A publicly released annotated resource (skills and evidence sentences) for six widely used RC datasets.

Strengths
- Theory-grounded taxonomy and clear motivation: The reorganization draws on textual entailment and cognitive psychology (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano), providing a principled lens for analyzing RC beyond superficial question/answer types. This strengthens interpretability and potential generality.
- Cross-dataset, uniform manual annotation: Applying a single protocol across six datasets enables meaningful comparisons. Selecting the minimal set of sentences needed and labeling required skills is a sensible way to factor out retrieval and focus on comprehension.
- Useful, concrete findings for dataset builders: The weak readability–difficulty correlation and genre effects (e.g., narratives vs. expository) are important, non-obvious takeaways that can guide curation strategies (e.g., constructing challenging questions from easy texts).
- Practical resource contribution: The released annotations (skills, needed sentences) can support future error analyses, model development, and curriculum design. Inter-annotator agreement (90.1% on a sample) suggests the scheme is learnable.
- Clarity and situating within prior work: The paper carefully discusses limitations of past datasets and prior skill taxonomies; the methodology and sampling decisions are transparent.

Weaknesses
- Operationalization of “difficulty” and subjectivity: Using the count of required skills as the primary proxy for question difficulty is debatable. Skills are not obviously additive, and some skills may be more demanding than others. The paper lacks validation against human difficulty (e.g., human accuracy/latency) or system performance, despite claiming relevance to system evaluation.
- Limited scale and statistical analysis: Only 100 questions per dataset, with IAA reported on 62 items and not broken down per skill. No confidence intervals or significance tests for correlations; a reported Pearson as high as 0.416 could be nontrivial, yet the narrative emphasizes “weak correlation.” Plots highlight Flesch–Kincaid, while the fuller set of readability features is under-analyzed in the main text.
- Annotation protocol choices constrain scope: Annotators saw the gold answers and were asked to pick necessary sentences. This excludes retrieval and candidate selection—critical RC components—and may bias toward shorter evidence and lower perceived difficulty in datasets like SQuAD/MS MARCO. The “nonsense” label is under-specified and may conflate unanswerable-within-context vs. poorly formed questions.
- Taxonomy overlap and reliability concerns: Distinctions among bridging, elaboration, and coreference can be fuzzy; meta-knowledge is rarely invoked yet separated as a skill. The paper does not report per-skill agreement, adjudication procedures, or examples of common disagreements—important for reliability and future use of the resource.
- Confounds and measurement details: Readability is computed on selected evidence sentences only; this is a reasonable design but begs for analysis controlling for context length, number of sentences, and genre. The “sentence distance” definition is nonstandard (sum of pairwise distances for >2 sentences) and its interpretability is unclear.

Questions to authors
- Per-skill reliability: What are the per-skill inter-annotator agreement scores? How were disagreements resolved (majority vote, adjudication)? Did you assess consistency on identifying the set of necessary sentences?
- Difficulty validation: Did you correlate number of skills with human accuracy/time or with baseline/system accuracy on the same items? If not, can you provide preliminary evidence that “more skills” predicts harder questions?
- Correlation robustness: Can you report per-dataset correlations between number of skills and each readability feature, with confidence intervals, and control for number of evidence sentences and evidence length? How sensitive are results to using all readability features vs. Flesch–Kincaid alone?
- “Nonsense” definition: What criteria were given to annotators? How did you distinguish unanswerable-within-context from answerable-by-world-knowledge? Any adjudication examples?
- Taxonomy clarity: Provide concrete borderline examples distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration vs. coreference. Did you observe systematic confusions?
- Resource details: Will you release annotation guidelines, code for readability feature extraction, and the evidence sentence indices to ensure reproducibility?

Overall assessment
This is a useful, timely analysis paper with a principled taxonomy and a practical annotated resource. However, its main empirical claims would be stronger with larger-scale annotation, per-skill reliability, clearer difficulty validation, and more rigorous statistical analysis. I lean toward a weak accept if the resource and guidelines are released and the authors address reliability and analysis concerns; otherwise, it is borderline.
