- Main finding: The paper proposes analyzing RC datasets via a 13-skill taxonomy and readability features and reports a weak correlation between skill count (question difficulty) and readability (text difficulty).

- Contribution: Presents a reorganized 13-skill taxonomy grounded in entailment phenomena and cognitive models.

- Contribution: Conducts cross-dataset human annotations (six RC datasets, 100 questions each) marking required sentences and multi-label skills, plus sentence-level readability measures.

- Contribution: Promises to release annotations as a community resource for dataset curation and diagnostics.

- Strength: Taxonomy conceptually integrates textual entailment categories with cognitive distinctions, offering a principled alternative to ad-hoc labels.

- Strength: Annotation methodology is dataset-agnostic, enabling comparison across heterogeneous RC formats (cloze, extractive, MC, multi-passage).

- Strength: Actionable insight that readability and question difficulty are largely independent, suggesting curricula that vary two axes.

- Strength: Paper transparently discusses limitations and provides additional analyses (sentence counts, distances) to contextualize multi-sentence reasoning needs.

- Strength: Released annotations could seed skill-aware modeling, diagnostics, and dataset auditing.

- Weakness: Equating difficulty with raw skill counts assumes equal and additive contribution of skills, which is unvalidated and potentially flawed.

- Weakness: Annotation reliability is under-specified—reported 90.1% agreement on 62 items seems raw, with no chance-corrected metrics or clarity on per-skill vs. sentence-selection agreement.

- Weakness: Sample size (100 items per dataset) is small for 13 skills and correlational claims; statistical reporting lacks p-values, CIs, and multiple-testing correction.

- Weakness: Cross-task comparability is confounded by controlling away retrieval/selection competence and by dataset-specific factors (context length, filtering, task format).

- Weakness: No linkage to model or human performance—missing validation that skill/readability profiles predict real-world difficulty.

- Question: Report chance-corrected agreement (e.g., Krippendorff’s α/Cohen’s κ) per skill and for sentence selection, and describe adjudication for “nonsense” disagreements.

- Question: Clarify readability metrics—why Flesch–Kincaid appears in figures while features are from Vajjala & Meurers (including Coleman–Liau); report whether both were computed and how they correlate.

- Question: Provide validation of difficulty—do skill counts correlate with human RT/accuracy or model EM/F1; can public model scores be used post-hoc?

- Question: Demonstrate sampling robustness—provide bootstrap CIs for skill distributions and check sensitivity to SQuAD sampling choices (one question per paragraph).

- Question: Will detailed annotation guidelines, raw annotations (skills, spans, nonsense flags), and adjudication notes be released for replication?

- Additional comment: Taxonomy could be clarified to reduce overlap (bridging vs. elaboration vs. world-knowledge) with concrete decision rules and examples.

- Additional comment: Consider alternative difficulty models (e.g., item-response theory) to infer latent difficulty and provide uncertainty estimates.

- Additional comment: Control for context length in correlational analyses, as it strongly affects sentence distance and perceived difficulty.

- Overall recommendation: Timely and well-motivated diagnostic study with a useful taxonomy and observations, but methodological issues (difficulty proxy validity, reliability reporting, limited statistics, lack of performance linkage) need addressing before stronger claims.
