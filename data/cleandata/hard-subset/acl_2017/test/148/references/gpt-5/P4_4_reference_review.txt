Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a two-pronged evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets that decouples the difficulty of answering questions from the difficulty of reading the context. The authors:
- Define and refine a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills needed to answer RC questions, reorganizing knowledge-reasoning categories with guidance from textual entailment and psycholinguistics (bridging vs. elaboration, retrieved vs. generated inferences).
- Apply readability metrics (primarily features from Vajjala and Meurers 2012, plus Flesch–Kincaid) to the context sentences deemed necessary to answer each question.
- Annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) on 100 questions each, analyzing skill usage, counts of required skills per question, readability statistics, sentence selection patterns, and correlations between skill counts and readability.

As contributions, the work primarily offers:
1) A refined, psycholinguistically grounded skill taxonomy for RC analysis and consistent annotation methodology across heterogeneous RC task formulations.
2) A cross-dataset, human-annotated analysis that uncovers a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty (measured as number of required skills), with dataset-specific insights, and publicly available annotations.
3) Empirical observations about sentence locality (number and distance of sentences needed), and dataset “nonsense” rates, informing dataset curation and evaluation practices.

Strengths
- Clear, theory-informed taxonomy that improves past work: The reorganization of prerequisite skills, especially the separation of bridging vs. elaboration and the explicit treatment of ellipsis and meta-knowledge, is grounded in well-established frameworks (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano; RTE literature). This yields a more principled inventory than prior ad hoc taxonomies and aligns RC evaluation with recognized inference categories.
- Cross-dataset, uniform annotation enabling meaningful comparisons: Applying the same skill labels and sentence-selection protocol to six diverse datasets (n=100 questions each) allows direct comparison of skill profiles and readability. This cross-cutting analysis highlights differences attributable to dataset genre and construction (e.g., MCTest narratives vs. SQuAD/Wikipedia).
- Empirical evidence on readability vs. skill difficulty: The repeated finding of weak correlations (Pearson r up to ~0.4, often much lower) between skill count and readability (Flesch–Kincaid and feature-based metrics) supports the central claim that text readability and question difficulty are largely decoupled. Concrete examples (MCTest: easy to read yet skill-demanding; SQuAD: harder to read yet often solvable from a single sentence) substantiate this.
- Practical insights for dataset design and system evaluation: The analysis of “nonsense” prevalence (higher in automatically curated datasets), sentence distance (locality of evidence), and per-skill frequencies provides actionable guidance: e.g., controlling for question-context overlap, including meta-information on question quality, and constructing curricula that vary readability and skill complexity independently.
- Reasonable annotation reliability and transparency: A reported 90.1% inter-annotator agreement on a sample, explicit multi-labeling of skills, and sentence highlighting improve interpretability of results. Public release of annotations increases utility and reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited statistical rigor and reporting for reliability and correlations: Agreement is reported as percent agreement on 62 questions without chance-corrected measures (e.g., Cohen’s Kappa) or per-skill reliability, leaving uncertainty about consistency across the 13 labels. Correlation analyses lack significance tests, confidence intervals, or corrections for multiple comparisons; the choice of Pearson alone may miss non-linear relationships.
- Assumption that “number of required skills” equals difficulty is unvalidated and coarse: All skills are implicitly weighted equally, although their cognitive and computational complexity likely differs. No user study or model-based analysis links skill counts to human or system error rates. This limits the strength of claims that skill count is a proxy for difficulty.
- Potential annotation bias and construct validity concerns: Annotators viewed the gold answer and selected sentences before labeling skills. This may bias skill identification toward minimal, hindsight explanations and underrepresent retrieval/search difficulty. As acknowledged, the methodology does not assess the competence of locating evidence or answer spans/candidates, an important component of RC.
- Readability metrics may be misaligned with RC difficulty for machines and adults: The selected features are optimized for second-language readability and general reading ease, which may not capture the aspects that drive RC difficulty (e.g., discourse structure, coreference density, entity/event complexity). Reliance on Flesch–Kincaid as the primary scalar in figures risks oversimplifying text complexity.
- Scope and scale limitations: Only 100 questions per dataset are annotated, which restricts generalizability given dataset sizes and variability. Some dataset choices and exclusions (e.g., CNN/DM) are reasonable but reduce coverage of widely used benchmarks. Several tables/figures appear to have formatting inconsistencies (e.g., averages vs. distributions), and certain methodological details (tooling for readability features, preprocessing) are under-specified.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Reliability: Can chance-corrected agreement (e.g., per-skill Cohen’s Kappa) be reported, along with confusion matrices among skills? How consistent is sentence selection across annotators?
- Difficulty metric: What empirical evidence supports using the unweighted skill count as question difficulty? Have alternative weightings or difficulty models (e.g., IRT, regression vs. human/system accuracy) been tested?
- Readability computation: Which tools and tokenization/segmentation settings were used for computing the Vajjala & Meurers features and Flesch–Kincaid? Were features computed only on the annotated sentences, and how were multi-sentence selections aggregated?
- Correlation analysis: Are correlations statistically significant, and do results hold under non-parametric measures (Spearman) or partial correlations controlling for context length? Could mixed-effects models (by dataset/item) strengthen claims?
- Bias controls: How was the potential bias from showing answers mitigated? Did annotators ever mark multiple minimal evidence sets, and how was that handled?
- Release details: Will per-question skill labels, sentence highlights, and “nonsense” tags be released for all datasets, with clear guidelines for reuse?

Overall, the paper is clear and technically sound in defining a principled annotation framework and executing a cross-dataset analysis. The primary novelty is the theory-driven skill taxonomy and empirical demonstration that readability and question difficulty are largely independent, supported by publicly released annotations. The main limitations concern statistical rigor, construct validity of the difficulty proxy, and limited sample size; addressing these would strengthen the work’s impact.
