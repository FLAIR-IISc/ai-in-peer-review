Summary and contributions
This paper proposes two complementary classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (i) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills (with a reorganized treatment of knowledge reasoning grounded in psycholinguistics and textual entailment) and (ii) readability metrics drawn from prior work on human readability. The authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) on 100 randomly sampled questions each, analyze frequencies and co-occurrence of skills, compute readability on the subset of sentences deemed necessary to answer, and study correlations between skill counts and readability. They find only weak correlations, arguing that question difficulty and text readability are largely independent. They release the annotations.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A cross-dataset, human-annotated analysis showing a weak correlation between question difficulty (approximated by the number/type of prerequisite skills) and readability of relevant context, with concrete, dataset-specific insights.
2) A refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy, reorganizing knowledge reasoning using bridging vs. elaboration and entailment phenomena, and adding meta-knowledge and ellipsis.
3) A publicly released annotation of six RC datasets with prerequisite skills and supporting sentence selections.

Strengths
- Clear, cross-dataset comparative analysis with concrete findings: The systematic annotation across six widely used datasets yields interpretable contrasts (e.g., MCTest being easy to read yet often requiring multi-sentence reasoning; QA4MRE being both hard to read and to answer; SQuAD being hard to read but relatively easy to answer). The weak correlations (Pearson 0.025–0.416) substantively support the main claim that readability and question difficulty are distinct axes.
- Thoughtful, theory-grounded taxonomy: The reorganization of knowledge reasoning via bridging vs. elaboration and the retrieved vs. generated inference distinction connects RC analysis to psycholinguistics and RTE literature. This grounding improves clarity over prior ad hoc “commonsense” buckets and is likely useful to dataset creators and error analysts.
- Practical annotation protocol and useful byproducts: Having annotators select the minimal sentences needed, then label required skills, makes subsequent analyses (e.g., sentence distance, readability on relevant context) feasible. The release of these annotations can catalyze further research (curriculum design, skill-targeted modeling, dataset auditing).
- Informative observations for dataset design and evaluation: The paper highlights pitfalls of automated curation (e.g., nonsense questions in MS MARCO/WDW), the importance of meta-information (NewsQA), and genre effects (narrative vs. expository), offering actionable guidance for future dataset construction.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological rigor in annotation reliability and aggregation: The reported inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) lacks detail (metric used, per-skill vs. per-example, chance correction, agreement on sentence selection). The protocol shows question, context, and gold answer jointly, which biases toward confirmatory labeling and underestimates the difficulty of retrieval/answer selection. How disagreements were resolved and how multi-label decisions were aggregated are unclear. Given the subjectivity of skills like bridging/elaboration, more granular reliability reporting is necessary.
- Sampling and comparability concerns reduce external validity: Only 100 questions per dataset may be underpowered to capture heterogeneity. Sampling strategies differ (e.g., one question per SQuAD paragraph; filtered NewsQA with “bad question” flags; selected segments in MS MARCO), which can introduce dataset-specific biases and hinder comparability. The handling of “nonsense” varies across datasets and annotation, further complicating cross-dataset conclusions.
- Readability metric choice and analysis are narrow: The chosen features (top-10 from Vajjala & Meurers) and FK grade are designed for human readability and may poorly reflect machine difficulty. Readability is computed only on annotator-selected sentences, conflating selection with ease of processing. The correlation analysis lacks controls for confounds (document length, number of selected sentences, domain/genre) and does not report confidence intervals or multiple-comparison corrections. Claims about “weak correlation” would be stronger with a multivariate model and significance testing.
- Novelty and scope are somewhat limited: The core idea (skill-based analysis of RC difficulty) builds closely on Sugawara et al. (2016/2017). The present work refines the taxonomy and broadens the dataset coverage, which is useful but incremental. Despite positioning metrics as useful for “system development,” no system-side experiments are provided (e.g., correlating model errors with skill profiles, curriculum benefits), so the “for systems” part remains speculative.
- Minor inconsistencies and missing details: There appears to be a discrepancy between text and Table 3 regarding average skills per question for QA4MRE (text mentions 4.6 vs. table 3.25). The definition of sentence “distance” is nonstandard and sensitive to context length, potentially skewing cross-dataset comparisons.

Questions to authors
- Reliability: Which agreement metric did you use (e.g., Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa, Krippendorff’s alpha)? Was agreement computed per skill label, per example, or on sentence selection? Please provide per-skill agreement and chance-corrected measures.
- Aggregation: How did you aggregate multiple annotators’ labels (majority vote, union, adjudication)? How did you handle disagreements on “nonsense” and on the set of required sentences?
- Readability computation: Precisely which sentences fed into readability? If annotators selected multiple sentences, were they concatenated? Were scores normalized for sentence count/length? Did you consider document-level measures as a control?
- Statistical analysis: Are the reported correlations statistically significant after correcting for multiple tests? Did you run multivariate regressions controlling for dataset, context length, and number of selected sentences?
- Taxonomy operationalization: Could you share a detailed codebook with decision rules and examples distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration, meta-knowledge, and ellipsis? This would improve reproducibility.
- Data release: Where can the annotations be accessed, and what is included (sentence IDs, per-annotator labels, adjudicated labels, code)? Under what license?

Overall assessment
This is a thoughtful and useful dataset-analysis paper that surfaces an important insight: difficulty to answer and difficulty to read are not the same and should be measured separately. The taxonomy refinement and released annotations will likely be valuable to the community. However, issues around annotation rigor, sampling/comparability, and limited statistical and system-level validation weaken the current form. I lean toward a weak reject, but with clear potential: tightening the reliability analysis, expanding and harmonizing sampling, strengthening statistics, and adding initial system-side validation would make this a strong contribution.
