Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a two-axis evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets, combining (a) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology (bridging vs. elaboration, ellipsis, meta-knowledge, etc.) and (b) readability metrics drawn from prior NLP work. The authors annotate six popular RC datasets with this framework and analyze correlations, notably showing only weak links between readability and question difficulty (operationalized as the number of required skills). They also release the annotations.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Conceptual framework: A theoretically motivated reorganization of prerequisite skills for RC (especially the knowledge reasoning categories) plus a paired readability axis to characterize datasets.
2) Cross-dataset empirical analysis: A consistent annotation of six datasets revealing qualitative differences (e.g., high ellipsis in WDW, narrative-driven skills in MCTest) and the weak correlation between readability and skill-demand.
3) Practical guidance and resources: Public release of annotations and a proposed curriculum for system development based on dataset positioning along the two axes.

Strengths
1) Novel, useful perspective tying RC evaluation to cognitive theory: The reorganization of “knowledge reasoning” into ellipsis, bridging, elaboration, and meta-knowledge, explicitly linked to Kintsch and McNamara & Magliano and textual entailment typologies, is a meaningful conceptual advance. It offers a clearer, more actionable vocabulary for analyzing what RC questions demand beyond surface matching.

2) Cross-dataset insight with practical implications: Annotating six datasets using a unified protocol yields compelling comparative findings (e.g., MCTest being easy-to-read but often skill-demanding; WDW’s ellipsis/bridging requirements due to question generation; QA4MRE’s broad knowledge/long-distance dependencies). The “weak correlation” result is important: dataset readability does not predict reasoning difficulty, which cautions against using readability as a proxy for dataset hardness.

3) Clear, replicable methodology and transparency about scope: The pipeline—sentence selection, multi-label skill annotation, and per-question readability on only the relevant sentences—makes the analysis focused on comprehension rather than retrieval. The explicit “nonsense” label helps capture dataset noise, and the paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., retrieval not evaluated), which increases credibility.

4) Actionability for dataset construction and system development: The proposal to place datasets along a continuum of readability × skill-demand and use that for curriculum/transfer learning is simple yet genuinely useful. The released annotations can seed future work on diagnostic evaluation, data curation, and model error analysis.

5) Breadth and grounded comparisons: The work balances theoretical grounding with concrete metrics (Vajjala & Meurers; Flesch–Kincaid; structural features) and provides numeric comparisons (skill frequencies, number/distance of sentences), offering multiple angles to interpret dataset properties.

Weaknesses
1) Validation of “skill count = difficulty” is assumed but not tested: The central assumption—that the number of required skills corresponds to question difficulty—lacks external validation. There is no correlation with human difficulty judgments, human response time, or model performance/error breakdowns. Without such validation, the weak correlation result and some conclusions should be viewed as indicative rather than definitive.

2) Limited and under-specified reliability analysis: The reported 90.1% agreement (on 62 items) is percent agreement, not a chance-corrected statistic (e.g., κ/α), and does not reflect multi-label structure or per-skill reliability. Given potential overlap (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration vs. ellipsis), more rigorous reliability reporting is necessary, especially for the “nonsense” label.

3) Readability metrics choice/inconsistencies and possible confounds: The paper cites Vajjala & Meurers’ top features but reports key correlations using Flesch–Kincaid (not listed among the “top 10” used), which reads as inconsistent. Readability is computed on only the annotator-selected sentences, which may compress variance and entangle the measurement with the skill annotation itself. A sensitivity analysis (full passage vs. selected sentences; multiple readability indices) would strengthen claims.

4) Sampling size and selection may limit generality: Only 100 questions per dataset and some dataset-specific filtering choices (e.g., SQuAD one question per paragraph) may not fully capture dataset diversity. While the comparisons are still informative, statistical power and representativeness are limited.

5) Taxonomy clarity and granularity: Despite improvements, category boundaries (bridging vs. elaboration vs. ellipsis; meta-knowledge) remain somewhat fuzzy and the paper provides few concrete, annotated examples to anchor distinctions. Some categories are very rare (e.g., meta-knowledge), raising questions about coverage and utility for current datasets.

Questions to authors
1) Reliability: What is the inter-annotator agreement per skill using a chance-corrected metric (e.g., Krippendorff’s α for multi-label)? How reliable is the “nonsense” label specifically?
2) Validation: Do you have preliminary evidence correlating skill counts (or specific skills) with human difficulty (e.g., time-to-answer) or model performance across datasets? If not, can you provide a small pilot?
3) Readability metrics: Why emphasize Flesch–Kincaid in Figures 3–4 when your stated top-10 features come from Vajjala & Meurers? How do correlations change if you use the full set, or readability computed on full contexts?
4) Guidelines and reproducibility: Will you release detailed annotation guidelines and example-laden decision rules distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration vs. ellipsis? This seems crucial for uptake.
5) Scaling: Do you envision (semi-)automatic tagging of prerequisite skills (e.g., via entailment parsers or lexical resources) to scale this framework? Any early experiments?

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a conceptually fresh and practically useful evaluation perspective that can influence dataset design and model analysis. While validation and reliability reporting could be stronger, the boldness of the framework and the cross-dataset insights justify acceptance, especially with the promised release of annotations.
