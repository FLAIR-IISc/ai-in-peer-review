Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a two-pronged evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (1) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills (13 categories) grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology (bridging vs. elaboration; retrieved vs. generated), and (2) readability features (largely from Vajjala & Meurers, plus FK grade). The authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these metrics using three annotators on 100 questions per dataset. They report: (a) per-dataset skill frequency profiles, (b) average number of skills required, (c) per-dataset readability statistics for sentences deemed necessary to answer the question, and (d) weak correlations between readability and number of skills. They argue that question difficulty (skills) and text readability are only weakly related and release annotations.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A refined, psychology- and entailment-informed taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC and its application across six datasets.
2) A cross-dataset manual annotation study that quantifies skill requirements and “nonsense” rates, with an exploratory analysis of readability vs. skill difficulty.
3) A dataset profiling that could inform dataset construction and curriculum design.

Strengths
- Cross-dataset, manual, skill-centric profiling: The paper goes beyond shallow question/answer-type tags and provides a multi-skill lens across six influential datasets. The comparative tables (e.g., skill frequencies, average skills per question) surface recognizable qualitative differences (e.g., narrative vs. expository; automatic vs. curated question generation).
- Taxonomy grounded in prior theory: The reorganization (bridging vs. elaboration; explicit handling of ellipsis, meta-knowledge, punctuation) is reasonably motivated by both textual entailment literature and cognitive models, improving over earlier ad-hoc categories.
- Potential utility for dataset design: The observation that readability and required skills are weakly correlated is practically relevant—one can construct skill-demanding questions over easy text and vice versa—which is a useful guideline for future dataset curation and curriculum design.
- Transparency about scope: The paper clearly acknowledges that the protocol elides the sentence-selection/answer-finding competencies and focuses on contextual entailment understanding, which avoids over-claiming.

Weaknesses
- Limited statistical rigor and small sample size: Only 100 questions per dataset are annotated; correlations (reported as 0.025–0.416) lack p-values, confidence intervals, or multiple-comparison control across many readability features. The central “weak correlation” claim is thus under-supported. No analysis is provided per dataset (beyond plots) to check if the trend holds within datasets, and there is no validation that “#skills” is a reliable proxy for difficulty (e.g., correlation with human accuracy or model error).
- Reliability metrics are inadequate: A single “90.1% agreement” over 62 questions is reported without specifying the measure (percent agreement?) and without chance correction (e.g., Cohen’s κ/Krippendorff’s α). With multi-label annotations across 13 skills plus a “nonsense” label, per-skill reliability is essential; otherwise the frequency profiles may be biased by annotator subjectivity. No adjudication protocol or training regimen is described.
- Readability protocol is question-leaky and under-specified: Readability is computed on sentences preselected using the question and answer, which systematically biases toward shorter, simpler spans and is not reflective of a real RC setting where full passages must be processed. No comparison to readability computed on the entire context is given. The toolchain to compute syntactic features (parser, tokenization, syllabification, AWL mapping) is unspecified, making results non-reproducible. FK grade is highlighted in figures but is not part of the top-10 set in Figure 2; the rationale for mixing metrics is unclear.
- Inconsistent sampling and comparability across datasets: Different filters are applied across datasets (e.g., removing “bad” and unanswerable questions in NewsQA but not uniformly elsewhere). WDW is a “hard” subset by design; MS MARCO tasks mix web snippets and are arguably closer to open-domain QA than RC. These choices likely affect “nonsense” rates and skill distributions, undermining cross-dataset comparability.
- Ambiguities and missing or incorrect details: The “sentence distance” metric is vaguely defined (“sum of distances of any two sentences” for >2 sentences—which pairs?). No normalization by document length. The assumption “more skills ⇒ more difficulty” is taken from prior work but not substantiated here (e.g., no correlation with human or model performance). The method does not analyze the impact of multi-sentence reasoning beyond distance aggregates. The taxonomy, while improved, still groups heterogeneous phenomena (e.g., bridging includes idioms and appositions alongside lexical semantics) and could benefit from finer-grained definitions and examples per label.
- Presentation and formatting issues: Multiple references are malformed (years replaced by “?”, stray TeX markers like \Q2016\E, duplicated/truncated entries), footnotes are misnumbered, and several tables/figures referenced (e.g., Table 1, Table 4, Table 5) are not included in the text provided. These issues hinder reproducibility and clarity.

Questions to authors
- Reliability: How exactly was inter-annotator agreement computed for multi-label annotations? Please report per-skill κ/α and for the “nonsense” label. What was the annotator training, and was there an adjudication phase?
- Difficulty validity: Can you validate “#skills” as a difficulty proxy by correlating it with human accuracy, reading time, or baseline/model error rates on the same items?
- Readability computation: Which parser, tokenizer, syllabifier, and AWL list implementation did you use? Why compute readability only on question-selected sentences? Can you report the same analyses on full contexts and show whether the conclusions hold?
- Statistics: Please provide p-values and CIs for all correlations, and report per-dataset correlations (not just pooled). Given multiple features, apply appropriate corrections or a multivariate model.
- Sampling/filters: Can you harmonize filtering across datasets to ensure comparable “nonsense” rates (e.g., remove “bad”/unanswerable across all where available)? For WDW, which split (strict/hard)? For MS MARCO, how did you handle multiple passages and abstractive answers?
- Sentence distance: Provide a precise definition (for k sentences, is it sum of pairwise absolute index differences, or max span, or something else?). Consider normalizing by passage length.
- Resources: Will you release the full annotation guidelines, annotated items, and code for readability features? Without these, reproduction is difficult.

Overall, the paper tackles an important gap—skill-centric evaluation of RC datasets—and the cross-dataset perspective is valuable. However, the empirical support is currently weakened by limited reliability analysis, questionable readability protocol, insufficient statistical rigor, and inconsistent sampling, along with significant presentation issues. Addressing these would substantially strengthen the contribution.
