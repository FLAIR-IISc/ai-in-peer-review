- Proposes two complementary evaluation metrics for RC: a 13-category, entailment- and psycholinguistics-informed skill taxonomy and readability measures for lexical/syntactic complexity.  
- Annotates 100 examples from six diverse RC datasets with required skills and supporting sentences, analyzes dataset profiles and readability vs. skill-based difficulty, and releases annotations.  
- Claims: refined taxonomy + cross-dataset annotations; empirical profiling showing weak correlation between readability and skill difficulty; practical guidance for dataset construction and curriculum use.

- Strength: Cross-dataset, genre-aware analysis with a consistent annotation protocol yields plausible, comparable skill distributions across varied sources.  
- Strength: Skill taxonomy is thoughtfully grounded in prior theory, clarifying commonsense distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration) and adding ellipsis/meta-knowledge.  
- Strength: Operationalization via annotator-selected evidence sentences supports consistency across formats and enables secondary analyses like sentence distance.  
- Strength: Actionable observations for dataset design, showing readability ≠ question difficulty and offering practical dataset-construction guidance.  
- Strength: Public release of annotations can seed further skill-aware benchmarking despite small size.

- Weakness: Small sample size (100 per dataset) and incomplete reliability reporting (single "90.1%" figure without definition, no per-skill kappas, no adjudication details).  
- Weakness: Readability analysis uses only annotator-selected sentences, potentially biasing correlations; statistical reporting lacks p-values, CIs, and multiple-comparison controls.  
- Weakness: No empirical validation that skill/readability profiles predict model performance, limiting claimed utility for system evaluation or curriculum learning.  
- Weakness: Handling of “nonsense”/unsolvable questions is ad hoc with unclear criteria and reliability, undermining dataset quality claims.  
- Weakness: Scope omissions (several major datasets excluded or partially used) and choice of subsets reduce generality.

- Question: Explain how inter-annotator agreement was computed for multi-label skills; report per-skill kappa/alpha and agreement for sentence selection and nonsense labels, plus training/adjudication procedures.  
- Question: Why compute readability on selected sentences only; provide analyses on full passages and control for passage length, dataset, and genre with p-values/CIs.  
- Question: Clarify which readability features were used in correlations (Flesch–Kincaid vs. Vajjala & Meurers features) and why.  
- Question: Define and exemplify the operationalization of “nonsense” and how questions answerable via world knowledge were handled, with reliability.  
- Question: Do skill counts/types predict model performance; provide even a small-scale correlation of skills with baseline QA accuracy.

- Overall: Well-motivated, theoretically grounded and useful as a dataset-auditing resource, but limited by weak empirical rigor, sample size, and lack of system-validation—better suited for workshop/dataset-analytics until strengthened.
