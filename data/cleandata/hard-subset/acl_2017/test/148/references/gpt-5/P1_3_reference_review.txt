Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a two-pronged evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills needed to answer questions (13 skills, reorganizing prior work with insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology), and (b) readability metrics (largely from Vajjala & Meurers 2012). The authors annotate six prominent RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), sampling 100 questions each, and analyze skill frequencies, number of skills per question, readability, and their correlation. The main empirical finding is a weak correlation between reading difficulty and answering difficulty, suggesting they should be treated as distinct axes when building or evaluating RC datasets. The annotations are to be released publicly.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of importance:
1) A cross-dataset, human-annotated analysis showing only weak correlation between readability and the number of skills required, plus dataset-specific skill profiles. This is a useful, empirically grounded observation for dataset design and system evaluation.
2) A refined and more principled prerequisite skill taxonomy (especially clarifying knowledge reasoning via bridging vs. elaboration, informed by entailment and psycholinguistics), with an accompanying annotation protocol.
3) A publicly available resource of skill annotations across six widely used datasets to facilitate future RC analysis and curriculum/transfer design.

Strengths
- Clear, actionable empirical insight: The systematic annotation across six datasets shows weak correlations (Pearson 0.025–0.416) between readability and skill count, with concrete illustrations (e.g., MCTest: easy to read but nontrivial to answer; SQuAD: hard to read but often solvable with local cues). This challenges common assumptions and provides a principled basis for multi-axis dataset curation.
- Breadth and comparability: Uniformly annotating six diverse datasets with a single skill scheme and consistent protocol enables apples-to-apples comparisons of dataset characteristics (e.g., high ellipsis/bridging/elaboration in Who-did-What; more “nonsense” in MS MARCO/WDW vs. none in filtered NewsQA).
- Skill taxonomy grounded in theory: The reorganization around bridging vs. elaboration (retrieved vs. generated inferences) and explicit separation of clause-level skills, logical/mathematical reasoning, and meta-knowledge offers improved conceptual clarity over prior ad hoc categories, aligning RC analysis with established theories from TE and cognitive psychology.
- Practical implications for dataset construction and system development: The paper articulates concrete uses (curriculum/transfer learning along the two axes; attention to genre effects; guidance on avoiding nonsensical questions; designing questions that tap global coherence) that are timely for the community.
- Transparency about limitations and methodology: The authors clearly acknowledge scope (e.g., evaluation focuses on contextual entailment understanding, not retrieval or candidate selection), sampling choices, and dataset-specific quirks, which helps interpret results.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the “skill count = difficulty” assumption: The paper assumes that more required skills imply higher question difficulty but does not validate this with human difficulty judgments or system performance correlations. Without such validation, the interpretation of skill counts as difficulty remains plausible but unproven.
- Small samples and potential sampling bias: Only 100 questions per dataset (dev subsets; specific sampling constraints) may not represent the full distribution, especially for large datasets (SQuAD, MS MARCO, NewsQA). Conclusions (e.g., “most questions require <=2 skills”) could be sensitive to sampling.
- Inter-annotator agreement reporting is insufficient: “90.1% agreement for 62 questions” is likely raw percent agreement and not per-label chance-corrected measures (e.g., Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa). Given the subjectivity of skills like bridging/elaboration and “nonsense,” more rigorous reliability reporting (per-skill kappas, adjudication protocol) is needed.
- Readability operationalization may be mismatched: The chosen top-10 features from Vajjala & Meurers (L2 readability) and FK grade are applied only to “sentences needed to answer.” This could bias readability downward and may not capture discourse-level complexity that RC depends on (connectives, coreference chains, cohesion). The correlation analysis relies heavily on FK grade; broader readability and discourse features (e.g., entity grid, coherence relations) could change conclusions.
- Known methodological constraint weakens conclusions about “comprehension”: Annotators saw the answer and identified necessary sentences; the framework cannot probe sentence retrieval or answer span selection—major components of many RC tasks—so results mainly characterize entailment complexity, not full-task difficulty. The paper acknowledges this, but some discussion (e.g., curriculum recommendations) may overgeneralize.

Questions to authors
- How exactly was inter-annotator agreement computed (percent agreement vs. kappa)? Can you report per-skill reliability and for the “nonsense” label? How were disagreements resolved to produce final labels?
- How were multiple skills aggregated (union across annotators, majority vote, adjudication)? Do the skill count distributions change materially under different aggregation schemes?
- Are the reported Pearson correlations statistically significant after correcting for multiple comparisons across metrics? Can you report confidence intervals or robust correlations (e.g., Spearman) given potential non-normality?
- Why use only the top-10 readability features and FK grade? Did you test discourse/cohesion features (e.g., entity transitions, connectives), which might be more predictive of RC difficulty?
- Can you provide any preliminary evidence that skill counts correlate with human response time/accuracy or system error patterns to support the “skill count = difficulty” claim?

Additional comments
- Please release the detailed annotation guidelines and examples per skill to improve reproducibility and facilitate follow-up studies.
- Consider reporting distributions (not just means) for number/distance of sentences and skill counts per dataset, and stratify by question types where applicable.
- A small-scale validation linking skill profiles to actual model performance (even with a few baselines) would substantially strengthen the practical value of the framework.
- The meta-knowledge skill is intriguing but rare; clarifying its boundary with elaboration and providing more examples would help annotators and users.

Overall, this is a timely, useful analysis paper with a principled taxonomy and a valuable cross-dataset annotation effort. With stronger reliability reporting and some validation of the difficulty proxy, it would make a solid contribution to the empirical study of RC datasets.
