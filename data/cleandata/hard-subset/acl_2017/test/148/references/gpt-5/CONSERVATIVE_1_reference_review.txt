Summary and main contributions
The paper proposes a framework to evaluate reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: (i) a refined taxonomy of “prerequisite skills” (13 categories) grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology, and (ii) readability features (largely from Vajjala & Meurers, plus FK grade). The authors annotate six RC datasets (100 questions each) with the skill taxonomy, compute readability on annotator-selected sentences deemed necessary to answer, and analyze correlations. They report that readability and “number of skills required” are only weakly correlated and release the annotations.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An updated, consolidated skill taxonomy for analyzing RC questions, integrating notions of bridging/elaboration and entailment phenomena.
2) A cross-dataset human annotation study (6 datasets × 100 Qs) using this taxonomy, with qualitative and simple statistical analyses (frequency, sentence distance, Pearson correlations).
3) The empirical observation (with some caveats) that text readability and the skill complexity of questions are weakly correlated.

Strengths
1) Focus on dataset quality and diagnostic characterization. The paper tackles an important and under-explored problem: systematically characterizing what RC datasets actually test. The taxonomy-and-annotation approach is a useful complement to the prevalent “single accuracy number” reporting, and the released annotations can benefit the community.

2) Thoughtful integration of psychology and textual entailment literature. The reorganization of “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs. elaboration, and the attention to retrieved vs. generated inferences, provide clearer conceptual anchors than prior ad hoc categories. Even if some boundaries remain fuzzy, the mapping to established theory is a step forward.

3) Cross-dataset scope and consistent annotation protocol. Covering six widely used datasets with a single set of labels enables meaningful comparisons (e.g., WDW’s high ellipsis, MCTest’s narrative-induced skills). The added sentence-distance analysis is a practical proxy for multi-sentence reasoning demands.

4) Actionable insights for dataset construction and system development. The observation that easy-to-read texts can support challenging questions (e.g., MCTest) is useful for dataset design. The curriculum-style suggestion (progression from easy-to-read/easy-to-answer to harder regimes) provides a practical framing for evaluation planning.

Weaknesses
1) Limited empirical rigor and scale; weak validation of the core assumptions. The central assumption that “number of required skills” correlates with question difficulty is asserted and supported only indirectly (via prior work); no human difficulty measures (time/accuracy) or model-based analyses are provided. The annotation covers only 100 questions per dataset, which is small relative to dataset variability, and correlational claims are based on limited, noisy measurements (annotator-selected sentences, FK computed on subsets). Without significance tests (p-values, CIs) per dataset or robust regression with controls (e.g., passage length), the “weak correlation” result is suggestive rather than conclusive.

2) Subjectivity and insufficient reporting of annotation reliability. Agreement is reported as percent agreement (90.1%) on 62 questions; more informative statistics (Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa or Krippendorff’s alpha) are needed, ideally per skill. The paper acknowledges fuzzy boundaries (bridging vs. elaboration, meta-knowledge) but does not quantify where disagreements arise. The “nonsense” label is particularly subjective and conflates solvability with reasonableness.

3) Methodological confounds from showing answers and restricting readability to selected sentences. Annotators saw context, question, and the gold answer, which risks hindsight bias in skill attribution and sentence selection. Readability is computed only on the annotator-selected sentences rather than full passages; this makes metrics heavily dependent on subjective selection and may understate the true reading burden, especially for datasets where retrieval or broader context matters. The methodology explicitly excludes the retrieval/locating competence critical to many RC tasks, limiting the generality of conclusions about dataset “difficulty.”

4) Incomplete statistical analysis and missing controls. Pearson correlations are reported but without per-dataset significance, multiple-comparison handling, or alternative correlation measures robust to nonlinearity/outliers. Potential confounds (document length, genre, question formulation—MC vs span vs cloze) are not controlled for. The heterogeneous sampling/filters across datasets (Appendix A) introduce selection bias that can affect frequency and “nonsense” rates.

5) Incremental novelty relative to prior work. The skill taxonomy extends Sugawara et al. (2017) with a clearer theoretical grounding, but the readability features and overall analysis approach are largely borrowed from established work. The paper claims relevance to system evaluation, but no system experiments are presented to demonstrate diagnostic utility (e.g., correlating model errors with skills). As such, the contribution is primarily descriptive and incremental.

Questions to authors
1) Reliability: Please report inter-annotator agreement with kappa/alpha, overall and per skill, and provide a confusion matrix showing where disagreements concentrate (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration vs. coref).

2) Sampling and significance: Provide p-values/CIs for the reported correlations per dataset; report robustness under bootstrap resampling and under alternative readability measures (e.g., full-passage FK vs selected-sentence FK).

3) Guidelines and examples: Can you share the full annotation guidelines and several adjudicated examples that illustrate borderline cases (bridging vs. elaboration, ellipsis vs. coref, meta-knowledge)?

4) “Nonsense” labeling: How was this defined operationally? Were annotators instructed to consider answerability with external knowledge? Can you quantify inter-annotator agreement specifically on “nonsense”?

5) Generalization to system analysis: Can you provide at least a preliminary analysis showing whether baseline models’ errors correlate with certain skills on your annotated splits, to support the claimed utility for system evaluation?

Overall, the paper addresses a valuable problem and provides a useful resource and taxonomy, but the empirical foundation and methodological rigor are not yet sufficient to fully substantiate the main claims. Strengthening the reliability analyses, controlling confounds, and adding targeted system/error analyses would significantly improve the contribution.
