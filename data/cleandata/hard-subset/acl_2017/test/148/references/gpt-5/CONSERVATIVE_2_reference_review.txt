Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes two complementary evaluation dimensions for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (1) a refined taxonomy of “prerequisite skills” required to answer questions, with an emphasis on separating knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration (grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology), and (2) readability metrics (largely from Vajjala and Meurers, 2012). The authors annotate 100 items from each of six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), marking sentences needed to answer and labeling required skills. They analyze skill frequencies, average number of skills per question, readability of required sentences, correlations between the two dimensions, and simple sentence-distance measures. They report weak correlations between readability and number of skills, arguing that reading difficulty and answering difficulty are largely orthogonal.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A cross-dataset human annotation and analysis indicating weak correlation between readability of the relevant text and the skill demand (proxy for question difficulty).
2) A reorganized skill taxonomy for RC grounded in prior entailment and psycholinguistic work.
3) A practical annotation protocol that tags sentences needed and skills required; authors claim to release annotations.

Strengths
- Cross-dataset perspective with concrete annotations. The work compares six widely used datasets under a uniform annotation scheme, offering useful descriptive statistics (e.g., skill distributions, “nonsense” rates, sentence distances). This alone is informative for dataset designers and practitioners.
- Attempt to ground the taxonomy in established theory. Organizing knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration and aligning several categories with prior RTE and comprehension research improves over ad-hoc “commonsense” groupings seen in earlier RC skill taxonomies.
- Clear qualitative takeaways for dataset construction. The finding that readability and required skills weakly correlate is plausible and practically important: one can craft challenging questions from easy texts (e.g., MCTest), and reading-level alone is not a proxy for RC challenge.
- Transparent admission of scope and limitations. The paper explicitly notes the inability to evaluate sentence/evidence selection competence within the current protocol and flags annotation challenges (e.g., “nonsense”).
- Potentially reusable artifacts. If released, the annotations could support follow-up studies (e.g., model error analyses by skill).

Weaknesses
- Weak validation of central assumptions. The paper equates “number of skills annotated” with difficulty but provides no validation against human difficulty (e.g., human accuracy/RT) or model performance. Without correlating skill counts to system error rates or human judgments, the core construct (skills → difficulty) remains largely untested in this setting.
- Limited and insufficiently rigorous reliability analysis. Reporting 90.1% agreement on 62 items without specifying the metric (percent agreement? micro/macro? per-skill?) is inadequate for a multi-label, subjective taxonomy. No Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa or Krippendorff’s alpha per skill is reported, and only a small subset was double-annotated. This undermines confidence in the annotations and downstream conclusions.
- Readability metrics and statistical analysis choices are not well-justified. The selected features are optimized for L2 readability; their applicability to RC-specific reading (esp. adult L1, Wikipedia/news prose) is not established. The correlation analysis reports coefficients (0.025–0.416) without significance tests, confidence intervals, or controls for confounds (e.g., number of sentences selected, passage length, question type). The reliance on Flesch–Kincaid for key plots is especially limiting.
- Sampling and comparability issues. Only 100 questions per dataset (and from dev/gold splits) may not be representative, and NewsQA is filtered using dataset-provided flags, whereas others are not. This uneven sampling/cleaning regime compromises cross-dataset comparability, especially for “nonsense” rates.
- Taxonomy overlap and subjectivity. Several categories are likely to be confusable (e.g., ellipsis vs. bridging; bridging vs. elaboration), and the mix of single-sentence skills (punctuation, clause relation) with multi-sentence discourse skills may bias skill counts toward texts with particular syntactic properties. The paper does not quantify how often annotators diverged on these boundaries.
- Protocol bias from seeing gold answers. Annotators view the answer and then select sentences and skills, which risks hindsight bias and may underestimate evidence-selection difficulty. While the paper acknowledges this, it still uses the results to discuss “difficulty,” blurring dataset analysis with hypothetical system behavior.
- Limited external utility without concrete release details. The paper claims public release of results but provides no link, format, or licensing details. Reproducibility (annotation guidelines, training, adjudication protocol) is also underspecified.

Questions to authors
- Inter-annotator agreement: What exact reliability metrics were used (per skill and overall)? How was agreement defined for multi-label annotations? How many items were double/triple-annotated, and how were disagreements resolved?
- Statistical rigor: Are the reported correlations statistically significant? Can you provide confidence intervals and p-values? Did you compute partial correlations controlling for passage length, number of selected sentences, and question type/answer type?
- Readability feature choice: Why prioritize the Vajjala & Meurers feature set for L2 readability over discourse-based or RC-oriented features (e.g., entity grid coherence, connective density)? Did you try alternatives, and do conclusions hold?
- Validation against model/human difficulty: Do items with higher skill counts correlate with lower accuracy for standard RC models (e.g., for SQuAD/NewsQA)? If not measured, can you provide such analyses to substantiate the “skills → difficulty” premise?
- Taxonomy clarity: Can you share the full annotation manual with decision rules and examples distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration vs. ellipsis, and how meta-knowledge is operationalized? What was the per-category prevalence and reliability?
- Sampling: How sensitive are conclusions to the 100-item sampling? Did you check stability across multiple random samples? Why were some datasets filtered (NewsQA) while others were not; could you normalize this across datasets?
- Release: Will you release the annotations, code, and guidelines? Under what license and with what identifiers to enable alignment to dataset versions?

Overall, this is a thoughtful descriptive study with potentially useful artifacts, but the empirical foundations (reliability, statistical rigor, and external validation) are not strong enough yet to fully support its broader claims. Strengthening the reliability analysis, validating skill counts against model/human difficulty, and expanding the statistical treatment would materially improve the paper’s credibility and impact.
