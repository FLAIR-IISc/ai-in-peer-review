- Summary: Proposes two metric classes for reading comprehension—(1) a refined, multidimensional prerequisite-skill taxonomy and (2) readability metrics—and annotates six RC datasets, finding weak correlation between skill count and readability; annotations released.

Contributions:
- Introduces a reorganized, entailment- and cognitive-psychology-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC.
- Performs a cross-dataset annotation study (6 datasets × 100 questions) characterizing skill and readability distributions and their weak correlation.
- Publicly releases annotations to support dataset design and future analyses.

Strengths:
- Conceptually integrates textual entailment and cognitive frameworks, improving over ad hoc commonsense categories.
- Provides a cross-dataset, skill-focused audit that yields actionable dataset-specific insights.
- Separates answering difficulty (skills) from reading ease (readability), empirically supporting a dual-axis perspective.
- Offers practical implications for dataset curation and curriculum design by emphasizing genre effects.
- Improves reproducibility by releasing annotations for downstream work.

Weaknesses:
- Equates skill count with question difficulty without validating against human or system performance measures.
- Annotation methodology lacks detail and rigorous reliability reporting (unclear IAA metric, small sample, no per-skill agreement).
- Sampling choices differ across datasets and likely bias cross-dataset comparisons and reported distributions.
- Readability features are surface-level and computed on annotator-selected sentences, risking confounds with selection and neglecting discourse-level metrics.
- Statistical analysis is underdeveloped: correlations lack confidence intervals, multiple-comparison corrections, covariate controls, and more robust modeling.

Questions to authors:
- What reliability statistic was used for multi-label annotation, and can you report per-skill reliability plus the full codebook with examples?
- Did all three annotators label all 600 questions; how were disagreements resolved and labels aggregated?
- Do you have human or model performance per question to validate skill count as a difficulty proxy, or can you test simple proxies (e.g., sentence count/distance)?
- How sensitive are readability conclusions to annotator-selected sentences versus full passages?
- Please report confidence intervals/p-values for correlations and clarify whether results hold after controlling for sentence count/length, genre, and question type, and whether you corrected for multiple tests.
- How was “nonsense” defined operationally, and how do sampling decisions affect nonsense rates and skill distributions?
- What exactly will be released (annotations, code for features, sampling scripts) and will per-question identifiers be included for alignment?

Additional suggestions:
- Augment readability with discourse/coherence features (entity grid, rhetorical relations, coreference density) and lexical sophistication metrics.
- Provide a formal mapping between entailment phenomena and skill categories, including borderline cases to improve annotation consistency.
- Link skill profiles to model error patterns (even for baseline models) to demonstrate practical diagnostic value.
- Re-run analyses with mixed-effects models to control for dataset-level effects and relevant covariates.

Overall recommendation:
- Useful and thoughtful diagnostic work, but needs stronger methodological rigor (reliability, validation, statistical controls) before its metrics can be accepted as reliable proxies for difficulty.
