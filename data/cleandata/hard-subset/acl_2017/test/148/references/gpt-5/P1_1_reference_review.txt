Summary
The paper studies how “answering difficulty” and “reading difficulty” interact in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. It proposes two classes of evaluation metrics: (a) a refined taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills for answering questions, drawing on psycholinguistics and textual entailment, and (b) readability metrics based on Vajjala & Meurers (2012) and traditional measures (e.g., Flesch–Kincaid). Using a consistent human annotation protocol (with answer revealed), the authors annotate 100 questions each from six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), compute distributions of required skills, analyze the number and distance of sentences needed, and correlate skill counts with readability. They find only weak correlations, arguing that reading difficulty and answering difficulty are largely decoupled, and offer dataset-specific observations and guidance for dataset construction and system development.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A theoretically grounded refinement of prerequisite skills for RC, reorganizing knowledge reasoning into bridging vs elaboration and adding ellipsis and meta-knowledge, aligning with psycholinguistic inference distinctions and entailment phenomena.
2) A cross-dataset human annotation study of six prominent RC datasets using the unified skill schema, with reported agreement and promised public release of annotations.
3) Empirical evidence that readability (grade level, linguistic feature complexity) weakly correlates with the number of skills required, plus actionable observations about dataset characteristics and implications for curriculum/transfer in system development.

Strengths
- Conceptual integration and clarity: The taxonomy thoughtfully connects RC skills to established theories (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano) and RTE phenomena (Dagan et al.). The bridging vs elaboration distinction, explicit inclusion of ellipsis, logical reasoning, and punctuation/clause relations make the schema interpretable and plausible for error analysis and dataset profiling.
- Useful cross-dataset comparison: Applying a consistent annotation protocol to six widely used datasets yields informative contrasts (e.g., SQuAD: harder to read but relatively easy to answer; MCTest: easy to read yet requires multiple-sentence reasoning; QA4MRE: high skill diversity and sentence distance; WDW: high ellipsis). These confirm practitioner intuitions with concrete percentages and distributions (Tables 2–3, 6).
- Practical insight: The central finding—weak correlation between readability and skill count—matters for dataset design and benchmarking strategy. The paper argues convincingly that difficult questions can be constructed from easy texts (and vice versa), discouraging overreliance on superficial text complexity as a proxy for task difficulty.
- Annotation quality and transparency: The study reports an overall inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% (on a sample) and documents the protocol, selected metrics, and sampling procedures; exclusions of problematic datasets are justified. The commitment to release annotations increases impact and reproducibility.
- Constructive guidance: The discussion proposes a curriculum for system development based on dataset profiles, and highlights genre effects and question types (local vs global coherence), offering concrete directions for future dataset construction.

Weaknesses
- Difficulty proxy and validation: Equating question difficulty with the count of binary skill labels is a strong assumption. Not all skills are equally hard; interactions matter; and labeling is subjective. There is no validation against human difficulty (e.g., response time/accuracy) or system performance to substantiate this proxy. Without weighting or calibration, conclusions about “difficulty” are tentative.
- Annotation design limits what is measured: Annotators saw the gold answer (and options for MC), and first selected “sentences needed,” then assigned skills. This setup cannot assess retrieval/selection difficulty (which is central to RC) and risks hindsight bias inflating agreement and compressing difficulty. The authors acknowledge this, but it undermines claims about evaluating system competence beyond contextual entailment.
- Limited sampling/statistical rigor: Only 100 questions per dataset (and 62 for agreement) may not capture dataset diversity. Correlations are reported as “weak” without confidence intervals, significance tests, or controls (e.g., passage length). Some analyses (e.g., SQuAD mostly single-sentence; MS MARCO lower sentence distance) echo known dataset characteristics and would benefit from formal testing.
- Readability metrics and scope: The features are drawn from second-language readability and are applied only to the subset of “needed” sentences, which may underestimate discourse-level complexity. Discourse-level features (coherence relations), entity grid, or document structure are not used; the link to RC systems’ processing difficulty is acknowledged as imperfect but not further explored.
- Agreement reporting and coding reliability: The 90.1% agreement is reported without clarifying the measure (percent agreement vs. kappa), per-skill reliability, or coder training/adjudication details. Given the fine-grained distinctions (especially bridging vs elaboration), more reliability analysis is necessary to inspire confidence in the label distributions.

Questions to authors
- Agreement: What statistic is the 90.1% (percent agreement, Cohen’s kappa, Krippendorff’s alpha)? Please report per-skill reliability and details of annotator training and adjudication.
- Difficulty proxy: Did you explore alternative difficulty measures (e.g., weighted skills, interaction terms) or validate skill counts against human difficulty (time/accuracy) or model performance? Any correlation with baseline/model accuracy across datasets?
- Readability scope: Why restrict readability to “needed sentences”? Did you compare results with passage-level readability to assess sensitivity? Any analysis including discourse/coherence features (e.g., entity grid, RST)?
- Statistical significance: Are the reported correlations statistically significant? Please include CIs/p-values and control for confounds like passage length and number of selected sentences.
- Reproducibility: Will you release the full annotations, guidelines, and code for extracting readability features? How were linguistic features computed (tokenization, syllable counts, parsers), and are tools/versions documented?
- Nonsense labeling: What precise criteria were used for “nonsense”? How did you ensure consistency across datasets, especially when some provide meta flags (NewsQA) and others don’t? Could differences in sourcing (and filtering) bias the “nonsense” rates?

Overall, the paper addresses an important gap—characterizing RC datasets beyond surface categories—by proposing a principled skill taxonomy and demonstrating its utility across datasets. Strengthening the validation of the difficulty proxy, providing more rigorous reliability/statistical analyses, and expanding readability modeling would considerably enhance the contribution.
