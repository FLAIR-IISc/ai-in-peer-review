Summary
The paper argues that current RC datasets are poorly characterized by superficial categories (question/answer types) and proposes two complementary evaluation axes: (a) prerequisite skills required to answer a question and (b) readability of the context. The authors refine a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, grounding the knowledge-reasoning portion in distinctions from textual entailment and psycholinguistics (bridging vs elaboration, retrieved vs generated). They annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) on 100 questions each, marking required sentences and the skills needed, compute readability features on those sentences, and analyze correlations. Key finding: weak correlation between readability and number of required skills; datasets can be easy to read yet hard to answer (e.g., MCTest), or hard to read yet comparatively easy to answer (e.g., SQuAD). They release annotations and discuss implications for dataset design and system development.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Cross-dataset manual analysis linking skill requirements and readability, with evidence that readability and question difficulty (measured as number of skills) are only weakly correlated. This is a useful, empirically supported insight for dataset design and evaluation.
2) A refined, theoretically grounded prerequisite skill taxonomy for RC, especially reorganizing knowledge reasoning into bridging/elaboration with connections to TE and cognitive psychology.
3) A publicly released set of annotations (skills, sentences) for six popular RC datasets to support further analysis.

Strengths
- Importance and timeliness: The work addresses a recognized gap: lack of principled, comparable difficulty descriptors for RC datasets beyond surface categories. By focusing on skill requirements and readability, the paper proposes interpretable, actionable metrics for both dataset creators and system builders.
- Theoretically grounded taxonomy: The reorganization of knowledge reasoning using bridging vs elaboration and retrieved vs generated inferences is well-motivated by prior work (TE literature, Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). This adds conceptual clarity absent in earlier skill lists and improves the chance of generalization across datasets.
- Cross-dataset, uniform protocol: Annotating six datasets with a single methodology allows meaningful qualitative comparisons. The results highlight concrete contrasts (e.g., MCTest’s higher multi-sentence requirements and causal/coreference phenomena; WDW’s higher ellipsis; SQuAD’s higher readability complexity but fewer skills).
- Useful empirical findings: The weak correlations between readability and required skills (and illustrative plots) provide evidence that dataset difficulty cannot be inferred from readability alone. The sentence-distance analysis further distinguishes datasets by the extent of cross-sentence reasoning.
- Practical implications: The discussion outlines how these metrics can support curriculum/transfer learning and dataset construction, offering a plausible pathway to more controlled evaluation of RC systems.

Weaknesses
- Limited sample size and sampling details: Only 100 questions per dataset were annotated, often from development sets, with dataset-specific filtering (e.g., NewsQA’s metadata filters). This raises concerns about representativeness and possible sampling bias. The small sample also limits power of correlation analyses.
- Annotation methodology and reliability under-specified: Annotators viewed gold answers and selected sentences before labeling skills, which can bias judgments of “required skills.” The inter-annotator agreement is reported as 90.1% on 62 questions but the metric (e.g., raw agreement vs Cohen’s kappa) and unit of analysis (per-skill, per-question, sentence selection) are unclear. No per-skill reliabilities are reported, and the subjective “nonsense” label lacks operational detail.
- Operationalization of skills is still subjective: While the taxonomy is grounded, mapping concrete questions to multi-label skill requirements is inherently subjective. The paper lacks extensive guidelines, example adjudications, or validation against external signals (e.g., human difficulty ratings or model error patterns) to demonstrate the construct validity of the labels.
- Readability metrics and interpretation: The chosen readability features stem from L2 acquisition and traditional indices (e.g., FK grade) and are applied only to sentences deemed necessary by annotators. This selection can confound readability estimates with annotation decisions. The authors acknowledge misalignment with machine readability; however, conclusions about weak correlation would be stronger if triangulated with alternative text complexity measures (e.g., discourse structure) or computed both on full passages and selected sentences.
- Statistical rigor: Correlations are presented without p-values, confidence intervals, or corrections for multiple comparisons across many metrics. Given small N per dataset and noisy labels, it is hard to assess the robustness of reported effects. Similarly, some dataset-level comparisons would benefit from uncertainty estimates.
- Limited validation with systems: The paper argues for system development benefits but does not empirically relate the skill/readability metrics to system performance across questions (e.g., correlating per-question errors with skill counts or types). Such validation would significantly strengthen the practical relevance.

Questions to authors (optional)
- How exactly was inter-annotator agreement computed (metric, unit, per-skill vs per-question, including sentence selection and “nonsense”)? Can you report per-skill kappa values and brief annotator training/guidelines?
- How were readability metrics computed for multi-sentence selections (aggregation method, tokenization/syllabification specifics)? Did you compute metrics on full contexts to assess selection bias?
- Are correlations statistically significant after multiple-comparison correction? Please provide p-values/intervals.
- Can you validate the skill labels by correlating with per-question system accuracy from strong baselines or human difficulty ratings?
- Will you release the full annotation protocol, code, and data (including sentence spans) to ensure reproducibility?

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem with a thoughtful, theory-informed taxonomy and provides useful cross-dataset insights backed by manual annotation. However, methodological limitations (small and potentially biased samples, under-specified reliability, and limited statistical validation) reduce confidence in the generality of the findings. I view the contribution as valuable primarily as an analysis/resource paper. With clearer reliability reporting, stronger statistical treatment, and at least some validation against system behavior, this would be a solid accept; as is, it is borderline but promising.
