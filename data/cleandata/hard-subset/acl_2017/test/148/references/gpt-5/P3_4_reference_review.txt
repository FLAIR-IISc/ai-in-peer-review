Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes a two-pronged evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) a taxonomy of “prerequisite skills” required to answer questions, refined from prior work and grounded in cognitive psychology (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment analyses (Dagan et al.), and (b) readability metrics (primarily lexical/syntactic features following Vajjala & Meurers). The authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these metrics, analyze cross-dataset trends, and report a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty (approximated by the number of prerequisite skills). They also provide dataset-specific observations (e.g., “nonsense” rates, evidence sentence distances) and discuss implications for dataset design and model development.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by significance)
1) A cross-dataset, cognitively informed annotation and analysis showing that readability of context and question difficulty are only weakly correlated; the resulting qualitative comparison across six RC datasets is useful to researchers.
2) A reorganized prerequisite skills taxonomy that operationalizes knowledge reasoning via bridging vs elaboration distinctions and integrates elements of entailment phenomena.
3) Practical guidance and actionable observations for dataset construction and system development (e.g., curriculum considerations, genre effects, evidence sentence distance/number).

Strengths
- Conceptual integration and taxonomy refinement: The paper thoughtfully connects cognitive models of comprehension (construction–integration, bridging vs elaboration) and NLI/entailment phenomena to a concrete, operational skill taxonomy for RC. The distinctions (e.g., coreference vs bridging vs elaboration; meta-knowledge) are clearly motivated and likely to help future dataset analyses and diagnostics.
- Comparative empirical analysis across six datasets: A uniform annotation protocol applied to diverse datasets yields informative contrasts (e.g., SQuAD = harder-to-read but simpler inference; MCTest = simpler-to-read but richer multi-sentence inference; WDW’s ellipsis and “nonsense” issues; QA4MRE’s multi-clause, technical complexity). These observations are valuable for both dataset creators and practitioners choosing benchmarks.
- Evidence-focused annotation and diagnostics: Selecting sentences necessary to answer and reporting average number and distance of evidence sentences is a useful structural diagnostic of dataset demands (e.g., QA4MRE’s long-distance evidence vs SQuAD’s local evidence).
- Practical implications: The discussion on how to stage system development (from easy-to-read/easy-to-answer upward), the influence of genre (narrative vs expository), and question design beyond local coherence provides concrete takeaways that go beyond a dataset audit.
- Resource promise: The plan to release annotations can facilitate reproducibility and enable others to build on this framework.

Weaknesses
- Difficulty proxy is underspecified and potentially crude: Equating “number of prerequisite skills” with difficulty assumes all skills carry equal weight and ignores within-skill difficulty and interactions. For instance, a single elaboration step can be harder than multiple simple clause relations. A weighted or skill-specific difficulty model (validated against human response times or system error profiles) would be more convincing.
- Annotation validity and reliability concerns: The reported 90.1% inter-annotator agreement is on 62 questions with an unspecified metric. Given multi-label, multi-class judgments, per-skill agreement (e.g., Krippendorff’s alpha/kappa by skill) is essential, particularly for hard-to-differentiate categories like bridging vs elaboration. The paper needs clearer annotation guidelines, adjudication procedures, and reliability statistics.
- Oracle setting limits external validity: Annotators saw the gold answer and selected evidence sentences; retrieval and answer selection (central to RC) are excluded by design. As a result, the analysis primarily captures local entailment demands, not end-to-end RC difficulty. This should be made explicit in conclusions, and claims about dataset difficulty tempered accordingly.
- Readability measurement choices and inconsistency: Although the paper cites Vajjala & Meurers features, the core correlation plots use Flesch–Kincaid (FK), which is not among the chosen top-10 features listed. Moreover, readability is computed on annotator-selected evidence sentences, which can confound the relationship (evidence selections themselves reflect perceived difficulty). Results should be reported for both full contexts and evidence-only segments, and the choice of FK justified vis-à-vis the other metrics; multiple-comparison corrections and effect sizes are needed.
- Limited sample size and sampling bias: Only 100 questions per dataset (often from dev sets) may be underpowered for correlation analyses and not representative of the full distribution. No confidence intervals or significance tests accompany correlation coefficients (0.025–0.416). The “nonsense” label is subjective and filtered differently across datasets (e.g., relying on NewsQA meta flags), complicating cross-dataset comparisons.
- Relation to system performance is untested: The framework’s practical utility for predicting model difficulty is asserted (curriculum/transfer ideas) but not evaluated. Even a small-scale correlation between skill counts and strong baseline errors (or human accuracy/RT) would strengthen the paper.

Questions to authors
- Annotation reliability: Please report per-skill inter-annotator agreement (e.g., kappa or alpha) and macro/micro average across skills. How were disagreements resolved? Can you share the full annotation guidelines with positive/negative examples for bridging vs elaboration vs coreference and for “nonsense”?
- Readability computation: Which exact metrics were used in correlation analyses (FK vs the listed Vajjala & Meurers features)? Why privilege FK in Figures 3–4? Were readability features computed on entire contexts as well as evidence-only sentences? If not, can you provide that comparison?
- Statistical analysis: For the correlations, what are the sample sizes, p-values, and confidence intervals? Did you apply any correction for multiple testing across metrics? How robust are the findings to bootstrapping the 100-question samples?
- Difficulty proxy: Did you consider weighting skills by estimated difficulty or frequency, or modeling specific combinations (e.g., multi-sentence + coreference + bridging) as especially hard? Any evidence from human annotator effort/time or from baseline model error rates supporting the proxy?
- Resource release: Will you release all annotations (skills, evidence sentences, nonsense tags) and code for readability computation to facilitate replication and extension?

Additional comments (minor)
- Tighten references and fix formatting issues.
- Clarify selection of SQuAD items (one question per paragraph): might bias toward simpler questions.
- Consider complementing with discourse features (RST) where available, or acknowledge the limitation more prominently.
