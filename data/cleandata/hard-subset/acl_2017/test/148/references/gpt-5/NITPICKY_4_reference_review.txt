Summary
This paper proposes two complementary classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (1) prerequisite skills (a refined taxonomy of 13 skills, reorganizing prior work with distinctions from textual entailment and psycholinguistics), and (2) readability (a set of linguistic features largely from Vajjala & Meurers, 2012). The authors annotate six RC datasets with the skill taxonomy, select sentences deemed necessary to answer each question, compute readability on those sentences, and analyze correlations between “answering difficulty” (approximated by the number of required skills) and readability. They report weak correlations and qualitative differences across datasets (e.g., QA4MRE is both hard to read and answer; MCTest is easy to read but harder to answer).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A refined, more principled taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, especially reorganizing knowledge reasoning into bridging vs elaboration, plus additions (ellipsis, meta-knowledge), grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology.
2) A multi-dataset human annotation study (6 datasets; 100 questions each) labeling required sentences and skills, with a dataset-level comparative analysis; public release promised.
3) An empirical finding that readability and answering difficulty are only weakly correlated, suggesting they capture distinct dimensions of dataset difficulty.

Strengths
- Topic importance and framing: The paper addresses a real need—evaluating RC datasets beyond coarse question/answer types—and productively bridges insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology to RC evaluation.
- Cross-dataset analysis: Annotating multiple prominent datasets using a uniform protocol provides useful qualitative comparisons (e.g., MCTest vs QA4MRE vs SQuAD). The observations (e.g., automated question sourcing causing “nonsense” questions) are valuable for dataset design.
- Skill taxonomy reorganization: The bridging/elaboration split and explicit inclusion of ellipsis are thoughtful and better grounded than many ad-hoc RC skill lists. The taxonomy is broad yet not overly specific, and plausibly reusable.
- Sentence selection as an analysis lever: Having annotators identify the minimal sentences needed for answering is a pragmatic step towards isolating comprehension phenomena (even if it introduces biases; see below).
- Practical implications: The curriculum/transfer learning discussion is sensible, and the idea to place datasets on a continuum using these metrics is actionable for system development.

Weaknesses
- Validation of the “difficulty = number of skills” proxy: The core difficulty measure is subjective and unvalidated. There is no item-level validation against human difficulty judgments or system accuracy. Without such validation, the main correlation claim (weak relation between readability and answering difficulty) rests on an unverified construct.
- Insufficient and potentially misleading reliability reporting: “Inter-annotator agreement was 90.1% for 62 questions,” but the metric (percent agreement? multi-label variant? κ/α?) is unspecified, the sample is small, and aggregation across 13 labels is unclear. No per-skill agreement or adjudication protocol is reported. Given the subtle boundaries (e.g., bridging vs elaboration), reliability is crucial.
- Methodological biases in readability measurement: Readability is computed only on annotator-selected sentences, not the full context. This conflates the dependent and independent variables (since sentence selection is part of the annotation and likely correlated with skill labels), and can bias readability downward (short, local evidence). It also makes results incomparable across datasets with different context lengths. Additionally, Flesch–Kincaid is used in analysis but not listed among the chosen features (Fig. 2 lists Coleman–Liau but not F-K), creating inconsistency.
- Missing computational details for readability features: Computing CN/C, DC/C, CoOrd, ModVar, AdvVar requires parsing/POS-tagging. The paper does not specify the tools, versions, training domains, preprocessing (tokenization, sentence splitting), or AWL handling (lemmatization/stemming, case). Parser errors (especially on children’s narratives) could substantially affect metrics. No robustness checks are presented.
- Sampling inconsistencies and small sample sizes: Only 100 questions per dataset, with differing selection protocols (e.g., one question per SQuAD paragraph; mixed main/aux for QA4MRE; filtered NewsQA; MS MARCO segments with is_selected=1). These choices introduce nontrivial, unquantified biases, reducing comparability and statistical power.
- Statistical reporting and table inconsistencies: Correlations (0.025–0.416) are reported without p-values/CI or multiple-comparison corrections; it is unclear if computed per-dataset or pooled. Table 3’s average for QA4MRE (3.25) conflicts with text (4.6). Sentence distance is defined ambiguously (“sum of distances of any two sentences” for >2). Figures/Tables are partially missing (e.g., Tables 1, 4, 5), and several values/axes in the provided snippets are hard to interpret.
- Nonsense labeling is underspecified: The “nonsense” category is subjective, and criteria are not operationalized. The high rates for some datasets (e.g., WDW, MS MARCO) may reflect annotation choices rather than inherent dataset flaws; more rigorous definitions and cross-checks with dataset-provided flags are needed.
- Overlapping skill definitions and limited exemplification: Boundaries among coreference vs bridging vs elaboration are subtle; the paper would benefit from more annotated examples and decision rules. Meta-knowledge appears rarely used (1%), raising questions about its necessity or the annotation’s sensitivity.

Questions to authors
1) How did you compute inter-annotator agreement for a multi-label setting? Please specify the statistic, unit of analysis (per-skill, per-question), sample size rationale, and per-skill agreement. How were disagreements resolved?
2) How were skill labels aggregated for analysis (majority vote? union? intersection)? Did you assess how aggregation choice affects the “number of skills” measure?
3) Can you validate the “number of required skills” proxy against human difficulty (e.g., response time/accuracy) or system performance at the item level? Without this, why should we treat it as difficulty?
4) Why compute readability on selected sentences rather than full contexts? Did you compare both variants? How sensitive are the conclusions to this choice?
5) Which tools and settings were used to compute syntactic/lexical readability features (parser, tagger, sentence splitter, tokenization, AWL processing)? Any domain adaptation? Error analysis?
6) Please reconcile the inconsistency between the reported QA4MRE average number of skills (4.6 in text) and Table 3 (3.25).
7) You use Flesch–Kincaid in Figures 3–4, but it is not listed in Figure 2. Which readability metrics are actually used in correlation analyses? Provide the full Table 5 with per-metric, per-dataset correlations and statistical significance.
8) How exactly was “nonsense” operationalized? Did you measure agreement on the nonsense label? For datasets with built-in flags (e.g., NewsQA), how do your labels align?
9) The selection protocols differ across datasets (e.g., SQuAD: 1 question per paragraph). Did you analyze sensitivity to these choices or provide re-weighting to improve comparability?
10) The definition of sentence “distance” for >2 sentences is unclear. Please formalize it and justify its interpretability.

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem with a promising taxonomy and interesting cross-dataset observations, but the methodological and reporting issues above undermine confidence in the main claims. I encourage a revision focusing on validation of the difficulty proxy, clearer/consistent statistics, detailed computational/annotation protocols, and sensitivity analyses.
