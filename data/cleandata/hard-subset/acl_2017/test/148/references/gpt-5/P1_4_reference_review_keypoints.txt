- Summary: Proposes a refined taxonomy of RC prerequisite skills, annotates six RC datasets with skills and minimal evidence sentences, computes readability on those sentences, and analyzes skill–readability correlations (finding weak correlations) while releasing the resource.
- Primary contributions: cross-dataset annotation/resource, theoretically motivated taxonomy, and empirical finding that readability weakly correlates with required skills.

Strengths
- Theory-informed taxonomy: Reorganizes "commonsense" into bridging, elaboration, ellipsis, meta-knowledge, etc., grounded in textual entailment and cognitive models.
- Cross-dataset, controlled annotation: Uniform protocol across six datasets with evidence selection and reported overall IAA (90.1% on 62 items).
- Evidence-span readability: Measuring readability on minimal answer sentences yields finer-grained, more relevant metrics than full-passage readability.
- Practical dataset insights: Identifies dataset-specific demands (e.g., MCTest multi-sentence reasoning, SQuAD single-sentence) and quality issues in curated datasets.
- Sentence-level analyses: Number and distance of required sentences provide interpretable measures of multi-sentence reasoning needs.

Weaknesses
- Unvalidated difficulty proxy: Using skill count as question difficulty lacks validation against human or system performance.
- Annotation bias risk: Annotators saw questions and answers, which may bias toward minimal justifications and undercount skills used in real solving.
- Limited sampling and statistical rigor: Only 100 items per dataset, inconsistent reported numbers, and missing per-dataset correlations/confidence intervals.
- Subjectivity in skill categories: Boundaries between categories (bridging vs. elaboration, ellipsis, meta-knowledge) remain ambiguous with limited per-skill IAA reported.
- Readability metric choice and interpretation unclear: Emphasis on Flesch–Kincaid vs. other top features is unexplained and robustness across metrics is not shown.

Questions for authors
- Can you validate skill-count as a difficulty proxy via correlations with human accuracy/response time or model performance?
- How was inter-annotator agreement computed; please report per-skill and evidence-selection agreement and reconciliation procedure.
- How was "nonsense" operationalized, were decision rules and IAA for this label provided?
- How representative are the 100-question samples and was any sensitivity analysis performed across seeds?
- Please reconcile numeric inconsistencies (e.g., QA4MRE 4.6 vs. Table 3's 3.25) and provide confidence intervals.
- Why emphasize Flesch–Kincaid in figures; are correlations consistent across Coleman–Liau, syntactic ratios, and lexical variation, with r/p values per dataset?

Additional comments
- References and formatting need cleanup for consistency.
- Please specify availability and license for guidelines, code, and data.
- Consider reporting full-passage readability for comparison and annotator-based variance in evidence spans.

Overall
- Timely, useful diagnostic study and resource; needs stronger validation, statistical rigor, and clearer annotation reliability to increase impact.
