Summary and Claimed Contributions
This paper proposes a two-pronged framework to evaluate reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (i) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills required to answer questions (drawing on textual entailment and psychology), and (ii) readability metrics reflecting surface-level linguistic complexity. The authors annotate six RC datasets with the skill taxonomy and sentence-level evidence, compute readability over the selected sentences, and analyze relationships between skill requirements and readability. The central empirical finding is a generally weak correlation between readability and question difficulty (operationalized as the number of skills required). They release the annotations.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order of strength) are:
1) A comparative, cross-dataset annotation resource that tags questions with prerequisite skills and supporting sentences, enabling qualitative and quantitative analyses across six RC datasets.
2) A refined, empirically usable skill taxonomy that integrates bridging vs. elaboration and other inference distinctions from TE and cognitive psychology.
3) Empirical evidence that question difficulty and text readability are only weakly correlated, with dataset-specific insights useful for dataset design and system evaluation.

Strengths
- Cross-dataset, evidence-centric annotation and release: Annotating six heterogeneous datasets with multi-label prerequisite skills and sentence highlights is valuable infrastructure. It enables apples-to-apples comparisons beyond accuracy or shallow categories (wh-/answer type) and supports downstream analyses (e.g., sentence distance, skill frequency). Public release enhances reproducibility and community utility.

- Thoughtful integration of theory for skill taxonomy: The reorganization of “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs. elaboration, and inclusion of other phenomena (ellipsis, meta-knowledge, logical/mathematical reasoning), is better grounded than prior ad hoc lists. The taxonomy is sufficiently broad to cover common RC phenomena without being overly fine-grained.

- Clear, dataset-level insights with practical implications: The paper identifies characteristic skill profiles (e.g., MCTest higher in causal/spatiotemporal/co-reference; WDW higher in ellipsis) and documents “nonsense” prevalence in automatically curated datasets. These findings can guide dataset construction (e.g., controlling for question types/skills) and system evaluation (e.g., curriculum/transfer learning trajectories).

- Evidence that readability is not a proxy for RC difficulty: The weak correlations (and the QA4MRE/MCTest/SQuAD contrasts) concretely show why evaluating datasets solely by surface complexity is misleading. This is a useful corrective that encourages richer diagnostic metrics for both datasets and systems.

- Solid, transparent methodology for sentence-level analysis: Selecting required sentences and reporting sentence count/distance per question is a helpful addition that begins to operationalize multi-sentence reasoning demands, with interpretable patterns across datasets.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation scope omits key RC competencies by design: Annotators see the gold answer (and options for MC), then select supporting sentences and skills. This evaluates contextual entailment understanding, not the end-to-end RC pipeline (evidence retrieval, disambiguation, distractor resistance). While acknowledged, the limitation is substantial: many modern RC errors stem from retrieval and answer selection. The conclusions about dataset “difficulty” are therefore partial.

- Annotation reliability and operationalization are under-specified: Only percent agreement (90.1%) over 62 items is reported; no chance-corrected measures (e.g., Krippendorff’s alpha) for multi-label coding, no per-skill agreement, and limited detail on an annotation manual or adjudication. Several skills (e.g., coreference vs. bridging; bridging vs. elaboration; meta-knowledge) can overlap. Without clearer operational criteria and reliability per skill, the validity of fine-grained frequency comparisons is hard to assess.

- Sampling and statistical analysis are limited: Using 100 questions per dataset (with bespoke sampling per dataset) risks biases (e.g., SQuAD “one Q per paragraph” may under-sample multi-sentence questions; NewsQA filtering may remove genuine hard/ambiguous cases). Correlations are reported but significance, CIs, and multiple-comparison handling are not detailed. Findings such as “weak correlation” would be stronger with more rigorous statistical treatment and ablations (e.g., controlling for genre, passage length).

- Readability feature set is narrow and may be misaligned: The analysis relies mainly on a small set of lexical/syntactic features (plus FK and Coleman–Liau) and excludes discourse-level features known to affect comprehension. Measuring readability only over “required sentences” may underestimate global coherence demands and document-level complexity. The conclusion that readability and difficulty are weakly correlated might shift under richer readability/discourse metrics.

- “Nonsense” labeling is ad hoc and potentially conflated: The criterion for nonsense/unsolvable is not fully operationalized, and agreement on this label is not reported. Some questions solvable via background knowledge may be labeled solvable without context, blurring the target construct. Since “nonsense” rates influence dataset conclusions (e.g., MS MARCO, WDW), this warrants tighter definitions, examples, and reliability measures.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide the full annotation guidelines with concrete positive/negative examples for borderline categories (coreference vs. bridging, bridging vs. elaboration, meta-knowledge vs. elaboration)? How did you resolve overlaps?

- What are the chance-corrected inter-annotator reliability scores (e.g., Krippendorff’s alpha) for multi-label skill annotations overall and per skill? What is the reliability for “nonsense” labeling and for sentence selection?

- Why was IAA computed on only 62 questions? Is there a reason this subset is representative?

- Did you test statistical significance and report confidence intervals for the correlations in Table 5? Were corrections applied for multiple tests across features and datasets?

- How sensitive are your findings to measuring readability over entire passages vs. only selected sentences? Can you report correlations under both settings?

- Where will the released annotations and code be hosted, and under what license? Is there a plan to include the coding manual and annotator training materials?

Additional Comments and Suggestions
- Strengthen validity: report per-skill IAA, provide a detailed coding manual, and add chance-corrected metrics. Include adjudication procedures and examples of disagreements.

- Expand analysis: include discourse-level readability features (e.g., entity grid/cohesion, discourse relations), and report results both for selected sentences and full contexts.

- Improve statistics: add CIs, hypothesis tests, and mixed-effects models controlling for genre, passage length, and dataset. Consider partial correlations between readability and skill count controlling for confounds.

- Link to system performance: correlate skill counts and sentence distance with baseline/model accuracies across datasets to validate that “more skills” and “longer distances” correspond to higher empirical difficulty.

- Revisit sampling: increase sample sizes per dataset and provide uniform sampling protocols to reduce bias; report sensitivity analyses across sampling strategies.

Overall, this paper provides a useful resource and a principled framing. With stronger reliability reporting, broader readability modeling, and tighter statistical analysis, it could become a solid reference for RC dataset diagnostics and system evaluation.
