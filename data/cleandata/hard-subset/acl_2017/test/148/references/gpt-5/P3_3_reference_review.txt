Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes two complementary classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (i) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills needed to answer a question from its context (13 skill categories, reorganizing prior work via connections to textual entailment and psycholinguistics), and (ii) readability measures capturing lexical and syntactic surface complexity. The authors annotate 100 examples from each of six RC datasets with both the required skills and the subset of context sentences deemed necessary, then analyze dataset profiles and the correlation between “difficulty to answer” (approximated by number of skills required) and readability. They report weak correlations, arguing that text readability and question difficulty are largely independent, and release the annotations.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) A refined, psychology- and entailment-informed taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC and a cross-dataset human annotation study applying it to six datasets.
2) Empirical profiling of datasets (skill frequencies, number of skills, sentence distance) and the finding that readability of context does not strongly correlate with skill-based question difficulty.
3) Practical guidance and qualitative insights for dataset construction and curriculum-like use of datasets; release of annotations.

Strengths
1) Cross-dataset, genre-aware analysis with consistent annotation protocol. Annotating MCTest (narratives), SQuAD/NewsQA (Wikipedia/news), QA4MRE (technical), MS MARCO/WDW (web/news, semi-automatic) under a unified framework yields informative, comparable profiles. The reported skill distributions (e.g., high ellipsis in WDW, high bridging/elaboration in QA4MRE) match intuitive properties of the sources and creation pipelines, lending face validity to the framework.

2) Thoughtful refinement of the skill taxonomy grounded in prior theory. The reorganization of “commonsense” into bridging vs. elaboration, plus the inclusion of ellipsis and meta-knowledge, maps cleanly to traditions in textual entailment and psycholinguistics (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano). This improves conceptual clarity over earlier ad hoc taxonomies.

3) Useful, reproducible operationalization via sentence selection. Asking annotators to first mark the sentences needed to answer, then label skills on that evidence supports consistent comparisons across formats (span extraction, MCQ, multi-passage). This also enables secondary analyses (e.g., sentence distance), offering a proxy for multi-sentence reasoning demands.

4) Actionable observations for dataset design. The demonstration that “easy-to-read but hard-to-answer” datasets (e.g., MCTest) are feasible, and that readability cannot stand in for question difficulty, is a timely corrective for dataset construction. The paper’s discussion on genre, sourcing methods, and meta-information (e.g., “is question bad”) is practically valuable.

5) Public release of annotations. Even if small, such annotations can seed further work on skill-aware benchmarking, evaluation, and dataset auditing.

Weaknesses
1) Limited empirical rigor and sample size; incomplete reliability reporting. Only 100 items per dataset are annotated, and agreement is reported as a single “90.1%” over 62 questions with no definition (percent agreement? per-label? multi-label? Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa?), no per-skill reliability, and no details on adjudication or training. Given the subjectivity of categories like bridging vs. elaboration, stronger reliability evidence is needed to support fine-grained frequency claims and cross-dataset comparisons.

2) Readability analysis design choices undercut conclusions. Readability is computed only on annotator-selected sentences, not full passages. This selection may homogenize texts (removing hard sentences not needed for the answer), artificially weakening correlations. Moreover, while Vajjala & Meurers-inspired features are listed, the correlation plots use Flesch–Kincaid grade level, which is not among the top features emphasized earlier; statistical methods (Pearson r only) lack p-values, confidence intervals, or multiple-comparison controls. The reported r values (0.025–0.416) could arise from design and sampling artifacts; stronger, preregistered analyses (e.g., mixed models controlling for passage length, genre, dataset) would bolster claims.

3) Utility for system evaluation is asserted but not demonstrated. The paper motivates the metrics as helpful for system development (e.g., curriculum learning), but there is no empirical validation showing that skill profiles predict model performance, error types, or generalization. Without correlating the skill/readability profiles with actual system outcomes, it is difficult to assess the practical impact of the proposed metrics for model evaluation.

4) Handling of “nonsense”/unsolvable questions is ad hoc. Annotators can label “nonsense,” and conclusions about dataset quality (e.g., MS MARCO/WDW) rely on these judgments, yet criteria and reliability are not reported. Some questions can be answered without the context via world knowledge; how these are treated vis-à-vis “nonsense” is unclear. This undermines claims about dataset cleanliness.

5) Scope and coverage limitations. Several major datasets are excluded (e.g., CNN/DM, CBTest, LAMBADA), sometimes for fixable reasons (e.g., anonymization, casing). While understandable, the omissions reduce generality. The study also conflates MC160/MC500 and uses only dev/gold subsets, which may not reflect training distributions.

Questions to authors
- How exactly was inter-annotator agreement computed for multi-label skill annotations? Please report per-skill kappa (or alpha) and agreement on sentence selection and “nonsense” labels, plus annotation guidelines and adjudication procedures.
- Why compute readability on selected sentences only? Could you add analyses on full passages and compare results? Also report p-values/confidence intervals for all correlations and control for passage length, dataset, and genre.
- Can you clarify the use of Flesch–Kincaid vs. the Vajjala & Meurers feature set in correlations? Which metrics were finally used in Table 5, and why?
- How was “nonsense” operationalized? Provide examples, decision rules, and reliability. How are questions solvable without context (via world knowledge) treated?
- Do your skill counts predict model performance? Even a small-scale analysis (e.g., correlating baseline QA accuracy per item with skill count/types) would substantiate the system-development claims.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated and thoughtfully grounded analysis that can help the community reason about dataset properties beyond surface categories. However, the empirical design (small samples, incomplete reliability/statistics, and limited validation) weakens the strength of the conclusions, and the claimed utility for system evaluation is not demonstrated. I view this as a valuable dataset-auditing resource and a step toward standardized skill taxonomies, but in its current form it feels more suitable for a workshop or as a dataset-analytics companion than for acceptance at ACL. Strengthening reliability reporting, statistical rigor, and adding even a modest system-performance validation would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
