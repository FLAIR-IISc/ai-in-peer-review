Summary
The paper proposes a two-pronged evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) a refined taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills (drawing on psycholinguistics and textual entailment literature) to characterize the cognitive/linguistic demands of answering questions, and (b) a set of readability features (largely from Vajjala & Meurers 2012) to characterize the difficulty of reading the relevant text. The authors annotate 100 questions each from six RC datasets with the skill taxonomy and select the sentences required to answer. They then compute readability on the selected sentences and analyze frequencies of skills, number of skills per question, and correlations between readability and skills, concluding that readability and answering difficulty are only weakly correlated. They provide dataset-level qualitative observations and argue the framework can guide dataset construction and system development.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A refined operational taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, especially reorganizing “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs elaboration (and related categories) grounded in psycholinguistics and TE literature.
2) A cross-dataset human annotation study (6 datasets, 100 questions each) that profiles skill requirements and readability on the answer-relevant sentences, yielding the empirical observation that readability and question difficulty correlate weakly.
3) Dataset-level diagnostic insights (e.g., QA4MRE harder on both axes; MCTest easy-to-read yet relatively demanding; WDW’s higher ellipsis due to sourcing), and a promise to release annotations.

Strengths
- Problem framing and grounding: The need to go beyond coarse question/answer types is real, and anchoring the skill taxonomy in Kintsch/McNamara and TE literature is conceptually solid. The bridging/elaboration split is appropriate and useful for RC analysis.
- Cross-dataset perspective: Annotating six diverse datasets with a uniform protocol is valuable and yields interpretable contrasts that practitioners can use (e.g., MCTest as easy-to-read but cognitively nontrivial).
- Sentence-level relevance selection: Asking annotators to mark the sentences needed to answer focuses the readability computation on the actually-read text, which is a thoughtful design choice for analyzing human-like comprehension demands.
- Practical takeaways: The curriculum-style suggestion (stage systems/datasets along the two axes) is sensible. The observations on dataset curation (e.g., automated sourcing leading to “nonsense”) are actionable for dataset builders.

Weaknesses
- Methodological bias due to answer exposure: Annotators saw the gold answer while labeling skills and selecting sentences. This risks hindsight bias, inflating agreement and potentially underestimating the true reasoning required (e.g., shortcutting to the minimal span that justifies the known answer). A blinded protocol (annotate with only question/context; reveal answer post hoc) or at least a control subset is needed.
- Inadequate reliability reporting: “Inter-annotator agreement was 90.1% for 62 questions” is reported as raw agreement, not chance-corrected (e.g., Cohen’s/Fleiss’ κ), and only on a ~10% subsample. Per-skill κ and agreement on sentence selection are necessary, as is justification for the small reliability sample.
- Statistical rigor and uncertainty: Correlations are reported as “weak 0.025–0.416” without confidence intervals, significance tests, or correction for multiple metrics. With only 100 items per dataset (and mixed sources), claims about weak correlation should be tested (e.g., partial correlations controlling for dataset/context length; mixed-effects models). No variance estimates are provided for skill frequencies or mean skills per question.
- Readability measurement choice and comparability: Readability is computed only on annotator-selected sentences, which themselves are influenced by answer exposure; selection strategies differ across datasets (e.g., SQuAD’s single-sentence answers vs QA4MRE’s longer contexts), confounding cross-dataset comparisons. Moreover, some reported measures (e.g., Flesch–Kincaid) are not in the top-10 features adopted from Vajjala & Meurers; the rationale for mixing metrics is unclear. Implementation details (tokenization, syllabification, AWL version) are missing.
- Ambiguous/overlapping skill definitions and operationalization: The boundary between bridging and elaboration is hard to enforce; “ellipsis” spans many phenomena; “meta-knowledge” is underspecified; “punctuation” as a skill is debatable. Without detailed guidelines and adjudication criteria, reproducibility is limited. The taxonomy is presented as model-agnostic, but its constructs are coarse and may conflate linguistic with task artifacts (e.g., MC choice vs span extraction).
- Presentation and reproducibility issues: The paper has numerous formatting and referencing errors (broken citations/years, dangling footnote markers, malformed URLs, missing/garbled entries), unclear or missing tables/figures referenced in text, and an ambiguous definition of the “sentence distance” measure (“sum of distances of any two sentences”). The promised public release of annotations/tools is not evidenced in the submission.

Questions to authors
1) Can you report chance-corrected IAA (κ) per skill and for sentence selection, on the full set or a larger subset? Also provide agreement/confusion between bridging vs elaboration vs ellipsis.
2) How sensitive are your results to answer exposure? Please re-run a blinded annotation on a subset (without revealing the gold answer) and compare skill frequencies, selected spans, and readability.
3) How exactly were readability metrics computed (tokenization, syllable counting algorithm, AWL list version, sentence segmentation)? Why include Flesch–Kincaid in figures if it was not among the chosen top-10 features?
4) Can you provide significance tests and confidence intervals for correlations and means, and control for dataset/context length (e.g., mixed-effects or partial correlations)?
5) Please clarify and formalize the sentence distance metric. Why sum of pairwise distances rather than max/min/average? How does this scale with number of sentences?
6) Will you release the full annotation guidelines, adjudication protocol, code, and the annotated spans/skills to ensure reproducibility?

Recommendation
Reject. The idea of a two-axis evaluation (skills vs readability) and the cross-dataset annotation are promising, but the current submission has methodological biases (answer exposure), insufficient reliability/statistical analysis, ambiguous operational definitions, and substantial presentation/reproducibility issues. With a blinded protocol, proper reliability/statistical treatment, clearer definitions/guidelines, and cleaned presentation plus released resources, this work could be much stronger.
