- Studies interaction between answering difficulty and reading difficulty in RC datasets, proposing a 13-skill taxonomy and readability metrics, and annotating 100 questions each from six datasets.
- Contribution: refines prerequisite skills for RC, reorganizing reasoning into bridging vs elaboration and adding ellipsis and meta-knowledge aligned with psycholinguistics and RTE.
- Contribution: conducts a cross-dataset human annotation study on six popular RC datasets with a unified skill schema and promises to release annotations.
- Contribution: provides empirical evidence that readability weakly correlates with required skill counts and offers dataset-specific observations and implications for curriculum/transfer.
- Strength: taxonomy is theoretically grounded and interpretable, connecting RC skills to established cognitive and entailment theories.
- Strength: consistent cross-dataset annotation yields useful contrasts (e.g., SQuAD harder to read but easier to answer; MCTest requires multi-sentence reasoning).
- Strength: central finding—that readability and answering difficulty are largely decoupled—has practical implications for dataset design and benchmarking.
- Strength: reports high annotation quality and transparency, with a reported 90.1% agreement and documented protocol and sampling procedures.
- Strength: offers constructive guidance for system development curricula and dataset construction based on genre and coherence effects.
- Weakness: equating difficulty with count of binary skill labels is a strong assumption—skills vary in difficulty and interactions matter; no validation against human or model difficulty.
- Weakness: annotation design (annotators saw gold answers and selected sentences first) cannot assess retrieval difficulty and risks hindsight bias inflating agreement.
- Weakness: limited sampling (100 questions per dataset) and lack of statistical rigor (no CIs, p-values, or controls) may weaken claims about correlations and dataset characteristics.
- Weakness: readability features focus on sentence-level L2 metrics and omit discourse-level complexity (entity grid, coherence relations), potentially underestimating text difficulty.
- Weakness: agreement reporting is incomplete—90.1% lacks clarification of metric, per-skill reliability, and coder training/adjudication details.
- Question: clarify the 90.1% agreement statistic (percent agreement vs. kappa/alpha) and provide per-skill reliability and annotator training/adjudication details.
- Question: did you explore weighted skills, interaction effects, or validate skill counts against human response time/accuracy or model performance?
- Question: why restrict readability to “needed sentences” and did you compare results to passage-level readability or discourse/coherence feature analyses?
- Question: are reported correlations statistically significant—please include confidence intervals, p-values, and controls for confounds like passage length and number of sentences?
- Question: will you release full annotations, guidelines, and code for readability feature extraction, and provide details on tools/versions used for linguistic features?
- Question: what exact criteria defined “nonsense,” and how was consistency ensured across datasets with differing metadata and filtering?
- Overall: paper fills an important gap with a principled skill taxonomy and cross-dataset profiling but needs stronger validation of the difficulty proxy, more rigorous reliability/statistical analyses, and expanded readability modeling.
