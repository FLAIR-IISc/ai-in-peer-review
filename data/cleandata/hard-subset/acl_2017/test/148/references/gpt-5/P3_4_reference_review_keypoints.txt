- Paper proposes a two-pronged RC evaluation: a cognitively grounded prerequisite-skills taxonomy and readability metrics for contexts/questions.  
- Authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) and analyze cross-dataset trends.  
- Main reported finding: readability and question difficulty (proxied by number of prerequisite skills) show only weak correlation.  
- Dataset-specific diagnostics: reported “nonsense” rates, evidence sentence counts and distances, and other dataset idiosyncrasies.  

Contributions:
- Cross-dataset, cognitively informed annotation showing weak readability–difficulty correlation and providing qualitative dataset comparisons.  
- Reorganized prerequisite-skills taxonomy distinguishing bridging vs elaboration and integrating entailment phenomena.  
- Practical, actionable guidance for dataset construction and system development (curriculum, genre effects, evidence distance).  

Strengths:
- Strong conceptual integration of cognitive comprehension models and NLI into an operational skill taxonomy.  
- Informative comparative analysis across six diverse RC datasets with clear contrasts (e.g., SQuAD vs MCTest).  
- Evidence-focused annotations (evidence sentence selection, number, distance) provide useful structural diagnostics.  
- Practical implications for staging system development and dataset/question design.  
- Promised release of annotations to facilitate reproducibility and future work.  

Weaknesses:
- Difficulty proxy is underspecified and crude: counting skills assumes equal weight and ignores within-skill difficulty/interactions.  
- Annotation validity/reliability concerns: 90.1% agreement on 62 questions uses an unspecified metric; per-skill agreement and adjudication procedures are missing.  
- Oracle annotation setup (annotators see gold answers and select evidence) limits external validity for end-to-end RC.  
- Readability measurement choices inconsistent: Figures use Flesch–Kincaid despite citing Vajjala & Meurers features; readability computed on evidence-only may confound results.  
- Small sample size and sampling bias: only 100 questions per dataset, no confidence intervals/p-values, and subjective “nonsense” labeling varies by dataset.  
- Framework not validated against system or human performance; practical utility for predicting model difficulty is untested.  

Questions for authors:
- Provide per-skill inter-annotator agreement (kappa/alpha), adjudication details, and full annotation guidelines with examples.  
- Clarify which readability metrics were used in analyses, why FK is highlighted, and comparisons of full-context vs evidence-only readability.  
- Report statistical details for correlations: sample sizes, p-values, confidence intervals, corrections for multiple tests, and robustness checks (bootstrapping).  
- Did you consider weighting skills or modeling specific skill combinations; any supporting evidence from annotator time or baseline model errors?  
- Will you release all annotations (skills, evidence sentences, nonsense tags) and code for readability computation?  

Minor comments:
- Tighten and fix reference/formatting issues.  
- Clarify SQuAD selection procedure (one question per paragraph) as it may bias difficulty.  
- Consider adding or acknowledging discourse-level features (e.g., RST) as a limitation or future direction.
