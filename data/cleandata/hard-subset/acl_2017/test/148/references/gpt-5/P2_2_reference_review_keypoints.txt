- Paper proposes two evaluation axes for RC datasets: prerequisite skills and context readability.
- Authors refine a 13-item prerequisite skill taxonomy, grounding knowledge reasoning in bridging vs. elaboration and retrieved vs. generated inferences.
- Six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) were annotated (100 questions each) for required sentences and skills; readability features computed on selected sentences.
- Key finding: weak correlation between readability and number of required skills; datasets can be easy to read but hard to answer (e.g., MCTest) or hard to read but comparatively easy to answer (e.g., SQuAD).
- Annotations and analyses are released to support dataset design and system development.
- Contribution: cross-dataset manual analysis showing readability and skill requirements are only weakly correlated, informing dataset design and evaluation.
- Contribution: theoretically grounded refinement of skill taxonomy, reorganizing knowledge reasoning with connections to textual entailment and psycholinguistics.
- Contribution: public release of skill and sentence annotations for six popular RC datasets.
- Strength: addresses an important gap by proposing interpretable metrics beyond surface categories for RC difficulty.
- Strength: taxonomy is well-motivated by prior TE and cognitive psychology literature, adding conceptual clarity.
- Strength: uniform annotation protocol across datasets enables meaningful qualitative comparisons and highlights concrete dataset contrasts.
- Strength: empirical findings (including sentence-distance analysis) demonstrate that readability alone does not determine dataset difficulty.
- Strength: discussion offers practical implications for curriculum learning, transfer, and dataset construction.
- Weakness: small sample size (100 questions per dataset) and dataset-specific filtering raise concerns about representativeness and sampling bias.
- Weakness: annotation procedure and inter-annotator agreement are under-specified (metric, unit of analysis, per-skill reliabilities not reported).
- Weakness: skill labeling remains subjective with limited operational guidelines, adjudication examples, or external validation for construct validity.
- Weakness: readability metrics applied only to annotator-selected sentences may confound readability with annotation choices and omit discourse-level measures.
- Weakness: statistical reporting lacks p-values, confidence intervals, and multiple-comparison corrections, limiting robustness claims.
- Weakness: no empirical validation linking skill/readability metrics to system performance or human difficulty ratings.
- Questions for authors: report exact IAA computation, per-skill kappas, and annotator guidelines.
- Questions for authors: clarify readability computation for multi-sentence selections and whether metrics were computed on full contexts.
- Questions for authors: provide statistical significance tests and corrections for multiple comparisons.
- Questions for authors: validate skill labels against system accuracy or human difficulty ratings.
- Questions for authors: confirm release of full annotation protocol, code, and sentence-span data.
- Overall assessment: important, theory-informed analysis and resource with useful insights but limited by methodological issues; promising as an analysis/resource paper contingent on stronger reliability, statistical treatment, and empirical validation.
