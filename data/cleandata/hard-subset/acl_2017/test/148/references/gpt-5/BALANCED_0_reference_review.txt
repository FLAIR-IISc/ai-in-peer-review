Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes two complementary classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (1) prerequisite skills required to answer questions (a refined taxonomy grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology), and (2) readability metrics characterizing the surface difficulty of the context text. The authors annotate six RC datasets with these metrics, analyze distributions of skills and readability, and report that question difficulty (as measured by number of skills) and text readability are only weakly correlated. They release the annotations.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A cross-dataset manual annotation study characterizing RC questions by a refined set of prerequisite skills and linking these to text readability. This yields practical, comparative insights into dataset properties (e.g., MCTest is easy to read but can require multiple skills; SQuAD passages are hard to read but questions may be answerable with few skills).
2) A reorganization of the “prerequisite skills” taxonomy, especially knowledge reasoning, drawing on bridging vs. elaboration inferences and entailment phenomena, to provide a more principled categorization.
3) Public release of annotations that may support future dataset construction and targeted system evaluation.

Strengths
1) Conceptual integration across fields: The paper thoughtfully bridges cognitive psychology (construction–integration model; bridging vs. elaboration) and textual entailment analyses to ground the RC skills taxonomy. This improves on ad hoc skill lists and provides clearer interpretability.
2) Cross-dataset, fine-grained qualitative analysis: Annotating six datasets with the same framework enables comparative observations that are useful to both dataset builders and system designers (e.g., different genres and sourcing methods emphasize different skills; automatic question sourcing can increase ellipsis/bridging but also “nonsense” items).
3) Practical guidance and actionable insights: The finding of weak correlation between readability and skill requirements cautions against using surface readability to proxy question difficulty. The proposed curriculum-like pathway for system development, and observations about narrative vs. expository genres, are concrete and helpful.
4) Transparent annotation protocol and additional signals: Selecting sentences needed to answer and labeling “nonsense” offers extra utility; the sentence-distance analysis starts to probe multi-sentence reasoning and locality.
5) Resource value: If made available as claimed, the annotations could support future benchmarking, error analysis, and dataset design.

Weaknesses
1) Methodological limitations and potential biases in difficulty measurement: The central assumption that “number of required skills” linearly reflects question difficulty is unvalidated. Skills vary in granularity and cognitive load; multiple easy skills might not be harder than one difficult skill. No weighting, calibration, or human difficulty validation is provided, and there is no model-based validation correlating skill counts with system error rates.
2) Small, potentially unrepresentative samples and limited reliability reporting: Only 100 questions per dataset are annotated. Inter-annotator agreement is reported as 90.1% for 62 questions, but the metric (percentage agreement vs. kappa/F1) and per-skill agreement are not provided. Sentence selection agreement is also unreported. These issues make it difficult to assess reliability, especially for subjective labels like bridging/elaboration and “nonsense.”
3) Inconsistencies and missing details in results: There are apparent discrepancies (e.g., text says QA4MRE averages 4.6 skills while Table 3 shows 3.25). Flesch–Kincaid is used in figures but not listed among the top-10 features adopted (Figure 2); clarity on which readability features are used where is needed. Correlation magnitudes are reported without per-dataset breakdowns, confidence intervals, or significance tests. Some referenced tables/figures (e.g., Tables 4–5) are not fully shown.
4) Readability measurement may be confounded and weakly tied to RC: Readability is computed only on annotator-selected sentences, which can bias correlations (skill identification and sentence selection are not independent). The chosen features (e.g., AWL, Coleman–Liau) target human readability and are highly collinear with length-based cues; more RC-relevant features (e.g., discourse structure, coreference density) are not explored.
5) Limited scope regarding “system performance” claims: Despite the stated goal to analyze both system performance and dataset quality, there is no empirical linkage between the proposed metrics and model behavior (e.g., stratified evaluation of systems by skills/readability, or regression predicting error by skills). As such, the “evaluation metrics for systems” aspect remains largely prospective.

Questions to authors
1) Reliability: What is the precise inter-annotator agreement metric (e.g., Cohen’s kappa, Jaccard/F1 over multilabels)? Please report per-skill agreement, agreement on sentence selection, and agreement on “nonsense” labels.
2) Difficulty construct: Did you validate that questions annotated with more skills are actually harder for humans or systems (e.g., time-to-answer, error rates)? If not, can you provide preliminary correlations with human accuracy or baseline system performance?
3) Readability feature set: Which features were used in which analyses (e.g., Flesch–Kincaid vs. the Vajjala & Meurers top-10)? Were the metrics computed on entire contexts or only on the annotated sentences? Can you report results under both choices?
4) Statistical analysis: Can you provide per-dataset Pearson correlations with p-values, and consider multivariate regression controlling for confounds (context length, number of required sentences, sentence distance, genre)?
5) Inconsistencies: Please reconcile the discrepancy between the stated average number of skills for QA4MRE (4.6 in text vs. 3.25 in Table 3), and ensure all referenced tables/figures and computation details are included.
6) “Nonsense” definition: How was “nonsense” operationalized across datasets? Could you provide examples and quantify uncertainty (e.g., proportion with annotator disagreement)?
7) Distance metric: Why define multi-sentence distance as the sum of pairwise distances rather than span width or max gap? How sensitive are conclusions to this choice?

Overall recommendation
Reject. The paper offers a thoughtful, potentially useful framework and resource, but the evaluation is limited by sampling, reliability reporting, methodological assumptions about difficulty, and incomplete/inconsistent analyses. Strengthening the empirical validation (agreement, statistics, model linkage), clarifying inconsistencies, and expanding the analysis would substantially improve the work.
