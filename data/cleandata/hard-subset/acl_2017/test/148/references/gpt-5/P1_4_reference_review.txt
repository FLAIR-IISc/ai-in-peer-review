Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates how “answering difficulty” and “reading difficulty” relate in reading comprehension (RC). The authors:
- Propose a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, reorganizing commonsense/knowledge reasoning into bridging, elaboration, ellipsis, meta-knowledge, and other skills, grounded in textual entailment literature and cognitive psychology.
- Annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these skills, also selecting the minimal set of sentences needed to answer and computing readability features on those sentences.
- Analyze correlations between the number of required skills (as a proxy for question difficulty) and readability metrics, reporting weak correlations and dataset-specific insights; they release the annotations.

In my view, the primary contribution is the cross-dataset annotation and analysis methodology plus the resulting resource; second is the refined, theoretically motivated skill taxonomy; third is the empirical finding that readability weakly correlates with required skills.

Strengths
- Cross-disciplinary, theory-informed taxonomy: The reorganization of “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs. elaboration, tied to textual entailment phenomena and cognitive models (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano), is a useful synthesis. It improves on ad-hoc “commonsense” buckets often seen in RC analysis, and the definitions (e.g., differentiating grammatical/lexical bridging from general knowledge elaboration) are meaningful, if challenging to annotate.
- Consistent, comparative annotation across six datasets: Using a uniform protocol with three annotators and selecting evidence sentences enables a controlled comparison across heterogeneous benchmarks. The reported IAA (90.1% on 62 items) suggests reasonable reliability, and the per-dataset breakdowns yield actionable insights (e.g., MCTest’s multi-sentence causality/coref demands vs. SQuAD’s single-sentence, high readability burden).
- Measuring readability on the evidence span: Computing readability on only the sentences deemed necessary to answer a question is a thoughtful design choice; RC systems and humans focus on these spans, and full-passage readability can be misleading. This yields finer-grained dataset characterizations.
- Useful empirical takeaways for dataset design and evaluation: The weak correlation between readability and skill count challenges a common assumption that harder text implies harder RC, and the analysis highlights quality issues in automatically curated datasets (nonsense questions in WDW/MARCO) while valuing NewsQA’s meta labels. The qualitative guidance for curriculum/transfer-style system development is practical.
- Additional sentence-level analyses: The number and distance of required sentences per question offer a simple, interpretable lens on multi-sentence reasoning demands across datasets, complementing the skill taxonomy.

Weaknesses
- Validity of “number of skills = difficulty” is unvalidated: The central proxy for question difficulty is the count of required skills. The paper cites prior intuition, but offers no validation against human difficulty (e.g., response time/accuracy) or system difficulty (performance across baselines). Without such validation, the correlation claims (or lack thereof) between “difficulty” and readability are tentative. A simple check correlating skill counts with human accuracy, or with scores of standard RC models on the same items, would strengthen conclusions.
- Post-hoc annotation with answers shown risks bias: Annotators saw the question and gold answer (and options for MC), then labeled skills. This can bias toward minimal justifications and undercount skills used during genuine problem solving. The design also precludes evaluating retrieval and answer selection competence, which are integral to RC. The authors acknowledge some limitations, but the implications for reported frequencies and correlations are not discussed.
- Sampling and statistical rigor: Only 100 questions per dataset were annotated, from dev/gold subsets, which may not be representative. Correlation reporting lacks details (e.g., per-dataset r values, confidence intervals, significance tests). Moreover, the text claims QA4MRE averages 4.6 skills per question, whereas Table 3 lists 3.25—this numerical inconsistency undermines confidence. More rigorous sampling, uncertainty estimates, and consistency checks are needed.
- Ambiguity and subjectivity in several skill categories: Despite better grounding, boundaries between bridging vs. elaboration, and the scope of ellipsis and meta-knowledge, remain subjective. Only an overall IAA is reported on a small sample; per-skill agreement would be important to assess reliability of the taxonomy and the reported distributions (e.g., high ellipsis in WDW).
- Readability feature choices and interpretation: The paper highlights FK grade in figures, while the stated top features come from Vajjala & Meurers and include Coleman–Liau, syntactic ratios, and lexical variation. It is unclear why FK is the main plotted metric, how it compares to the other features, and whether results are robust across metrics. Since features were designed for human readability, a stronger discussion of why/how they transfer to RC would help.

Questions to authors
- Can you validate the “number of skills” proxy? For example: correlations with human accuracy/response time on a subset, or with scores from baseline RC systems across the annotated questions?
- How was inter-annotator agreement computed (unit, metric)? Please report per-skill agreement and agreement on evidence sentence selection. How were disagreements resolved (majority vote, adjudication)?
- How exactly was “nonsense” operationalized? Was there an annotation guide and decision rules? What was IAA on “nonsense” vs. “solvable” labels?
- Sampling: Do you have statistics indicating the representativeness of the 100-question samples per dataset (topic, passage length, answer types)? Any sensitivity analysis with different random seeds?
- Can you reconcile the discrepancy between the textual claim (QA4MRE average 4.6 skills) and Table 3 (3.25)? Please audit all reported numbers and include confidence intervals.
- Readability metrics: Why emphasize Flesch–Kincaid in figures when it’s not among the top-10 features you list? Are the weak correlations consistent across Coleman–Liau, syntactic ratios (DC/C, CN/C), and lexical variation? Please report r values and p-values per dataset and metric.

Additional comments
- The references contain many formatting issues and inconsistencies; please clean up.
- Releasing the annotation guidelines, code, and data is valuable; please specify availability and license.
- Consider reporting readability on full passages for comparison, and discussing how evidence-span readability varies across annotators.

Overall, this is a timely diagnostic study with a useful resource and an improved taxonomy. Strengthening the methodological validation, statistical rigor, and clarity around annotation reliability would significantly increase its impact.
