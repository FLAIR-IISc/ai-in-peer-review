- Summary: Proposes framework evaluating RC datasets along required prerequisite skills and context readability; annotates 100 questions from six datasets and finds weak correlation between readability and number of skills, releasing annotations.

- Contribution 1: Reorganized, theory-informed taxonomy of prerequisite skills (clarifies bridging vs. elaboration; adds ellipsis and meta-knowledge) with a uniform annotation protocol.

- Contribution 2: Comparative analysis showing weak correlation between question difficulty (number of skills) and readability of evidence sentences, offering dataset-design insights.

- Contribution 3: Public release of annotated resources (skills and evidence sentences) for six widely used RC datasets.

- Strength: Theory-grounded taxonomy drawing on textual entailment and cognitive psychology improves interpretability and generality.

- Strength: Uniform manual annotation across six datasets enables meaningful cross-dataset comparisons.

- Strength: Concrete, actionable findings for dataset builders (e.g., genre effects; constructing hard questions from easy texts).

- Strength: Practical resource contribution with high reported IAA (90.1% on a sample), suggesting the scheme is learnable.

- Strength: Clear writing and careful situating within prior work; transparent methodology and sampling choices.

- Weakness: Operationalizing difficulty as simple count of skills is debatable; skills may not be additive and lack validation against human or system difficulty measures.

- Weakness: Limited scale and statistical analysis—only 100 items per dataset, sparse IAA reporting, no confidence intervals or significance testing for correlations.

- Weakness: Annotation protocol excludes retrieval by giving gold answers and may bias evidence selection and perceived difficulty; “nonsense” label under-specified.

- Weakness: Taxonomy distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration vs. coreference, meta-knowledge) can be fuzzy; no per-skill agreement or adjudication procedures reported.

- Weakness: Readability analyses may be confounded by choices (computed on selected evidence sentences only); nonstandard “sentence distance” measure and lack of controls for context length/genre.

- Question: What are per-skill inter-annotator agreement scores and how were disagreements resolved?

- Question: Was number of skills validated against human accuracy/response time or system performance to confirm it predicts difficulty?

- Question: Can you provide per-dataset correlations between number of skills and each readability feature with confidence intervals and controls for evidence length/number of sentences?

- Question: How was the “nonsense” label defined and distinguished from unanswerable-within-context vs. world-knowledge questions; any adjudication examples?

- Question: Can you supply concrete borderline examples separating bridging, elaboration, and coreference, and report observed confusions?

- Question: Will you release annotation guidelines, readability extraction code, and evidence sentence indices to ensure reproducibility?

- Overall assessment: Useful, timely, and principled work with a practical resource, but empirical claims need stronger scale, per-skill reliability, difficulty validation, and more rigorous statistical analysis; reviewer leans toward weak accept if issues are addressed.
