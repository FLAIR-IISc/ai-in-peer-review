- Paper proposes a two-pronged framework characterizing RC datasets by prerequisite skills and readability of required text.  
- Refines and extends a 13-category skill taxonomy, reorganizing knowledge reasoning (bridging vs. elaboration), adding ellipsis and meta-knowledge, and grounding categories in entailment and cognitive theory.  
- Annotates six RC datasets with multi-label skill requirements and sentence highlights, computes readability metrics on highlighted sentences, and finds weak correlations between readability and number of skills.  
- Releases annotations and offers practical guidance for dataset design and curriculum-style system development.  

- Strength: Skill taxonomy is clear, theoretically grounded, comprehensive yet practical for RC analysis.  
- Strength: Cross-dataset annotation using a consistent protocol yields actionable comparative insights (e.g., SQuAD hard-to-read but easy-to-answer).  
- Strength: Methodology isolates comprehension by highlighting only answer-relevant sentences and showing annotators the answer.  
- Strength: Empirical evidence shows low-to-moderate correlations between standard readability metrics and number of skills required.  
- Strength: Provides concrete curriculum sequencing recommendations for dataset/system development.  

- Weakness: Equates difficulty with skill count without external validation; skills vary in cognitive demand and may not be additive.  
- Weakness: Small annotation sample (100 items per dataset) and limited agreement reporting (no chance-corrected multilabel statistics or per-skill breakdowns).  
- Weakness: Uneven preprocessing/filtering across datasets (e.g., NewsQA filtering, MS MARCO and SQuAD selection choices) may bias comparisons.  
- Weakness: Readability metrics used are L2/traditional measures applied to highlighted sentences, potentially misaligned with RC difficulty and missing RC-specific features.  
- Weakness: No link shown between proposed metrics and system performance; statistical reporting of correlations lacks significance tests, CIs, and multiple-comparison controls.  
- Weakness: Some skill categories overlap or are ambiguous in practice, coding guidelines are insufficiently detailed, and some metrics (e.g., sentence distance) are unvalidated.  

- Question: How was inter-annotator agreement computed for multilabel skills—please report chance-corrected measures per skill and overall, plus annotator training/adjudication details?  
- Question: What were the decision rules/examples for labeling “nonsense,” and how consistent were annotators on this label?  
- Question: Do skill counts correlate with human difficulty measures (accuracy, response time) or model error rates, and can you provide validation or plans to validate this assumption?  
- Question: Please provide statistical details for readability–skills correlations (sample sizes, p-values, confidence intervals, multiple-test corrections); which correlations remain significant?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to sampling choices and preprocessing decisions across datasets, and will you release full guidelines, sentence spans, and code for reproducibility?
