Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a unified evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets that combines (a) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills, with a theoretically grounded reorganization of knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration (informed by textual entailment and cognitive psychology), and (b) readability metrics drawn from prior work on second-language readability.
- Using this framework, the authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) on 100 questions each, highlighting the skills required, relevant sentences, and readability of the sentences needed to answer. Annotations are released.
- Empirical analyses reveal a weak correlation between question difficulty (number of prerequisite skills) and readability, arguing that “hard to read” is not the same as “hard to answer,” and offering actionable guidance for dataset design and system development curricula.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual framing grounded in psychology and entailment: Recasting “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs. elaboration and situating skills within textbase/situation-model levels is a fresh and principled way to operationalize RC difficulty. This bridge across communities is valuable and likely to influence how we talk about RC capability.
- Task-agnostic, cross-dataset annotation enabling apples-to-apples comparisons: Annotators consistently label the prerequisite skills and highlight the sentences necessary to answer across six diverse datasets. This comparative view is rare and useful to both dataset curators and system builders.
- Clear, actionable empirical insight: The result that readability weakly correlates with required skills challenges a common assumption and motivates design of “easy-to-read but hard-to-answer” datasets (e.g., MCTest) and curriculum/transfer strategies. The analyses of skill frequencies, number of skills per question, and sentence distance further concretize dataset characteristics.
- Resource contribution: The publicly available annotations and the refined taxonomy are likely to be reused for dataset creation, diagnostic evaluation, and error analyses of RC systems.
- Thoughtful discussion with practical guidance: The paper offers concrete implications (genre choice, question type emphasizing global coherence, curriculum learning), which can steer future dataset construction and evaluation practices.

Weaknesses
- Limited scale and sampling/selection biases: Only 100 questions per dataset, drawn from development sets and with dataset-specific filters (e.g., NewsQA excluding difficult cases, MS MARCO restricting to is_selected segments). This raises concerns about representativeness and the stability of reported correlations and distributions.
- Incomplete reliability reporting: The reported 90.1% inter-annotator agreement for 62 items lacks detail (agreement metric, per-skill agreement, multi-label handling, kappa/F1). Given the fine-grained and potentially overlapping skills (notably bridging vs. elaboration), more rigorous reliability analysis is essential to trust the annotations.
- Limited validity linking to system performance: The framework is positioned to guide system development, but the paper does not test whether skill counts or readability predict model or human accuracy, nor whether the proposed curriculum notion yields measurable gains. Without predictive/construct validity, the practical utility for benchmarking remains partially speculative.
- Readability metric choices and consistency: The paper cites Vajjala & Meurers features, but then relies on Flesch–Kincaid for core plots, and mixes in Coleman–Liau. These measures target human L2 readability and may not align with machine RC difficulty. The operational decision to compute readability only on the “needed sentences” may bias results and is not compared to full-passage measures or discourse-level readability (e.g., entity-grid/RST).
- Methodological constraint: Annotators saw the gold answers and highlighted relevant sentences; the framework therefore bypasses two key RC competencies—locating evidence and selecting/producing answers. While acknowledged, this limits claims about “RC difficulty” to a subset (entailment/understanding), and some dataset insights (e.g., SQuAD being “easy to answer”) may reflect this design choice.

Questions to authors
- Agreement metrics: How was the 90.1% agreement computed for multi-label skill assignment (exact match, per-label, Jaccard, kappa)? Can you report per-skill agreement and confusions, especially for bridging vs. elaboration and meta-knowledge?
- Predictive validity: Do the number/type of skills correlate with baseline or SOTA model accuracy per question? Can your taxonomy explain specific systematic failures (e.g., models underperform on bridging+coreference)?
- Readability operationalization: Why mix Vajjala & Meurers features with Flesch–Kincaid? Which readability metric best correlates with skill counts across datasets? Did you compare readability on “needed sentences” versus whole passages?
- Sampling robustness: How sensitive are your findings to the sampling/filters (e.g., including NewsQA answer-absent, or using SQuAD multiple questions per paragraph)? Any bootstrapped CIs for correlations?
- Annotation artifacts: Could releasing the detailed guidelines and adjudication rules improve reproducibility? How did you instruct annotators to distinguish bridging from elaboration, and to handle borderline cases (e.g., noun compounds vs. lexical relations)?
- Sentence distance: Distance depends on passage length; did you normalize or control for document size when comparing datasets? Any analysis of whether larger distances predict lower model accuracy?

Overall, this paper makes a conceptually novel and practically useful step toward principled RC dataset evaluation. Despite limitations in scale and validation, the taxonomy and cross-dataset analyses open a promising direction and provide a reusable resource. I lean toward acceptance, provided the authors strengthen reliability/validity reporting and clarify the readability methodology.
