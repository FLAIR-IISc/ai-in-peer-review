Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- Contribution 1 (primary): A refined, theory-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC that reorganizes “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs. elaboration (and adds ellipsis and meta-knowledge), aligning with textual entailment and cognitive psychology. This addresses the inadequacy of superficial question/answer-type tags for characterizing RC difficulty.
- Contribution 2: A cross-dataset annotation and analysis of six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) using that taxonomy, together with sentence selection and readability features, yielding dataset-level profiles (e.g., frequency of skills, average number of skills per question, sentence distance).
- Contribution 3: An empirical finding that readability of the context and “difficulty to answer” (operationalized as number of prerequisite skills) are only weakly correlated (Pearson 0.025–0.416), with concrete case studies (e.g., MCTest: easy to read, comparatively harder to answer; SQuAD: harder to read, easier to answer).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and synthesis: The skill taxonomy is thoughtfully grounded in established literatures (RTE; Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano), moving beyond ad-hoc “commonsense” buckets. The bridging vs. elaboration split, and inclusion of ellipsis and meta-knowledge, is sensible and addresses prior gaps. This provides a useful vocabulary for dataset analysis and model diagnosis.
- Cross-dataset, uniform analysis: Applying a single annotation protocol across six widely used RC datasets yields meaningful comparative insights, e.g., high ellipsis/bridging/elaboration in WDW due to query–context separation; high structural complexity and punctuation in QA4MRE; “nonsense” prevalence differences (e.g., WDW 27%, MS MARCO 14% vs. NewsQA ~1% due to filtering). These observations are actionable for dataset design and for choosing benchmarks in curriculum/transfer settings.
- Practical resource value: The authors state they will release annotations. Even with 100-question samples per dataset, such labeled examples of prerequisite skills and sentence selection can seed evaluation hooks and facilitate targeted training/evaluation (e.g., per-skill probing, dataset curation).
- Methodological transparency about scope: The paper explicitly delineates that the method evaluates understanding of contextual entailments (not retrieval or answer candidate selection), which helps readers interpret claims and limitations appropriately.
- Additional analyses beyond labels: Readability computed on sentences actually needed to answer improves construct validity; the sentence “distance” analysis provides a simple proxy for multi-sentence reasoning demand, revealing longer-distance dependencies in QA4MRE and more single-sentence answering in SQuAD/MS MARCO.

Weaknesses
- Reliability under-specified: The reported inter-annotator agreement is “90.1% for 62 questions,” but it is unclear how multi-label agreement was computed, whether it is percent agreement or chance-corrected (e.g., per-skill Cohen’s kappa/Jaccard/alpha), and why only 62 questions were used. Without chance correction and per-skill breakdown, label reliability and the robustness of frequency comparisons remain uncertain.
- Difficulty proxy not validated: Equating “number of required skills” with question difficulty is plausible but unvalidated here. There is no correlation with human difficulty ratings or with system performance. Thus, the central correlation claim (readability vs. difficulty) rests on an assumption rather than independent validation, weakening the strength of the conclusion.
- Experimental design and sampling limitations: Only 100 questions per dataset and various sampling heuristics (e.g., one question per SQuAD paragraph; specific NewsQA filters; dev sets only) limit generalizability and may bias the observed distributions (e.g., the high “nonsense” in WDW and MS MARCO). Confidence intervals or sensitivity analyses to sampling seeds are missing.
- Annotation protocol biases the construct: Annotators see the gold answer and candidate options, and select only the sentences “needed to answer,” which explicitly removes the search/selection component. While acknowledged, some dataset-level conclusions (e.g., “MS MARCO is relatively easy”) may not hold when retrieval and span selection are included. The analysis would be stronger if complemented with system or human task performance correlations.
- Readability analysis is narrow and lacks statistical rigor: The selected features come from L2 readability; the paper uses FK grade level and a subset of Vajjala & Meurers features computed on selected sentences, but does not report parsing/feature extraction quality or statistical significance (p-values, CIs) for correlations, nor non-parametric (Spearman) robustness. Given feature multicollinearity and small N, the reported Pearson coefficients (max ~0.416) may be unstable.
- Definition and use of “nonsense” are underspecified: The criteria for labeling a question as nonsense are not formalized, and the paper notes annotator disagreements. Yet these labels are used to characterize dataset quality. More precise guidelines and inter-annotator reliability on this label are needed.
- Inconsistencies/clarity issues: The text claims QA4MRE “on average required 4.6 skills,” but Table 3 shows 3.25. Such discrepancies, plus missing detail on agreement computation and readability feature selection, reduce clarity.

Questions to authors
- How exactly was inter-annotator agreement computed for multi-label skills (overall and per skill)? Please report chance-corrected measures (e.g., per-skill kappa/alpha) and agreement on the “nonsense” label.
- Please reconcile the QA4MRE average number of skills discrepancy (4.6 in text vs. 3.25 in Table 3). Which is correct?
- Which readability metrics were ultimately used in the correlation analysis: FK alone or the full set (NumChar, NumSyll, AWL, MLS, CoOrd, DC/C, CN/C, AdvVar, ModVar, Coleman)? Were correlations tested for statistical significance and robustness (Spearman, bootstrapped CIs)?
- Will you release the full annotation guidelines and code to compute readability features and sentence distance? Can you report sensitivity to sampling (e.g., multiple random subsets) and provide CIs for skill frequencies and correlations?

Overall assessment
- Originality: Moderate. The taxonomy refinement is thoughtful but builds on prior work; the cross-dataset analysis is useful.
- Soundness: Mixed. The methodology is sensible for entailment-focused analysis, but the reliability and statistical validation are insufficiently detailed, and the difficulty proxy is unvalidated.
- Clarity: Generally clear, with some inconsistencies and missing methodological specifics.
- Significance/Resources: Moderate practical value if annotations/guidelines are released; the finding of weak readability–difficulty correlation is interesting but requires stronger validation.

Recommendation: Borderline. I would lean toward acceptance as a short/resource/analysis paper if reliability and statistical reporting are strengthened and discrepancies corrected; otherwise, as a full paper, I lean weak reject due to validation and methodological reporting gaps.
