Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a framework to characterize reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two complementary axes: (1) prerequisite skills needed to answer a question, and (2) readability of the necessary text. The authors refine a skills taxonomy by grounding it in textual entailment and psycholinguistics (bridging vs. elaboration, etc.), and annotate six RC datasets with these skills while computing readability metrics on the minimal sentences deemed necessary for answering. They report that readability and question difficulty (approximated by the count of required skills) are only weakly correlated, and provide dataset-specific profiles (e.g., MCTest is easy to read but often requires multi-sentence, reasoning-heavy skills).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A theoretically motivated refinement of prerequisite skills for RC, especially reorganizing knowledge reasoning into ellipsis, bridging, elaboration, and meta-knowledge, linked to entailment phenomena and comprehension theory.
2) A cross-dataset annotation and analysis of six prominent RC datasets that highlights weak correlation between readability and required skills, and surfaces dataset-specific properties (e.g., presence of “nonsense” questions, reliance on ellipsis).
3) Release of per-question annotations and a practical methodology for dataset profiling that can inform dataset construction and curriculum design.

Strengths
- Conceptual grounding and taxonomy improvement: The reorganization of knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration and the addition of meta-knowledge are well-motivated by prior work in textual entailment and psycholinguistics. Compared with prior taxonomies, this is clearer, less ad hoc, and more likely to be useful for error analysis and dataset design.
- Dataset-agnostic annotation protocol: By selecting only the sentences needed to answer and labeling skills at the question level, the methodology applies across task formats (extractive, multiple-choice) and focuses the readability evaluation on the actually used text. This adds interpretability (e.g., sentence distance analysis) and avoids conflating context length with required reasoning.
- Comparative insights across diverse datasets: Profiling QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA yields actionable observations: (i) weak readability–difficulty correlation, (ii) auto-curated datasets can yield sophisticated reasoning (ellipses, bridging) but also nonsense questions, (iii) narrative vs. expository genre effects on skills. These insights can guide dataset curation and system evaluation.
- Practical utility for curriculum/transfer: The two-axis view suggests a concrete curriculum for system development (easy-to-read/easy-to-answer → easy-to-read/harder-to-answer → hard-to-read/hard-to-answer), and the annotations can seed targeted training/evaluation splits.
- Annotation reliability appears promising: A reported 90.1% agreement (albeit on a subset) suggests the taxonomy may be learnable by annotators, an important prerequisite for scaling.

Weaknesses
- Difficulty proxy lacks validation: Using the number of required skills as “difficulty” assumes skills contribute equally and additively, and that more skills imply harder questions. There is no validation against human difficulty (accuracy/RT) or model performance. Without such evidence, the core claim (weak correlation between readability and difficulty) remains largely correlational and potentially sensitive to the operationalization.
- Annotation methodology and reliability are under-specified: Annotators saw the gold answer (and options), which likely biases skill attribution and can inflate agreement. The agreement metric (what measure?) is reported only for 62 questions; no per-skill kappa/F1, no adjudication protocol, and no details on how disagreements were resolved or aggregated into the final labels. The “nonsense” label is subjective; guidelines and IAA for this critical decision are not reported.
- Readability metrics are limited and statistical analysis incomplete: The chosen features (largely L2/readability indices and FK grade) underweight discourse phenomena known to affect comprehension (RST, entity grids). Table 5 correlations are described as weak (0.025–0.416), but significance tests, effect sizes, and controls (e.g., per-dataset stratification) are not presented. Computing readability only on selected sentences may confound with span length and selection bias; comparisons to full-context readability are not provided.
- Sampling and coverage limit generality: Only 100 questions per dataset, with dataset-specific sampling schemes (e.g., one SQuAD question per paragraph), may not capture true distributions. Excluding CNN/DM, CBTest, and LAMBADA reduces coverage; while the reasons are reasonable, they weaken claims about RC broadly. Filtering decisions (e.g., NewsQA) further limit comparability.
- Presentation and reproducibility gaps: Some referenced tables/values are missing or partially described; references have formatting errors; the inter-annotator agreement metric is unspecified. The practical impact hinges on releasing the annotations, guidelines, and code—these need to be clearly committed and documented.

Questions to authors
1) How exactly was inter-annotator agreement computed (metric, unit of analysis)? Please report per-skill agreement (e.g., multi-label kappa/F1), agreement on “nonsense,” and the adjudication/aggregation protocol.
2) Can you validate the “number of skills” difficulty proxy? For example, correlate it with human accuracy/response times or with error rates of baseline RC systems on the annotated items.
3) Are the correlations in Table 5 statistically significant? Please report p-values, confidence intervals, and analyses stratified by dataset; also test partial correlations controlling for sentence count/length.
4) What happens if readability is computed on the full context instead of only selected sentences? Does the core conclusion (weak correlation) hold, and how sensitive are results to the selection step?
5) How were annotators instructed to mark “nonsense”? Provide the guidelines and IAA for this label; can you quantify how often “solvable without context” vs. “truly unsolvable” occurred?
6) Will you release the full annotation guidelines, per-question labels (including highlighted sentences), and tooling? If so, when and under what license?

Overall, this is a useful and timely analysis paper with solid theoretical grounding and clear practical intent. Addressing the methodological rigor issues (agreement, statistical validation, and difficulty proxy) would substantially strengthen the contribution.
