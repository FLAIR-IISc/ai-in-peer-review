Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes an evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets based on two complementary classes of metrics: (a) prerequisite skills needed to answer a question, and (b) readability of the relevant context. The authors (i) refine and expand a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, notably reorganizing knowledge reasoning into bridging vs elaboration (plus meta-knowledge), grounded in the textual entailment and psycholinguistics literature; (ii) compute readability features largely following Vajjala and Meurers; and (iii) annotate six RC datasets with this scheme, selecting the sentences deemed necessary to answer each question. They report skill frequencies, the number of skills per question, readability values, and correlations, and conclude that readability and question difficulty (as proxied by required skills) are only weakly correlated. The annotated results will be released.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Cross-dataset human analysis showing only weak correlation between readability and skill demand, with dataset-specific profiles; this is useful evidence for dataset design and evaluation.
2) A refined, literature-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, especially the treatment of knowledge reasoning via bridging, elaboration, and meta-knowledge, and an explicit sentence selection step.
3) A publicly released annotation resource profiling six major datasets under a unified scheme.

Strengths
- Clear, literature-grounded taxonomy: The reorganization of skills, especially distinguishing bridging vs elaboration and aligning other skills to predicate logic and discourse notions, is thoughtful and better anchored in textual entailment and cognitive models than earlier ad hoc taxonomies. This increases interpretability and potential reuse.
- Cross-dataset comparability: Annotating six diverse datasets under one protocol (including both crowd-sourced and automatically constructed datasets) highlights dataset-specific phenomena (e.g., QA4MRE’s higher logical/bridging demand; MCTest’s higher causal/spatiotemporal demands; WDW’s ellipsis). This comparative lens is valuable for practitioners selecting or constructing datasets.
- Useful negative result: The empirical finding of only weak correlations between readability metrics (including FK grade) and the number of skills required supports the claim that “easy-to-read ≠ easy-to-answer,” which has implications for evaluation and dataset curation.
- Practical guidance and release: The discussion on how to use these metrics for curriculum/transfer learning and dataset profiling is concrete, and the planned release of annotations and sentence selections will likely be useful to the community.
- Methodological transparency: The paper is clear about limitations (e.g., not assessing retrieval of evidence sentences; issues with “none of the above”) and provides details on sampling, annotation inputs, and readability feature choices.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the “skill count = difficulty” proxy: The central assumption that the number of required skills measures question difficulty is plausible but not validated against model performance or human difficulty (e.g., error rates, response times, or human difficulty ratings). Without such validation, the main conclusion (weak relationship between readability and difficulty) remains suggestive rather than definitive.
- Sampling and statistical analysis are thin: Only 100 questions per dataset were annotated, and correlations are reported without significance tests, confidence intervals, or controls for confounds (e.g., sentence length, number of selected sentences, dataset/domain effects). The reported range of Pearson correlations (0.025–0.416) is not contextualized with p-values or partial correlations, limiting interpretability.
- Agreement reporting is insufficient for a multilabel task: “90.1% agreement” on 62 questions does not indicate which agreement metric was used, how multilabel decisions were handled, or per-skill reliability. Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa (or similar) per skill and for sentence selection would strengthen claims of annotation quality.
- Annotation protocol may bias skill attribution: Annotators see the gold answer and select necessary sentences. This simplifies the task compared to RC and may inflate or distort perceived skills (e.g., ignoring retrieval/confidence calibration, or undercounting alternative reasoning paths). The visibility of the answer also risks hindsight bias in labeling inferences as necessary. The paper partially acknowledges this but does not quantify its impact.
- Readability feature choice and scope: The chosen features are designed for human readability and ESL proficiency; applying them only to the selected “necessary” sentences may introduce length and extraction bias. Other features known to affect comprehension and RC difficulty (discourse structure, coreference chains, lexical cohesion, entity density, surprisal) are omitted, potentially underpowering the readability–difficulty analysis.

Questions to authors
- Can you report inter-annotator agreement per skill using a standard coefficient (e.g., kappa) and for sentence selection (e.g., overlap Jaccard), and indicate how multilabel agreement was computed?
- Did you evaluate whether the “number of skills” correlates with model error on the same items (e.g., for a baseline system) or with human difficulty (time/accuracy)? Even a small-scale check would substantiate the difficulty proxy.
- How were annotators trained and calibrated on the refined taxonomy? Is the annotation manual available, and will you release the per-question sentence selections?
- What are the p-values/confidence intervals for the reported correlations? Do the results hold with partial correlations controlling for sentence count/length or with alternate readability features?
- Some skill categories may overlap (e.g., bridging vs elaboration vs coreference vs idioms). How did you adjudicate borderline cases, and how consistent were annotators on these boundaries?

Overall assessment
This is a useful and timely analysis paper that organizes a clearer skill taxonomy and provides cross-dataset annotations and insights. While the methodological limitations (validation, agreement reporting, and statistics) weaken the strength of the main claim, the resource and comparative profiling are valuable to the community. With stronger reliability reporting and at least a small validation of the skill-count difficulty proxy against model or human performance, this would be a solid accept; as is, it is a borderline but positive contribution, primarily as a dataset-analysis resource and taxonomy paper.
