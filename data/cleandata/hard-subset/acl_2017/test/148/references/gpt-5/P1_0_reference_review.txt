Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a two-pronged framework to characterize reading comprehension (RC) datasets along (a) prerequisite skills required to answer questions and (b) readability of the text to be read. The authors:
1) Refine and extend a taxonomy of prerequisite skills (13 categories), reorganizing “knowledge reasoning” with insights from textual entailment (bridging vs. elaboration) and cognitive psychology (retrieved vs. generated inferences), and introducing ellipsis and meta-knowledge.
2) Annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with multi-label skill requirements and sentence highlights, and compute readability metrics on the needed sentences. They report weak correlations between readability and the number of skills, suggesting reading difficulty and answering difficulty are largely independent.
3) Release the annotations and offer practical guidance for dataset design and curriculum-style system development.

Strengths
- Clear, useful skill taxonomy grounded in theory:
  - The reorganization of knowledge reasoning into bridging/elaboration and explicit inclusion of ellipsis and meta-knowledge connects RC analysis with established work in entailment and cognitive models. The taxonomy is comprehensive yet not too granular, and many categories (coreference, spatiotemporal, logical reasoning, clause relations) are operationally relevant to RC.

- Careful cross-dataset analysis with actionable observations:
  - Annotating six diverse datasets using a consistent protocol provides a comparative lens that goes beyond superficial question/answer types. Findings like SQuAD being hard to read but relatively easy to answer, and MCTest being easy to read yet skill-demanding, are informative and align with practitioner intuitions.

- Methodology that isolates comprehension from retrieval/confounding factors:
  - By highlighting only sentences needed to answer and letting annotators see the answer, the study focuses analysis on contextual entailment rather than retrieval or candidate generation. This choice clarifies what linguistic skills are engaged by the content itself.

- Empirical evidence for weak coupling between readability and answering difficulty:
  - The reported low-to-moderate correlations (Pearson 0.025–0.416) between Flesch–Kincaid/other readability features and the number of skills needed is an important empirical point for dataset creators and evaluation designers.

- Practical implications and tools for curriculum design:
  - The discussion on sequencing datasets (easy-to-read/easy-to-answer → easy-to-read/hard-to-answer → hard-to-read/hard-to-answer) is concrete and could help structure evaluations and training regimes for future RC systems.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation of the “skill count = difficulty” assumption:
  - The core measurement equates difficulty with the number of required skills, but there is no external validation (e.g., human response times/accuracy, or correlation with model error rates). Some skills are more cognitively demanding than others, and different combinations may not be additive. This weakens the strength of claims about “question difficulty.”

- Small, potentially biased annotation sample and agreement reporting:
  - Only 100 questions per dataset are annotated, which may not capture the diversity of large datasets like SQuAD or WDW. Inter-annotator agreement is reported as 90.1% on 62 questions, but not with a chance-corrected statistic (e.g., kappa/alpha) suitable for multilabel judgments, and without per-skill breakdowns. The criteria for “nonsense” questions are also subjective. Together, these raise concerns about reliability and generalizability.

- Uneven preprocessing and filtering across datasets:
  - For NewsQA, “bad” questions were filtered out, while other datasets retain “nonsense,” making cross-dataset comparisons of skill frequencies and difficulty uneven. MS MARCO uses only is_selected=1 passages, and SQuAD uses one question per paragraph—these choices may bias the measured profiles.

- Readability metrics may be misaligned with RC difficulty and are applied narrowly:
  - Features are largely borrowed from L2 readability and traditional indexes (e.g., AWL, FK grade). Applying them only to the highlighted sentences (rather than entire passages) is reasonable but may distort standard readability measures. The paper acknowledges the mismatch but does not explore RC-specific readability proxies (discourse coherence, entity grid, surprisal, syntactic depth/embedding) or control for confounds like passage length.

- Missing link to system performance and limited statistical analysis:
  - Although motivated as useful for system evaluation, no analysis is provided that relates the proposed metrics to model performance across questions/datasets. Correlation reporting lacks details (significance tests, confidence intervals, correction for multiple comparisons), and a maximum r≈0.416 may be nontrivial depending on the context.

- Ambiguity and overlap in some skill categories and coding:
  - Despite improvements, distinctions between bridging, elaboration, and coreference may overlap in practice; metaphors/metonymy/idioms are grouped but coding guidelines are not described in sufficient detail to ensure consistent annotation by new annotators. The “sentence distance” metric is idiosyncratic (sum of pairwise distances for >2 sentences) and unvalidated.

Questions to authors
- Reliability:
  - How was inter-annotator agreement computed for multilabel skills? Please report chance-corrected measures (e.g., Jaccard/κ/α) per skill and overall, and describe annotator training and adjudication.
  - What were the explicit decision rules and examples for labeling “nonsense”? How consistent were annotators on this label?

- Validating difficulty:
  - Do skill counts correlate with human difficulty (accuracy, time) or with model error rates on the same items? If not available, can you provide preliminary evidence or discuss plans to validate this key assumption?

- Statistical details:
  - For the readability–skills correlations, please report sample sizes, p-values, confidence intervals, and control for multiple tests. Which correlations remain significant?

- Generality and sampling:
  - How sensitive are results to sampling choices (e.g., more questions per dataset, multiple questions per SQuAD paragraph, including/excluding “bad” questions consistently across datasets)?

- Annotation artifacts and resources:
  - Will you release the full annotation guidelines, the highlighted sentence spans, and code for computing readability features? Can you clarify whether annotators were the paper authors and, if so, whether independent annotators replicated the results?

Overall, this is a timely and useful dataset-centric analysis with a thoughtful taxonomy and practical insights. Strengthening reliability, validating the difficulty proxy, and tightening statistical and sampling protocols would substantially improve its impact.
