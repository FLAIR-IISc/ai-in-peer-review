Summary and contributions
This paper proposes two complementary classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills (with a reorganization of knowledge-reasoning skills into bridging vs. elaboration, plus additions such as ellipsis and meta-knowledge), and (b) readability metrics derived from Vajjala and Meurers (2012). The authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) on 100 questions each, including selected sentences deemed necessary to answer, per-question multi-label skill assignments, a “nonsense” flag for unsolvable items, and readability scores computed on the selected sentences. They report weak correlations between readability and number of required skills and offer dataset-wise profiles (e.g., MCTest easy-to-read yet nontrivial to answer; SQuAD hard-to-read but relatively easy-to-answer). Annotations will be released.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A principled, psychology- and entailment-informed refinement of prerequisite skills for RC, especially the redefinition of knowledge reasoning as bridging vs. elaboration and the inclusion of ellipsis and meta-knowledge.
2) A comparative annotation study across six RC datasets that surfaces dataset-specific skill profiles and shows only weak correlation between readability and question difficulty (operationalized via skill counts).
3) A public annotation resource that can inform dataset construction and system analysis.

Strengths
- Conceptual grounding and taxonomy refinement: The reorganization of “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs. elaboration, mapping to retrieved/generated inferences, is well motivated by cognitive psychology and RTE literature. The resulting 13-skill inventory is more theoretically anchored than prior ad-hoc lists and potentially reusable across datasets.
- Cross-dataset, apples-to-apples analysis: Annotating six diverse datasets with a common framework enables concrete, comparative insights (e.g., QA4MRE requires more diverse skills, WDW often needs ellipsis, MCTest emphasizes narrative-centric skills). The per-dataset profiles are informative for both dataset designers and practitioners choosing benchmarks.
- Useful negative result on readability–difficulty correlation: The finding that readability features (FK grade and related metrics) only weakly correlate with skill-based difficulty is important for dataset design. It cautions against relying on surface readability as a proxy for question difficulty and supports designing challenging questions over easy texts.
- Transparency about methodology scope and limitations: The paper explicitly states that the annotation setup evaluates contextual entailment understanding rather than retrieval/selection competence, and later provides a small analysis of sentence distance to partially address this gap. This clarity helps interpret results.
- Resource contribution: The plan to release annotations increases the work’s practical value, enabling downstream analyses (e.g., per-skill model diagnostics) and guiding future dataset construction.

Weaknesses
- Limited and weakly characterized reliability: The reported 90.1% agreement over 62 questions is insufficiently specified for a multilabel, multi-skill annotation task. Percent agreement on a small subset is hard to interpret; per-skill agreement, appropriate multi-label kappa/alpha, and label-wise F1 would be more informative. Given that key claims hinge on these annotations, stronger reliability reporting is needed.
- Validity of “difficulty = number of skills” assumption: Counting skills as a scalar difficulty measure is convenient but reductive. Skills vary in complexity and can co-occur with different degrees of inference. No external validation (e.g., human item difficulty, system error rates by skill count) is provided to justify the mapping from skill count to difficulty, which weakens the correlation analyses.
- Sampling and measurement biases: Only 100 questions per dataset (often from development sets) may not capture dataset variability. Readability is computed on the subset of sentences the annotators selected as “needed,” which can bias the readability estimate and is not necessarily representative of the passage the system must process. Annotators had access to the correct answer, which likely biases sentence selection and skill assignment (risk of post-hoc rationalization).
- “Nonsense” label and quality conclusions: The criteria for labeling “nonsense” items are briefly described but not operationalized with reliability. The relatively high rates for WDW and MARCO are attributed to automated curation, but without stronger evidence this risks overinterpretation. Moreover, NewsQA was filtered to exclude “bad” questions, making cross-dataset comparisons on this dimension uneven.
- Inconsistencies and reporting gaps: There appears to be a discrepancy between the narrative (e.g., QA4MRE average required skills reported as 4.6) and Table 3 (Ave. = 3.25). Details on the exact agreement metric, annotation manual, adjudication, and full readability results (Table 4, Table 5 are referenced but not fully shown here) are needed to fully assess rigor.

Questions to authors
- Reliability: What exact inter-annotator agreement metric(s) were used for the multilabel skill annotation? Can you report per-skill agreement (e.g., Cohen’s kappa or Krippendorff’s alpha per label) and example-level Jaccard/F1 across annotators?
- Annotation protocol: Did annotators always see the correct answer when selecting sentences and skills? If yes, did you consider a blind setup to avoid bias? Please share the annotation manual and examples distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration and ellipsis vs. coreference to assess operational clarity.
- Readability scope: Why compute readability only on selected sentences rather than the full context the model must process? How sensitive are the correlations to this choice?
- “Nonsense” labeling: How was this operationalized and adjudicated? What is the agreement on this label?
- Discrepancy: Please clarify the average number of required skills for QA4MRE (text says ~4.6; Table 3 shows 3.25). Which is correct?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen reliability and validity: Expand the annotated set, report robust agreement metrics, and validate the “skill-count as difficulty” assumption via human judgments and/or model error analyses stratified by skill count and type.
- Control for measurement choices: Report results when readability is computed on full contexts vs. selected sentences; assess the impact of annotator access to answers; analyze sensitivity to passage length and number of selected sentences.
- Enrich analysis: Provide per-skill correlations with readability, interactions among skills, and item-response-like analyses to separate dataset difficulty from annotator bias. Consider weighting skills or modeling difficulty with IRT rather than raw counts.
- System-side utility: Demonstrate how the annotations diagnose model weaknesses (e.g., per-skill performance of baseline systems) to substantiate the claimed utility for system development.
- Documentation and release: Release the full guidelines, adjudication protocol, and code to compute readability/metrics; correct inconsistencies and polish references.

Overall, this is a timely and useful direction with a solid conceptual basis and a promising resource contribution. With stronger reliability/validity evidence and clearer reporting, the work would have higher impact and interpretability.
