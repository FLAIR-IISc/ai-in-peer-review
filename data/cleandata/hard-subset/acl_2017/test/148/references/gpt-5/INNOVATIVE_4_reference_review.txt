Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a dual-metric framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (i) a refined, theory-informed taxonomy of prerequisite skills (especially reorganizing knowledge reasoning into bridging, elaboration, ellipsis, meta-knowledge) and (ii) readability metrics drawn from prior NLP work. The authors annotate six popular RC datasets with these skill labels, identify the minimal set of sentences needed to answer each question, compute readability over those sentences, and analyze correlations. The main empirical finding is that readability of the context and difficulty of questions (approximated by number of required skills) are only weakly correlated.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A principled, cross-disciplinary skill taxonomy for RC grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology (bridging vs elaboration), offering a clearer lens on “knowledge reasoning” than prior ad hoc categories.
2) A cross-dataset human annotation of prerequisite skills and supporting sentences, enabling apples-to-apples comparisons across diverse RC formulations; the release of these annotations is valuable infrastructure.
3) An empirical analysis showing weak correlation between readability and skill-demand, plus dataset-specific insights that can inform dataset construction and system curriculum design.

Strengths
- Novel framing connecting cognitive theory and TE to RC evaluation. The reorganization of “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs elaboration (with ellipsis and meta-knowledge) is a bold, constructive move that can shift how we analyze dataset demands and model capabilities. It moves beyond superficial question/answer type tags toward cognitively meaningful skills.

- Resource contribution with broad utility. Annotating six established datasets with a consistent skill taxonomy and providing the selected supporting sentences is likely to become a useful community artifact for dataset auditing, skill-targeted model evaluation, and curriculum design. The inclusion spans multiple genres and task formats, making the resource more general.

- Clear, actionable empirical insight. The finding that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated is well illustrated (dataset-level and question-level views), and has immediate implications: it cautions against using readability as a proxy for RC difficulty and highlights that one can craft challenging questions from easy-to-read texts (e.g., MCTest).

- Transparent analysis that surfaces dataset idiosyncrasies. Observations about nonsense questions (e.g., in MS MARCO and WDW), multi-sentence requirements (MCTest, QA4MRE), and genre effects are concrete and informative for data curation choices.

- Practical guidance for system development. The curriculum/transfer perspective (progressing from easy-to-read/easy-to-answer to harder regimes) is a valuable takeaway that many practitioners can adopt with the released metrics.

Weaknesses
- Limited statistical rigor and scale. Only 100 questions per dataset were annotated; inter-annotator agreement is reported as percent agreement on 62 questions (no per-skill Cohen’s kappa, no confidence intervals). Correlation analyses lack significance tests, adjustments for confounds (e.g., passage length, answer format), or mixed-effects modeling. Claims are plausible but would benefit from stronger statistical backing.

- Annotation methodology may introduce bias. Annotators saw the gold answers and chose the “needed” sentences, which can inflate perceived skill requirements and readability estimates and conflates comprehension with answer exposure. The “nonsense” label is subjective; guidelines and adjudication procedures are not sufficiently detailed. Without per-skill IAA, it’s hard to assess the reliability of finer distinctions (bridging vs elaboration, meta-knowledge).

- Taxonomy boundaries remain fuzzy and unvalidated. While the reorganization is thoughtful, distinctions (e.g., bridging vs elaboration, meta-knowledge vs elaboration) are conceptually close and may be unstable in practice. There is no downstream validation showing that the taxonomy predicts model error patterns or training transfer (i.e., external validity of the categories).

- Readability features and operationalization are narrow. The chosen features target L2 readability and sentence-level surface complexity; discourse-level measures were omitted. Readability is computed only over sentences selected by annotators, making it sensitive to selection bias and ignoring the genuine retrieval burden many RC tasks entail.

- Dataset coverage and generality. Several datasets are excluded, and the selected sets (and dev partitions) may not capture the full spectrum of modern RC phenomena (multi-hop, explicit retrieval, or grounded commonsense). The conclusions about general RC properties should be framed cautiously given this scope.

Questions to authors
- Can you report per-skill inter-annotator agreement (e.g., Cohen’s kappa) and describe adjudication? How often did disagreements involve bridging vs elaboration vs coreference?

- How were annotators trained? Can you share the detailed guidelines and examples that disambiguate skill categories?

- Are the reported correlations statistically significant? Did you control for confounders (e.g., passage length, number of supporting sentences, dataset identity) with a mixed-effects model?

- How stable are readability measures to variations in sentence selection (e.g., alternative plausible supporting spans)? Any robustness checks?

- Will the released annotations include sentence IDs, skill labels per question, and nonsense flags, with licensing? Any plan to expand to additional datasets?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. While parts of the methodology are imperfect and the statistics are light, the paper offers a novel, theory-grounded lens on RC evaluation, a useful multi-dataset annotated resource, and clear empirical insights that can influence how we build and assess RC datasets and systems. The conceptual boldness and potential utility outweigh the methodological limitations. Strengthening the reliability reporting and statistical analysis in the final version would solidify the contribution.
