Summary
This paper proposes two complementary evaluation perspectives for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) a refined taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills needed to answer questions (e.g., coreference, bridging, elaboration, logical/mathematical reasoning, clause relations), grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology; and (b) readability metrics (largely following Vajjala & Meurers), applied to the subset of sentences deemed necessary to answer each question. The authors annotate 100 questions from each of six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), report skill-frequency profiles, average number of skills per question, “nonsense” rates, and correlations between readability and skill counts. They find only weak correlations between readability and question difficulty (operationalized as number of skills), argue that RC dataset quality requires both views, and release annotations.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A reorganized, principled taxonomy of RC prerequisite skills that clarifies “knowledge reasoning” via bridging vs elaboration and draws from TE/psychology. This is the core conceptual contribution.
2) A multi-dataset comparative annotation and analysis showing weak correlation between readability and skill requirements, yielding actionable insights about dataset design and genre effects.
3) Public release of annotations that can support future dataset construction and targeted evaluation.

Strengths
- Conceptual grounding and taxonomy refinement: The reorganization of skills, especially distinguishing bridging (lexical/grammatical inference) from elaboration (commonsense/world knowledge), is well-motivated by TE literature and cognitive theories (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano). This makes the label set more theory-driven and potentially more stable than ad-hoc “commonsense” buckets.
- Cross-dataset, uniform analysis: Applying a consistent annotation protocol to six diverse datasets provides informative profiles: e.g., QA4MRE requiring more skills and longer sentence distances; MCTest being easy to read but relatively hard to answer; WDW’s high ellipsis due to question-generation. These results are useful for researchers choosing datasets for specific capabilities.
- Practical insights for dataset construction: The paper highlights genre effects (narratives vs expository), the impact of sourcing (automatic question generation leading to ellipsis and potential nonsense), and the value of meta-annotations (e.g., NewsQA’s “is question bad”). These are directly actionable for dataset creators.
- Clear negative result on readability vs difficulty: The weak correlations (Pearson 0.025–0.416) make a timely point: difficulty to read ≠ difficulty to answer. This cautions against overreliance on surface readability to judge RC challenge.
- Release of annotations: Making the labels public increases the paper’s utility and reproducibility, enabling finer-grained system evaluation and curriculum/targeted training.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation of “difficulty” proxy: The paper assumes “number of skills” correlates with question difficulty, but does not validate this against human difficulty (e.g., response time/accuracy) or system performance. Without linking skill counts to outcomes, conclusions about difficulty remain speculative.
- Sampling size and potential biases: Only 100 questions per dataset (and only 62 used for IAA) limit statistical power. Sampling choices differ across datasets (e.g., SQuAD: one question per paragraph; NewsQA filtered to exclude “bad” questions), which may distort cross-dataset comparisons (e.g., nonsense rates) and complicate fairness.
- Annotation protocol may bias conclusions: Annotators saw the question, the gold answer, and all choices. This reframes the task as textual entailment between selected sentences and the (question, answer) hypothesis, bypassing key RC abilities (locating supporting spans, disambiguating candidates). Readability is computed on annotator-selected sentences, further coupling both views in a way that could reduce apparent reading difficulty.
- Reliability and construct clarity: The reported 90.1% “agreement” over 62 questions lacks detail (what metric? per-skill? per-question? κ/α?). Some skills (bridging vs elaboration; meta-knowledge) are inherently fuzzy; the paper notes disagreements on “nonsense” but does not quantify reliability for that label. Clearer operational definitions, examples, and reliability per skill would strengthen confidence.
- Analysis depth and inconsistencies: Statistical testing for correlations (significance, CIs) and controls for confounds (document length, sentence distance) are absent. Some numerical inconsistencies appear (e.g., text claims QA4MRE average of 4.6 skills; Table 3 shows 3.25). A few referenced tables/figures are missing in the draft, and references contain formatting errors. These issues impede evaluation.

Questions to Authors
- How was inter-annotator agreement computed (metric, unit of analysis)? Please report per-skill κ/α and reliability for “nonsense.” Was there adjudication?
- Can you validate the “number of skills = difficulty” assumption by correlating with human accuracy/RT or baseline/model performance across annotated items?
- How robust are the readability–skill correlations when controlling for context length, number of required sentences, and sentence distance? Are the correlations statistically significant after corrections?
- To what extent do conclusions change if readability is computed on the full context vs only the selected sentences?
- Could you release the full annotation guidelines with positive/negative examples for each skill to improve reproducibility?

Overall evaluation
This is a valuable, thoughtfully grounded analysis paper. The taxonomy and cross-dataset annotations address an important gap and will be useful to the community. However, the empirical claims about difficulty would be stronger with validation against human/system outcomes, fuller reliability reporting, and more rigorous statistical analysis. With clarifications and minor corrections, I lean toward acceptance as a resources/analysis contribution that can inform dataset design and targeted evaluation.
