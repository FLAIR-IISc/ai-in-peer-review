Summary
The paper proposes a two-pronged evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets, separating “answering difficulty” from “reading difficulty.” Answering difficulty is operationalized via a refined set of 13 prerequisite skills (extending and reorganizing prior taxonomies with categories grounded in textual entailment and psychology, e.g., bridging vs. elaboration). Reading difficulty is measured using established readability features (primarily from Vajjala & Meurers, plus Flesch–Kincaid). The authors annotate six RC datasets (100 questions each) with required skills and the subset of sentences necessary to answer, compute readability metrics on those sentences, and analyze: (i) skill frequencies, (ii) number of skills per question, (iii) readability per dataset, and (iv) correlations between number of skills and readability. The main empirical finding is a weak correlation between readability and required skills, indicating that text readability and question difficulty are largely orthogonal. Dataset-specific profiles are presented, and the annotations will be released.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A refined, theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, explicitly distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration and reorganizing knowledge reasoning categories with links to textual entailment and cognitive psychology.
2) A cross-dataset empirical analysis (six datasets, 100 questions each) that profiles required skills, number of skills per question, and readability, and reports weak correlations between readability and skill count, suggesting separable axes of difficulty.
3) Public release of skill and sentence-level annotations to support dataset design and system evaluation.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and grounding: The skill taxonomy is carefully motivated by prior work in textual entailment and human comprehension (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano) and improves over earlier ad hoc categories. The bridging–elaboration distinction makes the taxonomy more interpretable and potentially more actionable for RC analysis.
- Cross-dataset, uniform annotation: Applying the same annotation protocol to six prominent datasets enables meaningful comparisons of their qualitative properties (e.g., WDW’s high ellipsis; MCTest’s narrative-driven causal/spatiotemporal relations). Reporting skill frequencies and average number of skills per question provides concrete, comparable indicators (Tables 2–3).
- Empirical evidence for decoupling readability and answering difficulty: Using FK grade level and other readability features on the subset of sentences deemed necessary to answer, the study finds only weak correlations (r ≈ 0.025–0.416) with skill count, supported visually (Figures 3–4) and numerically (Table 5). This offers a useful caution against using readability proxies to infer RC difficulty.
- Practical guidance and dataset insights: The discussion suggests how to sequence datasets for system development (curriculum/transfer perspectives) and highlights genre effects (narrative vs. expository), “nonsense” question prevalence, and the consequences of automated question curation. The sentence-distance analysis (Table 6) adds an informative view of cross-sentence reasoning demands.
- Reproducibility support: The authors commit to releasing the annotations, which can seed further analyses and benchmarking along the proposed axes.

Weaknesses
- Subjectivity and potential circularity in annotation: Annotators see the context, question, and the correct answer, then select necessary sentences and skills. This can bias sentence selection and skill assignment (confirmation effects), and precludes measuring retrieval/selection competence—acknowledged by the authors, but it limits the interpretation of both skill counts and readability (computed only on selected sentences).
- Limited sampling and heterogeneous selection: Only 100 questions per dataset, with differing sampling procedures (e.g., NewsQA filtered by meta flags; WDW prefiltered by its own heuristics; SQuAD one question per paragraph). These choices may skew dataset profiles and comparability, and reduce statistical power for correlation analyses.
- Inter-annotator agreement under-specified: A single 90.1% agreement statistic over 62 sampled questions is reported without detailing the metric (e.g., Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa for multi-labels, per-skill agreement, sentence selection agreement) or the adjudication process. Given the multi-label nature and sentence selection step, simple percent agreement likely overestimates reliability.
- Statistical analysis is minimal: Correlation results are reported without confidence intervals, p-values, or correction for multiple tests across features/datasets. Given small sample sizes and weak r values, it is hard to assess robustness. No analysis is presented linking skill counts to human or model accuracy to validate “number of skills” as a difficulty proxy.
- Readability feature–task mismatch: Readability features (e.g., FK grade, AWL proportion) target human reading ease and second-language acquisition, not necessarily machine RC difficulty. Although acknowledged, the study relies heavily on these features, and results may depend on the annotators’ sentence selection rather than the inherent text difficulty.
- External validity and coverage: Several large RC datasets (e.g., CNN/DailyMail, CBTest, LAMBADA) were excluded for various reasons, which is reasonable individually but narrows generalizability. The taxonomy’s granularity choices (e.g., separating meta-knowledge) are reasonable but need validation across broader genres/tasks.

Questions to authors
- Annotation reliability: How was agreement computed for multi-label skill assignment and for sentence selection? Please report per-skill kappa (or alpha) and agreement on sentence spans, the number of annotators per item, and the adjudication protocol.
- Difficulty proxy validation: Is there evidence that the number of required skills correlates with human difficulty (e.g., human accuracy, response time) or with baseline/system performance? If available, please report such correlations to support the “skill count as difficulty” assumption.
- Statistical robustness: For the correlations in Table 5 (and FK vs. skill count), please include confidence intervals, p-values, and per-dataset results, and discuss sensitivity to the 100-item sample (e.g., via bootstrapping).
- Readability computation: Which tools were used to compute the readability features? Were features computed on contiguous spans or on discontiguous selected sentences concatenated? How sensitive are results to computing readability on the full context vs. the selected sentences?
- Dataset sampling: Could you standardize sampling across datasets (e.g., stratify by article length/topic, or sample multiple questions per context) and report whether the main findings hold?
- Data release: Will the released annotations include sentence selections, per-annotator labels, and detailed guidelines to facilitate reuse and further reliability studies?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, theoretically informed framework and useful empirical insights, but would benefit from strengthened methodology and statistical validation to substantiate its conclusions and improve comparability across datasets.
