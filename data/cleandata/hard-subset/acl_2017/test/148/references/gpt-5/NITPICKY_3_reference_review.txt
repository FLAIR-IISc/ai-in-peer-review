Summary and key contributions
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) “prerequisite skills” (a refined taxonomy of skills needed to answer questions, reorganizing prior work using notions of bridging vs. elaboration from psycholinguistics and entailment literature), and (b) readability metrics (mainly features from Vajjala & Meurers, plus Flesch–Kincaid). The authors annotate six RC datasets (100 questions each) with required skills and compute readability on the sentences deemed necessary to answer, then study correlations between skill requirements and readability. They report a generally weak correlation between “difficulty to answer” (measured as number of skills) and “difficulty to read,” and qualitative observations per dataset (e.g., SQuAD is harder to read but relatively easy to answer; MCTest is the converse).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An annotated comparative analysis of six RC datasets with a unified (albeit redesigned) skill taxonomy and readability features, yielding qualitative insights (e.g., weak correlation between readability and skill requirements).
2) A reorganization of the “knowledge reasoning” part of RC skills into bridging vs. elaboration, with additional categories (ellipsis, meta-knowledge, punctuation).
3) Public release promise of the annotations (though no link is provided).

Strengths
- Timely, concrete dataset analysis across multiple widely used RC benchmarks. The cross-dataset perspective and the explicit attempt to decouple readability from question difficulty address an ongoing pain point in RC evaluation and dataset construction.
- Skill taxonomy grounded in psycholinguistics and entailment literature. Aligning “bridging” vs. “elaboration” with textual entailment phenomena is more principled than previous ad hoc “commonsense” buckets, and the taxonomy is largely compatible with multiple task formulations.
- Clear qualitative findings likely of practical use. The weak correlation between readability and number of skills (plus per-dataset qualitative profiles and “nonsense” questions) can guide dataset curation and system evaluation/curriculum design.
- Attention to multi-sentence reasoning and sentence selection. The analysis of sentence count and “distance” (even if imperfect) at least attempts to quantify discourse spread in context, which is often overlooked in dataset papers.

Weaknesses
- Methodological circularity and annotation bias. Annotators saw the question and the gold answer, then selected the sentences and skills. This turns the task into post-hoc entailment labeling, biases toward minimal spans, and cannot assess retrieval/selection competence. The authors acknowledge this but still draw system-development conclusions. At minimum, the findings reflect “entailment given oracle answer,” not RC difficulty per se.
- Ambiguity and overlap in the skill taxonomy plus mixing heterogeneous levels. “Bridging” includes idioms and apposition; “elaboration” includes noun compounds and possessives; “punctuation” is a low-level surface feature while “logical reasoning” is high-level—these are not mutually exclusive and likely co-occur (double counting). “Meta-knowledge” is under-specified. Clear operational labeling guidelines, examples, and disambiguation instructions are missing.
- Statistical rigor and reporting gaps. Correlations are reported (0.025–0.416) without p-values, confidence intervals, or multiple-comparison correction. Only Pearson’s r is used (nonlinearities and heteroscedasticity are plausible). The sample is small (n=100 per dataset) and drawn with heterogeneous schemes across datasets (e.g., SQuAD only one Q per paragraph), yet results are generalized. Several referenced tables/figures (Table 1, 4, 5; Figure 1) are missing, impeding verification.
- Internal inconsistencies and unclear metrics. The text claims QA4MRE averages 4.6 skills per question, but Table 3 lists “Ave. 3.25.” Definitions like “nonsense” are treated as a “skill” in Table 2, conflating quality control with skill frequency. The sentence-distance definition (“sum of distances of any two sentences”) is ambiguous; usual practice would be span width or max pairwise distance. Readability features are said to follow Vajjala & Meurers’ top-10 but correlation plots emphasize Flesch–Kincaid, which was not in the listed top-10; rationale for metric selection is unclear.
- Readability measured only on sentences selected by annotators. This confounds readability with annotation choice and shrinks variance; models/users must read the entire passage, not just oracle-selected sentences. It also biases the correlation analysis downward and undermines the “decoupling” claim.
- Dataset selection and sampling raise threats to validity. Exclusion of CNN/DM and CBTest is reasonable, but including WDW and MARCO (both with known QC issues) while generalizing conclusions is risky. Only 100 questions per dataset, with different selection criteria (Appendix A), and limited IAA (62 questions total) make results fragile.
- Reproducibility and presentation quality. No link to released annotations. The IAA “90.1%” is reported without a statistic (e.g., Cohen’s κ, Jaccard, F1) and on a tiny sample. References contain numerous formatting errors and placeholders (“?,” “\Q2016\E”), and the paper has multiple formatting/grammar inconsistencies—ironically in a work arguing for rigorous evaluation quality.

Questions to authors
- What exact inter-annotator agreement measure did you use for multi-label skills? Please report per-skill agreement, overall κ (or alternative), and variance across datasets.
- How were annotators trained? Provide the full annotation guidelines with concrete positive/negative examples per skill, and instructions for differentiating bridging vs. elaboration vs. coreference, and for when multiple labels are applied.
- Please reconcile the inconsistency between the textual claim that QA4MRE averages 4.6 skills and Table 3’s “Ave. 3.25.” Also clarify what the “2.0 1.5 …” row and columns in Table 3 represent; the header is unclear.
- How exactly is “nonsense” operationalized? Why is it tabulated alongside skills? Did you adjudicate disagreements?
- Readability metrics: which exact set did you compute (top-10 from V&M, Coleman–Liau, F-K)? On what text span (entire passage vs. selected sentences)? Please provide ablations showing how results change if computed over the full context.
- Correlations: report all coefficients with sample sizes, p-values, CIs, and Bonferroni/Holm corrections; include scatter plots for all datasets; consider nonparametric and partial correlations (controlling for sentence length).
- Sentence distance: provide a precise formal definition and compare to simpler alternatives (max pairwise distance, span width).
- Release: provide a URL and detailed data card (splits, sampling seeds, annotation format), plus code to reproduce metrics.
