Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a framework to characterize reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: (1) prerequisite skills required to answer a question, reorganized with grounding in textual entailment and cognitive psychology; and (2) readability, using established linguistic features. The authors annotate six RC datasets with the skill taxonomy, compute readability on the sentences deemed necessary to answer each question, and analyze correlations between question difficulty (approximated by the number of required skills) and readability. They report weak correlations, arguing that reading difficulty and question difficulty are largely orthogonal. They release annotations.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A cross-dataset qualitative analysis showing weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, supported by new manual annotations (primary).
2) A revised, more principled taxonomy of prerequisite skills, especially clarifying knowledge reasoning via bridging vs elaboration (secondary).
3) A small, publicly available annotation resource linking questions to required sentences and skills across six datasets (secondary).

Strengths
- Clear, actionable insight on dataset properties: The empirical finding that readability (e.g., Flesch–Kincaid, syntactic/lexical features) only weakly correlates with question difficulty (number of skills; Pearson up to ~0.41) is well illustrated by QA4MRE vs MCTest and SQuAD. This is a useful corrective to common assumptions and directly informs dataset design and curriculum/transfer strategies discussed in Section 6.

- Principled reorganization of skills: The taxonomy ties knowledge-based reasoning to well-established distinctions (bridging vs elaboration; retrieved vs generated) and to entailment phenomena. Compared to earlier ad hoc lists, this increases conceptual clarity and may aid future diagnostic evaluation and error analysis.

- Cross-benchmark perspective with consistent protocol: Annotating six heterogeneous datasets under a common scheme (with per-skill frequencies and average skills/question) provides comparative insights (e.g., WDW’s high ellipsis due to its construction; QA4MRE’s broader reasoning demands; SQuAD’s sentence-locality), and the analysis of sentence counts/distances adds a useful dimension about locality of evidence.

- Transparent methodology and open resource intent: The paper documents sampling choices and acknowledges limitations. Making the annotations public can enable reproducibility and downstream work (skill-aware training, dataset balancing).

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical rigor in annotation reliability and scope: The reported 90.1% is percent agreement (not chance-corrected) on only 62 questions; no per-skill reliability, adjudication protocol, or guideline details are provided. Given the fine-grained, overlapping skills (e.g., bridging vs elaboration vs ellipsis, coreference vs bridging), chance-corrected measures (Fleiss/Cohen’s kappa) per label and per dataset are essential. The sample size (100 questions per dataset) limits statistical power, especially for correlation analyses and subgroup comparisons.

- Potential annotation and measurement bias: Annotators saw the gold answer and selected “necessary” sentences before labeling skills. This introduces hindsight bias and may undercount skills exploited by systems that do not follow the annotator’s reading path. Readability is computed on annotator-selected sentences rather than full passages, conflating ease of reading with ease of sentence identification. The “nonsense” label mixes unanswerable, poorly posed, and possibly answerable-with-external-knowledge cases, potentially distorting frequency counts and skill distributions.

- Unvalidated proxy for difficulty: The core assumption that “number of required skills” monotonically tracks question difficulty is plausible but unvalidated against human difficulty (accuracy, response time) or model difficulty (error rates). Without such validation or item-response modeling, conclusions about difficulty should be more cautious.

- Narrow and sometimes mismatched feature set for readability: The chosen features come from L2 readability work; discourse/semantic cohesion indicators (e.g., entity grid, coreference density, connectives) known to affect comprehension are not included. Only the Flesch–Kincaid index is used in main correlation plots, and statistical testing (confidence intervals, multiple comparisons) is limited or absent.

- Limited connection to system behavior: The paper argues that the metrics can guide system development, but provides no empirical link between metrics and model performance. Even simple baselines (e.g., correlating skill counts with baseline QA accuracy per question) would substantiate the practical value.

Questions to authors
- Reliability: Please report per-skill inter-annotator agreement with chance correction (e.g., Fleiss’ kappa), per dataset if possible. How were disagreements adjudicated?

- Guidelines and examples: Can you share the detailed annotation manual, decision criteria distinguishing bridging vs elaboration vs ellipsis, and illustrative borderline cases?

- Bias control: Why were annotators shown the gold answers? Did you try blinded annotation or a two-stage process (sentence selection, then skill labeling) to reduce hindsight bias?

- Difficulty validation: Do you have human difficulty proxies (time/accuracy) or system-level analyses correlating error rates with skill counts or specific skills?

- Readability scope: How sensitive are your conclusions to computing readability over entire passages vs selected sentences? Did you explore discourse-level features (e.g., entity transitions, connectives)?

- Nonsense handling: How was “nonsense” operationalized and adjudicated? Can you separate unanswerable vs off-topic vs ambiguous?

Overall assessment
This work addresses a timely need for principled RC evaluation and provides useful insights and a resource. However, the empirical foundation (annotation reliability, sample size, potential biases, and validation of the difficulty proxy) is not yet strong enough for publication at ACL. With stronger reliability reporting, expanded/validated annotations, more comprehensive readability features, and at least preliminary links to human/model difficulty, the paper would make a compelling contribution.

Recommendation: Weak Reject. Constructive path forward: (1) expand and rigorously assess annotations (per-skill kappa, blinded protocol, adjudication); (2) validate “skills = difficulty” against human and model performance, possibly via item-response theory; (3) incorporate discourse/cohesion readability features and report robust statistics; (4) release full guidelines, examples, and code to maximize impact.
