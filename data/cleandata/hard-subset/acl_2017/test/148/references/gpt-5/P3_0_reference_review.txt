Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes two complementary classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (1) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills needed to answer a question, grounded in textual entailment literature and cognitive psychology (bridging vs. elaboration; retrieved vs. generated inferences), and (2) readability metrics inspired by prior work in second language acquisition. The authors annotate six RC datasets with these metrics, analyze frequencies of skills, counts of skills per question, readability, and their correlations, and report that question difficulty (proxied by the number of skills) is only weakly correlated with text readability. They release the annotations.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by importance
1) A reorganized, multidimensional taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC that integrates insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology, aiming for broader coverage than prior RC-specific taxonomies.
2) A cross-dataset annotation study (6 datasets, 100 questions each) that characterizes the distribution of skills and readability and highlights a weak correlation between the two, with dataset-specific insights (e.g., MCTest: easy-to-read yet relatively skill-demanding).
3) Public release of annotations that may inform dataset design and future system analyses.

Strengths
- Conceptual integration across fields: The reclassification of knowledge reasoning into bridging and elaboration, grounded in Kintsch’s and McNamara & Magliano’s frameworks and aligned with textual entailment phenomena, is clear and well-motivated. It advances beyond ad hoc “commonsense” categories often seen in RC analyses.

- Cross-dataset, skill-focused audit: Annotating six heterogeneous datasets with the same skill framework provides a useful comparative lens. The reported distributions (e.g., high ellipsis in Who-did-What; broad reasoning in QA4MRE; sentence-local answers in SQuAD/MS MARCO) produce actionable insights for dataset curation.

- Dual-axis perspective on difficulty: Separating “answering difficulty” (skills) from “reading ease” (readability) addresses a common conflation in RC evaluation. The reported weak correlations and the MCTest vs. SQuAD contrast provide concrete empirical support for analyzing these axes separately.

- Practical implications for dataset development and evaluation: The suggested curriculum along the two axes and the emphasis on genre’s role (narrative vs. expository) offer a useful blueprint for constructing balanced benchmarks.

- Reproducibility step: Making annotations public increases the utility of the work for subsequent model-based analyses (e.g., error slicing, curriculum design).

Weaknesses
- Subjectivity and limited validation of the skill taxonomy as a difficulty proxy: The central assumption—“number of required skills ≈ question difficulty”—is plausible but unvalidated here against human difficulty (e.g., item response theory, human accuracy/time) or system performance per question. Without such validation, the interpretation of “skill count” as difficulty is tenuous.

- Annotation methodology lacks detail and rigorous reliability reporting: Inter-annotator agreement is reported as 90.1% on 62 items, but the metric (percent agreement vs. kappa) is unclear, and multi-label reliability per skill is not provided. The small IAA sample and lack of per-skill agreement, adjudication protocol, training, and codebook examples limit confidence in consistency and replicability.

- Sampling and comparability concerns: The selection strategies differ per dataset (e.g., NewsQA filtered for “good” questions; MS MARCO segments restricted to is_selected=1; SQuAD one question per paragraph). These choices likely bias cross-dataset comparisons, including the reported “nonsense” rates and skill distributions, and should be controlled or at least thoroughly analyzed as confounds.

- Readability analysis is narrow and partially misaligned: The chosen features (largely surface/lexico-syntactic and FK grade) may not capture discourse-level or genre-specific readability differences that matter for RC (e.g., coherence relations, entity grid, coreference chain density). Moreover, readability is computed only over sentences deemed necessary by annotators, which confounds readability with the annotation itself, and the paper does not control for number/length of selected sentences in correlation analyses.

- Statistical analysis is underdeveloped: Correlations are reported (0.025–0.416) without confidence intervals, multiple-comparison corrections, or per-dataset significance testing. The analysis does not model key covariates (e.g., sentence count, distance between sentences, genre), nor does it examine non-linearities or interaction effects.

Questions to authors
1) Reliability: What reliability statistic did you use (Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa, Krippendorff’s alpha) for multi-label skill annotation? Can you report per-skill reliability and provide the full annotation guidelines/codebook with canonical examples and decision rules?

2) Aggregation: Did all three annotators label all 600 questions? How were disagreements resolved, and how did you aggregate labels for analysis (majority vote, union, adjudication)?

3) Validation of “skill count” as difficulty: Do you have any human performance measures (accuracy, response time) or model performance per question to correlate with skill counts? If not, could you at least analyze whether skill count correlates with simple proxies (e.g., number of required sentences, sentence distance) to rule out trivial length effects?

4) Readability confounds: Since readability is computed on annotator-selected sentences, how sensitive are your conclusions to sentence selection? Have you compared results against readability computed on full passages, and do the correlations persist?

5) Statistical detail: Please report confidence intervals and p-values for correlations, and clarify whether correlations remain after controlling for sentence count/length, genre, and question type. How many tests were run, and did you correct for multiple comparisons?

6) Nonsense classification: How was “nonsense” operationalized? Given dataset-specific filtering (e.g., NewsQA), can you quantify how sampling decisions affect nonsense rates and downstream skill distributions?

7) Resource release: What exactly will be released (annotations, code for readability features, sampling scripts)? Will you include per-question identifiers to enable alignment with benchmark splits?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider augmenting readability with discourse/coherence features (e.g., entity grid, rhetorical relations) and lexical sophistication indices beyond AWL; these may better capture difficulty relevant to RC.

- Provide a more formal mapping from entailment phenomena to your skill categories, including borderline cases (e.g., apposition vs. coreference vs. bridging) to improve consistency.

- Extend analyses to link skill profiles to model error patterns (even for a couple of baseline models), which would substantiate the claim that the taxonomy is practically diagnostic for system development.

- Where feasible, re-run correlations with mixed-effects models to account for dataset (random effect) and control covariates.

Overall recommendation
This is a useful diagnostic study with a thoughtful taxonomy and informative cross-dataset audit. However, stronger methodological rigor (reliability, validation, and statistical controls) is needed to firmly establish the proposed metrics as proxies for difficulty and to generalize the conclusions. With clarifications and additional analyses, this work can make a valuable contribution to RC dataset design and evaluation.
