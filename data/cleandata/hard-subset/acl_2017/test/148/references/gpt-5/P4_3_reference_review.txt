Summary
The paper proposes two complementary evaluation dimensions for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills required to answer a question, grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration inferences), and (b) readability metrics capturing lexical and syntactic difficulty of the required context. The authors annotate 100 questions each from six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), marking sentences needed to answer, skills required, and “nonsense” items. They compute readability on the selected sentences and analyze correlations between per-question skill counts and readability. Main empirical finding: only weak correlations between readability and the number of prerequisite skills, suggesting that ease of reading does not imply ease of answering. They release the annotations and discuss implications for dataset construction and system development.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Cross-dataset, annotation-based analysis of RC difficulty using a unified skill taxonomy and readability features, with public release of annotations. The finding of weak correlation between readability and skill-based difficulty is empirically supported across six datasets.
2) A refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC—particularly the reorganization of knowledge reasoning into ellipsis, bridging, elaboration, and meta-knowledge—grounded in prior work on textual entailment and human text comprehension.
3) An annotation protocol that isolates sentence-level evidence, supports multi-label skill tagging, flags “nonsense” items, and enables derived measures such as sentence distance.

Strengths
- Cross-dataset comparability and breadth: The study covers six widely used RC datasets under a uniform annotation scheme (100 questions each), enabling meaningful qualitative and quantitative comparisons (e.g., skill frequencies, “nonsense” rates, sentence distances). The reported inter-annotator agreement (90.1% on a held-out sample) indicates reasonable consistency.
- Grounded taxonomy: The refinement of prerequisite skills draws on established distinctions in psychology (bridging vs. elaboration; generated vs. retrieved inferences) and textual entailment literature, improving theoretical grounding over prior ad hoc categories.
- Actionable insights for dataset design: The key result—that readability and skill-based difficulty correlate weakly—provides practical guidance for curating datasets (e.g., one can construct challenging questions over simple texts). Dataset-specific observations (e.g., MCTest being easy to read but relatively hard to answer; WDW’s automatic sourcing improving knowledge-reasoning demands but risking “nonsense” items) are informative.
- Transparent limitations: The paper clearly states that the protocol evaluates entailment-level understanding rather than retrieval or candidate selection, which helps interpret the scope of results.
- Resource value: Public release of annotations and metrics offers a reusable asset for the community to analyze datasets and potentially to train or evaluate models with skill-aware supervision.

Weaknesses
- Difficulty proxy and weighting: Equating question difficulty with the unweighted count of prerequisite skills is a strong assumption. Different skills likely vary in difficulty and composition; the framework does not model interaction strength, depth of inference, or ambiguity. This limits the interpretability of “average skills” as a difficulty measure.
- Statistical rigor of correlation analysis: Correlations between readability and skill counts are reported as weak, but significance levels, confidence intervals, and controls for dataset/genre confounds are not detailed. Without partial correlations or mixed-effects modeling, it is difficult to separate genre/document length effects from readability per se.
- Sampling and annotation details: Only 100 items per dataset may be underpowered to characterize highly diverse corpora; sampling criteria differ across datasets (e.g., NewsQA filtering), potentially biasing comparisons. “Nonsense” labeling is subjective; inter-annotator agreement is reported as percent agreement (metric unspecified), over 62 items, and not broken down per skill or per label, making reliability difficult to assess in a multi-label setting.
- Limited validation against systems: Although the paper motivates metrics for evaluating both datasets and systems, there is no analysis relating the proposed metrics to model performance or error profiles. This weakens claims about utility for system evaluation or curriculum design.
- Clarity and consistency issues: There are discrepancies (e.g., text claims QA4MRE averages 4.6 skills/question, while Table 3 shows 3.25), incomplete or inconsistent references, and some truncated metric descriptions. These issues hinder reproducibility and clarity.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Inter-annotator agreement: How was IAA computed for multi-label skills (per-skill F1, Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa, Krippendorff’s alpha)? Please report IAA per skill and for “nonsense,” and describe adjudication procedures.
- Sampling and robustness: How sensitive are the results to sampling choices (e.g., number of questions per dataset, alternative subsets, removing “nonsense” items)? Did you run bootstrapping or sensitivity analyses?
- Correlation analysis: Please report statistical significance, confidence intervals, and effect sizes for all correlations. Can you provide partial correlations controlling for dataset/genre, and/or mixed-effects models with dataset/document as random effects?
- Difficulty proxy: Did you consider weighting skills (by rarity, annotator difficulty, or human solution time) or modeling compositionality (e.g., number of sentences × skill type) as alternative difficulty measures? Any preliminary validation against human or system accuracy?
- Readability metrics: Beyond the selected features and Flesch–Kincaid, did you test discourse-based metrics or document-level measures? How do results change if readability is computed over full passages versus only the annotated sentences?
- Reproducibility: Will you release annotation guidelines, the annotation tool, and code for computing metrics (including sentence distance), along with dataset splits and the exact item lists?
