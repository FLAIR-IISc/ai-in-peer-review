Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes a framework to evaluate reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two orthogonal axes: (1) prerequisite skills required to answer a question, reorganized into a 13-skill taxonomy grounded in textual entailment phenomena and cognitive psychology (bridging vs. elaboration), and (2) readability of the relevant text using established linguistic features. The authors annotate six prominent RC datasets with these metrics and report empirical patterns, most notably a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A conceptually grounded, two-axis framework for characterizing RC datasets, combining an updated skill taxonomy (aligned with entailment and psychological inference types) and readability features. This is a useful reframing of dataset quality beyond surface question/answer types.
2) A cross-dataset human annotation study (6 datasets, 100 questions each) that yields comparative diagnostics (skill frequencies, skill counts, sentence distances) and the empirical finding that readability and question difficulty are only weakly correlated.
3) A publicly planned release of annotations that can seed more principled dataset construction and curriculum design.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual framing: Positioning RC dataset analysis along prerequisite skills and readability is a clean, compelling abstraction that moves beyond ad hoc type labels. The reorganization of knowledge-reasoning skills into bridging vs. elaboration and retrieved vs. generated inferences draws from well-established literatures (RTE, Kintsch, McNamara), giving the taxonomy stronger theoretical underpinnings than prior work.
- Cross-dataset diagnostic value: Applying a consistent protocol across QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA surfaces meaningful contrasts (e.g., MCTest being easy-to-read yet relatively hard-to-answer; high ellipsis in WDW; long-distance evidence in QA4MRE). These are actionable insights for both dataset designers and system builders.
- Empirical insight with practical implications: Demonstrating weak correlation between readability and skill demand debunks a common proxy assumption. This supports the idea of curriculum/transfer setups that deliberately combine easy texts with hard questions and vice versa, and cautions against relying on readability as a stand-in for question difficulty.
- Transparent methodology and reasonable agreement: The annotation protocol (sentence selection + multi-label skills) is clearly described, with 90.1% agreement reported. Although the agreement statistic could be stronger (see weaknesses), the operationalization looks replicable.
- Forward-looking guidance: The discussion connects findings to development practice (curriculum learning, genre choice, global vs. local coherence), offering a concrete agenda that could influence how the community builds and stages RC benchmarks.

Weaknesses
- Limited statistical depth and small sample per dataset: The analysis relies mainly on correlations over 100 questions per dataset. This raises concerns about representativeness and statistical power. Significance testing, confidence intervals, or robustness checks (e.g., bootstrapping; sensitivity to sampling) are not reported.
- Annotation protocol biases: Annotators saw the answer and were instructed to highlight needed sentences; thus the study deliberately excludes retrieval/selection difficulty, which is a substantial component of real RC. Readability is computed only on selected sentences, which can bias grade levels downward and may distort dataset-level readability comparisons (SQuAD’s “difficult to read” claim is especially sensitive to this choice).
- Reliability reporting is insufficient: The 90.1% agreement is on 62 questions and is percentage agreement, not a chance-corrected measure (e.g., Cohen’s kappa, Krippendorff’s alpha) appropriate for multi-label annotations. Given the subtlety of bridging vs. elaboration, more rigorous reliability reporting is necessary.
- Skill taxonomy still partly ad hoc in practice: While the theoretical grounding is welcome, some categories (e.g., meta-knowledge, bridging vs. elaboration boundaries) will be hard to operationalize consistently without a detailed guideline and adjudication protocol. The paper does not provide the full annotation manual or examples of borderline cases.
- Missing link to system performance: The paper stops short of correlating the proposed metrics with actual model performance across datasets or question subsets. Demonstrating predictive value for modeling outcomes (e.g., skill profile vs. BERT-style accuracy) would substantially strengthen the utility claim of the framework.

Questions to authors
- Can you report chance-corrected inter-annotator reliability (e.g., per-skill kappa/alpha) on a larger subset? How often did annotators disagree between bridging and elaboration, and what were typical confusions?
- How sensitive are the readability results to computing features on (a) the full paragraph/document vs. (b) only selected sentences? Can you provide a robustness analysis?
- Did you assess statistical significance for the reported correlations (Table 5) and differences across datasets (e.g., average skill counts)? Please include p-values or CIs.
- Can you correlate your skill profiles with performance of representative RC systems on the same items, to validate that “more skills” corresponds to greater model difficulty?
- Will you release the full annotation guidelines, the per-question labels/sentence spans, and code for computing features? If yes, when and where?

Additional comments
- The sampling protocols (Appendix A) likely introduce dataset-specific biases (e.g., SQuAD: one question per paragraph). Please discuss how this might affect comparability.
- Table formatting contains minor inconsistencies; clarify averages in Table 3 (some headers look off), and define all metrics (e.g., FK grade) when first used.
- Even if discourse features (Pitler & Nenkova) are unavailable, a limited proxy (e.g., entity grid) could add a complementary discourse signal; consider as future work.

Overall assessment
This paper takes a bold and timely step toward principled RC dataset diagnostics by unifying a skill taxonomy with readability analysis and executing a cross-dataset human study. Despite methodological limitations and modest statistical depth, the core idea is novel and useful, and the empirical findings are informative. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clearer reliability reporting and stronger statistical analysis, because the proposed framework is likely to influence how the community analyzes and constructs RC benchmarks.
