Summary
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (i) a refined taxonomy of “prerequisite skills” grounded in psycholinguistics and textual entailment, and (ii) readability features drawn from prior work. The authors annotate six RC datasets (100 questions each) with multi-label skill annotations and compute readability on the sentences deemed necessary for answering. They report that readability and skill-based difficulty are only weakly correlated, and provide qualitative analyses by dataset, plus a simple measure of sentence distance.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A consolidated, literature-backed taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, reorganizing prior categories (especially for knowledge reasoning) in terms of bridging/elaboration and entailment phenomena.
2) A small-scale, cross-dataset human annotation study (six datasets, 100 Qs each) applying that taxonomy, with public release promised.
3) An empirical observation that readability (Flesch–Kincaid and related features) is only weakly correlated with the number of skills required, highlighting the need to treat reading difficulty and question difficulty separately.

Strengths
- Grounding in established theory: The skill taxonomy is sensibly motivated by the construction–integration model and RTE literature, lending conceptual clarity to “bridging” vs. “elaboration” and situating skills within well-known frameworks.
- Cross-dataset, model-agnostic analysis: Applying a single annotation protocol to six popular datasets yields informative, comparable profiles (e.g., MCTest as easy-to-read yet multi-sentence, knowledge-heavy; SQuAD as hard-to-read but often answerable from one sentence).
- Practical utility for dataset curation: The findings reinforce a useful design principle: readability and question difficulty can be manipulated independently, suggesting concrete levers for dataset construction and curriculum design.
- Transparency about limitations: The paper is forthright about what the methodology does not test (retrieval of relevant sentences, candidate selection), and discusses genre, nonsense questions, and sentence distance as complementary factors.
- Resource contribution: If the annotations and guidelines are released, they could support follow-on analyses and benchmarking.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological rigor and statistical validation: The study annotates only 100 questions per dataset and reports correlations without clear statistical testing, confidence intervals, or controls for dataset-level confounds. The use of Flesch–Kincaid as an omnibus readability indicator is simplistic, and the chosen readability features target second-language readability rather than machine RC difficulty. It is unclear whether correlations are computed within datasets, pooled with controls, or if effects persist after accounting for genre and document length.
- Annotation reliability under-specified: The “90.1%” inter-annotator agreement over 62 questions is reported without defining the metric (exact-match over multi-label sets? per-skill agreement?) and without chance-corrected statistics (e.g., Cohen’s κ, Krippendorff’s α) or per-skill reliability. Given the subtle distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration; meta-knowledge; “nonsense”), reliability is crucial. Seeing the correct answer and options may also induce hindsight bias.
- Predictive validity is not demonstrated: The central assumption that “number of required skills corresponds to difficulty” is not validated against human difficulty (e.g., time/accuracy) or system difficulty (error rates of baseline/strong models). Without such validation, it is hard to assess whether the taxonomy meaningfully explains RC performance.
- Sampling and representativeness concerns: The paper samples from development/gold sets with dataset-specific filters (e.g., NewsQA removing “bad” questions, MARCO using is_selected segments), which may bias difficulty and “nonsense” rates. The sample size and selection choices make it risky to generalize to the full datasets.
- Reporting/consistency issues: There are inconsistencies (e.g., the average number of skills in Table 3 vs. the text; unclear how Flesch–Kincaid was computed when the top-10 features don’t include it; how readability was aggregated over multiple sentences). References and some tables/figures are messy, which hampers reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Inter-annotator agreement: What exact metric produced 90.1%? Please report per-skill agreement and chance-corrected measures (κ/α), and provide a confusion analysis among closely related skills (bridging vs. elaboration vs. coreference).
- Correlation analysis: Are correlations computed within each dataset or pooled? Please report statistical significance, confidence intervals, and partial correlations controlling for dataset identity, context length, and genre.
- Readability computation: Which sentences were included for readability? How was Flesch–Kincaid computed and aggregated across sentences? Why prioritize the Vajjala & Meurers top-10 features for SLA over discourse-level metrics more aligned with comprehension difficulty?
- Predictive validity: Do your skill counts predict human response times/accuracy or the errors of standard RC baselines/SOTA models on these items? Even a small-scale validation would substantiate your central assumption.
- Annotation protocol: What training and detailed guidelines did annotators receive? Will you release them? How did you mitigate hindsight bias given that answers and options were visible?
- Sampling: Please justify the dataset-specific filters (e.g., NewsQA “bad question” removal, MARCO is_selected), and discuss how they affect generalizability. Could you expand beyond 100 items per dataset or provide bootstrapped uncertainty?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important problem and is thoughtfully grounded in prior theory, with a potentially useful resource. However, the empirical study is too small and under-validated to support strong conclusions, and key methodological details (reliability, statistical testing, predictive validity) are missing. I view the contribution as incremental relative to prior skills-based analyses and not yet sufficiently rigorous for ACL. I recommend rejection in its current form; a strengthened version with robust reliability reporting, clearer statistics, and validation against human/system difficulty would be much more compelling.
