Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a two-pronged evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (i) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills required to answer questions (grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology), and (ii) readability metrics capturing syntactic and lexical difficulty. The authors annotate six RC datasets with these metrics, analyze cross-dataset patterns, and report a weak correlation between readability and the number of prerequisite skills, arguing that “reading difficulty” and “answering difficulty” are distinct dimensions.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of significance:
1) A theory-informed reorganization of prerequisite skills for RC, especially the split of knowledge reasoning into bridging vs elaboration (with ellipsis and meta-knowledge), aligning with RTE and the C–I model.
2) A systematic, multi-dataset annotation and analysis that highlights qualitative differences across popular RC datasets and the weak coupling between readability and skill demand.
3) Release of annotated data intended to support dataset construction and curriculum-style system development.

Strengths
1) Conceptual grounding and taxonomy refinement. The paper improves earlier taxonomies by explicitly rooting “knowledge reasoning” in established distinctions (bridging vs elaboration; retrieved vs generated inferences) and clarifying boundaries with coreference, idioms, and clause-level phenomena. This is a genuine step toward a more principled diagnostic framework for RC beyond surface question/answer types.

2) Cross-dataset comparative analysis. Annotating six datasets with a consistent protocol provides useful, actionable insights: QA4MRE is skill-heavy and hard to read; MCTest is easy to read but requires multi-sentence reasoning; SQuAD is hard to read yet often solvable via local evidence. The identification of “nonsense” questions in automatically curated datasets is also practically valuable.

3) Clear separation of two difficulty dimensions. The empirical finding that readability and number of skills weakly correlate is important for both dataset design and evaluation. It cautions against using readability as a proxy for question difficulty and suggests that challenging RC tests can be built from easy-to-read texts (e.g., narratives with multi-sentence causal/coherence demands).

4) Transparent annotation protocol and sampling choices. The paper describes how annotators select supporting sentences and assign skills, and it documents dataset inclusion/exclusion criteria and sampling procedures. This supports reproducibility and makes limitations explicit.

5) Practical implications for dataset creators and system builders. The paper’s discussion on corpus genre, question type (local vs global coherence), and curriculum-style progression is thoughtful and likely to influence future dataset design and evaluation strategies.

Weaknesses
1) Methodological confound: seeing answers and preselecting sentences. Annotators are shown the question and the gold answer (and options), and then select necessary sentences and skills. This bypasses retrieval/disambiguation and likely underestimates difficulty for both humans and systems. It also makes skill assignment dependent on a chosen rationale rather than on the full task, weakening claims about “dataset difficulty.”

2) Reliability reporting is incomplete. The reported 90.1% “agreement” (on 62 questions) is percentage agreement, not a chance-corrected statistic (e.g., Cohen’s kappa/Fleiss’ kappa) and does not address multi-label settings, per-skill agreement, or agreement on sentence selection. Given the fine-grained and potentially overlapping categories (e.g., coreference vs bridging vs elaboration), more rigorous IAA is essential to establish construct validity.

3) Readability metrics and correlation analysis need strengthening. The chosen readability features are from L2 readability (Vajjala & Meurers) and may not capture discourse-level complexity relevant to RC (e.g., entity grid/transition, discourse connectives/PDTB relations). Metrics are computed only on annotator-selected sentences, which biases the view of “reading difficulty” and likely attenuates correlations. Correlation reporting lacks full statistical detail (CIs, p-values, within- vs across-dataset controls). A mixed-effects model controlling for dataset and passage length would be more informative.

4) Taxonomy overlap and discriminant validity. Despite the theory grounding, practical boundaries among bridging, elaboration, coreference, and meta-knowledge can be fuzzy. The paper does not report how often annotators disagreed on these distinctions, nor does it validate that categories predictably differentiate datasets or correlate with independent measures (human time/accuracy, or model performance).

5) Sampling and “nonsense” labeling. Only 100 questions per dataset are annotated; SQuAD uses one question per paragraph; NewsQA excludes “bad” questions by metadata filtering, while “nonsense” for others is judged by annotators without a precise operational criterion. The representativeness and comparability of “nonsense rates” across datasets is thus uncertain.

6) Lack of validation against system or human performance. The paper argues for utility in system development, but does not correlate the proposed metrics with actual model accuracy or human difficulty (time/errors). Even a small-scale study or retrospective correlation (e.g., with baseline vs advanced models) would substantiate the framework’s predictive value.

Questions to authors
- Please report inter-annotator agreement per skill (with chance-corrected measures) and agreement on sentence selection. How often do annotators pick different sentences/skills for the same item?
- How precisely was “nonsense” defined? Did you use any adjudication protocol or rubric? Can you quantify uncertainty (e.g., items flagged as borderline)?
- Were correlations computed within datasets (controlling for dataset as a random effect), and are they significant after correction? Can you share p-values/interval estimates?
- Why compute readability only on selected sentences? Have you compared results to computing on the whole passage or on the sentence-span union? How sensitive are findings to this choice?
- Can you release full annotation guidelines, examples, and the tool to facilitate reuse and replication?

Additional comments
- Consider augmenting readability with discourse-level features (entity-grid coherence, explicit connectives, coreference chain density) and structural features (paragraph/sentence count, sentence position).
- Normalize “distance” by document length to make cross-dataset comparisons more meaningful.
- Provide error analyses showing typical items requiring bridging vs elaboration, and confusion cases among skills.
- The work’s core message is valuable; reinforcing the statistical rigor and reliability, and adding validation against model/human difficulty, would substantially increase its impact.
