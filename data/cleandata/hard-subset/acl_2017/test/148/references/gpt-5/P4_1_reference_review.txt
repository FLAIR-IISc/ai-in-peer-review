Summary
This paper studies how the difficulty of answering questions in reading comprehension (RC) relates to the difficulty of reading the source text. It proposes two classes of evaluation metrics: (i) prerequisite skills required to answer a question, grounded in textual entailment phenomena and cognitive psychology, and (ii) readability metrics derived from prior NLP readability work. The authors refine a prior skill taxonomy, annotate six RC datasets with multi-label prerequisite skills and readability features, and analyze correlations between the number of skills required and readability. The main empirical finding is that the difficulty of questions (as proxied by the number of required skills) correlates only weakly with readability, implying that easy-to-read texts can yield difficult questions and vice versa. The annotations and analyses are intended to inform dataset construction and system development.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A refined, theory-informed taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, reorganizing knowledge reasoning into bridging and elaboration and integrating additional skills (ellipsis, meta-knowledge), with explicit ties to textual entailment and human comprehension literature. This provides clearer operational categories than earlier work.

2) An empirical annotation and cross-dataset analysis of six RC datasets using the proposed skills and readability metrics, including frequencies of skills per dataset, skill counts per question, sentence distance statistics, and reported inter-annotator agreement.

3) An empirical finding of weak correlation between question difficulty (approximated by number of prerequisite skills) and text readability (e.g., Flesch–Kincaid and selected features), yielding actionable insights for dataset design and system evaluation.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing with actionable insight: The paper addresses a real evaluation gap in RC—disentangling “answering difficulty” from “reading difficulty.” The analyses concretely demonstrate weak correlations between these dimensions across datasets, which can guide curriculum design, dataset creation, and diagnostic evaluation.

- Theory-grounded taxonomy: The reorganization of skills draws on established distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration; retrieved vs. generated inferences) and entailment phenomena. Compared to earlier ad hoc categories, this offers a more principled and comprehensive set of labels that capture local cohesion, knowledge reasoning, and sentence-level phenomena.

- Cross-dataset comparative analysis: Annotating six diverse datasets with a common schema enables informative comparisons (e.g., MCTest as easy-to-read yet skill-demanding; QA4MRE as both read- and answer-difficult; SQuAD as read-difficult but answer-easier). The dataset-specific observations are useful for practitioners selecting benchmarks.

- Transparent limitations and methodological clarity: The paper explicitly states that the methodology evaluates contextual entailment understanding rather than sentence retrieval competence, and it includes a basic analysis of sentence counts and distances to partially characterize multi-sentence requirements.

- Public release of annotations: Making the annotations available increases utility for the community, enabling replication and downstream diagnostic uses.

Weaknesses
- Limited scale and reliability reporting: Only 100 questions per dataset were annotated, and inter-annotator agreement is reported as a single percentage on 62 questions without chance-corrected measures (e.g., per-label Cohen’s kappa or Krippendorff’s alpha) or per-dataset breakdown. Given the subjectivity of multi-label skill tagging, stronger reliability evidence is needed.

- Proxy for difficulty may be coarse: Equating difficulty with the number of required skills assumes additivity and equal weight for all skills. This does not capture interaction effects (e.g., certain combinations are disproportionately harder), nor gradation within a skill. A weighted or hierarchical difficulty model could yield more robust conclusions.

- Readability metrics selection and operationalization: The features are borrowed from second language acquisition work and applied only to sentences deemed necessary by annotators. This introduces circularity (readability conditioned on subjective sentence selection) and may not reflect the processing demands of the full context. The correlation analysis would be stronger with: (i) multiple readability indices (including discourse-level features), (ii) robustness checks across whole-context vs. necessary-sentences, and (iii) controls for confounds such as context length and genre.

- Ambiguity in some skill definitions and labeling protocol: Categories like meta-knowledge, bridging vs. elaboration, and analogy are potentially overlapping. The paper would benefit from more detailed guidelines, decision rules, and examples illustrating boundary cases, as well as a report of label co-occurrence patterns and per-label agreement.

- Incomplete and uneven treatment of dataset artifacts: The “nonsense” labeling is used to critique datasets sourced automatically, but criteria and reliability for this label are not fully specified, and selection/filtering steps differ across datasets (e.g., NewsQA filtering). This could bias comparisons. Excluding prominent datasets (e.g., CNN/DM) further limits generality, even if the reasons are understandable.

Questions to authors
- Inter-annotator agreement: How was the 90.1% agreement computed for multi-label annotation (per-item exact match, per-label overlap)? Can you report chance-corrected agreement (e.g., kappa/alpha) per skill and per dataset, and agreement on “nonsense” vs. “solvable”?

- Skill taxonomy and labeling guidelines: Can you provide the full annotation manual with concrete, disambiguating examples for bridging vs. elaboration, ellipsis vs. coreference vs. bridging, and meta-knowledge? How were annotators trained, and what was the calibration process?

- Difficulty modeling: Did you consider weighting skills (e.g., via human difficulty ratings or system error analyses) or modeling interactions among skills? Do your findings hold if you exclude low-prevalence skills or reweight by per-skill agreement?

- Readability analysis: Can you report the full set of correlations (Table 5) with sample sizes and significance, and include additional readability measures (e.g., discourse connectives, entity grid, syntactic depth) and controls for context length? Do correlations change when computed on full passages instead of annotator-selected sentences?

- Sentence selection competence: Given that annotators saw questions and answers, how sensitive are results to the selection of “sentences needed”? Can you quantify variability in sentence selection across annotators and its impact on readability metrics and skill counts?

- Public release: Where can the annotations and code be accessed? Do you include per-instance labels, selected sentences, and IAA metadata to facilitate reuse?

Overall, the paper is clear and technically sound as a descriptive, diagnostic study, with moderate novelty primarily in the refined taxonomy and broader cross-dataset analysis. Strengthening reliability reporting, enriching the readability and difficulty modeling, and clarifying annotation protocols would materially improve the robustness and impact.
