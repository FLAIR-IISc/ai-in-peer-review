Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a framework to analyze reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: (1) a refined taxonomy of “prerequisite skills” required to answer questions, grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology; and (2) readability features capturing lexical and syntactic difficulty. The authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) on 100 questions each, marking sentences needed and skills required, then compute readability on the selected sentences. The main empirical finding is a weak correlation between the number of prerequisite skills (as a proxy for question difficulty) and readability (as a proxy for text difficulty), implying these are largely independent.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A reorganized, 13-skill taxonomy for RC grounded in entailment phenomena (bridging vs. elaboration, etc.) and cognitive models (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano), improving on prior RC skill lists.
2) A cross-dataset, human annotation study that characterizes RC datasets by skill distributions and sentence-level readability, with a clear, practically relevant finding: reading difficulty does not strongly predict question difficulty.
3) Released annotations (promised) that can support future dataset curation and system diagnostics.

Strengths
- Conceptual integration across disciplines: The taxonomy ties together textual entailment categories with psychological distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration; retrieved vs. generated), offering a more principled alternative to ad-hoc “commonsense” or “logic” labels used in prior RC analyses. This is a useful step toward standardizing skill labels across datasets and tasks.

- Dataset-agnostic diagnostic methodology: Annotators identify necessary sentences and multi-label skills independent of dataset format. This allows meaningful comparison of heterogeneous RC tasks (cloze, extractive, multiple choice, multi-passage) and reveals genre/task-driven differences (e.g., narratives in MCTest vs. expository news).

- Actionable insight for dataset design and system development: The empirical evidence that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated supports building evaluation curricula that vary along two axes. The examples (MCTest: easy-to-read yet skillful; SQuAD: hard-to-read yet often single-sentence) are compelling and align with practitioner intuition.

- Transparent limitations and thoughtful discussion: The paper explicitly scopes out search/selection competence and discusses confounds (nonsense questions, genre, task formulation). The additional analysis of sentence counts and distances provides further texture about multi-sentence reasoning needs.

- Potential community resource: If released, the annotations can seed follow-up work on skill-aware modeling, diagnostics, and dataset auditing.

Weaknesses
- Measurement validity of “difficulty”: Equating question difficulty with the count of required skills assumes all skills contribute equally and additively, which is debatable. Some skills (e.g., coreference vs. elaboration) are not comparable in cognitive or computational cost; frequency also does not imply difficulty. Without external validation (e.g., correlation with human error rates or model performance), this proxy remains uncalibrated.

- Annotation reliability and methodology under-specified: The reported 90.1% inter-annotator agreement is on 62 questions and appears to be raw agreement (not chance-corrected). It is unclear whether agreement is measured per-skill, per-instance macro averaging, or for sentence selection. Multi-label settings typically require κ/α or label-wise F1; sentence selection agreement is critical since readability is computed on selected spans. The “nonsense” label is subjective and noted to cause disagreements; more rigorous criteria or adjudication protocols are needed.

- Sampling size and statistical analysis: Using 100 questions per dataset is limited for estimating distributions across 13 skills and for correlational claims, especially when broken down by dataset. The correlation analyses report Pearson r values (0.025–0.416) without p-values, confidence intervals, or multiple-comparison corrections. Figures use Flesch–Kincaid grade level while the chosen top-10 features include Coleman–Liau, creating a disconnect; rationale and consistency are needed.

- Comparability across tasks and confounds: The study intentionally controls away retrieval and answer selection by showing annotators the answer and focusing on entailment. However, this makes the conclusions less informative about end-to-end RC difficulty as experienced by systems (and humans). Differences in context length, dataset filtering (e.g., NewsQA), and task format (cloze vs. extractive vs. MC) likely induce confounds that are not controlled in the analyses.

- Limited linkage to model performance: The motivation mentions evaluating both dataset quality and system performance, yet the study does not relate the skill/readability profiles to actual model or human performance. Even simple regressions predicting EM/F1 from skill counts, sentence distance, and readability would strengthen the practical relevance and validate the “difficulty” notion.

Questions to authors
- Agreement: Please report chance-corrected agreement (e.g., Krippendorff’s α or Cohen’s κ) per skill and for the multi-label setting; also provide agreement on sentence selection. How often did annotators disagree on “nonsense”? Was there adjudication?

- Readability metrics: Why are Flesch–Kincaid grade levels used in Figures 3–4 while the feature set is based on Vajjala & Meurers’ top features (including Coleman–Liau)? Were FK and Coleman both computed? If so, how do their correlations compare?

- Validation of difficulty: Do skill counts correlate with human response time/accuracy or with standard model baselines across datasets? If not available, can you provide a post-hoc analysis using publicly reported model scores?

- Sampling: Given only 100 items per dataset, how stable are the skill distributions (e.g., via bootstrap CIs)? For SQuAD, using one question per paragraph may bias toward easier single-sentence items—did you check robustness to sampling different questions per paragraph?

- Guidelines and release: Will you release detailed annotation guidelines and raw annotations (skills, sentence spans, nonsense flags) to enable replication and extension?

Additional comments
- The taxonomy is useful but could be further clarified to reduce overlap (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration vs. world-knowledge noun compounds) with concrete decision rules and examples.
- Consider alternative difficulty models (e.g., item-response theory) to infer latent difficulty and skill parameters with uncertainty estimates.
- It would be valuable to control for context length in correlational analyses, given its strong influence on both sentence distance and perceived difficulty.

Overall recommendation
This is a timely, well-motivated diagnostic study with a principled taxonomy and broadly useful empirical observations. However, the methodological weaknesses (validity of the difficulty proxy, under-specified reliability, limited statistical rigor, and lack of linkage to performance) reduce the strength of the claims. With clearer reliability reporting, more rigorous statistics, and some validation against human/model performance, this work would make a solid contribution as a resource and framework paper.
