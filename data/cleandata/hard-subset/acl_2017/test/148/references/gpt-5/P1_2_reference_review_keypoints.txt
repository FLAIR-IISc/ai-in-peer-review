- Paper proposes a framework characterizing reading-comprehension datasets along two axes: prerequisite skills and readability of the minimal text needed to answer.  
- Authors refine a skills taxonomy grounded in textual entailment and psycholinguistics and annotate six RC datasets, computing readability on selected sentences.  
- Contribution: reorganize knowledge reasoning into ellipsis, bridging, elaboration, and meta-knowledge tied to entailment/comprehension theory.  
- Contribution: cross-dataset annotation/analysis of six RC datasets showing weak correlation between readability and required skills and surfacing dataset-specific profiles.  
- Contribution: release per-question annotations and a practical profiling methodology intended to inform dataset construction and curriculum design.  
- Strength: taxonomy improvement is conceptually grounded, clearer than prior taxonomies, and useful for error analysis and dataset design.  
- Strength: dataset-agnostic annotation protocol focuses on sentences actually used to answer, increasing interpretability and avoiding confounds with context length.  
- Strength: comparative profiling yields actionable insights (weak readability–difficulty correlation, presence of nonsense questions, genre effects).  
- Strength: two-axis view suggests practical curricula and targeted training/evaluation splits for systems.  
- Strength: reported annotation agreement (~90.1% on a subset) suggests the taxonomy may be learnable and scalable.  
- Weakness: treating the number of required skills as a difficulty proxy is unvalidated and assumes equal additive contributions of skills.  
- Weakness: annotation methodology and reliability are under-specified (annotators saw answers, limited agreement reporting, no per-skill kappa/F1 or adjudication details).  
- Weakness: readability metrics are limited (focus on simple indices), and statistical analysis lacks significance tests, effect sizes, and controls.  
- Weakness: sampling (100 questions/dataset) and dataset selection/filtering limit generality and may not capture true distributions.  
- Weakness: presentation and reproducibility gaps—missing/poorly formatted tables, unspecified IAA metric, and unclear commitment to releasing artifacts.  
- Question: report exact inter-annotator agreement method and per-skill/“nonsense” agreement plus adjudication protocol.  
- Question: validate the “number of skills” difficulty proxy by correlating it with human accuracy/response times or model error rates.  
- Question: provide statistical significance, p-values, confidence intervals, and stratified/partial correlation analyses for readability correlations.  
- Question: assess sensitivity of results to computing readability on full context versus selected sentences.  
- Question: provide guidelines and IAA for the “nonsense” label and quantify solvable-without-context vs truly unsolvable cases.  
- Question: confirm whether and when full annotation guidelines, per-question labels, and tooling will be released and under what license.  
- Overall: useful, timely analysis with solid theoretical grounding, but needs stronger methodological validation (agreement, statistical tests, difficulty proxy) and clearer reproducibility commitments.
