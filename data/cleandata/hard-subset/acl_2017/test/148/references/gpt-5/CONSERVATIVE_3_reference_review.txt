Summary
The paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) a refined taxonomy of “prerequisite skills” (drawing on textual entailment and cognitive psychology distinctions such as bridging vs. elaboration) and (b) readability features (adopted from prior work). The authors annotate 100 questions each from six RC datasets with skill labels and identify answer-relevant sentences, compute readability on those sentences, and analyze correlations. They report weak correlation between readability and the number of required skills and offer qualitative insights about dataset characteristics.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A reorganized taxonomy of prerequisite RC skills that integrates notions from textual entailment and human text comprehension (notably, bridging vs. elaboration, and some added categories such as meta-knowledge and ellipsis).
2) A cross-dataset annotation of six popular RC datasets (100 questions each) with skill labels and selected sentences, plus basic analyses of skill distributions, multi-sentence requirements, and readability.
3) The empirical observation that readability (surface/lexico-syntactic features) is only weakly correlated with the proposed skill-demand proxy, suggesting that “hard to read” context does not necessarily imply “hard to answer.”

Strengths
- Cross-dataset perspective: Annotating six widely used RC datasets under a unified framework provides a comparative view that is often missing. The qualitative observations (e.g., MCTest being easy to read but relatively demanding for multi-sentence reasoning; QA4MRE being challenging on multiple axes) are useful to dataset designers.
- Attempted theoretical grounding: The refinement of skills is motivated by established constructs (bridging vs. elaboration; RTE phenomena). Even if not definitive, it moves beyond ad hoc “question type” taxonomies.
- Practical takeaways for dataset construction: The paper highlights pitfalls in automated curation (nonsense questions) and argues for explicit meta-information about question validity; it also suggests that targeting narrative texts can elicit different skill profiles than expository texts.
- Released annotations (claimed): If made available as stated, this resource could facilitate future analyses and more principled probing of systems.
- Inter-annotator agreement is reported as high (90.1%), suggesting reasonable consistency, though details are missing (see weaknesses).

Weaknesses
1) Validation of the “skill count = difficulty” proxy is insufficient. The central claim that number of skills reflects question difficulty is not validated against human difficulty judgments or model error patterns. There is no analysis showing that questions with more annotated skills are harder for standard baselines or SOTA systems. Without this, the main empirical takeaway (weak correlation with readability) rests on an unproven proxy.
2) Annotation methodology is under-specified and potentially biased. Annotators saw the gold answer and selected relevant sentences; this hindsight protocol conflates comprehension with answer verification and bypasses sentence retrieval, which is a major component of RC. The “nonsense” label is subjective; criteria and adjudication are not described. Only 62 of 600 questions were used to compute agreement (why 62?), and “90.1% agreement” (percent agreement) is not an appropriate reliability statistic for multi-label categorical annotation. No per-skill reliability (e.g., Cohen’s kappa or Krippendorff’s alpha) is reported. It is unclear how multiple annotators’ labels were merged (union/intersection/adjudication).
3) Limited sample size and sampling biases. Only 100 questions per dataset (often from dev sets) may not represent dataset distributions; further, for SQuAD, only one question per paragraph was used; for MS MARCO, only segments with is_selected = 1 were considered; NewsQA was pre-filtered based on meta annotations. These decisions may systematically distort both skill profiles and readability, complicating cross-dataset comparability.
4) Statistical rigor is weak. Correlations are reported (0.025–0.416) without confidence intervals, significance tests, or controls for confounds (e.g., document length, number of sentences selected, genre). Multiple comparisons (across many readability features) are not corrected. The choice to compute readability only on annotator-selected sentences may induce circularity and understate the role of global context.
5) Novelty and contribution scope. Readability features are largely off-the-shelf; the correlation finding (readability ≠ question difficulty) is intuitively expected and has been noted informally in prior work. The taxonomy is incremental rather than fundamentally new, and the empirical section lacks strong validation against systems. As a result, the overall contribution feels more like a preliminary resource note than a conclusive evaluation framework.
6) Clarity and consistency issues. Some referenced tables/figures (e.g., Table 4/5) are not visible here, and there is at least one numerical inconsistency (text claims QA4MRE averages 4.6 skills while Table 3 shows 3.25). References contain formatting errors. These issues reduce confidence in the presentation.

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- Agreement and reliability: What exact metric was used for the reported 90.1% agreement? Why only 62 questions? Please report per-skill kappa/alpha, prevalence-adjusted metrics, and your adjudication/merging protocol.
- Skill count validity: Do more-skill questions empirically correlate with lower human accuracy or higher model error rates on the same items? Can you report correlations with baseline and SOTA system accuracies per question?
- Sampling sensitivity: How robust are your findings if (a) readability is computed on the full context rather than selected sentences, and (b) different sampling strategies are used (e.g., more questions per paragraph, different splits)?
- Statistics: Please provide confidence intervals and p-values for correlations; control for confounders (context length, number of selected sentences); and correct for multiple comparisons.
- Annotation guidelines: How were annotators trained? How were “nonsense” decisions operationalized? Can you share the detailed guidelines and examples?
- Resource release: Exactly what annotations will be released (skills per question, selected sentences, nonsense flags)? Under what license and with what documentation?

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and offers potentially useful annotations, but the methodological validation and statistical rigor are not yet at the level needed to support strong claims. Strengthening reliability reporting, validating the skill proxy against human/model difficulty, and tightening statistical analyses would substantially improve the work.
