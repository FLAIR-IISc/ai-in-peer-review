Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a two-axis framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (1) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills grounded in psycholinguistics and textual entailment (including a clearer split between bridging vs. elaboration inferences, and new categories like ellipsis and meta-knowledge), and (2) readability metrics drawn from prior work on human readability. The authors annotate six RC datasets (100 questions each) with skill labels and compute readability over the sentences deemed necessary to answer each question. Key findings include a weak correlation between question difficulty (approximated by the number of required skills) and text readability, plus dataset-specific profiles (e.g., MCTest is easy to read but can be hard to answer; SQuAD is harder to read but often solvable from a single sentence). They release the annotations.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A conceptually motivated reorganization of RC prerequisite skills that integrates insights from psycholinguistics and textual entailment, especially the explicit treatment of bridging vs. elaboration and ellipsis.
2) A two-axis evaluation scheme (skills x readability) and a cross-dataset analysis across six RC benchmarks showing a weak skills–readability correlation and actionable dataset characterizations.
3) A publicly released annotation resource that can guide dataset construction and system evaluation.

Strengths
- Conceptual integration with clear practical payoff: The skill taxonomy is more theoretically grounded than prior ad hoc lists. Distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration and adding ellipsis/meta-knowledge ties RC analysis to well-studied inference categories. This is a useful reframing of “difficulty” that many RC papers hand-wave.
- Cross-dataset comparative analysis that surfaces concrete insights: The study shows, with evidence, that readability and question difficulty weakly correlate (Pearson 0.025–0.416), and that datasets cluster differently (e.g., QA4MRE: hard-to-read and hard-to-answer; SQuAD: hard-to-read but often solvable with a single sentence; MCTest: easy-to-read yet requires multi-sentence reasoning). These observations can inform both dataset design and model evaluation.
- Annotation protocol is explicit and yields high agreement: Annotators see question, answer, and select the minimally necessary sentences, then tag skills. The reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% (on 62 items) is encouraging. The per-dataset profiles (e.g., WDW’s higher ellipsis; NewsQA’s advantage from meta flags) appear face-valid.
- Practical relevance for the community: The proposed curriculum/transfer learning angle is compelling—using the two axes to stage system development from easy-to-read/easy-to-answer to hard/hard. The release of annotations increases the work’s utility, potentially enabling analyses of model errors by skill.
- Useful secondary analyses: The sentence-selection stats (average number and distance of required sentences) provide another lens on dataset structure and are aligned with qualitative expectations (e.g., MCTest multi-sentence, QA4MRE longer distance).

Weaknesses
- Scale and statistical power are limited: Only 100 questions per dataset are annotated; IAA is reported on 62 items overall, not per skill. The correlation results, while plausible, may be underpowered, and confidence intervals are not reported. The main claim (weak skills–readability correlation) risks being seen as unsurprising without stronger statistical backing or broader sampling.
- Measurement validity and subjectivity: The central operationalization of difficulty as the “number of required skills” is simplistic. Skills likely differ in cognitive load, frequency, and interdependence. Without per-skill weighting or validation against human or model difficulty, the metric is coarse. The bridging vs. elaboration boundary can be subtle; the paper lacks per-skill IAA or a coding manual to assess consistency.
- Readability features are narrow and dated for RC: The chosen readability metrics (e.g., FK/Coleman, Vajjala & Meurers 2012 features) capture surface lexical/syntactic complexity, but omit discourse structure and long-range coherence—crucial for RC difficulty. The analysis largely collapses to FK grade level for correlation plots. This risks underestimating text-side contributors to RC difficulty.
- Potential annotation bias: Annotators see the gold answer, which may bias sentence selection and skill attribution, and the methodology intentionally does not assess retrieval/selection competence. As the authors note, this limits what “difficulty” captures and may inflate single-sentence solvability for datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO.
- Limited validation against system behavior: The paper does not link its skill/readability metrics to model performance or error profiles. Even a small-scale correlation between skill counts and baseline/model accuracy would strengthen the claim that these metrics are actionable for system development.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a detailed coding guide and report per-skill inter-annotator agreement? In particular, how did annotators disambiguate bridging vs. elaboration and handle ambiguous cases (e.g., noun compounds, appositions)?
- How sensitive are your findings to the choice of readability aggregate? If you replace FK with a discourse-aware readability proxy or include cohesion features (e.g., entity grid, connectives), does the skills–readability correlation change?
- Did you assess the reliability of the “number of required sentences” and sentence distance metrics across annotators? Any per-dataset IAA for sentence selection?
- Can you share any preliminary evidence linking your skill labels to model performance (e.g., do off-the-shelf baselines fail disproportionately on questions labeled with bridging+coreference or higher skill counts)?
- What is the format and coverage of the released annotations (per-question skill labels, sentence spans, sentence indices)? Will code for computing readability on selected sentences be released?

Overall, I find the direction worthwhile: a theoretically informed, two-axis lens on RC dataset difficulty with cross-dataset annotations and actionable insights. While parts of the empirical validation are limited, the conceptual contribution and the resource can catalyze better diagnostics and dataset design.
