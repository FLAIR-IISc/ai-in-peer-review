Summary
This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (1) a refined taxonomy of “prerequisite skills” required to answer questions (including reorganized knowledge reasoning categories grounded in bridging vs. elaboration), and (2) readability metrics drawn from prior work. The authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six RC datasets with required skills and selected sentences, compute readability on those sentences, and analyze correlations. They report weak correlations between readability and the number of required skills, and provide qualitative observations about dataset characteristics. They intend the annotations to aid dataset construction and system development.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A reorganized, more theory-aligned taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, especially the refinement of knowledge reasoning into bridging/elaboration and related categories.
2) A cross-dataset manual annotation study (six datasets, 100 questions each) with associated analyses showing weak correlation between readability and skill count, and dataset-level profiling (e.g., “nonsense” questions, sentence distances).
3) Release of annotated data (claimed), intended as a resource for analyzing datasets and guiding system development.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: Beyond accuracy, we need ways to characterize dataset difficulty and quality. The focus on skills and readability is timely and useful for the community.
- Theory grounding: The reorganization of knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration draws on established work in psychology and textual entailment. This aligns RC analysis with well-studied constructs and is more principled than ad hoc labels.
- Multi-dataset analysis: Annotating six prominent datasets and contrasting their profiles (e.g., SQuAD vs. MCTest vs. QA4MRE) provides a comparative perspective that practitioners can leverage when selecting datasets and interpreting results.
- Practical observations: Findings that readability and “difficulty” (as operationalized) are weakly correlated, and that narratives can yield hard questions despite easy text, are valuable for dataset design. The identification of “nonsense” questions in certain automatically curated sets is actionable.
- Annotation includes sentence selection and a simple sentence-distance measure, yielding insights about local vs. global context needed per dataset that can inform modeling and evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Limited and subjective validation: The core assumption that “number of required skills” is a valid proxy for question difficulty is not empirically validated against human difficulty (time/accuracy) or model performance. Not all skills are equally hard, yet all are weighted equally. Without predictive validation (e.g., correlating skill profiles with system error patterns), the practical utility remains speculative.
- Small-scale and potentially biased annotation: 100 questions per dataset, percentage agreement reported on only 62 questions, and use of raw agreement rather than chance-corrected measures (e.g., kappa) reduce confidence. Annotators saw the gold answer when labeling, which can bias both sentence selection and skill attribution toward entailment-style reasoning and away from genuine RC behavior (retrieval, disambiguation).
- Readability metrics may be mismatched to the task: The chosen features (e.g., Vajjala & Meurers, Coleman-Liau, FK grade) are designed for human readability (often L2 contexts), not necessarily for machine comprehension. The correlation analysis appears centered on FK grade in figures; while Table 5 covers more metrics, effect sizes are small to moderate with no clear statistical testing or robustness checks. Computing readability only on annotator-selected sentences introduces circularity and may attenuate or distort correlations.
- Methodological ambiguity and under-specification: The taxonomy is presented at a high level with many categories (some overlapping), but coding guidelines, decision rules, and per-skill reliability are not detailed. “Nonsense” labeling criteria are not rigorously defined, and dataset-specific filtering (e.g., NewsQA) undermines cross-dataset comparability. The sentence distance metric is nonstandard, unnormalized by passage length, and its interpretability is unclear.
- Limited novelty and impact beyond analysis: The work does not propose automated metrics, predictive models, or validated tools for practitioners; it primarily offers manual annotations and qualitative/weakly quantitative analyses. Exclusion of major datasets (CNN/DM, CBTest, LAMBADA) narrows generalizability. Several claims (e.g., genre-driven skill distributions) are plausible but not controlled for confounds.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide chance-corrected inter-annotator agreement (e.g., Cohen’s kappa) per skill and for sentence selection, on the full sample? Also share the detailed annotation guidelines and examples to assess reproducibility.
- How stable is the taxonomy? Did you pilot alternative groupings, and how often did annotators disagree between, e.g., bridging vs. elaboration, or ellipsis vs. bridging?
- Can you validate the “skill count” proxy by correlating it with (a) human difficulty (time/accuracy) and (b) model performance across diverse architectures? Even a small-scale study would strengthen the central claim.
- Did you test correlations and differences for statistical significance (with corrections for multiple comparisons)? Please report effect sizes and p-values for Table 5 and key dataset comparisons.
- How sensitive are the results to computing readability on the entire passage vs. only selected sentences? Could you provide an analysis showing robustness to this choice?
- Given annotators saw answers, how might hindsight bias affect skill attribution? Have you tried a blinded variant (annotate skills before seeing the answer) to estimate the effect?
- Will you release the full annotations, code, and selection scripts? If so, under what license and with what documentation?

Additional comments
- The reference list appears inconsistent and contains formatting issues; please clean up citations.  
- Consider weighting skills or modeling them hierarchically; equal weighting likely misrepresents difficulty.  
- If space permits, include more concrete, labeled examples illustrating each skill category, especially where categories may overlap.
