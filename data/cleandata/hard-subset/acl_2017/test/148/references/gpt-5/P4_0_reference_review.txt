Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes evaluation metrics to analyze reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: (a) prerequisite skills required to answer questions, refined from prior taxonomies and informed by textual entailment and cognitive psychology; and (b) readability features derived from prior work on linguistic complexity. The authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with these metrics, report inter-annotator agreement, and study correlations between number of skills and readability. They find weak correlation between the difficulty of answering (approximated by number of prerequisite skills) and readability of the context.

Contributions, in order of strength (as assessed)
1) An empirical, cross-dataset annotation and analysis showing only weak correlation between readability metrics and number of prerequisite skills required, with dataset-specific profiles that surface known and new artifacts (e.g., nonsense question incidence, genre effects).
2) A refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC that organizes “knowledge reasoning” via bridging vs. elaboration and integrates phenomena from textual entailment and psychology.
3) A publicly available annotation of six RC datasets with prerequisite skills and readability features intended to support dataset design and system analysis.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and motivation: The paper articulates why simple accuracy and superficial categories are insufficient for RC evaluation. The contrastive examples and the explicit linkage to cognitive theories (C–I model; bridging vs. elaboration) provide a coherent rationale for the two metric classes.

- Systematic, multi-dataset analysis: Six diverse datasets are annotated under a unified protocol (100 questions each), enabling apples-to-apples comparisons. The analyses (skill frequencies, number of skills per question, readability profiles, sentence distance) surface concrete, interpretable differences, e.g., MCTest’s higher discourse/causal demands despite low readability, WDW’s elevated ellipsis due to query sourcing, and MS MARCO/WDW’s higher “nonsense” rates.

- Taxonomy grounded in prior literature: The reorganization of prerequisite skills, especially separating bridging vs. elaboration and integrating entailment phenomena (synonymy, hypernymy, thematic roles, idioms), reduces ambiguity compared to earlier taxonomies and aligns with well-known distinctions in comprehension research.

- Evidence of annotation reliability: The reported 90.1% inter-annotator agreement, while not fully specified, indicates non-trivial consistency for a multi-label, fine-grained skill scheme and supports the feasibility of the framework.

- Practical implications for dataset/system development: The discussion connects findings to actionable paths (curriculum/transfer learning by dataset difficulty profiles; attention to genre; moving from local to global coherence in question types). These recommendations are grounded in the presented analyses.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: The methodological components largely adapt existing constructs (skills from Sugawara et al., readability from Vajjala & Meurers, bridging/elaboration from psychology/TE). The main new result (weak correlation between readability and skill count) is plausible and not unexpected. The work’s value is primarily empirical consolidation rather than conceptual or algorithmic innovation.

- Insufficient detail on annotation reliability: The agreement metric (90.1%) is reported without specifying the statistic (percent agreement vs. Cohen’s kappa/alpha), treatment of multi-label items, per-skill agreement, or adjudication procedures. Given the fine-grained scheme, these details are critical to assess validity and replicability.

- Sampling and comparability concerns: Only 100 questions per dataset are used, with different sampling rules across datasets (e.g., filtering “bad” questions in NewsQA; one question per SQuAD paragraph). These decisions may bias distributions (e.g., nonsense incidence, readability ranges), affecting cross-dataset comparability and correlation estimates.

- Proxy for difficulty is unvalidated: The assumption that “number of prerequisite skills” monotonically reflects question difficulty is not validated against human difficulty (time/accuracy) or system performance. Without corroboration, conclusions about “difficulty to answer” remain indirect.

- Readability measurement limitations: Readability features (including Flesch–Kincaid) are computed on annotator-selected “needed” sentences, which can be short spans not ideal for such metrics, potentially introducing instability. The choice of Flesch–Kincaid as the representative metric in correlation plots may underrepresent syntactic/lexical complexity captured by other features. Additionally, sentence-distance comparisons are not normalized by context length, confounding cross-dataset conclusions.

Questions to authors
- Inter-annotator agreement: What statistic was used (e.g., Cohen’s kappa, Krippendorff’s alpha), how was multi-label agreement computed, and what were per-skill agreement rates? How were disagreements resolved?

- Sampling and filtering: How sensitive are results to the sampling/filters (e.g., including “bad” questions in NewsQA, different SQuAD sampling strategies)? Can you report robustness checks or confidence intervals?

- Validation of “number of skills = difficulty”: Do you have correlations with human performance (accuracy/RT) or with baseline/system accuracy across items to substantiate this proxy?

- Readability computation details: Which tools and parameters were used for feature extraction (tokenization, sentence splitting, syllable counts, AWL lists)? Can you report the distributional properties of feature values for short spans and assess their stability?

- Data release and reproducibility: Where can the annotations and code be accessed? Do releases include sentence selections, skill labels per item, and readability features to enable exact replication?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically sound in its use of established constructs, and provides useful empirical annotations and observations. The main limitations concern novelty, validation of the difficulty proxy, and methodological details around reliability and sampling that, if addressed, would strengthen the contribution.
