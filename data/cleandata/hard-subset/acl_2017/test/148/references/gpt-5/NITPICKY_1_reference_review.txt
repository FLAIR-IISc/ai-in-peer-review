Summary and claimed contributions
- Proposes a two-class evaluation framework for RC datasets: (i) prerequisite skills (a refined, 13-item taxonomy grounded in TE and psycholinguistics) and (ii) readability metrics (primarily Vajjala & Meurers features; FK grade for visualization).
- Annotates 6 RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) on 100 questions each, labeling sentences required to answer and the prerequisite skills, plus readability of those sentences.
- Main empirical finding: weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty (operationalized as the number of prerequisite skills).

Strengths
- Addresses a real gap: most RC benchmarks provide superficial category labels; a cross-dataset, skill-oriented analysis is valuable for dataset diagnosis and system development.
- Thoughtful attempt to ground a skills taxonomy in both textual entailment (entailment phenomena) and cognitive theories (bridging vs elaboration), moving beyond ad hoc “commonsense” buckets.
- Cross-dataset comparison yields concrete, sometimes counterintuitive insights (e.g., easy-to-read narratives can pose multi-skill demands; SQuAD is syntactically/lexically harder yet often answerable via single sentences).
- Practical contribution of releasing annotations (stated), which could enable follow-up research on fine-grained evaluation, curriculum design, and modeling.
- The sentence-selection analysis (number and distance of required sentences) is a useful angle on dataset structure, with potential implications for multi-sentence reasoning evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Core operational assumption (more required skills = higher difficulty) is unvalidated. There is no correlation analysis with human difficulty (e.g., accuracy, response times) or model performance per question subset. Without such validation, the central conclusion about “difficulty” remains speculative.
- Annotation methodology risks bias and confounds:
  - Annotators saw the gold answer and highlighted sentences; this can dramatically change perceived “required skills” compared to realistic RC where locating evidence and eliminating distractors is part of the task. The authors explicitly exclude search/selection competence, but then draw dataset-level conclusions that mix in that selection via sentence-level readability estimates.
  - “Nonsense” label is ill-defined and inconsistently handled across datasets (e.g., NewsQA was filtered by metadata; other sets were not). This undermines dataset comparability and reported “nonsense” rates.
  - Inter-annotator agreement is reported as 90.1% on 62 questions, but the measure is unclear (percent agreement on multi-label? Per-label agreement? Cohen’s kappa/J/alpha?). Given the multi-label, fine-grained taxonomy, proper reliability statistics per category are essential. The sample size (62) is small relative to 600 annotated items.
- Small and potentially non-representative samples: 100 questions per dataset, sometimes with additional filtering (Appendix A). No confidence intervals/standard errors are reported for percentages, skill counts, or correlations. Some datasets have heterogeneous distributions across documents; sampling one question per SQuAD paragraph further complicates representativeness.
- Readability analysis is under-specified and potentially misaligned:
  - Readability is computed only over annotator-selected sentences, not entire contexts. This injects subjective selection bias and limits comparability with human readability studies (which assume full text). It also conflates “how much of the text is needed” with “how hard the text is.”
  - Correlation analysis: Pearson coefficients are reported (0.025–0.416) without statistical significance, multiple-comparison control, or partialling out confounds (context length, number of selected sentences, genre). Figures emphasize FK grade, but Table 5 (not shown) presumably covers 10 features; the narrative does not clarify which features correlate and why.
- Taxonomy design issues:
  - Overlap/ambiguity among categories (e.g., bridging vs elaboration vs coreference vs clause relation; “punctuation” vs “clause relation”; “analogy” covering metaphors/metonymy/synecdoche) risks double-counting and inconsistent coding. Clear decision criteria and examples per label are missing.
  - Theoretical integration is uneven: importing TE phenomena and cognitive categories is promising, but mapping to concrete RC skill manifestations needs more rigorous operational definitions and examples to ensure coder consistency.
- Inconsistencies and errors:
  - Table/text mismatch: text states QA4MRE average required skills ≈4.6, while Table 3 shows 3.25. This is a serious internal inconsistency that calls results into question.
  - Numerous formatting/reference errors (broken citations with “?,” stray LaTeX escapes, malformed links, inconsistent abbreviations like “Logical rsng.”), and figures/tables referenced but incompletely described. Appendix B’s sentence distance definition (“sum of distances of any two sentences” when >2) is ambiguous and non-standard.
- Scope underdelivers on “system evaluation”: no experiments showing that the proposed metrics predict model errors, or that curriculum/transfer guided by these metrics helps training. Claims about system development utility are speculative.

Questions to authors
- How exactly is inter-annotator agreement computed for multi-label skill annotation? Please report per-skill agreement (kappa/alpha), macro/micro averages, and agreement on sentence selection.
- Please reconcile the QA4MRE average required skills discrepancy (4.6 vs 3.25). Which value is correct? Were different subsets used?
- What precise criteria and instructions did annotators use to distinguish bridging vs elaboration vs coreference vs clause relation? Provide the full coding manual and examples for borderline cases.
- Are correlations statistically significant after correcting for multiple tests? Did you control for number of selected sentences and document length? What happens if readability is computed on full contexts instead?
- How sensitive are your findings to the “seeing the answer” setup? If annotators do not see answers, do skill counts or sentence spans change materially?
- Can you provide per-dataset breakdowns of skill co-occurrence matrices and an analysis of label redundancy?

Additional comments
- Report confidence intervals/standard errors for all percentages and averages; include per-dataset sample characteristics (context lengths, sentence counts).
- Clarify “nonsense” definition and harmonize filtering across datasets for fair comparison.
- Consider validating the “skill count = difficulty” hypothesis against human measures or model performance on subsets.
- Fix citation/formatting errors and ensure all figures/tables referenced are present, labeled, and reproducible.
- Provide code and data for readability computation, including tokenization/syllabification choices and AWL lexicon versioning.

Overall assessment: The problem is important and the cross-dataset lens is useful, but methodological and reporting issues (notably unvalidated difficulty proxy, annotation biases, reliability, sampling, and internal inconsistencies) substantially weaken the contribution. I recommend rejection in the current form; with stronger validation, clearer operationalization, and corrected analyses, this could become a valuable resource paper.
