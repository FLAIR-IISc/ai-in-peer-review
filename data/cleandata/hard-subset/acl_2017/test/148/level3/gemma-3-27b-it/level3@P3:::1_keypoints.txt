- Paper analyzes relationship between text readability and MRC difficulty across six datasets, annotating them with prerequisite skills and readability metrics, finding a weak correlation.  
- Main contribution: publicly released annotated datasets enabling further study.  
- Secondary contribution: attempt to formalize MRC dataset evaluation beyond simple categories via prerequisite skills + readability.  
- Empirical observation: readability metrics weakly correlate with machine question difficulty, suggesting easy-to-read but hard-to-answer datasets are possible.  
- Strength: relevant motivation to better understand RC dataset properties and their impact on models.  
- Strength: broad coverage by analyzing six diverse datasets.  
- Strength: commitment to making annotations publicly available for reproducibility.  
- Strength: novel combination of prerequisite skill analysis with readability metrics.  
- Major weakness: central goal and scope are unclear—uncertain whether focus is human readability, machine comprehension, or both.  
- Major weakness: conflates human and machine reading comprehension and applies human-focused readability metrics to machines without sufficient justification.  
- Major weakness: overlooks important prior work in readability beyond a narrow citation set.  
- Major weakness: paper attempts too many objectives (dataset evaluation, skill taxonomy, readability–difficulty relation), weakening impact.  
- Major weakness: conclusion that readability doesn't affect QA difficulty is dubious and dependent on chosen features/methods; needs clearer caveats that it pertains to machines.  
- Major weakness: Section 2.2’s detailed discussion of human comprehension models is largely irrelevant and distracts from the main argument.  
- Major weakness: annotation methodology is poorly described—unclear annotator counts, instructions, and perspective (human vs. machine).  
- Request: explicitly state whether the work targets machine or human reading comprehension and separate analyses if both are claimed.  
- Request: provide full annotation details—numbers per dataset, annotator instructions, and whether judgments were machine-oriented.  
- Request: justify selection and applicability of chosen readability metrics for evaluating machine comprehension.  
- Request: give detailed definitions for prerequisite skills and inter-annotator agreement per skill (not just overall 90.1%).  
- Request: explain how annotator disagreements were resolved and quantify their impact on results.  
- Recommendation: revise title to explicitly indicate focus on machine comprehension.  
- Recommendation: thoroughly proofread English to fix grammatical errors and clarity issues.  
- Recommendation: when claiming examples show lack of correlation, explicitly state the claim is about machine comprehension.  
- Clarify whether skills/examples/citations in Section 3.1 are intended for humans or machines and reconcile human-focused citations with machine claims.  
- Clarify Table 6: explicitly define what “distance” measures and how to interpret it.
