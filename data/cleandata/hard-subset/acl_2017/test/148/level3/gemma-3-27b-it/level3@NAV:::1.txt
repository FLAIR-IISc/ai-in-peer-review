## Review: Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability

This paper attempts to connect the concepts of text readability, as traditionally studied in educational psychology, with the performance of machine reading comprehension (MRC) systems. The authors analyze six RC datasets using both “prerequisite skills” – skills needed to answer questions – and traditional readability metrics. While the idea of bridging these areas is interesting, the paper suffers from significant conceptual and practical issues, hindering its overall contribution.

The primary, albeit weak, contribution lies in the empirical analysis of the six datasets. The annotated data, even if the methodology is flawed (see below), offers a resource for the MRC community. Additionally, the paper highlights the observation, already noted in prior work, that readability doesn't *guarantee* question difficulty for machines, potentially encouraging researchers to focus on more nuanced features.

However, the paper is fundamentally weakened by a lack of clear focus. It’s unclear whether the central goal is to improve our understanding of *human* reading comprehension or *machine* reading comprehension. This ambiguity permeates the work, leading to a conflation of the two areas. The authors repeatedly cite work from psychology relating to human comprehension when the paper ostensibly concerns machine performance. This is a critical flaw.

Further, the paper attempts to tackle too much at once. Revisions to prerequisite skill classification, application of a broad range of readability metrics, and dataset annotation are all undertaken, but none are explored in sufficient depth. Section 2.2, in particular, feels like an irrelevant digression into the construction-integration model without a clear link to the presented work.  The conclusion, stating readability doesn’t affect QA difficulty, feels contingent on *their* chosen features (POS/dependency) and isn’t a universally valid claim. 

Significant weaknesses also exist in the methodology. The annotation process lacks detail – how many questions each annotator labeled, and crucially, were they instructed to assess difficulty from a machine or human perspective? The paper also overlooks relevant prior work in readability research beyond Vajjala & Meurers (2012) – for example, DuBay (2004), which clarifies the difference between "readability of text" and "difficulty of reading content".  Finally, the manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors and requires thorough proofreading.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Were annotators explicitly instructed to judge question difficulty from the perspective of a machine reading comprehension system, or a human reader?
2.  Can you clarify how the "distance" metric (Table 6, Appendix B) is intended to inform our understanding of reading comprehension difficulty?
3.  How do you respond to the criticism that the conflation of human and machine reading comprehension undermines the core claims of the paper?
