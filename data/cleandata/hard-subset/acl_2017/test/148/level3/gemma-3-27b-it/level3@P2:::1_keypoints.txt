- Paper studies how readability and prerequisite-skill metrics relate to question difficulty across six RC datasets.  
- Main contributions: combined readability + skill metrics analysis, refined prerequisite-skill taxonomy, and public release of annotated datasets.  

Strengths:
- Thorough comparative analysis applying both readability and skill-based metrics to six prominent RC datasets.  
- Skill reorganization grounded in textual entailment and human comprehension theory, adding bridging and elaboration categories.  
- Publicly released annotations and sampling methods enable replication and further research.

Weaknesses:
- Core goal is ambiguous—mixes machine RC and human reading-comprehension objectives, weakening focus and contribution clarity.  
- Annotation methodology is under-specified: guidelines, annotator perspective (machine vs human), inter-annotator agreement, and disagreement resolution are unclear.  
- Insufficient engagement with prior readability literature (e.g., DuBay 2004) and related work, weakening metric choice justification.  
- Scope is overly broad—covering dataset evaluation, skill taxonomy, and system implications dilutes impact; claims about readability’s effect depend on chosen features.

Questions for authors:
- Please provide detailed annotator instructions and examples for applying prerequisite skills.  
- How were annotator disagreements resolved and consistency ensured? Report inter-annotator agreement.  
- How do the selected readability metrics map to challenges specific to machine reading-comprehension vs human readers?  
- How sensitive are the findings to the choice of readability metrics and did you evaluate alternative measures?

Additional comment:
- Manuscript needs careful English proofreading and should explicitly state “machine comprehension” when claiming readability doesn’t correlate with difficulty.
