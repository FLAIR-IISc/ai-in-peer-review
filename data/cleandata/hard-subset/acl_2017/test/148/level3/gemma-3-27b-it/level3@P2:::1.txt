This paper investigates the relationship between readability metrics and question difficulty in reading comprehension (RC) datasets, aiming to provide a more nuanced evaluation of these datasets beyond simple accuracy. The authors apply two classes of metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – to six existing datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) and analyze the correlations between them. The primary contributions, as I see them, are: (1) the application of a combined set of readability and skill-based metrics to a variety of RC datasets, providing a detailed comparative analysis of their characteristics; (2) a refinement of existing prerequisite skills for RC, drawing from research in textual entailment and human text comprehension; and (3) the public release of annotations for these datasets using the proposed metrics, serving as a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** The application of both readability metrics and a refined set of prerequisite skills to six prominent RC datasets offers a valuable and detailed comparative analysis. This goes beyond typical dataset evaluations focusing solely on accuracy and provides a more granular understanding of dataset qualities. The tables presenting the results (Tables 2, 3, and 6) are well-organized and informative.
2. **Refined Skill Categorization:** The reorganization of prerequisite skills, informed by insights from textual entailment and psychology, is a notable contribution.  The attempt to ground the skill categorization in established theories, rather than relying solely on ad-hoc definitions, strengthens the framework. The inclusion of bridging and elaboration, inspired by human text comprehension, is particularly commendable.
3. **Publicly Available Annotations:** Making the annotated datasets publicly available is a significant contribution to the community. This resource will enable researchers to replicate the findings, conduct further analyses, and potentially use the annotations to improve both dataset construction and RC system development.  The provided sampling methods are helpful for understanding how the data was selected.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Conceptual Clarity:** The core goal of the paper remains somewhat ambiguous. It oscillates between investigating RC datasets for improved machine comprehension *and* offering insights into human reading comprehension. This conflation weakens the overall focus and makes it difficult to assess the significance of the findings. The title should be revised to explicitly state the paper’s focus on machine reading comprehension.
2. **Methodological Opacity & Annotation Process:** The description of the annotation process lacks sufficient detail. It is unclear precisely *how* datasets were annotated with the proposed “metrics.” What specific guidelines were given to annotators? Were they instructed to evaluate questions from a machine or human perspective?  Further clarification is needed regarding the inter-annotator agreement and how disagreements were resolved.
3. **Insufficient Engagement with Related Work:** The paper overlooks important prior work in the readability literature.  The discussion in Section 2.2, while theoretically interesting, feels somewhat tangential and doesn't directly contribute to the core argument. The authors should explicitly acknowledge and address relevant research on readability assessment (e.g., referencing DuBay 2004 to differentiate “readability of text” and “difficulty of reading content"). The omission of critical literature weakens the justification for the chosen metrics and the interpretation of results.
4. **Overly Ambitious Scope:** The paper attempts to address too many questions simultaneously, resulting in a diluted focus. The discussion covers dataset evaluation, skill categorization, and potential system development strategies.  Narrowing the scope to a more specific contribution (e.g., solely focusing on the skill categorization) would likely strengthen the paper. The claim that readability doesn’t affect question difficulty relies heavily on the specific readability features used (POS/dependency) and should be framed accordingly.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please provide more detailed instructions given to the annotators, including examples of how they were to interpret and apply the prerequisite skills?
2.  How were disagreements between annotators resolved during the annotation process?  What steps were taken to ensure consistency?
3.  Can you elaborate on how the chosen readability metrics specifically relate to the challenges faced by machine reading comprehension systems, as opposed to human readers?
4.  Given the claim that readability does not strongly correlate with question difficulty, how sensitive are these findings to the choice of readability metrics? Did you explore other readability measures?

**Additional Comments:**

The manuscript would benefit from thorough English proofreading. There are numerous instances of grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that detract from the clarity of the presentation. For example, when claiming examples demonstrate readability doesn’t correlate with question difficulty, specify that this is for *machine* comprehension.
