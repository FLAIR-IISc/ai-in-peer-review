- Paper proposes two metric classes (prerequisite skills and readability) to analyze RC datasets and finds weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty.
- Main contributions: skill-based categorization, annotated versions of six RC datasets, and empirical evidence reinforcing a nuanced view of readability vs. difficulty.
- Strength: Provides annotated resource and granular skill labels for six prominent RC datasets to aid comparative analysis and dataset design.
- Strength: Integrates NLP readability measures with psychological theories of human comprehension to motivate metric choices.
- Strength: Identifies distinctive characteristics of datasets (e.g., QA4MRE high skill demands; MCTest easier-to-read but still challenging).
- Strength: Empirical results argue that increasing text complexity alone does not necessarily raise machine RC difficulty.
- Weakness: Conceptual clarity is lacking; the paper conflates analysis for machine comprehension with models of human reading comprehension.
- Weakness: Inadequate engagement with the readability literature and insufficient justification for the chosen readability metrics.
- Weakness: Overambitious and scattered scope—attempts to cover dataset evaluation, skill taxonomy, human vs. machine comprehension, and dataset critique without sufficient focus.
- Weakness: Annotation methodology is under-described (number of labels per annotator, instructions, disagreement resolution) despite a reported 90.1% agreement.
- Weakness: Conclusions depend heavily on the specific feature set and skill choices; alternative features might yield different correlations.
- Question: Provide detailed annotation protocol—per-annotator labeling load, instructions (machine vs. human perspective), and disagreement resolution.
- Question: Justify selection of readability metrics and explain their relevance to machine comprehension challenges.
- Question: Clarify the primary scope: analysis for machine comprehension datasets or investigation of human readability vs. machine performance.
- Additional: Manuscript needs careful editing for grammar and clarity; title should explicitly target machine comprehension.
- Additional: Authors should address the distinction between "readability" and "difficulty of reading content" (e.g., DuBay 2004) and explicitly state when claims refer to machine comprehension.
