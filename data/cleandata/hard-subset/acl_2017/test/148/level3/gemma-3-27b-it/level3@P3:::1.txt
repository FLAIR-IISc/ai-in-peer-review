## Review

This paper attempts to connect text readability with machine reading comprehension (MRC) performance, analyzing six existing datasets through the lens of “prerequisite skills” and readability metrics. While the motivation for understanding dataset characteristics is valuable, this submission suffers from a significant lack of clarity and focus, ultimately weakening its contributions.

**Summary of the Paper:** The authors investigate the relationship between readability and question difficulty in six reading comprehension datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA). They annotate these datasets with metrics representing both prerequisite skills needed to answer questions and traditional readability measures.  Their primary finding is a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, suggesting that creating datasets that are easy to read but difficult to answer is possible.

**Main Contributions (as perceived):**

1. **Dataset Analysis & Annotation:** The most significant contribution is the annotated datasets themselves. Providing these annotations to the research community allows for further investigation into dataset properties and potentially informs the development of better datasets and models.
2. **Attempt to Formalize MRC Dataset Evaluation:** The effort to move beyond superficial dataset categories (question type, answer type) and introduce more nuanced evaluation metrics (prerequisite skills, readability) is a worthwhile endeavor, even if the execution is flawed.
3. **Observation of Weak Correlation:**  The empirical observation, albeit weakly correlated, that readability doesn't strongly dictate question difficulty for *machine* comprehension is a potentially interesting finding.



**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Motivation:** Understanding the strengths and weaknesses of existing RC datasets is crucial for advancing the field.  Identifying potential issues with dataset quality (as highlighted by Chen et al., 2016) is a valuable contribution.
*   **Broad Dataset Coverage:** Analyzing six datasets provides a reasonable basis for drawing broader conclusions about the relationship between readability and question difficulty.
*   **Publicly Available Data:** The commitment to making the annotated datasets publicly available is a major positive. This enables reproducibility and further research.
* **Novelty in Metric Combination:** Combining prerequisite skills analysis with readability metrics represents a potentially novel approach to evaluating RC datasets.



**Weaknesses:**

*   **Unclear Goal & Scope:** The paper’s central goal remains remarkably unclear even after multiple readings. Is the focus on understanding human readability, machine comprehension, or both? The constant shifting between perspectives is deeply confusing.
*   **Conflation of Human and Machine Reading Comprehension:** The authors repeatedly conflate human and machine reading comprehension. These are distinct areas with different underlying mechanisms, and treating them as interchangeable undermines the validity of the analysis. "Readability of text" (as cited in DuBay, 2004) specifically relates to human comprehension, and applying those metrics directly to assess *machine* comprehension is questionable.
*   **Missing Prior Work:**  The paper overlooks significant prior work in the readability literature, particularly beyond Vajjala & Meurers (2012).  A more thorough review of existing readability metrics and their limitations is necessary.
*   **Overly Ambitious Scope:** The paper attempts to answer too many questions simultaneously – dataset evaluation, skill categorization, and the relationship between readability and difficulty – which weakens the overall impact.
*   **Dubious Correlation Conclusion:** The conclusion that readability doesn’t affect QA difficulty is highly dependent on the specific features and methods used (POS/dependency features are mentioned).  The authors need to more clearly state that this conclusion applies to *machine* reading comprehension, not human readability.
*   **Section 2.2 Irrelevance:** Section 2.2 contains a largely unrelated discussion of human text comprehension models (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) that adds little value to the core argument. While referencing these works is appropriate for *context*, the level of detail is excessive and distracting.
*   **Annotation Methodology Concerns:** It’s unclear how the datasets were annotated with “metrics.”  How many questions did each annotator label? Were annotators specifically instructed to judge questions from a *machine* reading perspective, or were they asked to consider how *humans* would approach the task?




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Clarification of Focus:**  Can you explicitly state whether this work primarily targets machine reading comprehension or human reading comprehension?  If both, please delineate the specific contributions and analyses relevant to each.
2.  **Annotation Details:** Please provide detailed information about the annotation process. How many questions were annotated per dataset?  What specific instructions were given to annotators regarding the perspective they should adopt (human vs. machine)?
3.  **Justification for Metric Selection:**  Why were the specific readability metrics chosen (Figure 2)? How were these metrics adapted or justified for evaluating *machine* comprehension?
4. **Skill Definition & Inter-Annotator Agreement:** Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the criteria used for assigning prerequisite skills? Beyond the overall 90.1%, was there analysis of inter-annotator agreement *per skill* to identify areas of ambiguity?
5. **Impact of Annotation Disagreements:** How were disagreements among annotators resolved?  What impact do these discrepancies have on the reported results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The title should be revised to explicitly indicate that the work focuses on machine comprehension (e.g., "Evaluation Metrics for *Machine* Reading Comprehension...").
*   The manuscript requires careful English proofreading. Numerous grammatical errors and omissions detract from the clarity of the presentation.
*  When claiming examples demonstrate a lack of correlation, explicitly specify "for machine comprehension".
* Clarify whether the referenced skills/applications in Section 3.1 are for humans or machines. Why are human-focused citations used for machine claims?
* Table 6's meaning is unclear - clarify what "distance" represents.
