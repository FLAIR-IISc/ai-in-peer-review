## Review for ACL_2017 – Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability

This paper explores the relationship between text readability and question difficulty in reading comprehension (RC) datasets, proposing the use of two classes of metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – to analyze six existing datasets (MCTest, SQuAD, QA4MRE, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA). The authors annotate these datasets with their proposed metrics and present an analysis of the results, concluding that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution of this work lies in the attempt to provide a more nuanced analysis of RC datasets beyond simple accuracy metrics. Specifically, the authors’ effort to categorize prerequisite skills for answering questions, drawing from both textual entailment research and psychology of human text comprehension, is a valuable step towards better understanding the challenges inherent in these datasets. The publicly available annotated datasets represent a secondary contribution, providing a resource for future research. Finally, the observation that readability and difficulty are not strongly correlated, while not entirely novel, is reinforced by the empirical analysis presented.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Analysis and Resource Creation:** The annotation of six prominent RC datasets with both readability and prerequisite skill metrics provides a valuable resource for the community. This allows for a more detailed comparative analysis of datasets and can guide future dataset creation efforts. The granularity of the skill categorization is potentially useful for targeted model development.
2. **Integration of Multiple Perspectives:** The paper attempts to bridge the gap between NLP techniques for evaluating text and psychological models of human comprehension. Drawing from the work of McNamara and Magliano (2009) and others provides a theoretical foundation for the chosen metrics and highlights the importance of considering cognitive factors in RC.
3. **Identification of Dataset Characteristics:** The analysis reveals interesting characteristics of each dataset, such as the high prerequisite skill demands of QA4MRE and the relatively easier-to-read, yet still challenging, nature of MCTest. This kind of detailed profiling is useful for researchers selecting appropriate datasets for specific tasks.
4. **Empirical Support for a Nuanced View:** The findings, while not definitive, suggest that simply increasing text complexity does not necessarily lead to more challenging RC tasks for machines. This challenges the assumption that making datasets “harder” solely by using complex language is an effective approach to improving model robustness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Conceptual Clarity:** The central goal of the paper remains unclear. It oscillates between analyzing RC datasets from the perspective of machine comprehension and referencing models of *human* reading comprehension without consistently differentiating between the two. This conflation weakens the overall argument and leads to confusion.
2. **Insufficient Contextualization within Readability Literature:** While the authors leverage readability metrics, they fail to adequately engage with the extensive prior work in the field of readability assessment. Important work, like that of DuBay (2004), is missing, and the justification for selecting the specific metrics used (Figure 2) is not fully explained.
3. **Overambitious Scope & Scattered Focus:** The paper attempts to address too many issues simultaneously – dataset evaluation, skill categorization, human vs. machine comprehension, and a critique of existing datasets. This broad scope weakens the focus and diminishes the impact of the individual contributions. Section 2.2, in particular, feels largely unrelated to the core argument.
4. **Methodological Concerns Regarding Annotation:** The annotation procedure is described insufficiently. It is unclear how many questions each annotator labeled, and crucially, whether annotators were explicitly instructed to evaluate questions from a machine comprehension perspective, or to emulate human reasoning. The inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is reported, but details on disagreements and resolution strategies are missing.
5. **Dependence on Feature Selection & Metric Limitations:** The conclusion regarding the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is heavily reliant on the *specific* readability features and prerequisite skills chosen. The authors acknowledge this, but do not fully address the potential for different feature sets to yield different results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Annotation Protocol:** Please provide a detailed description of the annotation process, including the number of questions each annotator labeled, the specific instructions given to annotators regarding the perspective (machine vs. human), and how disagreements were resolved.
2. **Justification for Metric Selection:** Can you provide a more detailed rationale for the selection of the readability metrics presented in Figure 2, specifically addressing how these metrics relate to challenges faced by machine comprehension systems?
3. **Clarification of Scope:** Could you explicitly state whether the primary focus of this work is on analyzing RC datasets for machine comprehension tasks, or on investigating the relationship between human readability and machine performance?

**Additional Comments:**

The manuscript requires careful editing for grammatical errors and clarity. The title should be revised to explicitly indicate that the work targets machine comprehension (e.g., "Evaluating Reading Comprehension Datasets for Machine Learning: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"). The authors should also address the distinction between "readability of text" and "difficulty of reading content" as noted by DuBay (2004). Finally, when referencing examples (like Figure 1) to support the claim that readability doesn’t correlate with question difficulty, please specify that this observation applies to *machine* comprehension.
