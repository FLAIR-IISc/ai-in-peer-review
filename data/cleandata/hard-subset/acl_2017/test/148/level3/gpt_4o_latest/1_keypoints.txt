- Paper proposes two evaluation metric classes (prerequisite skills and readability) and annotates six RC datasets to study human readability vs. machine comprehension.
- Main finding: weak correlation between readability and question difficulty—texts easy for humans can yield hard questions for machines.
- Refines classification of prerequisite skills for RC using insights from psychology and textual entailment.
- Releases annotated datasets and analysis results to inform future RC dataset construction and system development.
- Strength: effectively bridges human readability and machine comprehension, offering a novel interdisciplinary perspective.
- Strength: comprehensive comparative analysis of six RC datasets, revealing dataset-specific insights (e.g., MCTest: easy-to-read but challenging-to-answer).
- Strength: reorganization of prerequisite skills could improve granularity in RC task evaluation.
- Weakness: primary objective is ambiguous and human vs. machine focus is conflated in title, abstract, and text.
- Weakness: omits key prior work on readability metrics, weakening theoretical grounding.
- Weakness: includes tangential theoretical discussions (Section 2.2) that dilute the paper’s focus.
- Weakness: annotation methodology lacks sufficient detail on annotator training and clarity about the machine-focused task.
- Weakness: paper attempts to address too many topics (readability, question difficulty, dataset quality, system development), resulting in insufficient depth.
- Question: clarify whether the paper’s primary goal targets machine comprehension, human readability, or both.
- Question: provide detailed annotator training procedures and fuller inter-annotator agreement reporting beyond the 90.1% mention.
- Question: explain how the claim that readability does not affect question difficulty accounts for answer-detection features and variability across RC models.
- Additional: requires substantial proofreading, a title revision to reflect machine-centric focus, consistent distinction between human and machine comprehension, and a narrower, focused scope.
