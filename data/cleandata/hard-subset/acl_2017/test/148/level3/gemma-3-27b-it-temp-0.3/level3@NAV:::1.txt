## Review: Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability

This paper attempts to connect text readability with machine reading comprehension (MRC) performance by analyzing six existing datasets through the lens of “prerequisite skills” and traditional readability metrics. While the intention to provide a more nuanced evaluation of RC datasets is commendable, the paper suffers from significant conceptual and presentational issues.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the application of a combined framework – incorporating both linguistic readability features and a skill-based assessment – to analyze existing RC datasets. The annotation of these datasets, while potentially valuable as a resource, is currently hampered by methodological ambiguities (see below). A secondary contribution lies in the reorganization of prerequisite skills, drawing from both textual entailment research and psychology. However, this reorganization doesn't appear to offer substantial novelty over existing work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Connecting Readability and MRC:** The core idea of investigating the relationship between text difficulty and question answering difficulty is a valuable one, particularly given the limitations of existing dataset evaluation methods.
*   **Dataset Annotation:** The annotation of six datasets with the proposed metrics, if properly documented and released, could be a useful resource for the community.
*   **Skill-Based Analysis:** The attempt to move beyond simple accuracy and consider the cognitive skills required for question answering is a positive step.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Unclear Goal:** The paper’s central aim remains frustratingly vague. It’s unclear whether the focus is on improving *machine* comprehension, understanding *human* reading processes, or both. This ambiguity permeates the entire paper.
*   **Conflation of Human and Machine Comprehension:** The paper repeatedly conflates human and machine reading comprehension, which are distinct areas with different underlying mechanisms. Citations from psychology are used to justify claims about machine performance without sufficient justification.
*   **Missing Prior Work:** The paper overlooks significant prior work in the readability literature, particularly regarding the nuances of readability beyond simple lexical and syntactic features (e.g., DuBay, 2004).
*   **Overambitious Scope:** The paper attempts to address too many issues simultaneously (dataset evaluation, skill categorization, readability analysis), weakening the overall impact.
*   **Methodological Concerns:** The annotation process lacks clarity. How were the datasets annotated with "metrics"? How many questions did each annotator label? Were annotators instructed to judge questions from a machine or human perspective?
*   **Problematic Conclusion:** The claim that readability doesn’t affect QA difficulty is heavily reliant on the specific features and methods used (POS/dependency features). This should be explicitly stated as a limitation.
*   **Section 2.2:** The discussion of the construction-integration model in Section 2.2 feels largely unrelated to the core argument and detracts from the paper’s focus.
*   **Editorial Issues:** The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and omissions, hindering readability.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you clearly articulate the primary goal of this work – is it to improve machine reading comprehension, understand human reading processes, or both?
2.  Please clarify the annotation process, including the number of questions annotated per dataset, the instructions given to annotators, and the inter-annotator agreement for each skill.
3.  How do you justify using psychological research to draw conclusions about machine reading comprehension?
4.  Can you provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of your findings, particularly regarding the dependence of your conclusions on the chosen features and methods?
