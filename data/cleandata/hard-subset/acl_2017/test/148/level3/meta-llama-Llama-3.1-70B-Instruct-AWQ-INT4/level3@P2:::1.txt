This paper presents a study on the evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors aim to investigate how the two difficulties of "answering questions" and "reading text" relate in RC.

The main contributions of this paper are: 

1. The adoption of two classes of evaluation metrics for RC datasets, which provides a more comprehensive understanding of the quality of datasets and the performance of systems.
2. The refinement of prerequisite skills for RC, specifically the reorganization of knowledge reasoning in terms of entailment phenomena and human text comprehension.
3. The annotation of six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics, which provides a valuable guide for the construction of new datasets and the development of RC systems.

The strengths of this paper lie in its attempt to connect text readability for humans with machine reading comprehension. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the RC datasets, highlighting their characteristics and correlations between the two classes of metrics.

However, there are several weaknesses and concerns with this paper. Firstly, the paper's goal is unclear, and the reviewer is still confused after multiple readings. The paper seems to conflate machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, which are distinct areas. The title should be clarified to target machine comprehension, not human reading comprehension.

Additionally, the paper tries to answer too many questions at once, weakening its contributions. The conclusion that readability does not affect QA difficulty depends on the features/methods used, and the paper should provide more robust evidence to support this claim.

Furthermore, the paper lacks clear distinctions and pointers differentiating the work from human-question readability studies. The authors should add clear explanations to differentiate their work from human-question readability studies.

The paper also requires editorial revisions, including English proofreading to address grammatical omissions and errors.

Questions to authors:

1. How did you annotate the datasets with the proposed metrics, and what was the instruction given to the annotators?
2. Can you clarify whether the referenced skills/applications are for humans or machines, and why human-focused citations are used for machine claims?
3. How many questions did the annotators label, and were they instructed to judge questions from a machine vs. human perspective?
4. Can you provide more robust evidence to support the conclusion that readability does not affect QA difficulty?
5. How do you plan to address the conflation of machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension in your future work?

Overall, this paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of RC, but it requires further clarification and revisions to strengthen its claims and contributions.
