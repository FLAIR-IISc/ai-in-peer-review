This paper presents an analysis of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors aim to investigate how the difficulties of "answering questions" and "reading text" relate in RC. The paper's main contribution lies in its attempt to connect text readability for humans with machine reading comprehension.

**Summary of the Paper**

The authors adopt two classes of metrics to evaluate RC datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. Prerequisite skills define the difficulty of comprehending the context to answer questions, while readability metrics describe the difficulty of reading contents in documents. The authors refine the prerequisite skills proposed by Sugawara et al. (2017) and Sugawara and Aizawa (2016) using insights from psychology research on human text comprehension. They also leverage a wide range of linguistic features proposed by Vajjala and Meurers (2012) to evaluate readability. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and present the results, highlighting the characteristics of the datasets and the correlation between the two classes of metrics.

**Contributions**

1. The authors adopt two classes of evaluation metrics to analyze RC datasets, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the datasets' characteristics.
2. They refine the prerequisite skills proposed by Sugawara et al. (2017) and Sugawara and Aizawa (2016) using insights from psychology research on human text comprehension.
3. They annotate six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics, making the results publicly available.

**Strengths**

1. The paper attempts to connect text readability for humans with machine reading comprehension, which is a novel approach in the field.
2. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of RC datasets using two classes of evaluation metrics.
3. The paper highlights the importance of considering both prerequisite skills and readability when evaluating RC datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper's goal is unclear, and the reviewer is still confused after multiple readings.
2. There is ambiguity whether the focus is machine comprehension or human text readability.
3. Important prior work in the readability literature is missing.
4. Section 2.2 contains unrelated theoretical discussion.
5. The paper tries to answer too many questions at once, weakening its contributions.
6. The conclusion that readability does not affect QA difficulty depends on the features/methods used (e.g., POS/dependency features).

**Questions to Authors**

1. How did you annotate the datasets with the proposed metrics? Were the annotators instructed to judge questions from a machine or human perspective?
2. Can you clarify how many questions annotators labeled and what was the inter-annotator agreement?
3. How do you differentiate your work from human-question readability studies? Can you provide clear distinctions and pointers?
4. Can you specify whether the referenced skills/applications are for humans or machines, and why human-focused citations are used for machine claims?

**Additional Comments**

1. The title should clarify that the work targets machine comprehension, not human reading comprehension.
2. The manuscript needs English proofreading; there are grammatical omissions and errors.
3. The paper conflates machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, which are distinct areas.
4. “Readability of text” is not equivalent to “difficulty of reading content” (cite DuBay 2004).
