This paper proposes a novel approach to evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets by introducing two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors aim to bridge the gap between human text readability and machine reading comprehension, which is a significant strength of the paper. However, several weaknesses and areas for improvement were identified.

The paper's goal is unclear, and the reviewer was left confused after multiple readings. The focus of the paper is also ambiguous, oscillating between machine comprehension and human text readability. Furthermore, the authors fail to acknowledge important prior work in the readability literature, which is a significant omission.

The paper attempts to answer too many questions at once, which weakens its contributions. The conclusion that readability does not affect question difficulty is dependent on the features and methods used, and the authors should have been more explicit about this limitation. Additionally, the title of the paper should be revised to clarify that the work targets machine comprehension, not human reading comprehension.

The manuscript requires English proofreading, as there are grammatical omissions and errors throughout the text. The authors should also provide more details about the annotation process, including how datasets were annotated with "metrics" and how many questions annotators labeled.

The paper conflates machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, which are distinct areas of research. The authors should have provided clearer distinctions and pointers to differentiate their work from human-question readability studies. Furthermore, the authors should have specified that their examples show readability does not correlate with question difficulty "for machine comprehension."

The annotation procedure is unclear, and the authors should have provided more information about how annotators were instructed to judge questions from a machine versus human perspective. The authors should also have clarified how many questions annotators labeled and what the inter-annotator agreement was for the entire dataset, not just a subset of questions.

The paper's main contributions are the adoption of two classes of evaluation metrics for RC datasets, the revision of the previous classification of prerequisite skills for RC, and the annotation of six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics. However, the authors should have prioritized these contributions and presented them in a clearer and more concise manner.

The authors' discussion of the results is informative, but it could be improved by providing more insights into the implications of their findings. The paper's conclusion is also somewhat vague, and the authors should have provided more specific recommendations for future work.

In terms of questions to the authors, the reviewer would like to ask:

* Can you provide more details about the annotation process, including how datasets were annotated with "metrics" and how many questions annotators labeled?
* How did you ensure that annotators were judging questions from a machine perspective, rather than a human perspective?
* Can you provide more information about the inter-annotator agreement for the entire dataset, not just a subset of questions?
* How do you plan to address the limitations of your study, including the dependence of your conclusions on the features and methods used?

Overall, this paper has some interesting ideas, but it requires significant revisions to clarify its goals, methods, and contributions. With further refinement, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of reading comprehension.
