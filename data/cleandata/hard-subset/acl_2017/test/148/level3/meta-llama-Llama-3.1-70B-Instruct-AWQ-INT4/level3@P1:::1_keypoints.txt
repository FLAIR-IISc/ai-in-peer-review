- Paper analyzes RC datasets using two metric classes: prerequisite skills and readability to relate question-answering difficulty and text reading difficulty.
- Authors refine prior prerequisite-skill taxonomy with insights from psychology and adopt linguistic features for readability evaluation.
- Six existing RC datasets are annotated with the proposed metrics and results are made publicly available.
- Contribution: combines prerequisite-skill and readability analyses to provide a broader dataset characterization.
- Contribution: refines Sugawara et al.’s prerequisite-skill definitions using human text-comprehension literature.
- Contribution: releases annotations for six RC datasets, enabling further analysis by the community.
- Strength: novel attempt to connect human text readability with machine reading-comprehension performance.
- Strength: comprehensive analysis using two distinct metric classes yields informative dataset characterizations.
- Strength: emphasizes the importance of evaluating both prerequisite skills and readability in RC datasets.
- Weakness: paper goal is unclear; reviewer remained confused after multiple readings.
- Weakness: ambiguity whether focus is on machine comprehension or human readability.
- Weakness: misses important prior work in the readability literature.
- Weakness: Section 2.2 contains theoretical discussion that appears unrelated to the main work.
- Weakness: attempts to answer too many questions at once, diluting the contribution.
- Weakness: conclusion that readability does not affect QA difficulty may depend on chosen features/methods, so is not definitive.
- Question: how were annotations performed—were annotators instructed to judge from a machine or human perspective?
- Question: how many questions were labeled and what was the inter-annotator agreement?
- Question: how does this work differ from prior human-question readability studies—please clarify distinctions and references?
- Question: specify whether referenced skills/applications target humans or machines, and justify human-focused citations for machine claims.
- Additional comment: title should clarify the target is machine comprehension rather than human reading comprehension.
- Additional comment: manuscript requires English proofreading to fix grammatical errors.
- Additional comment: paper conflates machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, which are distinct research areas.
- Additional comment: “readability of text” is not equivalent to “difficulty of reading content” (cite DuBay 2004).
