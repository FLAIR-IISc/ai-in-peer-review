### **Summary of the paper**
This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: "prerequisite skills" (representing the difficulty of answering a question) and "readability" (representing the difficulty of reading the text). The authors refine a set of 13 prerequisite skills based on prior work and theories from psychology. They apply this framework to analyze and annotate 100 questions from each of six popular RC datasets, including MCTest and SQuAD. The primary conclusion of this analysis is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, suggesting that it is possible to create datasets that are easy to read but hard for systems to answer. The authors plan to make their annotations publicly available.

### **Main Contributions**
1.  **A large-scale comparative analysis of six RC datasets.** The paper's main contribution is the systematic annotation and empirical evaluation of multiple diverse datasets using a unified framework. This provides valuable insights into the characteristics and relative difficulties of these benchmarks (e.g., showing SQuAD to be difficult-to-read but easy-to-answer, while MCTest is easy-to-read but comparatively difficult-to-answer).
2.  **A publicly available resource for RC dataset analysis.** The annotations of prerequisite skills for 600 questions across six datasets represent a useful resource for the community, enabling more fine-grained analysis of RC systems and informing the design of future datasets.

### **Strengths**
1.  **Addresses an important and under-explored problem.** The paper investigates the relationship between text complexity and question complexity in RC datasets. Understanding this relationship is crucial for building more robust systems and for creating better evaluation benchmarks. The attempt to connect human-centric readability concepts with the challenges of machine comprehension is a novel and worthwhile research direction.
2.  **Comprehensive empirical study.** The analysis covers six different and widely-used RC datasets, which vary in their domain, style, and creation methodology. This breadth allows for a compelling comparative analysis and strengthens the generalizability of the findings. The quantitative results presented in Tables 2-5 provide a useful at-a-glance comparison for researchers selecting datasets for their work.
3.  **Potential for practical application in system development.** The authors' suggestion to use these metrics for curriculum learning—training models progressively on datasets from easy-to-read/easy-to-answer to easy-to-read/difficult-to-answer—is a practical and interesting application of this work. The provided analysis could directly inform such a training strategy.

### **Weaknesses**
1.  **Fundamental lack of clarity and conceptual conflation.** The paper's most significant weakness is its consistent failure to distinguish between human and machine reading comprehension. The goal is unclear: is it to evaluate datasets for NLP systems or to analyze them from a human psycholinguistic perspective? This ambiguity permeates the paper, from the general title to the heavy reliance on psychological models of human cognition (Section 2.2) to justify a framework for evaluating machine systems. This conflation makes the paper's core argument confusing and difficult to evaluate. For instance, the introduction claims an example from MCTest is difficult because it requires "a combination of several skills," but these skills (e.g., coreference) are challenging in very different ways for humans versus current NLP models.
2.  **Vague and potentially flawed annotation methodology.** The validity of the paper's core findings rests on the annotation of "prerequisite skills," but the process is critically under-specified. It is not stated whether annotators were instructed to judge the skills required from a human's perspective or a machine's. This distinction is paramount; a task trivial for a human may be extremely difficult for a machine. Without this clarification, the resulting annotations are difficult to interpret and may not be a valid measure of *machine* difficulty. The paper should explicitly state the instructions given to annotators.
3.  **Oversimplified conclusion about readability and difficulty.** The paper makes the strong claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the difficulty of their questions." This conclusion is based on a specific, and mostly surface-level, set of readability metrics and a particular definition of "difficulty" (the number of required skills). This relationship might change significantly with more sophisticated syntactic readability features (e.g., derived from dependency parses) or a different operationalization of difficulty (e.g., based on the performance of state-of-the-art models). The paper also conflates the "readability of text" with the "difficulty of reading content," a distinction that is well-established in the readability literature (e.g., DuBay, 2004, *The Principles of Readability*).
4.  **Insufficient grounding in related literature.** While the paper cites work in psychology, it misses key prior work in the computational readability literature. Furthermore, the extensive discussion of the Construction-Integration model in Section 2.2 feels disconnected from the paper's application. A stronger justification is needed for why these human-centric theories are the right framework for analyzing challenges for computational systems. The manuscript would also benefit from significant English proofreading to fix grammatical errors and improve clarity.

### **Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please clarify the exact instructions provided to your annotators? Were they asked to identify the skills that a human reader would need, or the skills that a generic computational model would need to answer the question? How does this choice affect the interpretation of your findings about dataset difficulty?
2.  Your primary conclusion is that readability and question difficulty are only weakly correlated. How robust do you believe this finding is? How might it change if you were to incorporate more complex syntactic readability features or define question difficulty based on the empirical performance of current state-of-the-art RC models?
3.  Could you elaborate on the connection between the human comprehension theories in Section 2.2 and their applicability to machine reading comprehension? Why are psychological constructs like "bridging" vs. "elaboration" a more suitable classification for machine-required skills than a framework derived from analyzing existing NLP system capabilities and failures?
