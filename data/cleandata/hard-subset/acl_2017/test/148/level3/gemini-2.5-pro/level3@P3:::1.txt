### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: "prerequisite skills" needed to answer questions and "readability" of the context passages. The authors refine a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging) and adopt 10 standard readability metrics (e.g., sentence length, Coleman-Liau index). They annotate 100 questions from each of six major RC datasets (including MCTest, SQuAD, and MS MARCO) with the required skills and compute readability scores for the relevant context sentences. The central finding is a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the number of skills required to answer a question about it. This leads to the conclusion that easy-to-read passages can still form the basis for difficult questions, a desirable property for creating challenging RC datasets.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the novel attempt to jointly analyze machine reading comprehension (MRC) datasets using the distinct dimensions of text readability and question complexity (proxied by "prerequisite skills"). This conceptual framing, which separates the difficulty of processing the text from the difficulty of reasoning to find an answer, is a valuable lens for dataset creators and analysts.
2.  A secondary contribution is the public release of skill annotations for six diverse MRC datasets. This resource could enable further meta-analysis and comparative studies of the reasoning types required by popular benchmarks.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Valuable Research Direction:** The paper's core idea—decoupling text complexity from question complexity—is compelling and timely. As MRC models become more powerful, understanding the different facets of difficulty in our evaluation benchmarks is crucial. The paper provides a concrete framework for this, and the visualization in Figure 3, which positions datasets in a 2D space of skills vs. readability, effectively illustrates the concept and highlights interesting dataset characteristics (e.g., SQuAD is "hard-to-read, easy-to-answer," while MCTest is "easy-to-read, harder-to-answer").
2.  **Broad Empirical Scope:** The analysis spans six distinct RC datasets, covering a wide range of genres (narratives, Wikipedia, news) and creation methodologies (crowdsourced, automatically generated). Applying a unified annotation scheme across these datasets provides a valuable comparative snapshot of the current MRC landscape and lends credence to the observations about their individual strengths and weaknesses.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear Focus and Conflation of Human vs. Machine Comprehension:** The paper's primary weakness is its persistent ambiguity about its subject. It is difficult to discern whether the goal is to analyze human readability or machine comprehension. This lack of a clear focus leads to a critical conceptual flaw: the paper conflates cognitive processes in humans with computational requirements for machines.
    *   The theoretical foundation (Section 2.2) is drawn exclusively from the psychology of human reading (e.g., Kintsch's C-I model). However, the paper never justifies why models of human cognition are an appropriate framework for analyzing artificial neural networks, which operate on entirely different principles of pattern matching and vector manipulation.
    *   This conflation creates confusion throughout. For example, the "prerequisite skills" are presented without clarifying if they represent cognitive steps for a person or necessary modules for an algorithm. The discussion conflates "readability of text" with the broader concept of "difficulty of reading content," a distinction well-established in the readability literature (e.g., DuBay, 2004, *The Principles of Readability*). Readability formulas measure surface-level features, not the conceptual density that might challenge both humans and machines.

2.  **The Central Claim is Weakly Supported:** The main conclusion—that readability does not directly affect question difficulty—is fragile because it rests on narrow and questionable definitions of both "readability" and "difficulty."
    *   "Question difficulty" is defined as a simple count of manually annotated skills. This assumes that all skills are equally difficult and that their difficulty is additive, which is a strong and unsupported assumption. Is a question requiring "Bridging" and "Elaboration" truly twice as difficult as one only requiring "Coreference Resolution"? Without extrinsic validation (e.g., correlation with model error rates), this metric is not convincing.
    *   The "readability" metrics are limited to traditional, surface-level linguistic features. While standard, these may not capture the textual complexities that actually challenge modern MRC models (e.g., long-range dependencies, subtle paraphrasing, syntactic ambiguity). The observed lack of correlation might simply indicate that *these specific features* do not correlate with *this specific definition of difficulty*, rather than proving a general principle.

3.  **Scattered Contributions and Incomplete Literature Review:** The paper attempts to do too much: revise a skill taxonomy, propose an evaluation framework, annotate six datasets, and perform an analysis. As a result, each part feels underdeveloped. The revision of the skill taxonomy in Section 3.1 is not sufficiently motivated, and the literature review on readability is sparse, missing a significant body of work in computational readability that uses more sophisticated features and models beyond those in Vajjala and Meurers (2012). A more focused paper would have been substantially stronger.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  **Annotation Perspective:** During the annotation of "prerequisite skills," were the annotators instructed to judge the requirements from the perspective of an ideal human reader or from the perspective of a current NLP system? This distinction is critical, as a human might rely on "Elaboration" (world knowledge) for a question a machine could solve with simple lexical overlap.
2.  **Validation of Difficulty Metric:** Your proxy for question difficulty is the number of required skills. Have you performed any extrinsic validation to support this? For instance, does the average number of skills per question in a dataset negatively correlate with the performance of state-of-the-art MRC models on that dataset?
3.  **Theoretical Grounding:** Could you elaborate on the choice to ground your framework in psychological models of human comprehension (Section 2.2) when your object of study is machine comprehension datasets and systems? What is the explicit bridge between a theory like the C-I model and the function of a Transformer-based MRC model?

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Clarity of Purpose:** The paper would be significantly improved by stating its goal clearly and consistently. The title, "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension," is ambiguous. A more precise title, such as "Analyzing Machine Reading Comprehension Datasets via Text Readability and Question Complexity," would better frame the work for the intended audience.
*   **Specify Claims:** When making claims based on examples (e.g., Figure 1), please explicitly state that readability may not correlate with question difficulty *for machine comprehension systems*, as this is the domain of your investigation.
*   **English Proofreading:** The manuscript requires careful proofreading for grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., "our methodology was used to evaluate the understanding of contextual entailments in a broader sense for RC").
*   **Missing Literature:** The readability section would be strengthened by acknowledging and discussing more recent work on computational readability that leverages richer feature sets (e.g., syntactic parse features, discourse connectives).
