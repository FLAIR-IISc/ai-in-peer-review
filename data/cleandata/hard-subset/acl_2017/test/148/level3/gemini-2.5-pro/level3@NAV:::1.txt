**Summary of the Paper**
This paper analyzes six reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills to determine question difficulty and readability scores to assess text complexity. By annotating datasets with these metrics, the authors investigate the relationship between these two dimensions. The central claim is that text readability only weakly correlates with question difficulty for RC tasks, implying that an easy-to-read passage can still be part of a challenging question-answering task.

**Contributions**
The primary contribution of this work is its novel attempt to connect the well-studied field of human text readability with the difficulty of machine reading comprehension tasks. The effort to jointly analyze datasets along these two axes is an interesting direction that moves beyond standard performance metrics. A secondary contribution is the annotated dataset resource, though its value is contingent on crucial methodological clarifications.

**Strengths**
The paper’s main strength lies in its core idea. Investigating the interplay between linguistic features that make a text difficult for humans and the skills required for a machine to answer questions about it is a creative and potentially impactful research direction. If properly executed, this line of inquiry could inform better dataset design, facilitate more nuanced model evaluation, and potentially lead to curriculum learning strategies for training more robust RC models.

**Weaknesses**
1. **Fundamental Conceptual Confusion:** The paper's most significant weakness is its persistent conflation of machine and human reading comprehension, treating them as analogous concepts. This leads to an unclear research goal and problematic assumptions, such as equating "readability of text" with "difficulty of reading content" while ignoring key literature that distinguishes them (e.g., DuBay, 2004). This conceptual ambiguity undermines the credibility of the entire analysis.

2. **Opaque Methodology and Unsupported Claims:** Key methodological details are absent, making the results difficult to interpret. It is unclear how annotators were instructed to judge "prerequisite skills"—from a human cognitive perspective or from the perspective of a computational model. Consequently, the main conclusion that readability does not affect QA difficulty is not well-supported, as it heavily depends on the specific, human-oriented readability features used and may not hold for different model architectures or feature sets.

3. **Lack of Focus and Incomplete Literature Review:** The paper attempts to cover too much ground, from cognitive psychology (Sec 2.2) to skill taxonomies, which weakens its core contribution. The theoretical discussions often feel disconnected from the final analysis of machine systems. The work also omits important prior work in the readability literature. Finally, the manuscript requires significant proofreading for grammatical errors and omissions.

**Questions to Authors**
1. During the annotation process, were annotators instructed to evaluate "prerequisite skills" from the perspective of a human reader or a typical machine comprehension model?
2. How do you justify the direct application of readability metrics developed for human second-language learners to the evaluation of computational models, which operate on entirely different principles?
3. Could you explicitly add distinctions to differentiate your work from human-question readability studies? We also strongly suggest revising the title to clarify that the focus is on *machine* comprehension.
