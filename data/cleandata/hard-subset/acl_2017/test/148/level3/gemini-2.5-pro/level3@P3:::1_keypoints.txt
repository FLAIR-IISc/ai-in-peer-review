- Proposes a framework evaluating RC datasets along two axes: prerequisite skills required and passage readability.  
- Refines a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills and adopts 10 standard readability metrics.  
- Annotates 100 questions from each of six major RC datasets and computes readability scores for context sentences.  
- Central finding: weak correlation between text readability and number of skills required, implying easy-to-read passages can yield hard questions.  
- Contribution: novel joint analysis separating text readability from question complexity as a lens for dataset analysis.  
- Contribution: publicly releases skill annotations for six diverse MRC datasets.  
- Strength: compelling idea to decouple text-processing difficulty from reasoning difficulty, with effective 2D visualization of datasets.  
- Strength: broad empirical scope across six datasets spanning genres and creation methodologies.  
- Weakness: ambiguous focus—unclear whether analysis targets human readability or machine comprehension, leading to conceptual conflation.  
- Weakness: theoretical grounding relies on human cognition models (e.g., C-I model) without justification for application to neural MRC systems.  
- Weakness: "prerequisite skills" are not clearly specified as human cognitive steps or algorithmic requirements.  
- Weakness: question difficulty metric (count of skills) assumes equal/additive difficulty without extrinsic validation.  
- Weakness: readability metrics are surface-level and may miss complexities that challenge modern MRC models.  
- Weakness: paper attempts many contributions resulting in underdeveloped taxonomy revision and analysis.  
- Weakness: literature review on readability is incomplete, missing richer computational readability work.  
- Question: Were annotators instructed to judge skills from an ideal human reader's perspective or from a current NLP system's perspective?  
- Question: Is there extrinsic validation of the difficulty metric, e.g., correlation between skill count and model error rates?  
- Question: Why ground the framework in psychological models of human comprehension when studying machine systems—what is the explicit bridge?  
- Suggestion: Clarify the paper's goal and retitle to reflect focus on machine reading comprehension datasets.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly state that claims concern machine comprehension systems and validate claims accordingly.  
- Suggestion: Proofread for English and expand the readability literature review to include richer features (syntactic, discourse).
