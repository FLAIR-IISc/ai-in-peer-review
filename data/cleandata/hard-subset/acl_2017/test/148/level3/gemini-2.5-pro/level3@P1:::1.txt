### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two dimensions: the difficulty of answering questions (quantified by "prerequisite skills") and the difficulty of reading the context text (quantified by "readability" metrics). The authors refine a pre-existing set of prerequisite skills, select a set of established readability metrics, and apply this framework to annotate and analyze six popular RC datasets. The paper's main empirical finding is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, suggesting that it is possible to create datasets that are easy to read but hard for systems to answer (e.g., MCTest).

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel Analytical Framework:** The primary contribution is the proposal of a two-dimensional framework for analyzing RC datasets. This moves beyond simple accuracy-based evaluation and provides a valuable vocabulary for discussing dataset characteristics (e.g., "easy-to-read but hard-to-answer"). This conceptualization encourages a more nuanced understanding of the challenges posed by different datasets.
2.  **Cross-Dataset Annotation and Analysis:** The authors provide a systematic analysis of six diverse RC datasets using a unified annotation scheme. The resulting annotations, which are being made publicly available, are a valuable resource for researchers looking to diagnose model performance or select appropriate datasets for specific research goals.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Research Direction:** The paper addresses a crucial and often overlooked aspect of progress in machine reading comprehension: understanding the intrinsic properties of the datasets we use for evaluation. By attempting to disentangle text complexity from question complexity, the work provides a path toward more principled dataset design and more targeted model development. This is a significant step beyond simply chasing state-of-the-art numbers on leaderboards.

2.  **Valuable Empirical Findings:** The analysis yields several interesting insights into existing datasets. For example, the characterization of SQuAD as "difficult to read but easy to answer" and MCTest as its inverse ("easy to read but difficult to answer") is a compelling and useful observation. This kind of analysis can help explain why models that perform well on one dataset may not generalize well to another, and it provides a concrete basis for curriculum learning or transfer learning approaches, as the authors suggest.

3.  **Refinement of Prerequisite Skills:** The paper builds upon prior work by refining the set of prerequisite skills needed for RC. Grounding the reorganization of skills like knowledge reasoning in theories of textual entailment and human psychology (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration) adds a layer of theoretical rigor to the classification scheme, making it more robust and potentially more useful for future analyses.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Focus and Audience:** The paper's most significant weakness is a persistent ambiguity about whether its focus is on machine comprehension or human text readability. It heavily cites and discusses theories of human comprehension (e.g., the C–I model in Section 2.2) but then applies the derived framework to evaluate datasets intended for machines. The distinction between what makes a text difficult for a human versus a machine is fundamental and is largely unaddressed. This conflation weakens the motivation and makes the conclusions less impactful. For instance, the title itself, "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension," is ambiguous and would benefit from specifying "Machine Reading Comprehension."

2.  **Superficial Engagement with Readability Literature:** The paper's treatment of readability feels incomplete. It equates "difficulty of reading content" with "readability," a simplification that is contested in the readability literature (e.g., DuBay, 2004, *The Principles of Readability*). The chosen metrics are largely traditional, surface-level linguistic features (e.g., word length, sentence length). While these are standard, there is a large body of work on computational readability that uses more sophisticated features (e.g., syntactic parse features, discourse connectives) which are more likely to correlate with the challenges faced by modern NLP models. By omitting this prior work, the paper's conclusion about the weak correlation between readability and difficulty might be an artifact of the limited set of readability features used.

3.  **Inconsistent Theoretical Grounding:** While the use of psychology literature is a potential strength, its integration is not always clear. Section 2.2 provides a detailed overview of human comprehension models, but the paper does not convincingly argue why these human-centric models are the appropriate foundation for analyzing tasks for computational agents. Similarly, in Section 3.1, skills are motivated with citations from both computational linguistics (Dagan et al., 2013) and psychology (Clark, 1975) without a clear rationale for how these different perspectives are being reconciled for the purpose of evaluating *machine* systems.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The core of your methodology rests on the annotation of prerequisite skills. Could you clarify the instructions given to the annotators? Specifically, were they asked to assess the skills required from the perspective of an ideal human reader, or were they meant to consider the challenges a machine learning model might face? This distinction is critical, as a human might effortlessly resolve an ellipsis that would be very difficult for a current model.

2.  Your main conclusion is that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated. Given that the readability metrics used are primarily surface-level, how do you think this conclusion might change if you incorporated more sophisticated syntactic features (e.g., parse tree depth, rate of long-distance dependencies) that might better capture complexity for a machine reader?

3.  In Section 1, you state that your methodology "could not evaluate the competence of looking for sentences that need to be read." This seems like a key part of reading comprehension. Could you elaborate on why you chose to exclude this aspect and how its inclusion might change the characterization of datasets like SQuAD, where finding the answer span in a paragraph is a central part of the task?

### Additional Comments

The manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading to correct several grammatical errors and awkward phrasings.
