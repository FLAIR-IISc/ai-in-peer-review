- Paper studies RC dataset quality using a 13-skill prerequisite taxonomy and lexical/syntactic readability features, annotating six datasets and reporting weak correlations between readability and skill counts.
- Main contributions: multi-dataset audit with released annotations, revised skill taxonomy grounded in textual entailment/psycholinguistics, and empirical finding that human readability metrics weakly predict machine RC difficulty.
- Strength: Provides reusable cross-dataset annotations and artifacts, enabling benchmarking, curriculum design, and error analysis; reports overall inter-annotator agreement (~90% on a subset).
- Strength: Reframes RC difficulty as countable prerequisite skills; the bridging vs. elaboration split is theoretically meaningful.
- Strength: Empirical evidence that easy-to-read texts can be hard for machines (weak Pearson correlations and dataset-specific contrasts like SQuAD vs. MCTest).
- Strength: Offers actionable observations for dataset construction (pitfalls of automatic curation, genre effects, utility of meta-information).
- Weakness: Unclear scope and audience; conflates human-focused readability constructs with machine RC difficulty without consistently qualifying claims.
- Weakness: Paper attempts many goals (taxonomy, annotation, readability analysis, correlations, dataset guidance, distance analysis), diluting depth and operational focus.
- Weakness: Methodological ambiguity in annotation—insufficient detail on guidelines, per-skill agreement, calibration, perspective (human vs. machine), and adjudication; potential hindsight bias from showing answers/highlighted sentences.
- Weakness: Readability feature set is limited to surface human-focused measures; conclusions require broader features (discourse/cohesion), robustness checks, and sensitivity analyses.
- Weakness: Literature and positioning are incomplete—needs clearer distinction from human readability studies and RC difficulty estimation work, with more recent NLP methods cited.
- Question: Is the primary objective to characterize machine RC datasets or to relate human readability to machine difficulty—please restate and qualify all claims as “for machine RC” if applicable.
- Question: Were annotators judging skills from a machine or human perspective; please provide detailed guidelines, per-skill IAA, training materials, and adjudication protocol; define “nonsense.”
- Question: How is “number of required skills” computed when skills repeat or form chains; are skills equally weighted and were alternative aggregations explored?
- Question: Which tools computed POS/dependency/readability features; were features computed over answer sentences or full contexts; were passage length, number of sentences, or genre controlled in correlations?
- Question: Do correlations hold under other readability indices (e.g., SMOG, perplexity), with dataset fixed effects, or when excluding single-sentence questions?
- Suggestion: Retitle paper to explicitly target machine RC (e.g., include “Machine Reading Comprehension”).
- Suggestion: Qualify examples and conclusions with “for machine RC” and clearly separate human readability from machine difficulty.
- Suggestion: Streamline theoretical Section 2.2 to operationalized elements and move broader background to an appendix.
- Suggestion: Expand related work on readability (e.g., DuBay 2004) and RC difficulty estimation; clarify contribution relative to them.
- Suggestion: Provide English proofreading to fix grammatical errors and improve clarity.
