Summary
This paper analyzes six RC datasets using two families of dataset-level metrics: (a) prerequisite skills reflecting the types of inferences needed to answer questions, and (b) readability features reflecting human text difficulty. The study reports weak correlations between the two, arguing that RC datasets can be easy to read yet difficult to answer, and vice versa.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: A cross-dataset empirical analysis linking per-question skill requirements to per-question readability and releasing the annotations.
- Secondary: A refinement of a prerequisite-skill taxonomy by integrating entailment phenomena and notions from cognitive psychology.
- Tertiary: The empirical claim that readability weakly correlates with machine QA difficulty on the chosen datasets/features.

Strengths
- The paper addresses an important gap: how dataset text difficulty relates to machine QA difficulty. The two-axis view (skills vs. readability) is a useful lens for dataset diagnosis.
- The cross-dataset comparison (MCTest, SQuAD, QA4MRE, WDW, MS MARCO, NewsQA) is valuable and reveals nuanced differences (e.g., MCTest easy-to-read yet skillful).
- The attempt to ground the skill taxonomy in textual entailment and cognitive psychology increases interpretability and may help systematic dataset design.
- Public release of annotations can benefit future dataset construction and error analysis.

Weaknesses
- Scope and positioning are unclear: the paper conflates machine reading comprehension with human reading comprehension and uses human-focused theories/citations to support claims about machine performance without bridging steps. Section 2.2 reads theoretically interesting but insufficiently tied to measurable machine-RC outcomes.
- The central conclusion (“readability does not affect QA difficulty”) is method-dependent: it relies on a specific set of readability features (e.g., Vajjala & Meurers, traditional FK) and POS/dependency–style proxies. Stronger claims require ablations with discourse/cohesion, entity-grid, coreference/load measures, and modern LM-derived features.
- Important readability literature is missing or underused; in particular, “readability of text” ≠ “difficulty of reading content” (see DuBay, 2004). The paper should explicitly separate and operationalize these constructs.
- Goal ambiguity and overreach: the work tries to answer too many questions at once (dataset evaluation, taxonomy revision, system-development guidance), diluting the main contribution. Section 3.1/3.2 and 2.2 exacerbate confusion about whether “skills” and cited applications are for humans or machines.
- Methodological opacity: it remains unclear how “metrics” were annotated vs. computed (manual vs. automatic), how many questions each annotator labeled, and whether annotators judged difficulty from a machine or human perspective. Editorially, the manuscript needs English proofreading; the title should clarify that the target is machine RC.

Questions to Authors
- Clarify the primary target: Is the paper about machine RC difficulty? If so, please consistently specify “for machine comprehension” when asserting lack of correlation (including examples).
- Detail the annotation protocol: number of items per annotator, instructions (human vs. machine perspective), and full IAA over all skills. How were readability metrics computed per question (on selected sentences only)? Why mix FK with the Vajjala-Meurers features?
- Provide ablations with richer readability/complexity features (e.g., discourse relations, entity grids, coreference chains) to test robustness of the main claim.
- Distinguish your work from human-question readability studies and add appropriate citations (e.g., DuBay, 2004), clarifying construct boundaries.
