Summary
This paper proposes two classes of metrics for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (i) prerequisite skills (a refined taxonomy inspired by psychology and textual entailment) and (ii) readability (a set of surface, lexical, and syntactic features largely drawn from Vajjala & Meurers, 2012). The authors annotate six datasets with the skill taxonomy, compute readability features on the sentences deemed necessary to answer each question, and report weak correlations between readability and the number of prerequisite skills. They argue that RC datasets can be easy to read yet difficult to answer and vice versa.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) Empirical cross-dataset analysis showing only weak correlation between text readability and the number of prerequisite skills needed to answer questions in machine RC datasets.
2) Release of human annotations indicating which prerequisite skills are required per question across six datasets.
3) A reorganized taxonomy of prerequisite skills, integrating distinctions from psychology (bridging vs elaboration) and textual entailment.

Strengths
- Timely attempt to connect human readability concepts with machine reading comprehension. The paper addresses an under-explored question: how surface/lexical/syntactic readability relates to the difficulty of answering RC questions with machines.
- Cross-dataset perspective with unified annotation. The same skill taxonomy is applied to six popular datasets, enabling interesting contrasts (e.g., MCTest as easy-to-read but relatively skill-demanding; QA4MRE as difficult on both axes). Tables 2–3 and Figures 3–4 provide useful signals for dataset curation and benchmarking.
- Skill taxonomy consolidation. Organizing “knowledge reasoning” via bridging vs elaboration, along with a clearer separation of clause-level skills, is a constructive step toward more diagnostic analyses of RC datasets.
- Practical insight for dataset design. The empirical finding that readability and machine QA difficulty are weakly correlated, and that one can construct easy-to-read yet hard-to-answer datasets, is valuable for future RC dataset construction and curriculum design.

Weaknesses
- Unclear goal and conflation of domains. The paper oscillates between human reading comprehension and machine comprehension without clearly committing to the latter. Readability features and psychology citations are human-focused, yet the claims and datasets are for machine RC. This ambiguity affects the framing, methodology, and interpretability of results.
- Ambiguity about what “readability” means vs “difficulty of reading content.” Readability (surface/lexical/syntactic ease) is not equivalent to the difficulty of comprehending content, especially for machines; see DuBay (2004) on readability formulas vs comprehension. The conclusion “readability does not affect question difficulty” should be restricted to machine comprehension and to the specific features chosen.
- Missing important readability prior work and rationale for feature choice. The paper relies mainly on the top-10 features from Vajjala & Meurers (2012) and the Coleman–Liau index, but omits broader readability literature (e.g., DuBay 2004; other classical indices; discourse-based quality features like Pitler & Nenkova 2008 which the authors note but cannot use). This narrows the validity of the correlation analysis.
- Section 2.2 feels tangential. The construction–integration model overview is lengthy and not tightly linked to the operationalization for machine RC. It reads as an unrelated theoretical detour; the mapping from these human cognition concepts to machine-focused metrics and claims is not made concrete.
- Methodological clarity. It remains unclear what is “annotated with metrics”: prerequisite skills are human-labeled, but readability “metrics” are computed. The annotation instructions do not specify whether annotators judged “difficulty” from a machine or human perspective, and the paper mixes claims about human and machine difficulty (e.g., examples in Fig. 1). The use of human-focused citations in Section 3.1 to justify machine claims exacerbates the ambiguity.
- Scope creep weakens the contribution. The paper attempts to (i) propose a new taxonomy, (ii) annotate six datasets, (iii) evaluate readability, (iv) analyze correlations, and (v) recommend system-development curricula. This breadth comes at the expense of a clearly supported central claim and rigorous validation.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Scope/goal: Is the main research question about machine reading comprehension only? If so, please revise the framing and claims to explicitly say “for machine comprehension,” including in the abstract, introduction, and conclusions.
- Readability operationalization: Why were these exact readability features chosen? Did you try alternatives (e.g., other classical readability formulas, lexical sophistication, discourse features) or ablations? Are results robust to feature sets?
- Correlations: Did you control for confounders such as context length, sentence number, and genre? Were statistical significance tests applied to correlation coefficients and cross-dataset differences?
- Annotation protocol: Please clarify (a) per-dataset counts per annotator, (b) whether annotators labeled all 100 questions independently, (c) guidelines/examples provided, (d) whether annotators were instructed to judge from a machine vs a human perspective, and (e) agreement per skill category (not only overall).
- Skill taxonomy validity: How were “bridging” vs “elaboration” distinguished in practice during annotation? Provide concrete boundary examples and an error analysis.
- “Nonsense” labeling: How was solvability defined? Could annotators mark a question solvable with external knowledge but not with the provided context? How did you adjudicate disagreements?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Make the conceptual distinctions explicit early: machine RC vs human comprehension; readability (surface/lexico-syntactic ease) vs content difficulty vs QA difficulty. Cite DuBay (2004, The Principles of Readability) and related readability literature to support this separation.
- When drawing conclusions (e.g., “readability does not directly affect the question difficulty”), consistently qualify “for machine comprehension with the chosen readability features and our skill annotations.”
- Section 2.2: tighten substantially and focus on the parts directly operationalized; move background to related work or shorten.
- Section 3.1: Clarify whether the skills are intended as human-inspired labels for machine RC difficulty, and justify using human-focused theories to draw machine RC conclusions. Consider adding machine-specific evidence or references.
- Title: Revise to emphasize machine RC (e.g., “Evaluation Metrics for Machine Reading Comprehension Datasets: Prerequisite Skills and Text Readability”).
- Writing: The manuscript needs English proofreading; there are grammatical omissions, agreement errors, and typos throughout (e.g., article usage, subject–verb agreement, punctuation).
- Distinguish this work from human-question readability studies and cite relevant prior work; add pointers to where your approach diverges.
- In Section 3.1 and 3.2, clarify “annotated with metrics” vs “computed features,” and precisely describe the pipeline (sentence selection, feature computation on selected sentences, normalization).
- When using examples to argue non-correlation of readability and difficulty, explicitly say “for machine comprehension.”

Overall, the dataset-level analysis and public annotations are useful, but the paper needs clearer scope, stronger methodological grounding for machine RC, broader readability coverage, and tighter writing to support its central claims.
