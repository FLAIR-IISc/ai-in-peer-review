- Summary: Proposes two metric classes (prerequisite-skill taxonomy and readability features), annotates six RC datasets, finds weak correlation between readability and required skills, and argues readability and question difficulty can be decoupled.

- Contribution 1: Empirical cross-dataset analysis showing weak correlation between text readability and number of prerequisite skills for RC questions.

- Contribution 2: Release of human annotations marking prerequisite skills per question across six datasets.

- Contribution 3: Reorganized prerequisite-skill taxonomy integrating psychology distinctions (bridging vs elaboration) and textual entailment.

- Strength: Timely attempt to link human readability concepts with machine RC difficulty, addressing an under-explored question.

- Strength: Cross-dataset unified annotation enables useful contrasts and benchmarking signals (e.g., MCTest vs QA4MRE).

- Strength: Skill taxonomy consolidation clarifies knowledge-reasoning distinctions and clause-level skills.

- Strength: Practical insight for dataset design—readability and machine QA difficulty are weakly correlated, allowing construction of easy-to-read but hard-to-answer datasets.

- Weakness: Unclear goal and domain conflation—paper mixes human reading comprehension theory with machine RC claims without committing to machine-focused framing.

- Weakness: Ambiguity between “readability” (surface/lexico-syntactic ease) and actual content or QA difficulty; conclusions need restriction to machine comprehension and chosen features.

- Weakness: Incomplete coverage of readability literature and weak rationale for chosen feature set (omits DuBay 2004, classical indices, discourse features).

- Weakness: Section 2.2 (construction–integration model) is lengthy and not clearly operationalized for machine RC.

- Weakness: Methodological clarity issues—unclear what is human-annotated vs computed, whether annotators judged machine or human difficulty, and mixed human/machine claims.

- Weakness: Scope creep—attempts taxonomy design, annotation, readability evaluation, correlation analysis, and curriculum recommendations, diluting a clear central claim and validation.

- Question: Scope/goal—Is the research question explicitly about machine reading comprehension? Revise framing if so.

- Question: Readability features—Why these features; were alternatives or ablations tried; are results robust to feature sets?

- Question: Correlations—Were confounders (context length, sentence count, genre) controlled and statistical significance tested?

- Question: Annotation protocol—Provide per-dataset annotator counts, whether annotators labeled all items independently, guidelines/examples, machine vs human judgment instructions, and per-skill agreement.

- Question: Taxonomy validity—How were bridging vs elaboration distinguished in practice; provide boundary examples and error analysis.

- Question: “Nonsense” labeling—How was solvability defined; could annotators mark solvable only with external knowledge; how were disagreements adjudicated?

- Suggestion: Make machine RC vs human comprehension and readability vs content/QA difficulty distinctions explicit early and cite DuBay (2004).

- Suggestion: Qualify conclusions to “for machine comprehension with the chosen readability features and skill annotations.”

- Suggestion: Tighten Section 2.2 and move peripheral background to related work.

- Suggestion: Justify use of human-focused theories for machine RC claims or add machine-specific evidence/references.

- Suggestion: Revise title to emphasize machine RC, improve English proofreading, and distinguish from human-question readability studies.

- Suggestion: Clarify pipeline—what is annotated vs computed, sentence selection, feature computation, and normalization.

- Overall: Dataset-level analysis and released annotations are useful, but the paper needs clearer scope, stronger methodological grounding for machine RC, broader readability coverage, and tighter writing to support its claims.
