Summary and contributions
This paper studies how human-oriented text readability relates to the difficulty of answering questions in machine reading comprehension (RC) datasets. It introduces two evaluation classes: (i) prerequisite skills required to answer a question (a refined taxonomy extending Sugawara et al., 2017), and (ii) readability features adapted from prior work. The authors annotate six RC datasets with the skill taxonomy and compute readability features on the sentences deemed necessary to answer.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical, cross-dataset annotation-based analysis showing a weak correlation between readability metrics and the number of prerequisite skills required; the finding that datasets can be easy to read yet require non-trivial skills (e.g., MCTest).
2) A refinement and reorganization of a prerequisite-skill taxonomy for RC, informed by textual entailment and cognitive psychology distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration, etc.).
3) A publicly released set of annotations (if indeed released), enabling comparative diagnostic analyses of RC datasets.

Strengths
- Cross-dataset analysis with consistent methodology: Annotating six widely used datasets with a unified skill taxonomy and applying the same readability features yields informative, comparable profiles (e.g., MCTest vs. SQuAD vs. QA4MRE). The inter-annotator agreement (reported as 90.1% on a sample) suggests the scheme is learnable.
- Useful diagnostic insight: The observation that easy-to-read passages (MCTest) can still require multiple skills and multi-sentence reasoning is valuable for dataset construction and curriculum design. The plots contrasting Flesch–Kincaid vs. skill counts are informative.
- Taxonomy refinement: Organizing “knowledge reasoning” into bridging vs. elaboration and clarifying other skills (e.g., object tracking, logical reasoning, ellipsis) helps make skill labels more grounded and interoperable with entailment research.
- Practical implications for dataset curation: The analysis of “nonsense” questions and the impact of sourcing (e.g., Who-did-What, MS MARCO vs. NewsQA’s meta flags) offers concrete guidance for building higher-quality RC datasets.

Weaknesses
- Unclear scope and goal: The paper oscillates between human readability and machine comprehension, leaving the central question ambiguous. Is the aim to predict machine difficulty from human readability features, to propose a human-centric taxonomy for machine diagnostics, or both? The main claim (“readability does not directly affect question difficulty”) needs to be stated explicitly as “for machine comprehension” throughout.
- Conflation of constructs: “Readability of text” (as operationalized by surface lexical/syntactic features) is not equivalent to “difficulty of reading content” or “machine difficulty.” Classic readability critiques (e.g., DuBay, 2004) emphasize this distinction. The conclusions heavily depend on the chosen readability proxies (Vajjala & Meurers features and Flesch–Kincaid); alternate discourse or cohesion features (e.g., entity grid, discourse connectives, RST) could lead to different results.
- Related work coverage: Important readability literature is missing or only briefly acknowledged. Beyond Flesch–Kincaid and Vajjala & Meurers, there is extensive work on readability, cohesion, and comprehension difficulty that should be discussed and contrasted, including measures more aligned with discourse coherence and text quality (e.g., Pitler & Nenkova is cited but not leveraged due to annotation gaps).
- Methodology and annotation clarity: “Annotated with metrics” is vague. Details are needed: unit of agreement (per-label vs. per-question), adjudication, instructions to annotators (human vs. machine perspective), examples and calibration, how “nonsense” was operationalized, whether labels were multi-hot and how disagreements were resolved, and whether the analysis used union/majority labels. Reporting IAA on only 62 questions is limited; please expand and specify the statistic (κ/F1/percentage).
- Overreach and focus: Section 2.2’s theoretical discussion (C–I model, inference dichotomies) feels disconnected from the empirical analysis and inflates scope. The paper attempts to address dataset quality, skill taxonomy, readability, and implications for system development; narrowing the focus would strengthen the contribution and claims.
- Causal inference and confounds: The correlation analyses do not control for obvious confounds (passage length, number of sentences selected, genre, sampling/filtering choices). The finding of weak correlation is descriptive; claims about “does not directly affect” should be tempered, or analyses expanded with controls and robustness checks.

Questions to authors
- Goal and audience: Is the primary target machine RC difficulty prediction/diagnosis? If so, please reframe claims and title accordingly and explicitly separate human readability constructs from machine difficulty constructs.
- Readability features: Which exact features were used in the correlation (only Flesch–Kincaid or the full set)? Did you test discourse/cohesion features or entity-based models? How robust are correlations across feature subsets?
- Annotation protocol: How many annotators per question? What instructions did they receive about judging from a machine vs. human perspective? What was the unit/statistic of IAA? How were disagreements resolved? Did annotators receive gold examples or training?
- “Nonsense” definition: How was “nonsense” operationalized? Could “answerable via world knowledge without the passage” be flagged separately from “unsolvable”?
- Sampling and controls: How sensitive are the findings to paragraph length, number of selected sentences, genre, or dataset-specific filters (e.g., NewsQA)? Can you report partial correlations controlling for these?
- Release: Will the full annotation set and guidelines be released? In what format?

Additional comments
- Title: Consider “Evaluation Metrics for Machine Reading Comprehension Datasets: Prerequisite Skills and Readability” to avoid confusion with human reading comprehension research.
- Wording: When contrasting examples, say “readability for humans” vs. “question difficulty for machine comprehension.”
- Sectioning: Tighten or move parts of Section 2.2 to appendix and explicitly connect each theoretical element to a concrete annotation decision.
- Distinctions: Add a subsection clarifying the differences among human readability, human comprehension difficulty, and machine RC difficulty, with citations (e.g., DuBay, 2004).
- Proofreading: The manuscript would benefit from English editing (articles, agreement, punctuation).
- Future analyses: Consider correlating skill counts with actual system performance on the same items, which would strengthen the practical value of the taxonomy.
