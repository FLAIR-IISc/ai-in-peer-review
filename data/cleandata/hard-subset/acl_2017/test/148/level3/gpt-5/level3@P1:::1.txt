Summary
This paper analyzes the quality of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of evaluation metrics: (i) prerequisite skills required to answer a question (a refined taxonomy of 13 skills, including bridging, elaboration, ellipsis, coreference, logical/mathematical reasoning, etc.) and (ii) readability features (a set of lexical/syntactic indices inspired by prior NLP readability work). The authors annotate six datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), compute skill frequencies and the number of skills required per question, estimate readability on sentences deemed necessary to answer, and study correlations between the two classes. They report weak correlations between readability and skill counts, concluding that it is possible to build datasets that are easy to read yet difficult to answer (e.g., MCTest).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A multi-dataset empirical audit of RC datasets via a prerequisite-skill taxonomy and readability indicators, with public release of annotations.
2) A revised skill taxonomy for RC grounded in textual entailment and psycholinguistics (notably distinguishing bridging vs. elaboration).
3) An empirical finding that human readability metrics weakly correlate with machine RC difficulty (measured as number of skills).

Strengths
- Cross-dataset diagnostic analysis with concrete, reusable artifacts: Annotating six well-known RC datasets with a consistent skill framework and releasing the results is practically valuable. It enables targeted benchmarking, curriculum design, and error analyses. The study reports inter-annotator agreement (~90% on a subset), giving some evidence of annotation reliability.
- Useful reframing of “difficulty” for RC: Counting prerequisite skills needed per question is an interpretable proxy for machine difficulty and aligns with prior observations (more skills → harder). The taxonomy’s explicit split of knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration is meaningful and grounded in TE and cognitive literature.
- Empirical evidence that “easy-to-read” ≠ “easy-to-answer” for machines: The weak correlations (e.g., Pearson 0.025–0.416 with Flesch–Kincaid and other features) and dataset-specific contrasts (SQuAD: hard to read, relatively easy to answer; MCTest: easy to read, comparatively hard to answer) are informative for dataset design and evaluation.
- Broad, actionable observations for dataset construction: The paper highlights pitfalls of automatic curation (nonsense questions), the role of genre (narratives vs. expository texts), and the utility of meta-information (e.g., NewsQA’s bad-question flags), which can shape future dataset practices.

Weaknesses
- Unclear scope and audience; conflation of human vs. machine constructs: The paper oscillates between human text comprehension theory and machine RC without consistently specifying which claims pertain to which. For example, “readability of text” (human-focused) is treated as “difficulty of reading content” for machines; these are not equivalent (cf. DuBay 2004). When stating that readability does not affect question difficulty, it should be qualified as “for machine RC under the chosen feature set.”
- Goal and contribution focus are diffuse: The work attempts to (i) propose a taxonomy, (ii) annotate six datasets, (iii) analyze readability, (iv) measure correlations, (v) discuss dataset/system development, and (vi) analyze sentence distance. The breadth dilutes depth. Section 2.2’s lengthy theoretical discussion feels only partly utilized and could be streamlined to what is operationalized.
- Methodological clarity and validity of “annotating metrics”: It remains ambiguous how annotators operationalized each skill (guidelines, examples, calibration), whether they labeled from a human or machine perspective, and how disagreements (including “nonsense”) were resolved. Reporting only overall agreement on 62 items is insufficient; per-skill agreement and adjudication procedures are needed. Showing question, answer, and highlighted sentences risks hindsight bias and ignores retrieval/answer selection competencies (acknowledged, but the implications for conclusions should be expanded).
- Readability feature set and inference overreach: The central conclusion (weak correlation) depends on the particular feature selection (largely surface lexical/syntactic measures). The paper acknowledges that these were designed for human readability and may not reflect machine-readability. Stronger claims require (a) a broader feature set (discourse, cohesion, narrative structure), (b) robustness checks (partial correlations controlling for passage length and dataset), and (c) sensitivity analyses across alternative readability models.
- Literature coverage and positioning: Important readability scholarship and modern NLP approaches beyond the cited works are underrepresented. The paper should more clearly distinguish itself from human readability prediction studies and from RC difficulty estimation works, and justify importing human inference taxonomies to machine settings.

Questions to authors
- Scope and claims: Is the paper’s primary objective to characterize machine RC datasets, or to relate human readability to machine difficulty? Please restate the main research question and ensure that all claims (including in the abstract and conclusion) explicitly say “for machine RC.”
- Annotation perspective and reliability: Were annotators instructed to judge prerequisite skills from a machine’s perspective or a human’s? Please provide detailed guidelines, training materials, per-skill inter-annotator agreement, and the adjudication protocol. How were “nonsense” cases decided?
- Skill counting: How is the “number of required skills” computed when multiple instances of the same skill occur? Are skills weighted equally? Did you explore alternative aggregations (e.g., maximum cognitive layer, presence of reasoning chains)?
- Readability pipeline: Which tool(s) produced POS/dependency/feature counts? Were features computed only over sentences deemed necessary, or over entire contexts? Did you control for passage length, number of required sentences, or genre when computing correlations?
- Robustness: Do the correlations hold when (a) using other readability indices (e.g., FKGL vs. SMOG vs. language-model perplexity), (b) controlling for dataset as a fixed effect, and (c) excluding questions requiring only a single sentence?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Retitle to make the target explicit (e.g., “Evaluating Machine Reading Comprehension Datasets via Prerequisite Skills and Human Readability Features”).
- When presenting examples and conclusions, qualify statements with “for machine RC,” and distinguish “human readability” from “machine difficulty.”
- Streamline Section 2.2 to the theoretical elements that are directly operationalized; move broader background to an appendix.
- Expand related work on readability (e.g., surveys like DuBay 2004) and on RC difficulty estimation; position your contribution relative to them.
- English proofreading is needed to fix grammatical errors and improve clarity.
