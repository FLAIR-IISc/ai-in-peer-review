- Paper proposes two metric classes for RC datasets—prerequisite skills (question difficulty) and readability (text difficulty)—applied to six datasets and reports weak correlation between them.

- Contribution: annotated six RC datasets with prerequisite-skills labels, creating a community resource.

- Contribution: offers a revised classification of prerequisite skills for knowledge reasoning (bridging vs. elaboration) grounded in textual entailment and psychological research.

- Strength: dataset annotations are a useful reference for researchers analyzing RC datasets.

- Strength: observes that linguistic readability features do not strongly correlate with question difficulty, a potentially important insight for dataset design.

- Strength: the revised skill classification meaningfully improves prior work on knowledge-reasoning skills.

- Weakness: conceptual confusion—paper fails to distinguish human text comprehension from machine reading comprehension, undermining its claims.

- Weakness: omits and inadequately engages with key prior work on readability for machine comprehension (e.g., Pitler & Nenkova; Vajjala & Meurers).

- Weakness: annotation methodology is ambiguous—unclear sample sizes, annotator perspective (human vs. machine), definition/use of “nonsense,” and dispute resolution despite reported 90.1% agreement.

- Weakness: conclusions are overstated because they rely on a limited set of readability features (e.g., Flesch-Kincaid) and do not test alternative metrics or consider machine-specific readability needs.

- Weakness: paper attempts too many goals (dataset evaluation, metric development, system implications) and lacks a focused core contribution.

- Question: clarify whether metrics target human comprehension, machine comprehension, or both, and justify using human readability measures if aimed at machines.

- Question: explain and justify the theoretical link between psychological research on human comprehension and machine RC, citing supporting work.

- Question: provide full annotation details—how many questions per dataset, number of annotators, instructions (perspective), and how disagreements were resolved.

- Question: clarify how examples like MCTest (easy-to-read for children) relate to machine comprehension and distinguish human readability from machine requirements.

- Question: justify use of Flesch-Kincaid as the readability metric, and report whether other readability measures were tested or might better correlate with machine comprehension.
