- Paper studies relationship between text readability and question difficulty in RC datasets, proposing two metrics: prerequisite skills and readability.
- Authors claim readability does not directly affect question difficulty and that datasets can be easy-to-read but hard-to-answer.
- Main contribution: annotated six RC datasets with the proposed metrics, creating a useful resource.
- Attempts to refine prerequisite-skill classification using insights from textual entailment and human comprehension research.
- Goal is unclear and the manuscript frequently conflates human reading comprehension with machine RC.
- Title and abstract fail to specify the focus on machine comprehension, creating fundamental ambiguity.
- Misses key prior readability work (e.g., DuBay 2004) and includes irrelevant human-psychology discussion with weak connection to machine RC.
- The central claim depends on specific linguistic features (POS/dependency) and is not shown to be generalizable.
- Annotation methodology is unclear: annotator instructions, stance (machine vs. human), and identification of "nonsense" questions are not described.
- Paper attempts to address too many questions at once, weakening its core contribution and analysis.
- Section 5 conflates human and machine perspectives and misapplies human-focused studies to machine claims.
- The distinction between "readability of text" and "difficulty of reading content" is not properly addressed.
- Manuscript requires substantial English editing.
- Recommendation: reject due to conceptual confusion and methodological flaws.
- Suggested fixes: clearly separate human vs. machine comprehension, state machine focus in claims/title, clarify annotation protocol, and incorporate key readability literature.
- Questions for authors: How is "readability" defined for machine vs. human comprehension? Which readability features were used and why? How did annotator perspective affect annotations?
