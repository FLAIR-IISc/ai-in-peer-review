

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes two classes of metrics for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills (measuring question difficulty) and readability (measuring text difficulty). The authors apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) and claim there is only a weak correlation between the two metrics. 

As I see them, the main contributions are: 
1. The annotation of six RC datasets with prerequisite skills metrics, providing a valuable resource for the community.
2. A revised classification of prerequisite skills for RC, particularly for knowledge reasoning, based on textual entailment and psychological research.

However, the paper's primary contribution is significantly weakened by fundamental conceptual and methodological issues that prevent it from delivering a clear, actionable contribution.

## Strengths
1. The annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics provides a useful reference for the community, particularly for researchers interested in dataset analysis.
2. The paper makes a potentially valuable observation that readability (as measured by linguistic features) does not strongly correlate with question difficulty in RC datasets, which could inform future dataset construction.
3. The revised classification of prerequisite skills for knowledge reasoning (bridging vs. elaboration) represents a meaningful improvement over previous work in the area.

## Weaknesses
1. **Unclear conceptual focus**: The paper fails to clearly distinguish between human text comprehension and machine reading comprehension. It conflates these two distinct areas without justification, making the contribution ambiguous. The title and abstract claim to address "reading comprehension" without clarifying whether this is for humans or machines. The paper's methodology is framed as evaluating "RC datasets" but relies heavily on human comprehension theories without establishing the relevance to machine systems. This conceptual confusion is the most severe weakness and undermines the entire contribution.

2. **Missing critical literature**: The paper fails to cite key prior work in readability assessment for machine comprehension (e.g., works by Vajjala & Meurers, 2012, which the authors themselves cite but don't properly engage with in the context of machine comprehension). It also omits important work on readability metrics specifically for machine reading (e.g., works by Pitler & Nenkova, 2008).

3. **Methodological ambiguity**: The annotation procedure is unclear. The paper states that annotators selected sentences needed for answering questions and annotated them with prerequisite skills, but doesn't specify: (a) how many questions were annotated per dataset (100 seems insufficient for robust analysis), (b) whether annotators were instructed to evaluate from a human or machine perspective, and (c) how the "nonsense" category was defined and applied. The paper claims 90.1% inter-annotator agreement but doesn't provide details on the annotation process or how disagreements were resolved.

4. **Overstated conclusions**: The claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty" is heavily dependent on the specific readability features used (e.g., Flesch-Kincaid grade level). The paper doesn't explore whether different readability metrics would yield different correlations, nor does it consider that machine comprehension might have different readability requirements than human comprehension.

5. **Excessive scope**: The paper tries to address multiple questions simultaneously (dataset evaluation, metric development, system development implications) without focusing on one core contribution, weakening its overall impact.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please clarify whether your evaluation metrics are intended for human comprehension, machine comprehension, or both. If for machine comprehension, how do you justify using metrics developed for human readability assessment?

2. In Section 3.1, you reference psychological research on human text comprehension to develop metrics for machine reading comprehension. Please explain the theoretical justification for this approach and cite relevant work that has established this connection.

3. Please provide details on the annotation process: How many questions were annotated per dataset? How many annotators were involved? What instructions were given to annotators regarding the perspective (human vs. machine)? How were disagreements resolved?

4. The paper claims that "it is possible to create an RC dataset that is easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer" (MCTest is cited as an example). However, MCTest is written for children, making it "easy to read" by human standards. How does this relate to machine comprehension? Please clarify the distinction between human readability and machine comprehension requirements.

5. The paper uses Flesch-Kincaid grade level as a readability metric. What evidence is there that this metric correlates with machine comprehension difficulty? Have you tested other readability metrics that might better align with machine comprehension requirements?
