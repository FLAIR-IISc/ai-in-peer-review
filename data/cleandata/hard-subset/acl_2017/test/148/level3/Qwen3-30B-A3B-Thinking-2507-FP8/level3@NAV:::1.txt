

This paper investigates the relationship between text readability and question difficulty in reading comprehension (RC) datasets, proposing two evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors claim to demonstrate that readability does not directly affect question difficulty in RC tasks, suggesting it's possible to create datasets that are easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer.

The strongest contribution is the annotation of six RC datasets with the proposed metrics, providing a valuable resource for dataset analysis. The paper also attempts to refine the classification of prerequisite skills, incorporating insights from textual entailment and human text comprehension research.

However, the paper suffers from critical conceptual and methodological weaknesses. First, the goal remains unclear throughout, with frequent conflation between human reading comprehension and machine RC. The title and abstract fail to specify the focus on machine comprehension, creating fundamental ambiguity. Second, the paper misses key prior work in readability literature (e.g., DuBay 2004), and Section 2.2 contains irrelevant theoretical discussion about human psychology without clear connection to machine comprehension. Third, the claim that readability doesn't affect QA difficulty depends on specific features (POS/dependency) and is not generalizable. The annotation methodology is unclear, particularly regarding how annotators were instructed to judge from a machine vs. human perspective, and how "nonsense" questions were identified.

The paper attempts to address too many questions simultaneously, weakening its core contribution. The analysis in Section 5 conflates human and machine perspectives (e.g., citing human-focused studies for machine claims), and the distinction between "readability of text" and "difficulty of reading content" is not properly addressed. The manuscript also requires substantial English editing.

I recommend rejection due to conceptual confusion and methodological flaws. To address these issues, the authors should: 1) Clearly distinguish human vs. machine comprehension throughout, 2) Specify "for machine comprehension" when making claims about question difficulty, 3) Clarify annotation methodology, 4) Correct the title to reflect the machine comprehension focus, and 5) Incorporate key readability literature.

Questions to authors: How do you define "readability" for machine comprehension versus human reading? What specific features of readability were used in your analysis, and why were they chosen over others? How did annotators' perspectives (human vs. machine) affect the annotation process?
