- Summary: Proposes two metric classes (prerequisite skills and readability) for RC datasets, applied to six datasets; finds readability weakly correlated with question difficulty.

- Contribution: Refined classification of prerequisite skills informed by textual entailment and human comprehension.

- Contribution: Application of readability metrics to RC datasets to examine text vs. question difficulty.

- Contribution: Annotated six RC datasets with these metrics, yielding a resource for dataset construction and system development.

- Strength: Systematic annotation across six datasets provides concrete evidence that readability and question difficulty are not strongly correlated.

- Strength: Nuanced prerequisite-skill taxonomy, especially the bridging vs. elaboration inference distinction.

- Strength: Well-organized presentation of annotation results with clear tables and figures.

- Weakness: Conflates human and machine reading comprehension by applying psychological literature to machine systems without clarifying distinctions.

- Weakness: Fails to engage key prior work on NLP readability (e.g., Vajjala & Meurers 2012) and does not differentiate from human-focused readability research.

- Weakness: Annotation methodology is unclear about the number of questions annotated and whether annotators evaluated from a human or machine perspective.

- Weakness: Overly broad scope (human psychology, MRC, readability) leads to a lack of focus; Section 2.2 is disconnected from main goals.

- Weakness: Conclusion that readability does not affect question difficulty is too broad and dependent on the specific readability metrics used; limitations not acknowledged.

- Question: Clarify whether annotation was conducted from a human or machine perspective and justify using human psychological findings for machine evaluation.

- Question: State whether the conclusion about readability depends on the specific metrics used and discuss how alternative metrics might change results.

- Question: Define "nonsense" questions and the criteria used to label them, and explain their relevance to machine comprehension.

- Question: Explain how findings from human text comprehension research are intended to translate to machine reading-comprehension systems.
