

Summary: This paper proposes two classes of metrics for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills (to assess question difficulty) and readability (to assess text difficulty). The authors apply these metrics to six RC datasets, concluding that readability does not strongly correlate with question difficulty, suggesting it's possible to create RC datasets that are easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer.

Main Contributions: 
1. A refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC, organized using insights from textual entailment and human text comprehension.
2. Application of readability metrics to RC datasets to analyze the relationship between text difficulty and question difficulty.
3. Annotation of six RC datasets with these metrics, providing a valuable resource for dataset construction and system development.

Strengths:
The paper's most significant contribution is the systematic annotation of six RC datasets using the proposed metrics. This dataset analysis provides concrete evidence that readability and question difficulty are not strongly correlated, which is a valuable insight for dataset construction. The refined classification of prerequisite skills (particularly the distinction between bridging and elaboration inferences) offers a more nuanced framework than previous work. The annotation results are well-organized and presented with clear tables and figures that demonstrate the characteristics of each dataset.

Weaknesses:
The paper's most fundamental weakness is its conflation of human reading comprehension and machine reading comprehension. The authors repeatedly cite psychological research on human text comprehension (e.g., McNamara & Magliano 2009) but apply these insights to machine systems without clarifying the distinction. This creates significant conceptual confusion throughout the paper, particularly in Section 3.1 where human-focused citations are used to justify machine comprehension metrics.

The paper fails to engage with key prior work in readability studies for machine comprehension. For example, it misses foundational work on readability metrics for NLP systems (e.g., Vajjala & Meurers 2012) and does not clearly distinguish its approach from existing human readability research. The authors should have explicitly differentiated their work from human-focused readability studies, which is a critical omission.

The methodology section is unclear about the annotation process. It's not specified how many questions were annotated (the paper mentions "100 questions from each dataset" but doesn't clarify if this was per annotator or total), and it's ambiguous whether annotators were instructed to evaluate from a human or machine perspective. The annotation procedure states "the annotators selected sentences needed for answering," but doesn't clarify if they were to imagine themselves as a machine or as a human reader.

The paper attempts to cover too many topics simultaneously: human text comprehension theory, machine reading comprehension, and readability metrics. This lack of focus weakens the contributions. Section 2.2 on psychology is particularly disconnected from the paper's main goal of evaluating RC datasets for machine systems.

The conclusion that readability does not affect question difficulty is overly broad. The correlation coefficients reported (0.025-0.416) are weak, but the authors don't acknowledge that this conclusion depends on the specific readability metrics used (e.g., Flesch-Kincaid grade level). The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this limitation and specified that the lack of correlation is specific to the metrics employed.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper claims to evaluate "reading comprehension" but conflates human and machine processes. Please clarify the specific perspective (human vs. machine) from which the annotation was conducted, and explain why human psychological findings are appropriate for machine comprehension evaluation.

2. The paper states "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" but the correlation coefficients are weak. Please clarify whether this conclusion is specific to the metrics used (Flesch-Kincaid grade level, etc.) and discuss how different readability metrics might change this conclusion.

3. The paper mentions "nonsense" questions in some datasets but doesn't define what constitutes a "nonsense" question. Please clarify the criteria used for labeling questions as "nonsense" and explain why this is relevant to machine comprehension evaluation.

4. The paper references psychological work on human text comprehension but doesn't explain how these findings translate to machine systems. Please provide a clearer justification for applying human-focused psychological research to machine comprehension tasks.
