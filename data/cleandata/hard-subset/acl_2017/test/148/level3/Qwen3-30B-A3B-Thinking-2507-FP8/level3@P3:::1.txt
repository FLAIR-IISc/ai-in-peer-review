

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the relationship between text readability and question difficulty in reading comprehension (RC) datasets, proposing two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA), claiming to demonstrate that readability does not directly correlate with question difficulty.

The primary contribution I identify is the creation of a standardized annotation framework for RC datasets that categorizes prerequisite skills for question answering. This framework organizes 13 specific skills, including coreference resolution, causal relations, and knowledge reasoning (bridging/elaboration). The second contribution is the application of this framework to multiple RC datasets, providing quantitative metrics for dataset comparison. The third contribution is the dataset annotation results, which are made publicly available.

## Strengths

1. **Systematic Annotation Framework**: The paper provides a detailed, organized classification of prerequisite skills for RC, building on prior work by Sugawara et al. (2017). This framework offers a more nuanced understanding of what RC tasks require beyond superficial question types. The specific categorization of knowledge reasoning into bridging and elaboration skills, grounded in psychological research, is particularly valuable for researchers developing RC systems.

2. **Dataset Analysis**: The authors' analysis of six major RC datasets reveals meaningful differences in their characteristics, such as the high number of required skills in QA4MRE (average 3.25 skills per question) versus the lower requirements in MS MARCO (average 1.19). This provides concrete evidence that datasets vary significantly in their cognitive demands, which can inform dataset selection and system development.

3. **Practical Implications for System Development**: The paper's discussion on using these metrics for curriculum learning in RC system development is a strong practical contribution. The suggestion to build systems that first solve easy-to-read and easy-to-answer datasets, then progress to more challenging combinations, offers a clear methodology for incremental system improvement.

## Weaknesses

1. **Conceptual Conflation**: The paper fundamentally conflates human text comprehension with machine reading comprehension, which are distinct research areas. The authors cite psychological literature on human text comprehension (Kintsch, 1988; McNamara and Magliano, 2009) and apply it directly to machine systems without justification. This is problematic because machines process text differently than humans, and what is difficult for humans may be easy for machines (and vice versa), as the authors themselves acknowledge in Figure 1. The paper fails to make this crucial distinction clear, which undermines its central claim.

2. **Unsubstantiated Claims and Methodological Ambiguity**: The conclusion that "readability does not directly affect question difficulty" depends heavily on the specific features used (e.g., POS/dependency features) and the methodology of the study. The paper does not adequately address that this finding may be specific to the metrics used, not a general property of RC datasets. Additionally, it's unclear how the "readability" metrics (designed for humans) relate to machine processing. The paper cites DuBay (2004) that distinguishes "readability of text" from "difficulty of reading content," yet fails to address this distinction.

3. **Missing Prior Work and Poorly Defined Scope**: The paper omits key prior work in readability assessment for NLP (e.g., Vajjala and Meurers, 2012), which is cited but not properly contextualized. More critically, the paper fails to clarify its scope: Is it about human readability, machine comprehension, or both? The title and abstract suggest machine comprehension, but the methodology relies on human-focused psychological frameworks. The authors cite human-focused studies to support machine claims without justification.

4. **Annotation Methodology Unclear**: It's unclear how the datasets were annotated with "metrics" - specifically, how many questions were annotated (only 100 per dataset), whether annotators were instructed to judge from a machine or human perspective, and why the annotation focused on "sentences needed for answering" rather than the full comprehension process. The paper states annotators were instructed to "select sentences needed for answering," but doesn't clarify if they were told to think like a machine or human.

5. **Overambitious Scope**: The paper attempts to answer multiple questions simultaneously (human vs. machine comprehension, dataset quality, system development methodology) without sufficient focus. This results in a weak contribution that doesn't deeply address any single question, as evidenced by the lack of clear differentiation from human readability studies.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify whether your "readability" metrics were designed for human or machine comprehension? If for humans, how do you justify using them to evaluate machine comprehension difficulty?

2. The paper states "readability does not directly affect question difficulty" - to what extent does this finding depend on the specific features used (POS, dependency features)? Can you provide evidence that this relationship would hold across different feature sets?

3. In Section 3.1, you reference "skills" that are claimed to be relevant to machine comprehension, yet cite human psychology studies. How do you reconcile the human-focused psychological framework with your machine comprehension focus?

4. When annotating datasets, were annotators instructed to judge from a machine perspective or human perspective? If human, how does this relate to the machine comprehension focus of the paper?

5. The paper conflates "readability of text" with "difficulty of reading content" (per DuBay 2004). How do you address this distinction in your framework, and how does it affect your interpretation of results?
