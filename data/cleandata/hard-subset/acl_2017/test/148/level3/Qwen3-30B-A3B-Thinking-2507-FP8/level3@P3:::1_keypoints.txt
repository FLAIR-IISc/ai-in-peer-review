- Proposes two metric classes (prerequisite skills and readability) and applies them to six RC datasets, claiming readability does not directly correlate with question difficulty.
- Introduces a standardized annotation framework categorizing 13 prerequisite skills (e.g., coreference, causal relations, bridging/elaboration knowledge).
- Applies the framework across multiple datasets and releases the annotation results publicly.

- Provides a systematic, nuanced classification of prerequisite skills building on prior work (Sugawara et al., 2017).
- Explicitly separates knowledge-reasoning into bridging and elaboration, grounded in psychological research.
- Dataset analysis shows substantial differences in cognitive demands across datasets (e.g., QA4MRE avg 3.25 skills vs. MS MARCO 1.19), informing dataset choice and system design.
- Offers practical implications for curriculum learning: train on easier/readable datasets before harder combinations.

- Conflates human text comprehension with machine reading comprehension, applying human-focused psychology without justification.
- Claims about readability's effect on question difficulty are unsubstantiated and may depend on the specific features used (POS/dependency), limiting generality.
- Omits or poorly contextualizes key prior NLP readability work and fails to clearly define whether the scope is human or machine comprehension.
- Annotation methodology is unclear: small sample size (100 per dataset), unclear annotator perspective (human vs. machine), and focus on sentences needed rather than full comprehension.
- Overambitious scope tries to address human vs. machine comprehension, dataset quality, and system development methodology simultaneously, weakening depth.

- Were the readability metrics designed for human or machine comprehension, and if human, how are they justified for evaluating machine difficulty?
- How dependent is the conclusion that "readability does not directly affect question difficulty" on the chosen feature set (POS, dependency)?
- How do you reconcile using human psychology studies to define "skills" claimed to be relevant for machine comprehension?
- Were annotators instructed to judge from a machine or human perspective, and how does that choice affect the results?
- How does your framework address DuBay’s distinction between "readability of text" and "difficulty of reading content," and how does that impact your interpretation?
