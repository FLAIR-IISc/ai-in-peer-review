- Proposes two neural sequence models (single-sequence and multi-sequence) using Grid-RNNs for Japanese predicate argument structure (PAS) analysis.  
- Single-sequence model estimates arguments for each predicate independently.  
- Multi-sequence model jointly predicts arguments for all predicates, modeling multi-predicate interactions.  
- Models do not rely on parser-predicted syntactic information, inducing features from word sequences.  
- Claims state-of-the-art performance on the NAIST Text Corpus 1.5, outperforming baselines.  
- Multi-sequence model significantly improves zero argument identification.  
- Grid-RNNs effectively model contextual information and long-distance dependencies in Japanese.  
- Strength: novel neural architecture bridging text readability and machine comprehension (as claimed).  
- Strength: empirical SOTA results and detailed analyses (per case role, network depth effects).  
- Weakness: paper goal/main topic is unclear and ambiguous.  
- Weakness: unclear distinction between machine comprehension and human text readability.  
- Weakness: missing important prior work in the readability literature.  
- Weakness: Section 2.2 contains unrelated theoretical discussion that disrupts flow.  
- Weakness: paper attempts to answer too many questions at once, diluting focus.  
- Question: clarify the paper's goal and main topic.  
- Question: explain how machine comprehension vs. text readability are distinguished in the work.  
- Question: provide pointers to differentiate this work from human-question readability studies.  
- Question: how will you address error propagation from pipeline syntactic processing?  
- Question: provide details on dataset annotation: metrics, number of questions, and annotators' task understanding.
