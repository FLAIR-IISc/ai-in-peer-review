This paper presents an analysis of six reading comprehension datasets through the lens of “prerequisite skills” and “readability.” The core contribution is the application of these two classes of metrics to existing datasets, revealing a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty – a finding the authors suggest allows for the creation of datasets that are easy to read but hard to answer. A secondary contribution is a revised categorization of prerequisite skills, grounded in textual entailment and psychological models of human comprehension. Finally, the authors publicly release the annotated datasets, providing a valuable resource for the RC community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Combined Metrics:** The explicit combination of “prerequisite skills” (assessing question complexity) and “readability” (assessing text complexity) is a valuable approach. While both concepts have been explored independently, applying them *together* to analyze existing RC datasets provides a more nuanced understanding of dataset characteristics. This is especially strong because the authors correctly identify the limitations of solely relying on accuracy as a measure of dataset quality.
2. **Thorough Revision of Prerequisite Skills:**  The authors' attempt to refine the existing categorization of prerequisite skills, drawing on insights from textual entailment and psychological theories (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), is a significant improvement. Grounding the skills in established theoretical frameworks adds rigor and interpretability, addressing a weakness identified in previous work (Sugawara et al.). The inclusion of skills like “Ellipsis” and distinguishing between “Bridging” and “Elaboration” demonstrates a thoughtful approach.
3. **Publicly Available Annotated Datasets:** The release of the annotated datasets is a practical and highly valuable contribution. This resource will be directly useful to other researchers interested in dataset analysis, curriculum learning, or developing systems tailored to specific skill requirements. The annotations allow for controlled experimentation and benchmarking.
4. **Clear Demonstration of Weak Correlation:** The analysis convincingly demonstrates the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.  This is supported by both descriptive statistics (Tables 2 & 3) and the presented argument that datasets like MCTest can be easy to read yet still challenging. This finding directly addresses the research question posed in the introduction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Subjectivity of Skill Annotation & Inter-Annotator Agreement:** While the reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% is reasonable, the inherent subjectivity in assigning “prerequisite skills” remains a concern. The paper doesn’t fully address how disagreements were resolved, or the potential impact of annotator bias on the results.  The descriptions of some skills (e.g., “Meta-knowledge”) are also somewhat vague, potentially leading to inconsistent application.
2. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** The chosen readability metrics (Vajjala & Meurers, 2012) are a good starting point, but may not capture the full complexity of what makes a text “difficult” for an RC system.  Metrics focused on discourse structure or semantic complexity could provide a more complete picture. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't fully explore alternative metrics.
3. **Methodological Limitation: Sentence Selection Not Evaluated:** The authors explicitly acknowledge that their methodology doesn’t assess a system’s ability to *identify* relevant sentences within the context. This is a crucial aspect of RC, and the focus solely on understanding the *content* of the selected sentences limits the broader applicability of the analysis. The analysis assumes the correct sentences are provided.
4. **Weak Statistical Significance:** While the tables present frequencies and averages, there's a lack of statistical testing to determine the *significance* of observed differences between datasets.  For example, simply stating that QA4MRE has a higher average number of required skills isn't sufficient; a statistical test is needed to confirm that this difference is not due to chance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the process for resolving disagreements between annotators? Were there specific guidelines used to ensure consistent application of the prerequisite skill definitions?
2.  What measures were taken to ensure the representative selection of questions from each dataset (as described in Appendix A)? Were there any potential biases introduced by the sampling method?
3.  How might the results change if the analysis included a metric to evaluate the sentence selection component of RC systems? Are there plans to incorporate this into future work?
4.  Could you provide statistical tests (e.g., ANOVA, t-tests) to demonstrate the significance of the observed differences between datasets regarding the number of required prerequisite skills and readability metrics?



**Additional Comments:**

* The references are well-formatted and comprehensive.
*  The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, though some sections could benefit from more concise phrasing.
* Exploring the relationship between dataset genre and the observed patterns (as briefly mentioned in the discussion) would be a valuable avenue for future research.
