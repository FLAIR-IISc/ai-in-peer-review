- Paper analyzes six RC datasets using “prerequisite skills” and “readability,” finding a weak correlation that enables datasets that are easy to read but hard to answer.
- Revised prerequisite-skill taxonomy grounded in textual entailment and psychological models.
- Public release of annotated datasets for the RC community.

- Novelty: combining prerequisite skills and readability provides a more nuanced dataset analysis than accuracy alone.
- Rigorous revision of prerequisite skills, adding categories (e.g., Ellipsis) and distinctions (Bridging vs Elaboration).
- Public annotations enable controlled experiments, curriculum learning, and benchmarking.
- Empirical evidence (descriptive stats, examples like MCTest) convincingly shows weak readability–difficulty correlation.

- Skill annotation is subjective; 90.1% IAA reported but disagreement resolution and potential annotator bias are not fully addressed; some skill definitions (e.g., Meta-knowledge) are vague.
- Readability metrics are limited (Vajjala & Meurers 2012) and omit discourse/semantic complexity measures.
- Methodology ignores sentence-selection ability of RC systems, assuming relevant sentences are provided and limiting applicability.
- No statistical significance testing reported to confirm observed dataset differences.

- How were annotator disagreements resolved and what guidelines ensured consistent skill labeling?
- How were questions sampled from each dataset and could sampling introduce bias?
- How would including sentence-selection metrics affect results, and are there plans to add this?
- Can statistical tests (ANOVA, t-tests) be provided to demonstrate significance of differences?

- References are well-formatted and comprehensive.
- Paper is generally well-written and organized but could be more concise in places.
- Investigating the role of dataset genre in observed patterns is a promising future direction.
