This paper presents an analysis of six reading comprehension datasets through the lens of “prerequisite skills” and “readability.” The core idea – that reading difficulty doesn’t necessarily correlate with question difficulty – is a valuable observation. The authors refine existing prerequisite skill categorizations and annotate the datasets to demonstrate this point. The main contributions, as I see them, are: 1) The empirical demonstration, via dataset annotation, that readability and question difficulty are often weakly correlated in existing RC datasets. This is a significant finding highlighting limitations of current datasets. 2) A revised and more theoretically grounded categorization of prerequisite skills for RC, drawing on research from textual entailment and psychology. This provides a potentially useful framework for analyzing and building better datasets. 3) The publicly available annotation of six widely used RC datasets with the proposed metrics, providing a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Important Observation and Empirical Support:** The central argument – that readability is not a reliable indicator of question difficulty – is crucial for understanding the challenges in RC. The authors support this claim with a quantitative analysis of six datasets, making the argument more compelling than a purely theoretical one. The results clearly show that datasets can be “easy to read but hard to answer,” as exemplified by MCTest.
2. **Refined Prerequisite Skill Categorization:** The reorganization of prerequisite skills, incorporating insights from textual entailment and psychological models of comprehension, is a significant improvement over previous work. The distinctions between bridging and elaboration, and the inclusion of ellipsis and meta-knowledge are particularly valuable additions. This demonstrates a deeper theoretical understanding than simply adopting existing categorizations.
3. **Resource Creation:** Making the annotated datasets publicly available is a substantial contribution to the research community. This allows other researchers to validate the findings, explore the metrics further, and use the data to develop new datasets or improve existing models. This facilitates reproducibility and encourages further research in this area.
4. **Clear Motivation and Context:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem – the lack of nuanced evaluation metrics for RC datasets – and situates the work within the broader context of NLP and psychology. The paper effectively explains why these metrics are needed and how they address existing limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Annotation Methodology Limitations:** The authors acknowledge that their annotation methodology only evaluates "understanding of contextual entailments" and doesn’t assess the ability to *find* relevant sentences within the context. This is a significant limitation, as sentence selection is a crucial component of many RC systems. Focusing solely on entailment neglects important aspects of the RC process.
2. **Weak Correlation Doesn’t Imply Independence:** While the authors demonstrate a *weak* correlation between readability and difficulty, they don’t thoroughly explore the nature of this relationship. It would be beneficial to investigate *which* readability features, if any, are predictive of question difficulty, even with a low correlation. Exploring partial correlations or interaction effects could reveal more nuanced insights.
3. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** The choice of readability metrics is somewhat limited to surface-level linguistic features. While these are relevant, they don’t capture aspects like domain-specific knowledge or conceptual complexity, which could also contribute to question difficulty. A broader range of readability measures might provide a more comprehensive picture.
4. **Dataset Specificity of Findings:** The observed correlations and skill distributions may be specific to the datasets analyzed. The authors acknowledge this, but a more generalizable framework for characterizing RC datasets would be desirable. Investigating a wider range of datasets with different characteristics would strengthen the conclusions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that your methodology doesn't evaluate the competence of sentence selection. Do you have plans to explore methodologies that *do* assess sentence selection ability, and how would those integrate with your current framework?
2.  While you demonstrate a weak correlation overall, did you observe any specific readability metrics that exhibited stronger (even if still modest) correlations with prerequisite skill requirements for certain datasets or question types?
3.  Could you elaborate on the challenges you faced in achieving inter-annotator agreement, and how those challenges might have influenced the annotation results? What steps were taken to mitigate annotator bias?
4.  The paper focuses on the difficulty of questions. Are there plans to explore how these metrics could be used to assess the *quality* of correct answers, beyond simply whether an answer is right or wrong?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures and tables are generally well-presented and easy to understand.
*   The related work section is concise but effectively covers relevant literature.
*   The inclusion of the annotation procedure in the appendix is helpful for transparency and reproducibility.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential biases in the datasets themselves, and how those biases might affect the analysis.
