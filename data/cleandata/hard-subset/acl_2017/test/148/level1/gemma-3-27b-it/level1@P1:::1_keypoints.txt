- Empirical demonstration that readability and question difficulty are often weakly correlated across six RC datasets, highlighting dataset limitations.
- Revised, theoretically grounded categorization of prerequisite skills for RC, incorporating textual entailment and psychological insights.
- Public release of annotations for six widely used RC datasets using the proposed metrics.

- Important observation with quantitative support: readability is not a reliable indicator of question difficulty (e.g., MCTest).
- Refined prerequisite-skill taxonomy with useful distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration, ellipsis, meta-knowledge).
- Creation of a publicly available resource enabling reproducibility and further research.
- Clear motivation and contextualization linking NLP evaluation gaps to psychological models of comprehension.

- Annotation methodology evaluates contextual entailment but omits assessment of sentence-selection ability, a key RC component.
- Demonstrating weak correlation between readability and difficulty without deeper analysis of which readability features might still be predictive.
- Readability metrics used are limited to surface-level features and omit domain knowledge or conceptual complexity measures.
- Findings may be dataset-specific; broader dataset coverage or a more generalizable framework is needed.

- Do you plan to extend the methodology to evaluate sentence-selection competence and integrate it with your framework?
- Were any specific readability features found to have stronger (even modest) correlations with skill requirements for particular datasets or question types?
- What challenges affected inter-annotator agreement, how might they have biased results, and what mitigation steps were taken?
- Are there plans to use these metrics to assess answer quality beyond binary correctness?

- Figures, tables, and appendix annotation procedure are clear and helpful for transparency.
- Related work coverage is concise and effective.
- Consider discussing potential dataset biases and their effects on the analysis.
- Presentation of results and resources is valuable for community use.
