This paper presents an analysis of six reading comprehension datasets through the lens of “prerequisite skills” and “readability,” aiming to demonstrate that these two facets aren’t necessarily correlated, and to offer a more nuanced evaluation of RC datasets than simple accuracy metrics. The core contribution is the application and refinement of existing psychological models of comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) to analyze the characteristics of these datasets. A secondary contribution is the publicly released annotation of these datasets with the defined metrics. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important issue – the need for better evaluation of RC datasets beyond simple accuracy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Gap:** The paper directly addresses a vital issue in the RC field: the superficiality of current dataset evaluation.  Simply reporting accuracy scores doesn’t provide insight into *why* a model succeeds or fails, or the inherent difficulty of the dataset itself.  The proposed framework offers a more granular, interpretable analysis. The observation that high readability doesn't necessarily equate to easy comprehension is a valuable and intuitively correct point.
2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the work in established psychological models of text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) is a significant strength.  It moves the evaluation beyond ad-hoc heuristics and provides a principled basis for the chosen metrics.  The attempt to integrate textual entailment perspectives (Dagan et al.) is also commendable.  This theoretical anchoring lends credibility to the analysis.
3. **Publicly Available Annotations:** The annotation of six datasets with the defined metrics is a valuable resource for the community. This allows other researchers to build upon this work, validate the findings, and potentially use the annotations for training or evaluating new models. The clear description of the annotation procedure is helpful.
4. **Detailed Analysis & Insights:** The analysis of the datasets, and the resulting observations about each (e.g., QA4MRE’s difficulty, MCTest’s unique characteristics, MS MARCO’s potential issues), provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of existing resources. The tables presenting the prerequisite skill frequencies are informative.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** The annotation process, while described, inevitably involves subjective judgment in assigning prerequisite skills. The paper mentions 90.1% inter-annotator agreement, but doesn’t detail the nature of the disagreements.  Low agreement on certain skills could undermine the reliability of the analysis. A detailed error analysis of annotation disagreements would be beneficial.  The paper doesn’t adequately address potential biases introduced by the annotators.
2. **Limited Correlation Analysis:** While the paper observes a lack of strong correlation between readability and prerequisite skills, the correlation analysis presented (Table 5) appears somewhat limited.  The choice of Pearson correlation might not be the most appropriate for capturing non-linear relationships. Further exploration of different correlation measures or regression models could reveal more nuanced patterns.
3. **Methodology's Limitations:** The paper acknowledges that the methodology doesn’t evaluate the system’s ability to *select* relevant sentences, only to understand the entailed information *given* the selected sentences. This is a crucial limitation. A truly comprehensive evaluation would need to address both aspects of reading comprehension.
4. **Superficial Discussion of System Development Implications:** The discussion of how to leverage these metrics for system development (Section 6) is rather high-level and lacks concrete examples. A more detailed proposal of a curriculum learning approach, informed by the dataset analysis, would strengthen this aspect of the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the inter-annotator agreement, including specific examples of disagreements and how they were resolved? What steps were taken to minimize annotation bias?
2.  Were alternative correlation measures or regression models explored to investigate the relationship between readability and prerequisite skills beyond Pearson correlation? If so, what were the results?
3.  Given the acknowledged limitation regarding sentence selection, do you envision any extensions to your methodology to address this aspect of reading comprehension?
4.  Could you elaborate on the practical steps for using the annotated datasets within a curriculum learning framework? For instance, how would you prioritize datasets or questions based on the assigned metrics?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Appendix A provides a clear explanation of the sampling method.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   Minor: Some sentences are a bit lengthy and could benefit from being broken down for improved readability.



Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field. The weaknesses are not critical and could be addressed through further analysis and clarification. The paper’s strengths – the strong theoretical foundation, the insightful analysis, and the publicly available annotations – make it a worthwhile addition to ACL 2017. I recommend acceptance.
