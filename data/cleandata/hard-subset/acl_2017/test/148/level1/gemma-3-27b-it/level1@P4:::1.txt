This paper investigates the relationship between question difficulty and text readability in reading comprehension (RC) datasets, proposing the use of “prerequisite skills” and “readability” metrics for dataset evaluation. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and analyze the results, concluding that readability does not strongly correlate with question difficulty.

The primary contribution of this work is the systematic application and analysis of a combination of prerequisite skill and readability metrics to a diverse set of RC datasets. While the individual metrics are not novel, the combined approach and the resulting dataset-level analysis provide a useful characterization of existing benchmarks and highlight potential weaknesses in their design. This dataset-level analysis is valuable for the RC community, providing insights beyond simple accuracy scores. A second contribution is the refinement of the prerequisite skills classification, integrating insights from textual entailment and psychology to create a more theoretically grounded categorization. Finally, the publicly released annotated datasets are a valuable resource for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** The application of both prerequisite skill and readability metrics to six datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) offers a broad comparative analysis. This allows for dataset-specific observations (e.g., MCTest’s easy readability but comparable difficulty) and general trends (weak correlation between readability and difficulty). This is a significant contribution to understanding the characteristics of these popular datasets.
2. **Refined Prerequisite Skill Classification:** The authors’ attempt to ground the categorization of prerequisite skills in theories from textual entailment (Dagan et al., 2013) and psychology (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara & Magliano, 2009) strengthens the theoretical basis of their analysis. This revised classification goes beyond simply listing skills, providing a framework for understanding *why* certain questions are difficult.  The inclusion of bridging and elaboration skills, specifically, addresses limitations identified in previous work (Sugawara et al., 2017).
3. **Publicly Available Resources:** The annotated datasets are a valuable resource for the RC community.  Researchers can leverage these annotations to train models specifically targeting particular skills or to analyze model performance with respect to these skills.  This promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research in dataset design and evaluation.
4. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The use of tables and figures effectively communicates the findings. Table 2, summarizing the frequencies of prerequisite skills, and Table 3, showcasing the number of required skills, provide a concise overview of the dataset characteristics. Figures 3 and 4 offer a visual representation of the relationship between readability and skill requirements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Subjectivity in Annotation:** Despite reporting inter-annotator agreement, the annotation of prerequisite skills is inherently subjective. The paper does not detail the specific instructions given to annotators beyond selecting sentences and labeling skills, and differences in interpretation could influence the results.  A more detailed description of the annotation guidelines, along with examples, would increase confidence in the annotations.
2. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** The readability metrics chosen (Vajjala & Meurers, 2012) are primarily focused on syntactic and lexical complexity. They do not capture semantic complexity, which might be a crucial factor in question difficulty. Expanding the range of readability metrics to include measures of semantic coherence and conceptual density could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper establishes a weak correlation between readability and difficulty, but does not investigate the *reasons* for this lack of strong correlation.  While the authors hypothesize about dataset construction and question design, further analysis is needed to understand the underlying mechanisms. For example, are difficult questions designed to specifically counter easy-to-read texts?
4. **Methodology Limitations:** The stated limitation that the methodology does not evaluate the competence of *selecting* necessary sentences is a significant one. Focusing only on the skills needed *given* the selected sentences limits the scope of the analysis.  A more holistic evaluation would assess a system’s ability to identify the relevant information within a document.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more detail about the annotation guidelines used, including examples of how annotators were instructed to handle ambiguous cases or disagreements?
2. Were any steps taken to mitigate potential biases in annotator interpretations of the prerequisite skills?
3.  You mention the limitation that your methodology doesn't evaluate sentence selection. Do you have plans to address this limitation in future work, perhaps by incorporating a sentence selection evaluation metric?
4.  Could you elaborate on why certain datasets (e.g., MS MARCO) exhibited a higher frequency of "nonsense" questions, and what implications this has for dataset quality control?

**Additional Comments:**

* The references are comprehensively listed.
* The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
* The related work section provides a good overview of the existing literature on RC datasets and human text comprehension.
* The observation about the potential for creating easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer questions is a key takeaway from this work and has important implications for dataset design.
