## Review for "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

This paper presents an analysis of six reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors aim to understand the relationship between question difficulty and text readability, finding a weak correlation and demonstrating the possibility of creating datasets that are easy to read but difficult to answer. The primary contribution is the application of a combined metric framework – leveraging existing work in psychology and NLP – to systematically analyze existing RC datasets. A secondary contribution is the revised categorization of prerequisite skills, grounding it in textual entailment and human text comprehension theories. Finally, the annotated datasets themselves are a valuable resource for the community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Systematic Dataset Analysis:** The application of both prerequisite skills and readability metrics provides a more nuanced understanding of RC datasets than simply relying on accuracy scores. This detailed analysis is a significant contribution, revealing characteristics of each dataset that were previously unquantified.
2. **Bridging Psychology and NLP:** The authors effectively connect research in human text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) with NLP techniques for evaluating RC datasets. This interdisciplinary approach strengthens the theoretical foundation of their work and provides a more informed perspective on dataset quality.
3. **Publicly Available Annotation:**  Making the annotated datasets publicly available is a strong positive. This resource will be valuable for researchers developing new RC models and for evaluating the quality of existing datasets. It facilitates reproducibility and further research in this area.
4. **Insightful Findings:** The finding that readability doesn’t necessarily correlate with question difficulty is important. It highlights the need for more sophisticated dataset construction and evaluation methods beyond simply controlling text complexity.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Annotation Methodology Limitations:** The methodology relies on annotators identifying *required* skills, but doesn’t assess the ability to identify *relevant* information within the text. This limits the scope of the evaluation, as a system might struggle with information selection even if it possesses the necessary skills.
2. **Limited Scope of Readability Metrics:** While the chosen readability metrics are well-established, they may not fully capture the nuances of text complexity relevant to RC.  The reliance on established metrics without exploring RC-specific readability features feels somewhat limiting.
3. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper demonstrates a weak correlation, but doesn't explore potential causal relationships.  Further investigation into *why* readability and difficulty are weakly correlated could strengthen the conclusions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the criteria used to define "nonsense" questions and how this impacts the results, particularly for datasets like MS MARCO?
2.  How sensitive are the prerequisite skill annotations to inter-annotator disagreement? Were any resolution strategies employed, and if so, how might they have influenced the results?
3.  Do you plan to release the annotation guidelines used for the prerequisite skill labeling? This would be valuable for others attempting to replicate or extend this work.
