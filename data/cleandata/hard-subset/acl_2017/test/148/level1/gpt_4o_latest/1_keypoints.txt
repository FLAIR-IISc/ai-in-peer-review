- Introduces two evaluation metrics—prerequisite skills and readability—and shows a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty in RC datasets.  
- Refines a prerequisite-skills taxonomy for RC, grounded in psychology and textual entailment research.  
- Annotates six RC datasets with the proposed metrics and publicly releases the annotations.  
- Novel analysis framework that moves beyond accuracy and question-type labels to assess dataset quality.  
- Key finding: readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated, challenging assumptions in dataset design.  
- Public annotations of six diverse datasets enhance reproducibility and utility for the community.  
- Theoretical grounding in psychological and linguistic theories lends credibility to the metrics.  
- Provides practical implications, e.g., using metrics to design stepwise curricula for RC systems.  
- Limited dataset selection (excludes CNN/DailyMail, CBTest), which reduces generalizability.  
- Annotation subjectivity: insufficient detail on disagreement resolution and inter-annotator agreement.  
- No evaluation of RC systems using the metrics, weakening practical validation of the framework.  
- Readability metrics may not align with machine comprehension; the paper lacks exploration of this gap.  
- Limited discussion and no proposed solutions for annotation challenges like "nonsense" or "no answer" questions.  
- Questions for authors: how were annotator disagreements resolved and what are the inter-annotator agreement stats?  
- Questions for authors: have you evaluated RC systems using the annotated datasets to validate metric utility?  
- Questions for authors: can readability metrics be adapted to better reflect machine comprehension capabilities?  
- Overall: valuable and novel contribution, but impact is limited by lack of system evaluation and narrower dataset scope; addressing these would improve utility and generalizability.
