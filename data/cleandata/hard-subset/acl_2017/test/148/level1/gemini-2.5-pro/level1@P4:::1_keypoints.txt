- Paper proposes a framework evaluating RC datasets along two axes: reasoning complexity (13 refined prerequisite skills) and text readability (10 metrics), annotating 100 questions per six datasets and finding a weak correlation between the two.
- Contribution 1: Comprehensive multi-dataset empirical analysis with public release of skill annotations.
- Contribution 2: Quantitative demonstration that readability is not a strong predictor of reasoning complexity.
- Contribution 3: Refined, psychology- and entailment-grounded taxonomy of prerequisite reading-comprehension skills.
- Strength 1: Provides a concrete, comparative methodology and actionable annotations that help characterize dataset challenges beyond leaderboard scores.
- Strength 2: Methodology is well-grounded in prior human comprehension models and validated readability metrics, with high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%).
- Strength 3: Delivers actionable insights for dataset and system design (e.g., curriculum learning, designing simple-language but reasoning-hard datasets).
- Weakness 1: Simplistic difficulty metric—unweighted skill counts assume equal, additive difficulty across skills without validation against model error.
- Weakness 2: Manual, subjective annotations (e.g., Bridging, Elaboration) limit scalability and representativeness given the small sample size per dataset.
- Weakness 3: Framework omits information-retrieval/evidence-location as a core skill, understating the full RC task for datasets where finding the answer span is challenging.
- Question 1: Have you considered weighting skills (e.g., learned from model errors) rather than using an unweighted sum to measure question difficulty?
- Question 2: How would including an "information retrieval/evidence selection" skill change the analysis and dataset rankings (e.g., SQuAD)?
- Question 3: Is it feasible to automate skill prediction (train a classifier on annotations) to scale analysis to entire datasets and provide real-time feedback?
