**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes and applies a two-dimensional framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on "question difficulty" and "text readability". Question difficulty is measured by annotating the prerequisite skills required to answer a question, using a refined taxonomy of 13 skills grounded in psychology and textual entailment literature. Text readability is measured using a set of 10 standard linguistic features. The authors apply this framework to analyze 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (including MCTest, SQuAD, and MS MARCO). Their primary finding is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, leading to the insight that a dataset can be easy-to-read yet contain difficult questions (e.g., MCTest), or be difficult-to-read yet contain simple questions (e.g., SQuAD). The authors release their annotations to facilitate future research.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A Large-Scale, Comparative Analysis of RC Datasets:** The most significant contribution is the systematic empirical analysis across six different RC datasets. This work provides the community with a clear, data-driven characterization of the challenges posed by these resources. The core finding—that text readability and reasoning complexity are largely orthogonal dimensions—is an important and timely corrective to potential misconceptions in the field and has strong implications for dataset design and model evaluation. The public release of the detailed annotations is a valuable resource in itself.
2.  **A Refined Taxonomy for RC Skills:** The paper presents a well-motivated taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills. By building on previous work and grounding the refinements in established theories from psychology (e.g., Kintsch's model, bridging vs. elaboration inferences) and textual entailment, the authors provide a more principled and comprehensive vocabulary for analyzing the reasoning requirements of RC tasks than was previously available.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Critical and Timely Problem:** At a time when the community was rapidly producing large-scale RC datasets, this paper provides a much-needed methodology for looking "under the hood" to understand what is actually being measured. The work compellingly argues that simple accuracy is an insufficient metric and that we need a more nuanced understanding of dataset characteristics. The introduction's contrast between a SQuAD and an MCTest example perfectly motivates the need to decouple reading difficulty from reasoning difficulty.
2.  **Well-Grounded and Principled Framework:** The methodology is not ad-hoc. The two main axes of evaluation ("prerequisite skills" and "readability") are directly inspired by established psychological models of text comprehension (McNamara and Magliano, 2009). Furthermore, the refinement of the skill taxonomy, particularly the knowledge reasoning skills, draws thoughtfully from literature in both psychology (bridging/elaboration) and NLP (textual entailment), lending it significant theoretical weight and credibility.
3.  **Broad and Insightful Empirical Evaluation:** The analysis spans six datasets with diverse origins (expert-authored, crowdsourced, automatically generated), providing a panoramic view of the 2016-2017 RC landscape. The per-dataset findings are illuminating; for example, identifying QA4MRE as the most complex, highlighting the reasoning depth in Who-did-What despite its automatic generation, and quantifying the relative simplicity of SQuAD questions despite the complexity of the text. These results provide concrete guidance for researchers selecting datasets for specific research goals.
4.  **Actionable Implications for Future Research:** The paper concludes with a thoughtful discussion on how this framework can guide the development of RC systems, suggesting its use in a curriculum learning setting. This demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed metrics beyond pure analysis and points the way toward more systematic progress in the field.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Potential Subjectivity and Unclear Reliability of Annotations:** The paper's central claims rest on the manual annotation of prerequisite skills. While a 90.1% inter-annotator agreement is reported, this figure lacks context. It is crucial to know what metric this represents (e.g., simple agreement, Krippendorff's Alpha) and how it was calculated for a multi-label task with 13 categories. A chance-corrected coefficient would be far more convincing. The definitions for some skills, such as the distinction between "Bridging" (lexical/grammatical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (commonsense knowledge), appear subtle and could be difficult for annotators to distinguish consistently. A more detailed breakdown of the IAA and a discussion of difficult cases would significantly strengthen the work's empirical foundation.
2.  **Simplifying "Difficulty" as the "Number of Skills":** A core assumption of the analysis is that question difficulty is directly proportional to the number of required skills. This is a significant oversimplification. It is highly plausible that some skills (e.g., Logical Reasoning) are inherently more complex than others (e.g., Punctuation). A single question requiring complex logical inference might be much harder for a system than another question requiring coreference resolution and understanding a causal marker. The paper does not address this potential unequal weighting, which could affect the interpretation of the correlation results.
3.  **Limited Sample Size:** The analysis is based on a random sample of 100 questions per dataset. For massive datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO, this represents a very small fraction of the data. While precedent exists for such sample sizes in manual analyses, it raises questions about how representative these findings are of the entire datasets. The conclusions, particularly regarding the weak correlation, would be more robust if supported by a larger sample or at least a discussion of this limitation and its potential impact.
4.  **Omission of Influential Datasets:** The exclusion of the CNN/Daily Mail dataset is a notable omission. At the time of this work, it was one of the most widely used and discussed large-scale RC datasets. While the authors cite known quality issues (Chen et al., 2016), an analysis of it—even with caveats—would have provided a more complete picture of the landscape and a valuable comparison point.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the inter-annotator agreement metric used? Was it a chance-corrected coefficient like Krippendorff's Alpha, and how was agreement calculated for this multi-label annotation task?
2.  The selection of "sentences that needed to be read" is a critical preliminary step for both the skill annotation and the readability/distance calculations. Could you comment on the reliability of this sentence selection step? Was inter-annotator agreement measured for it, and what guidelines were provided to annotators?
3.  Your current definition of difficulty is the unweighted sum of required skills. Did you consider or do you have any thoughts on assigning different weights to skills based on their intrinsic complexity? For instance, could this weighting change the observed correlation with readability?
