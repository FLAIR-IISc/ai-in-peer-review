**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes and applies a dual-axis evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets, analyzing them based on 'prerequisite skills' required for question answering and standard 'readability' metrics for the context text. The authors manually annotate 100 questions from each of six major RC datasets (including MCTest and SQuAD) using a refined 13-skill taxonomy. The core finding is that text readability and question difficulty are only weakly correlated.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A valuable empirical analysis across six diverse RC datasets that provides strong evidence to decouple text difficulty from reasoning complexity. The public release of these annotations is a significant asset to the community, enabling more nuanced dataset selection and model evaluation.
2.  A revised and more principled taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills for RC, grounded in psychology and textual entailment literature, which provides a useful tool for fine-grained analysis of the reasoning required by different tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper's central finding—that readability does not dictate question difficulty—is significant, counter-intuitive, and well-supported by the cross-dataset analysis (Figures 3, 4). This insight challenges a common implicit assumption in the field and provides a clear directive for future dataset creation: focus on crafting complex reasoning chains, which can be done even with simple language.
2.  The proposed evaluation framework is comprehensive, successfully integrating two distinct dimensions of difficulty. The revised skill taxonomy is more structured and thorough than in previous work, allowing for a detailed characterization of datasets (e.g., SQuAD is "hard-to-read, easy-to-answer," while MCTest is "easy-to-read, hard-to-answer").
3.  The study's breadth, covering six different datasets with varying sources and formats, strengthens the generality of its conclusions and provides the community with a useful comparative overview of the challenges posed by each.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The primary weakness is the limited scale of the annotation. While analyzing 100 questions per dataset can reveal broad trends, it may not be a sufficiently representative sample to capture the full diversity within large-scale datasets like SQuAD or MS MARCO, potentially affecting the statistical robustness of the conclusions.
2.  The annotation of 13 distinct skills, despite high reported inter-annotator agreement, is inherently subjective. The distinction between certain skills (e.g., Bridging vs. Elaboration) can be subtle, and the paper does not detail the process for training annotators or resolving disagreements, which could impact the replicability and reliability of the annotations.
3.  The methodology explicitly bypasses the crucial RC skill of identifying relevant sentences within a larger context, as annotators were provided with the necessary sentences. While acknowledged by the authors and briefly analyzed in Table 6, omitting this "search" component from the main skill-based evaluation means the framework does not capture a key aspect of real-world RC difficulty.
