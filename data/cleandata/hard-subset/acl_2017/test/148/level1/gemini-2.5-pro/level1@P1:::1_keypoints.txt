- Proposes a two-dimensional framework for RC datasets: a 13-item "prerequisite skills" typology and ten readability metrics, applied to 100 questions from six datasets.  
- Central finding: weak correlation between passage readability and number of skills required, so datasets can be easy-to-read but hard-to-answer (or vice versa).  

Contributions:
- Provides a comparative, empirically-grounded analysis of six prominent RC datasets and releases annotations for community use.  
- Demonstrates empirically that readability and question-complexity are largely orthogonal dimensions.  
- Refines a principled typology of prerequisite skills grounded in prior NLP and cognitive literature.  

Strengths:
- High community value and impact for dataset selection, failure diagnosis, and curriculum learning research.  
- Principled, transparent methodology with grounded skill choices and high reported inter-annotator agreement (90.1%).  
- Broad scope across genres and question sources, with clear visualizations supporting the main results.  

Weaknesses:
- Uses raw count of skills as a proxy for difficulty, ignoring that some skills vary greatly in complexity.  
- Lacks validation against system performance to show that higher skill counts correspond to lower model accuracy.  
- Excludes search/localization difficulty by pre-selecting supporting sentences, downplaying evidence-finding challenges in long documents.  
- Limited sample size (100 questions per dataset) may not capture rare phenomena or full dataset diversity.  

Questions for authors:
- Have you correlated annotated skill counts with RC model performance (e.g., F1 by skill-count) to validate your difficulty proxy?  
- Can you provide per-skill inter-annotator agreement to show which abstract skills were harder to annotate?  
- What guidelines did annotators follow when selecting supporting sentences, especially for cases needing broader context?
