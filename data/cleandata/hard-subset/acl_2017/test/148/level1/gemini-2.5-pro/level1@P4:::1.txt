**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets along two axes: the complexity of reasoning required to answer questions and the linguistic difficulty of the source text. To measure the former, the authors refine a taxonomy of 13 "prerequisite skills" (e.g., coreference resolution, causal relation, elaboration) based on prior work in NLP and psychology. The difficulty of a question is then approximated by the number of skills required to answer it. To measure the latter, the authors adopt a set of 10 standard readability metrics covering lexical and syntactic features. The authors apply this framework to manually annotate 100 questions from each of six prominent RC datasets (e.g., MCTest, SQuAD, MS MARCO). The core finding from this analysis is that there is only a weak correlation between text readability and the number of skills required, demonstrating that these are largely independent dimensions of difficulty.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A comprehensive, multi-dataset empirical analysis that systematically characterizes and compares the types of reasoning skills required by six different RC datasets. The public release of these skill annotations provides a valuable resource for the research community to better understand the challenges posed by these benchmarks.
2.  An explicit, quantitative demonstration that the linguistic difficulty of a text (readability) is not a strong predictor of the reasoning complexity required to answer questions about it. This is a key insight for the design of future RC datasets.
3.  A refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills for reading comprehension, which is grounded in established theories from psychology and textual entailment, providing a more structured vocabulary for discussing RC phenomena than prior work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Valuable Contribution to Dataset Analysis and Understanding:** The paper addresses a critical need in the field. With the proliferation of RC datasets, it is essential to move beyond leaderboards based on a single accuracy metric and develop a deeper understanding of what challenges each dataset actually presents. This work provides a concrete methodology and a rich set of annotations to facilitate this. The comparative analysis across six diverse datasets offers clear insights (e.g., contrasting SQuAD's high readability/low skill complexity with MCTest's low readability/higher skill complexity) that are useful for researchers selecting datasets for specific purposes or designing new ones. The public release of the annotations is a significant strength that will enable further research.
2.  **Sound and Well-Grounded Methodology:** The proposed framework is built on a solid foundation. The "prerequisite skills" are a thoughtful refinement of prior work and are explicitly linked to established concepts in human comprehension models (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment research. The use of standard, previously validated readability metrics from Vajjala and Meurers (2012) is also appropriate. The annotation process is clearly described, and the reported inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% suggests that the skill definitions are sufficiently clear for reliable human judgment.
3.  **Actionable Insights for System and Dataset Development:** The paper's central finding—the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty—is an important result. It provides strong evidence against using source text complexity (e.g., Wikipedia vs. children's stories) as a simple proxy for task difficulty. This has direct implications for the community, encouraging the creation of datasets with simple language but complex reasoning challenges. Furthermore, the discussion in Section 6 provides concrete suggestions on how this framework can be used to guide system development in a curriculum learning setting, starting with easy-to-read, easy-to-answer tasks and progressing to more complex combinations.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Simplistic Quantification of Question Difficulty:** The paper's primary proxy for question difficulty is a simple count of the number of prerequisite skills required. This methodology rests on the strong assumption that all skills are equally difficult and that their difficulty is additive. This is unlikely to be true; a question requiring "Elaboration" (commonsense knowledge) may be fundamentally harder for a current system than one requiring "Punctuation" and "Coreference resolution". The analysis does not attempt to validate this assumption, for instance, by correlating skill counts or specific skill types with the error rates of existing RC models. This simplification limits the depth of the difficulty analysis.
2.  **Subjectivity and Limited Scalability of the Framework:** The annotation of prerequisite skills is a manual, knowledge-intensive process. While the authors report high inter-annotator agreement, the definitions for skills like "Bridging" and "Elaboration" still require significant subjective judgment. This manual nature means the analysis is limited to a small sample (100 questions) from each dataset, which may not be fully representative of large-scale corpora like SQuAD or MS MARCO. The framework, in its current form, cannot be automatically applied to analyze entire datasets or to classify the difficulty of new questions, limiting its practical utility for large-scale evaluation.
3.  **Exclusion of Information-Seeking as a Core Skill:** The annotation methodology explicitly factors out the challenge of locating the relevant sentences in the context, focusing only on the reasoning required once those sentences are identified. This is a major simplification of the RC task as defined by many datasets (e.g., SQuAD, NewsQA), where finding the answer span within a larger document is a core component of the challenge. While the paper includes a brief, post-hoc analysis of sentence distance in the discussion, this aspect is not integrated into the main "prerequisite skills" framework. A more holistic evaluation would treat information retrieval as a critical skill in itself, potentially changing the relative difficulty rankings of the datasets.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The metric for question difficulty is the unweighted sum of required skills. Have you considered an alternative formulation where skills are weighted? For example, one could potentially learn weights by measuring the correlation of each individual skill with machine errors on a development set. Do you believe this would provide a more accurate measure of difficulty?
2.  The methodology intentionally separates the task of locating relevant evidence from the task of reasoning from it. How do you think the inclusion of an "information retrieval" or "evidence selection" skill would alter your analysis and the resulting characterization of the six datasets? For instance, would SQuAD still be considered "easy to answer" if the difficulty of locating the single correct sentence in a long paragraph was factored in?
3.  The proposed framework is very useful for manual analysis but is not scalable. What are your thoughts on the feasibility of automating this process? Could a classifier be trained on your annotations to predict the required skills for unseen questions, thus enabling analysis of entire datasets and providing real-time feedback for dataset creators?
