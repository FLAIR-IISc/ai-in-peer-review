**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a comprehensive analysis of six reading comprehension (RC) datasets, evaluating them along two axes: the prerequisite skills required to answer questions and the readability of the context passages. The authors refine an existing taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills (e.g., coreference resolution, causal relation, bridging) grounded in cognitive psychology and textual entailment literature. They also employ ten standard readability metrics. By annotating 100 questions from each of six diverse datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), the authors conduct a comparative analysis.

From my perspective as a reviewer, the main contributions are:

1.  **A significant empirical finding about the nature of RC datasets:** The paper's primary contribution is the well-supported conclusion that the readability of a text and the difficulty of questions about it (measured by the number of reasoning skills required) are only weakly correlated. This is an important, counter-intuitive result that challenges a common assumption and has direct implications for how the community should approach dataset creation and model evaluation. The analysis effectively characterizes datasets like SQuAD as "easy-to-answer but hard-to-read" and MCTest as "easy-to-read but hard-to-answer."

2.  **A principled, multi-faceted framework for dataset analysis:** The work introduces and applies a strong methodological framework for comparing RC datasets beyond superficial statistics like size or question type. By grounding the "prerequisite skills" in established psychological and linguistic theory and combining it with standard readability metrics, the paper provides a much-needed tool for understanding the specific challenges posed by different benchmarks.

3.  **Creation of a valuable new resource for the community:** The authors have created and pledged to release a dataset of annotations spanning six major RC benchmarks. This resource will be valuable for future meta-analyses, error analyses of RC systems, and for guiding research in areas like curriculum learning.

**Strengths**

1.  **Timely and Impactful Analysis:** At a time (2016-2017) when numerous large-scale RC datasets were being released, this paper provides a crucial service by offering a deeper, qualitative comparison. It moves the conversation beyond simply creating bigger datasets to understanding what those datasets actually measure. The central finding—that readability does not imply question difficulty—provides a clear and actionable insight for both dataset creators and researchers developing new RC models. It encourages a more nuanced approach to curriculum design for training models, as suggested in the discussion.

2.  **Strong Methodological Grounding:** The authors do not simply propose an ad-hoc set of metrics. The two-axis evaluation framework is well-motivated by established theories of human text comprehension from psychology (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). The refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy, particularly splitting "commonsense reasoning" into more granular categories like Bridging and Elaboration based on work in textual entailment, adds rigor and clarity to the analysis. This strong theoretical underpinning makes the subsequent empirical results more credible and interpretable.

3.  **Comprehensive and Broad Evaluation:** The application of the framework to six distinct and widely-used datasets is a major strength. The selected datasets cover a variety of genres (narratives, encyclopedic articles, news, web snippets) and creation methodologies (expert-authored, crowdsourced, automatically generated). This breadth allows for robust comparisons and lends weight to the paper's general conclusions about the landscape of RC tasks. The results are presented clearly in tables and figures, effectively highlighting the unique profile of each dataset.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scale of Annotation and Potential for Sampling Bias:** The analysis, while broad in terms of the number of datasets, is based on a relatively small sample of 100 questions per dataset. For massive datasets like SQuAD and MS MARCO, which contain over 100,000 questions, it is questionable whether this sample size is sufficient to draw statistically robust conclusions about the entire corpus. The paper would be stronger if it included a discussion of potential sampling error, provided confidence intervals for the reported skill frequencies, or otherwise justified why this sample is representative of the whole.

2.  **Subjectivity in Skill Annotation:** The process of annotating prerequisite skills is inherently subjective. While the paper reports a high inter-annotator agreement of 90.1%, crucial details are missing. It is unclear if this is simple percentage agreement or a more robust metric like Cohen's or Fleiss' Kappa, which corrects for chance agreement. Furthermore, the distinction between certain skills, such as "Bridging" (lexical knowledge) and "Elaboration" (general/commonsense knowledge), can be ambiguous in practice. A more detailed description of the annotation guidelines and a more rigorous reporting of IAA would significantly strengthen the claims.

3.  **Oversimplification of "Question Difficulty":** The paper makes the explicit assumption that question difficulty is directly proportional to the number of required prerequisite skills. This is a reasonable first-order approximation but is a clear simplification. It is unlikely that all skills are equally difficult for a computational model to master. For instance, `Logical reasoning` may be fundamentally more challenging than resolving a simple `Punctuation` ambiguity. This assumption may lead to a skewed perception of dataset difficulty; for example, a dataset with many questions requiring two "easy" skills might be ranked as harder than a dataset with fewer questions that each require one very "hard" skill.

4.  **Exclusion of the Sentence Selection Challenge:** The authors acknowledge that their methodology evaluates the reasoning component of RC *after* the relevant sentences have been identified, and does not assess the "competence of selecting necessary sentences." While they provide a brief, interesting analysis of this in the discussion (Table 6), this competence is a central and difficult part of many modern RC tasks, especially those with long contexts. By focusing primarily on the "entailment" sub-problem, the analysis may present an incomplete picture of the total difficulty of datasets like SQuAD or QA4MRE, where finding the answer span or relevant sentences is a key challenge.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the calculation of the 90.1% inter-annotator agreement? Specifically, what metric was used (e.g., Fleiss' Kappa), and was it calculated on a per-skill basis or for the overall annotation of a question?

2.  Your operationalization of difficulty as the count of required skills is a core assumption. Have you considered that different skills might contribute differently to overall difficulty? For instance, could you explore a weighted sum of skills, and how might that alter your conclusion that SQuAD is "easy-to-answer"?

3.  Given the small sample size (100 questions) relative to the full datasets, how did you ensure the random sample was representative? For SQuAD, for example, sampling one question per paragraph might underrepresent articles that are dense with questions. Could the observed trends be an artifact of this sampling strategy?
