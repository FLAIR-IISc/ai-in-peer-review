**Paper Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a two-dimensional framework for evaluating and characterizing reading comprehension (RC) datasets, focusing on the difficulty of answering questions and the difficulty of reading the text. The first dimension, "prerequisite skills," is a refined typology of 13 reasoning skills (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging, elaboration) required to answer a question. The second, "readability," uses ten standard linguistic metrics to measure the lexical and syntactic complexity of the context passages. The authors apply this framework to manually annotate 100 questions from each of six diverse RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, and NewsQA). The central finding is that there is only a weak correlation between a text's readability and the number of skills required to answer questions about it. This suggests that RC datasets can be, for example, easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer (like MCTest) or difficult-to-read but easy-to-answer (like SQuAD).

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive, comparative analysis of six prominent RC datasets.** This is the paper's primary contribution. It provides the community with a valuable, empirically-grounded characterization of these datasets, moving beyond anecdotal evidence. The analysis offers clear insights into the relative strengths and weaknesses of each corpus, and the public release of the annotations is a significant asset for future research.
2.  **The empirical demonstration that readability and question complexity are largely orthogonal dimensions of difficulty.** This is an important scientific finding that challenges a common implicit assumption in the field. It highlights the need for more nuanced dataset design and provides a useful vocabulary (e.g., "easy-to-read, hard-to-answer") for discussing different types of RC challenges.
3.  **A refined and well-grounded typology of prerequisite skills for RC.** While building on prior work, the authors' effort to ground the skill definitions, particularly the knowledge reasoning categories, in established literature from psychology and textual entailment makes the framework more principled and robust.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Value and Impact for the Community:** This work provides a much-needed meta-analysis of the rapidly growing landscape of RC datasets. The resulting characterizations are extremely useful for researchers aiming to select appropriate datasets for specific research goals (e.g., focusing on multi-sentence reasoning vs. lexical complexity) or to diagnose the failures of their models. The discussion on how these metrics could inform curriculum learning for RC systems is particularly insightful and forward-looking.
2.  **Principled and Transparent Methodology:** The evaluation framework is well-motivated. The choice of prerequisite skills is grounded in prior NLP work and cognitive psychology, and the readability metrics are standard and well-established. The authors are transparent about their annotation process, reporting a high inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) which lends credibility to their findings and the resulting dataset.
3.  **Broad Scope and Strong Empirical Support:** The analysis covers six different datasets that span a wide range of genres (narrative, encyclopedic, news), question sources (human-authored, automatically generated), and task formats. This breadth makes the comparative analysis rich and the central finding about the weak correlation between readability and skill-based difficulty more compelling and likely to be generalizable. The visualizations (e.g., Figure 3) are effective at communicating this main result.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Simplification of Question Difficulty:** The paper's primary proxy for question difficulty is the *number* of required skills. This is a significant simplification, as not all skills are equally difficult. A question requiring a single, complex "Elaboration" inference might be substantially harder for a machine (and a human) than a question requiring "Coreference Resolution" and "Schematic Clause Relation". The analysis would be stronger if it acknowledged this and either attempted to weight the skills or discussed the limitations of this "sum of skills" assumption more directly.
2.  **Lack of Validation against System Performance:** The paper analyzes datasets in isolation from the systems designed to solve them. The claim that a higher number of skills corresponds to greater difficulty is an assumption, albeit a reasonable one. The work would be significantly strengthened by validating this claim empirically. For example, showing that the accuracy of one or more state-of-the-art RC models is negatively correlated with the annotated number of required skills would provide crucial evidence that the proposed metric truly captures a notion of difficulty relevant to current systems.
3.  **Exclusion of Search/Localization Difficulty:** The methodology explicitly sidelines the challenge of *finding* the relevant information within a document. Annotators first identify the supporting sentences and then analyze the skills required to reason over them. This is a critical omission, as the ability to locate relevant evidence, especially in long documents (as in QA4MRE and SQuAD), is a core competence of reading comprehension. The brief analysis of sentence distance in the Discussion section is a welcome addition, but it is disconnected from the main "prerequisite skills" framework and doesn't fully address this limitation.
4.  **Limited Sample Size:** While following precedent, annotating only 100 questions per dataset feels small, especially for large corpora like SQuAD. It is possible that this random sample does not fully capture the diversity of phenomena present in the full datasets, and that the reported frequencies of certain skills (especially rare ones like "Analogy" or "Mathematical Reasoning") might be sensitive to the sample chosen.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your central assumption is that the number of skills serves as a proxy for question difficulty. Have you considered validating this by correlating the skill counts for your annotated questions with the performance of existing RC models? For instance, do questions you label with 3+ skills have a systematically lower F1 score on a model like BiDAF than questions labeled with only one skill?
2.  The inter-annotator agreement of 90.1% is quite high overall. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown? Were certain skills, particularly the more abstract reasoning types like "Elaboration" or "Bridging," significantly more difficult for annotators to agree upon compared to more concrete skills like "Punctuation" or "Causal Relation"?
3.  The first step of your annotation pipeline is selecting the necessary sentences. This seems like a crucial and potentially subjective step. Could you elaborate on the guidelines given to annotators for this process? For instance, how did you handle cases where broader paragraph-level context might be helpful but not strictly required to deduce the answer?
