**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a study on the evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors adopt two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. They apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets, including MCTest and SQuAD, and analyze the characteristics of the datasets according to each metric and the correlation between the two classes.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors develop a comprehensive framework for evaluating RC datasets using two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability.
2. They refine the classification of prerequisite skills for RC, including skills such as object tracking, mathematical reasoning, and knowledge reasoning.
3. They annotate six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics and provide a detailed analysis of the results.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the RC datasets using the proposed metrics, which can help researchers understand the strengths and weaknesses of each dataset.
2. The authors' framework for evaluating RC datasets is comprehensive and can be applied to various RC tasks.
3. The paper highlights the importance of considering both prerequisite skills and readability when evaluating RC datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper's focus on prerequisite skills and readability might overlook other important aspects of RC datasets, such as question types and answer types.
2. The authors' annotation process relies on human annotators, which may introduce subjectivity and variability in the results.
3. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed metrics can be used to improve RC systems.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how you plan to use the analysis in this study to construct a system that can be applied to multiple datasets?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of annotator subjectivity and variability in the annotation process?
3. Can you provide more examples of how the proposed metrics can be used to improve RC systems, such as by identifying areas where the system needs to improve?

**Additional Comments**

The paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of RC by proposing a comprehensive framework for evaluating RC datasets. However, further research is needed to address the limitations of the study and to explore the applications of the proposed metrics in improving RC systems.
