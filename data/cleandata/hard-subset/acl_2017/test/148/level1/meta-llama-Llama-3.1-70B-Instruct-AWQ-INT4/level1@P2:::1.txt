This paper presents an evaluation of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors refine the prerequisite skills proposed in previous studies and define new metrics for readability based on linguistic features. They then annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and analyze the results. The main contributions of this paper are:

1. The adoption of two classes of evaluation metrics to analyze RC datasets, which provides a more comprehensive understanding of the quality of these datasets.
2. The refinement of prerequisite skills for RC, which includes the reorganization of knowledge reasoning skills based on entailment phenomena and human text comprehension.
3. The annotation of six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics, which provides a valuable guide for the construction of new datasets and the development of RC systems.

Strengths:

1. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the RC datasets using the proposed metrics, which highlights the characteristics of each dataset and provides insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each.
2. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the prerequisite skills and readability metrics, making it easy to understand the methodology and results.
3. The paper discusses the implications of the results for the development of RC systems, including the importance of considering the genre of the corpus and the type of questions used.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper relies heavily on the annotation of a limited number of questions from each dataset, which may not be representative of the entire dataset.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the inter-annotator agreement, which is crucial for evaluating the reliability of the annotation.
3. The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the proposed metrics, such as the fact that they may not capture all aspects of RC.

Questions to Authors:

1. How did you ensure that the annotators were consistent in their annotation, and what measures did you take to address any discrepancies?
2. Can you provide more details on the annotation process, such as the time taken to annotate each question and the level of expertise required?
3. How do you plan to address the potential limitations of the proposed metrics, and what future work do you propose to improve the evaluation of RC datasets?

Additional Comments:

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work, including a comparison of the proposed metrics with existing evaluation methods.
* The authors could provide more insights into the implications of the results for the development of RC systems, such as how to use the proposed metrics to improve the performance of these systems.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the readability metrics, including a discussion of the limitations and potential biases of these metrics.
