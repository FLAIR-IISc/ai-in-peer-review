**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors refine the prerequisite skills for RC, which cover the textbase and situation model, and define 13 skills, including object tracking, mathematical reasoning, and knowledge reasoning. They also adopt readability metrics based on linguistic features, such as vocabulary and sentence complexity. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and analyze the characteristics of the datasets. The results show that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the difficulty of their questions, and it is possible to create an RC dataset that is easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. The authors propose two classes of evaluation metrics for RC datasets, which provide a more comprehensive understanding of the datasets' characteristics.
2. The authors refine the prerequisite skills for RC, which cover the textbase and situation model, and define 13 skills that are relevant to RC.
3. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics, which provides a valuable resource for the research community.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the RC datasets, which highlights the importance of considering both prerequisite skills and readability when evaluating RC systems.
2. The authors propose a refined set of prerequisite skills for RC, which is based on a comprehensive review of the literature and provides a more nuanced understanding of the skills required for RC.
3. The authors' annotation of six existing RC datasets provides a valuable resource for the research community and enables the comparison of different RC datasets.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The authors' annotation methodology has some limitations, such as the use of only three annotators and the lack of consideration of the competence of selecting necessary sentences.
2. The authors' analysis of the correlation between prerequisite skills and readability is limited to a simple Pearson correlation coefficient, which may not capture the complexity of the relationship between these two variables.
3. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their proposed metrics can be used to improve the development of RC systems.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how you plan to use the analysis in this study to construct a system that can be applied to multiple datasets?
2. How do you plan to address the limitations of your annotation methodology, such as the use of only three annotators and the lack of consideration of the competence of selecting necessary sentences?
3. Can you provide more insights on how the proposed metrics can be used to improve the development of RC systems, and what specific steps can be taken to leverage these metrics in system development?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and provides a thorough analysis of the RC datasets. The authors' proposal of two classes of evaluation metrics for RC datasets is a significant contribution to the field. However, the paper could benefit from more details on how the proposed metrics can be used to improve the development of RC systems. Additionally, the authors' annotation methodology could be improved by considering more annotators and the competence of selecting necessary sentences.
