**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a study on evaluating the quality of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets, including MCTest and SQuAD, and analyze the characteristics of the datasets and the correlation between the two classes of metrics. The study reveals that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the difficulty of questions and that it is possible to create an RC dataset that is easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer.

**Contributions of the Paper**

1. **Adoption of two classes of evaluation metrics**: The authors adopt two classes of metrics, prerequisite skills and readability, to evaluate the quality of RC datasets.
2. **Revision of previous classification of prerequisite skills**: The authors revise the previous classification of prerequisite skills for RC, organizing skills of knowledge reasoning in terms of entailment phenomena and human text comprehension in psychology.
3. **Annotation of six existing RC datasets**: The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with the organized metrics, providing a valuable guide for the construction of new datasets and the development of RC systems.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Comprehensive analysis of RC datasets**: The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of six existing RC datasets, highlighting their characteristics and correlations between the two classes of metrics.
2. **Novel approach to evaluating RC datasets**: The authors' approach to evaluating RC datasets using two classes of metrics is novel and provides a more nuanced understanding of the quality of RC datasets.
3. **Implications for RC system development**: The study's findings have implications for the development of RC systems, suggesting that systems should be designed to handle both easy-to-read and difficult-to-answer datasets.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited scope of analysis**: The analysis is limited to six existing RC datasets, and the authors do not provide a comprehensive analysis of all existing RC datasets.
2. **Subjective annotation process**: The annotation process is subjective, and the authors rely on human annotators to label the datasets, which may introduce bias.
3. **Limited generalizability**: The study's findings may not be generalizable to other RC datasets or domains, and further research is needed to confirm the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the subjective nature of the annotation process in future studies?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the annotation process, including the instructions given to annotators and the criteria used to evaluate the quality of annotations?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the study to include other RC datasets and domains, and what are the implications of the study's findings for the development of RC systems in other domains?
