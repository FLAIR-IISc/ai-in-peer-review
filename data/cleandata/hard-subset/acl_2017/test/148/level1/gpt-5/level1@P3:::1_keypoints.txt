- Paper proposes two complementary dataset-quality metrics for RC: a 13-category prerequisite skill taxonomy and readability features.
- Authors annotated 100 questions from six RC datasets and analyzed skill frequency, co-occurrence, and readability on sentences deemed necessary to answer.
- Main empirical claim: readability and question difficulty (proxied by number of required skills) are only weakly correlated.
- Contribution: a theory-informed, entailment- and psycholinguistics-grounded taxonomy reorganizing reasoning phenomena (bridging/elaboration/ellipsis/meta-knowledge).
- Contribution: cross-dataset, apples-to-apples annotation and comparative analysis across six datasets with released annotations.
- Contribution: actionable dataset-level insights (e.g., SQuAD tends to be single-sentence answerable; MCTest requires more multi-sentence reasoning).
- Strength: strong conceptual grounding connecting RC phenomena to established models (C–I, RTE) increasing interpretability.
- Strength: useful cross-dataset diagnostic approach across multiple task formats (MC and span extraction).
- Strength: clear methodological scope (annotators saw question, context, answer and focused on contextual entailment).
- Strength: practical implications for curriculum design, dataset construction, and genre-aware evaluation.
- Strength: resource contribution—public release of annotations enables follow-up work.
- Weakness: difficulty operationalized as simple skill count, ignoring weights and interaction effects and lacking validation against human/system difficulty.
- Weakness: small and heterogeneous sampling (100 items/dataset, differing selection protocols) may bias results.
- Weakness: insufficient reliability reporting—single percent agreement reported without per-skill kappa or detailed adjudication.
- Weakness: readability computed only on annotator-selected sentences, confounding annotator selection with textual difficulty and compressing variance.
- Weakness: unconventional sentence-distance metric (sum of pairwise distances) instead of standard span width, reducing interpretability.
- Weakness: lack of linkage between diagnostics and actual system performance or error patterns on the same items.
- Questions for authors: request detailed annotator guidelines, per-skill inter-annotator agreement, and adjudication procedures.
- Questions for authors: justify difficulty-as-count, report experiments with weighted/interaction models, and correlations with human/model performance.
- Questions for authors: report within-dataset correlations with CIs, and results when computing readability on full contexts.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity checks to alternative readability metrics (e.g., dependency length, LM surprisal) and parse robustness across genres.
- Questions for authors: clarify “nonsense” label criteria, disagreement rates, and treatment of world-knowledge-only items.
- Questions for authors: justify sentence-distance formula and show results using span width instead.
- Questions for authors: commit to releasing code for feature extraction and the full annotation protocol for replicability.
