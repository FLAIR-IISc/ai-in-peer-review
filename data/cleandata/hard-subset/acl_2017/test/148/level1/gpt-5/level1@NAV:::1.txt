Summary
The paper proposes an evaluation framework for reading comprehension (RC) datasets based on two metric classes: prerequisite skills (a refined taxonomy grounded in textual entailment and cognitive psychology) and readability (a suite of linguistic features). The authors annotate six RC datasets and show only weak correlations between readability and question difficulty, arguing it is feasible to build datasets that are easy to read yet difficult to answer. They release annotations and discuss implications for dataset construction and system development.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A cross-dataset human annotation study that characterizes RC datasets along prerequisite skills and readability, revealing weak correlations and distinct dataset profiles.
2) A refinement and theoretically grounded reorganization of prerequisite skills, especially clarifying knowledge reasoning via bridging vs. elaboration and related categories.
3) A resource contribution: publicly released annotations and analyses to support future dataset design and evaluation.

Strengths
- Theory-informed taxonomy with practical utility: The reorganization of skills (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration, meta-knowledge, ellipsis) is grounded in established work (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano; RTE literature) and addresses prior ad hoc categorizations.
- Systematic, multi-dataset analysis: Six diverse datasets are annotated under a unified protocol, producing comparable profiles. The reported 90.1% agreement indicates a reliable procedure.
- Actionable insights for dataset curation: The empirical finding of weak readability–difficulty correlation (and concrete cases like MCTest vs. SQuAD) guides how to construct curricula and stress-test datasets without relying on surface text complexity.
- Useful diagnostics on dataset quality: Identification of “nonsense” question prevalence (e.g., MARCO, WDW) and sentence-distance analyses provide practical signals for filtering and task design.
- Open resource: Making annotations available enhances transparency and facilitates follow-up research.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The work extends Sugawara et al. (2017) with incremental taxonomy changes and broader coverage; methodological advances are modest.
- Small sample sizes and limited statistics: Only 100 questions per dataset; correlation analyses lack significance tests and controls (e.g., passage length, genre, question type), risking underpowered or confounded conclusions.
- Readability metrics are narrow: Primarily surface/lexico-syntactic features (plus FK grade) may miss discourse/pragmatic factors most relevant to RC; modern readability or coherence measures are absent.
- Scope gap to end-to-end RC: The framework excludes sentence selection and answer extraction, so “question difficulty” may not translate to full-system difficulty; computing readability only on annotator-selected sentences may introduce circularity.
- Annotation details under-specified: Per-skill agreement, handling of co-occurring skills, and adjudication for “nonsense” are not fully reported.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Please report per-skill inter-annotator agreement and agreement on sentence selection; provide statistical significance/confidence intervals for correlations.
- How sensitive are results to using full passages vs. selected sentences for readability?
- Can you share detailed guidelines and borderline examples distinguishing bridging from elaboration?
- Do these metrics predict model performance across datasets, and can they inform automatic filtering during dataset construction?
