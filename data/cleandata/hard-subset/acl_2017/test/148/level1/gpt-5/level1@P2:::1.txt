Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes and applies two complementary classes of evaluation metrics to characterize reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills (13 categories, reorganizing “knowledge reasoning” into ellipsis, bridging, elaboration, and meta-knowledge), and (b) readability metrics (primarily Vajjala & Meurers’ top features plus Flesch–Kincaid). The authors annotate 100 questions each from six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), marking sentences needed to answer and the skills required. The central empirical claim is that readability and question difficulty (operationalized as number of prerequisite skills) are only weakly correlated, implying that easy-to-read contexts can support difficult questions and vice versa.

As I see them, the contributions (strongest first):
1) An empirical cross-dataset analysis showing weak correlations between readability and skill-based difficulty, with dataset-specific profiles (e.g., MCTest easy-to-read yet skill-demanding; SQuAD harder-to-read yet relatively easy-to-answer).
2) A refined, theoretically grounded taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, integrating insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology (bridging vs. elaboration).
3) A publicly released set of annotations linking questions to required skills and sentences, enabling further research on dataset quality and system analysis.

Strengths
- Clear empirical insight with actionable implications: The central result—weak correlation between readability and skill demand (Pearson up to ~0.416, often much lower)—is supported by cross-dataset plots (Figures 3–4) and a comprehensive feature set (Table 5). This challenges a pervasive but tacit assumption and suggests concrete guidelines for dataset construction (Section 6), e.g., decoupling text simplicity from inferential complexity.
- Useful, theory-informed skill taxonomy: The reorganization of knowledge reasoning into bridging vs. elaboration (with ellipsis and meta-knowledge) is well motivated by RTE and cognitive models (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano). It improves interpretability over prior ad hoc categories and maps naturally to system capabilities.
- Comparative profiling across diverse datasets: The analysis highlights meaningful differences (Table 2–3), e.g., QA4MRE’s higher rates in bridging/elaboration and multi-clause structures; WDW’s higher ellipsis due to its construction; MARCO’s prevalence of “nonsense” items. These observations surface curation artifacts that matter for both training and evaluation.
- Transparent methodology and partial reliability check: The procedure describes annotator workflow (sentence selection, multi-label skills, “nonsense” option) and reports inter-annotator agreement (90.1% raw agreement on 62 questions). While not exhaustive, it provides a basis for reproducibility, and the intent to release annotations adds value.
- Practical lens on system evaluation: The discussion usefully proposes curriculum-like dataset staging by readability and skill-demand, and briefly analyzes sentence distances (Table 6), which relates to retrieval vs. reasoning burdens.

Weaknesses
- Proxy for “difficulty” is narrow and unvalidated: Equating difficulty with the count of prerequisite skills is plausible but untested against independent measures (human difficulty ratings, response times, or model performance). Without such validation, the key conclusion risks being an artifact of the annotation scheme. Suggest correlating skill counts with human accuracy/time or baseline/system performance.
- Limited and uneven sampling threatens representativeness: Only 100 questions per dataset (some with pre-filters, e.g., NewsQA exclusions; MCTest drawn from dev; SQuAD one question per paragraph) may not capture dataset diversity. Differences in task formulation and sampling choices (Appendix A) could bias cross-dataset comparisons (e.g., MS MARCO segments filtered by is_selected=1).
- Reliability reporting is insufficient: The paper reports a single percent agreement figure on a small subset (62 questions), with no per-skill agreement, no multi-label kappa/alpha, and no detail on annotator training or adjudication. Given the fine-grained, subjective nature of many skills (bridging vs. elaboration; ellipsis), stronger reliability evidence is needed.
- Statistical analysis is underdeveloped: Correlation results (Table 5) lack confidence intervals and significance testing, and multiple comparisons are not addressed. Figures focus on Flesch–Kincaid despite the availability of richer readability features. It would help to report effect sizes with CIs, correct for multiple tests, and show robustness (e.g., partial correlations controlling for sentence count/length).
- Methodological scope excludes key competencies: Annotators saw the gold answer and were asked to select necessary sentences, so the analysis explicitly does not assess retrieval, answer localization, or distractor handling (for MC). This limits claims about overall dataset “difficulty” for RC systems. Results should be framed as conditional on having the relevant context and answer identified.
- Minor internal inconsistency: The text claims QA4MRE averaged 4.6 required skills per question, but Table 3 shows 3.25. This should be reconciled.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide per-skill inter-annotator agreement (e.g., Krippendorff’s alpha) and agreement on sentence selection? Were annotators trained with a codebook and examples? Was there adjudication?
- Do skill counts correlate with human difficulty (time/accuracy) or with the error rates of baseline/models across datasets? Any preliminary evidence?
- What is the availability and format of the released annotations (licenses, identifiers mapping to dataset items)? Will code for readability feature extraction be released?
- How sensitive are conclusions to sampling choices (e.g., more SQuAD questions per paragraph; including/excluding “nonsense”)? Any bootstrapped CIs over correlations?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting statistical significance and CIs for correlations, and controlling for confounds such as number/length of required sentences.
- Clarify how “nonsense” was defined and adjudicated; some “solvable without context” items were flagged—how were those treated?
- Given Table 6, exploring how sentence distance interacts with skill demands could yield finer insights (e.g., bridging across distant sentences).

Overall, this is a clear, useful analysis paper with a thoughtfully grounded taxonomy and practically relevant findings. With stronger validation of the difficulty proxy, expanded reliability/statistical reporting, and clearer framing of scope, it would make a solid contribution.
