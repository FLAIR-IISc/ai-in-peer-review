Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes and applies two complementary classes of metrics to evaluate reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (i) prerequisite skills needed to answer questions, using a refined taxonomy aligned with textual entailment and psycholinguistic theories; and (ii) readability metrics capturing lexical and syntactic difficulty of the context. The authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with prerequisite skills, compute readability only on sentences deemed necessary to answer, and analyze correlations between the two classes.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical cross-dataset analysis showing only weak correlation between question difficulty (operationalized as the number of prerequisite skills) and text readability, with qualitative characterizations per dataset (e.g., MCTest easy-to-read yet relatively difficult to answer; SQuAD hard-to-read but often locally answerable).
2) A refined, theoretically motivated taxonomy of prerequisite skills, especially reorganizing knowledge reasoning into bridging, elaboration, ellipsis, and meta-knowledge, drawing from textual entailment and cognitive psychology.
3) Release of annotated analyses to facilitate dataset design and system development.

Strengths
- Clear, motivated problem framing and cross-disciplinary grounding: The paper convincingly argues for evaluating RC datasets beyond surface categories, grounding the skill taxonomy in established textual entailment phenomena and psycholinguistic distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration; retrieved vs. generated inference). This conceptual alignment increases interpretability and potential reuse of the taxonomy.

- Breadth of empirical coverage across datasets and genres: Annotating six datasets spanning narratives, news, Wikipedia, and web passages provides a broader view than prior analyses limited to one or two datasets. The per-dataset observations (e.g., WDW’s higher ellipsis due to question–context decoupling; QA4MRE’s broader reasoning and structural complexity; NewsQA’s value of meta “question quality” labels) are informative for dataset curation.

- Methodological unification across task formulations: The protocol of selecting sentences needed to answer and labeling required skills enables comparison across MC, cloze, and span-extraction formats. The additional analysis of sentence number and distance offers a useful proxy for local vs. global reasoning demands.

- High-level reproducibility commitment: The authors state they will release annotations and analyses. This can support follow-up work on RC curricula and targeted evaluation, as suggested in the discussion.

- Practically relevant insight: The weak readability–difficulty correlation highlights that dataset readability indices are insufficient proxies for question difficulty. This informs both dataset design (e.g., crafting challenging questions over simple texts) and model evaluation (e.g., stratifying tests by skill counts rather than text readability).

Weaknesses
- Limited reliability reporting and small annotation sample: Only 100 questions per dataset and inter-annotator agreement reported as percent agreement (90.1%) over 62 questions are provided. Percent agreement overestimates reliability; no per-skill, Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa, or adjudication protocol is reported. Without per-label reliability and confidence intervals, robustness of the distributions and cross-dataset differences is uncertain.

- Task framing may bias difficulty assessment: Annotators saw context, question, and gold answer (and MC options), and pre-selected “sentences needed,” which removes retrieval and answer selection difficulty. As acknowledged, the method cannot evaluate sentence retrieval or candidate generation, but it also risks underestimating reasoning difficulty (hindsight bias) and conflating subjective judgments of “nonsense” with solvable-but-hard cases.

- Difficulty operationalization is assumed, not validated: The premise that “number of skills” equates to question difficulty is plausible but unvalidated against human performance (accuracy, response time) or model performance. No skill weighting or interaction is considered, and overlapping categories (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration vs. ellipsis) may inflate counts. This weakens claims about difficulty comparisons across datasets.

- Correlation analysis is shallow and potentially confounded: Reported Pearson correlations (0.025–0.416) are not accompanied by sample sizes per analysis, p-values, or controls for dataset identity and context length. Readability features are drawn from L2 readability literature (Vajjala & Meurers); their applicability to RC difficulty is not established. The use of Flesch–Kincaid and a top-10 feature set may omit discourse and cohesion features more relevant to RC.

- Heterogeneous sampling and filtering across datasets: Different selection strategies (e.g., one question per SQuAD paragraph, NewsQA filters excluding “bad” questions, using only selected MS MARCO segments) and use of development/gold sets complicate comparability. The “nonsense” label is subjective and unevenly manifest across datasets due to curation differences, potentially biasing frequency and skill statistics.

Questions to authors
- Reliability: Please report inter-annotator agreement per skill (e.g., macro/micro-averaged kappa), the aggregation/adjudication procedure for final labels, and annotator training/background. Provide time per item and examples of disagreements, especially for bridging vs. elaboration vs. ellipsis.

- Validating difficulty: Can you correlate skill counts with human accuracies or response times (if available) or with baseline/system performance stratified by skill count? Alternatively, provide ablations (e.g., excluding “single-sentence” items) to test robustness.

- Correlation statistics: Please report the exact Pearson coefficients with sample sizes and significance tests, and, if possible, a mixed-effects regression controlling for dataset identity and document length. Do conclusions hold if using other readability features (e.g., cohesion indices) or composite readability scores?

- Taxonomy clarity: Can you provide the full annotation guidelines with decision rules and illustrative positive/negative examples for each skill, especially for borderline cases (e.g., idioms vs. metonymy vs. apposition; ellipsis vs. bridging)?

- Release details: Will you release per-question skill labels, selected sentences, “nonsense” flags, and code/scripts for readability computation to ensure reproducibility?

- Sentence distance: Given dependence on document length, have you normalized distance by paragraph/document length, or analyzed within-dataset quartiles to mitigate length confounds?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, the taxonomy is thoughtfully motivated, and the cross-dataset analysis is useful. The main claims would be stronger with more rigorous reliability reporting, validation of the difficulty proxy, and tighter statistical analysis.
