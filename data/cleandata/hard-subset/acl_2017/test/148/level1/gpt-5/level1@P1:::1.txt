Summary and contributions
This paper proposes two complementary classes of metrics to characterize the difficulty and quality of reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) prerequisite skills required to answer questions, and (b) readability features of the supporting text. The authors refine a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills informed by research on textual entailment and human text comprehension (e.g., bridging vs. elaboration), and compute readability using a set of established lexical/syntactic features. They annotate 100 questions each from six RC datasets (MCTest, SQuAD, QA4MRE, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), label the sentences needed to answer each question, and analyze frequencies of skills, average number of required skills, readability, and correlations between the two classes.

As I see them, the main contributions (in order):
1) A comparative empirical analysis across six influential RC datasets with newly created human annotations of prerequisite skills and supporting sentences, revealing dataset-specific profiles and the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty.
2) A reorganized, theoretically motivated taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, integrating insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology (bridging vs. elaboration, ellipsis, meta-knowledge).
3) Public release of the annotations, enabling downstream dataset design and system diagnostics.

Strengths
- Theoretically grounded skill taxonomy. The refinement of prerequisite skills is thoughtful and well-sourced (Kintsch; McNamara & Magliano; Dagan et al.). The explicit separation of bridging vs. elaboration and addition of ellipsis and meta-knowledge reduce ambiguity compared to prior work, and offer a better lens on knowledge reasoning than coarse “commonsense” labels.
- Cross-dataset, uniform analysis with concrete, interpretable findings. The study spans diverse datasets and task formulations and uses a single annotation protocol. The results capture intuitive and actionable differences (e.g., WDW’s high ellipsis due to query/context decoupling; MCTest’s multi-sentence narrative reasoning; SQuAD’s single-sentence answerability despite high readability grade level; QA4MRE’s broad skill demands).
- Useful qualitative insight: readability does not determine question difficulty. The empirical finding of only weak correlations between readability and skill counts, plus the clear examples (e.g., MCTest easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer), helps decouple two often-conflated axes of difficulty. This is valuable for dataset design and for interpreting system progress.
- Practical implications for dataset construction and system development. The discussion on genre effects, global vs. local coherence, and curriculum/transfer learning provides concrete guidance to dataset builders and system designers. The release of annotations should facilitate targeted error analysis and curriculum construction.
- Solid, if limited, annotation reliability. Reporting 90.1% agreement suggests that the skill taxonomy can be applied consistently, which is encouraging for community adoption.

Weaknesses
- Difficulty operationalization is under-validated. The core assumption that “number of required skills” approximates question difficulty is plausible but untested. There is no validation against human difficulty (e.g., accuracy, response time) or system difficulty (e.g., model error rates). Without such evidence, it is hard to judge whether the metric predicts what practitioners care about.
- Limited sampling and potential selection biases. Only 100 questions per dataset (dev/gold splits) and dataset-specific filters (e.g., NewsQA exclusions) may substantially affect the reported distributions (including “nonsense” rates) and correlations. The paper references Chen et al.’s sample sizes but does not analyze sensitivity to sampling or report confidence intervals.
- Annotation protocol may bias both skill counts and readability. Annotators saw the gold answer, selected the supporting sentences, and then annotated skills. This collapses retrieval and localization into a given and introduces a circularity: “necessary sentences” are defined post hoc, and readability is computed on those sentences rather than the actual context the system must process. This likely weakens or distorts correlations with readability and underestimates difficulty dimensions related to evidence selection.
- Agreement metrics are insufficiently reported. A single overall agreement number (on 62 questions) is not enough for a multi-label, multi-class setting with subtle distinctions such as bridging vs. elaboration. No per-skill Cohen’s kappa/alpha, confusion analysis, or adjudication process details are provided. Given known ambiguity between these categories, reliability needs deeper reporting.
- Narrow readability modeling and limited statistics. Readability is measured with a small set of features (largely lexical/syntactic and a traditional index) and then correlated via Pearson’s r with limited discussion of statistical significance, confidence intervals, or mixed-effects modeling to control for dataset/genre confounds. Discourse/coherence features (e.g., entity grid, connectives, Coh-Metrix) could be more informative for RC difficulty.
- Incomplete coverage and mixed criteria for dataset inclusion. Excluding CNN/DailyMail and CBTest on grounds of noise/task mismatch is understandable, but it weakens the generality of the conclusions; even a smaller, hand-cleaned slice would help test whether the methodology scales to cloze/anonymized settings.

Questions to authors
- How were annotators trained and calibrated? Please provide the annotation manual, examples for challenging distinctions (bridging vs. elaboration vs. coreference), and details of adjudication. What was the average time per item?
- Can you report per-skill agreement (e.g., Cohen’s kappa or Krippendorff’s alpha), a confusion matrix among related skills, and agreement on “nonsense” labeling? How often did annotators disagree on supporting sentences?
- How robust are the conclusions if readability is computed on entire contexts (or passages within a fixed window around answers) rather than only on selected sentences?
- Do the skill counts (or specific skills) predict system difficulty? For example, do baseline neural/feature-based models show higher error rates on items with more skills or with specific skill types (e.g., bridging+ellipsis)?
- Did you test correlations within each dataset (controlling for genre) and report significance? Mixed-effects or partial correlations might separate dataset-level from item-level effects.
- How often can questions be answered without reading the context (world knowledge or question-answer lexical overlap)? How did you treat such items in the analysis?

Overall, this paper addresses an important gap—better, theory-informed metrics for RC dataset quality—and provides useful annotations and insights. Strengthening the validation of the proposed difficulty metric, expanding reliability and statistical reporting, and mitigating sampling/annotation biases would substantially increase its impact. I recommend acceptance if the authors can address the validity and reporting concerns; otherwise, it remains a valuable workshop-level or short paper contribution.
