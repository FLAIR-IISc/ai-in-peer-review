Summary
This paper proposes two complementary classes of metrics to assess the quality of reading comprehension (RC) datasets: (a) prerequisite skills required to answer questions (a refined, 13-category taxonomy grounded in textual entailment and psycholinguistics), and (b) readability metrics (lexical/syntactic/traditional features). The authors annotate 100 questions each from six datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA), analyze frequency and co-occurrence of skills, compute readability on sentences deemed necessary to answer, and study correlations between “question difficulty” (proxied by number of required skills) and readability. They find only weak correlations, arguing that reading ease does not directly predict question difficulty, and provide dataset-level observations (e.g., MCTest is easy to read yet comparatively hard to answer).

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A pragmatic, theory-informed taxonomy and annotation scheme for prerequisite skills in RC, reorganizing “knowledge reasoning” into bridging/elaboration/ellipsis/meta-knowledge based on entailment and comprehension theory.
- A comparative analysis of six RC datasets along the skill taxonomy and readability features, with publicly released annotations, yielding actionable qualitative insights (e.g., prevalence of single-sentence answerability in SQuAD, multi-sentence reasoning in MCTest).
- An empirical finding that readability and question difficulty (as operationalized) are only weakly correlated, challenging assumptions that “hard text” implies “hard RC.”

Strengths
- Conceptual grounding and unification: The reorganization of skills explicitly connects RC phenomena to well-established frameworks (C–I model, bridging vs. elaboration, RTE phenomena). This alignment increases interpretability and potential reuse across RC and TE communities.
- Cross-dataset, apples-to-apples analysis: Annotating the same skill inventory across six datasets with different task formats (including MC and span extraction) is valuable for dataset diagnostics. The findings (e.g., higher ellipsis in WDW, higher multi-clause structures and punctuation in QA4MRE) are plausible and informative for dataset selection and system evaluation.
- Methodological clarity about scope: The paper is explicit that annotators saw question, context, and answer and focused on contextual entailment, not retrieval or candidate selection. This constrained scope allows cleaner measurement of reasoning demands, avoiding confounds from search UI or answer extraction heuristics.
- Practical implications: The curriculum/transfer framing (progressing from easy-to-read/easy-to-answer to hard-to-read/hard-to-answer) and the genre discussion (narrative vs. expository) are thoughtful and actionable for dataset construction and for planning system evaluation.
- Resource contribution: Releasing the annotations is a concrete and useful artifact for the community, enabling follow-up studies (e.g., skill-aware training, error analyses).

Weaknesses
- Operationalization of “difficulty” is thin: Equating difficulty with the count of required skills assumes all skills have equal weight and independent contributions. This likely misrepresents cognitive and algorithmic difficulty (e.g., coreference + bridging may be easier than complex logical reasoning alone; interaction effects matter). No validation against human difficulty (time/accuracy) or system error profiles is provided.
- Limited and heterogeneous sampling: 100 questions per dataset is small given dataset diversity, and selection protocols differ across datasets (e.g., MCTest tasks vs. SQuAD paragraphs; NewsQA filtering by meta-tags), potentially inducing bias. The relatively high “nonsense” rate in some datasets may reflect sampling and filtering choices more than inherent quality.
- Agreement reporting is insufficient: A single 90.1% agreement over 62 questions is reported without specifying the measure (percent agreement vs. multi-label Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa) or per-skill reliability. Given the subtle boundary between bridging and elaboration (and meta-knowledge), robust inter-annotator agreement statistics per label are crucial to trust the taxonomy’s operationalization.
- Readability analysis may be confounded: Readability features are computed only on “sentences needed to answer,” not on full contexts. This conflates annotator selection behavior with textual difficulty and likely compresses variance (especially for long contexts like QA4MRE). Correlating Flesch–Kincaid with skill counts without controlling for sentence length, question type, or dataset yields weak correlations that may be driven by between-dataset differences; appropriate within-dataset mixed-effects models or partial correlations are needed. Statistical significance and CIs for correlations are not reported.
- Sentence distance metric is unconventional: The “sum of distances of any two sentences” for multi-sentence questions is nonstandard and hard to interpret; the typical choice is the span width (max index – min index). This choice may inflate distances and complicate comparisons across datasets.
- Scope limitation not linked to systems: While the paper motivates system development use, there is no empirical link between the skill/readability diagnostics and actual model performance or error patterns on the same items. Even a small analysis correlating skill counts with baseline/system accuracy would strengthen claims of utility.

Questions to authors
- How were annotator guidelines operationalizing bridging vs. elaboration vs. ellipsis written, and what concrete decision rules/examples were provided? Please report per-skill inter-annotator agreement (e.g., multi-label kappa) and adjudication procedures.
- Why was “difficulty” defined as a simple count? Did you explore weighted counts or models capturing interactions (e.g., logistic regression predicting “hard” based on skill indicators)? Any correlation with human difficulty (time, error) or baseline model accuracy?
- For the correlation analyses, did you compute within-dataset correlations (controlling for dataset as a random effect) and report confidence intervals? Are the weak correlations statistically significant? Could you share results when computing readability on full contexts instead of only the selected sentences?
- How sensitive are your findings to the choice of readability metrics? Flesch–Kincaid is coarse and style-sensitive; did you try alternatives (e.g., dependency length, surprisal from language models), or control for parse errors across genres?
- On “nonsense” labeling: what were the precise criteria? How often did annotators disagree on “nonsense,” and how did you resolve items solvable by world knowledge without context?
- Can you clarify the sentence distance calculation and justify the chosen formula? How do results change if using span width instead?
- Will you release code for feature extraction and the full annotation protocol to ensure replicability?

Overall, this is a timely and useful diagnostic study with thoughtful grounding, but the empirical conclusions would be stronger with more rigorous reliability reporting, tighter statistical controls, and a more defensible operationalization of difficulty.
