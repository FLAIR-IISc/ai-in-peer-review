Contributions:
- Proposes two metric classes for RC datasets: prerequisite skills and readability.
- Refines prerequisite-skill taxonomy grounded in textual entailment and human comprehension research.
- Systematically analyzes six RC datasets and releases annotations, showing weak correlation between question difficulty and readability.

Strengths:
- Provides a theoretically grounded alternative to superficial question-type categorizations, linked to psychological research.
- Thoughtful reclassification of skills (e.g., splitting knowledge reasoning into bridging and elaboration) addressing prior limitations.
- Comprehensive empirical annotation and analysis across six diverse RC datasets, with a compelling MCTest case study.
- Public release of annotations as a useful community resource.

Weaknesses:
- Annotation method cannot evaluate the competence of selecting necessary sentences, a critical RC capability.
- No empirical evidence showing the proposed metrics correlate with system (or human) performance.
- Insufficient analysis explaining why MCTest is easy-to-read but hard-to-answer beyond noting its narrative genre.
- Inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) reported without contextualization or detailed disagreement resolution procedures.
- Lacks concrete guidance/examples for how metrics should inform dataset construction or step-by-step dataset design.

Questions to authors:
- How could the methodology be extended to evaluate sentence-selection competence?
- Do the prerequisite-skill metrics correlate with system or human performance on the datasets?
- Can you elaborate why narrative genre in MCTest increases required prerequisite skills despite high readability?
- How should researchers use these annotations to improve dataset quality during construction, beyond high-level discussion?
- How can NewsQA’s metadata-based filtering of nonsense questions be integrated into new dataset construction?
