

# Peer Review: "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two complementary metrics: (1) prerequisite skills (cognitive demands required to answer questions) and (2) readability (linguistic complexity of text). The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and demonstrate that readability and question difficulty exhibit only weak correlation, suggesting it's possible to create datasets that are easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer. The main contributions are: (1) a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets, (2) a refined classification of prerequisite skills based on psychological and NLP insights, and (3) annotated datasets for community use.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical grounding and conceptual clarity**: The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation by connecting RC evaluation to psychological theories of text comprehension (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). The reorganization of prerequisite skills into a coherent taxonomy (bridging vs. elaboration, retrieved vs. generated inferences) represents a significant improvement over prior work. This taxonomy is well-motivated by both NLP research (Dagan et al.) and cognitive psychology (Kintsch), addressing a key gap in the literature where previous RC skill classifications were often ad hoc.

2. **Important empirical insight**: The finding that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated (r=0.025-0.416) is a significant contribution that challenges common assumptions in the field. The MCTest example (easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer) is particularly compelling evidence that these dimensions should be evaluated separately. This insight has direct implications for dataset construction, as it shows that simply making texts more complex won't necessarily create more challenging RC tasks.

3. **Practical utility**: The authors have made their dataset annotations publicly available, creating a valuable resource for the community. Their detailed analysis of six datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, etc.) provides concrete insights into the characteristics of existing RC benchmarks, helping researchers select appropriate datasets for different development stages and identify weaknesses in current benchmarks.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited discussion of practical impact**: While the paper identifies a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, it doesn't sufficiently explain how this insight should change RC system development practices. The authors briefly mention curriculum learning but don't provide specific guidance on how to use their metrics to design better training pipelines or evaluate systems. Without concrete examples of how researchers should apply this framework, its practical value remains somewhat abstract.

2. **Annotation methodology limitations**: The paper acknowledges that their methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read," yet they don't adequately address how this limitation affects their conclusions. The sentence distance metric (Table 6) shows that MCTest requires reading multiple sentences more frequently than other datasets, but the authors don't explore how this relates to the actual performance of RC systems. Without addressing this gap, the utility of their framework for system evaluation is limited.

3. **Insufficient comparison to existing evaluation approaches**: The paper criticizes existing RC dataset evaluations as "superficial" but doesn't provide a thorough comparison of their framework with other existing evaluation methodologies (e.g., question type classification, answer type analysis). A more detailed comparison would strengthen their argument for the necessity of their approach and clarify its advantages.

4. **Lack of systematic validation**: The paper relies on human annotation of skills, but doesn't provide enough detail about the annotation process, inter-annotator agreement beyond the 90.1% figure, or how they handled disagreements on ambiguous cases (e.g., "nonsense" questions). Without this detail, it's difficult to assess the reliability of their findings.

5. **Overlooked dataset characteristics**: The authors note that NewsQA includes meta-information about question quality, but they don't explore how this might affect their analysis. The fact that NewsQA has no "nonsense" questions (Table 2) could be a significant dataset characteristic that warrants deeper analysis rather than being treated as an afterthought.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you integrate your metric framework into the machine learning pipeline for RC systems? Could you provide a specific example of how a system developer might use these metrics to improve model performance?

2. The paper states that their methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read." How would you address this limitation in future work, and what would be the implications for evaluating RC systems?

3. The weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is a key finding, but the paper doesn't discuss how this might vary across different RC task formulations (e.g., multiple-choice vs. open-ended). Would you expect this relationship to hold across different question types?

4. How do you account for the fact that some datasets (like Who-did-What) were automatically generated, potentially introducing biases that might affect your metric calculations?
