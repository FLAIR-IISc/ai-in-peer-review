- Proposes an evaluation framework for RC datasets using two metrics: prerequisite skills and readability.  
- Annotates six RC datasets with these metrics and releases the annotations for community use.  
- Contributions claimed: a theoretically grounded framework, a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills, and public annotated datasets.  

- Strength: Strong theoretical grounding linking RC evaluation to cognitive psychology and prior NLP work, producing a coherent skills taxonomy.  
- Strength: Empirical finding that readability and question difficulty are weakly correlated, showing texts can be easy-to-read but hard-to-answer.  
- Strength: Practical utility through detailed analysis of six datasets and publicly available annotations for researchers.  

- Weakness: Limited discussion of how the metrics should concretely change RC system development or training practices.  
- Weakness: Annotation methodology can't evaluate sentence-selection competence and the impact of this limitation on conclusions is underexplored.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to existing evaluation approaches (e.g., question/answer type analyses) to justify the new framework.  
- Weakness: Lack of systematic validation details—annotation process, deeper inter-annotator agreement reporting, and handling of ambiguous cases.  
- Weakness: Overlooks dataset-specific characteristics (e.g., NewsQA's lack of "nonsense" questions or auto-generated datasets) that could affect analyses.  

- Q: How should developers integrate these metrics into ML pipelines—any concrete example for improving models?  
- Q: How will you address the framework's inability to evaluate sentence-selection competence in future work and what are the implications?  
- Q: Does the weak readability–difficulty correlation hold across different RC formats (multiple-choice vs. open-ended)?  
- Q: How do you account for biases from automatically generated datasets (e.g., Who-did-What) that might skew metric calculations?
