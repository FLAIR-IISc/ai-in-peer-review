

# Peer Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes two complementary classes of metrics for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills (covering cognitive requirements for answering questions) and readability (measuring linguistic complexity of text). The authors apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets, revealing a weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty. The main contributions as I see them are:

1. **Empirical demonstration of the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty** - This is the most significant contribution, as it challenges the common assumption that difficult-to-read texts produce difficult-to-answer questions, providing crucial insights for dataset construction.

2. **Theoretical refinement of prerequisite skills classification** - The authors build on Sugawara et al.'s work but provide a more comprehensive and theoretically grounded classification based on textual entailment and human text comprehension research, addressing previous work's lack of theoretical foundation.

3. **Publicly available dataset annotations** - The authors make their annotations of six RC datasets publicly available, creating a valuable resource for the community.

## Strengths
1. **Addresses a critical gap in RC research**: The paper correctly identifies that current RC datasets are evaluated only on superficial metrics (question types, answer types) without meaningful analysis of what makes questions difficult. This is a significant problem as Chen et al. (2016) showed many questions in existing datasets may not properly test RC systems. The authors' metrics provide a more nuanced way to evaluate dataset quality.

2. **Strong theoretical grounding**: The authors successfully integrate insights from psychology (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and NLP (textual entailment research) to develop their metrics. This theoretical foundation is much stronger than previous work on prerequisite skills, which the authors rightly identify as having "provisional categorization" and "weak theoretical background."

3. **Practical implications for dataset construction**: The paper's key finding—that it's possible to create an RC dataset that is easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer (exemplified by MCTest)—has direct implications for dataset design. This insight helps researchers understand that readability and question difficulty are separate dimensions that should be considered independently when constructing datasets.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient justification for the revised skill classification**: While the authors claim to have "revised" the previous classification of prerequisite skills, they don't adequately demonstrate why their 13-skill taxonomy is superior to previous work. The paper states that they "reorganized the category of knowledge reasoning in terms of textual entailment and human text comprehension," but doesn't provide evidence that this organization is more comprehensive, less redundant, or more useful for RC than previous classifications.

2. **Methodological limitations not fully addressed**: The authors explicitly acknowledge their methodology "cannot evaluate the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read" (Section 6). This is a fundamental limitation since RC systems must both locate relevant information and understand it. The paper doesn't adequately address how this limitation affects the utility of their metrics for evaluating RC systems, nor does it propose how to overcome this limitation.

3. **Lack of practical implementation guidance**: While the authors claim their metrics "will help researchers to develop a method for the step-by-step construction of better RC datasets," the paper provides no concrete methodology for how to use these metrics in practice. The discussion in Section 6 is vague about how to "utilize the two classes of metrics for system development" beyond the general idea of "curriculum learning."

4. **Inadequate handling of "nonsense" questions**: The paper identifies that some datasets (MS MARCO, Who-did-What) contain nonsense questions, but doesn't provide a clear solution for identifying or handling such questions in future dataset construction. This is particularly concerning given Chen et al. (2016) highlighted this as a significant issue in RC datasets.

## Questions to Authors
1. The authors claim their revised classification of prerequisite skills is "comprehensive but not overly specific." Can they provide evidence that their 13-skill taxonomy covers all necessary skills without being redundant? How did they determine that these 13 skills are sufficient for evaluating RC datasets?

2. The paper states that the readability metrics "do not necessarily correlate with those for RC systems." How might the authors propose to develop metrics that are more directly relevant to RC systems rather than human readability?

3. Given the limitation that their methodology doesn't evaluate "the competence of selecting sentences that need to be read," how do the authors propose to address this gap in future work? Would they suggest a separate metric for this aspect of RC?
