- Proposes a framework evaluating RC datasets by prerequisite skills (cognitive demands) and readability (text complexity).
- Reorganizes prerequisite-skills taxonomy based on textual entailment and human comprehension literature.
- Analyzes six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) using the refined taxonomy.
- Key empirical finding: readability weakly correlates with question difficulty (Pearson r = 0.025–0.416), showing easy-to-read yet hard-to-answer datasets (e.g., MCTest).
- Strength: Fills a gap by moving evaluation from superficial question types to cognitive-demand-based assessment.
- Strength: Robust cross-dataset empirical analysis with visual evidence supporting weak readability–difficulty correlation.
- Strength: Taxonomy revision is well-grounded in psychology and improves on prior work (Sugawara et al.).
- Weakness: Annotation method cannot evaluate competence in selecting necessary sentences, omitting a core RC skill.
- Weakness: No concrete guidance or examples for creating datasets with targeted properties (e.g., easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer).
- Weakness: Small sample size (100 questions per dataset) may not represent large-scale dataset complexity, especially technical sets like QA4MRE.
- Question: How will you address the sentence-selection competence limitation and extend the framework to evaluate it?
- Question: Can you provide concrete examples showing how your metrics would guide constructing datasets with specific properties?
