

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary
This paper introduces two classes of metrics for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: "prerequisite skills" and "readability." The authors refine the prerequisite skills classification based on psychological research on human text comprehension and textual entailment, then apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA). Their analysis demonstrates a weak correlation between the readability of context texts and the difficulty of questions, suggesting it's possible to create RC datasets that are easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer.

## Main Contributions
1. A refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, organized based on textual entailment and human text comprehension research (moving beyond previous ad-hoc categorizations).
2. Comprehensive annotation of six RC datasets using their metrics, revealing significant insights about the relationship between question difficulty and text readability.
3. Public release of their annotation results, providing a valuable resource for future RC dataset construction and system development.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical grounding and refinement of prerequisite skills**: The authors make a strong contribution by organizing knowledge reasoning skills based on established psychological frameworks (Kintsch, 1993; McNamara and Magliano, 2009) and textual entailment research (Dagan et al., 2013). This moves beyond previous work (Sugawara et al., 2017) that had a weak theoretical foundation and provisional categorization. Their reorganization of skills into bridging vs. elaboration inferences, and their distinction between retrieved vs. generated inferences, provides a more comprehensive and theoretically sound framework for analyzing RC question difficulty.

2. **Important empirical insight**: The paper's most significant contribution is demonstrating the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty (Pearson's r = 0.025-0.416). This directly addresses a critical gap in the field—most RC datasets are evaluated only with superficial metrics, but this analysis shows that text readability does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty. The MCTest example (easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer) is particularly compelling evidence for this insight, which has important implications for dataset construction and system development.

3. **Practical value for the community**: The authors' public release of their annotation results provides an immediate, tangible resource for the RC community. Their analysis of six datasets reveals meaningful differences in dataset characteristics (e.g., QA4MRE being the most difficult, MS MARCO the easiest), which can guide researchers in selecting appropriate datasets for specific development goals.

## Weaknesses

1. **Methodological limitation not adequately addressed**: The authors explicitly acknowledge their methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting necessary sentences" (Section 6). This is a critical limitation because selecting relevant sentences from a passage is a fundamental RC skill. Their analysis of sentence distance (Table 6) suggests this is a significant factor (e.g., QA4MRE requires longer distances between relevant sentences), yet they fail to address how this limitation impacts their conclusions about question difficulty. Without addressing this, their metrics only capture part of the RC process.

2. **Annotation procedure and reliability concerns**: The paper states inter-annotator agreement was 90.1%, but provides no details on how disagreements were resolved. The authors admit "there were questions for which there were disagreements regarding the decision of nonsense" and that "annotators reported that the prerequisite skills did not easily treat questions whose answer was 'none of the above'." Without addressing how these disagreements were handled, the reliability of their annotation results is questionable.

3. **Lack of concrete guidance for application**: While the authors mention using their metrics for "curriculum learning" (Section 6), they provide no concrete examples of how researchers could use these metrics to guide dataset construction or system development. The paper describes what they found but doesn't sufficiently explain how the community can use these findings to build better RC systems or datasets.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper acknowledges that their methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting necessary sentences." How would you address this limitation in future work, and how might it impact the interpretation of your results about question difficulty?

2. The authors mention "nonsense" questions as a problematic annotation category. Could you provide more details on how you handled disagreements about whether a question was nonsensical versus solvable with external knowledge? Did you have a specific definition for "nonsense" that guided your annotation?

3. The paper discusses how their metrics could be used for curriculum learning, but provides no concrete examples. Could you elaborate on a specific scenario where these metrics would guide the step-by-step development of an RC system, including the specific datasets that would be used at each stage?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a theoretically grounded framework for evaluating RC datasets beyond superficial metrics. The refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills is well-justified and represents a significant improvement over previous work. The key insight about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty is important for the field and has clear implications for dataset construction.

However, the paper would be substantially strengthened by addressing the methodological limitation regarding sentence selection competence, providing more details about the annotation process and reliability, and offering concrete guidance for how the metrics can be applied in practice. As it stands, the paper is a good contribution but would benefit from more thorough discussion of its limitations and clearer pathways for application.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The paper's insights are valuable and timely, and with the suggested clarifications, it will provide a strong foundation for future work in RC dataset evaluation.
