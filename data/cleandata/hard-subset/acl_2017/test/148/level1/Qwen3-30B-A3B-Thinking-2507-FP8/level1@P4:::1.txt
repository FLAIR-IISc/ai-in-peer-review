

# Review of "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability"

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes two classes of metrics for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The primary contributions are:
1. A refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC, organized according to textual entailment phenomena and human text comprehension research, addressing limitations in prior work.
2. A systematic analysis of six RC datasets using these metrics, demonstrating a weak correlation between question difficulty (measured by prerequisite skills) and text readability.
3. The publicly available annotation of these datasets with the proposed metrics, providing a valuable resource for the community.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a significant gap in RC research by proposing metrics that move beyond superficial question-type categorizations. The authors ground their methodology in psychological research (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch), providing a theoretically sound framework that connects to both human comprehension and system development. This theoretical foundation is a substantial improvement over prior ad-hoc categorizations.

2. The refined classification of prerequisite skills is well-motivated and addresses clear limitations in prior work. The authors carefully explain their modifications to Sugawara et al.'s classification, particularly the reorganization of knowledge reasoning into bridging and elaboration based on established psychological distinctions. This provides a more comprehensive and theoretically grounded framework for analyzing RC tasks.

3. The empirical analysis is comprehensive and well-executed. The authors systematically annotate six diverse RC datasets, providing clear evidence of the weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability. Their analysis of MCTest as an example of an easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer dataset is particularly compelling and directly challenges common assumptions in the field.

4. The publicly available dataset annotations constitute a valuable resource for the community. The authors make their annotations available, enabling future research on dataset construction and system development. This practical contribution significantly enhances the paper's impact.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper does not sufficiently address the methodological limitation that their annotation approach cannot evaluate the "competence of selecting necessary sentences" (Section 6), which is a critical aspect of RC. This is a significant limitation as RC systems must first identify relevant text passages before understanding them. Without addressing this, the validity of their analysis is questionable.

2. The paper lacks evidence that their metrics correlate with actual system performance. While they discuss how the metrics could inform system development (Section 6), they do not provide empirical evidence that these metrics predict system performance on the datasets. This limits the practical utility of their framework for system evaluation.

3. The paper doesn't thoroughly explain why MCTest is "easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer" beyond noting its narrative genre. The connection between narrative genre and the required prerequisite skills needs more detailed analysis. The paper states MCTest is "easy-to-read" based on readability metrics, but doesn't sufficiently explain why its narrative structure leads to higher skill requirements.

4. The inter-annotator agreement (90.1%) is reported but not contextualized. Without comparison to other studies or discussion of what constitutes acceptable agreement for these metrics, it's difficult to assess whether the annotations are reliable. The paper also doesn't discuss how they resolved disagreements beyond mentioning "nonsense" questions.

5. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how their metrics could directly inform dataset construction. While they mention "step-by-step construction of better RC datasets" in the abstract, they don't provide specific guidelines or examples of how the metrics could be used to improve dataset quality during creation.

## Questions to Authors
1. How could your methodology be extended to evaluate the "competence of selecting necessary sentences" (mentioned in Section 6), which you acknowledge is a limitation of your current approach?

2. Can you provide evidence that your prerequisite skills metrics correlate with actual system performance on the annotated datasets, or at least with human performance?

3. The paper states that MCTest is "easy-to-read" based on readability metrics, but doesn't fully explain how the narrative genre leads to higher skill requirements. Could you elaborate on this connection?

4. How would you recommend researchers use your annotations to improve the quality of new RC datasets during their construction phase, beyond the general discussion in Section 6?

5. The paper mentions that NewsQA provides "meta information on the reliability of the questions," which enabled them to avoid nonsense questions. How could this approach be integrated into the construction of new datasets?
