

This paper proposes a novel framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two complementary metrics: prerequisite skills (cognitive demands to answer questions) and readability (text complexity). The authors analyze six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, NewsQA) using their refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills, which they reorganize based on textual entailment and human text comprehension research. Their key contribution is demonstrating that readability does not directly correlate with question difficulty, showing that it's possible to create datasets that are easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer (e.g., MCTest).

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a critical gap in RC evaluation—most datasets are assessed with superficial metrics like question type rather than cognitive demands. Their two-class framework provides a much-needed nuanced evaluation approach.
2. The empirical analysis across six diverse datasets robustly demonstrates the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty (Pearson's r=0.025-0.416), with compelling visual evidence in Figures 3-4.
3. The revised prerequisite skills taxonomy is well-grounded in psychology literature and addresses limitations in prior work by Sugawara et al., providing a more comprehensive classification.

Weaknesses:
1. The annotation methodology cannot evaluate "the competence of selecting necessary sentences" (Section 6), which is a significant limitation for RC evaluation as it misses a key component of the reading comprehension process.
2. The paper doesn't provide concrete guidance on how to actually create datasets with the desired properties (easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer), limiting practical utility for dataset creators.
3. The sample size of 100 questions per dataset may not adequately represent the full complexity of these large-scale datasets, particularly for datasets like QA4MRE with technical content.

Questions to Authors:
How would you address the limitation regarding sentence selection competence in your framework, and what modifications would be needed to evaluate this aspect? Could you provide examples of how your metrics would guide the creation of a dataset with specific properties (e.g., easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer)?
