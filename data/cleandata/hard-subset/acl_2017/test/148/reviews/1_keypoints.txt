- Strength: The paper attempts to connect text readability for humans with machine reading comprehension.  
- Weakness: The paper’s goal is unclear; reviewer still confused after multiple readings.  
- Weakness: Ambiguity whether the focus is machine comprehension or human text readability.  
- Weakness: Important prior work in the readability literature is missing.  
- Weakness: Section 2.2 contains unrelated theoretical discussion.  
- Weakness: The paper tries to answer too many questions at once, weakening its contributions.  
- Weakness/claim: The conclusion that readability does not affect QA difficulty depends on the features/methods used (e.g., POS/dependency features).  
- Editorial: The title should clarify that the work targets machine comprehension, not human reading comprehension.  
- Editorial: The manuscript needs English proofreading; there are grammatical omissions and errors.  
- Methodology question: It is unclear how datasets were annotated with "metrics."  
- Conceptual: The paper conflates machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, which are distinct areas.  
- Conceptual: “Readability of text” is not equivalent to “difficulty of reading content” (cite DuBay 2004).  
- Suggestion: Add clear distinctions and pointers differentiating the work from human-question readability studies.  
- Specific fix: When claiming examples show readability doesn’t correlate with question difficulty, specify “for machine comprehension.”  
- Section 3.1 concern: Unclear whether referenced skills/applications are for humans or machines, and why human-focused citations are used for machine claims.  
- Annotation concern: Clarify how many questions annotators labeled and whether annotators were instructed to judge questions from a machine vs. human perspective.
