**Strengths:**

This article brings together two distinct fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts. This interdisciplinary approach has the potential to yield valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary goal of the paper is unclear, requiring multiple readings to grasp the main idea. The article's focus oscillates between machine comprehension and text readability for humans, creating ambiguity. Furthermore, the paper overlooks significant work in the readability field. Section 2.2 contains an unrelated discussion of theoretical topics, which detracts from the main argument. The paper attempts to address multiple questions simultaneously, weakening its overall impact. For instance, the impact of text readability on Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets should be analyzed separately from prerequisite skills.

**General Discussion:**

The title is ambiguous and should be revised to clarify that it refers to machine comprehension of text, rather than human reading comprehension. The terms "reading comprehension" and "readability" typically connote human reading comprehension. The authors' claim that "dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is dependent on the methods and features used for answer detection, such as POS/dependency parse features.

The paper requires thorough proofreading to address English errors and omissions, such as the incomplete sentence "the question is easy to solve simply look..." on page 1. The authors should clarify how they annotated datasets with "metrics." The paper often conflates machine reading comprehension of texts and human reading comprehension of texts, which, although related, are distinct areas.

The authors should distinguish between "readability of text" and "difficulty of reading contents" (DuBay, 2004). To reduce ambiguity, the authors could provide more explicit pointers to differentiate their work from readability of questions for humans. For example, on page 1, the sentence "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" should be revised to "for machine comprehension."

In Section 3.1, the authors should clarify whether they are referring to skills for humans or machines. If the focus is on machines, the authors should justify citing papers on human skills and explain how they relate to machines. Additionally, the authors should provide details on the annotation process, including the number of questions annotators had to annotate and whether the annotators were instructed to keep machines in mind when annotating questions.
