**Summary of the Paper**

The paper "Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability" proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets, including MCTest and SQuAD, and analyze the characteristics of the datasets according to each metric and the correlation between the two classes.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose two classes of evaluation metrics for RC datasets: prerequisite skills and readability.
2. They refine the prerequisite skills for RC, organizing thirteen skills, including knowledge reasoning, which is divided into several metrics according to the distinctions of human inferences.
3. They annotate six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics and make the results publicly available.

**Strengths**

1. The paper brings together two fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts.
2. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the RC datasets using the proposed metrics.
3. The paper highlights the importance of evaluating RC datasets using multiple metrics, rather than just simple accuracy.

**Weaknesses**

1. The goal of the paper is not entirely clear, and the text is highly ambiguous, making it difficult to understand what the authors are trying to convey.
2. The paper misses important work in the readability field and includes unrelated discussions of theoretical topics.
3. The authors try to answer too many questions at once, making the paper weak.
4. The paper mixes machine reading comprehension of texts and human reading comprehension of texts, which are distinct areas.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you annotate datasets with "metrics"? Can you provide more details on the annotation process?
2. How many questions did the annotators have to annotate, and were they clear that they should annotate the questions keeping in mind machines and not people?
3. Can you provide more pointers distinguishing your work from readability of questions for humans, as the paper is highly ambiguous?
4. How sure are you that the papers you cite for humans are also referring to machines, and can you provide more justification for citing them in the context of machine comprehension?

**Additional Comments**

1. The title is a bit ambiguous and could be clarified to refer specifically to machine comprehension of text.
2. The paper could benefit from proofreading to correct omissions and improve clarity.
3. The authors should be more precise in their terminology, for example, "readability of text" is not the same as "difficulty of reading contents."
4. The paper could be strengthened by providing more analysis on the correlation between the proposed metrics and the performance of RC systems.
