This paper presents an analysis of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors refine the prerequisite skills proposed by Sugawara et al. (2017) and Sugawara and Aizawa (2016) using insights from psychology research, and leverage a wide range of linguistic features proposed by Vajjala and Meurers (2012) to evaluate readability. They annotate six existing RC datasets with these metrics and present the results, highlighting the characteristics of the datasets and providing a valuable guide for the construction of new datasets and the development of RC systems.

The main contributions of this paper are: (1) the adoption of two classes of evaluation metrics to analyze RC datasets, (2) the refinement of prerequisite skills for RC, and (3) the annotation of six existing RC datasets with these metrics.

Strengths of the paper include:

1. The integration of two fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts.
2. The presentation of a comprehensive analysis of RC datasets using two classes of evaluation metrics.
3. The provision of a valuable guide for the construction of new datasets and the development of RC systems.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

1. The goal of the paper is not entirely clear, and the article is highly ambiguous about what it talks about - machine comprehension or text readability for humans.
2. The paper misses important work in the readability field.
3. Section 2.2 has completely unrelated discussion of theoretical topics.
4. The paper tries to answer too many questions at the same time, making itself quite weak.
5. The title is ambiguous and should be clarified to refer to machine comprehension of text.
6. The paper needs to proofread the English, as there are some important omissions.
7. The paper mixes machine reading comprehension of texts and human reading comprehension of texts, which are somewhat similar but also quite different.
8. The paper should provide more pointers distinguishing its work from readability of questions for humans.

Questions to Authors:

1. How do you annotate datasets with "metrics"? Can you provide more details on the annotation process?
2. Are you referring to prerequisite skills for humans or for machines? If for machines, why are you citing papers for humans, and how sure are you they are referring to machines too?
3. How many questions did the annotators have to annotate? Were the annotators clear that they annotate the questions keeping in mind machines and not people?
4. Can you provide more information on the inter-annotator agreement and how you handled disagreements?
5. How do you plan to use the analysis in this study to construct a system that can be applied to multiple datasets?
