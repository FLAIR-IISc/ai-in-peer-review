**Strengths:**
This article brings together two distinct fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts. The intersection of these fields has the potential to yield valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary goal of the paper is not entirely clear, requiring multiple readings to grasp the main idea. The article suffers from ambiguity, making it difficult to discern whether it focuses on machine comprehension or text readability for humans. Furthermore, the authors have overlooked significant work in the readability field.

Section 2.2 contains a discussion of theoretical topics that seems unrelated to the rest of the paper. The paper attempts to address multiple questions simultaneously, which may weaken its overall impact. For instance, the question of whether text readability affects RC datasets could be analyzed separately from the prerequisite skills discussed in the paper.

**General Discussion:**
The title is somewhat ambiguous and could be clarified to specify that it refers to machine comprehension of text, rather than human reading comprehension. The terms "reading comprehension" and "readability" typically imply human reading comprehension.

The authors claim that their dataset analysis suggests that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty. However, this conclusion may depend on the methods and features used for answer detection, such as POS/dependency parse features.

The paper requires thorough proofreading, as there are several important omissions, including the phrase "the question is easy to solve simply look..." on page 1.

The authors should provide more information on how they annotate datasets with "metrics." The paper sometimes conflates machine reading comprehension of texts and human reading comprehension of texts, which, although related, are distinct areas.

The authors should note that "readability of text" is not equivalent to "difficulty of reading contents" (DuBay, 2004). To reduce ambiguity, the authors could provide more pointers to distinguish their work from readability of questions for humans. For example, on page 1, the sentence "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" could be clarified to specify "for machine comprehension."

In Section 3.1, it is unclear whether the authors are referring to skills for humans or machines. If the focus is on machines, it is unclear why papers on human skills are cited, and how relevant they are to machine comprehension. Additionally, the authors should provide more information on the annotation process, including the number of questions annotated and whether the annotators were clear about annotating questions with machines in mind.
