**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes two classes of evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors applied these metrics to six existing RC datasets and demonstrated their characteristics according to each metric and the correlation between the two classes. The study aims to investigate how the difficulties of "answering questions" and "reading text" relate in RC.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors adopt two classes of evaluation metrics for RC datasets, providing a more comprehensive analysis of the datasets' quality and performance.
2. The study revises the previous classification of prerequisite skills for RC, organizing knowledge reasoning in terms of entailment phenomena and human text comprehension in psychology.
3. The authors annotate six existing RC datasets with their organized metrics, making the results publicly available.

**Strengths**

1. The paper combines two fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts, providing a unique perspective on RC datasets.
2. The study provides a valuable guide for the construction of new datasets and the development of RC systems.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper's goal is not entirely clear, and the text is highly ambiguous, making it difficult to understand what the authors are discussing.
2. The article misses important work in the readability field and includes unrelated discussions of theoretical topics in Section 2.2.
3. The paper tries to answer too many questions at once, making it weak. Questions such as "does text readability have impact on RC datasets" should be analyzed separately from prerequisite skills.
4. The title is ambiguous and should be clarified to refer to machine comprehension of text, not human reading comprehension.
5. The paper needs to proofread the English, as there are important omissions and grammatical errors.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you annotate datasets with "metrics"? Can you provide more details on the annotation process?
2. Can you clarify whether you are referring to prerequisite skills for humans or machines in Section 3.1?
3. How many questions did the annotators have to annotate, and were they clear that they should annotate the questions keeping in mind machines, not people?
4. Can you provide more pointers distinguishing your work from readability of questions for humans, as the article is highly ambiguous?
