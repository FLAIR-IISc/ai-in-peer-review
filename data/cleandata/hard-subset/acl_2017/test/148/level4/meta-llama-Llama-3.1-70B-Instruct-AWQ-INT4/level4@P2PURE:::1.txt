- Strengths:
This article attempts to bridge two distinct fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts.

- Weaknesses:
The primary objective of this paper is unclear, requiring multiple readings to grasp the main idea, which still remains ambiguous.
The article lacks clarity in its focus, oscillating between machine comprehension and human text readability.
Important work in the readability field is overlooked.
Section 2.2 contains an unrelated discussion of theoretical topics.
The paper tries to address multiple questions simultaneously, weakening its overall impact. For instance, the question of whether text readability affects RC datasets should be analyzed separately from prerequisite skills.

- General Discussion:
The title is somewhat ambiguous and could be improved by specifying that it refers to machine comprehension of text, rather than human reading comprehension, as the terms "reading comprehension" and "readability" typically connote the latter.
The claim that "dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" may be dependent on the methods and features used for answer detection, such as POS/dependency parse features.
The paper requires thorough proofreading to correct errors and omissions, including the sentence "the question is easy to solve simply look..." on page 1.
The method of annotating datasets with "metrics" is unclear.
The paper conflates machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, which, despite some similarities, are distinct areas.
The concept of "readability of text" differs from "difficulty of reading contents." For reference, see DuBay, W.H. 2004. The Principles of Readability. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact information.
To reduce ambiguity, it would be beneficial to provide more distinctions between the work presented and readability of questions for humans. For example, on page 1, the sentence "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" could be clarified by adding "for machine comprehension."
In Section 3.1, it is unclear whether the skills referred to are for humans or machines. If the focus is on machines, it is questionable why papers on human skills are cited, and how relevant they are to machine comprehension.
The number of questions annotated by annotators and their understanding of the task (i.e., annotating for machines rather than humans) are unclear.
