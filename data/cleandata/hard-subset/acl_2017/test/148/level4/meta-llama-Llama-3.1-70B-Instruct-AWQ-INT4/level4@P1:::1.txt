**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper presents an analysis of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets and demonstrate the characteristics of each dataset according to these metrics. The main contributions of this paper are:

1. The adoption of two classes of evaluation metrics for RC datasets, which provides a more comprehensive understanding of the quality of these datasets.
2. The revision of the previous classification of prerequisite skills for RC, specifically the organization of skills related to knowledge reasoning in terms of entailment phenomena and human text comprehension.
3. The annotation of six existing RC datasets with the proposed metrics, which provides a valuable guide for the construction of new datasets and the development of RC systems.

**Strengths**

1. The paper brings together two fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts, providing a unique perspective on RC datasets.
2. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the RC datasets using the proposed metrics, which highlights the characteristics of each dataset.

**Weaknesses**

1. The goal of the paper is not entirely clear, and the text is often ambiguous, making it difficult to understand the main message.
2. The paper misses important work in the readability field, and the discussion of theoretical topics in Section 2.2 is unrelated to the rest of the paper.
3. The paper tries to answer too many questions at once, making it weak and difficult to follow.
4. The title is ambiguous and should be clarified to indicate that the paper refers to machine comprehension of text, not human reading comprehension.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify the goal of the paper and provide a more focused message?
2. How do you annotate datasets with "metrics"? Can you provide more details on the annotation process?
3. Can you distinguish your work from readability of questions for humans and provide more pointers to clarify the difference?
4. Are the prerequisite skills discussed in Section 3.1 referring to humans or machines? If machines, why are papers on human comprehension cited, and how sure are you that they are relevant to machines as well?
5. How many questions did the annotators have to annotate, and were they clear that they should annotate the questions with machines in mind, not people?
