- Strengths:
- The article successfully bridges two fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts.

- Weaknesses:
- The paper's primary objective is unclear. Despite reading the paper four times, its main focus remains ambiguous.
- The article lacks clarity in its discussion, frequently blurring the lines between machine comprehension and text readability for humans.
- The authors have overlooked significant work in the readability field.
- Section 2.2 contains an unrelated discussion of theoretical topics.
- The paper attempts to address multiple questions simultaneously, weakening its overall impact. For instance, the question of whether text readability affects RC datasets should be analyzed separately from the prerequisite skills discussed.
- Furthermore, the relationship between text readability and RC datasets is not fully explored, particularly in the context of machine comprehension.

- General Discussion:
- The title is somewhat ambiguous and could be clarified to specify that it refers to machine comprehension of text, rather than human reading comprehension. The terms "reading comprehension" and "readability" typically connote human reading.
- The authors claim that their "dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty." However, this conclusion may depend on the methods and features used for answer detection, such as POS/dependency parse features.
- The paper requires thorough proofreading to address errors and omissions in the English text, including the sentence "the question is easy to solve simply look..." on page 1.
- The authors should provide a clear explanation of how they annotated datasets with "metrics."
- The paper frequently conflates machine reading comprehension of texts and human reading comprehension of texts, which are distinct areas of research.
- The authors should note that "readability of text" and "difficulty of reading contents" are not interchangeable terms (DuBay, 2004). 
- To reduce ambiguity, the authors could provide more context distinguishing their work from readability research focused on humans. For example, on page 1, the sentence "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" could be clarified to specify "for machine comprehension."
- Section 3.1 lacks clarity regarding whether the skills discussed are relevant to humans or machines. If the focus is on machines, the authors should justify citing papers focused on human reading comprehension and explain how they apply to machines.
- The authors should provide details on the annotation process, including the number of questions annotated and whether annotators were instructed to consider machine comprehension rather than human understanding.
