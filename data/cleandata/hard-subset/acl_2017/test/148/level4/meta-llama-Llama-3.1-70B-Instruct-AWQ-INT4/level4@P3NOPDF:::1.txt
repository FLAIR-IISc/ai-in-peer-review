**Review of [Paper Title]**

**Strengths:**

This article attempts to bridge two distinct fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts. This interdisciplinary approach has the potential to contribute to a deeper understanding of the relationship between text readability and machine comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary goal of this paper is not clearly defined, leading to confusion about the main research question being addressed. After multiple readings, the reviewer still struggled to grasp the central argument. 

A significant issue with the article is its ambiguity regarding the focus on machine comprehension versus text readability for humans. This lack of clarity makes it challenging to discern the paper's main contribution. 

Furthermore, the article overlooks important work in the readability field, which weakens its foundation and credibility. 

Section 2.2 contains a discussion of theoretical topics that appears unrelated to the rest of the paper. This meandering section detracts from the overall coherence of the article.

The paper attempts to address multiple questions simultaneously, which dilutes its focus and impact. For instance, the question of whether text readability affects machine comprehension datasets would benefit from a separate, in-depth analysis, distinct from the discussion of prerequisite skills.

**General Discussion:**

The title of the paper is somewhat ambiguous and could be clarified to specify that it refers to machine comprehension of text, rather than human reading comprehension. The terms "reading comprehension" and "readability" typically connote human reading abilities.

The authors claim that their dataset analysis suggests that the readability of machine comprehension datasets does not directly impact question difficulty. However, this conclusion may be dependent on the specific methods and features used for answer detection, such as POS/dependency parse features.

The paper requires thorough proofreading to address errors and omissions in the English text. For example, the sentence "the question is easy to solve simply look..." on page 1 is incomplete.

The authors mention annotating datasets with "metrics," but it is unclear what this entails.

Throughout the paper, there is a conflation of machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, which, although related, are distinct areas of research. 

The authors appear to conflate "readability of text" with "difficulty of reading contents." However, these concepts are not interchangeable, as discussed in DuBay (2004).

To enhance the clarity and distinctness of their work, the authors could provide more explicit comparisons with research on readability for humans. For instance, on page 1, the sentence "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" could be revised to specify "for machine comprehension."

In Section 3.1, it is unclear whether the authors are referring to skills for humans or machines. If the focus is on machines, it is puzzling that they cite papers related to human skills, and it is uncertain whether these papers are relevant to machine comprehension.

Finally, the authors should provide more information about the annotation process, including the number of questions annotators had to annotate and whether they were instructed to keep machine comprehension in mind during the annotation process.
