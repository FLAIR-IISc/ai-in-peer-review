**Review**

**Strengths:**

- The paper successfully integrates two distinct fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts, showcasing a promising interdisciplinary approach.

**Weaknesses:**

- The paper's primary objective is unclear, requiring multiple readings to grasp, and even then, its focus remains ambiguous.
- The article's scope is unclear, oscillating between machine comprehension and text readability for humans, leading to confusion.
- The paper neglects significant contributions in the readability field, which are essential for a comprehensive understanding of the topic.
- Section 2.2 contains unrelated discussions of theoretical topics, disrupting the paper's coherence.
- The paper attempts to address multiple questions simultaneously, weakening its overall impact. For instance, the impact of text readability on RC datasets and prerequisite skills should be analyzed separately.

**General Discussion:**

- The title is somewhat ambiguous and would benefit from clarification that it refers specifically to machine comprehension of text, rather than human reading comprehension, as the terms "reading comprehension" and "readability" typically imply human-centric concepts.
- The claim that "dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is method-dependent, as the use of features like POS/dependency parse may influence the outcome.
- The paper requires thorough proofreading to address English omissions and errors, such as the incomplete sentence on page 1.
- The annotation process for datasets using "metrics" is unclear and warrants further explanation.
- The paper blurs the lines between machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, which, despite similarities, are distinct areas of research.
- The concept of "readability of text" should be distinguished from "difficulty of reading contents," as noted in DuBay (2004).
- To mitigate ambiguity, the authors should provide clearer distinctions between their work and readability for humans, particularly in statements like the one on page 1, which should be qualified as "for machine comprehension."
- Section 3.1 raises questions about the intended audience (humans or machines) and the relevance of cited papers, which appear to focus on human-centric research.
- The paper should specify the number of questions annotators had to annotate and whether they were instructed to consider machine comprehension when annotating.
