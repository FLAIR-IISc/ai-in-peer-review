## Review:

**Strengths:**

- This work represents a valuable intersection of research in human text readability and machine comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**

- The central objective of the paper remains unclear. Despite multiple readings, the core argument is difficult to discern.
- The paper suffers from ambiguity regarding its focus – is it machine comprehension or human text readability? This lack of clarity is pervasive.
- Important and relevant work within the field of readability assessment is not acknowledged.
- Section 2.2 includes discussion of theoretical concepts that appear unrelated to the paper’s primary investigation.
- The paper attempts to address an overly broad range of questions simultaneously, resulting in a diluted and weakened analysis. For example, the relationship between text readability and performance on Reading Comprehension (RC) datasets should be investigated independently from prerequisite skills.

**General Discussion:**

- The title could benefit from clarification to explicitly indicate a focus on *machine* reading comprehension, as “reading comprehension” and “readability” are commonly understood in the context of human reading.
- The conclusion that dataset readability does not directly affect question difficulty is potentially dependent on the specific methods and features employed for answer detection (e.g., POS or dependency parsing).
- The manuscript requires careful proofreading to address grammatical errors and omissions (e.g., the example on page 1: “the question is easy to solve simply look…”).
- The methodology for annotating datasets with “metrics” requires further explanation.
- The paper frequently conflates machine and human reading comprehension, despite their distinct natures and broad research areas.
- A distinction should be made between “readability of text” and “difficulty of reading contents,” as these are not synonymous (refer to DuBay, W.H. 2004. *The Principles of Readability*. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact information).
- Greater emphasis should be placed on differentiating this work from research on human readability. For instance, when stating that text readability does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty (page 1), it should be explicitly clarified that this refers to machine comprehension.
- Section 3.1 again lacks clarity regarding whether the discussed skills pertain to humans or machines. If the intention is to address machine skills, the citation of human-focused research requires justification and confirmation of its relevance to machine comprehension.
- Further details are needed regarding the annotation process: How many questions were annotated per annotator? Were annotators explicitly instructed to consider machine comprehension when performing the annotations, rather than human comprehension?
