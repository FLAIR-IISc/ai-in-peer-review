Summary and contributions:
- The paper evaluates the quality of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: (a) prerequisite skills required to answer questions and (b) readability features of the supporting text. The authors annotate six datasets (e.g., MCTest, SQuAD, QA4MRE) and report a weak correlation between question difficulty (approximated by the number of required skills) and text readability. They also reorganize the taxonomy of prerequisite skills, especially knowledge reasoning, drawing on textual entailment and cognitive psychology.
- As I see it, the main contributions are: (1) a cross-dataset annotation and comparative analysis indicating that text readability and RC question difficulty are only weakly correlated; (2) a refinement of the prerequisite skills taxonomy informed by entailment phenomena and human text comprehension literature; and (3) the release of annotated labels across six datasets.

Strengths:
- Cross-disciplinary perspective: The paper attempts to connect human readability research with evaluation of machine RC datasets. This broader lens is valuable for characterizing datasets beyond simple accuracy or superficial question/answer types.
- Multi-dataset analysis: Annotating six datasets with a common set of skills enables an informative comparison. Observations such as MCTest being easy to read yet comparatively hard to answer are useful for dataset design.
- Reported annotation quality: The paper provides inter-annotator agreement and describes how annotators selected supporting sentences, adding some transparency to the labeling process.
- Practical implications: The finding that readability and required skills are only weakly correlated (e.g., SQuAD vs. MCTest vs. QA4MRE) suggests a pathway for curriculum-style evaluation and dataset construction.

Weaknesses:
- Unclear scope and framing: The paper’s objective and audience are not clearly articulated. The title and narrative repeatedly blur human reading comprehension/readability and machine RC, which makes it difficult to determine whether the metrics target human readers, machine systems, or both. Section 2.2 delves into human comprehension theory without explicitly motivating how those constructs transfer to machine evaluation. The ambiguity also affects Section 3.1: are the listed skills intended as cognitive skills for humans, capabilities for models, or an abstraction that applies to both?
- Method-dependence of the main claim: The conclusion that readability “does not directly affect question difficulty” appears to depend on the modeling assumptions. Systems that lean more on lexical/syntactic features (e.g., POS/dependency parses) may be more sensitive to readability factors than others. Without controlling for system design, feature usage, or architecture, it is difficult to interpret this claim as general. The analysis would benefit from acknowledging and bounding this dependence.
- Definition and coverage of readability: The paper conflates “readability of text” with “difficulty of reading contents” in places. Readability is a well-defined area with established definitions and measures; the paper should ground its use of the term and more fully cover relevant literature (e.g., DuBay, 2004), and better distinguish readability of passages from readability of questions for humans versus machines.
- Annotation methodology is under-specified: It is unclear how annotators “annotate datasets with metrics.” Please clarify the unit of annotation (per question, per sentence), what constitutes evidence for each skill, and whether disagreements about “nonsense” were adjudicated. It is also unclear whether annotators were instructed to judge skills from a machine-system perspective rather than a human one. The number of questions annotated per dataset per annotator and the exact sampling protocol should be made explicit in the main paper (not only in the appendix).
- Writing and presentation issues: The paper needs careful proofreading and tightening of exposition; some sentences are ungrammatical or incomplete (e.g., “the question is easy to solve simply look…” on page 1). The title could be clarified to signal the focus on machine reading comprehension. Several sections (notably 2.2) read as tangential without a clear bridge to the empirical pipeline. These issues detract from clarity and make the core message hard to follow.

Questions to authors:
- Scope and definitions:
  - Are the prerequisite skills defined for human readers, machine systems, or both? If both, how do you operationalize the mapping from human-cognitive constructs to system capabilities?
  - How do you define “readability” in this work, and how does that relate to classical definitions (e.g., DuBay, 2004)? Please clarify the distinction between readability of context passages and difficulty of questions, for both humans and machines.
- Methodology:
  - How many questions did each annotator label per dataset? What was the overlap for agreement measurement, and how were disagreements resolved?
  - Were annotators explicitly instructed to assess the skills needed by a machine (not a human)? If so, how was this enforced?
  - What precise evidence must be present in the text to mark a skill as required? For instance, how do you separate bridging vs. elaboration in borderline cases?
  - How exactly did you “annotate with metrics”? For readability, were features computed only over sentences marked as necessary, or over entire contexts? How might this choice affect the results?
- Claims and analysis:
  - To what extent do your conclusions about weak correlation hold across different RC paradigms (span extraction vs. multi-choice vs. open QA) and model types? Have you tested correlations with system performance that uses different feature sets (e.g., heavy reliance on syntax vs. end-to-end neural models)?
  - How did you handle “nonsense” questions in analysis, and how sensitive are your results to their inclusion/exclusion?
  - Given Section 2.2’s theoretical framing, can you provide concrete examples where human-inference categories changed annotation outcomes compared to an alternative, purely linguistic/entailment-based taxonomy?

Additional comments:
- Consider revising the title and abstract to explicitly frame the work as evaluating machine reading comprehension datasets, to avoid conflating human vs. machine perspectives.
- Please expand and update the related work on readability and on readability of questions, and clarify how your work differs from human-oriented readability studies and from quality measures of RC questions.
