- Strengths:
  - The paper brings together two areas: human text readability and machine reading comprehension.

- Weaknesses:
  - The paper’s objective is unclear. I read it four times and still do not understand the central point.
  - The focus is ambiguous throughout—whether it is on machine comprehension or human text readability.
  - Key prior work in the readability literature is missing.
  - Section 2.2 contains theoretical discussions that appear unrelated to the main topic.
  - The paper attempts to address too many questions at once, which weakens the contribution. Questions such as “does text readability impact RC datasets?” should be examined separately from the discussion of prerequisite skills.

- General Discussion:
  - The title is ambiguous. Please clarify that the focus is machine reading comprehension rather than human reading comprehension, since the terms “reading comprehension” and “readability” typically refer to humans.
  - You state that your “dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty,” but this conclusion depends on the approach and features used for answer detection (e.g., whether POS/dependency parse features are employed).
  - The paper needs careful proofreading. There are notable omissions, such as “the question is easy to solve simply look..” on page 1.
  - How did you annotate datasets with “metrics”?
  - You conflate machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension; while related, they are also distinct and extensive research areas.
  - “Readability of text” is not the same as “difficulty of reading contents.” See:
    DuBay, W.H. 2004. The Principles of Readability. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact Information.
  - Please add clearer distinctions from work on question readability for humans, as the paper is highly ambiguous on this point. For example, on page 1, “These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions” should explicitly add “for machine comprehension.”
  - Section 3.1: Are these skills intended for humans or for machines? If for machines, why cite studies centered on humans, and how confident are you that their conclusions transfer to machine settings?
  - How many questions did the annotators label, and were they explicitly instructed to annotate with machine comprehension (not human readers) in mind?
