- Paper evaluates RC datasets using prerequisite-skill taxonomy and readability metrics, annotating six datasets and analyzing correlations.  
- Main claim: question difficulty (proxied by number of prerequisite skills) is weakly correlated with readability, enabling easy-to-read but hard-to-answer datasets.

Contributions:
- Cross-dataset qualitative analysis showing weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty across six RC datasets.  
- Reorganized prerequisite-skill taxonomy integrating textual entailment and cognitive psychology.  
- Annotated resource: skills and selected sentences for a sample of questions per dataset.

Strengths:
- Timely, multi-dataset scope addressing how dataset properties affect RC learning.  
- Skill taxonomy grounded in prior work and more comprehensive than ad hoc lists.  
- Empirical findings yield practical implications for dataset construction and curriculum design.  
- Transparent annotation protocol with reported inter-annotator agreement as an initial reliability signal.

Weaknesses:
- Framing and terminology blur human readability vs. machine RC difficulty; scope is ambiguous.  
- Proxying difficulty by skill count is under-validated against human or system difficulty measures.  
- Annotation methodology has confounds: annotators saw gold answers, selection bias in sentences, and vague “nonsense” category.  
- Inter-annotator agreement reporting is insufficient; multi-label chance-corrected metrics and per-skill agreements are missing.  
- Readability feature set may omit RC-relevant aspects (coreference, discourse, entity overlap); computing metrics on selected sentences may bias results.  
- Related work and scope are uneven: psychology section overlong, key readability/RC diagnostics under-cited, and multiple goals dilute depth.

Questions for authors:
- Can you validate that higher prerequisite-skill counts correlate with increased human or system difficulty (accuracy, response time, model errors)?  
- What exact agreement metrics were used for multi-label annotations; please report per-skill and chance-corrected scores and training protocol.  
- Which toolkits/preprocessing were used for readability metrics, and why compute readability on selected sentences rather than full contexts?  
- How is “nonsense” defined and exemplified; how do results change if such questions are excluded?  
- Will you release full annotation guidelines, per-question labels, selected sentences, and nonsense flags for replication?

Suggestions:
- Clarify scope in title and claims by specifying “for machine RC.”  
- Tighten theoretical sections to focus on operationalized constructs.  
- Expand related work on classical and modern readability measures and RC diagnostics.  
- Proofread and improve clarity in writing.  
- Complement skill-count analyses with system-based difficulty (e.g., baseline model errors) to strengthen practical relevance.

Overall:
- The paper addresses an important problem and provides a useful taxonomy and cross-dataset perspective, but needs stronger framing, validation, and methodological clarity before publication.
