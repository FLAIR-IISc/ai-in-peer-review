- Strengths:
  - The paper aims to connect two areas: human-oriented text readability and machine reading comprehension.

- Weaknesses:
  - The paper’s main objective is not clearly articulated. Even after several readings, the core research question and contribution remain unclear.
  - The manuscript frequently conflates machine reading comprehension with human text readability, creating persistent ambiguity.
  - Key prior work in readability is missing.
  - Section 2.2 digresses into theoretical topics that appear unrelated to the paper’s central claims.
  - The paper attempts to tackle too many questions at once, which weakens the overall focus. For instance, whether text readability affects RC datasets should be investigated separately from analyses of prerequisite skills.

- General Discussion:
  - The title is ambiguous. Please make explicit that the work concerns machine reading comprehension, not human reading comprehension or general readability, since those terms usually refer to human-focused phenomena.
  - The claim that “the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty” likely depends on the answer-finding method and feature set (e.g., POS tags, dependency parses). Please qualify this statement and clarify the modeling assumptions under which it holds.
  - The paper needs careful proofreading. There are notable errors, such as “the question is easy to solve simply look..” on page 1.
  - Clarify how datasets are “annotated with metrics.” Typically, readability metrics are computed automatically rather than annotated by humans. If you mean computed metrics, specify which ones and how they were applied. If humans supplied annotations, explain the protocol, instructions, and scale.
  - Avoid mixing machine and human reading comprehension without clear boundaries. While related, they are distinct research areas with different methods, assumptions, and evaluation criteria.
  - “Readability of text” is not equivalent to “difficulty of content.” Please consult standard definitions, e.g., DuBay, W. H. (2004), The Principles of Readability, Costa Mesa, CA: Impact Information.
  - More clearly distinguish your contributions from work on question readability for humans. For example, on page 1, where you state, “These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions,” add “for machine comprehension.”
  - Section 3.1: Specify whether the listed skills are intended for machines or for humans. If they are intended for machines, justify the use of citations from human studies and explain how those findings transfer to computational models or systems.
  - Provide details about the annotation process: How many questions were annotated? What were the instructions? Were annotators explicitly told to assess difficulty with respect to machine comprehension rather than human readers? Include information on inter-annotator agreement if applicable.
