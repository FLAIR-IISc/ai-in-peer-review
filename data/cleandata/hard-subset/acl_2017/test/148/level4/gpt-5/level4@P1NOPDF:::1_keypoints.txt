- Strength: Connects human-oriented text readability research with machine reading comprehension.  
- Unclear overall goal: Main research question and contribution are not clearly stated.  
- Ambiguous focus: Unclear whether the paper targets machine comprehension or human text readability.  
- Missing related work: Important prior work in readability is not cited.  
- Section 2.2 digresses into theoretical topics that seem unrelated to the main argument.  
- Overambitious scope: Attempts to answer too many questions at once, weakening the contribution.  
- Title ambiguity: Title should clarify focus on machine comprehension rather than human reading comprehension.  
- Overgeneralized claim: The statement that readability doesn’t affect RC difficulty is too broad and depends on models and feature choices.  
- Proofreading needed: Paper contains notable grammatical and stylistic errors.  
- Annotation procedure unclear: Needs detail on what is annotated, how, and by whom.  
- Terminology conflation: Repeatedly mixes machine and human reading comprehension—distinctions must be explicit.  
- Conceptual clarity: “Readability of text” is not equivalent to “difficulty of reading contents” (see DuBay 2004).  
- Distinguish from human-question readability work: Explicitly state when findings refer to machine comprehension.  
- Section 3.1 ambiguity: Unclear if listed skills are intended for humans or machines and whether human-study citations transfer to models.  
- Missing annotation details: Number of annotated questions and whether annotators judged difficulty for machines or humans are not reported.
