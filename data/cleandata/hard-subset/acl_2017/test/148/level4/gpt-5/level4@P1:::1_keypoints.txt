- Strength: The paper attempts to bridge human text readability and machine reading comprehension by evaluating RC datasets from both perspectives.
- Weakness: The paper’s main goal and central research question are unclear even after multiple readings.
- Weakness: The scope is ambiguous—uncertain whether the focus is machine RC, human readability, or both—which weakens the narrative.
- Weakness: Important prior work in readability is missing, leaving the related-work section incomplete.
- Weakness: Section 2.2 contains theoretical material that appears largely unrelated to the paper’s main problem.
- Weakness: The paper tries to answer too many questions at once; separate investigations (e.g., readability’s impact vs. prerequisite skills) are needed.
- Title ambiguity: The title should clarify that the evaluation targets machine reading comprehension, not human reading comprehension.
- Central-claim caveat: The claim that dataset readability does not affect question difficulty likely depends on the model/feature assumptions and should be qualified accordingly.
- Writing quality: The paper needs careful proofreading and correction of grammatical errors.
- Method clarity: It is unclear how datasets are “annotated with metrics”—the annotation pipeline and which steps are manual vs. automatic must be described precisely.
- Conceptual boundary: The paper frequently conflates machine and human reading comprehension and should clearly delineate which aspects concern each.
- Terminology: “Readability of text” is not the same as “difficulty of reading contents”; align definitions and terminology with standards like DuBay (2004).
- Disambiguation: Statements claiming weak correlation between readability and difficulty should explicitly specify “for machine comprehension.”
- Section 3.1 concern: It’s unclear whether the “prerequisite skills” taxonomy is intended for machines, humans, or both; justify or reframe its grounding in human literature.
- Annotation protocol: The paper lacks details on how many questions each annotator labeled and whether annotators were instructed to consider machine comprehension.
- Question 1 for authors: Clarify the primary research questions and intended contributions in one or two concise statements.
- Question 2 for authors: Provide a step-by-step description of dataset annotation, specifying which parts were manual and which were automated.
- Question 3 for authors: State whether prerequisite skills are defined for machines, humans, or both, and justify using human inference taxonomies for machine readers.
- Question 4 for authors: Examine or qualify the model-dependence of the claim that readability does not affect question difficulty across different feature sets/systems.
- Question 5 for authors: Report exact counts of annotated questions per dataset and per annotator, and include the annotator instructions.
- Question 6 for authors: Streamline Section 2.2 to include only theory that directly supports the metrics and analysis.
- Question 7 for authors: Revise the title, abstract, and terminology to remove ambiguity about human vs. machine comprehension and align with readability standards (e.g., DuBay 2004).
