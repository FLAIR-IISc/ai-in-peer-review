- Strength: Paper connects human text readability research with machine reading comprehension.

- Weakness: Central objective is unclear even after multiple readings.

- Weakness: Ambiguous focus between machine comprehension and human text readability.

- Weakness: Important prior work in the readability literature is missing.

- Weakness: Section 2.2 contains theoretical material that seems unrelated to the main topic.

- Weakness: Paper attempts to address too many questions at once, weakening contributions; topics like readability’s impact on RC datasets should be separated from prerequisite-skill analysis.

- Title ambiguity: clarify that the focus is machine reading comprehension rather than human reading comprehension.

- Conclusion caveat: Claim that readability doesn’t affect question difficulty depends on the answer-detection approach and features used (e.g., POS/dependency features).

- Presentation issue: Paper requires careful proofreading and contains notable omissions/typos.

- Methodological clarity needed: Unclear how datasets were annotated with “metrics.”

- Conceptual conflation: The paper mixes machine and human reading comprehension, which are distinct research areas.

- Terminology: “Readability of text” is not equivalent to “difficulty of reading contents” (e.g., DuBay 2004).

- Need clearer distinctions from work on question readability for humans; explicitly state when findings refer to machine comprehension.

- Section 3.1 ambiguity: Are listed skills intended for humans or machines, and are human-centered studies applicable to machines?

- Annotation details missing: How many questions were labeled and were annotators instructed to consider machine comprehension specifically?
