Review
- Strengths:
- This paper connects two domains: human text readability and machine reading comprehension.
- Weaknesses:
- The paper’s objective is not sufficiently clear. I read it four times and still do not understand the core message.
- The manuscript is highly ambiguous about whether it addresses machine comprehension or human readability.
- Important prior work in the readability literature is missing.
- Section 2.2 contains a discussion of theoretical topics that are unrelated.
- The paper seems to pursue too many questions at once, which weakens the contribution. Questions like “does text readability impact RC datasets?” should be analyzed independently of the prerequisite skills.
- General Discussion:
- The title is somewhat ambiguous; please make clear that you refer to machine reading comprehension, not human reading comprehension, since “reading comprehension” and “readability” typically imply the latter.
- You state that your “dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty,” but this depends on the approach/features used for answer detection (e.g., POS/dependency parsing features).
- The paper needs careful proofreading; there are significant omissions, such as “the question is easy to solve simply look..” on page 1.
- How are datasets “annotated” with metrics?
- You are conflating machine reading comprehension with human reading comprehension; while related, they are distinct and sizable research areas.
- “Readability of text” is not the same as “difficulty of understanding the content.” See: DuBay, W.H. 2004. The Principles of Readability. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact information.
- Please include clearer pointers distinguishing your work from human-oriented question readability, as the paper is highly ambiguous. For example, on page 1, “These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions” should specify “for machine comprehension.”
- Section 3.1: Again, are these skills discussed for humans or for machines? If for machines, why cite studies about humans, and how confident are you that those findings apply to machines?
- How many questions did the annotators label? Were the annotators explicitly instructed to judge with machines, not people, in mind?
