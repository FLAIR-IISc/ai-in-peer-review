- Strength: Paper attempts to connect human-oriented text readability research with machine reading comprehension.

- Weakness: Main objective and core research question are not clearly articulated.

- Weakness: The manuscript conflates machine reading comprehension with human text readability, causing persistent ambiguity.

- Weakness: Important prior work on readability is missing.

- Weakness: Section 2.2 digresses into theoretical topics that seem unrelated to the central claims.

- Weakness: Paper tries to address too many questions at once, weakening focus (e.g., dataset readability vs. prerequisite skills should be separate).

- Title ambiguity: Make explicit the focus on machine reading comprehension rather than human reading or general readability.

- Claim qualification: The statement that dataset readability does not affect question difficulty depends on answer-finding methods and feature sets; specify modeling assumptions.

- Proofreading: Manuscript contains notable errors and needs careful proofreading.

- Clarify “annotated with metrics”: Explain whether readability metrics were computed or human-annotated, and specify which metrics and methods were used.

- Boundary issue: Avoid mixing machine and human reading comprehension without clear distinctions of methods, assumptions, and evaluation.

- Definition precision: Distinguish “readability of text” from “difficulty of content” and cite standard definitions (e.g., DuBay 2004).

- Contribution clarity: More clearly separate contributions from work on question readability for humans and explicitly state when claims refer to machine comprehension.

- Section 3.1: Specify whether listed skills target machines or humans and justify using human-study citations for machine-oriented claims.

- Annotation details missing: Report how many questions were annotated, annotator instructions, whether annotations targeted machine difficulty, and inter-annotator agreement.
