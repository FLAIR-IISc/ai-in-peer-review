- Ambitious attempt to link human-focused readability research with machine reading comprehension (RC).  
- Cross-disciplinary framing could inform dataset design and model analysis.

- Main research questions and goals are unclear and not crisply stated.  
- The paper alternates between human and machine perspectives without consistently distinguishing them.

- Scope creep: multiple goals (readability effects, defining prerequisite skills) dilute depth of each analysis.  
- “Readability” is conceptually conflated with “difficulty,” which are distinct in the literature.

- Important prior readability work and distinctions between human vs. machine question difficulty are missing from related work.  
- Methods are opaque: how datasets were annotated with metrics, what features/models were used, and parameter choices are not specified.

- Section 2.2 contains theoretical discussion not clearly connected to the empirical work.  
- The conclusion that readability does not affect question difficulty appears model- and feature-dependent and lacks controlled analyses.

- Need to explicitly state primary research questions and, if multiple, treat them as separate contributions or focus on one.  
- Disambiguate and define “readability” vs. “machine question difficulty,” and adjust the title/terminology to reflect machine RC focus.

- Expand related work to cover classic readability formulas and modern NLP approaches, separating human passage readability, human question readability, and machine RC difficulty.  
- Detail how readability metrics were computed (granularity, equations, tools) and provide code/feature extraction scripts for reproducibility.

- Run analyses across multiple feature sets and model families (e.g., bag-of-words, syntactic features, neural baselines) to test robustness.  
- Control for confounds across datasets (domain, answer types, span positions, question style) and report dataset statistics and splits.

- Either shorten or explicitly connect Section 2.2 theory to operationalized features and hypotheses.  
- Clarify whether listed “skills” are defined for humans, machines, or both, and operationalize each skill with measurable features.

- Annotation protocol must specify number of items, annotators, instructions, and inter-annotator agreement, and indicate whether annotations target machine comprehension.  
- Provide examples of annotations and report agreement metrics (e.g., Cohen’s kappa).

- Temper claims about readability’s (non-)effect with explicit caveats about model/feature dependence and include ablation/partial-correlation analyses.  
- Improve writing, fix grammatical issues, and make figures/tables self-contained with clear units of analysis.

- Questions for authors: precise research questions/hypotheses; how readability metrics were computed and at what granularity; models/features used and potential biases; annotation details and agreement scores; how “skills” are defined for machines and justified via human studies; controls for dataset confounds; ablation results for syntactic features.

- Overall: promising and important topic but currently hampered by unclear goals, mixed human/machine scope, missing related work, and methodological ambiguity; requires substantial revision to be a valuable contribution.
