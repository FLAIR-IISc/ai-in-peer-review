Strengths:
- The paper connects two domains: human-oriented text readability assessments and machine reading comprehension.

Weaknesses:
- The paper’s core objective is insufficiently articulated; even after multiple careful readings, the central message remains unclear.
- There is persistent ambiguity about whether the work targets machine comprehension evaluation or human readability.
- Important prior work in the readability literature is missing.
- Section 2.2 diverges into theoretical discussions that seem largely unrelated to the main research questions.
- The scope is too broad, attempting to tackle several issues at once. For example, assessing whether readability influences RC dataset difficulty should be analyzed independently from the prerequisite-skills framework, rather than conflated.

General Discussion:
- The title is ambiguous; please make explicit that the focus is on machine reading comprehension rather than human reading comprehension, as “reading comprehension” and “readability” typically imply human-focused constructs.
- The claim that “readability does not directly affect question difficulty” is likely model- and feature-dependent. Systems leveraging POS tags, dependency parses, or other linguistic features might show different correlations, so the conclusion should be qualified accordingly.
- Please carefully proofread the paper; there are notable grammatical issues (e.g., “the question is easy to solve simply look..”).
- Clarify the procedure for “annotating datasets with metrics.” What exactly is being annotated, by whom, and how is the annotation protocol operationalized?
- The manuscript conflates machine reading comprehension with human reading comprehension; while related, they are distinct areas with different assumptions and methodologies. This distinction should be made explicit throughout.
- “Readability of text” is not equivalent to “difficulty of reading contents.” See: DuBay, W.H. 2004. The Principles of Readability. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact Information.
- Provide clearer separation between your work and human question-readability research. For example, when stating that text readability does not correlate with question difficulty, qualify this as “for machine comprehension.”
- Section 3.1: Are the prerequisite skills defined for human readers or machine systems? If they are intended for machines, justify relying primarily on human comprehension literature, and explain why these constructs transfer.
- Specify how many questions each annotator labeled and whether annotators were instructed to consider the needs of machine systems rather than human readers when identifying required skills.
