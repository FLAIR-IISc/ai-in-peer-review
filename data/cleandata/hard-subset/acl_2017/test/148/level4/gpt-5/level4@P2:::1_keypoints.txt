- Paper evaluates RC datasets using prerequisite skills and readability metrics, annotating six datasets and finding a weak correlation between question difficulty (skills) and text readability.

- Main contributions: cross-dataset annotation showing weak readability–difficulty correlation; refinement of prerequisite-skills taxonomy; release of annotated labels for six datasets.

- Strength: Cross-disciplinary perspective linking human readability research with machine RC evaluation.

- Strength: Multi-dataset analysis enables informative comparisons (e.g., MCTest is easy to read but relatively hard to answer).

- Strength: Reports inter-annotator agreement and supporting-sentence selection for transparency.

- Strength: Practical implication that weak readability–skill correlation suggests curriculum-style evaluation and dataset construction opportunities.

- Weakness: Unclear scope and framing—ambiguous whether metrics target human readers, machine systems, or both, and how human-comprehension theory transfers to machine evaluation.

- Weakness: Main claim may be method-dependent; conclusions might vary with model architectures or feature usage and require bounding/control.

- Weakness: Readability is under-defined and conflated with content difficulty; needs grounding in established literature (e.g., DuBay 2004) and clearer passage vs question distinctions.

- Weakness: Annotation methodology under-specified—unclear unit of annotation, evidence criteria for skills, adjudication of disagreements, sampling protocol, and annotator instructions re: machine vs human perspective.

- Weakness: Writing and presentation need proofreading and tightening; title, Section 2.2, and some sentences detract from clarity and focus.

- Question: Are prerequisite skills defined for humans, machines, or both, and how is the mapping from cognitive constructs to system capabilities operationalized?

- Question: How is “readability” defined in this work, and how does it relate to classical definitions and the distinction between passage and question readability?

- Question: How many questions did each annotator label per dataset, what overlap was used for agreement, and how were disagreements resolved?

- Question: Were annotators instructed to assess skills required by machines rather than humans, and how was that enforced?

- Question: What precise textual evidence is required to mark a skill as needed (e.g., how to distinguish bridging vs elaboration)?

- Question: How were readability features computed—over marked supporting sentences or entire contexts—and how might that choice affect results?

- Question: Do the weak-correlation conclusions hold across RC paradigms (span extraction, multiple-choice, open QA) and across different model types or feature sets?

- Question: How were “nonsense” questions handled in the analysis, and how sensitive are results to their inclusion or exclusion?

- Question: Can the authors provide concrete examples where human-inference categories changed annotation outcomes compared to a purely linguistic/entailment-based taxonomy?

- Comment: Revise title and abstract to explicitly frame the work as evaluating machine reading comprehension datasets.

- Comment: Expand and update related work on readability and question readability, and clarify how this work differs from human-oriented readability studies and RC question-quality measures.
