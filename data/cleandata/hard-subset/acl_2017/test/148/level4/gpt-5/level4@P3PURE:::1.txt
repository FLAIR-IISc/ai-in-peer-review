Summary
This paper aims to connect two areas—human-oriented text readability and machine reading comprehension (RC). While this cross-cutting perspective is promising, the paper’s core objective and scope are unclear, and key distinctions between human readability and machine comprehension are not consistently maintained.

Strengths
- The paper attempts to bridge two fields: human text readability and machine comprehension of texts.

Weaknesses
- The paper’s main goal is not clearly articulated. Even after multiple readings, it remains unclear what precise research question is being addressed.
- The scope is ambiguous throughout: it is often unclear whether the focus is machine comprehension or human readability.
- Important prior work in the readability literature is missing.
- Section 2.2 introduces theoretical topics that appear unrelated to the paper’s stated aims.
- The paper attempts to answer too many questions at once, which weakens the contribution. For example, “Does text readability impact RC datasets?” should be analyzed separately from prerequisite skill analyses.

Detailed comments and requests for clarification
1) Title and scope
- The title is ambiguous. Please clarify that the paper concerns machine comprehension of text, not human reading comprehension. In common usage, “reading comprehension” and “readability” signal human-focused constructs.

2) Claims about readability and question difficulty
- You state that your dataset analysis suggests that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty. This claim likely depends on the answer selection method and feature set (e.g., whether POS or dependency parse features are used). Please qualify the claim accordingly, or provide analyses showing robustness across feature sets and modeling choices.

3) Readability vs. difficulty
- “Readability of text” is not the same as “difficulty of reading contents.” Please ground your definitions and claims using established readability literature (e.g., DuBay, W.H. 2004. The Principles of Readability. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact Information) and clearly delineate how you operationalize “readability” versus “question difficulty” for machine comprehension.

4) Mixing human and machine perspectives
- The manuscript repeatedly mixes machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension. Although related, they are distinct research areas. Please clearly separate assumptions, measures, and literature relevant to machines vs. humans throughout.
- On page 1, where you write, “These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions,” please add “for machine comprehension” to avoid ambiguity.
- Section 3.1: Are the “skills” you discuss intended for humans or machines? If for machines, why do you primarily cite human-focused literature? Please justify the transfer of these concepts or add machine-focused citations and evidence that these skills apply to RC models.

5) Methodology and annotation details
- How do you “annotate datasets with metrics”? Please clarify whether these are automatically computed metrics, human annotations, or a combination. If human annotations are involved, describe the protocol and quality control.
- How many questions did annotators label? Were annotators explicitly instructed to assess difficulty with machine comprehension in mind rather than human readers?

6) Literature coverage
- The paper is missing important work from the readability field. Please expand the related work to include foundational and recent readability studies and clearly position your contribution relative to that body of work.

7) Structure and focus
- Section 2.2 includes a theoretical discussion that appears unrelated to the paper’s objectives. Please either (a) explicitly connect it to your research questions with measurable implications, or (b) remove or substantially shorten it.
- The paper currently tries to address multiple questions simultaneously (e.g., dataset readability effects, prerequisite skills). Narrowing the scope and isolating research questions would strengthen the contribution and clarity.

Writing and presentation
- The paper needs careful proofreading. There are important omissions/typos, e.g., on page 1: “the question is easy to solve simply look..”
- Please ensure consistent terminology distinguishing human readability from machine comprehension throughout.

Overall assessment
The idea of relating human readability to machine RC is interesting, but the paper’s goals, scope, and methodological details are not sufficiently clear, and the conflation of human- and machine-focused concepts undermines the main claims. I encourage the authors to:
- Clarify the central research question and narrow the scope.
- Clearly distinguish human readability from machine comprehension throughout (title, definitions, measures, literature).
- Provide methodological transparency on how metrics and annotations are obtained and used, including annotator instructions and counts.
- Reassess claims about readability’s impact on question difficulty with respect to the modeling choices and feature sets used.
- Expand and align the related work with readability research and machine RC.
