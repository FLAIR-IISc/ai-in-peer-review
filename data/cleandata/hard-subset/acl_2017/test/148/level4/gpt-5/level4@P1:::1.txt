Strengths:
- The paper aims to bridge two areas: human text readability and machine reading comprehension, by considering both in the evaluation of RC datasets.

Weaknesses:
- The paper’s main goal is not clearly stated. Even after several readings, the central research question and intended contribution remain unclear.
- The scope is ambiguous: it is not clear whether the primary focus is machine reading comprehension, human readability, or both. The paper frequently mixes the two, which weakens the narrative.
- Important prior work in readability is missing; the related work feels incomplete for that part of the contribution.
- Section 2.2 presents theoretical material that seems largely unrelated to the main problem the paper addresses.
- The paper attempts to answer too many questions at once. For example, “Does text readability impact RC datasets?” should be investigated independently from the proposal and analysis of prerequisite skills.

General Discussion:
- Title: The title is ambiguous. Please clarify that you are evaluating datasets for machine reading comprehension, not human reading comprehension, since “reading comprehension” and “readability” typically refer to human readers.
- Central claim: You state that your dataset analysis suggests that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty. This claim likely depends on how answers are found (e.g., whether the system uses POS/dependency features, sentence-level matching, or discourse cues). Please qualify this claim and discuss its model/feature dependence.
- Writing quality: The paper needs careful proofreading. For example, on page 1: “the question is easy to solve simply look...” should be corrected (e.g., “simply by looking at...”).
- Methodological clarity: How do you “annotate datasets with metrics”? The current phrasing is confusing. Do annotators label questions with prerequisite-skill categories, and are readability metrics computed automatically on selected sentences? Please describe the pipeline precisely.
- Conceptual boundaries: The paper frequently conflates machine reading comprehension with human reading comprehension. While related, they are distinct areas with different assumptions and evaluation targets. Please delineate clearly which aspects are for human readability vs. which are intended to characterize machine comprehension difficulty.
- Terminology: “Readability of text” is not the same as “difficulty of reading contents.” Please consult DuBay (2004), The Principles of Readability, for standard definitions and terminology, and align your usage accordingly.
- Disambiguation: Provide more explicit guidance distinguishing your work from research on readability of questions for humans. For instance, on page 1 (“These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions”), add “for machine comprehension” to avoid ambiguity.
- Section 3.1: Are the “prerequisite skills” intended to describe skills needed by machines, humans, or both? If for machines, why is the taxonomy so strongly grounded in human comprehension literature? Please justify the transfer and its limits, or reframe the claims accordingly.
- Annotation protocol: How many questions did each annotator label? Were annotators explicitly instructed to annotate with machine comprehension in mind (rather than human comprehension)? Please clarify annotator guidelines and the intended target of the skill labels.

Questions to Authors:
1) Please clarify the paper’s primary research questions and intended contributions in one or two concise statements. What is the main takeaway: a new evaluation framework, a new skill taxonomy, an empirical finding about readability vs. question difficulty, or something else?
2) How exactly were datasets “annotated with metrics”? Which parts are manual (e.g., selecting necessary sentences, assigning prerequisite skills) and which are automatic (e.g., readability features)? Provide a step-by-step description.
3) Are the prerequisite skills defined for machine readers, human readers, or both? If for machines, what is the empirical basis for adopting human inference taxonomies, and how do you validate their applicability to machine performance?
4) Your claim that readability does not directly affect question difficulty seems model-dependent. Did you examine correlations under different feature sets or system assumptions (e.g., lexical vs. syntactic vs. discourse features)? If not, can you qualify the claim to reflect this limitation?
5) How many total questions did annotators label, per dataset and per annotator? Were annotators instructed to consider “difficulty” and “skills” from the perspective of machine comprehension? Please include the exact annotator instructions.
6) Section 2.2 currently reads as disconnected from the rest of the paper. Can you streamline it to include only the theory that directly supports your metrics and analysis?
7) Please consider revising the title and abstract to remove ambiguity about human vs. machine comprehension, and revise terminology to align with readability standards (e.g., DuBay, 2004).
