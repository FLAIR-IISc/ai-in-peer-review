Summary
This paper aims to connect two areas—human-oriented text readability and machine reading comprehension (RC)—to analyze how readability characteristics relate to question difficulty and prerequisite skills in RC datasets. The idea of bridging these domains is timely and potentially impactful, but the paper’s goals, scope, and terminology are currently unclear, which makes it difficult to assess the validity and implications of the findings.

Strengths
- Ambitious and relevant attempt to link readability research with machine reading comprehension.
- Cross-disciplinary framing could yield useful insights for dataset design and model analysis.

Major Weaknesses
- Unclear objectives: The paper’s main research question(s) are not crisply stated, and the narrative alternates between human readability and machine RC without consistently distinguishing them.
- Scope creep: The work appears to tackle multiple questions simultaneously (e.g., effects of readability on RC datasets, definition and measurement of prerequisite skills), diluting the depth of each analysis.
- Conceptual ambiguity: “Readability of text” is conflated with “difficulty of reading contents.” These are related but distinct concepts in the readability literature.
- Insufficient related work: Important readability research is missing, and the paper does not clearly distinguish prior work on human question readability from the machine RC perspective.
- Methodological opacity: It is unclear how datasets were “annotated with metrics,” what features/models were used, and how modeling choices may have influenced the conclusions.
- Organization issues: Section 2.2 discusses theoretical topics that seem only loosely connected to the core research question and are not integrated into the empirical analysis.
- Evidence-claim mismatch: The conclusion that “readability does not directly affect question difficulty” appears to be model- and feature-dependent; this needs controlled analyses and clearer caveats.

Detailed Comments and Suggestions
- Clarify the goals and scope: State the primary research question(s) in the introduction. If you aim to (a) measure whether text readability correlates with machine question difficulty and (b) identify prerequisite skills for RC, treat these as separate contributions with dedicated sections and clean handoffs, or focus on one to strengthen the paper.
- Disambiguate human vs. machine perspectives throughout:
  - Title: Indicate that the study concerns machine reading comprehension, not human reading comprehension.
  - Terminology: Define “readability” (as used in human-focused literature) versus “machine question difficulty.” Cite and adhere to standard definitions (e.g., DuBay, 2004, The Principles of Readability) and avoid equating them.
  - Textual clarity: In places like page 1 (“These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions”), specify “for machine comprehension.”
- Related work: Expand the review to cover classic readability formulas and modern NLP-based readability approaches, and clearly separate:
  - Readability of passages for humans.
  - Readability/difficulty of questions for humans.
  - Difficulty of questions for machine RC systems.
  Explain how your work relates to and differs from each strand.
- Methods and reproducibility:
  - “Annotating datasets with metrics”: Explain precisely how readability metrics and any other features were computed and attached (per passage, per sentence, per question-context pair?). Provide equations, tools, and parameter settings.
  - Modeling dependence: If the claim is that readability does not affect machine question difficulty, run analyses across multiple feature sets and models (e.g., bag-of-words vs. POS/dependency-based features vs. neural baselines) and report consistent or divergent effects.
  - Control for confounds: Ensure dataset differences (domain, answer types, span positions, question style) are accounted for, or at least discussed as potential confounds.
  - Report dataset statistics, splits, and release code/feature extraction scripts to enable replication.
- Section 2.2: Either substantially shorten or explicitly connect the theoretical discussion to your empirical questions (e.g., a mapping from theory to operationalized features and hypotheses).
- Section 3.1 (“skills”): Clarify whether these skills are construed for humans, machines, or both. If citing human cognitive literature, justify the transfer to machine RC and operationalize each skill as a measurable capability or feature with testable hypotheses.
- Annotation protocol:
  - Specify how many questions were annotated, by whom, and with what instructions.
  - Make clear that annotations were made with machine comprehension in mind (if that is the intent).
  - Report inter-annotator agreement (e.g., Cohen’s kappa) and provide examples to illustrate criteria.
- Claims and interpretation:
  - Temper conclusions about the (non-)effect of readability on question difficulty with explicit acknowledgment of model/feature dependence.
  - Consider ablation studies and partial correlations to disentangle readability from other factors (e.g., lexical overlap, answer span locality).
- Writing and presentation:
  - Proofread for grammar and clarity. For example, fix “the question is easy to solve simply look..” on page 1.
  - Reduce ambiguity by consistently labeling which results pertain to machines versus humans.
  - Improve figure/table captions to make them self-contained and to specify the unit of analysis (question, passage, QA pair).

Questions for the Authors
- What are the precise research questions and hypotheses? Please list them explicitly.
- How exactly were readability metrics computed and attached to the data? At what granularity?
- Which models and features were used to assess “question difficulty,” and how might these choices bias the results?
- How many items were annotated, by how many annotators, with what instructions? What were the agreement scores?
- In Section 3.1, how are “skills” defined for machines, and how are human studies justified as evidence for machine capabilities?
- How do you control for confounds across datasets (domain, question type, answer distribution)?
- Can you provide ablations demonstrating whether POS/dependency features change the observed relationship between readability and difficulty?

Overall Assessment
The paper tackles an important and underexplored connection between readability and machine RC. However, the current version suffers from unclear goals, mixed scopes (human vs. machine), missing related work, and methodological ambiguity. With a sharper focus, clearer definitions, stronger experimental controls, and improved presentation, this work could become a valuable contribution. In its current form, it requires substantial revision.
