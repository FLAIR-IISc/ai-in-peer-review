Summary and contributions
This paper evaluates the quality of reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two classes of metrics: (a) prerequisite skills (a refined taxonomy of reasoning and linguistic capabilities presumed necessary to answer a question), and (b) readability (surface and syntactic features reflecting ease of reading). The authors annotate six RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, Who-did-What, MS MARCO, NewsQA) with prerequisite skills, compute readability metrics on selected sentences, and analyze correlations between the two. They argue that question difficulty (proxied by the number of prerequisite skills) is only weakly correlated with readability, suggesting it is feasible to build datasets that are easy to read yet hard to answer.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A cross-dataset qualitative analysis indicating weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty, with concrete contrasts across six popular RC datasets.
2) A reorganized taxonomy of prerequisite skills that integrates insights from textual entailment and cognitive psychology.
3) An annotated resource (skills plus selected sentences) for a sample of questions per dataset, which can support further diagnostic analyses.

Strengths
- Timely problem and multi-dataset scope: The paper addresses the important and under-explored question of how dataset properties influence what RC systems actually learn. Analyzing six widely used datasets provides breadth and offers useful, actionable observations (e.g., MCTest as easy-to-read yet relatively hard to answer; SQuAD as harder to read but often solvable from single sentences).
- Skill taxonomy grounded in prior work: The reorganization of prerequisite skills draws on established frameworks from textual entailment and psychology (bridging vs. elaboration, logic, coreference, etc.). Even if some mappings are imperfect, the taxonomy is clearer and more comprehensive than many ad hoc RC skill lists, and can be reused in diagnostic evaluations.
- Empirical regularities with practical implications: The reported weak correlations between readability metrics and the number of required skills, and the dataset-specific profiles (e.g., ellipsis in Who-did-What, expert-devised complexity in QA4MRE) are informative for dataset construction and curriculum design. The sentence-distance analysis also highlights different retrieval demands across datasets.
- Transparent annotation protocol and reliability signal: The paper describes a consistent annotation procedure across heterogeneous task formats and reports an inter-annotator agreement figure, which, despite limitations (see Weaknesses), provides an initial reliability check.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguous framing and terminology: The paper frequently blurs human readability and machine RC difficulty. The title and narrative read as if about human reading comprehension, while the experiments target machine RC dataset properties. Claims such as “readability does not directly affect question difficulty” should be explicitly scoped to machine RC. The manuscript would benefit from consistently distinguishing human readability, machine readability, and machine question difficulty throughout.
- Question difficulty proxy is under-validated: Equating difficulty with the number of annotated skills is only weakly justified. There is no validation against human difficulty (e.g., response time, accuracy) or system difficulty (e.g., performance of baseline models). Without such validation, correlational claims involving “difficulty” are tenuous. Similarly, the skill counts may be sensitive to annotator style and the granularity of the skill inventory.
- Methodological confounds in annotation: Annotators saw the gold answer and selected the sentences needed to answer, which simplifies the task and may bias both skill labels and readability computations (applied only to selected sentences). The “nonsense” category is under-defined, and its use appears to mix data quality issues with difficulty. The inter-annotator agreement is reported as raw agreement on a small subsample; for multi-label skills, chance-corrected measures (e.g., multi-label κ/α) and per-skill agreements are needed to assess reliability.
- Readability metrics selection and interpretation: The chosen features (largely from second-language readability and traditional indices) may not capture aspects most relevant for machine RC (e.g., coreference density, discourse structure, entity overlap, lexical ambiguity, or answer-sentence locality). The paper also mixes its feature set with Flesch–Kincaid in the correlation plots, which makes the methodological basis uneven. Given that readability is computed on annotator-selected sentences, the reported correlations may reflect selection bias rather than dataset-level readability.
- Related work coverage and scope control: The background on psychology is useful but overlong relative to its direct operationalization, while key readability and RC-diagnostic references are under-cited. The paper attempts to answer several questions at once (skill taxonomy design, dataset annotation, readability correlations), which dilutes the depth of each analysis.

Questions to authors
- Definition and validation of “difficulty”: Can you provide evidence that more prerequisite skills correlate with higher human or system difficulty? For example, correlations with human accuracy/response time on a subset, or with baseline neural/feature-based RC model errors?
- Annotation reliability: What exact agreement metric was used (for multi-label annotations)? Please report per-skill agreement and chance-corrected scores. How were annotators trained, and were they instructed to label skills from the perspective of machine RC rather than human readers?
- Readability methodology: Which toolkits and preprocessing were used for each metric (tokenization, sentence splitting, AWL mapping, syllabification)? Why compute readability only on selected sentences rather than full contexts or answer-containing spans, and how sensitive are your findings to this choice?
- “Nonsense” questions: How is “nonsense” operationally defined? Are examples provided? How do analyses change if such questions are excluded consistently across datasets?
- Release of annotations: Will the full annotation guidelines, per-question skill labels, selected sentences, and “nonsense” flags be released to enable replication?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Clarify scope in title and claims (e.g., “Evaluation Metrics for Machine Reading Comprehension Datasets...”) and add “for machine RC” where needed to prevent ambiguity.
- Tighten Section 2.2 to focus on constructs that are actually operationalized; otherwise it reads as a theoretical detour.
- Expand related work on readability (e.g., classical measures such as Dale–Chall, contemporary NLP-based readability predictors) and on RC diagnostics and entailment-oriented analyses.
- Proofread for English and clarity (e.g., “the question is easy to solve simply look...”).
- Consider complementing skill-count correlations with system-based difficulty (e.g., how many skills per question are associated with baseline model errors), which would strengthen the practical relevance of the findings.

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and offers a useful cross-dataset perspective and taxonomy, but the framing, methodological validation, and clarity need strengthening before publication.
