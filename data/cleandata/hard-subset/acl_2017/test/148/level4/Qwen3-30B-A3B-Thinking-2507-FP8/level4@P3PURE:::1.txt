

Strengths:  
The paper makes a valuable contribution by bridging two distinct fields: human text readability and machine comprehension of texts.

Weaknesses:  
The paper's primary objective remains unclear despite multiple readings; the core contribution is difficult to discern. The work is fundamentally ambiguous about its focus—whether it addresses machine comprehension of text or human readability, or both—and this confusion undermines the argument. The authors overlook significant prior work in the readability literature. Section 2.2 contains a tangential discussion of theoretical topics unrelated to the paper's central claims. The paper attempts to address too many interrelated questions simultaneously (e.g., the impact of text readability on RC datasets versus prerequisite skills for comprehension), resulting in a diluted and weak contribution. Specifically, the claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty" depends on the specific features/methods used for answer detection (e.g., POS/dependency parse features), which is not adequately acknowledged.

General Discussion:  
The title is ambiguous and should explicitly clarify that the work concerns *machine* comprehension of text, not human reading comprehension, given that "reading comprehension" and "readability" conventionally refer to human processes. The statement that "dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" requires qualification: this conclusion is contingent on the feature set/methodology employed for answer detection. The manuscript contains several grammatical errors and omissions requiring proofreading (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look..." on page 1). The annotation methodology for "metrics" is unclear—how are datasets annotated using these metrics? The paper conflates human and machine reading comprehension, which are distinct domains despite superficial similarities. The term "readability of text" is misaligned with "difficulty of reading contents"; the distinction should reference DuBay (2004): "The Principles of Readability" (Impact Information, 2004). The paper should explicitly distinguish its focus from human readability of questions (e.g., on page 1, add "for machine comprehension" after "difficulty of the questions"). Section 3.1 fails to specify whether cited prerequisite skills apply to humans or machines. If the skills are machine-focused, the cited literature (which primarily addresses humans) is irrelevant, and the justification for assuming machine applicability is insufficient. The annotation process for questions lacks critical detail: how many questions were annotated, and were annotators explicitly instructed to consider machine comprehension rather than human reading?
