

Strengths:  
This article presents a novel integration of text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts.

Weaknesses:  
The paper's primary objective remains unclear despite multiple readings. The core focus—whether addressing machine comprehension or human text readability—is ambiguous throughout. The work omits significant literature in the readability field. Section 2.2 contains tangential theoretical discussions unrelated to the paper's central thesis. The paper attempts to address multiple interrelated questions simultaneously (e.g., whether text readability impacts RC datasets, prerequisite skills for comprehension), weakening its contribution by conflating distinct research questions.  

General Discussion:  
The title "Reading Comprehension and Readability" ambiguously conflates human and machine processes; clarification specifying "machine comprehension" is essential, as "reading comprehension" and "readability" conventionally refer to human capabilities. The claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty" depends on the answer-detection method or features used (e.g., POS/dependency parse features), requiring explicit acknowledgment. The manuscript contains proofreading errors, including critical omissions such as "the question is easy to solve simply look" on page 1. The annotation methodology for "metrics" lacks clarity—how are datasets annotated with these metrics? The paper inconsistently mixes human and machine comprehension contexts (e.g., "the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" on page 1 should specify "for machine comprehension"). "Readability of text" is distinct from "difficulty of reading contents" (see DuBay, 2004, *The Principles of Readability*). Additional pointers distinguishing the work from human readability of questions are needed. Section 3.1. ambiguously references skills for humans versus machines; if addressing machines, citations for human skills require justification, and their applicability to machines must be validated. Finally, the annotation process lacks critical details: how many questions did annotators assess? Were annotators explicitly instructed to evaluate questions through a machine comprehension lens (not human reading)?
