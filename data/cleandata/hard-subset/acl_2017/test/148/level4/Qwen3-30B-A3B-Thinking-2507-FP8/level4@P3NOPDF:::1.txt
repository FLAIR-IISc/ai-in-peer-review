

**Review for ACL 2017 Submission**  

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a valuable contribution by bridging two distinct research areas: human text readability and machine comprehension of texts. This interdisciplinary angle holds potential for advancing both fields, particularly in understanding how text properties influence automated reading comprehension (RC) systems.  

**Major Weaknesses:**  
1. **Unclear Objective and Conceptual Ambiguity:** The paper’s central goal remains ambiguous throughout. The title and content conflate "readability" (typically human-focused) with "machine comprehension," creating significant confusion. For instance, the claim that "readability of text does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty" (p. 1) lacks critical specification: *for whom* is the text "readable" or the question "difficult"? The phrase must explicitly reference *machine comprehension* (e.g., "for machine comprehension systems"), as "readability" and "reading comprehension" conventionally pertain to human cognition (DuBay, 2004). Without this clarification, the paper risks misalignment with established literature and miscommunication of its contribution.  

2. **Inadequate Distinction Between Human and Machine Tasks:** The work conflates human readability (e.g., Flesch-Kincaid metrics) with machine comprehension prerequisites (e.g., syntactic complexity for RC models). Section 3.1 cites human-focused readability studies (e.g., "Principles of Readability") but fails to justify their relevance to machine tasks or explain how human readability metrics translate to machine performance. This undermines the paper’s premise: *Are the cited "prerequisite skills" meant for humans or machines?* If for machines, the cited literature (focused on humans) is irrelevant. If for humans, the paper’s focus on RC datasets becomes tangential. The authors must explicitly delineate these distinctions.  

3. **Missing Foundational Work:** The paper omits key literature in readability (e.g., DuBay, 2004; Kintsch, 1998) and machine RC (e.g., Rajpurkar et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2017). This gap weakens the theoretical grounding and fails to position the work within existing frameworks. For example, the claim that "readability does not directly affect question difficulty" (Section 4.1) is contradicted by prior work showing that lexical complexity *does* impact RC model performance (e.g., through feature engineering). The paper must engage with these studies to justify its conclusions.  

4. **Methodological and Presentation Flaws:**  
   - **Section 2.2** discusses theoretical linguistic principles (e.g., syntactic structures) without linking them to the paper’s RC focus, rendering it irrelevant.  
   - **Annotation Process:** The paper states datasets were annotated with "metrics" (Section 3.1), but fails to explain *how* this was done. Were annotators instructed to assess text for *machine* comprehension (e.g., "Is this text challenging for a model?"), or did they use human readability standards? Without this, the annotation’s validity for the paper’s goal is unclear.  
   - **Proofreading Errors:** Notable grammatical issues persist (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look..." on p. 1), which detract from professionalism.  
   - **Overambitious Scope:** The paper attempts to address multiple questions simultaneously (e.g., readability’s impact on RC, prerequisite skills, question difficulty) without separate analysis. This dilutes focus; the core question—"Does text readability affect machine comprehension?"—should be isolated from ancillary topics like "prerequisite skills," which belong in a distinct study.  

**Specific Concerns:**  
- **Section 4.1’s Claim:** The assertion that "readability does not directly affect question difficulty" depends entirely on the *feature set* used for answer detection (e.g., POS/dependency parses). If features are chosen to *compensate* for readability, this is a methodological artifact, not a general finding. The paper must clarify this dependency.  
- **Title Ambiguity:** The title ("The Impact of Text Readability on Reading Comprehension") is misleading. "Reading comprehension" typically denotes human ability; the paper’s focus is machine comprehension. The title must be revised to specify "machine reading comprehension" to avoid confusion.  
- **Human vs. Machine Annotation:** The paper does not clarify whether annotators were instructed to evaluate text *for machines* (e.g., "Will this text confuse an RC model?") or *for humans*. Without this, the "readability" annotations lack validity for the paper’s stated goal.  

**Recommendations for Revision:**  
1. **Clarify the Core Objective:** Explicitly define the paper’s focus as *machine comprehension* throughout. Replace ambiguous terms like "readability" with "machine-interpretability" or similar, and consistently specify the target task (e.g., "text readability for machine comprehension").  
2. **Restructure to Address One Core Question:** Isolate the primary research question ("Does text readability impact machine comprehension performance?") and remove tangential discussions (e.g., human readability theories, prerequisite skills).  
3. **Engage with Key Literature:** Cite foundational readability studies (DuBay, Kintsch) *and* RC-specific work (e.g., on lexical complexity’s effect on models) to contextualize the contribution.  
4. **Revise Methodology Details:**  
   - Explain the annotation protocol: *What* metrics were used? *How* were annotators trained (e.g., "Assess text difficulty *for an RC model*")?  
   - Clarify feature dependencies in Section 4.1 (e.g., "When using surface-level features, readability had no effect; with syntactic features, it did...").  
5. **Proofread Rigorously:** Correct grammatical errors and ambiguous phrasing (e.g., "simply look" → "simply by looking").  

**Conclusion:**  
The paper identifies a promising intersection of readability and machine comprehension but is currently undermined by conceptual confusion, methodological gaps, and insufficient engagement with literature. With a clear focus on *machine comprehension*, explicit distinction from human readability, and rigorous revision of methodology and presentation, this work could make a meaningful contribution. As written, the ambiguity and scope issues prevent a clear assessment of its technical merits. I recommend *major revision* to resolve these foundational issues before acceptance.
