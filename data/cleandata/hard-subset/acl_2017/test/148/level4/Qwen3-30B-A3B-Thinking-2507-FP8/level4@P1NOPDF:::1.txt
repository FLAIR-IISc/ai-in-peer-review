

Strengths:  
The paper makes a valuable contribution by bridging two distinct fields: human text readability and machine comprehension of texts. This interdisciplinary angle holds potential for meaningful insights.

Weaknesses:  
The paper's central objective remains unclear despite multiple readings. The core claim—whether addressing human readability, machine comprehension, or both—lacks precision throughout, causing significant confusion. For instance, the assertion that "the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" (p.1) ambiguously conflates human and machine contexts without explicit qualification. This ambiguity permeates the entire work, making it difficult to assess the paper’s specific contribution.  

The paper fails to cite key prior work in readability (e.g., DuBay, 2004), which undermines its foundational claims. Section 2.2’s discussion of theoretical topics is tangential to the paper’s stated focus, further muddying the argument. Crucially, the paper attempts to address multiple interrelated questions simultaneously—such as whether text readability impacts RC datasets, what prerequisite skills are needed, and how to measure readability—without adequately separating these distinct analyses. This conflation dilutes the contribution and weakens the overall argument.  

General Discussion:  
The title’s ambiguity requires immediate clarification: "reading comprehension" and "readability" conventionally refer to human cognition, yet the paper focuses on machine comprehension. The title should explicitly specify "machine" to avoid misinterpretation.  

The claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty" (Section 3.2) is method-dependent and oversimplified. As noted, this conclusion hinges on the features used for answer detection (e.g., POS/dependency parses), which the paper does not adequately address.  

The paper contains multiple grammatical errors and omissions (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look" on p.1), indicating insufficient proofreading. Additionally, the methodology for annotating datasets with "metrics" is unclear—how were these metrics defined and applied?  

The paper repeatedly conflates human and machine comprehension without justification. For example, Section 3.1 cites human-focused readability studies while discussing machine skills, raising questions about the applicability of these sources to the machine context. The paper must explicitly clarify whether cited work pertains to humans, machines, or both.  

Furthermore, the annotation process for "readability" metrics lacks critical details: How many questions did annotators evaluate? Were annotators explicitly instructed to assess readability *for machines*, not humans? Without this, the validity of the dataset annotations is questionable.  

Finally, the paper would benefit from stronger distinctions between:  
1. Human readability of text (e.g., Flesch-Kincaid metrics),  
2. Human question difficulty,  
3. Machine comprehension of text, and  
4. Machine comprehension of questions.  
For instance, the p.1 example should specify "for machine comprehension" to resolve ambiguity. The current phrasing risks misleading readers about the paper’s actual scope.  

In summary, the paper’s core contribution is obscured by unresolved ambiguity between human and machine contexts, insufficient engagement with readability literature, and methodological gaps. Addressing these issues is essential before the work can be considered for publication.
