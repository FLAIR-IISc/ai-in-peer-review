- Strength: Addresses an interesting, novel intersection of human text readability and machine reading comprehension (MRC).  
- Strength: Dual-focus approach has potential value for readability research and MRC systems.  
- Major weakness: Core objective is ambiguous—unclear whether focus is human readability, MRC difficulty, or both.  
- Major weakness: Paper conflates human readability and machine comprehension without clear distinctions.  
- Major weakness: Key terms like "readability" are used inconsistently, causing conceptual confusion.  
- Major weakness: Statements about readability vs. question difficulty fail to specify whether they target human or machine comprehension.  
- Major weakness: Fails to cite foundational readability work (e.g., DuBay 2004, Flesch, Gunning Fog) and relevant MRC literature on text difficulty.  
- Major weakness: Section 2.2’s theoretical discussion is tangential and disconnected from empirical goals.  
- Methodological issue: Cites human-focused readability studies without justification for their relevance to machine comprehension.  
- Methodological issue: Annotation methodology and specific metrics are inadequately explained and validated.  
- Methodological issue: Annotator instructions are unclear—no evidence annotators were told to assess machine comprehension difficulty.  
- Methodological issue: Broad claim that dataset readability does not affect question difficulty is unsupported and depends on feature choices (e.g., POS/dependency features).  
- Presentation issue: Title is misleading—should explicitly state "machine comprehension" if that is the focus.  
- Presentation issue: Paper contains grammatical errors and proofreading problems that undermine credibility.  
- Contribution issue: Insufficiently differentiates contribution from existing work on human question readability.  
- Recommendation: Major revision required to clarify objective, distinguish human vs. machine processes, expand related work, and fix methodological/terminological issues.  
- Required revisions: Sharpen research question(s) and treat human readability and MRC difficulty as separate claims.  
- Required revisions: Clarify terminology, consistently specify "machine comprehension," and cite DuBay (2004) for readability definition.  
- Required revisions: Expand related work to include classical readability metrics and MRC studies on difficulty.  
- Required revisions: Provide detailed annotation procedures, metrics, and annotator instructions emphasizing machine-assessment.  
- Required revisions: Reframe results to acknowledge dependence on feature engineering (e.g., POS/dependency features).  
- Required revisions: Proofread thoroughly and remove or refocus irrelevant theoretical material (Section 2.2).  
- Final assessment: Paper has potential but currently fails to meet publication standards due to fundamental confusion between human and machine processes.
