

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes two complementary evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills (measuring the cognitive demands of answering questions) and readability (assessing text complexity). The authors refine Sugawara et al.'s taxonomy of prerequisite skills using insights from psychology and textual entailment, and apply both metrics to six RC datasets (MCTest, SQuAD, QA4MRE, etc.). The primary contribution is a systematic methodology for evaluating RC datasets beyond superficial metrics like question type. The secondary contribution is a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills that better captures the cognitive demands of RC tasks. The tertiary contribution is the public release of annotated dataset metrics for future research.

**Strengths**  
1. The paper addresses a critical gap in RC research by moving beyond superficial dataset characteristics (e.g., question type) to provide nuanced evaluation metrics. The authors rightly note that existing datasets often lack meaningful quality assessment, making it difficult to benchmark systems accurately.

2. The refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills represents a significant improvement over prior work. By grounding their classification in psychological theories (Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano) and textual entailment research (Dagan et al.), the authors create a more theoretically sound and comprehensive framework. This taxonomy could prove valuable for dataset construction and system development.

3. The empirical analysis of six datasets reveals an important insight: readability and question difficulty are only weakly correlated (r=0.025-0.416). This finding challenges assumptions about the relationship between text complexity and RC difficulty, suggesting that datasets can be "easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer" (e.g., MCTest). This insight has clear implications for dataset design.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The paper suffers from fundamental ambiguity about its scope: is it evaluating RC for *humans* or *machines*? The title and content conflate these distinct concepts. The authors cite psychological theories of human text comprehension but apply the metrics to machine RC tasks without justification. This ambiguity undermines the paper's core contribution. For example, the claim that "readability does not directly affect question difficulty" depends entirely on the machine comprehension method used (e.g., POS/dependency parse features), which the paper fails to acknowledge.

2. The methodology for applying metrics to RC datasets is unclear. The paper states they "annotated six existing RC datasets with these metrics" but fails to explain *how* they translated human-comprehension concepts into machine-evaluation criteria. For instance, how do "bridging" and "elaboration" skills map to machine comprehension capabilities? The annotation procedure (Section 4.1) describes human annotation of skills required to answer questions, but doesn't clarify whether this reflects human or machine comprehension demands.

3. The paper misrepresents "readability" and fails to engage with key literature. The authors cite DuBay (2004) but misunderstand the term—readability specifically refers to *human* text comprehension difficulty, not "difficulty of reading contents" as claimed. The paper also omits foundational work in readability (e.g., Flesch, Gunning Fog) and conflates readability of text with readability of *questions* for humans. This undermines the validity of their metrics.

4. The theoretical discussion (Section 2.2) is poorly integrated with the RC focus. The detailed exposition of Kintsch's C-I model and psychological distinctions (retrieved vs. generated inferences) is relevant to human comprehension but not directly to machine RC, which the paper fails to clarify. This creates unnecessary confusion about the paper's purpose.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper claims "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty." However, this relationship is inherently dependent on the machine comprehension method. Please clarify whether your analysis assumes a specific model architecture (e.g., attention-based neural networks) or if this conclusion is model-agnostic.

2. The paper states it "annotated datasets with metrics" but doesn't explain how human comprehension skills map to machine evaluation criteria. Please clarify whether the prerequisite skills taxonomy was designed for human or machine comprehension, and how this affects the interpretation of your results.

3. The title refers to "Reading Comprehension" without distinguishing between human and machine comprehension. Given the field's established usage of "reading comprehension" for human tasks, please clarify your scope in the title and abstract to avoid confusion with human readability research.

**Additional Comments**  
The paper contains several grammatical errors (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look" on page 1) that undermine professionalism. The authors should also clarify their distinction from existing work on "question readability" (e.g., for human readers), which is a separate literature. The claim that "MCTest is easy-to-read but difficult-to-answer" is accurate for *human* comprehension but requires explicit qualification for machine evaluation, as machine comprehension of simple texts can be difficult due to surface-level features.
