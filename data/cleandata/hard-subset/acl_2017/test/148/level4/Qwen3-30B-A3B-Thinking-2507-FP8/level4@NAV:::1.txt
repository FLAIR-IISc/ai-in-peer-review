

Strengths:
- This article effectively bridges two distinct fields: human text readability and machine comprehension of texts.

Weaknesses:
- The paper's central objective remains unclear; I required four readings to grasp its core focus.
- The scope is ambiguously defined, oscillating between machine comprehension and human text readability.
- Critical prior work in the readability domain is omitted.
- Section 2.2 presents a theoretically disconnected discussion with no clear relevance to the paper's focus.
- The manuscript attempts to address multiple interrelated questions simultaneously (e.g., the relationship between text readability and RC dataset difficulty versus prerequisite skills), weakening its overall contribution.
- The title lacks specificity, failing to distinguish between human reading comprehension and machine text comprehension—terms commonly associated with human understanding.

General Discussion:
- Clarify in the title that the work concerns *machine* comprehension of text, not human readability.
- The claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty" is method-dependent (e.g., it varies with features like POS/dependency parses used for answer detection).
- The manuscript requires thorough proofreading; critical grammatical errors exist (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look" on page 1).
- The methodology for annotating datasets with "metrics" is not explained.
- The manuscript conflates human and machine text comprehension, despite their distinct domains and significant differences. Explicitly differentiate these concepts throughout.
- "Readability of text" is not synonymous with "difficulty of reading contents" (see DuBay, 2004, *The Principles of Readability*).
- Add explicit qualifiers (e.g., "for machine comprehension") when discussing relationships between text readability and question difficulty (e.g., page 1: "These examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with question difficulty for machine comprehension").
- Section 3.1 ambiguously references "skills" without specifying human or machine context. If referring to machines, justify the relevance of cited human-focused literature and confirm its applicability to machine comprehension.
- Clarify the annotation process: How many questions were annotated? Were annotators explicitly instructed to evaluate for machine comprehension (not human readers)?
