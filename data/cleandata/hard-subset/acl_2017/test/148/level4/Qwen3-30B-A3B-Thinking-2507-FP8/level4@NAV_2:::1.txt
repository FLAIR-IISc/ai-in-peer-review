

Summary: This paper introduces a novel framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets by applying two classes of metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The primary contributions are: (1) demonstrating that there is only a weak correlation between question difficulty and text readability in RC datasets, (2) providing a refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC based on textual entailment and human text comprehension research, and (3) annotating six existing RC datasets with these metrics and making the results publicly available.

Strengths:
- The paper successfully integrates two distinct research areas: human text readability and machine reading comprehension, creating a valuable analytical framework for RC dataset evaluation.
- The annotation methodology for prerequisite skills provides a systematic way to assess the cognitive demands of RC questions, which is particularly valuable for dataset design and system development.
- The empirical analysis of six RC datasets reveals important insights about the relationship between text readability and question difficulty, which has significant implications for dataset construction.

Weaknesses:
- The primary objective of the paper is unclear and requires multiple readings to understand. The authors' central contribution is not effectively articulated in the introduction.
- The paper suffers from fundamental ambiguity about its focus: it oscillates between human readability and machine comprehension without clear distinction, creating confusion for readers.
- The related work section omits substantial relevant literature in the readability domain, particularly works that directly address machine comprehension.
- Section 2.2 contains theoretical discussions that are not directly relevant to the paper's main contribution, disrupting the flow and focus of the argument.
- The paper attempts to address multiple research questions simultaneously (readability-question difficulty relationship, prerequisite skills classification, and dataset annotation), which weakens the overall impact and makes the contribution less focused.

Questions to Authors:
- The title should explicitly clarify that the work pertains to machine comprehension of text, not human reading comprehension, to avoid confusion with standard terminology in the field.
- The claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty" is method-dependent; how does this conclusion hold when using different answer detection features (e.g., POS/dependency parse features)?
- The phrase "the question is easy to solve simply look..." on page 1 contains a significant grammatical error that needs correction.
- The methodology for annotating datasets with "metrics" requires more detailed explanation, particularly regarding the annotation guidelines and inter-annotator agreement procedures.
- The paper conflates machine reading comprehension with human reading comprehension; please clarify whether the prerequisite skills are defined for humans or machines, and justify the use of human-focused literature for machine comprehension.
- The paper should explicitly distinguish its focus from human readability of questions by adding qualifiers like "for machine comprehension" in relevant places (e.g., on page 1, "difficulty of the questions" should specify "for machine comprehension").
- Please specify how many questions were annotated and whether annotators were explicitly instructed to consider machine comprehension rather than human comprehension during annotation.
