- Summary: Introduces two metric classes (prerequisite skills and readability) for RC datasets; finds weak correlation between text readability and question difficulty across six datasets.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, informed by textual entailment and human comprehension research.

- Contribution 2: Systematically applies readability metrics to RC datasets as a new dimension for dataset quality analysis.

- Contribution 3: Publishes annotations for six RC datasets with these metrics for community use.

- Strength 1: Addresses gap beyond superficial question-type labels by offering complementary dimensions (skills and readability) for dataset analysis.

- Strength 2: Empirical finding that readability weakly correlates with question difficulty challenges common assumptions and can guide dataset construction.

- Strength 3: Public release of annotated datasets is a valuable resource for reproducibility and future work.

- Weakness 1: Central thesis unclear—paper conflates human and machine reading comprehension and fails to clarify which the metrics target.

- Weakness 2: Inadequate contextualization—omits or misplaces key readability literature and mixes human comprehension theory with a machine-focused goal.

- Weakness 3: Methodological gaps—annotation procedures, instructions, sample sizes, and dispute resolution are insufficiently detailed, undermining metric validity.

- Weakness 4: Scope creep—attempts too many aims (readability vs. difficulty, taxonomy, annotation) leading to underdeveloped claims and diluted analysis.

- Weakness 5: Language and precision issues—grammatical errors and ambiguous phrasing reduce clarity and credibility.

- Question 1: How are human-centric comprehension studies justified or adapted for machine evaluation of prerequisite skills?

- Question 2: How many questions were annotated and what exact instructions did annotators receive regarding machine vs. human skill assessment?

- Question 3: Would conclusions about readability vs. difficulty change if machine-oriented readability features (e.g., POS/dependency parses) were used?

- Question 4: Why exclude CNN/Daily Mail and CBTest, and could these exclusions bias conclusions about dataset quality?

- Additional comment 1: Clarify in title/abstract that the focus is on machine reading comprehension, not human reading.

- Additional comment 2: Explicitly distinguish this work from human readability research and state adaptations for machine comprehension.

- Additional comment 3: Fix grammatical errors and ambiguous wording throughout the paper.

- Additional comment 4: Add qualifying phrases like "for machine comprehension" when claiming readability does not correlate with question difficulty.
