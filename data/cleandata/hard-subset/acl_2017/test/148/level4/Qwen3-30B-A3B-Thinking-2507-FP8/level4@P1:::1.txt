

**Summary**  
This paper introduces two classes of metrics for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills (assessing question difficulty) and readability (assessing text difficulty). The authors apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets, finding only a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty. They argue that RC datasets can be both easy-to-read and difficult-to-answer, challenging common assumptions about dataset quality.  

**Main Contributions**  
1. A refined taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC tasks, reorganized using insights from textual entailment and human comprehension research.  
2. A systematic application of readability metrics to RC datasets, providing a new lens for dataset quality analysis.  
3. Annotated datasets (six RC datasets) with these metrics, made publicly available for future research.  

**Strengths**  
1. The paper addresses a critical gap in RC research: the lack of robust metrics beyond superficial categories like question type. By introducing two complementary evaluation dimensions—prerequisite skills and readability—it offers a more nuanced framework for dataset analysis.  
2. The empirical findings are valuable: the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty challenges the assumption that easier-to-read texts inherently produce easier questions. This insight could guide future dataset construction.  
3. The public release of annotated datasets provides a useful resource for the community, enabling more systematic comparisons and method development.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Clarity of central thesis**: The paper's core contribution is obscured by ambiguity about whether the metrics apply to human or machine comprehension. The title and text conflate "reading comprehension" (typically human) with machine comprehension, and the paper cites psychological research on human cognition without clarifying how these findings translate to machine evaluation. For example, the claim that "readability does not directly affect question difficulty" (p. 1) is context-dependent on the evaluation method—e.g., if POS/dependency parse features are used—but this nuance is missing.  
2. **Inadequate contextualization**: The paper omits key work in readability metrics (e.g., Vajjala and Meurers 2012, which they later cite). Section 2.2’s discussion of human text comprehension theory (e.g., Kintsch’s C–I model) is tangential to the paper’s machine-focused goal, creating confusion. The authors should clarify how human-comprehension insights inform machine evaluation.  
3. **Methodological gaps**: The annotation process lacks critical details. How were annotators instructed to evaluate skills (for humans or machines)? The paper claims 90.1% inter-annotator agreement but doesn’t specify how disagreements were resolved or how many questions were annotated. For instance, Section 4.1 states annotators "selected sentences needed to answer questions," but it’s unclear whether this was for machine reading or human comprehension. Without this, the validity of the metrics is questionable.  
4. **Scope creep**: The paper attempts to address too many questions simultaneously: the relationship between readability and question difficulty, the taxonomy of prerequisite skills, and the annotation methodology. This dilutes the analysis—e.g., the claim about readability not affecting difficulty (Section 5) is underdeveloped due to the lack of methodological rigor.  
5. **Language and precision**: The paper contains grammatical errors (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look..." on p. 1) and ambiguous phrasing (e.g., "readability of text" vs. "difficulty of reading contents"). These undermine the paper’s credibility.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Section 3.1 cites psychological studies on human text comprehension to define prerequisite skills for RC. How do you justify applying these human-centric metrics to machine evaluation? Are the skills equally relevant for machines, or have you adapted them specifically for machine comprehension?  
2. How many questions did annotators evaluate, and how were they instructed to assess "prerequisite skills" (with respect to machine vs. human comprehension)? The paper states annotators "selected sentences needed to answer questions" but doesn’t clarify the target evaluation paradigm.  
3. The paper claims readability doesn’t affect question difficulty, but this depends on the features used for readability assessment. How would your conclusions change if machine-oriented features (e.g., POS/dependency parse) were used instead of human readability metrics?  
4. Why were CNN/Daily Mail and CBTest excluded from analysis? The paper states CNN/Daily Mail contains errors (Chen et al. 2016), but does this exclusion bias your conclusions about dataset quality?  

**Additional Comments**  
- Clarify in the title and abstract that the focus is on *machine* reading comprehension, not human reading. For example, replace "Reading Comprehension" with "Machine Reading Comprehension" to avoid confusion with human comprehension.  
- Explicitly distinguish your work from readability research for humans (e.g., "This paper focuses on readability metrics for machine comprehension, not human readability").  
- Fix grammatical errors (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look..." → "the question is easy to solve by simply looking...").  
- In Section 5, add "for machine comprehension" to phrases like "the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" to avoid ambiguity.
