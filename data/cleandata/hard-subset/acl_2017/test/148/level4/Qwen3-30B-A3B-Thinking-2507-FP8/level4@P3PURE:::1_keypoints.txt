- Strength: The paper bridges human text readability and machine text comprehension, which is a valuable interdisciplinary contribution.

- Weakness: The primary objective and core contribution are unclear after multiple readings.

- Weakness: The paper is ambiguous about whether it addresses machine comprehension, human readability, or both, weakening the argument.

- Weakness: Significant prior work in the readability literature is overlooked.

- Weakness: Section 2.2 includes tangential theoretical discussion unrelated to the paper’s central claims.

- Weakness: The paper attempts to answer too many interrelated questions simultaneously, diluting its contribution.

- Weakness: The claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty" depends on the specific features/methods used for answer detection and is not adequately qualified.

- General: The title is ambiguous and should explicitly state the focus on machine comprehension rather than human reading comprehension.

- General: The conclusion about dataset readability and question difficulty must be qualified as contingent on the feature set and methodology used.

- General: The manuscript contains grammatical errors and needs careful proofreading.

- General: The annotation methodology for applying readability metrics to datasets is unclear.

- General: The paper conflates human and machine reading comprehension, which are distinct and should be separated.

- General: The term "readability of text" is conflated with "difficulty of reading contents"; the distinction should reference DuBay (2004) and be clarified.

- General: Section 3.1 fails to specify whether listed prerequisite skills apply to humans or machines; if machine-focused, the human-centered citations are inappropriate.

- General: Critical annotation details are missing: number of questions annotated and whether annotators were instructed to judge machine comprehension rather than human reading.
