

Summary and Contributions:
This paper proposes two complementary evaluation metrics for reading comprehension (RC) datasets: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors reorganize prerequisite skills based on insights from textual entailment and human text comprehension research, and apply these metrics to six existing RC datasets. The main contributions are: (1) a refined classification of prerequisite skills for RC that better aligns with human text comprehension theories; (2) a demonstration of the weak correlation between dataset readability and question difficulty; and (3) a publicly available annotation of six RC datasets using their proposed metrics.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a valuable framework for systematically evaluating RC datasets beyond superficial metrics like question type. By introducing prerequisite skills as a measure of question difficulty, the authors offer a more nuanced understanding of what makes RC tasks challenging for systems.
2. The reorganization of prerequisite skills is well-motivated and addresses limitations in prior work. The authors' approach to classifying knowledge reasoning using distinctions from textual entailment and psychology research creates a more comprehensive and theoretically grounded taxonomy.
3. The public release of their annotated datasets provides a valuable resource for the RC community, enabling future work on dataset construction and system development.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's central goal and scope remain unclear throughout. The title and content ambiguously conflate human reading comprehension with machine comprehension, creating confusion about whether the metrics apply to human or machine performance. The authors never explicitly clarify whether their "prerequisite skills" are intended for human or machine understanding, which is critical for the paper's contribution.
2. The paper fails to engage with key literature in readability research. The authors cite DuBay (2004) but misrepresent "readability" as "difficulty of reading contents" rather than the established concept of text readability (DuBay, 2004). This oversight undermines the paper's foundation.
3. The methodology for annotating datasets with "metrics" is insufficiently explained. The paper states they "annotated six existing RC datasets with these metrics" but doesn't clarify how annotators determined which skills were required, how they handled ambiguous cases, or whether annotators were instructed to consider machine comprehension rather than human comprehension.
4. The paper attempts to address too many questions simultaneously, leading to a lack of focus. The core question—whether readability affects question difficulty—gets muddled with discussions of prerequisite skills, annotation methodology, and theoretical frameworks that aren't directly relevant to the main claim.
5. The paper contains significant grammatical issues and unclear phrasing (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look at one sentence" on page 1), which undermine its credibility and readability.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify whether your "prerequisite skills" are intended for human or machine comprehension, and explain how this distinction impacts your methodology and results.
2. Your claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty" appears to depend on your specific feature set. How would your conclusions change if different features (e.g., POS/dependency parse features) were used to evaluate readability?
3. Please explain how your annotation procedure ensured that annotators consistently evaluated skills from a machine comprehension perspective rather than human comprehension.
4. The paper mentions that "readability" is defined as "difficulty of reading contents," but this misrepresents the established concept of readability (DuBay, 2004). Please clarify your definition and how it relates to standard readability measures.
5. How do you distinguish your work from research on question readability for humans? The paper currently fails to provide clear pointers differentiating these two concepts.
