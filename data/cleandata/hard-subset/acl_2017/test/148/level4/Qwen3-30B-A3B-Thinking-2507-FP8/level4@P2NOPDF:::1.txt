

**Strengths:**  
The paper addresses an interesting intersection between human text readability and machine comprehension, offering a novel perspective on how these domains might interact. This dual-focus approach has potential value for both readability research and machine reading comprehension (MRC) systems.

**Major Weaknesses:**  
1.  **Unclear Core Objective:** The paper’s primary goal remains ambiguous throughout. After multiple readings, the central research question—whether text readability impacts MRC performance, human readability, or both—remains unclear. The paper attempts to address multiple distinct questions (e.g., "Does readability affect RC dataset difficulty?" vs. "What prerequisite skills are needed for MRC?") without sufficient separation, weakening the overall contribution. These should be addressed as distinct sub-questions.  
2.  **Fundamental Ambiguity in Scope:** The paper conflates human readability and machine comprehension without clear distinction. Key terms like "readability of text" (which typically refers to human comprehension, per DuBay 2004) are used interchangeably with "difficulty of reading contents" and machine comprehension tasks. This creates significant confusion. For example, on page 1, the statement "the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions" must specify *for machine comprehension*, as human readability and MRC difficulty are distinct concepts.  
3.  **Critical Omissions in Related Work:** The paper fails to cite foundational work in readability (e.g., DuBay 2004, Flesch, Gunning Fog) and key MRC studies addressing text difficulty. Section 2.2’s theoretical discussion is tangential and unrelated to the paper’s stated focus, further muddying the narrative.  
4.  **Methodological and Terminological Issues:**  
    -   Section 3.1 cites human-focused readability studies without justification for their relevance to machine comprehension, raising questions about the validity of the cited work for the paper’s purpose.  
    -   The annotation methodology for "metrics" (e.g., Section 3.1) is inadequately explained. How were datasets annotated? What specific metrics were used, and how were they validated?  
    -   Annotator instructions are unclear: Were annotators explicitly instructed to assess *machine* comprehension difficulty (not human readability)? The paper lacks evidence that this distinction was communicated.  
    -   The claim that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect question difficulty" (Section 3.2) is overly broad. As noted, this conclusion depends on the features used for answer detection (e.g., POS/dependency parses), which the paper does not adequately address.  

**General Discussion & Minor Issues:**  
-   **Title Ambiguity:** The title ("Readability of Text in Machine Comprehension") is misleading. "Reading comprehension" and "readability" conventionally refer to *human* processes. The title should explicitly specify "machine comprehension" (e.g., "Readability of Text for Machine Comprehension").  
-   **Proofreading Errors:** The paper contains significant grammatical errors and omissions, such as "the question is easy to solve simply look.." (Page 1). These undermine credibility.  
-   **Lack of Differentiation:** The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from existing work on *human* question readability (e.g., studies analyzing question difficulty for people). Clearer pointers (e.g., "This work focuses on machine comprehension, not human readability") are needed throughout, especially in the introduction and discussion.  
-   **Section 2.2 Irrelevance:** The theoretical discussion in Section 2.2 appears disconnected from the paper’s empirical goals and should be removed or significantly refocused.  

**Recommendation:**  
This paper has potential but requires substantial revision to clarify its core objective, rigorously distinguish between human readability and machine comprehension, and address the critical omissions and ambiguities outlined above. Without these changes, the contribution is unclear and the conclusions lack sufficient grounding. The current version does not meet the standards for publication at ACL.  

**Summary of Required Revisions:**  
1.  **Sharpen the research question:** Clearly define whether the focus is *human readability*, *machine comprehension difficulty*, or *both*, and address them as separate claims.  
2.  **Clarify terminology:** Consistently specify "machine comprehension" when discussing MRC tasks; cite DuBay (2004) to anchor the definition of "readability."  
3.  **Expand related work:** Include key readability metrics (e.g., Flesch, Gunning Fog) and MRC studies on text difficulty.  
4.  **Revise methodology:** Detail annotation procedures, metrics, and annotator instructions (emphasizing *machine* assessment).  
5.  **Reframe results:** Acknowledge that readability’s impact on MRC may depend on feature engineering (e.g., POS/dependency features).  
6.  **Proofread meticulously:** Correct grammatical errors and ambiguous phrasing.  

The paper’s central confusion between human and machine processes must be resolved before it can make a meaningful contribution to the field.
