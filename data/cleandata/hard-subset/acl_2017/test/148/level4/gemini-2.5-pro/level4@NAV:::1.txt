**Strengths:**
*   A notable strength of this paper is its attempt to integrate two distinct fields: human-centered text readability and machine reading comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper's primary objective is not clearly articulated. Even after multiple readings, the central research question and the main takeaway remain elusive.
*   There is significant ambiguity regarding the paper's core subject. It frequently conflates machine comprehension with human text readability, making it difficult to follow the central argument.
*   The literature review overlooks several key contributions and foundational work in the field of text readability.
*   The discussion in Section 2.2 appears disconnected from the paper's core thesis, and its relevance to the overall research is not established.
*   The paper's scope may be overly ambitious. By attempting to address multiple complex questions simultaneously (e.g., the impact of readability on RC datasets alongside an analysis of prerequisite skills), the central argument is diluted and lacks focus. These questions would benefit from separate, more in-depth investigations.

**General Discussion:**
*   The title is ambiguous. It is recommended to clarify that the focus is on machine comprehension, as "reading comprehension" and "readability" are terms conventionally associated with human cognition.
*   The claim that "the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is not sufficiently qualified. Its validity is highly dependent on the specific methodology and features used for answer detection (e.g., models relying on syntactic features like POS tags or dependency parses may be more sensitive to readability).
*   The manuscript requires careful proofreading to address grammatical errors and omissions, such as the sentence on page 1: "the question is easy to solve simply look..".
*   The phrasing "annotate datasets with 'metrics'" is unconventional. Could the authors clarify this process? Typically, metrics are calculated for a dataset, whereas annotation refers to labeling by human judges.
*   A recurring issue is the conflation of machine and human reading comprehension. These are two distinct and expansive research areas, and treating them interchangeably undermines the clarity and rigor of the paper.
*   The paper appears to incorrectly equate "readability of text" with "difficulty of reading contents." For a formal distinction, the authors are referred to foundational work such as: DuBay, W.H. 2004. *The Principles of Readability*.
*   To mitigate the ambiguity mentioned above, the authors should explicitly frame their claims within the context of machine comprehension throughout the paper. For instance, the statement on page 1, "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions," should be amended to specify "...difficulty of the questions *for machine comprehension*."
*   In Section 3.1, it is unclear whether the prerequisite skills discussed apply to humans or machines. If the focus is on machines, the paper should justify the use of citations from human cognition literature and clarify how these concepts transfer to computational models.
*   The description of the annotation process is incomplete. Please provide details on the number of questions annotated. Crucially, were the annotators explicitly instructed to evaluate difficulty from the perspective of a machine rather than a human reader? This distinction is critical to the validity of the annotation data.
