### **Summary**

This paper attempts to connect two important fields: text readability, which has traditionally focused on human readers, and machine reading comprehension (RC). While the motivation is interesting, the paper suffers from a fundamental lack of clarity regarding its primary research question and a consistent conflation of human and machine comprehension. The scope is overly broad, and the central claims are not sufficiently supported due to conceptual ambiguity and methodological weaknesses.

### **Strengths**

*   The primary strength of this work is its attempt to explore the intersection of text readability and machine reading comprehension. Investigating how linguistic features traditionally used to assess readability for humans might impact the performance of RC systems is a novel and worthwhile direction.

### **Weaknesses**

*   **Unclear Research Goal:** The paper's central objective is not clearly defined. After multiple readings, it remains difficult to discern the primary research question the authors are trying to answer. This lack of focus makes the paper's narrative and contributions hard to follow.

*   **Ambiguity Between Human and Machine Comprehension:** A significant flaw is the consistent failure to distinguish between concepts related to human cognition and machine processing. The paper frequently uses terms like "reading comprehension" and "readability" without specifying the context, blurring the line between how humans read and how machines process text. This conceptual confusion undermines the validity of many of the paper's arguments.

*   **Insufficient Literature Review:** The paper overlooks important and foundational work in the field of text readability. A more thorough engagement with this literature would provide a stronger theoretical grounding for the study.

*   **Overly Broad Scope:** The paper attempts to address too many complex questions simultaneously, which dilutes its focus and weakens its contributions. For instance, the question of whether text readability impacts RC dataset performance is a substantial topic that warrants a dedicated investigation, separate from an analysis of various "prerequisite skills." This multi-pronged approach prevents a deep analysis of any single issue.

*   **Irrelevant Discussion:** The discussion of theoretical topics in Section 2.2 appears disconnected from the paper's main research question and its subsequent experiments, further contributing to the lack of focus.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Title:** The title is ambiguous. The terms "reading comprehension" and "readability" are traditionally associated with human cognition. It would be beneficial to clarify in the title that the paper’s focus is specifically on *machine* reading comprehension.

*   **Conflating Readability and Difficulty:** The paper seems to equate "text readability" with the "difficulty of reading the contents." These are distinct concepts. Readability formally refers to surface-level linguistic features of a text (e.g., sentence length, word complexity), whereas content difficulty relates to the semantic complexity of the information being conveyed. For a more precise definition, the authors may wish to consult foundational work such as:
    *   DuBay, W.H. 2004. *The Principles of Readability*. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact Information.

*   **Claims on Readability and Question Difficulty:** The paper claims that its "dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty." This is a strong claim, but its validity is highly dependent on the model and features used for answer detection. For example, models that rely heavily on syntactic features (e.g., POS tags, dependency parses) might be more sensitive to text readability than models based on large pre-trained transformers. This contingency should be acknowledged and discussed.

*   **Clarity of Claims:** To mitigate the ambiguity mentioned above, the authors should consistently and explicitly state when they are referring to machine comprehension versus human comprehension. For example, the statement on page 1, "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions," should be revised to "...difficulty of the questions *for a machine comprehension system*."

*   **"Prerequisite Skills" (Section 3.1):** This section discusses prerequisite skills for reading comprehension, but it is unclear whether these are being considered from a human or a machine perspective. The section cites literature on human cognition; if these concepts are being applied to machines, the paper needs to explicitly justify this transfer and explain how these human-centric skills translate to computational models.

*   **Annotation Methodology:** The description of the annotation process lacks critical details.
    *   What was the volume of questions that each annotator handled?
    *   More importantly, were the annotators explicitly instructed to evaluate the questions from the perspective of a *machine*, rather than a human reader? This distinction is crucial for the interpretation of the results.

*   **Clarification on Terminology:** The paper mentions "annotating datasets with metrics." This phrasing is unclear. Typically, one *computes* metrics for a dataset, rather than *annotating* with them. Could the authors clarify this process?

*   **English Proofreading:** The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading by a native English speaker. There are several grammatical errors and awkward phrasings that hinder readability (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look.." on page 1).
