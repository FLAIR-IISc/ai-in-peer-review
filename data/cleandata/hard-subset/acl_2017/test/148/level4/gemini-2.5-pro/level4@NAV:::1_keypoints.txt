- Strength: Attempts to integrate human-centered text readability with machine reading comprehension.

- Weakness: Primary objective is unclear; central research question and main takeaway are elusive.

- Weakness: Paper ambiguously conflates machine comprehension with human text readability, obscuring the core subject.

- Weakness: Literature review omits several key contributions and foundational work on readability.

- Weakness: Section 2.2 appears disconnected from the paper’s core thesis and its relevance is not established.

- Weakness: Scope is overly ambitious—addressing multiple complex questions dilutes the central argument and needs more focused, separate investigations.

- General: Title is ambiguous and should clarify the paper focuses on machine comprehension rather than human reading comprehension/readability.

- General: Claim that dataset readability does not affect question difficulty is underqualified and depends on methodology and model features.

- General: Manuscript needs careful proofreading to fix grammatical errors and omissions.

- General: Phrase “annotate datasets with ‘metrics’” is unconventional—authors should clarify whether they compute metrics or perform human annotations.

- General: Recurring conflation of machine and human reading comprehension undermines clarity and rigor; the distinction must be maintained.

- General: Paper incorrectly equates “readability” with “difficulty”; should reference foundational work (e.g., DuBay 2004) for formal distinction.

- General: Authors should explicitly frame all claims within the context of machine comprehension (e.g., amend statements to specify “for machine comprehension”).

- General: In Section 3.1, it’s unclear if discussed prerequisite skills apply to humans or machines; justify use of human-cognition citations if applying them to models.

- General: Annotation process description is incomplete—report number of questions annotated and whether annotators judged difficulty from a machine or human perspective.
