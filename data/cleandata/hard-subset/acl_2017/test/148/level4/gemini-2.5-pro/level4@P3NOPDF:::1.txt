### Summary

This paper investigates the relationship between text readability and the performance of machine reading comprehension (RC) systems. The authors analyze existing RC datasets to determine if traditional readability metrics correlate with question difficulty and propose a set of prerequisite skills for machine comprehension. The core idea of connecting the established field of text readability with the modern challenges of machine comprehension is interesting and timely.

However, the paper suffers from a fundamental lack of clarity and focus, primarily stemming from a persistent ambiguity between human reading comprehension and machine comprehension. This core issue, combined with methodological weaknesses and an incomplete literature review, significantly undermines the paper's contributions.

### Strengths

The primary strength of this paper is its attempt to bridge two distinct but related fields: text readability, which has a long history in the context of human comprehension, and the rapidly growing field of machine reading comprehension. Exploring how intrinsic text characteristics influence the performance of machine learning models is a valuable and promising research direction. The premise of the paper is intriguing and, if executed clearly, could offer valuable insights into dataset creation and model evaluation.

### Weaknesses

Despite the interesting premise, the paper has several major weaknesses that prevent it from being acceptable in its current form.

1.  **Central Ambiguity: Human vs. Machine Comprehension:** The most significant flaw is the consistent failure to distinguish between concepts related to humans and those related to machines.
    *   The terms "reading comprehension" and "readability" are most commonly associated with human cognition. The title itself is ambiguous and should be clarified to specify a focus on *machine* comprehension.
    *   This confusion permeates the entire paper. For example, the statement on page 1, "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions," is presented without the crucial qualifier "for machine comprehension."
    *   Section 3.1 discusses prerequisite skills for comprehension by citing literature based on human cognition. However, it fails to provide any justification for why these skills are directly transferable to machine models or how they would be operationalized in a computational context. This conflation is a critical conceptual error.

2.  **Lack of a Clear Research Question and Focus:** The paper feels unfocused, as if it is trying to answer too many questions at once, which ultimately weakens its overall argument. The analysis of whether text readability impacts RC dataset difficulty should be a self-contained, rigorous investigation. Instead, it is entangled with a separate and underdeveloped discussion of prerequisite skills. These two threads are not well-integrated, leaving the reader confused about the paper's primary goal and contribution.

3.  **Methodological and Conceptual Flaws:**
    *   The claim that "the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is too strong and is not sufficiently supported. This conclusion is highly dependent on the specific models and feature sets used for answer detection. For instance, a system relying heavily on syntactic features like POS tags or dependency parses might be more sensitive to text complexity than a system based on surface-level n-gram matching. This nuance is not addressed.
    *   There appears to be a misunderstanding of the term "readability." The paper seems to equate it with the "difficulty of reading contents." However, as established in the literature (e.g., DuBay, 2004), readability formulas are typically based on surface-level proxies for syntactic complexity (e.g., sentence length, word length) and are not measures of semantic or conceptual difficulty. This distinction is crucial and should be clearly articulated.
    *   The methodology for annotation is unclear. The paper states that datasets are annotated with "metrics," which is confusing terminology. A detailed description of the annotation protocol is needed.
    *   The discussion in Section 2.2 feels disconnected from the core empirical work of the paper, further contributing to the lack of focus.

4.  **Inadequate Literature Review:** The paper misses important and foundational work in the field of text readability. A more thorough engagement with this literature would provide a stronger foundation for the study and help clarify the conceptual issues mentioned above.

5.  **Writing and Presentation:** The manuscript requires significant proofreading. There are numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings that impede clarity (e.g., on page 1, "the question is easy to solve simply look..").

### Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors

*   **Annotation Process:** Could you please clarify the annotation protocol? How many questions did the annotators have to annotate? What were the exact instructions given to them? Critically, were the annotators explicitly told to evaluate question difficulty from the perspective of a machine algorithm, not a human reader?
*   **Title and Framing:** I strongly recommend revising the title and abstract to make it unequivocally clear that this paper is about *machine* comprehension, not human cognition. Throughout the manuscript, please add qualifiers like "for machines" or "for RC systems" where appropriate to resolve ambiguity.
*   **Readability Definition:** Please provide a clear, operational definition of "readability" as used in this paper and situate it within the existing literature. Acknowledge the difference between surface-level readability metrics and deeper conceptual difficulty.
*   **Section 3.1:** Can you provide a stronger rationale for applying skills derived from human learning to machines? If these skills are relevant, how do they map to specific computational operations or model architectures?
*   **Unrelated Content:** Consider removing or significantly revising Section 2.2 to better integrate it with the main narrative of the paper.

### Conclusion

While the paper addresses an interesting and relevant topic, it is undermined by a fundamental lack of clarity, a diffuse focus, and significant methodological and conceptual weaknesses. The constant and unresolved ambiguity between human and machine comprehension makes the paper's arguments and conclusions difficult to evaluate and trust. I cannot recommend this paper for publication in its current state. The authors should undertake a major revision to sharpen their research question, clearly define their terms, and rigorously separate the analysis of machine performance from analogies to human cognition.
