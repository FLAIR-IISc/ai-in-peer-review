- Paper explores linking text readability and machine reading comprehension but has an unclear core objective and persistent ambiguity between human and machine comprehension, requiring major revision.
- Strength: novel and promising attempt to connect linguistic readability features with machine question-answering performance.
- Weakness 1: Research goal and scope are unclear—multiple questions are mixed without a unifying research question; analyses should be more focused.
- Weakness 2: The paper conflates human and machine comprehension throughout; terms like "reading comprehension" and "readability" must be qualified and human-centric citations justified for machines.
- Weakness 3: Literature review is insufficient and overlooks foundational readability work (e.g., DuBay 2004); conflates readability with content difficulty.
- Weakness 4: Methodology and claims lack clarity—conclusion that readability doesn't affect question difficulty is under-supported and model-dependent; annotation procedures are described vaguely and may not specify whether difficulty was judged for machines.
- Weakness 5: Structural and presentation issues—Section 2.2 feels disconnected, and the manuscript needs proofreading for grammar and phrasing.
- Detailed comment: Title should explicitly state focus on machine comprehension.
- Detailed comment: Introduction examples should be qualified with "for machine comprehension."
- Detailed comment: Clarify what "annotating datasets with metrics" means.
- Detailed comment: In Section 3.1, state whether prerequisite skills target humans or machines and justify using cognitive science citations if for machines.
- Detailed comment: Provide full annotation study details—number of questions per annotator, exact instructions, and whether annotators evaluated machine difficulty.
- Detailed comment: Add explicit distinctions from human readability research to resolve the manuscript's pervasive ambiguity.
