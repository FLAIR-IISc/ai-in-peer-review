### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a methodology for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: the difficulty of answering questions and the difficulty of reading the context passages. To measure the former, the authors propose a refined taxonomy of 13 "prerequisite skills" (e.g., coreference resolution, bridging inferences) required to answer a given question. The difficulty is then quantified by the number of skills a question requires. For the latter, they employ ten standard "readability" metrics from the NLP literature that capture lexical and syntactic complexity. The authors apply this framework to analyze six prominent RC datasets, including MCTest and SQuAD, by manually annotating 100 questions from each. Their primary finding is that there is only a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the skill-based difficulty of its questions. This suggests that a text can be easy for a human to read but still pose a difficult challenge for an RC system, a conclusion the authors propose can guide future dataset creation and system development.

### **Contributions**

1.  **Empirical Analysis of RC Datasets:** The main contribution is a systematic, multi-dataset analysis that empirically demonstrates a weak correlation between text readability and question complexity (as defined by the prerequisite skills). This finding challenges the implicit assumption that syntactically complex texts are inherently harder for RC tasks and provides a valuable insight for the design of future datasets.

2.  **A Refined Skill Taxonomy for RC Analysis:** The paper presents a revised and expanded taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills, drawing upon concepts from both NLP (textual entailment) and cognitive psychology (human text comprehension). This framework provides a structured vocabulary for researchers to qualitatively analyze and compare the inferential challenges posed by different RC questions.

3.  **Publicly Released Annotations:** The authors have created and will release a new resource: annotations of prerequisite skills for 600 questions across six different RC datasets. This data could be valuable for other researchers looking to perform fine-grained analysis of RC systems or datasets.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel Synthesis of Research Areas:** The paper's core strength lies in its novel approach of bridging two traditionally separate fields: human-centric text readability and machine reading comprehension. By systematically investigating the relationship between these two facets of difficulty, the work provides a new and valuable lens for evaluating RC datasets. This moves the community beyond simple accuracy metrics and towards a more nuanced understanding of what makes a particular RC task challenging.

2.  **Broad and Relevant Dataset Analysis:** The analysis spans six diverse and important RC datasets, including both early benchmarks (QA4MRE) and more recent, large-scale ones (SQuAD, MS MARCO). This breadth makes the findings generalizable and highly relevant to the current RC research landscape. The comparative results (e.g., SQuAD being easy-to-answer but hard-to-read, while MCTest is the opposite) provide concrete, actionable characterizations of these popular benchmarks.

3.  **Clear and Potentially Impactful Conclusion:** The central conclusion—that readability and question difficulty are not tightly coupled—is thought-provoking. It directly supports the idea of creating datasets that are lexically and syntactically simple but require complex reasoning, which could be a productive direction for future work. The authors' suggestion to use these metrics to create a "curriculum" of datasets with increasing difficulty is a compelling application of their framework.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Ambiguity Between Human and Machine Comprehension:** The paper suffers from a persistent ambiguity in its framing, often conflating concepts from human cognition with the task of machine comprehension without sufficient justification. The theoretical background (Section 2.2) and the skill taxonomy (Section 3.1) are heavily based on psychology literature (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano). However, the paper does not adequately argue how these human-centric skills map onto the actual operations of modern neural RC systems. It is not self-evident that a skill like "Elaboration" for a human corresponds to a distinct, identifiable process in a transformer-based model. This weakens the claim that the skill taxonomy is a valid measure of difficulty *for machines*.

2.  **The Central Claim is Not Validated Empirically:** The paper's main conclusion that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is based on a comparison between readability scores and the *number of human-annotated skills*. This is a measure of theoretical difficulty, not empirical difficulty. The conclusion would be far more compelling if it were supported by experiments demonstrating a similar lack of correlation between readability and the actual performance of a diverse set of state-of-the-art RC models. It is entirely plausible that for certain models, particularly those reliant on syntactic features, increased text complexity (a component of readability) would directly increase task difficulty, regardless of the number of annotated "skills."

3.  **Superficial Treatment of Readability:** While the use of readability metrics is central to the paper, the concept of "readability" is treated somewhat superficially. The paper equates it with "difficulty of reading contents" and measures it using a standard set of linguistic features. However, the field of readability research is extensive and offers a more nuanced understanding of the concept (e.g., DuBay, 2004, "The Principles of Readability"). A deeper engagement with this literature would have strengthened the paper's foundations and provided a more robust context for its claims.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  During the annotation process for the prerequisite skills, were the annotators instructed to consider the difficulty from the perspective of a *machine* or a *human* reader? The distinction is critical, as a task requiring complex "Elaboration" for a human might be trivial for a large language model with vast world knowledge, and vice-versa. Could you clarify the exact instructions and how you ensured consistency in this regard?

2.  Your primary conclusion is that readability does not correlate with skill-based difficulty. How do you expect this finding to hold up when measured against empirical system performance? Could you speculate on whether you would expect to see a stronger or weaker correlation if "difficulty" was defined as the error rate of a modern RC system like BiDAF or BERT on these questions?

3.  The paper's title and introduction use the general term "Reading Comprehension," which could be interpreted as referring to human cognition. Given that the paper's primary audience and context is NLP, would you consider clarifying in the title and framing that the focus is specifically on *machine* reading comprehension to avoid ambiguity?
