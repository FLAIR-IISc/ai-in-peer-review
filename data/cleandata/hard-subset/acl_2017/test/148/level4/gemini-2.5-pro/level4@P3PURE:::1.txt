### **Review Summary**

This paper attempts to connect the fields of human-centric text readability and machine reading comprehension (RC). While the idea of exploring this intersection is novel, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity, a poorly defined research goal, and a consistent conflation of concepts related to human and machine cognition. The central thesis is difficult to discern, and the paper tries to answer too many questions at once, which ultimately weakens its contribution.

### **Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its attempt to bridge two distinct fields: text readability, which has traditionally focused on human readers, and the rapidly growing area of machine reading comprehension. Investigating how readability metrics might inform or correlate with the performance of RC systems is a potentially interesting research direction.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear Goal and Contribution:** The central thesis of the paper is difficult to discern. Even after multiple readings, the primary research question and the main contributions remain unclear. The paper is highly ambiguous about whether its focus is on analyzing RC datasets through the lens of human readability or on proposing new methods for machine comprehension.

2.  **Conflation of Human and Machine Comprehension:** A significant and recurring weakness is the ambiguity between human text readability and machine reading comprehension. The paper frequently uses terminology and cites literature from human cognition without adequately justifying its relevance to computational models. This conflation makes the arguments difficult to follow and evaluate.

3.  **Overly Broad Scope:** The paper attempts to address too many research questions simultaneously, which dilutes its focus and weakens its overall argument. For instance, the question of whether text readability impacts RC dataset difficulty should be investigated as a standalone topic, separate from the analysis of "prerequisite skills." This lack of focus makes the paper feel unfocused and its conclusions weak.

4.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The literature review appears to be incomplete, overlooking key foundational work in the field of text readability. This is compounded by an apparent misunderstanding of core concepts (see detailed comments).

5.  **Unrelated Content:** Section 2.2 includes a discussion of theoretical topics that do not seem directly relevant to the paper's core investigation, further contributing to the lack of clarity.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Title:** The title is ambiguous. Given that "reading comprehension" and "readability" are traditionally associated with human cognition, the title should be revised to explicitly state that the focus is on *machine* comprehension.

*   **Clarity on Claims:** Throughout the paper, the authors should explicitly clarify when their claims apply to machines versus humans. For example, the sentence on page 1, "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions," should be amended to specify "...difficulty of the questions *for machine comprehension*."

*   **Unsupported Conclusion on Readability:** The paper claims that its "dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty." This conclusion may be premature, as it is highly dependent on the specific methods and features used for answer detection. The effect of readability might be more pronounced for models that rely heavily on syntactic features like POS tags or dependency parses, a possibility the paper does not explore.

*   **Definition of Readability:** The paper seems to equate "readability of text" with "difficulty of reading contents." This is an incorrect interpretation of the concept. The authors should consult established literature on this topic to ensure a correct theoretical grounding (e.g., DuBay, W.H. 2004. *The Principles of Readability*. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact Information).

*   **Ambiguity in "Prerequisite Skills" (Section 3.1):** In Section 3.1, the discussion of prerequisite skills is again ambiguous. It is unclear whether these skills are for humans or machines. If the focus is on machines, the paper must justify citing literature on human cognition and explain how these concepts translate to computational models.

*   **Annotation Methodology:** The methodology for annotation is underspecified and confusing. The phrase "annotating datasets with metrics" is unclear. Furthermore, crucial experimental details are missing:
    *   How many questions did each annotator label?
    *   What specific instructions were given to the annotators? Were they explicitly told to evaluate question difficulty from the perspective of a *machine*, rather than a human? Without this clarification, the validity of the annotation data is questionable.

*   **Proofreading:** The manuscript requires thorough proofreading for English grammar and style. There are several distracting errors and awkward phrasings (e.g., "the question is easy to solve simply look.." on page 1).
