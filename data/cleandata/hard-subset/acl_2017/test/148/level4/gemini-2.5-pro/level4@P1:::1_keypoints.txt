- Proposes a framework evaluating RC datasets along prerequisite skills and text readability.  
- Adapts a psychology/NLP-derived skills taxonomy and standard readability metrics for analysis.  
- Applies the framework to six RC datasets (e.g., MCTest, SQuAD, QA4MRE) and releases annotations.  
- Main empirical finding: text readability has only a weak correlation with question difficulty (as measured by prerequisite skills).  
- Contribution: comparative analysis of six datasets providing dataset-level insights (e.g., SQuAD: hard texts, simple questions; MCTest: simple texts, complex reasoning).  
- Contribution: a revised taxonomy of prerequisite reading-comprehension skills grounded in textual entailment and cognitive theories.  
- Contribution: publicly released annotated samples to support fine-grained RC analysis and dataset construction.  
- Strength: novel and important framing that brings human comprehension concepts into RC dataset evaluation.  
- Strength: comprehensive empirical coverage across multiple prominent datasets enabling useful comparisons.  
- Strength: actionable insight that readability and question difficulty are largely orthogonal, informing dataset design and curriculum learning.  
- Weakness: unclear whether the framework targets human or machine comprehension; transfer from human cognitive skills to neural RC models is not justified.  
- Weakness: central claim is contingent on model architecture; some systems (e.g., parser-dependent) may be sensitive to readability, which the paper does not address.  
- Weakness: superficial treatment of readability literature and weak linkage between theoretical grounding and the specific skills defined.  
- Question: request theoretical justification for applying human-psychology-derived skills to machine RC and their relevance to current neural models.  
- Question: ask for annotation guidelines showing how annotators were instructed to judge skills required for machines rather than humans.  
- Question: ask whether the readability–difficulty relationship might be model-dependent, especially for syntactically sensitive architectures.
