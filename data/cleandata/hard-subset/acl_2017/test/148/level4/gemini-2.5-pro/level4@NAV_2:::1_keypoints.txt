- Proposes a framework evaluating RC datasets on two axes: prerequisite reasoning skills and passage readability.
- Refines an existing skills taxonomy and applies it to six popular RC datasets.
- Provides a publicly released annotation of 13 prerequisite skills across those datasets for fine-grained analysis.
- Empirically finds a weak correlation between passage readability and question difficulty, implying readable texts can still yield hard questions.
- Novel perspective separating text-processing difficulty (readability) from reasoning difficulty (skills).
- Large-scale comparative study that highlights dataset-specific profiles (e.g., SQuAD: hard text, few skills; MCTest: easy text, complex reasoning).
- Publicly available annotations enable replication, targeted error analysis, and skill-focused model evaluation.
- Conceptual ambiguity about whether the framework targets human cognition or machine comprehension, weakening interpretation for NLP.
- Equates question difficulty with the number of skills required, ignoring variation in intrinsic skill difficulty and interaction complexity.
- Overly strong claim that readability does not affect difficulty, overlooking how complex syntax can degrade model components.
- Superficial treatment of readability literature and reliance on a limited set of metrics without justifying suitability for machine readers.
- Ask authors to clarify the intended audience and how interpretations change by audience.
- Request explicit justification for mapping human cognitive skills (e.g., elaboration) to modern neural RC model operations.
- Ask whether annotators judged skills from a human perspective or an abstract machine perspective and how that choice affected results.
- Ask how the claim that readability doesn't affect question difficulty reconciles with known model failures on syntactically complex text.
