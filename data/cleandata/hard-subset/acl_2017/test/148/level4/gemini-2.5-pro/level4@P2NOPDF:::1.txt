### Summary

This paper explores the intersection of two important areas: human-centric text readability and machine reading comprehension (RC). The authors analyze the relationship between traditional readability metrics and the difficulty of questions in existing RC datasets, and propose a set of prerequisite skills for comprehension. While the motivation to connect these fields is commendable, the paper's core objective is unclear, and it suffers from a persistent ambiguity between human and machine comprehension, weakening its central arguments and conclusions. The manuscript requires substantial revision to clarify its contribution, methodology, and positioning within the literature.

### Strengths

*   The primary strength of this work is its novel attempt to bridge the fields of text readability and machine comprehension. Investigating how linguistic features of a text impact a machine's ability to answer questions is a promising and underexplored research direction.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unclear Research Goal and Scope:** The paper's main objective is difficult to discern. It appears to address multiple complex questions simultaneously (e.g., the impact of readability on RC datasets, defining prerequisite skills for comprehension) without a clear, unifying research question. This lack of focus makes the paper feel disjointed and its contributions hard to evaluate. For instance, the analysis of whether text readability affects RC dataset difficulty should ideally be a focused study in itself, separate from the introduction of prerequisite skills.

2.  **Pervasive Ambiguity between Human and Machine Comprehension:** A major weakness is the consistent conflation of concepts related to human reading with those related to machine comprehension. This ambiguity begins with the title and persists throughout the paper.
    *   Terms like "reading comprehension" and "readability" are typically associated with human cognition. The paper needs to explicitly and consistently qualify these terms (e.g., "machine reading comprehension," "readability for machines") to avoid confusion.
    *   The paper cites literature on human cognitive skills (Section 3.1) to motivate a framework for machines. The authors must justify why these human-centric skills are applicable to computational models and acknowledge the fundamental differences between human and machine processing.

3.  **Insufficient Literature Review:** The paper overlooks significant prior work in the field of text readability. A more comprehensive review would better contextualize the work and strengthen its theoretical underpinnings. For example, the authors appear to equate "readability of text" with "difficulty of reading contents," a nuance that is thoroughly discussed in foundational readability literature (e.g., DuBay, 2004).

4.  **Lack of Clarity in Methodology and Claims:**
    *   The claim that "dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is presented without sufficient support. This conclusion is highly dependent on the model and features used; for example, systems relying heavily on syntactic features like POS tags or dependency parses might be more sensitive to text complexity. This point needs a more nuanced discussion.
    *   The description of the annotation process is vague. Key details are missing, such as how datasets were "annotated with metrics." Furthermore, it is unclear if the human annotators were instructed to assess question difficulty from the perspective of a machine, which is a critical distinction for the validity of the annotations.

5.  **Structural and Presentation Issues:**
    *   The discussion of theoretical topics in Section 2.2 feels disconnected from the paper's main narrative. The relevance of this section to the overall research goal should be clarified or the section should be removed.
    *   The manuscript requires thorough proofreading. There are grammatical errors and awkward phrasings that hinder readability (e.g., "...the question is easy to solve simply look.." on page 1).

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Title:** The title is ambiguous. I strongly recommend revising it to explicitly state that the focus is on **machine** comprehension to set the correct context for the reader from the outset.
*   **Introduction (Page 1):** When stating, "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions," please add the crucial qualifier "for machine comprehension" to avoid misinterpretation.
*   **Methodology:** Could you please clarify what is meant by "annotating datasets with metrics"? This phrase is unclear.
*   **Section 3.1 (Prerequisite Skills):** When discussing skills for comprehension, are you referring to skills for humans or for machines? If for machines, please justify the use of citations from cognitive science and psychology and explain how these human-centric models of reading translate to computational systems.
*   **Annotation Process:** Could you provide more details about the annotation study? Specifically: How many questions did each annotator review? What were the exact instructions given to the annotators? Were they explicitly told to evaluate the difficulty for a machine to answer, and if so, how was this concept explained to them?
*   **Distinguishing from Human Readability:** The paper would be significantly improved by adding more explicit pointers that distinguish its goals from the traditional study of readability for human readers. This would help resolve the ambiguity that currently permeates the manuscript.
