### **Summary**

This paper proposes a framework for analyzing reading comprehension (RC) datasets using two distinct categories of metrics: (1) prerequisite skills required to answer a question, and (2) traditional readability scores of the source text. The authors revise and expand a previous taxonomy of prerequisite skills, grounding it in theories from psychology and textual entailment. They conduct a manual annotation study, applying this skill taxonomy to 100 questions from each of six major RC datasets (including MCTest and SQuAD). By comparing the required skills against readability metrics, the authors conclude that text readability and question difficulty (as measured by the number of skills) are only weakly correlated. They suggest this finding could inform the creation of datasets that are easy to read but challenging to answer, facilitating a more structured development of RC systems.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A Large-Scale, Manual Annotation of Six RC Datasets:** The primary contribution is the detailed annotation of 600 questions across six prominent RC datasets using a fine-grained, 13-category skill taxonomy. This annotated resource, which the authors intend to make public, is valuable for the community, enabling more nuanced analysis of dataset biases and model capabilities.
2.  **Empirical Analysis and Characterization of Popular Datasets:** The paper provides a comparative analysis that highlights the unique characteristics of each dataset. For instance, it empirically demonstrates that SQuAD passages have high readability scores but its questions often require fewer complex skills, while MCTest exhibits the opposite pattern. These insights offer a useful perspective for researchers selecting or designing datasets for specific research goals.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Interesting Research Direction:** The core idea of systematically evaluating RC datasets by decoupling text complexity (readability) from question complexity (prerequisite skills) is novel and well-motivated. It challenges the implicit assumption that syntactically or lexically complex texts automatically lead to more difficult RC tasks. This approach opens up a promising avenue for more principled dataset design and analysis.
2.  **Significant Annotation Effort and Valuable Resource:** The manual annotation of six different datasets is a substantial undertaking. Creating a resource that allows researchers to analyze datasets not just by question type (e.g., "what," "who") but by the underlying reasoning skills required (e.g., "Coreference resolution," "Elaboration") is a strong, concrete contribution to the field.
3.  **Actionable Insights for Dataset and Model Development:** The analysis yields clear and interesting findings, such as the characterization of MCTest as "easy-to-read, difficult-to-answer." The discussion in Section 6, which proposes using these metrics to guide a "curriculum learning" approach for RC systems, is a compelling application of the paper's analysis and provides a concrete roadmap for future work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear Theoretical Grounding and Conflation of Human vs. Machine Comprehension:** The paper's central argument is weakened by a persistent ambiguity about whether it is analyzing comprehension for humans or for machines. It heavily borrows concepts and metrics from human psychology and readability studies (e.g., Kintsch's C-I model, Flesch-Kincaid grade level) but applies them to the context of machine comprehension without sufficient justification. The link is not obvious: a feature that makes a text difficult for a human (e.g., long sentences with complex clauses) might be trivial for a modern transformer-based model to process. The paper fails to adequately defend its core premise that metrics designed for human cognition are relevant proxies for difficulty in a machine context.
2.  **Overly Broad Scope and Disjointed Narrative:** The paper attempts to achieve too many goals at once: it revises a skill taxonomy, introduces a theoretical framework from psychology, performs a large-scale annotation, analyzes six datasets, and proposes new directions for system development. This breadth comes at the expense of depth. For example, the background on psychological models in Section 2.2 feels disconnected from the subsequent quantitative analysis. A more focused paper—for instance, concentrating solely on the annotation and the empirical analysis of prerequisite skills for *machine* comprehension—could have presented a clearer and more compelling argument.
3.  **Superficial Treatment of Readability and Its Impact:** The analysis of readability relies on a set of features from a single 2012 study, which may not represent the state of the art in computational readability. More importantly, the strong conclusion that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is highly dependent on how one defines and measures both "readability" and "difficulty." This claim is not sufficiently conditioned on the type of model being evaluated. For instance, while a simple lexical matching system might ignore syntactic complexity, an RC system that relies heavily on dependency parse features could be significantly impacted by it. This nuance is missing from the discussion.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the primary audience and goal of this work? Is the framework intended to diagnose difficulties for current ML models, or to create datasets that are difficult for an abstract, "ideal" reasoning agent? Can you provide a stronger theoretical argument for why traditional readability metrics are suitable for gauging text difficulty for modern neural RC architectures like LSTMs or Transformers?
2.  During the annotation process for prerequisite skills, what specific instructions were given to the annotators? Were they asked to identify the skills required by an ideal, logical system to derive the answer, or were they meant to reflect on the cognitive steps a human would take? This is crucial for interpreting the validity of the skill annotations in a machine comprehension context.
3.  Your conclusion about the weak correlation between readability and skill-based difficulty is a central point. How might this conclusion change depending on the RC model architecture used? For example, would you expect the same weak correlation for a system based on TF-IDF and for a system based on a pre-trained language model like BERT?
