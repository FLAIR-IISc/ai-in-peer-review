### Strengths
*   The paper's primary strength is its novel attempt to connect two distinct research areas: human-centric text readability and machine reading comprehension.

### Weaknesses
*   The central objective of the paper is not clearly articulated. Even after multiple readings, the main thesis and contributions remain difficult to discern.
*   The paper suffers from a fundamental ambiguity, consistently failing to distinguish between machine comprehension and text readability for humans. This makes it difficult to evaluate the paper's contributions to either field.
*   The literature review appears incomplete, omitting several significant and relevant works in the field of text readability.
*   The discussion of theoretical topics in Section 2.2 seems disconnected from the paper's main argument and methodology. Its relevance to the core research question is not established.
*   The paper's scope is overly broad, attempting to address too many distinct research questions simultaneously. This lack of focus weakens the overall argument. For instance, the question of whether text readability impacts RC dataset performance is a substantial topic that warrants a separate, in-depth analysis from the discussion of "prerequisite skills."

### General Discussion
*   The title is ambiguous. Given that "reading comprehension" and "readability" are terms typically associated with human cognition, the title should be clarified to explicitly state that the focus is on *machine* comprehension.
*   The paper's claim that its "dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is not sufficiently qualified. This conclusion is highly dependent on the model and features used for answer detection. For example, a system relying heavily on syntactic features (e.g., POS tags or dependency parses) might be more sensitive to readability variations.
*   The manuscript requires careful proofreading for grammatical errors and omissions. For instance, the sentence on page 1, "the question is easy to solve simply look..", is incomplete.
*   The paper states that datasets were annotated with "metrics." Please clarify this process. It is unclear how a dataset can be annotated with a metric, which is typically a calculated value rather than an annotation label.
*   The paper frequently conflates machine reading comprehension with human reading comprehension. While related, these are distinct and large research areas with different challenges and evaluation paradigms. This conflation undermines the clarity of the paper's arguments.
*   There appears to be a misunderstanding of the term "readability." The paper equates "readability of text" with "difficulty of reading contents," which is not the standard definition. Readability typically refers to the structural complexity of the text, not the semantic difficulty of the concepts it contains. I recommend consulting established work on this topic, such as:
    *   DuBay, W.H. 2004. The Principles of Readability. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact Information.
*   To resolve the pervasive ambiguity, the authors should explicitly distinguish their work from studies on human readability throughout the manuscript. For example, the sentence on page 1, "These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions," should be amended to "...correlate with the difficulty of the questions *for machine comprehension*."
*   In Section 3.1, it is unclear whether the "prerequisite skills" discussed are for humans or machines. If the focus is on machines, the paper needs to justify citing literature based on human cognition and explain how these concepts translate to computational models.
*   The experimental methodology lacks key details about the annotation process. Please specify the number of questions annotated per annotator. Crucially, were the annotators explicitly instructed to assess difficulty from the perspective of a machine comprehension system, rather than a human reader? This distinction is critical to the validity of the collected data.
