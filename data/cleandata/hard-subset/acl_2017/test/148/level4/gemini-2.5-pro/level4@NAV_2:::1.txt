**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two distinct axes: "prerequisite skills," which represent the reasoning complexity required to answer a question, and "readability," which measures the linguistic complexity of the context passages. The authors refine an existing taxonomy of prerequisite skills, grounding their classification in theories from human psychology and textual entailment. This framework is then applied to empirically analyze and compare six popular RC datasets. The central finding of this analysis is that there is only a weak correlation between the readability of a text and the difficulty of its associated questions, suggesting it is possible to create datasets that are easy to read but challenging to answer.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A comprehensive, annotated resource for fine-grained analysis.** The primary contribution is the detailed annotation of six diverse RC datasets with a taxonomy of 13 prerequisite skills. This publicly released resource is valuable for researchers seeking to perform error analysis, diagnose model weaknesses, or construct curated training and evaluation subsets focused on specific reasoning types.
2.  **An empirical analysis decoupling passage readability from question complexity.** A notable secondary contribution is the quantitative investigation into the relationship between passage readability and question difficulty. The finding that these two dimensions are not strongly correlated provides concrete evidence for a widely held intuition and underscores the value of datasets like MCTest, which pair simple language with complex reasoning challenges.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Analytical Framework for Datasets.** The paper introduces a valuable perspective for analyzing RC datasets by explicitly separating the difficulty of processing the text (readability) from the difficulty of reasoning to derive an answer (prerequisite skills). This distinction is intuitive and provides a more nuanced lens for understanding dataset characteristics than relying on aggregate performance scores alone.
2.  **Large-Scale Comparative Study.** A significant strength is the application of this framework across six different RC datasets, which provides concrete, comparative insights into their unique properties. For example, the analysis effectively highlights that SQuAD contains text that is difficult to read but requires relatively few reasoning skills, whereas MCTest presents the opposite challenge. The results are informative for practitioners selecting a dataset for a specific research goal.
3.  **Publicly Available Annotations.** The decision to release the skill annotations is a commendable contribution to the community. This resource enables other researchers to replicate the findings, build upon the analysis, or use the annotations to train and evaluate models in a more targeted manner, such as focusing on improving performance on a specific skill.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Conceptual Ambiguity Between Human and Machine Comprehension.** The paper's central weakness is a lack of clarity regarding its target domain: is it evaluating datasets for machine systems or analyzing them through the lens of human cognition? While the stated goal is to aid NLP, the methodology is heavily grounded in theories from human psychology (Sec 2.2, 3.1), and the defined skills (e.g., 'Elaboration') are described in cognitive terms. The paper fails to establish that these human-centric skills directly map to the computational operations of modern RC models. This ambiguity makes it difficult to interpret the results and their implications for building better machine learning systems.
2.  **Oversimplification of "Question Difficulty".** The analysis equates question difficulty with the *number* of required prerequisite skills. This is a strong and potentially problematic assumption. A single, complex reasoning step (e.g., multi-hop logical inference) could make a question far more difficult for a machine than three simple, co-occurring skills (e.g., coreference and punctuation). The study would be more convincing if it accounted for the intrinsic difficulty of each skill or the complexity of their interactions. Furthermore, the claim that readability does not affect difficulty is too strong, as it ignores that complex syntax (a key component of low readability) can directly cause failures in parsing or attention mechanisms, thus making a question harder for many models, irrespective of the high-level skills involved.
3.  **Insufficient Engagement with Readability Research.** While the paper uses readability as one of its two core pillars, its treatment of the topic is superficial. The work relies on a set of features from a single prior study without engaging with the extensive body of research in automated readability assessment. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of why the chosen metrics are the most appropriate for evaluating text for *machine* readers as opposed to human readers, and how its use of the term "readability" aligns with or differs from established definitions in that field.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the intended audience for this evaluation framework? Is it primarily for NLP researchers building models, for dataset creators, or for cognitive scientists? How might the framework and its interpretation change depending on this audience?
2.  The transfer of concepts from human psychology (e.g., bridging, elaboration) to the evaluation of machine systems is central to your work. Could you provide a more explicit justification for why these cognitive skills are the right units of analysis for current neural RC architectures? For example, how does a skill like 'Elaboration' map onto the operations within a Transformer-based model?
3.  Regarding the annotation process: were the human annotators instructed to assess the required skills from the perspective of an abstract machine agent, or from their own human perspective? How might this choice have influenced the resulting annotations and the paper's conclusions?
4.  You state that "readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the difficulty of their questions." How do you reconcile this claim with the fact that many models rely on linguistic components (e.g., parsers, tokenizers) that are known to perform less reliably on syntactically complex text (i.e., less readable text)?
