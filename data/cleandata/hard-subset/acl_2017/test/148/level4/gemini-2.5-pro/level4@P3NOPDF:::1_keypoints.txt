- Premise is interesting: connects traditional text readability with machine reading comprehension performance.  
- Major flaw: persistent ambiguity between human reading comprehension and machine comprehension throughout the paper.  
- Title and many statements are ambiguous and should explicitly specify a focus on machine comprehension.  
- Section 3.1 improperly imports human cognitive “prerequisite skills” without justifying their applicability to machine models.  
- Paper lacks a clear, focused research question and mixes two underdeveloped threads (readability impact and prerequisite skills).  
- Claim that readability does not affect question difficulty is too strong and not sufficiently supported given model- and feature-dependence.  
- Misunderstanding of "readability": authors conflate surface-level readability metrics with semantic or conceptual difficulty.  
- Annotation methodology is unclear—terminology, instructions, number of annotations, and whether annotators judged machine difficulty are unspecified.  
- Section 2.2 is disconnected from the empirical core and detracts from focus.  
- Literature review is incomplete and misses foundational work on text readability.  
- Writing and presentation need substantial proofreading; numerous grammatical errors impede clarity.  
- Reviewer requests: clarify annotation protocol, revise title/abstract to emphasize machines, define "readability" operationally, and map human skills to computational operations.  
- Recommendation: paper is not acceptable in current form and requires a major revision to sharpen question, define terms, and separate machine analysis from human cognition analogies.
