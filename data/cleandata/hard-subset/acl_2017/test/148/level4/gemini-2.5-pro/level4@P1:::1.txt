### **Summary**

This paper proposes a framework for evaluating reading comprehension (RC) datasets along two dimensions: the prerequisite skills required to answer questions and the readability of the context passages. The authors adapt a taxonomy of skills from psychology and NLP literature and apply it, along with a set of standard readability metrics, to annotate and analyze six prominent RC datasets (including MCTest, SQuAD, and QA4MRE). The central finding of their analysis is that the readability of a text has only a weak correlation with the difficulty of the associated questions, as measured by the number of prerequisite skills. The authors suggest this framework can guide the development of both more challenging datasets and more robust RC systems.

### **Contributions**

1.  **A comparative analysis of six RC datasets using a unified evaluation framework.** The primary contribution is the empirical study that characterizes and contrasts these datasets. This analysis provides valuable insights, most notably the finding that high text readability does not necessarily imply low question difficulty, with MCTest serving as a key example.
2.  **A revised taxonomy of prerequisite skills for reading comprehension.** The paper refines and expands upon a previous classification of skills, grounding it in concepts from both textual entailment and the psychology of human comprehension.
3.  **A publicly released set of annotations.** The authors have annotated samples from six datasets, which could serve as a useful resource for researchers looking to perform fine-grained analysis of RC models or to construct curated training/evaluation sets.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the important need for a deeper, more qualitative understanding of RC datasets beyond simple accuracy scores. By bringing together concepts from human text comprehension and machine reading, the authors propose a multi-faceted approach to evaluating dataset quality and difficulty. This is a welcome direction for the field.
2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Analysis:** The application of the proposed framework to six different datasets is a significant strength. This broad analysis allows for meaningful comparisons and reveals interesting characteristics of each dataset (e.g., SQuAD texts are difficult to read but the questions are relatively simple; MCTest texts are simple but require more complex reasoning).
3.  **Actionable Insights for Dataset and System Development:** The paper's conclusion—that question difficulty and text readability are largely orthogonal—is an important insight. This finding has practical implications, suggesting a path for creating more challenging RC datasets by focusing on complex reasoning over simple texts, and providing a potential roadmap for curriculum learning in RC system development.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Ambiguity of Scope (Human vs. Machine Comprehension):** The paper's greatest weakness is a persistent ambiguity about whether it is analyzing comprehension for humans or for machines. The framework, particularly the "prerequisite skills," is heavily derived from theories of human cognition (e.g., Kintsch, McNamara & Magliano), but it is applied to tasks intended for computational systems. The paper does not provide a strong justification for why this transfer is valid. Skills like "Elaboration" or "Meta-knowledge" are well-defined for humans but are highly abstract for current RC models. This conceptual blurring makes it difficult to interpret the results and weakens the paper's theoretical foundation. The title itself is ambiguous, as "reading comprehension" and "readability" are terms traditionally associated with human reading.
2.  **Contingency of the Main Conclusion:** The central claim that "the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty" is presented as a general finding. However, this conclusion is highly dependent on the type of RC model being considered. For example, a system that relies heavily on complex syntactic features (e.g., from a dependency parser) would likely be much more sensitive to poor readability than a system using primarily lexical matching or pre-trained embeddings. The paper fails to discuss this crucial contingency, thereby oversimplifying its main takeaway.
3.  **Insufficient Literature Review and Theoretical Grounding:** The paper's treatment of readability is somewhat superficial. It relies on a set of features from one work but misses the opportunity to engage with the broader literature on readability (e.g., DuBay, 2004). Furthermore, the connection between the theoretical discussion in Section 2.2 and the specific skills defined in Section 3.1 feels tenuous. The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly motivated why each psychological concept directly translates to a necessary skill for a machine, rather than simply presenting the concepts and then listing the skills.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical justification for applying a framework derived from human psychology directly to the evaluation of machine reading comprehension systems? How do you ensure that skills defined for human cognition are relevant and meaningful for current neural RC architectures?
2.  The annotation process is critical to your work. What specific instructions or definitions were given to the annotators to ensure they were evaluating the skills required for a *machine*, as opposed to a human, to answer a question? This seems particularly challenging for skills involving commonsense or world knowledge.
3.  Your central claim posits a weak link between readability and question difficulty. How do you expect this conclusion would hold for RC models that are architecturally dependent on syntactic parsing, where text complexity is known to impact performance? Would it not be more accurate to state that this relationship is model-dependent?
