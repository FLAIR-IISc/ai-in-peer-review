- Strengths:
- This work bridges two areas: text readability, traditionally studied for human understanding, and machine comprehension of text.

- Weaknesses:
- The paper’s central goal remains unclear despite multiple readings. The core argument is difficult to discern.
- The scope of the paper is ambiguous, fluctuating between machine comprehension and human text readability.
- The paper overlooks significant research within the field of readability.
- Section 2.2 contains theoretical discussion that appears unrelated to the paper’s main focus.
- The paper attempts to address too many research questions simultaneously, leading to a lack of focus and overall weakness. Investigating whether text readability impacts reading comprehension datasets should be considered separately from analyses of prerequisite skills.

- General Discussion:
- The title could be clarified to explicitly state the focus on *machine* comprehension, as “reading comprehension” and “readability” commonly refer to human understanding.
- The claim that “dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty” is potentially dependent on the answer detection methods and features employed (e.g., POS tags or dependency parses).
- The paper requires careful proofreading. Instances of grammatical errors and omissions (e.g., “the question is easy to solve simply look…” on page 1) detract from clarity.
- Clarification is needed regarding how datasets were annotated with “metrics.”
- The paper frequently conflates machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, despite them being distinct and substantial areas of research.
- The paper uses “readability of text” and “difficulty of reading contents” interchangeably, which is inaccurate. See DuBay (2004) for a clear distinction.
- Greater emphasis on differentiating this work from studies of human readability is needed to address the paper’s pervasive ambiguity. For example, when stating “These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions” (page 1), it should be clarified that this refers to *machine* comprehension.
- Section 3.1: It is unclear whether the discussed skills relate to human or machine understanding. If the intention is machine understanding, the citation of papers focusing on humans is questionable, and the extent to which these studies generalize to machines should be justified.
- Information regarding the annotation process is incomplete. Specifically, how many questions were annotated, and were annotators explicitly instructed to consider machine comprehension when assessing difficulty?
