## Review for ACL 2017

This paper attempts to connect the fields of text readability, traditionally focused on human comprehension, and machine reading comprehension (MRC). While the ambition of bridging these areas is commendable, the current manuscript suffers from significant clarity and focus issues.

**Strengths:**

The core idea of exploring a link between text readability and performance on machine comprehension datasets is potentially valuable. The paper identifies an interesting intersection of two relevant research areas.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of this paper is a lack of clear focus and articulation of its central goal. Despite multiple readings, the core contribution remains unclear. The paper appears to investigate several related but distinct questions simultaneously – the impact of text readability on MRC datasets, the prerequisite skills for MRC, and potentially even question readability – without prioritizing or clearly delineating them. This broad scope weakens the overall argument.

A significant source of confusion stems from the ambiguous framing of “reading comprehension.” The paper frequently conflates machine reading comprehension with human reading comprehension, despite these being distinct areas of study. This ambiguity is reflected in the title, which would benefit from explicitly specifying a focus on machine comprehension. Throughout the manuscript, it is crucial to clearly indicate whether discussions of skills or features pertain to machines or humans. For example, Section 3.1 cites work on human cognitive skills without explicitly justifying its relevance to machine comprehension or demonstrating that the cited work applies to machines.

The paper overlooks important contributions within the established readability literature. Furthermore, Section 2.2 includes theoretical discussions that appear tangential to the core research question. This detracts from the paper's coherence. 

The conclusions drawn from the dataset analysis – that readability does not directly affect question difficulty – are presented without sufficient nuance. This claim is likely dependent on the specific features and methods used for answer detection (e.g., POS tags, dependency parsing), a point that the paper fails to address. 

The manuscript requires substantial editing for clarity and grammatical accuracy. Instances of awkward phrasing and missing words (e.g., “the question is easy to solve simply look…”) detract from the professionalism of the work. 

The methodology for annotating datasets with “metrics” is unclear. It’s unclear what was annotated, how, and by whom. Specifically, details regarding the annotation process are missing, including the number of questions each annotator reviewed and whether annotators were explicitly instructed to consider machine comprehension when assessing difficulty.

The paper occasionally equates “readability of text” with “difficulty of reading contents,” which is conceptually inaccurate. The authors should consult established readability literature (e.g., DuBay, 2004) to refine their understanding of these terms. 

**General Discussion & Recommendations:**

To improve this paper, I recommend the following:

*   **Refocus the research question:** Clearly articulate a single, focused research question. Consider separating the investigation of text readability’s impact on MRC from the broader exploration of prerequisite skills.
*   **Clarify terminology:** Consistently distinguish between machine and human reading comprehension. Add clarifying phrases (e.g., "for machine comprehension") where ambiguity exists, such as on page 1 when discussing the correlation between text readability and question difficulty.
*   **Strengthen connections to relevant literature:** Thoroughly review and cite relevant work in both the readability and machine comprehension fields.
*   **Provide details on annotation process:** Clearly explain the annotation methodology, including the number of questions annotated per person and instructions given to annotators regarding the perspective (human vs. machine) they should adopt.
*   **Refine the analysis and discussion:** Provide a more nuanced discussion of the relationship between readability and question difficulty, acknowledging the role of specific features and methods used in MRC systems.
*   **Proofread carefully:** Address grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout the manuscript.



In its current state, the paper lacks the clarity and focus necessary for publication at a venue like ACL. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the manuscript and make its contributions more readily apparent.
