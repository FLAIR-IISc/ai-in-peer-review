- Strengths:
- This article effectively connects two fields: text readability for humans and machine comprehension of texts.

- Weaknesses:
- The paper’s central goal remains unclear. Despite reading it four times, I still struggle to fully grasp the core argument.
- The paper’s focus is ambiguous; it’s unclear whether it addresses machine comprehension or human text readability.
- The work overlooks important research in the field of readability.
- Section 2.2 contains a discussion of theoretical topics that feels largely unrelated to the main focus of the paper.
- The paper attempts to address too many questions simultaneously, resulting in a diluted and weakened argument. Questions such as the impact of text readability on reading comprehension datasets should be analyzed separately from the prerequisite skills discussed.

- General Discussion:
- The title is somewhat ambiguous. Clarifying that you are investigating machine comprehension, rather than human reading comprehension, would be beneficial. The terms “reading comprehension” and “readability” are commonly associated with human reading.
- Your statement that “dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty” is dependent on the specific methods and features used for answer detection – for example, the inclusion of POS or dependency parse features.
- The paper requires careful proofreading. There are noticeable errors, such as “the question is easy to solve simply look…” on page 1.
- Could you clarify how datasets were annotated with “metrics”?
- The paper conflates machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, which, while related, are distinct areas of research.
- The phrase "readability of text" should not be used interchangeably with “difficulty of reading contents.” Please refer to DuBay (2004) *The Principles of Readability* for further clarification on this distinction.
- More clearly distinguishing your work from research on readability for humans would be helpful, as the paper’s current presentation is ambiguous. For instance, on page 1, the statement “These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions” should be clarified to indicate that this observation applies specifically to machine comprehension.
- Section 3.1: Again, please clarify whether you are discussing skills relevant to humans or machines. If the focus is on machines, the citations to human-focused research seem inappropriate, and the extent to which those papers apply to machine comprehension needs to be justified.
- How many questions did the annotators review? Were the annotators explicitly instructed to consider machine comprehension when performing their annotations, rather than human understanding?
