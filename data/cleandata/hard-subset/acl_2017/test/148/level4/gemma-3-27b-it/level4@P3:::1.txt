## Review for ACL 2017 – Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability

This paper explores the relationship between dataset characteristics and reading comprehension (RC) task difficulty by adopting and applying two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors analyze six existing RC datasets, aiming to provide a more nuanced understanding of dataset quality beyond simple accuracy metrics.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Analysis of RC Datasets:** The most significant contribution is the detailed analysis of six popular RC datasets using both prerequisite skill and readability metrics. This provides a valuable empirical resource for the community, highlighting variations in dataset characteristics and suggesting potential areas for improvement.
2. **Refinement of Prerequisite Skill Categorization:** The authors attempt to refine existing categorization of prerequisite skills for RC, grounding it in theories from textual entailment and psychology. While the novelty of this categorization is incremental, the attempt to provide a stronger theoretical basis is valuable.
3. **Publicly Available Annotation:** The annotation of the six datasets with the proposed metrics and making the results publicly available constitutes a useful resource for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Timeliness and Relevance:** Evaluating the quality of RC datasets is a crucial and increasingly recognized need in the NLP community. This work addresses this need directly.
2. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** The application of the chosen metrics to a range of established datasets (MCTest, SQuAD, etc.) allows for a comparative analysis and reveals interesting trends, such as the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty. The results provide concrete evidence supporting the claim that seemingly simple datasets can still pose significant challenges to RC systems.
3. **Interdisciplinary Approach:**  The paper’s strength lies in bridging concepts from NLP, psychology, and linguistics to provide a more comprehensive evaluation framework for RC datasets.  Drawing upon theories of human text comprehension (McNamara & Magliano, Kintsch) provides a sound rationale for the chosen metrics.
4. **Resource Creation:**  The public release of the annotated datasets is a valuable contribution, enabling other researchers to build upon this work and further investigate the proposed metrics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Core Argument:** The central thesis of the paper – the relationship between readability and RC difficulty – is somewhat obscured by the breadth of the analysis. The paper attempts to address too many questions simultaneously, leading to a diluted focus and potentially weakening the overall impact.  A more focused investigation into the correlation (or lack thereof) between readability and difficulty, with clearer hypotheses, would be stronger.
2. **Ambiguity Regarding Target – Humans vs. Machines:** Throughout the paper, there's a critical ambiguity regarding whether the evaluation metrics are intended for *human* or *machine* reading comprehension. This confusion is particularly prevalent in Section 2.2 and 3.1, where psychological theories are invoked without clear justification for their relevance to machine understanding. The authors should explicitly state their focus and tailor the discussion accordingly.  Adding phrasing like "for machine comprehension" where appropriate is crucial.
3. **Limited Novelty in Prerequisite Skill Categorization:** While the refinement of prerequisite skills is a contribution, it lacks significant novelty. The improvements are largely organizational and do not represent a breakthrough in understanding the underlying cognitive processes required for RC.
4. **Annotation Details & Reliability:**  The paper lacks detail regarding the annotation process. It is unclear how annotators were trained and what specific guidelines were provided to ensure consistency.  While inter-annotator agreement is reported (90.1%), more information about potential disagreements and resolution strategies would be beneficial.  Were annotators explicitly instructed to consider the perspective of a *machine* when annotating?
5. **Missing Related Work:** The paper overlooks relevant work in the field of readability assessment, particularly within NLP. A more thorough review of this literature (e.g., citing DuBay, 2004) would strengthen the paper’s position and context.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the annotation process, including the training provided to the annotators and the specific guidelines they followed?
2.  How did you address disagreements among the annotators?
3.  Given the ambiguity regarding human vs. machine comprehension, could you clarify the intended target of your evaluation metrics and revise the paper to reflect this focus more consistently?
4.  What specific design choices within RC systems (e.g., feature engineering, model architecture) might be particularly sensitive to the readability and prerequisite skill characteristics you have identified?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The English language quality could be improved through careful proofreading. There are instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors.
*   Consider streamlining the paper by focusing more tightly on the correlation between readability and question difficulty, potentially relegating some of the broader discussions to future work.
*   Clarify the definition of "difficulty" within the context of RC. Is it measured by accuracy, time to answer, or other metrics?
