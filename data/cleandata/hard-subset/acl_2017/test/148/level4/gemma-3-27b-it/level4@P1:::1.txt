## Review: Evaluation Metrics for Reading Comprehension: Prerequisite Skills and Readability

**Summary:** This paper investigates the relationship between the difficulty of answering questions and the readability of text in reading comprehension (RC) datasets. The authors adopt two classes of evaluation metrics – prerequisite skills and readability – and apply them to six existing datasets (MCTest, SQuAD, QA4MRE, WDW, MARCO, and NewsQA). They aim to provide a more nuanced evaluation of dataset quality than is typically available, focusing on factors beyond simple accuracy. The analysis suggests a weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, indicating that datasets can be constructed that are easy to read but challenging to answer.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed analysis of six prominent RC datasets using a combination of prerequisite skill and readability metrics. This analysis reveals important characteristics of each dataset, highlighting differences in difficulty and complexity that are not captured by standard evaluation methods.
2. **Refined Prerequisite Skills Taxonomy:** The authors refine a previously proposed taxonomy of prerequisite skills for RC, grounding it in theories of textual entailment and human text comprehension. This revised categorization offers a more theoretically informed framework for understanding the cognitive demands of RC tasks.
3. **Publicly Available Annotations:** The annotated datasets are made publicly available, providing a valuable resource for researchers seeking to develop better RC datasets and systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Combination of Metrics:** The combination of readability metrics and a refined prerequisite skills taxonomy provides a more holistic and informative evaluation of RC datasets than relying on accuracy alone. This approach acknowledges the multifaceted nature of reading comprehension and offers insights into specific areas of difficulty.
2. **Detailed Dataset Analysis:** The thorough analysis of six diverse datasets offers valuable comparative insights. The observations regarding the characteristics of each dataset, such as the tendency of MS MARCO to contain nonsensical questions and the relatively high difficulty of QA4MRE, are particularly insightful.
3. **Practical Resource for the Community:** Making the annotated datasets publicly available is a significant contribution. This resource will enable other researchers to benchmark their models, design more challenging datasets, and further investigate the factors influencing RC performance.
4. **Theoretical Grounding:** The grounding of the prerequisite skills taxonomy in theories of textual entailment and human text comprehension adds credibility and theoretical depth to the work. The connections drawn to Kintsch’s and McNamara & Magliano’s frameworks are well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Scope:** The paper sometimes conflates human and machine reading comprehension, leading to ambiguity. While the analysis focuses on RC systems, the discussion of readability often defaults to human perceptions. This could be clarified throughout the paper.
2. **Correlation vs. Causation:** The claim that readability does not directly affect question difficulty should be presented more cautiously. The analysis demonstrates a weak correlation, but does not definitively prove a lack of causal relationship. The impact of specific answer detection methods (e.g., those relying on POS tags) should also be acknowledged.
3. **Annotation Methodology Concerns:** The methodology for annotating the datasets with prerequisite skills could be strengthened. Further detail is needed regarding the training of annotators and the procedures for resolving disagreements. It is important to explicitly state whether annotators were instructed to consider the perspective of a machine attempting to solve the task, or a human reader.
4. **Limited Discussion of Existing Readability Work:** While the authors leverage existing readability metrics, the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work in the field of human readability assessment. Acknowledging the established literature on readability (e.g., Dubay, 2004) would strengthen the paper's context.
5. **Overly Ambitious Scope:** The paper attempts to address multiple questions simultaneously, potentially diluting its focus. Separating the investigation of readability’s impact from the analysis of prerequisite skills might lead to more focused and impactful results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the training process for the annotators? What specific instructions were given to ensure consistency and alignment with the intended focus on machine reading comprehension?
2.  How were disagreements among annotators resolved? What was the inter-annotator agreement rate *after* any resolution process?
3.  Regarding the claim about the weak correlation between readability and question difficulty, can you discuss the limitations of using Pearson’s correlation coefficient for this type of analysis? Are there other statistical methods that might provide more nuanced insights?
4.  Could you provide examples of questions from the datasets that demonstrate the phenomenon of being easy to read but difficult to answer, and explain why these questions are challenging for RC systems?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The English language quality could be improved through careful proofreading. Several minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasing detract from the clarity of the paper. For example, "the question is easy to solve simply look.." (page 1) should be revised.
*   The title could be more specific, perhaps by explicitly mentioning "machine reading comprehension."
*   The discussion of “readability of text” versus “difficulty of reading contents” is valid but could be presented more concisely.
*   In Section 3.1, clarify whether the prerequisite skills are intended for human or machine comprehension and how the cited papers relate to the machine learning context.
