This paper investigates the characteristics of reading comprehension (RC) datasets through the lens of two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors apply these metrics to six existing datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, and NewsQA) to understand the relationship between question difficulty and text readability, and to provide a more nuanced evaluation of dataset quality than typically available. The primary contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a systematic application of both prerequisite skill and readability metrics to a diverse set of RC datasets, providing a comparative analysis of their inherent difficulty; (2) a refined categorization of prerequisite skills, integrating insights from textual entailment and human text comprehension research; and (3) the public release of the annotated datasets, enabling further research into dataset characteristics and the development of more robust RC systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Dataset Analysis:** The application of both prerequisite skill and readability metrics to six established RC datasets represents a significant undertaking. This comparative analysis provides valuable insights into the diverse characteristics of these datasets, going beyond simple accuracy metrics. The identification of datasets like MCTest, which are easy to read but difficult to answer, highlights the limitations of relying solely on text complexity as a measure of dataset quality.
2. **Refined Prerequisite Skill Categorization:** The authors’ attempt to reorganize and refine the categorization of prerequisite skills, drawing from both NLP (textual entailment) and psychology (human text comprehension), is a strong point. Integrating concepts like bridging and elaboration provides a more nuanced framework for understanding the cognitive demands of RC tasks, potentially leading to more targeted system development.
3. **Publicly Available Annotated Datasets:** The release of the annotated datasets is a valuable contribution to the research community. This resource will allow other researchers to build upon this work, explore different analytical approaches, and develop new methods for evaluating and improving RC datasets. It facilitates reproducibility and further investigation into the relationship between dataset characteristics and system performance.
4. **Highlighting Discrepancies in Dataset Design:** The work effectively demonstrates that high readability does not necessarily equate to easy questions, and vice-versa. This observation challenges common assumptions about dataset construction and emphasizes the need for more sophisticated evaluation methods that consider both textual complexity and the cognitive skills required for question answering.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clear Motivation & Scope:** While the paper identifies a need for more nuanced dataset evaluation, the central research question – the relationship between readability and question difficulty – feels somewhat under-defined and broad. The paper attempts to address multiple aspects of dataset quality, leading to a lack of focus and a somewhat meandering narrative. A tighter focus on the correlation (or lack thereof) between the two main metric classes would strengthen the core argument.
2. **Ambiguity Regarding Target Audience (Humans vs. Machines):** Throughout the paper, there’s a blurring of the lines between readability and prerequisite skills as they pertain to human readers versus machine comprehension systems. The citation of psychological studies on human text comprehension without explicitly clarifying their relevance to machine learning models creates confusion. A clearer distinction between these perspectives is crucial.
3. **Limited Correlation Analysis:** While the authors report a weak correlation between readability and prerequisite skills, the analysis remains largely descriptive. A more rigorous statistical analysis, exploring potential confounding factors and considering different correlation measures, would provide stronger support for their conclusions. Investigating *which* specific readability features correlate with *which* prerequisite skills could also offer valuable insights.
4. **Annotation Methodology Concerns:** The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the annotation process. Specifically, it’s unclear how annotators were trained to identify and label prerequisite skills, and how inter-annotator disagreements were resolved. Further details on the annotation guidelines and quality control measures are needed to assess the reliability and validity of the annotations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the annotation process? Specifically, what training was provided to the annotators to ensure consistent application of the prerequisite skill definitions, and how were disagreements resolved?
2.  The paper mentions a potential disconnect between readability metrics designed for humans and their applicability to machine comprehension. Could you discuss the limitations of using these human-centric metrics for evaluating RC datasets, and explore potential alternative metrics more directly relevant to machine learning models?
3.  Given the weak correlation observed between readability and prerequisite skills, what specific hypotheses could explain this disconnect? Are there other dataset characteristics (e.g., question style, knowledge domain) that might mediate this relationship?
4.  You mention that the annotation methodology cannot evaluate the competence of selecting relevant sentences. Do you envision any future work to incorporate this ability into a more comprehensive dataset evaluation framework?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider clarifying the title to explicitly indicate the focus on machine reading comprehension.
*   The sentence "the question is easy to solve simply look.." on page 1 requires editing.
*   Explore and cite relevant work in the field of readability assessment beyond Vajjala and Meurers (2012), such as the work of Dubay (2004) mentioned in the weaknesses above.
*   Adding a table summarizing the inter-annotator agreement for each prerequisite skill would strengthen the validation of the annotations.
