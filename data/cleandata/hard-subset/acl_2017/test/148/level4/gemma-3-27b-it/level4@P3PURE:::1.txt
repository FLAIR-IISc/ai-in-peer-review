## Review for ACL_2017

**Strengths:**

This article attempts to bridge two related but distinct fields: text readability, traditionally focused on human understanding, and machine comprehension of texts.

**Weaknesses:**

The core goal of this paper remains unclear. Despite multiple readings, the central argument is difficult to discern. The paper suffers from significant ambiguity regarding its focus – is it machine comprehension or human text readability? Furthermore, it overlooks important and relevant work within the readability literature. 

Section 2.2 contains a discussion of theoretical topics that appear largely unrelated to the paper’s central investigation. The paper attempts to address too many questions simultaneously, resulting in a lack of focus and diminished strength. For example, the question of whether text readability impacts performance on reading comprehension (RC) datasets should be analyzed separately from prerequisite skills required for successful comprehension.

**General Discussion:**

The title is somewhat ambiguous and would benefit from clarification to explicitly state the focus on *machine* comprehension, rather than human reading comprehension. The terms "reading comprehension" and "readability" commonly refer to human-level abilities. 

The claim that “dataset analysis suggested that the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty” is potentially dependent on the specific methods and features employed for answer detection (e.g., the use of POS or dependency parse features).

The paper requires thorough proofreading; there are several grammatical errors and omissions, such as the incomplete sentence on page 1 ("the question is easy to solve simply look…"). The methodology for annotating datasets with "metrics" is also unclear and requires further explanation.

The paper frequently conflates machine reading comprehension with human reading comprehension. While related, these are distinct areas of research. Moreover, the statement that “readability of text” is equivalent to “difficulty of reading contents” is inaccurate and contradicts established readability research (see, for example, DuBay, W.H. 2004. *The Principles of Readability*. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact information).

Greater emphasis should be placed on distinguishing this work from research on readability for humans, as the paper's current presentation is highly ambiguous. For example, on page 1, the statement “These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions” should be qualified with “for machine comprehension.”

Section 3.1 again raises the question of whether the discussed skills relate to humans or machines. If the intent is to address machine comprehension, the citation of papers focused on human readability is questionable, and the extent to which these papers are applicable to machine learning should be justified.

Finally, information regarding the annotation process is lacking. How many questions did the annotators review, and were they explicitly instructed to consider the perspective of machine comprehension when performing their annotations?
