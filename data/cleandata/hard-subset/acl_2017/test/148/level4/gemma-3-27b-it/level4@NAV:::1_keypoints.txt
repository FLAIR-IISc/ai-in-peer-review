- Strength: Novelly combines research on human text readability with machine text comprehension.
- Weakness: Central objective is unclear and the core argument is hard to discern.
- Weakness: Paper’s focus is ambiguous—uncertain whether it targets machine comprehension or human readability.
- Weakness: Fails to acknowledge and discuss important prior work in readability assessment.
- Weakness: Section 2.2 contains theoretical material that appears tangential to the main research question.
- Weakness: Attempts to address too many questions at once, diluting impact; relationship between text readability and RC dataset performance should be studied separately from prerequisite skill analysis.
- Title suggestion: Clarify that the work targets machine reading comprehension rather than human reading.
- Methodological caveat: The claim that readability does not affect question difficulty may depend on the specific answer-detection methods and features used (e.g., POS/ dependency-based features).
- Editorial issue: Manuscript needs careful proofreading to fix grammatical errors and omissions.
- Methodology concern: The process for annotating datasets with “metrics” is insufficiently explained.
- Conceptual confusion: The paper frequently conflates machine reading comprehension with human reading comprehension despite their differences.
- Conceptual clarity needed: Distinguish “readability of text” from “difficulty of reading contents” (e.g., DuBay 2004).
- Framing: Emphasize differences from human-readability research and explicitly state when findings refer to machine comprehension.
- Citation relevance: If the target is machines, justify citing papers focused on human skills and explain their relevance.
- Annotation detail needed: Specify how many questions annotators reviewed and whether annotators were instructed to consider machine comprehension.
