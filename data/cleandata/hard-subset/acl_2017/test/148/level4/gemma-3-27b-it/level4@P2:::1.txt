This paper investigates the relationship between dataset characteristics and reading comprehension (RC) difficulty by adopting two classes of evaluation metrics: prerequisite skills and readability. The authors analyze six existing RC datasets (QA4MRE, MCTest, SQuAD, WDW, MARCO, and NewsQA) using these metrics, aiming to provide a more nuanced understanding of dataset quality than simple accuracy measures allow. The primary contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a comprehensive analysis of six popular RC datasets through the lens of both prerequisite skills and readability metrics, revealing insights into their strengths and weaknesses; (2) a refined categorization of prerequisite skills for RC, incorporating insights from textual entailment and psychology research; and (3) the public release of the annotated datasets, providing a valuable resource for the RC community.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Metrics:** The application of readability metrics, traditionally used in educational psychology, to the analysis of RC datasets is a significant contribution. This provides a fresh perspective on dataset construction and evaluation beyond standard accuracy benchmarks. The pairing with prerequisite skills creates a more holistic evaluation framework.
2. **Detailed Dataset Analysis:** The analysis of six datasets provides a broad overview of the landscape of RC datasets. The quantitative results (Tables 2 & 3) provide concrete evidence supporting the authors’ claim that readability and question difficulty are not strongly correlated.  The observations about each dataset's characteristics are insightful.
3. **Refined Skill Categorization:** The reorganization of prerequisite skills, grounded in textual entailment and human text comprehension research, represents a thoughtful refinement of previous work.  The effort to connect these skills to specific cognitive processes (bridging, elaboration) adds theoretical grounding to the analysis.
4. **Publicly Available Resource:** The annotation of the datasets and their public release is a valuable contribution to the research community. This allows other researchers to build upon this work and conduct further investigations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Scope:** The paper frequently conflates human and machine reading comprehension. While the motivation stems from improving *machine* comprehension, the language and citations often refer to *human* cognitive processes without clearly distinguishing the two. This ambiguity weakens the impact of the findings. The introduction and title should more explicitly focus on machine comprehension.
2. **Weak Correlation, Not Necessarily Insignificance:**  The authors conclude that readability doesn't "directly affect" question difficulty based on weak correlations. However, correlation does not equal causation, and a weak correlation doesn't necessarily invalidate a potential relationship.  Further investigation, potentially with different metrics or analysis techniques, is needed to definitively rule out an influence.  The discussion of POS/dependency parse features is a good start, but needs further elaboration.
3. **Methodological Limitations in Annotation:** The annotation procedure relies on identifying *required* skills, but doesn’t address the systems’ ability to *discover* relevant information.  As the authors acknowledge, this limits the scope of the evaluation. The paper also lacks details on inter-annotator reliability beyond the single reported percentage.
4. **Overly Broad Scope & Lack of Focus:** The paper attempts to address numerous aspects – dataset evaluation, skill categorization, and annotation methodology – simultaneously. This breadth weakens the overall focus and makes it challenging to follow the central argument. A more focused investigation of the relationship between readability and question difficulty, independent of the prerequisite skill analysis, would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more detail on the annotation process, including specific guidelines given to the annotators regarding the target audience (machines vs. humans) and examples of annotation disagreements?
2.  How was inter-annotator reliability calculated (e.g., Cohen’s Kappa)? Can you provide a breakdown of agreement across different prerequisite skills?
3.  Given the limitation that your methodology doesn't evaluate sentence selection competence, do you plan to explore methods for evaluating that aspect of RC systems?
4.  You mention that the choice of features for answer detection might influence the correlation between readability and difficulty. Could you elaborate on how different feature sets might affect this relationship, and potentially explore this empirically?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The English in the paper needs careful proofreading. There are several instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors (e.g., "easy to solve simply look…").
*   Consider explicitly differentiating between “readability of text” and “difficulty of reading contents” as the authors acknowledge, and cite DuBay (2004).
*   Section 2.2 feels somewhat disconnected from the main focus of the paper. While providing background on human text comprehension is valuable, its relevance to machine comprehension should be clearly articulated.
*   The annotation procedure, while described, lacks specific detail on *how* the metrics were applied to the datasets. Examples would be helpful.
