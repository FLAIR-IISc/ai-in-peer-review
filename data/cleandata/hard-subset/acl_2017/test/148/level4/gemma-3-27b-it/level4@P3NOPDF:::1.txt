## Review for ACL 2017

This paper attempts to bridge the fields of text readability, traditionally studied in the context of human comprehension, and machine reading comprehension (MRC). While the intention to connect these areas is potentially valuable, the current manuscript suffers from a lack of clarity and focus, hindering its overall impact.

**Strengths:**

The core idea of investigating the relationship between text readability and performance on MRC datasets is a novel and interesting one. The paper highlights a potential interplay between these two areas that warrants further investigation.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary and most significant weakness of this paper is its lack of a clearly defined research question. Despite multiple readings, the central goal remains ambiguous. The manuscript attempts to tackle several interconnected, yet distinct, questions simultaneously—including the impact of text readability on RC datasets, prerequisite skills for MRC, and the correlation between readability and question difficulty—resulting in a diluted and ultimately weak investigation. These avenues would be better served by independent exploration.

A recurring issue throughout the paper is the imprecise use of terminology. The title, and much of the text, uses “reading comprehension” without consistently clarifying whether it refers to machine or human comprehension. This ambiguity is problematic, as “reading comprehension” and “readability” are frequently associated with human reading processes. The authors need to explicitly and consistently denote when discussing MRC versus human readability. For example, the statement on page 1 (“These two examples show that the readability of the text does not necessarily correlate with the difficulty of the questions”) would benefit from specifying “for machine comprehension.”

The paper overlooks relevant work in the established field of readability. A more thorough literature review, particularly of established readability metrics and their limitations, is necessary. 

Section 2.2 presents a largely irrelevant discussion of theoretical topics that detracts from the paper’s focus. The connection between these concepts and the core research question is unclear.

The methodology section raises several concerns. The phrase "annotate datasets with 'metrics'" is unclear and requires further explanation. It is crucial to clarify *how* the datasets were annotated and what specific judgments annotators were asked to make.  Crucially, the paper fails to detail the annotation process adequately. How many questions did annotators evaluate? Were annotators explicitly instructed to consider machine comprehension when making their assessments, or were they assessing readability for human readers? The potential for annotator bias due to a human-centric understanding of “readability” is significant and needs to be addressed.

The authors’ claim that “the readability of RC datasets does not directly affect the question difficulty” is potentially influenced by the specific methods and features used for answer detection. The paper doesn't acknowledge this dependence; factors like the reliance on POS or dependency parse features could alter this relationship.

Finally, the manuscript requires careful proofreading. Grammatical errors and omissions (e.g., “the question is easy to solve simply look…” on page 1) detract from the paper’s credibility.  The authors should pay attention to nuanced differences in meaning; for instance, “readability of text” is distinct from “difficulty of reading contents” (DuBay, W.H. 2004. *The Principles of Readability*. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact information).

**General Discussion:**

This paper presents an interesting premise but falls short of delivering a compelling analysis due to its ambiguity and lack of focus. The conflation of human and machine reading comprehension, combined with a superficial treatment of the readability literature and a poorly defined methodology, significantly weakens the contribution. 

To improve this work, I recommend the following:

*   **Refine the research question:** Focus on a specific aspect of the relationship between text readability and MRC, rather than attempting to address multiple questions simultaneously.
*   **Clarify terminology:** Consistently distinguish between human and machine reading comprehension throughout the paper.
*   **Strengthen the literature review:**  Thoroughly review relevant work in both readability and MRC, particularly concerning existing readability metrics.
*   **Provide a detailed methodology:**  Clearly explain the annotation process, including the number of questions evaluated and instructions given to annotators.
*   **Address potential biases:** Discuss and mitigate the risk of annotator bias due to a human-centric understanding of readability.
*   **Proofread carefully:**  Address grammatical errors and improve the overall clarity of the writing.



I rate this paper as **Weak Reject**. While the initial concept holds promise, the current manuscript requires substantial revision to address the identified weaknesses.
