## Review:

**Strengths:**

- This work represents a novel intersection of research in human text readability and machine comprehension of text.

**Weaknesses:**

- The central objective of this paper remains unclear. Despite multiple readings, the core argument is difficult to discern.
- The paper suffers from significant ambiguity regarding its focus – is it primarily concerned with machine comprehension or human text readability?
- Important and relevant work within the field of readability assessment is not acknowledged or discussed.
- Section 2.2 includes theoretical discussions that appear tangential to the primary research question.
- The paper attempts to address an overly broad range of questions simultaneously, resulting in a diluted and less impactful analysis. Specifically, the relationship between text readability and performance on reading comprehension (RC) datasets warrants independent investigation, separate from prerequisite skill analysis.

**General Discussion:**

- The title could benefit from clarification to explicitly indicate a focus on *machine* reading comprehension, as "reading comprehension" and "readability" are commonly understood in the context of human reading.
- The claim that dataset analysis indicates readability does not directly affect question difficulty is potentially dependent on the specific methods and features employed for answer detection (e.g., those leveraging POS or dependency parsing).
- The manuscript requires careful proofreading to address grammatical errors and omissions (e.g., the example on page 1: "the question is easy to solve simply look…").
- The methodology for annotating datasets with "metrics" requires further explanation.
- The paper frequently conflates machine reading comprehension and human reading comprehension, despite their distinct natures and research areas.
- A clarification regarding the distinction between "readability of text" and "difficulty of reading contents" is necessary. (See DuBay, W.H. 2004. *The Principles of Readability*. Costa Mesa, CA: Impact information.)
- Greater emphasis should be placed on differentiating this work from research on readability for human readers, given the current ambiguity. For example, when presenting findings like the correlation between text readability and question difficulty on page 1, explicitly specify that this refers to *machine* comprehension.
- Section 3.1 again raises concerns about the intended target of the analysis – humans or machines? If the latter, the citation of papers focused on human skills requires justification and a clear explanation of their relevance to machine comprehension.
- Further detail is needed regarding the annotation process: How many questions did annotators review, and were they explicitly instructed to consider the perspective of machine comprehension during annotation?
