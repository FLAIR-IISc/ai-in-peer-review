Summary
This paper proposes LSTM-based architectures to classify whether a connective in context signals a causal relation between two events. Two variants are compared: Pair LSTM (independent encoders for the two text spans) and Stated Pair LSTM (the second LSTM is initialized with the final state of the first). The models use pre-trained GloVe embeddings and dense layers for classification. Experiments on the AltLex corpus (both original and bootstrapped versions) compare against a strong frequency baseline and the feature-rich SVM system of Hidey and McKeown (2016). The neural approach yields modest but consistent gains, and the authors release TensorFlow code/models.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical finding that simple two-tower LSTM context encoders can modestly outperform a strong feature-based SVM baseline for causal connective disambiguation on AltLex, across both corpus variants.
- Comparative analysis of architectural and training choices (independent vs state-passed LSTMs; padding strategy; optimizer), with the interesting negative result that independent encoders tend to work better than sequential state passing.
- Release of TensorFlow code/models, facilitating replication and future work on causal disambiguation.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines. On non-bootstrapped AltLex, Pair LSTM Max (Adadelta) improves F1 on the Causal class (75.40 vs 73.76) and accuracy (77.90 vs 71.86). On bootstrapped AltLex, best neural configs also edge the SVM (F1 up to 81.96 vs 80.90; accuracy 80.68 vs 79.58). The gains are modest yet consistent.
- Addresses coverage limitations of syntax/lexicon-centric methods by leveraging contextual encoding; the models learn beyond connective priors (beating the per-connective majority baseline).
- Reproducibility. Public release of TensorFlow code/models is valuable for a low-resource subarea with few datasets.
- Some thoughtful analyses: class imbalance study with reduced Non-Causal set and significance testing; qualitative examples highlighting ambiguous cases (e.g., “which then”) and causative verb polysemy (e.g., “break” without causation).
- Clear delineation of the task into two subtasks (disambiguation vs argument identification), focusing this paper on the first; this scoping is appropriate for AltLex.

Weaknesses
- Conceptual framing and theory of causation are muddled. The Introduction reads like Related Work and does not clearly state positive contributions or a principled stance on causation. The paper seems to invoke a counterfactual (Lewis-style) notion but then rejects counterfactuality in places without engaging with known issues or committing to an explicit theory. This weakens the motivation for what the model is learning and how ground truth relates to causation vs discourse labeling.
- Ambiguities in examples and argument structure. The discussion around the example at line 256 is confusing: truth/falsity judgments and event decomposition are not justified, leaving the causal claim unclear. This undermines the intended illustration of the task.
- Methodological clarity issues:
  - Figure 1’s caption is misleading (it depicts only Pair LSTM) and the diagram is imprecise: unclear symbols, missing layer semantics, and ambiguous depiction of LSTM connections/state passing.
  - The padding description (line 480) is unclear/incoherent: pad(x) = {max, mean, mode} for x in R^{n×d} is not a padding operation; it appears to describe length selection rather than sequence padding. If padding is a TensorFlow/training artifact (fixed-length batching), that should be stated, along with why the chosen strategy would affect causal meaning encoding.
  - Hyperparameter reporting is inconsistent (e.g., lines 578 vs 636 for “best” settings, optimizer effects, padding choices), making it difficult to interpret conclusions about model behavior.
  - Many essential details are missing or scattered (tokenization, vocabulary/OOV handling, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, LSTM directionality/hidden sizes, batch size, early stopping, number of runs), and no variance/confidence intervals are reported. Given small gains, robustness is a concern.
- Overinterpretation of architectural results. The claim (line 587) that independent LSTMs outperforming state-passed LSTMs implies no relation between the arguments is unwarranted and contradicts expectations that some sub-sentential information is shared. Alternative explanations (initialization instability, vanishing/overwriting of transferred state, optimization dynamics, mismatch between state transfer and downstream fusion) are more plausible and should be tested via controlled ablations.
- Evaluation scope is narrow. Results are only on AltLex; claims about general causal disambiguation would be stronger with out-of-domain transfer (e.g., PDTB explicit/implicit causals, Causal-TimeBank) or synthetic test sets isolating specific ambiguities. The claim that the system disambiguates “which then” better than prior work is based on a single example; systematic testing on many constructed ambiguous instances is needed.

Questions to Authors
- Theory/labels: What theory of causation do you assume? If counterfactual is relevant, how do you reconcile rejecting it in examples? How do AltLex labels map to causation vs discourse relation (Contingency.Cause vs Temporal) in ambiguous cases?
- Example at line 256: Please formalize the event decomposition and truth-conditional judgments you use to argue (non)causality.
- Architecture details: Are LSTMs unidirectional or bidirectional? Hidden dimensions? Do you fine-tune GloVe? Where is dropout applied (embeddings, recurrent, dense)? What L2 strengths were actually used?
- Padding (line 480): Precisely define the three strategies. Are you selecting a target sequence length by max/mean/mode over training lengths, then padding/truncating? Or are you pooling over time? If it’s a batching constraint, why should it affect semantic encoding?
- Training protocol: How many random seeds per config? Report mean±std and significance vs baselines across seeds. Was class weighting or focal loss explored given imbalance? How were early stopping and learning rate schedules handled?
- Interpretation of state transfer: Did you try residual connections, attention between encoded spans, or sharing encoder parameters without state passing? Could the negative result be due to the specific initialization scheme rather than lack of interdependence?

Additional Comments
- Tighten the Introduction: clearly state contributions and your stance on what “causal” means in this task; move survey-like material to Related Work.
- Fix Figure 1 (caption and notation) and unify hyperparameter reporting into a single, reproducible experimental section.
- Add out-of-domain tests and a constructed evaluation set targeting frequent ambiguities (since/after/which then), to substantiate claims about improved disambiguation.
