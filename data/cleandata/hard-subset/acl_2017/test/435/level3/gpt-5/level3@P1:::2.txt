Summary
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans and a potentially causal lexical marker, decide whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that causality in language is not tied to fixed syntactic templates and therefore propose an LSTM-based architecture that encodes the two event contexts (first event e1 and the concatenation of the marker with e2) using pre-trained GloVe embeddings, followed by dense layers for classification. They evaluate on the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), including both the original and a “bootstrapped” extension, compare against (i) a connective-majority baseline and (ii) the feature-engineered SVM from Hidey & McKeown, and provide qualitative analysis on ambiguous markers.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- An empirically grounded demonstration that simple neural encoding of event context (LSTM over e1 and [marker+e2]) improves over a strong, feature-rich SVM baseline on AltLex, with better precision and a more balanced P/R tradeoff, especially on ambiguous cases.
- An exploration of architectural/training choices (independent vs state-initialized LSTMs; Adadelta vs Adam; length normalizations described as “max/mean/mode”) and their effect on performance, including observations that independent encoders outperform state-initialized ones and Adam yields a better precision–recall balance.
- Qualitative evidence that the model generalizes beyond lexical memorization (e.g., handling ambiguous markers like “which then” and non-causal uses of causative verbs).

Strengths
- Clear motivation and task setup. The paper convincingly argues that fixed lexical/syntactic patterns are insufficient for causality and motivates context encoding. The two-step framing (causal meaning classification vs argument identification) is sensible and focuses the scope of the work.
- Consistent, mostly positive empirical gains. On the non-bootstrapped set, the best model (Pair LSTM with max padding, Adadelta) improves over the SVM baseline by 2.13 F1 on the causal class and 7.72 accuracy; on the bootstrapped set, several configurations slightly outperform the SVM in F1, with notably higher precision and smaller P/R gaps. The model also beats a strong majority-class-per-connective baseline, showing learning beyond connective priors.
- Useful qualitative analysis. Examples show the model disambiguating cases where the connective’s training prior is misleading and handling highly ambiguous markers (“which then”). This supports the claim that contextual encoding is helpful.
- Sensible ablations and observations. Comparing “Stated Pair LSTM” (initializing the second LSTM from the first) vs independent encoders is informative, with negative results also reported. Optimizer and length strategy variants provide further insight into stability and P/R tradeoffs.
- Reproducibility efforts. Code and data are said to be released, which is valuable for a task with limited resources.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. Using LSTMs to encode sentence/pair contexts for relation classification is by now standard; the architecture (two encoders + MLP) is a straightforward application. The incremental gains over a strong SVM baseline (often ~0.5–1.5 F1 on the bootstrapped set) will be viewed as modest, and the superiority of neural models over feature engineering is expected.
- Experimental rigor and reporting gaps. It appears that the “best configuration” may have been selected on the test set rather than a dev set, risking overfitting and inflating reported gains. There is no significance testing for the main tables; while McNemar’s test is reported on the reduced, rebalanced subset, the primary results (full datasets) lack statistical validation. More detail is needed on splits, early stopping, and hyperparameter selection criteria.
- Relation to implicit discourse relation classification is underdeveloped. The task formulation is very close to implicit connective recognition in PDTB-style frameworks. There is no comparison to state-of-the-art implicit discourse relation classifiers (e.g., neural baselines with BiLSTM/attention over [Arg1, Arg2]) nor a clear argument why those methods would be inadequate here.
- Dataset and bootstrapping clarity. The bootstrapping procedure that expands AltLex by ~15% is insufficiently described, making it difficult to assess potential biases. The class imbalance is severe, and while a balanced subset is explored, macro-averaged metrics on the full set would be helpful. The annotation quality of the Wikipedia/Simple Wikipedia alignments is a potential concern that is not analyzed.
- Missing baselines and analysis. Simpler neural baselines (e.g., a single BiLSTM over [e1, marker, e2] with pooling; a 1-layer LSTM with average pooling; a CNN baseline) are absent, making it hard to attribute gains to the pairwise encoding vs just switching to any neural encoder. The model often favors precision over recall; there is little analysis of why (e.g., by connective type, sentence length, marker ambiguity, or domain artifacts). Also, the “max/mean/mode” description for padding is confusing (see questions).

Questions to Authors
- Task positioning: How does your setup concretely differ from implicit discourse relation classification? Could standard implicit DR baselines (e.g., BiLSTM over [Arg1, Arg2] with attention) be applied directly, and if so, how do you expect them to perform relative to your model?
- Model selection: What was the development protocol? Which configuration choices were made on a dev set vs inspected on the test set? Was early stopping used, and if so, on which metric and split?
- Significance: Can you report statistical significance for the main results in Table 5 on the full datasets (not just the reduced subset)?
- Padding/length strategy: The “max/mean/mode” notation in Equation (1) is unclear. Are these operations over sequence lengths (i.e., choosing a target length) or pooling operations over token representations? Please clarify the exact preprocessing.
- Input representation: Why concatenate the marker with e2 only (rather than encode [e1, marker, e2] jointly or treat the marker as a separate input)? Did you try a single-sequence BiLSTM with attention over the marker position?
- Bootstrapping: Please describe the bootstrapping procedure in detail (seed sets, expansion rules, noise control, manual validation rates).
- Reproducibility: The TSV files use 0–1–2 codes; what do these values denote? Do you have redistribution rights for any reposted data? Please clarify licensing and provide a README for replication.

Additional comments
- Include simpler neural baselines and a CNN baseline to isolate where gains come from. Report macro-F1 per class and per-connective breakdowns to probe ambiguity handling. Provide error analysis on false negatives to understand the precision–recall skew.
- The paper is well written overall; adding architectural diagrams and a clearer description of data splits and preprocessing would further improve clarity.
