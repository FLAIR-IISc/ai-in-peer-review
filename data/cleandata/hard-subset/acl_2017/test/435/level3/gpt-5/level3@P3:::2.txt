Summary
The paper addresses clause-level causal meaning disambiguation when the causal relation is not signaled by an unambiguous connective, focusing on the AltLex corpus. It proposes LSTM-based clause representations with pre-trained GloVe embeddings; the two events are encoded (either independently—Pair LSTM—or with the second initialized from the first—Stated Pair LSTM), followed by three tanh dense layers and a softmax classifier. The method is compared to the feature-rich SVM system of Hidey & McKeown (2016) and to a per-connective majority-class baseline. Results show modest gains in F1 (roughly 0.5–1.5 points) and improved precision, with qualitative examples indicating some generalization to ambiguous AltLexes.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An empirical study showing that simple LSTM-based encoders can slightly outperform a strong feature-engineered SVM on AltLex causal disambiguation, especially in precision, with qualitative evidence of contextual generalization.
2) Architectural observation that encoding the two events independently (Pair LSTM) tends to outperform initializing the second LSTM from the first (Stated Pair LSTM), and that choice of optimizer affects precision/recall trade-offs.
3) Analysis of connective distributions and a small set of examples illustrating model success on ambiguous/rare markers.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and writing: The paper carefully motivates why causality cannot be reduced to fixed lexical/syntactic patterns and articulates the two-step view (causal meaning vs. argument identification). The task definition and discussion of AltLex ambiguity (e.g., since/after) are lucid.
- Sound, simple neural baseline: The LSTM+GloVe architecture is appropriate for sequence meaning encoding and avoids dependence on hand-crafted lexical resources, addressing coverage limitations in prior work.
- Consistent, if modest, quantitative gains: Across both AltLex versions, the best LSTM configurations improve F1 over Hidey & McKeown by about 0.5–1.5 points (reported once as 2.13) and often increase accuracy, with a notable precision advantage—useful in applications where false positives are costly.
- Qualitative analysis: Examples where the model correctly classifies cases with ambiguous/uncommon markers (e.g., which then, noncausative uses of break) support the claim that contextual encoding helps beyond per-connective priors.
- Public code/data: Releasing code and data (even if older) aids reproducibility and community adoption.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing baselines: Applying a standard LSTM architecture to AltLex is incremental. The claim that LSTMs are better than CNNs is argued but not empirically validated; no CNN baseline or even simpler neural baselines (1-layer LSTM, avg-embeddings+MLP, bag-of-ngrams LR) are reported. This weakens methodological contributions.
- Evaluation protocol concerns: The paper appears to select the “best configuration” on the test set after exploring many hyperparameter choices (pooling mode, optimizer, dropout/regularization), which risks overfitting and inflating reported gains. It is unclear how development data was used for model selection.
- Statistical rigor and reporting inconsistencies: No significance testing is reported for the main comparisons to the SVM baseline, despite small margins. There are inconsistencies (e.g., F1 gain cited as 2.13% while tables suggest smaller gains; accuracy improvement stated as 7.72% whereas Table 5 differences appear smaller). Variance across random seeds is not reported.
- Dataset and task clarity: The “bootstrapping” step that expands the corpus (~15%) is not described, making it hard to assess distribution shifts or quality. The AltLex annotation quality (Wikipedia/Simple Wikipedia alignment) is potentially noisy; inter-annotator agreement and error analysis are missing. The TSV label coding (0-1-2) is undocumented, and the legality/rights to repost derived datasets are unclear.
- Relation to implicit discourse relation work: The task resembles PDTB implicit relation classification/implicit connective recognition. The paper does not clearly distinguish its setting or explain why prior neural models for implicit DR classification cannot be applied, nor does it attempt cross-benchmark evaluation.
- Model behavior not fully analyzed: The system tends to favor precision over recall. The paper does not probe why (class imbalance, thresholding, use of connective token l with e2, reliance on unambiguous markers) or provide per-marker performance on ambiguous items. The “zero-padding strategies” described as max/mean/mode appear to be pooling choices; the description is confusing.

Questions to Authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- Model selection: Which configuration choices were made on a development set, and which on the test set? Please provide results from a single, dev-selected configuration evaluated once on the test set, with mean±std over multiple seeds. Include significance testing vs. Hidey & McKeown (e.g., McNemar, bootstrap).
- Bootstrapping: What exactly is the bootstrapping procedure? What patterns were induced, how were candidates filtered/validated, and what is the estimated precision? Please report IAA or a human validation sample of bootstrapped items.
- Task relation: How does your task differ from PDTB implicit relation classification and implicit connective recognition? Why can’t prior methods from that literature (e.g., BiLSTM/attention, pair encoders) be applied? Could you compare to a standard implicit-relation neural baseline on AltLex?
- Baselines: Did you try a CNN (to substantiate your LSTM-vs-CNN claim), a 1-layer LSTM, or an avg-embeddings+MLP baseline? Please add these and a bag-of-ngrams LR baseline.
- Reproducibility: What do the 0/1/2 labels in the TSV files denote? Are you authorized to redistribute the dataset or only scripts to reconstruct it from Wikipedia/Simple English? Please clarify licensing.
- Analysis: Why does the model favor precision? Did you tune decision thresholds, use class weights, or try focal loss? Provide per-marker results on ambiguous markers, an ablation without the lexical marker l, and an error typology.
- Input design: Why concatenate l with e2 only? Did you try symmetric encoders, BiLSTMs with attention/cross-attention, or using broader sentential context?

Additional comments
- Please clarify the “zero-padding” terminology; max/mean/mode are pooling/aggregation choices, not padding.
- Consider reporting macro-averaged scores and calibration metrics, and adding results where training is balanced by class weighting rather than downsampling.
