Summary
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two events and a potentially causal lexical marker, decide if the relation is causal. The authors propose LSTM-based architectures that encode the two event spans (and the connective) and predict Causal vs. Non-Causal. Experiments on the AltLex dataset show modest but consistent improvements over a strong majority-by-connective baseline and the feature-based SVM system of Hidey and McKeown (2016), with larger gains on the non-bootstrapped split. The paper also explores optimizer and input-length pooling choices and reports that encoding events independently with two LSTMs performs better than initializing the second LSTM with the first. Code/models (TensorFlow) are reported to be released.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A context-sensitive neural approach to causal marker disambiguation that outperforms a strong feature-based baseline (SVM) on AltLex, especially in precision, supporting the claim that contextual encoding helps.
- An architecture finding: independent encoders for the two arguments (Pair LSTM) perform better than a “stated/initialized” variant, suggesting limited benefits of sequential state sharing for this task on AltLex.

Strengths
- Empirical advance on a relevant task: On AltLex (both non-bootstrapped and bootstrapped splits), the proposed models generally outperform both the majority-by-connective baseline and the feature-rich SVM, with clear precision gains and competitive recall. This supports the paper’s thesis that context modeling helps beyond lexical-connective priors and fixed resources.
- Focus on context rather than prespecified syntactic patterns or causative-verb lists. This directly addresses known coverage limitations in prior work and aligns with the AltLex motivation.
- Sensible experimental design choices: evaluation on both corpus versions (with different distributional properties), reporting P/R/F1 for the Causal class and Accuracy, and a useful reduced-class-imbalance analysis with significance testing (McNemar) that strengthens the claim of robustness.
- Reproducibility: providing TensorFlow code/models (as claimed) meaningfully increases impact and enables follow-up comparisons and diagnostics.
- Interesting negative result: the stated/initialized LSTM underperforms the independent encoders. Although the interpretation offered is tentative, the observation itself is a useful datapoint for future architectural choices.

Weaknesses
- Conceptual framing of “causation” is unclear and occasionally contradictory. The paper invokes counterfactual and temporal constraints but does not commit to a clear theory or reconcile known pitfalls (e.g., when counterfactuals do not align with intuitions). The discussion around “the argument…broke the window” is confusing (truth conditions and event decomposition are not explained), which undermines the task definition the models optimize for.
- Missing or underspecified baselines/ablations. To support the claim that “encoding sentence meaning is required,” comparisons to simpler and stronger baselines are needed: (i) averaged word embeddings + logistic regression; (ii) full-sentence BiLSTM/CNN without argument splitting; (iii) a connective-only classifier; (iv) a shallow lexicalized linear model over bags of words. Without these, it is hard to isolate what in the architecture drives gains.
- Padding/pooling methodology is unclear and possibly mis-specified. The paper describes choosing among max/mean/mode “padding” lengths with an equation that conflates padding with sequence pooling/length selection. If this is a batching artifact, it should be stated; if it affects representation (e.g., truncation), its impact must be carefully justified and evaluated. As written, it is difficult to reproduce or trust the interpretation of these choices.
- Figure 1 and architectural exposition are imprecise. The caption suggests it depicts both variants, but it shows only the Pair LSTM; symbols and layer semantics are ambiguous (e.g., where exactly LSTM outputs are taken, whether hidden vs final states, how the dense layers are applied). This is exacerbated by inconsistencies in hyperparameter reporting across sections, making it hard to map the figure to the configurations that achieved the best results.
- Interpretation overreach from limited evidence. The claim that the model “disambiguates which then” better than prior work is based on a single instance; more systematic stress tests (multiple constructed ambiguous cases and per-connector analysis, especially for “ambiguous-in-train” and “label-shift” cases) are needed. More broadly, gains on the bootstrapped split are small; statistical significance for the main tables is not reported, so the strength of the improvement is unclear.

Questions to Authors
- Task definition and inputs: For implicit/AltLex cases where l is not a canonical connective, how exactly are e1, l, and e2 segmented and concatenated? Are there cases without l, and if so, how are they handled?
- Padding/length strategy: Please clarify what “max/mean/mode” refers to. Is this the target padding length chosen from the empirical length distribution? Are sequences truncated? Does this choice affect only batching efficiency or also the learned representation?
- LSTM outputs: Do you use final hidden states, mean pooling over time, or something else as the sequence encoding before the dense layers? Please specify dimensions and exact operations.
- Baselines: Can you report results for (i) connective-only classifier, (ii) averaged embeddings + logistic regression, and (iii) single-sentence BiLSTM/CNN baselines?
- Generalization and lexical memorization: Can you provide per-connector results and a subset evaluation where test-time connectors are unseen in training (or are ambiguous in training)? This would more directly support the “context over lexicon” claim.
- Significance and variability: What are the standard deviations across random seeds? Are the improvements over B2 in Tables 5 statistically significant?

Additional Comments
- Please fix Figure 1 caption and redraw the diagram to clearly depict both architectures, with unambiguous symbols and layer descriptions.
- Ensure hyperparameter reporting is consistent across the paper (e.g., optimizer settings, dropout, regularization, and any LSTM size).
- The Introduction repeats Related Work and lacks a concise statement of contributions; tightening this section would improve clarity.
- Consider reporting macro-F1 in addition to Accuracy, given class imbalance, and include significance tests for main comparisons.
