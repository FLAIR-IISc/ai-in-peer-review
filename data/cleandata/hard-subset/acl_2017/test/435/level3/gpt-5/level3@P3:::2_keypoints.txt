- Paper tackles clause-level causal meaning disambiguation for AltLex where causality is not signaled by clear connectives, using LSTM encoders with pre-trained GloVe and a tanh+softmax classifier.
- Two encoding variants: Pair LSTM (events encoded independently) and Stated Pair LSTM (second LSTM initialized from first).
- Empirical finding: LSTM-based encoders yield modest F1 gains (~0.5–1.5 points) and improved precision over Hidey & McKeown (2016) SVM baseline, with qualitative examples of generalization.
- Contribution: Shows simple LSTM encoders can slightly outperform a strong feature-engineered SVM on AltLex, especially in precision, with contextual generalization evidence.
- Contribution: Architectural observation that independent encoding (Pair LSTM) outperforms initialized encoding (Stated Pair LSTM), and optimizer choice affects precision/recall trade-offs.
- Contribution: Analysis of connective distributions plus examples demonstrating success on ambiguous and rare markers.
- Strength: Clear problem framing and writing that motivates the distinction between causal meaning and argument identification and explains AltLex ambiguity.
- Strength: Sound, simple neural baseline that avoids reliance on hand-crafted lexical resources and addresses coverage limitations.
- Strength: Consistent, if modest, quantitative gains and a notable precision advantage useful when false positives are costly.
- Strength: Qualitative analysis showing model handles ambiguous/uncommon markers, supporting contextual encoding claims.
- Strength: Public release of code/data aids reproducibility and adoption.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—applying a standard LSTM to AltLex is incremental and lacks empirical comparison to CNNs or simpler neural baselines.
- Weakness: Evaluation protocol concerns—appears to select best configuration on test set after hyperparameter search, risking overfitting; unclear use of development data.
- Weakness: Lack of statistical testing, reporting inconsistencies (mismatched F1/accuracy claims), and no variance across random seeds.
- Weakness: Bootstrapping procedure that expands corpus is undocumented; dataset quality, annotation noise, IAA, and label coding (0/1/2) are unclear.
- Weakness: Relation to PDTB implicit relation work is not clarified, and prior neural models for implicit relations are not compared or discussed fully.
- Weakness: Model behavior analysis is incomplete—system favors precision over recall but reasons (class imbalance, thresholding, reliance on markers) and per-marker performance are not probed.
- Weakness: Confusing terminology (e.g., “zero-padding” used for pooling choices) and unclear input design decisions (concatenating marker l with e2 only).
- Question: Which hyperparameters/configurations were chosen on dev vs. test, and can you provide single dev-selected test results with mean±std over seeds and significance tests vs. SVM?
- Question: What exactly was the bootstrapping procedure, the induced patterns, filtering/validation steps, and estimated precision (include human validation/IAA)?
- Question: How does this task differ from PDTB implicit relation classification and implicit connective recognition, and why can’t prior methods be applied?
- Question: Did you try CNNs, 1-layer LSTM, avg-embeddings+MLP, or bag-of-ngrams LR baselines to substantiate claims?
- Question: What do the TSV labels 0/1/2 denote, and are you authorized to redistribute the derived dataset or only scripts to reconstruct it?
- Question: Why does the model favor precision—did you tune thresholds, use class weights, or try focal loss—and can you provide per-marker results, ablations, and an error typology?
- Question: Why concatenate marker l only with e2—did you try symmetric encoders, BiLSTMs with attention/cross-attention, or broader context?
- Additional comment: Clarify “zero-padding” terminology—max/mean/mode are pooling/aggregation choices, not padding.
- Additional comment: Consider reporting macro-averaged scores, calibration metrics, and results using class weighting instead of downsampling.
