Summary and contributions
The paper proposes LSTM-based models for disambiguating whether a sentence expresses a causal relation given two events and a potentially causal lexical marker. The core hypothesis is that contextual sentence meaning, captured via neural sequence encoders, is necessary to resolve the ambiguity of markers such as since/after or AltLex expressions. Two architectures are evaluated: Pair LSTM (independent LSTMs over e1 and l+e2, followed by dense layers) and Stated Pair LSTM (second LSTM initialized with the first’s final state). Experiments on both the “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” variants of the AltLex corpus benchmark the models against (i) a marker-majority baseline and (ii) the feature-rich SVM of Hidey and McKeown (2016). The best neural models deliver small but consistent gains in F1/accuracy, with error analysis highlighting cases where context helps.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that neural sequence encoders improve causal meaning disambiguation of lexical markers over a strong feature-based baseline on AltLex, with consistent gains across corpus variants and sampling regimes.
2) A practical neural setup for the task, exploring input factorization (e1 vs. l+e2), padding strategies, and optimizers, yielding the finding that independent LSTMs generally outperform a state-initialized pair.
3) Reproducibility resources (TensorFlow code/models), facilitating follow-up work.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines: On AltLex, the best neural configurations outperform the marker-majority and the SVM baseline in both “non-bootstrapping” (e.g., +2–8% F1/accuracy over prior work) and “bootstrapping” settings, and maintain advantages under a rebalanced sampling regime. The gains, while modest, are consistent and achieved without external lexical resources.
- Clear task formulation and scope: The paper usefully separates causal meaning classification from causal argument identification, arguing against hand-crafted syntactic patterns and in favor of context-based representations suited to AltLex’s broader coverage.
- Thorough comparative modeling: The paper reports multiple architectural/control choices (Pair vs. Stated Pair LSTMs; padding via max/mean/mode; Adadelta vs. Adam), revealing an interesting and repeatable pattern: independent encoders tend to outperform the state-initialized variant. This is informative for the community beyond the specific task.
- Focused error analysis: The qualitative examples (e.g., disambiguating non-causal uses of causative-looking verbs such as break; handling “which then”) illustrate that contextual cues, not just connective identity, are doing work.
- Reproducibility: Releasing code/models (as stated) increases the paper’s utility and credibility and should allow others to test on new data or extend the architecture.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical framing is muddled and duplicative: The introduction largely repeats related work and alternates between syntactic/semantic debates and cognitive claims without committing to a concrete operational theory of causation for NLP. The counterfactual discussion is invoked but not grounded, and the arguments around examples (e.g., the “argument broke the window” case around line 256) are confusing, with unclear event decomposition and truth-conditional judgments. This weakens the motivation for the neural design choices.
- Limited dataset and modest gains: All quantitative claims hinge on AltLex only. The improvements over Hidey & McKeown are relatively small, and no cross-dataset evaluation (e.g., PDTB explicit/implicit, Causal-TimeBank, or held-out AltLex subsets controlled by connective distribution) is provided. Given that many connectives are unambiguous in train/test, it is hard to assess generalization to genuinely hard cases.
- Architectural rationale underdeveloped and sometimes contradictory: The main interpretive claim—independent LSTMs outperform state-initialized ones because “assumptions about the relation between the meanings of the two inputs do not hold” (around line 587)—is not compelling. Alternative explanations (e.g., poorer optimization, information bottlenecks from truncation, or the l+e2 concatenation already encoding cross-argument context) are not examined via ablations (attention, shared weights, residual connections, bidirectionality).
- Reporting and clarity issues: Figure 1’s caption and diagram are misleading (appears to show only Pair LSTM; missing layer semantics; ambiguous connections). The padding discussion (around line 480) lacks clarity on whether padding is only for batching or intended as a modeling choice; its semantic relevance is not justified. Hyperparameter descriptions are inconsistent across sections (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636), making it difficult to interpret which settings produced which results. Statistical significance is reported only in one rebalanced setting; standard tests on the main comparisons are missing.
- Over-interpretation from single examples: The claim that the model handles highly ambiguous expressions like “which then” better than prior work is supported by a single test case. A more systematic stress test (constructing many minimal pairs varying the same expression across contexts) is needed to substantiate this.

Questions to authors
- Task definition and theory: What precise operational definition of “causal” guided your modeling and annotation usage? How are events segmented (verbs vs. NPs), and how should borderline pragmatic/temporal contingencies be treated? Please clarify your stance on counterfactuality and temporal precedence in your label space.
- Architecture details: Are the LSTMs bidirectional? Are weights shared between the two encoders? Did you try attention over e1 and e2 or over l within l+e2? How are sequences truncated/padded (max length), and is the “max/mean/mode” operation applied across time steps or across batch padding only?
- Hyperparameters and reproducibility: Please provide a single consolidated table of final hyperparameters per best model, random seed handling, early stopping criteria, and number of runs averaged. Are the released models/code exact to the reported results, including data splits?
- Controls and ablations: What is the performance of (i) markers-only, (ii) events-only, and (iii) replacing GloVe with learned embeddings? Did you test shared-encoder Siamese variants or simple averaging vs. LSTM encodings?
- Robustness to connective ambiguity: Can you report results specifically on ambiguous markers and AltLexes (controlled subset) and provide multiple constructed minimal-pair tests (e.g., “which then”, “since”, “after”) to validate the contextual disambiguation claim?
- Significance testing: Please add statistical significance for all main-table comparisons on the original splits.

Additional comments
- Improve Figure 1 (accurate captioning, clear depiction of both architectures, layer sizes/activations).
- Tighten and de-duplicate Introduction vs. Related Work; clearly state your concrete contributions early.
- Clarify the role and interpretation of the padding strategies and why they might matter semantically.
