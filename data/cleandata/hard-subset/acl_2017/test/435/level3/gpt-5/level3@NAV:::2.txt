Summary and contributions
The paper studies disambiguating causal vs. non-causal clause relations when the cue is not an explicit connective, using the AltLex corpus. The authors argue that sentence-level meaning must be encoded to resolve causal ambiguity and propose a GloVe-initialized LSTM architecture with three tanh dense layers and softmax, showing modest F1 improvements over prior feature-engineered systems. They provide quantitative results and qualitative examples illustrating generalization.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evaluation establishing a simple LSTM-based clause representation that outperforms the feature-based AltLex baseline by a small margin on F1 and accuracy.
2) An analysis highlighting cases where the model correctly generalizes beyond explicit connectives and engineered lexicons.
3) A problem framing that emphasizes causal-meaning classification separate from argument identification.

Strengths
- Clear problem statement and thorough related work positioning; the writing is precise and easy to follow.
- Solid empirical treatment on the only sizable public resource for this task (AltLex), with both “bootstrapping” and “non-bootstrapping” versions.
- The proposed LSTM architecture is simple, reproducible, and largely resource-agnostic (GloVe + LSTM), which reduces dependency on handcrafted lexicons.
- Qualitative analysis includes informative examples showing correct generalization (e.g., ambiguous markers and causative verbs in non-causal usage).
- Reported improvements in accuracy and F1 over Hidey & McKeown, with the best setting reaching higher precision, which is desirable for downstream reliability.

Weaknesses
- Potential test-set tuning: several configuration choices appear selected based on test performance, risking optimistic gains; no held-out dev selection is convincingly demonstrated.
- Missing significance testing and limited discussion of annotation reliability (English/Simple Wikipedia), undermining confidence in small F1 gains (~0.5–1.5%; elsewhere claimed as 2.13%).
- Incomplete methodological detail: the bootstrapping procedure that expands the corpus by ~15% is not described; unclear 0–1–2 TSV coding; uncertainty about redistribution rights for the dataset copy.
- Novelty is incremental: LSTM outperforming CNNs/feature-based methods for sequence semantics is expected; model design choices (three tanh layers, padding strategies) lack principled justification.
- Insufficient baselines/ablations: no 1-layer LSTM, BiLSTM, or simpler pooling baselines; limited analysis of why precision is favored over recall and how to tune this trade-off.

Questions to authors
- How does your task differ from implicit connective recognition, and why can’t prior PDTB-style implicit relation models be applied directly?
- Which configuration choices were fixed on dev vs. tuned on test? Provide dev-set model selection and test-only reporting.
- Detail the bootstrapping procedure used to extend AltLex and its quality controls.
- Clarify the 0–1–2 coding in the released TSVs and the licensing/permissions for dataset reposting.
- Reconcile the reported improvements (0.5–1.5% vs. 2.13% F1). Add significance tests.
- Add simpler baselines (1-layer LSTM, avg-embeddings MLP) and an analysis of precision–recall trade-offs.
