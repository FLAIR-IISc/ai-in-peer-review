Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical finding: Contextual neural encoders modestly but consistently improve disambiguation of whether a connective signals a causal relation on the AltLex corpus; the authors release TensorFlow code/models. This is the primary contribution.
- Method: A simple two-input LSTM architecture (Pair LSTM vs Stated Pair LSTM), with comparisons across padding strategies and optimizers. Novelty is incremental.
- Preliminary qualitative analysis of ambiguous expressions, suggesting potential generalization beyond hand-engineered features.

Strengths
- Reproducibility and transparency: Code/models are released; experiments consider both “bootstrapping” and “non-bootstrapping” AltLex splits; baselines include a strong frequency-based baseline and prior SVM work.
- Consistent empirical support: Neural models outperform B1 and often B2, with improvements in precision and overall accuracy, and the trend holds across splits and some downsampling analyses.
- Useful design observations: Reporting on independent vs sequential LSTMs, optimizers, and padding strategies offers practical insights for future work.
- Corpus-driven framing: Moves away from brittle syntactic/lexical heuristics toward contextualized representations aligned with current practice.

Weaknesses
- Conceptual framing of causation is unclear. The paper invokes counterfactual (Lewis-style) ideas without committing to a theory or addressing known issues; the Introduction reads like a second Related Work and does not articulate a crisp positive claim or stance.
- Method exposition has ambiguities. Figure 1’s caption is misleading (showing only Pair LSTM), the diagram lacks symbol definitions/layer semantics, and LSTM connections are not precisely specified.
- Padding discussion (line 480) is confusing: it is unclear whether padding is purely a TF/training artifact and why max/mean/mode over sequence length would encode causal meaning; masking is not discussed.
- The interpretation that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones (line 587) is speculative and counter to expectations about shared sub-sentential information; no controls for parameter count or shared weights are provided.
- Reporting inconsistencies: Hyperparameter settings (e.g., lines 578 vs 636) are hard to reconcile, making it difficult to attribute gains; evidence for disambiguating “which then” hinges on a single example; evaluation is limited to AltLex and gains over B2 are modest.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- What explicit theory of causation underpins your labels and modeling assumptions? Please clarify your stance and its implications for annotation and modeling.
- Clarify the line 256 example: event decomposition, truth/falsity judgments, and the resulting causal claim.
- Precisely specify padding/aggregation: Are padding tokens masked? Why should aggregation choice affect meaning?
- Provide a consolidated hyperparameter table per result, parameter counts, and whether encoders share weights; add a systematic test suite of constructed ambiguous cases (e.g., many “which then” instances).
