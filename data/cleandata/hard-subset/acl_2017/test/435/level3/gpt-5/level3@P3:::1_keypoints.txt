- Paper proposes two LSTM-based models (Pair LSTM and State-Passed Pair LSTM) to classify whether a connective signals a causal relation, using pre-trained GloVe and dense classifiers on AltLex.
- Authors compare models to a frequency baseline and a feature-rich SVM, reporting modest but consistent neural gains and releasing TensorFlow code/models.

Contributions:
- Empirical finding: simple two-tower LSTMs modestly outperform a strong SVM baseline on AltLex.
- Comparative analysis of architecture/training choices, with the negative result that independent encoders beat state-passed LSTMs.
- Public release of code/models to facilitate replication.

Strengths:
- Consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines on both original and bootstrapped AltLex.
- Models address coverage limits of syntax/lexicon methods by using contextual encodings and beat per-connective majority baselines.
- Reproducibility through public code/models for a low-resource subarea.
- Thoughtful analyses: class imbalance experiments, significance testing, and qualitative ambiguous examples.
- Clear task scoping separating disambiguation from argument identification.

Weaknesses:
- Conceptual framing of causation is muddled; no clear theoretical stance tying labels to a principled notion of causation.
- Some example analyses (e.g., line 256) are confusing and not justified, undermining illustrative claims.
- Figure 1 and its caption are misleading and diagram notation is imprecise.
- Padding description is unclear/incoherent and may conflate length selection with padding semantics.
- Hyperparameter reporting is inconsistent and scattered, complicating reproducibility and interpretation.
- Many essential experimental details are missing (tokenization, OOV handling, embedding fine-tuning, LSTM directionality/size, batch size, early stopping, number of runs).
- No variance/confidence intervals reported despite small gains, raising robustness concerns.
- Overinterpretation: concluding independence of arguments from better performance of independent LSTMs is unwarranted without controlled ablations.
- Evaluation is narrow (only AltLex); no out-of-domain or synthetic tests to validate general causal disambiguation claims.

Questions for authors:
- What theory of causation underlies the labels, and how are counterfactual notions treated or reconciled with annotations?
- Please formalize the event decomposition and truth-conditional judgments for the example at line 256.
- Are LSTMs unidirectional or bidirectional, what are hidden sizes, and are GloVe embeddings fine-tuned?
- Where is dropout applied, and what L2 regularization strengths were used?
- Precisely define the three padding strategies (max/mean/mode): are these target lengths for padding/truncation or something else?
- How many random seeds were used per config, were means±std reported, and was class weighting or focal loss explored for imbalance?
- How were early stopping and learning-rate schedules handled?
- Did you try alternatives to state transfer (residuals, attention, shared parameters) to test the interpretation of the negative result?

Additional suggestions:
- Tighten the Introduction to state clear contributions and a stance on “causal” vs move survey material to Related Work.
- Fix Figure 1 caption/notation and unify hyperparameter reporting into a single reproducible section.
- Add out-of-domain evaluations and a constructed test set targeting frequent ambiguous connectives (e.g., since/after/which then).
