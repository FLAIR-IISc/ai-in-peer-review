Summary
This paper studies causal meaning disambiguation: given two events and a potentially causal lexical marker, predict whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that causal meaning depends on sentence-level context and propose LSTM-based architectures that encode (e1) and (l+e2), followed by dense layers. Experiments on the AltLex dataset (both “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” versions) show small but consistent gains over a feature-rich SVM baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), with additional analyses on optimizer, padding strategy, and a class-rebalanced subset. Qualitative examples illustrate cases where the model correctly disambiguates ambiguous markers.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A simple contextual neural encoder (LSTM+MLP) improves causal meaning disambiguation over a strong feature-based SVM on the AltLex dataset, including the more challenging bootstrapped split.
2) Architectural/experimental comparison: Clear comparison between two LSTM variants (independent vs. state-initialized), padding strategies, and optimizers, including a class-rebalanced setting that probes robustness to label skew.
3) Task framing: A clean decomposition of causal relation processing into (i) causal meaning classification and (ii) argument identification, and a focus on the former independent of hand-crafted causal lexica.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements and robustness: The proposed models outperform both a lexical-prior baseline and a strong SVM with external lexical resources on two corpus versions. Gains are observed in F1 and accuracy, and the model exhibits reasonable behavior across several hyperparameter choices. The qualitative examples convincingly show generalization on genuinely ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then”) and non-causative uses of typically causative verbs (“break”).
- Clear motivation and task setup: The paper is well written, with a careful discussion of linguistic background and why context encoding is preferable to pattern-based rules or fixed windows (CNN kernels). The two-step task formulation is sensible.
- Reduction in feature engineering: The approach eschews handcrafted lexical/syntactic features and curated lexica, relying only on pre-trained word embeddings, which suggests better portability to new domains/languages.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The architecture (two LSTMs + MLP) is straightforward and expected to outperform feature-based methods; no new modeling ideas, training objectives, or insights into causal semantics are introduced. The empirical gain is modest (reported improvements on the order of ~1–2 F1 points in one setting).
- Evaluation protocol clarity and statistical rigor: The paper reports many configurations (optimizers, padding, two architectures) and appears to identify the “best” on the test set; no development set procedure is described. This risks overfitting to the test set and inflating reported gains. Statistical significance is not reported for the primary results (only a McNemar test on the reduced corpus for one configuration), leaving uncertainty about robustness.
- Dataset/label issues and task scope: The model assumes gold (e1, l, e2) spans, which limits practical applicability; event and marker identification are non-trivial. AltLex labels (derived via alignment/bootstrapping over Wikipedia/Simple Wikipedia) may be noisy; the bootstrapping process is not described here, and no annotation quality analysis is provided. The performance may be largely driven by unambiguous markers; while the paper notes class/marker distributions, it lacks stratified results on ambiguous markers to demonstrate true contextual disambiguation.
- Missing baselines/ablations: Important baselines are absent, such as (i) average-of-embeddings + MLP/logistic regression, (ii) a single LSTM over the concatenated input without splitting into (e1) and (l+e2), (iii) a purely lexical baseline using only the marker, and (iv) random vs pre-trained embeddings. Ablations on the contribution of the marker token(s) vs events would clarify what the model learns.
- Analysis depth: The model often favors precision over recall; the paper notes this but does not analyze causes (class imbalance, thresholding, calibration) or attempt remedies (class weighting, oversampling, threshold tuning). More error analysis by phenomenon (e.g., temporal vs causal uses of “since/after”) would strengthen the claims.

Questions to authors
- Validation protocol: How were hyperparameters and architecture variants selected? Was there a held-out development set? If not, please provide a proper dev set tuning procedure and report a single fixed configuration on the test set, with variance across random seeds and significance testing.
- Relation to implicit discourse relation classification: How does this task differ from implicit connective recognition in PDTB-style work? Could prior methods (e.g., implicit discourse relation classifiers) be applied directly to AltLex instances? What prevents a direct comparison?
- Bootstrapping details: Please describe the bootstrapping procedure that expanded AltLex (selection criteria, noise estimates, any human validation) and its effect on label noise. Any inter-annotator agreement figures?
- Marker/ambiguity breakdown: Can you report per-marker results and results restricted to ambiguous markers? Also control for marker frequency to ensure improvements are not driven by unambiguous cases.
- Data/code clarity and licensing: If you are releasing data/code, what is the meaning of the 0/1/2 label coding in the TSV files? Do you have redistribution rights for all materials (including any augmented data)?
- Precision vs recall: Did you experiment with class weighting, focal loss, or decision threshold tuning to balance precision and recall? Any insights into why Adam vs Adadelta shifts this balance?

Additional comments
- Please clarify the “zero-padding strategies” description/equations. As written (pad(x) = {max, mean, mode}), it is unclear whether you are choosing sequence length, pooling, or padding targets; some mathematical notation appears inconsistent with the text.
- Consider adding stronger yet simple baselines (bag-of-embeddings + MLP; concatenated-sentence BiLSTM) and ablations (without marker tokens; random vs pre-trained embeddings).
- Reporting: Provide confidence intervals or significance tests for the main results; report averages over multiple random seeds. Include per-class metrics and confusion matrices by marker.
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. Releasing code and exact preprocessing (tokenization, casing, truncation strategy, max lengths) would aid reproducibility.
