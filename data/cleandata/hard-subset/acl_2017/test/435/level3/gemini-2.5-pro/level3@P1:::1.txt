**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which it defines as classifying whether a lexical marker connecting two events signals a causal relation. The authors argue that since causation is not tied to fixed syntactic structures, neural models that can encode the broader semantic context of the events are more suitable than traditional feature-based methods. They propose two LSTM-based architectures ("Pair LSTM" and "Stated Pair LSTM") that take the two events as input and predict whether the relationship is causal. The models are evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where they achieve modest but consistent improvements over the previous state-of-the-art system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The application of a dual-input LSTM architecture to the task of causal connective disambiguation, demonstrating that encoding the full context of the related events is an effective strategy.
2.  An empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus showing that the proposed neural approach outperforms a strong feature-based SVM baseline, particularly in achieving a better balance between precision and recall.
3.  An analysis suggesting that neural models can learn to disambiguate markers that are ambiguous or used in non-prototypical causal contexts, moving beyond reliance on lexical cues alone.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Approach:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of identifying causal relations in text. The motivation for using neural networks is sound: the expression of causality in language is highly variable and context-dependent, making it a good fit for models like LSTMs that can capture semantic nuance without relying on pre-defined syntactic patterns or lexical resources. This is a clear step forward from heavily feature-engineered approaches.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed models consistently outperform both a majority-class baseline and the previous state-of-the-art system from Hidey and McKeown (2016) on the AltLex dataset. The improvement in F1-score (e.g., 73.76% to 75.40% on the non-bootstrapped corpus) and overall accuracy is a meaningful contribution and provides solid evidence for the effectiveness of the neural approach. The experiments are conducted on two versions of the corpus, adding to the robustness of the findings.

3.  **Thoughtful Analysis:** The paper includes an analysis section (4.3) that moves beyond aggregate metrics to examine specific, challenging examples. The discussion of how the model correctly classifies sentences containing causative verbs like "break" in non-causal contexts, or handles ambiguous connectives like "which then", provides valuable insight into the model's capabilities and supports the central claim that context encoding is key.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear Theoretical Framing and Introduction:** The introduction reads more like a literature review than a focused motivation for the work. It duplicates content from the Related Work section and fails to clearly articulate the paper's specific hypothesis and contributions upfront. More critically, the paper lacks a clear theoretical stance on causality. It briefly mentions and dismisses the counterfactual constraint using the example "The argument between John’s parents broke the window," but the reasoning is opaque. The assertion that the "human mental model of causality does not usually relate an argument with the breaking of a window" is presented without justification, leaving the reader unsure of the precise definition of causality the system is intended to capture.

2.  **Clarity of Model Description and Diagrams:** The model's presentation could be significantly improved. Figure 1 is labeled as depicting the proposed architecture but only shows the "Pair LSTM" variant, not the "Stated Pair LSTM". The diagram itself is imprecise, using undefined symbols and omitting key details about the dense layers and the nature of the LSTM outputs being concatenated. Furthermore, the discussion of zero-padding strategies (line 480) is confusing. It is unclear whether testing max, mean, and mode padding is motivated by a hypothesis about encoding causal meaning or is simply an exploration of a technical hyperparameter for handling variable-length inputs. The connection to the core task needs to be clarified.

3.  **Unsupported Interpretation of Architectural Choices:** The paper reports that the "Pair LSTM" (independent encoders) slightly outperforms the "Stated Pair LSTM" (sequentially connected) and concludes that "it is better to encode each argument independently." This is a strong claim based on a small performance difference. The paper offers no deeper analysis for why this might be the case, which is counter-intuitive given that information from the first event should presumably help in interpreting the second. This finding warrants more investigation and discussion.

4.  **Anecdotal Evidence for Disambiguation Capability:** The claim that the model successfully disambiguates challenging connectives like "which then" is supported by only a single example in Table 8. While illustrative, this is anecdotal. To make a convincing case, a more systematic analysis is needed, perhaps by evaluating the model on a curated test set of sentences with ambiguous markers or by analyzing its performance across all instances of specific ambiguous connectives present in the test set.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the operational definition of causality you are using for this task? Your dismissal of the counterfactual constraint with the "argument broke the window" example is hard to follow. What specific properties of that event pair make it non-causal in your view, and how does your model learn this distinction without being explicitly taught a causal theory?

2.  You found that the "Pair LSTM" outperformed the "Stated Pair LSTM," leading you to conclude that independent encoding of the events is superior. This is an interesting but counter-intuitive result. Do you have any hypotheses as to why passing the LSTM state from the first event to the second did not improve, or even slightly harmed, performance? Could this be an artifact of the dataset or the specific architecture?

3.  In your analysis of ambiguous connectives, you highlight the model's success on a single example with "which then." To strengthen this claim, would it be possible to provide a more systematic analysis? For instance, could you report performance specifically on connectives that are highly ambiguous (e.g., appear with near 50/50 causal/non-causal labels) in the training data?
