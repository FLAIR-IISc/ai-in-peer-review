### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network architecture based on Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units for classifying whether a lexical marker connecting two events signals a causal relation. The central hypothesis is that encoding the full context of the events with LSTMs is more effective for this disambiguation task than prior methods that rely on syntactic patterns or feature engineering from lexical resources. The models are evaluated on the AltLex corpus, showing improvements over the previous state-of-the-art system.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that a standard dual-encoder LSTM architecture can achieve state-of-the-art performance on the challenging AltLex dataset for causal marker disambiguation. This provides solid evidence that learned neural representations are effective for this task.
2.  The public release of the TensorFlow implementation and trained models, which is a valuable contribution that enhances reproducibility and enables future work to build upon these results.
3.  An analysis of model performance on specific ambiguous connectives (e.g., "which then") and contexts (e.g., causative verbs in non-causal sentences), offering qualitative insights into the model's ability to capture nuanced meaning beyond surface-level cues.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a clear and positive result, demonstrating that the proposed LSTM-based models outperform the previous state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus. While the margin is not massive on the full "bootstrapping" dataset, the improvement is consistent and the proposed model achieves a better balance between precision and recall, suggesting it is a more reliable classifier than the feature-heavy baseline.
2.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Approach:** The core idea of using LSTMs to encode the event contexts is logical and well-aligned with current best practices in NLP. The authors correctly argue that causality is a semantic phenomenon not strictly tied to specific syntactic constructions, making representation learning a more promising direction than methods reliant on predefined patterns or the limited coverage of lexical resources like VerbNet.
3.  **Contribution to Reproducibility:** The authors are to be commended for planning to release their code and models. This is a significant strength, as it allows the community to verify the results, build upon the work, and apply the models to new data, fostering further research in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Conceptual and Theoretical Clarity:** The paper’s framing is weak. The introduction duplicates much of the related work section and fails to crisply articulate the paper's specific contribution. More critically, the paper is vague about its underlying definition of causality. It introduces the counterfactual constraint (a standard philosophical and linguistic test) only to dismiss it based on a single, poorly explained example ("The argument between John’s parents broke the window"). The authors' reasoning about this example is opaque, making it difficult to assess their conceptual grasp of the phenomenon they aim to model. This theoretical ambiguity undermines the claim that the work is about modeling the "human mental model of causality."
2.  **Imprecise Model Description and Justification:** The description of the model architecture and experimental choices is lacking in clarity. Figure 1 is labeled as depicting the "Pair LSTM" architecture, but the caption refers to the "Stated Pair LSTM" as well, and the diagram itself is imprecise (e.g., ambiguous LSTM connections, unlabeled layers). Furthermore, the discussion of zero-padding strategies (max, mean, mode) is confusing; it is not explained whether this is a purely technical implementation detail or if the choice is believed to have a meaningful impact on encoding causality. A key architectural finding is that independent LSTMs outperform a sequentially-initialized model, but the authors' one-sentence interpretation (line 587) is speculative and insufficient. Why would encoding the arguments independently be better for a relational task? This counter-intuitive result requires a much deeper analysis.
3.  **Overstated Claims Based on Anecdotal Evidence:** The analysis relies heavily on a few examples to support broad claims. The assertion that the model correctly disambiguates the ambiguous marker "which then" is based on a single instance from the test set. This is anecdotal evidence, not a systematic evaluation. To convincingly demonstrate robustness to ambiguity, a more controlled analysis (e.g., using constructed minimal pairs) would be necessary. This weakness, combined with inconsistencies in reporting the best-performing hyperparameters across different sections (e.g., the best optimizer seems to change between Table 5 and Table 7), makes it hard to draw firm conclusions about the model's behavior.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the theoretical definition of causality you are operating with? After dismissing the counterfactual constraint, it is unclear what criteria a relation must meet to be considered "causal" in your framework. A clear definition would strengthen the paper's conceptual foundation.
2.  Your interpretation that independent LSTMs ("Pair LSTM") outperform a connected model ("Stated Pair LSTM") because "it is better to encode each argument independently" is surprising for a task that is inherently relational. Could you provide a more detailed hypothesis or analysis for this result? Did you, for example, examine the learned representations to see how they differ?
3.  Could you clarify the role of the padding strategy? Is the choice between max, mean, and mode padding simply a hyperparameter to be tuned, or do you have a linguistic or technical intuition for why one might be better at capturing causal meaning than another?
4.  Regarding the impressive disambiguation of "which then," have you considered a more systematic evaluation to support this claim? For instance, by creating a small challenge set of ambiguous connectives in different contexts to more rigorously test the model's capabilities beyond a single example.
