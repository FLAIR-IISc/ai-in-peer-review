### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the task of classifying whether a relation between two clauses is causal or non-causal, focusing on cases where the connecting lexical marker is ambiguous (e.g., "since," "after"). Using the AltLex dataset from Hidey & McKeown (2016), the authors propose a neural network architecture to tackle this disambiguation problem. They argue that LSTMs are better suited than CNNs for encoding the necessary clausal meaning. Their model employs a "Pair LSTM" architecture with two separate input streams: one for the first clause and another for the lexical marker plus the second clause. These inputs are represented using GloVe embeddings, processed by LSTMs, and then passed through three dense tanh layers before a final softmax classification layer. The proposed system reports a new state-of-the-art result, outperforming the feature-engineered SVM baseline from Hidey & McKeown (2016). The paper includes both quantitative and qualitative analyses to support its claims.

### **Contributions**

1.  **Primary Contribution:** The paper provides a solid empirical result showing that a standard LSTM-based neural architecture, without complex feature engineering, can outperform a strong, feature-rich SVM baseline on the AltLex causal disambiguation task. This validates the effectiveness of neural representation learning for this specific discourse-level problem.
2.  **Secondary Contribution:** The work includes a useful qualitative analysis with concrete examples where the neural model correctly disambiguates ambiguous causal markers that the previous state-of-the-art system misclassifies. This offers valuable insight into the model's ability to generalize from contextual cues rather than relying solely on the lexical marker's prior distribution.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written, with a clear introduction to the linguistic concept of causation and a well-motivated setup for the computational task. The authors effectively situate their work within the existing literature and clearly define the problem of disambiguating causal lexical markers.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves superior performance compared to the previously established state-of-the-art on the AltLex dataset. The reported F1 score improvement of approximately 0.5–1.5% over the baseline of Hidey & McKeown (2016) on the main "bootstrapping" dataset demonstrates a tangible advance on this benchmark.
3.  **Reproducibility and Analysis:** The authors have provided their code, which is a commendable practice that facilitates reproducibility and further research. The inclusion of both quantitative results across multiple configurations and a qualitative error analysis (Table 8) strengthens the paper by giving readers a more nuanced understanding of the model's behavior and improvements.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Potential Flaw in Experimental Protocol:** The paper's primary weakness is that the best model configuration appears to have been selected based on test set performance. Table 5 presents results for numerous hyperparameter settings (e.g., padding strategy, optimizer) directly on the test set. Standard machine learning practice requires hyperparameter tuning and model selection to be performed on a validation/development set, with the final model evaluated only once on the held-out test set. This approach risks overfitting to the test set and may inflate the reported performance gains.
2.  **Limited Novelty and Insufficient Baselines:** While effective, the proposed architecture is a relatively standard application of LSTMs for a text classification task. In the current NLP landscape, it is expected that a well-tuned neural model will outperform older methods based on handcrafted features. The paper's contribution would be stronger if it had included simpler neural baselines (e.g., a bag-of-words model, a single-layer LSTM) to better contextualize the performance of the proposed multi-layer architecture and demonstrate that its complexity is warranted.
3.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing and Dataset Concerns:** The reported improvements over the baseline are modest (e.g., 81.56% vs. 80.90% F1 for the best Adam configuration on the bootstrapping set). Without statistical significance tests, it is difficult to ascertain whether these gains are meaningful or simply a result of experimental variance. Furthermore, the AltLex dataset itself, being derived from alignments between Wikipedia and Simple Wikipedia, may contain annotation noise, which could limit the reliability of small score differences.
4.  **Omission of Important Methodological Details:** The paper relies on a "bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus, which is ~15% larger than the base version, but fails to explain the bootstrapping procedure. For the paper to be self-contained, a brief description of this process is necessary. Additionally, the analysis of the model's tendency to favor precision over recall would benefit from a more in-depth discussion.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The task of "causal meaning classification" for unmarked or ambiguously marked clauses seems closely related to the well-established task of implicit discourse relation recognition (specifically, the 'Contingency.Cause' relation in the PDTB framework). Could you clarify the key differences between these tasks and explain why methods developed for implicit relation recognition were not used as stronger baselines?
2.  Could you please clarify your model selection process? Table 5 reports results for multiple model configurations on the test set. Was the final best-performing configuration selected using a development set, or was it chosen based on these reported test set scores?
3.  For the benefit of readers, could you briefly describe the bootstrapping procedure used to create the extended version of the AltLex corpus?
4.  Regarding the released code and data: the TSV files contain a 0-1-2 coding scheme whose meaning is unclear. Could you document this? Also, given that the AltLex dataset has its own distribution license, have you confirmed the rights to re-distribute this data?
