### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes LSTM-based neural network models for classifying whether a lexical marker signals a causal relation between two text spans (events). The authors argue that neural models, by encoding the meaning of the surrounding context, are better suited to handle the diverse and ambiguous linguistic expressions of causality than previous methods relying on syntactic patterns or curated lexical resources. They evaluate their models on the AltLex corpus, comparing a "Stated Pair LSTM" (where the LSTMs for the two events are sequential) and a "Pair LSTM" (where they are independent). The results show a modest but consistent improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, a feature-based SVM system. The authors have also made their TensorFlow code and models publicly available.

### Contributions
1.  **Primary Contribution:** The paper provides a solid empirical demonstration that a standard LSTM-based architecture can outperform a complex, feature-engineered system for the task of causal marker disambiguation on the AltLex benchmark. While the use of LSTMs for text classification is not novel, this work serves as a valuable benchmark and confirmation of the effectiveness of neural methods on this specific task, reinforcing the trend of moving away from manual feature engineering.

2.  **Secondary Contribution:** The public release of the models and TensorFlow code is a welcome contribution. It enhances the reproducibility of the reported results and provides a strong, accessible baseline for future research on causality detection with this dataset.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses an Important and Difficult Problem:** The paper tackles the challenging problem of identifying causal relations, a key component of deep language understanding. By focusing on the AltLex corpus, it correctly identifies and addresses the limitation of previous work that focused on a small, fixed set of unambiguous causal connectives.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Recent Benchmark:** The proposed models achieve state-of-the-art performance on the AltLex corpus, outperforming the previously best-performing system. The improvements in both F1-score and Accuracy, particularly on the "non-bootstrapping" dataset (2.13% F1 and 7.72% Accuracy), are meaningful and provide solid evidence for the efficacy of the neural approach.

3.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors’ decision to release their code and trained models is a significant strength. This practice is crucial for scientific progress, allowing other researchers to verify the results, build upon the work, and easily compare new methods against this new state-of-the-art baseline.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Clarity in Motivation and Theoretical Grounding:** The paper's framing is weak. The Introduction reads like a literature review, largely duplicating the content of the Related Work section, and fails to articulate a clear, positive contribution beyond the general claim that "neural networks are able to encode the meaning". Furthermore, the paper's theoretical stance on causation is ambiguous. It briefly introduces a counterfactual definition (line 250) only to dismiss it using a confusing example (lines 256-260) without committing to a clear, alternative formal definition. This leaves the reader unsure of what precise notion of "causality" the model is intended to capture.

2.  **Poorly Explained and Presented Technical Details:** Key aspects of the methodology are unclear, hindering understanding and reproducibility.
    *   **Figure 1:** The figure is imprecise and its caption is misleading, as it only depicts the "Pair LSTM" architecture. The diagram uses undefined symbols, fails to specify the semantics of the dense layers (e.g., number of units, purpose), and the connections from the LSTMs to the subsequent layers are ambiguous (e.g., is it the final hidden state, an average, or max-pooling over time?).
    *   **Padding (Line 480):** The sentence discussing padding strategies is confusing. Padding is a standard technical requirement for batch processing of variable-length sequences; framing this as a choice to "encode the causal meaning" is misleading and needs clarification. The authors should simply state that this is a necessary step for the model architecture and report which method worked best.
    *   **Hyperparameters (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636):** The reporting of hyperparameters is inconsistent and difficult to interpret. For example, Table 5 shows "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" as the best model for the non-bootstrapping corpus, but Table 7 uses "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" for analysis on a reduced version of that same corpus without justification. The general description of hyperparameter ranges is also too vague to be useful. This makes it hard to draw firm conclusions about model behavior.

3.  **Superficial Analysis and Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper's analysis of its own results is often weak and rests on insufficient evidence.
    *   **Independent vs. Sequential LSTMs (Line 587):** The finding that independent LSTMs ("Pair LSTM") outperform a sequential model ("Stated Pair LSTM") is interesting but the explanation is circular: "...it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation...". This simply restates the result. This finding runs counter to the intuition that contextual information from the first event should inform the processing of the second, and a more rigorous analysis or hypothesis is needed.
    *   **Anecdotal Evidence for Disambiguation (Section 4.3):** The claim that the system better disambiguates the connective "which then" is based on a single example. This is anecdotal and insufficient to support a general claim of superiority. To be convincing, this would require a systematic analysis, for example, by testing on a curated set of minimal-pair sentences designed to be ambiguous.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the specific theory or operational definition of causality that your work assumes? The discussion around line 256 dismisses the counterfactual view but does not propose a clear alternative, making the conceptual grounding of the task unclear.
2.  Your finding that the independent "Pair LSTM" outperforms the "Stated Pair LSTM" is counter-intuitive. Could you offer a more detailed hypothesis for why passing the LSTM state from the first event to the second hinders performance? Could this be an artifact of the dataset or a specific implementation detail?
3.  The claim of superior disambiguation for "which then" is based on a single example. Have you considered a more systematic evaluation for ambiguous connectives to substantiate this claim?
