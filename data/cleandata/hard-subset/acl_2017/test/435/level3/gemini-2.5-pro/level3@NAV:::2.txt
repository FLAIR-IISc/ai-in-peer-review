**Summary and Contributions**

This paper tackles the task of disambiguating causal versus non-causal relations between clauses that are not marked by explicit connectives, using the AltLex dataset. The authors propose a "Pair LSTM" model using GloVe embeddings to encode the two clauses, followed by dense layers for classification. The primary contribution is an empirical demonstration that this standard neural architecture can achieve a modest improvement over a strong, feature-engineered state-of-the-art system on this specific task. A secondary contribution is a qualitative analysis providing examples where the neural model generalizes better than the prior work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper is well-written and presents a clear, well-motivated problem in discourse relation classification. The background and task definition are easy to follow.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Improvement:** The proposed model achieves a small but consistent F1-score improvement (approx. 0.5-1.5%) over the previous state-of-the-art (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). This demonstrates the effectiveness of the neural approach for this specific problem.
3.  **Qualitative Analysis:** The inclusion of both quantitative results and qualitative error analysis (Table 8) is a significant strength. It provides valuable insight into specific cases where the model succeeds, particularly with ambiguous lexical markers, lending credibility to the claim that context encoding is key.
4.  **Reproducibility Efforts:** The authors have made an effort towards reproducibility by uploading their code and data, which is commendable.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Experimental Methodology:** The most critical weakness is that the best-performing model configuration appears to be selected based on test set performance rather than a development set. This practice risks overfitting to the test set and may inflate the reported performance gains, potentially invalidating the central claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art.
2.  **Incremental Novelty and Missing Baselines:** The novelty of the proposed method is limited. The finding that a standard LSTM architecture can outperform a feature-engineered system is a common result in NLP. The paper's contribution would be stronger if it included simpler neural baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM, or a non-sequential bag-of-words model) to justify the specific architectural choices.
3.  **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** The reported performance gains are small. Without statistical significance testing, it is unclear whether the improvements over the baseline are meaningful or simply due to random variation. Furthermore, the paper does not discuss the potential impact of noise in the AltLex dataset, which is derived from Wikipedia/Simple Wikipedia alignments.
4.  **Insufficient Detail and Positioning:** The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish this task from the well-studied problem of implicit discourse relation recognition and does not explain why methods from that area were not used as baselines. Additionally, crucial details about the dataset creation, such as the bootstrapping procedure, are omitted.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify whether a development set was used for model selection and hyperparameter tuning? The paper seems to imply that the best configuration was chosen based on test set results.
2.  Can you please elaborate on the bootstrapping procedure used to extend the corpus, as this is not described in the paper?
3.  How does this task fundamentally differ from implicit discourse connective recognition, and why were established methods from that field not considered as stronger baselines?
