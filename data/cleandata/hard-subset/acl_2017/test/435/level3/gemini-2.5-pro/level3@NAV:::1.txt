### **Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"**

#### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a set of LSTM-based models for classifying whether lexical markers indicate a causal relationship between two events. The central claim is that neural network encodings of context are better suited for this task than methods relying on explicit syntactic features, given the diversity of causal expressions. The authors evaluate their models on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating modest but consistent improvements over a feature-based SVM baseline, and they release their models and code.

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that an LSTM-based approach can outperform a strong feature-based baseline for causal connective disambiguation on the AltLex dataset.
2.  The public release of the TensorFlow code and trained models, which is a valuable contribution to reproducibility and future research in this area.

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Relevant Problem and Motivated Approach:** The paper tackles the important and challenging task of causal relation classification. The motivation to use neural models to capture diverse contextual cues, rather than relying on brittle syntactic patterns or lexical resources, is well-founded and represents a promising research direction.
2.  **Positive Empirical Results:** The proposed models consistently, if modestly, outperform the state-of-the-art baseline on the AltLex corpus. This provides valuable evidence supporting the hypothesis that neural representations are effective for this task.
3.  **Publicly Available Resources:** The decision to release the code and models is commendable. This practice is crucial for verifying results, facilitating extensions, and allowing other researchers to build upon this work directly.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Rigor:** The paper's primary weakness is a pervasive lack of clarity that undermines its scientific contribution. The introduction reads like a literature review, failing to crisply state the paper's contribution. The theoretical foundation is murky; the authors vaguely invoke a counterfactual theory of causation without engaging with it meaningfully. This imprecision extends to the technical details: Figure 1 is misleading and poorly labeled, and descriptions of implementation details like padding (line 480) are unclear.
2.  **Unsupported Claims and Weak Analysis:** Several key claims are insufficiently supported. The interpretation for why independent LSTMs outperform a sequential architecture (line 587) is speculative and contradicts common intuitions about information flow without providing further analysis. Similarly, the claim of superior performance on the ambiguous connective "which then" is based on a single anecdote; this requires systematic evaluation with constructed examples to be convincing.
3.  **Inconsistent Reporting:** The reporting of experimental settings is inconsistent and difficult to follow. For instance, the hyperparameters mentioned in the methods section (e.g., line 578) do not clearly align with the best settings reported in the results (e.g., line 636). This makes it difficult to understand the model's behavior and compromises the reproducibility of the specific results presented.
