### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of classifying whether a relationship between two text spans, connected by a potentially ambiguous lexical marker, is causal or not. The authors argue that encoding the full context of the events is necessary for this disambiguation task. They propose a dual-input LSTM-based neural network architecture that encodes the two event spans separately and then uses several dense layers to make a final classification. The model is trained and evaluated on the AltLex corpus, which contains examples of causal and non-causal relations. The authors report that their model outperforms the previous state-of-the-art system from Hidey and McKeown (2016), supporting their hypothesis that neural context encoding is effective for this problem.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **A new state-of-the-art result on the AltLex corpus for causal relation classification.** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation of an LSTM-based architecture, demonstrating modest but consistent improvements over a strong feature-engineered baseline.
2.  **A qualitative analysis demonstrating the model's ability to disambiguate causality based on context.** The authors provide compelling examples where their model correctly classifies challenging instances with ambiguous markers or causative verbs used in non-causal contexts, which the previous system failed on.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and easy to follow. The introduction provides a solid background on the linguistic study of causality and situates the computational problem clearly. The task definition and the authors' hypothesis are stated explicitly and are well-supported throughout the paper.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on both the "non-bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" versions of the AltLex corpus. The improvements over the previous work by Hidey and McKeown (2016) are consistent, with F1 score gains of up to 2.13% on the non-bootstrapped data and around 1% on the bootstrapped data. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the neural approach for this task.
3.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.3 (Table 8) is a key strength of the paper. It goes beyond simply reporting metrics and provides concrete examples of the model's ability to handle lexical and contextual ambiguity. For instance, showing that the model can correctly identify a non-causal use of the verb "break" or disambiguate the marker "which then" effectively demonstrates the power of its contextual representations, directly supporting the paper's central claim.
4.  **Reproducibility Efforts:** The authors have included code with their submission. This is a commendable practice that facilitates the verification of their results and allows other researchers to build upon their work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential Flaw in Experimental Methodology:** The paper presents an ablation study of different model configurations (e.g., padding strategies, optimizers) with results reported directly on the test set (Table 5). This suggests that the final model architecture and hyperparameters may have been selected based on test set performance. This is a significant methodological concern, as it can lead to an overestimation of the model's true performance and calls into question the reliability of the reported state-of-the-art claims. The authors should have used a separate development set for model selection.
2.  **Limited Novelty of the Proposed Method:** The core contribution of the paper is empirical rather than methodological. The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a fairly standard application of LSTMs for a sentence-pair classification task. By 2017, it is widely expected that a well-tuned neural network with pre-trained embeddings will outperform a system based on hand-engineered lexical and syntactic features. While the results are valuable, the architectural contribution itself is incremental.
3.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The reported improvements over the baseline are modest (e.g., 81.56% vs. 80.90% F1 on one of the main experiments). Without statistical significance tests, it is difficult to ascertain whether these gains are meaningful or simply due to random variation, especially on a dataset constructed via automatic alignment between Wikipedia and Simple Wikipedia, which may contain inherent noise.
4.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Tasks:** The task of identifying the relationship signaled by an alternative lexicalization is closely related to, if not a subset of, implicit discourse relation classification. However, the paper does not frame its work in this context or compare it to established methods from that field. This omission makes it harder to gauge the paper's contribution to the broader area of discourse parsing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify your experimental procedure for model selection and hyperparameter tuning? Was a separate development set used for this purpose? Table 5 reports results for multiple configurations on the test set, which raises concerns about tuning on the test data.
2.  The paper states that the "bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus was created by applying a "bootstrapping method". Could you please provide more details on what this method entails? This information is important for understanding the composition and potential biases of the dataset.
3.  How do you see this task as distinct from the broader task of implicit discourse relation classification, for which the AltLex corpus is also a key resource? Could you elaborate on why standard models from that literature were not considered as baselines?
4.  For reproducibility, could you clarify the format of the provided data files? Specifically, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files is not immediately clear. Additionally, have you confirmed that you have the rights to redistribute the AltLex dataset?
