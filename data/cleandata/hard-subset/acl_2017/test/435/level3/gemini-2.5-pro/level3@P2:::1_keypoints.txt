- Proposes a dual-encoder LSTM architecture to classify whether a lexical marker between two events signals causality, evaluated on the AltLex corpus with improved performance.
- Claims contributions: empirical SOTA on AltLex, public release of TensorFlow code and models, and qualitative analysis on ambiguous connectives/contexts.
- Strong empirical results: outperforms prior state-of-the-art with a better precision–recall balance.
- Well-motivated approach: LSTMs argued to better capture semantic context than syntactic patterns or lexical-resource features.
- Reproducibility: planned release of code and trained models is a valuable contribution.
- Weak conceptual/theoretical framing: introduction duplicates related work and lacks a clear, consistent definition of causality; counterfactual constraint dismissed without clear justification.
- Imprecise model description: ambiguous figure/captioning, unclear LSTM connections, and confusing discussion of zero-padding strategies.
- Insufficient justification for architectural choices: surprising finding that independent LSTMs beat sequentially-initialized model is only speculatively explained.
- Overreliance on anecdotal evidence: claims (e.g., disambiguation of “which then”) are based on single examples rather than systematic evaluation.
- Inconsistent reporting: discrepancies in best-performing hyperparameters across tables undermine confidence in results.
- Question for authors: clarify the theoretical definition of causality used in the work.
- Question for authors: provide deeper analysis or inspection of learned representations to explain why independent LSTMs perform better.
- Question for authors: clarify the role and rationale of padding strategies—technical detail or linguistically motivated choice?
- Suggestion/question: perform a controlled/challenge-set evaluation of ambiguous connectives rather than relying on single test instances.
