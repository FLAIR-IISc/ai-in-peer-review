### **Summary**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves classifying the relationship between two events connected by a lexical marker as either "Causal" or "Non-Causal". The authors argue that neural networks, specifically Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, are effective for this task because they can encode the contextual meaning of the events, which is crucial for disambiguation. The proposed model uses a two-input LSTM architecture to process the two event clauses, followed by dense layers for classification. The method is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it achieves a modest improvement over the previous state-of-the-art system from Hidey and McKeown (2016), which relied on hand-engineered features.

### **Contributions**

1.  **An empirical validation of neural models for causal relation classification.** The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating that a standard LSTM-based neural architecture, without complex feature engineering, can outperform a strong feature-based SVM baseline on the AltLex dataset. This reinforces the effectiveness of representation learning for discourse-level tasks.
2.  **Analysis of model performance on ambiguous markers.** The paper provides a useful analysis of the model's behavior, including a qualitative look at specific examples where the neural model succeeds in disambiguating ambiguous causal markers that the baseline system misclassifies.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Presentation.** The paper is well-written and does an excellent job of motivating the problem of causal disambiguation. It provides a concise overview of related work and clearly positions its own contribution. The proposed method is described clearly and is easy to follow.
2.  **Solid Empirical Results.** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the two versions of the AltLex corpus. The evaluation is reasonably thorough, including experiments on the original and "bootstrapped" datasets, as well as on a balanced subset of the data to account for class imbalance.
3.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis.** The error analysis in Section 4.3 (Table 8) is a key strength. It provides concrete examples where the model correctly disambiguates challenging cases, such as the non-causal use of the verb "break" or the ambiguous connective "which then". This analysis effectively supports the authors' central claim that encoding the full context is essential for this task.
4.  **Efforts Towards Reproducibility.** The authors have provided their code and data as supplementary material. This is a commendable practice that facilitates verification and allows other researchers to build upon their work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty.** The main weakness is the incremental nature of the contribution. The proposed architecture (using LSTMs with pre-trained embeddings for a classification task) is a standard and widely used approach in modern NLP. The finding that such a model outperforms a traditional feature-engineered SVM, while a valid result, is not surprising and has been shown across many other NLP tasks. The work would be more impactful if it introduced a more novel architectural element tailored to causality or provided deeper insights derived from the model.
2.  **Concerns with Experimental Rigor.** The experimental methodology raises two significant concerns:
    *   **Model Selection on Test Set:** The paper appears to select the best model configurations (e.g., padding strategy, optimizer) based on their performance on the test set. This is not standard practice, as it can lead to an overestimation of performance. A dedicated development set should be used for all hyperparameter tuning and model selection to ensure the reported test results reflect true generalization ability.
    *   **Lack of Significance Testing:** The performance gains over the state-of-the-art are relatively small (e.g., ~1.5% F1 on the bootstrapping corpus). Without statistical significance tests, it is difficult to determine if these improvements are genuine or simply due to random variation in the data or model initialization.
3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Tasks.** The paper does not discuss the relationship between its task and the well-established task of implicit discourse relation recognition (e.g., identifying the "Contingency:Cause" relation in the PDTB). Many powerful neural models have been developed for that task, and it is unclear why they were not considered as relevant baselines or points of comparison.
4.  **Lack of Simpler Baselines.** The comparison is limited to a majority class baseline and the previous feature-based SOTA. Including simpler neural baselines (e.g., a bag-of-words model or a simpler 1-layer LSTM) would help to better situate the contribution of the specific architectural choices made.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify your procedure for hyperparameter tuning and model selection? Was a development set used, or were these choices made based on test set performance? Furthermore, could you perform statistical significance tests to confirm that your model's improvements over the baseline (B2) are statistically significant?
2.  How do you see this task differing from implicit discourse relation recognition? It seems closely related to identifying the *Contingency:Cause* relation. Could you elaborate on why models from that line of research were not adapted or compared against as baselines?
3.  Regarding the supplementary material: the data files seem to use a 0-1-2 coding system. Could you please clarify the meaning of these codes in your author response? Also, could you confirm that you have the rights to redistribute the AltLex dataset? This is crucial for long-term reproducibility.
