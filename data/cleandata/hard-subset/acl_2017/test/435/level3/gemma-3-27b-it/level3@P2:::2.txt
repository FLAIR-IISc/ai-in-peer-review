This paper addresses the challenging task of identifying causal relationships between clauses when these relationships are not explicitly signaled by discourse connectives, leveraging the AltLex corpus. The authors argue for the effectiveness of LSTMs for encoding contextual information crucial for disambiguation, employing GloVe embeddings followed by an LSTM network and several dense layers culminating in a softmax classifier. The proposed system achieves incremental improvements over the state-of-the-art, as established by Hidey & McKeown (2016), with reported F1 gains ranging from 0.5-1.5%. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem and proposed approach, supported by both quantitative and qualitative analysis.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of a relatively simple LSTM-based neural network architecture for causal relation classification on the AltLex corpus. While neural networks are increasingly prevalent in NLP, this work specifically focuses on a task requiring nuanced understanding of context to resolve ambiguity in causal expressions. A secondary contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation on the AltLex dataset, offering a clear baseline and comparison point for future research. The detailed analysis, including illustrative examples where the model correctly identifies causal links, provides valuable insights into the model's strengths.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper effectively highlights the limitations of previous approaches relying on explicit connectives or lexical resources, and motivates the need for context-aware methods. The background discussion on the psychological and linguistic aspects of causality is helpful.
2. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing their model against strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art. The use of both versions of the AltLex corpus (bootstrapped and non-bootstrapped) provides a robust assessment of performance.
3. **Insightful Analysis:**  The inclusion of qualitative examples demonstrating correct classifications (and implicit comparisons to failures of the baseline) enhances the understanding of the model's capabilities.  The discussion of the challenges posed by ambiguous connectives, like "since" and "after," is particularly valuable.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors have made code and data (albeit older versions) publicly available, promoting reproducibility. This is a significant positive aspect, despite some minor issues detailed in the weaknesses.
5. **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is clearly structured and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas from motivation to method to results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the results demonstrate an improvement over the existing baseline, the core idea of using LSTMs for sequence encoding is not novel. The primary contribution seems to be the application of a standard LSTM architecture to a specific, but relatively niche, task. It is not immediately clear why this particular architecture was chosen over other recurrent variants (e.g., GRUs) and lacks justification compared to other recent approaches in discourse parsing.
2. **Experimental Setup Concerns:** The reported improvement over the state-of-the-art (2.13% F1) is potentially inflated due to the selection of the best configuration directly on the test set, rather than using a development set for hyperparameter tuning. This practice can lead to overfitting and overly optimistic performance estimates.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper lacks any statistical significance testing to demonstrate whether the observed improvements are truly meaningful or simply due to chance. Given the relatively small size of the dataset, statistical significance is crucial.
4. **Data and Corpus Concerns:** The availability of the dataset is appreciated, but questions remain regarding the rights to redistribute the AltLex corpus. Additionally, the documentation for the data (specifically the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files) is lacking. The bootstrapping procedure used to create the second version of the corpus is not adequately explained, making it difficult to assess its impact on the results.
5. **Precision-Recall Trade-off:** The system exhibits a tendency towards higher precision at the expense of recall. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth analysis explaining *why* this trade-off exists would be beneficial.  Could simpler models or feature engineering techniques improve recall without significantly sacrificing precision?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does this task fundamentally differ from implicit connective recognition, and why are existing methods for implicit connective recognition not directly applicable to this problem?
2. Could you provide more details on the bootstrapping procedure used to create the second version of the AltLex corpus? What criteria were used to select sentences for bootstrapping, and what impact did this process have on the dataset's characteristics?
3. Can you justify the choice of LSTM over other recurrent neural network architectures (e.g., GRUs) for this task?
4. What measures were taken to ensure the quality of annotations in the AltLex corpus, given that it was constructed using data from English and Simple English Wikipedia?



**Additional Comments:**

It would be beneficial to include a simpler baseline model (e.g., a single-layer LSTM) to provide a clearer benchmark for the more complex architecture. Exploring different word embedding strategies or pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT) could also be considered. I recommend including statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests) to support the claims of improvement over the baseline. Finally, a detailed analysis of the error cases would help identify areas for future improvement.
