This paper proposes a neural network architecture, specifically utilizing LSTMs, for the task of causal relation classification. The authors argue that encoding the meaning of a sentence via neural networks is crucial for disambiguating causal meaning, particularly given the limitations of syntax-based approaches and the diverse ways causality can be expressed. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus and report improved performance compared to a baseline system. The primary contributions, as I see them, are the application of LSTMs to this specific task and the demonstration of competitive results on a challenging corpus designed to address limitations in existing resources.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Task and Corpus:** The task of causal relation classification is a fundamental problem in natural language understanding, and the use of the AltLex corpus is a significant advantage. AltLex directly addresses the limitations of previous datasets by including a wider range of expressions indicating causality beyond standard lexical markers. This allows for a more robust evaluation of systems attempting to capture nuanced causal reasoning.
2. **Sound Architectural Choice:** Employing LSTMs is a reasonable approach given the sequential nature of language and the need to capture contextual information. The authors correctly identify the limitations of purely pattern-matching or feature-engineered systems and move towards a more representation-learning based method.  The exploration of both connected and independent LSTM architectures is a valuable investigation.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The reported results demonstrate that the proposed LSTM-based models outperform the baseline system (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus.  While the improvements are not dramatic, they provide empirical support for the claim that neural networks can effectively learn to disambiguate causal meaning.
4. **Code Release:** The authors mention releasing their TensorFlow code and models, which is a positive contribution to reproducibility and further research.  This allows other researchers to build upon their work and conduct further investigations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Unclear Theoretical Framing:** The paper lacks a clear and consistent articulation of the underlying theory of causation being employed. While the introduction references various perspectives, it doesn't commit to a specific framework (e.g., counterfactuals, mechanisms) or address the inherent complexities of defining causality. This weakens the overall argument and makes it difficult to interpret the results in a broader theoretical context. The discussion around the example at line 256 further highlights this issue; the authors’ judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition are made without a grounding in a clear causal theory.
2. **Overly Descriptive Introduction:** The introduction reads excessively like a literature review, devoting significant space to outlining existing work on causality without clearly articulating the novel contribution of this paper.  It lacks a strong thesis statement and a clear justification for the proposed approach. The overlap between the introduction and the Related Work section is substantial and redundant.
3. **Presentation and Clarity Issues:** Several aspects of the paper suffer from a lack of clarity.  Figure 1 is misleading and imprecise, with unclear symbols, missing layer semantics, and ambiguous LSTM connections. The discussion surrounding the padding strategy (line 480) is unclear, and the explanation for why independent LSTMs outperform connected ones (line 587) is not adequately justified and runs counter to intuition. The inconsistent reporting of hyperparameters and best settings further complicates interpretation.
4. **Limited Analysis & Generalizability:** The claim of improved performance on ambiguous cases like "which then" (Table 8) is based on a single example. This is insufficient to support a broader claim about the model’s ability to handle complex linguistic constructions. A more rigorous evaluation involving a systematically constructed test set with a variety of ambiguous cases is needed to assess the model’s generalization ability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you clarify the theoretical framework underpinning your definition of causality? Specifically, are you relying on a counterfactual account, a mechanistic account, or another perspective?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the independent LSTM architecture outperforms the connected one? What specific characteristics of the data or the task might explain this finding?
3.  Can you address the clarity issues in Figure 1 by providing a more precise and informative diagram of the neural network architecture?
4.  Will you release a more comprehensive evaluation on ambiguous constructions, including a systematically created test set, to validate the claim of improved performance on these cases?
5.  Could you provide a more detailed rationale for the hyperparameter choices and explain the inconsistencies in the reported best settings throughout the paper?

**Additional Comments:**

The authors should consider re-organizing the introduction to more clearly state their contribution and provide a more concise overview of related work. The discussion sections need greater analytical depth and should avoid unsupported assertions. A more rigorous and systematic evaluation is necessary to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed approach and to strengthen the claims made in the paper.
