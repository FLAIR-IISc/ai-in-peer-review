- Paper proposes LSTM-based neural architectures for causal relation classification and reports improved performance on the AltLex corpus.
- Main contributions: applying LSTMs to causal relation classification and demonstrating competitive results on a challenging corpus.
- Strength: tackles a relevant NLU task using the AltLex corpus, which captures diverse causal expressions beyond lexical markers.
- Strength: sound architectural choice—LSTMs are appropriate for sequential language modeling; both connected and independent LSTM variants are explored.
- Strength: empirical results outperform the baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), showing neural models can learn to disambiguate causality.
- Strength: authors release TensorFlow code and models, aiding reproducibility.
- Weakness: lacks a clear theoretical framing of causality (no commitment to counterfactual, mechanistic, etc.), weakening interpretation of results.
- Weakness: introduction is overly descriptive and redundant with Related Work, lacking a concise thesis and clear novel contribution statement.
- Weakness: presentation and clarity problems—Figure 1 is imprecise, padding strategy and LSTM connections are unclear, and explanations (e.g., why independent LSTMs win) are inadequate.
- Weakness: inconsistent reporting of hyperparameters and best settings, complicating reproducibility and interpretation.
- Weakness: limited analysis and generalizability—claims about handling ambiguous constructions (e.g., “which then”) rest on single examples rather than systematic evaluation.
- Question: Which theoretical framework of causality do the authors adopt (counterfactual, mechanistic, or other)?
- Question: Why do independent LSTMs outperform connected ones—what data/task characteristics explain this?
- Question: Can Figure 1 be clarified with a more precise diagram of the network architecture?
- Question: Will the authors provide a more comprehensive evaluation on ambiguous constructions via a systematically created test set?
- Question: Can the authors justify hyperparameter choices and resolve inconsistencies in reported best settings?
- Additional comment: reorganize the introduction to clearly state contributions, tighten related work overlap, deepen analytical discussion, and provide a more rigorous systematic evaluation.
