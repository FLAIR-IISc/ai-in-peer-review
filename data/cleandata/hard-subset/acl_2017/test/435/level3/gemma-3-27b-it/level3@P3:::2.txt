## Review

This paper tackles the challenging problem of detecting causal versus non-causal clause relations when causality isn't explicitly marked by connectives, leveraging the AltLex dataset. The authors argue that LSTMs are superior to CNNs for clause representation and present a system based on GloVe embeddings, an LSTM layer, three dense tanh layers, and a softmax output. The system achieves a modest improvement (0.5-1.5% F1) over the prior state-of-the-art. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem and its context within the broader field of causality and discourse relations.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Validation of LSTM Effectiveness:** The core contribution lies in demonstrating, through experimentation on the AltLex dataset, that LSTM-based architectures are more effective than potentially simpler alternatives (implicitly, CNNs) for discerning causal relationships in context. While the performance gain is incremental, it provides evidence supporting the benefit of sequential modeling for this task.
2. **Application to the AltLex Dataset:** The paper provides a solid baseline for future work on the AltLex dataset, a valuable resource for studying implicit causality. The comprehensive experimental setup and analysis, including qualitative examples, help establish a clearer understanding of the dataset’s characteristics and the challenges it presents.
3. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the problem of *causal meaning disambiguation* and articulates why existing approaches (relying on explicit connectives or predefined lexical features) are insufficient to handle the nuances of implicit causality expressed through AltLex.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is clearly presented, and the technical details are explained in a straightforward manner.
2. **Strong Problem Motivation:** The authors effectively highlight the limitations of existing approaches to causality detection and convincingly argue for the need to address implicit causal relations. The discussion of how human causal reasoning extends beyond explicit markers is particularly insightful.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, and the authors explore a range of configurations for their LSTM-based architecture. The inclusion of both the “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” versions of the AltLex corpus allows for a more robust evaluation.
4. **Qualitative Analysis:** The inclusion of specific examples in Section 4.3, demonstrating cases where the model succeeds and the prior work fails, is a strong point. This helps to ground the quantitative results and provides valuable insight into the model’s behavior.
5. **Identification of Dataset Challenges:** The authors acknowledge and discuss the imbalances within the AltLex dataset (e.g., ambiguous connectives, differences between training and test sets) and their potential impact on the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Questionable Test Set Selection:** A significant concern is the potential for overly optimistic results due to the selection of the best configuration based on performance on the *test set* rather than a dedicated development set. This practice risks overfitting to the test data and inflating the reported gains.
2. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper does not report any statistical significance tests to assess whether the observed improvements are statistically significant. This makes it difficult to determine whether the gains are due to genuine improvements in performance or simply random chance.
3. **Dataset Annotation Quality:** The reliance on English and SimpleEnglish Wikipedia for dataset creation raises concerns about the inherent noise and potential biases in the annotations. The paper briefly acknowledges the data imbalance but does not fully address the potential impact of annotation quality on the results.
4. **Incremental Novelty:** While the empirical results are positive, the contribution of demonstrating that LSTMs outperform CNNs is somewhat expected given the nature of the task and the strengths of LSTMs in sequential modeling. The novelty may be incremental rather than groundbreaking.
5. **Bootstrapping Procedure Opaque:** The paper mentions that the “bootstrapping” version of the AltLex corpus extends the original by ~15%, but provides no details on the bootstrapping procedure itself. Understanding this procedure is crucial for evaluating the validity and representativeness of the expanded corpus.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Was the final model configuration selected based on performance on a held-out development set, or directly on the test set? If the latter, please re-evaluate using proper dev set validation and report the revised results.
2. Can you provide details on the bootstrapping procedure used to create the expanded AltLex corpus? What criteria were used to select instances for bootstrapping, and how was the quality of the bootstrapped data assessed?
3. Could you elaborate on why simpler baselines, such as a single-layer LSTM, were not included in the experimental evaluation?
4. The paper observes that the best configuration favors precision over recall. Can you provide additional analysis to explain this observation? Are there specific linguistic features or biases in the dataset that might contribute to this pattern?
5. Are the code and data publicly available, and if so, can you clarify the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding scheme used in the TSV files? Are there any restrictions on the reposting or redistribution of the AltLex dataset?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list appears incomplete (e.g., missing information for several cited works).
*   Exploring attention mechanisms within the LSTM architecture could potentially further improve performance by allowing the model to focus on the most relevant parts of the input sequence.
