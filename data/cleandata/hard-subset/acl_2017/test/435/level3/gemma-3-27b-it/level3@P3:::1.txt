## Review of “Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context”

This paper proposes LSTM-based models for classifying whether connectives indicate intended causal relations, arguing that neural representations capture diverse causal expressions better than syntactic methods. Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support for the approach, and the authors release TensorFlow code/models. While the empirical results are positive, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses in its presentation, theoretical grounding, and analysis.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration on AltLex:** The primary contribution is the application of LSTM networks to the AltLex corpus and the demonstration that these models achieve competitive, if not state-of-the-art, performance on causal relation classification. This provides a baseline for future work on this relatively new dataset.
2. **Implementation and Release of Code:** The release of TensorFlow code and models is a valuable contribution, facilitating reproducibility and further research.
3. **Highlighting the Limitations of Existing Approaches:** The paper effectively points out the shortcomings of syntax-based and lexicon-dependent methods for identifying causal relations, motivating the need for more flexible, context-aware approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The models consistently outperform the baseline (B1) and achieve competitive performance with the state-of-the-art (B2) on the AltLex corpus. The improvements in F1-score and accuracy, reported in Table 5, are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Reproducibility:** The release of code and models greatly enhances the reproducibility of the work, a crucial aspect of scientific research.
3. **Focus on a Challenging Task:** The task of causal disambiguation, especially with AltLex expressions, is genuinely challenging, requiring models to go beyond simple lexical cues and consider broader contextual information.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors explore different LSTM architectures (Stated Pair LSTM vs. Pair LSTM), padding strategies, and optimizers, demonstrating a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Poorly Defined Contribution & Literature Review:** The introduction reads primarily as a literature review *without* establishing a clear research question or hypothesis. It lacks a concise statement of the paper’s central contribution and fails to take a firm stance on the problem of causation. The overlap between the introduction and the Related Work section is redundant.
2. **Unclear Theory of Causation:** The paper invokes a vaguely Lewis-style counterfactual view of causation, but without acknowledging its known problems (e.g., manipulation arguments) or committing to a specific, well-defined theory. This lack of theoretical clarity undermines the interpretation of the results.
3. **Ambiguous Example Analysis:** The discussion surrounding the example at line 256 is confusing. The authors' judgments regarding truth/falsity and event decomposition are unexplained, rendering the causal claim difficult to follow. This weakens the argument for the model’s ability to capture nuanced causal reasoning.
4. **Misleading and Imprecise Figures:** Figure 1’s caption is misleading as it only depicts the “Pair_LSTM” architecture, while the text describes both architectures. Furthermore, the diagram itself is imprecise, with unclear symbols, missing layer semantics, and ambiguous LSTM connections.
5. **Unclear Padding Discussion:** The sentence about padding at line 480 is unclear. If padding is an artifact of the TensorFlow implementation, this should be explicitly stated. The relevance of padding to encoding causal meaning needs clarification.
6. **Unjustified Interpretation of Results:** The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones (line 587) is interesting but the authors’ interpretation—that it contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information—is not well-justified. It's equally plausible that causal relationships aren't necessarily encoded sequentially within the sentence.
7. **Inconsistent Hyperparameter Reporting:** Hyperparameter choices and reported best settings are inconsistent and difficult to interpret across the paper (e.g., lines 578 vs 636), hindering a clear understanding of model behavior and making replication more challenging.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you clearly articulate the central hypothesis of this work and how it differs from prior approaches?
2. What specific theory of causation underlies your approach, and how does your model operationalize that theory?
3. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the example at line 256, clarifying your judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition?
4. Please revise Figure 1 to accurately represent both LSTM architectures and provide more detailed layer semantics.
5. Is the padding strategy a result of the TensorFlow implementation, or does it have a linguistic motivation? If the former, please state it explicitly.
6. Can you provide a more nuanced explanation for why independent LSTMs might outperform sequential ones, considering alternative explanations beyond the absence of shared sub-sentential information?
7. Please clarify the inconsistencies in hyperparameter reporting throughout the paper.

**Additional Comments:**

The claim regarding improved disambiguation of “which then” (line 659) relies on a single example. A systematic evaluation with a constructed dataset of ambiguous examples would strengthen this claim.
