## Review of “Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context”

This paper proposes LSTM-based models for classifying whether connectives indicate intended causal relations, arguing that neural representations can better capture diverse causal expressions than syntactic methods. The authors experiment on the AltLex corpus and release their TensorFlow code and models, which is a positive contribution to reproducibility. However, the paper suffers from several issues impacting its overall strength and clarity.

The primary weakness lies in the conceptual framing. The introduction reads largely as a literature review *instead* of motivating the research and clearly stating its contribution. It lacks a focused argument regarding causation, and the eventual claim – that neural networks can encode meaning for disambiguation – feels underwhelming given the extensive build-up. The underlying theory of causation is ill-defined. While hinting at a counterfactual view (Lewis-style), the authors don't engage with known problems or commit to a specific theoretical stance. 

Further, several technical aspects require clarification. The discussion surrounding the example at line 256 is confusing due to unexplained judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition. Figure 1’s caption is misleading, the diagram itself is imprecise, and the explanation of padding at line 480 is unclear – is it an artifact of TensorFlow and if so, why is it relevant? The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones is also problematic; the authors' interpretation contradicts expected behavior regarding shared sub-sentential information. Finally, inconsistent hyperparameter reporting (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636) hinders clear conclusions about model behavior. The assertion of superior performance on “which then” rests on a single example, requiring systematic testing with constructed ambiguous cases.

Despite these weaknesses, the release of code and models, alongside the empirical results showing consistent (though modest) improvements over baselines, represent valuable contributions. The use of the AltLex corpus is also a positive aspect. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Code Release:** Providing TensorFlow code and models promotes reproducibility and allows for further research.
*   **AltLex Corpus Utilization:** Using the AltLex corpus, designed to address the limitations of previous resources, is a significant step.
*   **Modest Performance Gains:** The reported improvements, even if modest, demonstrate the potential of the proposed neural approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Poorly Defined Motivation/Contribution:** The introduction lacks a clear statement of the paper's core contribution and fails to establish a strong research direction.
*   **Unclear Theory of Causation:** The invoked theory of causation is not explicitly stated or critically examined.
*   **Technical Imprecisions:** Several technical details are unclear or poorly explained, hindering understanding and potentially affecting reproducibility.



**Questions to Authors:**

*   Can you clearly articulate the primary contribution of this work beyond simply applying LSTMs to the AltLex corpus? What specific aspect of causal disambiguation does your approach advance?
*   What is the rationale for choosing a Lewis-style counterfactual view of causation, and how do you address its limitations in the context of natural language understanding?
*   Please clarify the event decomposition and truth judgments applied in the example at line 256.
*   Can you provide a more detailed and accurate description of the LSTM architecture in Figure 1?
*   Can you explain the purpose of the padding and its relevance to the encoding of causal meaning?
