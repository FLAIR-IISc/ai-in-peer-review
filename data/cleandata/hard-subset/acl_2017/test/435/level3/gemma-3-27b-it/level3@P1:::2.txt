This paper addresses the task of identifying causal relationships between clauses when those relationships are not explicitly marked by discourse connectives, leveraging the AltLex corpus. The authors argue that neural networks, specifically LSTMs, are well-suited to encoding the contextual information necessary for this disambiguation task, outperforming previous methods relying on hand-engineered features. They propose an architecture utilizing GloVe embeddings, followed by an LSTM layer and three dense layers with tanh activation functions, culminating in a softmax output.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the demonstration of a competitive neural network approach to causal relation classification on the AltLex corpus. While the task itself isn’t entirely novel, the application of LSTMs and the systematic evaluation on this specific dataset, designed to address the limitations of previous corpora, is valuable. The detailed experimental setup and accompanying analysis, including illustrative examples of correct classifications, strengthen this contribution. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough overview of prior work in causality detection, highlighting the challenges associated with limited syntactic or lexical cues.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of identifying implicit causal relations and explains why existing approaches, often reliant on limited lexical cues, fall short. The introduction effectively establishes the need for methods capable of understanding causal meaning from context.
2. **Strong Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing their proposed model against established baselines (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) and conducting ablation studies through different configurations (Stated Pair LSTM vs. Pair LSTM). The use of two versions of the AltLex corpus (“non bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping”) adds robustness to the evaluation.
3. **Detailed Analysis & Qualitative Examples:**  The paper goes beyond simply reporting numbers, providing insightful qualitative analysis. The inclusion of specific examples where the model succeeds (and presumably where it fails, though not explicitly discussed) enhances understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses. Table 8 is particularly helpful in illustrating cases where the proposed system outperforms the baseline.
4. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The writing is clear and concise, and the technical details are presented in a logical manner.  The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of the field.
5. **Data and Code Availability:** The authors have made their code and (an earlier version of) the dataset available, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the results are promising, the core contribution – applying neural networks to this task – feels somewhat incremental. It's generally expected that neural models will outperform feature-engineered systems given sufficient data.  The paper would benefit from a more compelling articulation of *why* LSTMs are particularly well-suited for this task beyond simply stating it.
2. **Evaluation Concerns – Potential Test Set Optimization:**  The description of the experiments raises concerns about potential overfitting to the test set. The paper states the best configuration was chosen based on results on the test set, rather than using a development set for model selection. This practice can lead to inflated performance estimates.
3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:**  The paper reports performance gains without providing statistical significance testing. Given the relatively small size of the AltLex corpus, statistical testing is crucial to determine whether the observed improvements are genuine or due to chance.
4. **Bootstrapping Procedure Underspecified:** The paper mentions a "bootstrapping" process used to extend the corpus, increasing its size by roughly 15%, but provides no details on how this bootstrapping was performed. This lack of transparency makes it difficult to assess the potential impact of this procedure on the data quality and the model's performance.
5. **Dataset Provenance and Rights:**  The paper notes concerns about reposting rights for the AltLex dataset. Clarifying the data licensing and accessibility is important for encouraging broader use and further research. Additionally, the reliance on Simple Wikipedia as a source introduces potential biases related to the corpus’s creation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Task Differentiation:** How does the task of identifying causal relations in this context differ from standard implicit discourse connective recognition? Are existing methods for the latter readily applicable, and if not, why?
2. **Bootstrapping Details:** Could you provide a detailed description of the bootstrapping procedure used to extend the AltLex corpus? What criteria were used to select new examples, and how was the quality of the bootstrapped data ensured?
3. **Dev Set Performance:** What were the performance results on a dedicated development set used for model selection and hyperparameter tuning?
4. **Precision vs. Recall:**  Why does the model tend to favor precision over recall? Are there specific characteristics of the data or the model architecture that contribute to this behavior?

**Additional Comments:**

- It would be helpful to include simpler baseline models (e.g., a single-layer LSTM) to establish a clearer baseline for comparison.
- Investigating the impact of different word embedding strategies (e.g., fine-tuning the GloVe embeddings) might provide further insights.
- Examining the types of errors the model makes (e.g., specific lexical markers it struggles with) could reveal areas for improvement.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear focus and well-executed experiments. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly regarding evaluation methodology, statistical significance, and data transparency – would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase confidence in the reported results. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
