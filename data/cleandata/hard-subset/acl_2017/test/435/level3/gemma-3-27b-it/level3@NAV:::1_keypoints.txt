- Proposes LSTM-based models to classify whether connectives signal causal relations, arguing neural representations outperform syntactic methods.
- Experiments conducted on the AltLex corpus with TensorFlow code and models released, aiding reproducibility.
- Introduction reads like a literature review and fails to clearly motivate the research or state the core contribution.
- The claim that neural networks can encode meaning for disambiguation feels underwhelming given the paper’s buildup.
- The paper’s theory of causation is ill-defined; it hints at a Lewis-style counterfactual view but does not commit or address known problems.
- The example discussion around line 256 is confusing due to unexplained truth/falsity judgments and unclear event decomposition.
- Figure 1’s caption and diagram are misleading/imprecise; the LSTM architecture description needs more detail and clarity.
- The explanation of padding (line 480) is unclear—uncertain whether it’s a TensorFlow artifact and why it matters for causal encoding.
- The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones is problematic and contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information.
- Inconsistent hyperparameter reporting (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636) prevents clear conclusions about model behavior.
- The assertion of superior performance on “which then” is based on a single example and requires systematic testing with constructed ambiguous cases.
- Despite issues, releasing code/models and achieving modest empirical improvements are valuable contributions.
- Use of the AltLex corpus is a positive and appropriate choice for the study.
