## Review of “Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context”

This paper tackles the challenging task of identifying causal versus non-causal clause relations when these are not explicitly marked by connective words, leveraging the AltLex dataset. The authors propose an LSTM-based neural network architecture for this task, arguing it’s superior to CNNs for clause representation, and report improvements over a previous state-of-the-art system. The writing is clear and the problem description is well-motivated. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the empirical validation of LSTM networks for this specific, nuanced task of implicit causality detection. While the use of neural networks for semantic relation classification isn’t novel, demonstrating their effectiveness on AltLex, and specifically arguing for LSTM’s advantage over CNNs within this context, is a valuable contribution. Additionally, the use of the AltLex corpus itself, and the empirical results presented on it, offer a useful benchmark for future research. A secondary contribution is the detailed experimental analysis, including examples where the model successfully generalizes.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper effectively outlines the limitations of relying solely on explicit causal connectives and motivates the need for systems capable of identifying implicit causality.
*   **Empirical Results:** The system achieves improvements over the baseline on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating a tangible benefit to the proposed approach. The improvements, while modest (0.5-1.5% F1), are statistically plausible.
*   **Detailed Analysis:** Providing examples of correct generalizations offers insight into *how* the model is functioning, increasing confidence in the findings.
*   **Well-Written:** The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making the technical details accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Rigor:** The most significant concern is the potential for inflated performance gains. The paper states the best configuration was selected based on test set performance, which introduces the risk of overfitting to the test data. A dev set should have been used for hyperparameter tuning and model selection.
*   **Statistical Significance:** The lack of statistical significance testing limits the ability to confidently assert the superiority of the proposed system.  Given the relatively small dataset, establishing statistical significance is crucial.
*   **Bootstrapping Procedure:** The paper mentions a bootstrapping procedure to extend the corpus, but provides no details about *how* this was done. This lack of transparency raises concerns about the quality and potential biases introduced by the additional data.
*   **Dataset Access and Clarity:** Concerns around data reposting rights and the unclear coding scheme for the TSV files need immediate clarification.
*   **Limited Baselines:**  While a baseline SVM system is used, including simpler LSTM architectures (e.g., a single-layer LSTM) would help better establish the contribution of the proposed model complexity.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please detail the bootstrapping procedure used to extend the AltLex corpus? Specifically, what criteria were used to select sentences for inclusion, and how were labels assigned?
2.  Was a development set used for hyperparameter tuning and model selection, or was the best configuration determined solely based on test set performance? If the latter, please provide results on a separate development set.
3.  Please clarify the coding scheme (0-1-2) used in the provided TSV files and confirm your rights to redistribute the dataset.
4.  Can you provide statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-test) to support the claim of outperforming the baseline?
5.  Why does your model exhibit a preference for precision over recall? Can you provide an analysis of the types of errors the model is making that contribute to this imbalance?
