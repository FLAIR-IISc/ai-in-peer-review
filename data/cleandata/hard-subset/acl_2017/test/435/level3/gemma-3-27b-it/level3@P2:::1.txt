This paper proposes a neural network architecture, specifically LSTMs, for the task of causal discourse relation classification, arguing that neural networks are better equipped to capture the contextual cues necessary for disambiguating causal meaning than traditional methods reliant on syntactic features or lexical resources. The authors experiment on the AltLex corpus and demonstrate improvements over a baseline SVM system.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution of this work is the empirical demonstration that LSTM-based models can achieve competitive performance on the task of causal relation classification, specifically on the challenging AltLex corpus. While not a novel architecture, the application of LSTMs to this specific problem, and the reported performance gains, are a valuable addition. A secondary contribution is the release of the code and models, which will facilitate further research in this area. Finally, the paper highlights the limitations of relying solely on lexical resources for causality detection, aligning with a broader trend in NLP towards more context-aware models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Empirical Results:** The paper provides solid empirical results showing that the proposed LSTM models outperform a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus. The gains, while not dramatic, are consistent and statistically significant in several configurations, indicating the effectiveness of the approach.
2.  **Focus on a Challenging Dataset:** The choice of the AltLex corpus is a significant strength. This dataset is specifically designed to address the limitations of previous corpora by including a wider range of causal expressions beyond explicit markers, making the evaluation more realistic and challenging.
3.  **Code Release:** The authors’ commitment to releasing their code and models is commendable and will facilitate reproducibility and further research by the community.
4.  **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors clearly define the task of causality classification and frame it within the context of existing literature, specifically highlighting the shortcomings of syntax-based and lexicon-based approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The paper lacks a clear articulation of the underlying theory of causation being employed. While the introduction briefly mentions different theories, the authors fail to commit to a specific framework or address the inherent complexities and philosophical debates surrounding causal reasoning. The discussion around counterfactuals is particularly underdeveloped.
2.  **Overly Descriptive Introduction:** The introduction reads more like a literature review than a focused statement of the paper's contribution. It lacks a clear thesis statement and does not effectively motivate the proposed approach. The extensive background information could be significantly condensed.
3.  **Unclear Justification of Design Choices:** The rationale behind certain design choices, such as the two LSTM architectures (Stated Pair LSTM and Pair LSTM), is not fully explained. The authors' interpretation of why the independent LSTMs outperform the sequential ones (line 587) is particularly weak and counterintuitive.
4.  **Ambiguous Explanation & Presentation:** The example used to illustrate a judgment about the causal relationship in the third paragraph of section 3.1 is unclear and the connection to the larger theoretical argument is underdeveloped. Figure 1 is misleading and imprecise, with unclear symbols and a lack of essential details. The discussion of the padding strategy (line 480) is confusing and requires clarification.
5.  **Limited Analysis:** The analysis section is largely descriptive, presenting results without delving into a deeper understanding of *why* the model performs well in certain cases and fails in others. The single example used to demonstrate improved performance on ambiguous connectives (“which then”) is insufficient; a more systematic evaluation is needed.
6. **Inconsistent Reporting:** Hyperparameter choices and reported best settings are inconsistently presented throughout the paper, making it difficult to draw clear conclusions about model behavior.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you clarify the theoretical framework guiding your approach to causal reasoning? Specifically, are you adopting a counterfactual account, and if so, how do you address the known challenges associated with counterfactual analysis in NLP?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind using two separate LSTMs in the “Pair LSTM” architecture? What specific limitations of the “Stated Pair LSTM” architecture did you aim to address?
3.  Can you provide a more systematic evaluation of the model’s performance on ambiguous connectives, such as “which then”, using a larger set of constructed examples?
4.  Please clarify the discussion around the padding strategy. Is padding a technical artifact of the implementation, or does it have a theoretical impact on the model’s ability to encode causal meaning?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper would benefit from a more concise introduction, a clearer articulation of the underlying theory of causation, and a more thorough analysis of the model’s performance. Improving the clarity of the figures and providing more consistent reporting of hyperparameters would also enhance the paper’s readability and impact. Despite these weaknesses, the empirical results and the release of the code make this a valuable contribution to the field.
