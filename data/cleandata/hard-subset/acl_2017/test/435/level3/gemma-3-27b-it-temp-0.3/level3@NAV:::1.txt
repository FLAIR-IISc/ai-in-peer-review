## Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

This paper proposes LSTM-based models for classifying whether connectives indicate intended causal relations, arguing that neural representations can better capture diverse causal expressions than syntactic methods. The authors experiment on the AltLex corpus and release their TensorFlow code and models, which is a positive contribution to reproducibility.

The primary contribution, as I see it, lies in the application of LSTMs to the AltLex corpus for causal relation classification, and the public release of the code. While the core idea of using neural networks for this task isn't novel, applying it specifically to AltLex and providing implementation details is valuable. A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration, albeit modest, that LSTMs can outperform a baseline SVM system on this specific dataset. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The introduction is overly descriptive and reads more like a literature review than a focused introduction to the problem and the authors’ specific approach. It lacks a clear statement of the paper’s contribution and a defined stance on the complexities of causation. The underlying theory of causation is vaguely alluded to (Lewis-style counterfactuals) without critical engagement with its limitations. The discussion around the example at line 256 is unclear and lacks justification for the authors’ causal judgments.

Furthermore, the presentation of the model (Figure 1) is misleading and imprecise. The caption is inaccurate, and the diagram itself lacks clarity regarding layer semantics and LSTM connections. Several technical details are unclear, such as the purpose of padding (line 480) and its impact on encoding causal meaning. The authors’ interpretation of the independent LSTM outperforming sequential LSTMs (line 587) is unsubstantiated and counterintuitive. Inconsistencies in hyperparameter reporting further complicate the interpretation of results. Finally, the claim of improved performance on “which then” is based on a single example and requires systematic evaluation with a constructed test set.

**Strengths:**

*   **Publicly Available Code:** The release of TensorFlow code and models promotes reproducibility and allows for further research.
*   **Application to AltLex:** Applying neural networks to the AltLex corpus is a valuable step towards addressing the challenge of non-canonical causal markers.
*   **Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate a modest but consistent improvement over a baseline, suggesting the potential of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Poorly Defined Introduction:** The introduction lacks focus and a clear statement of contribution.
*   **Unclear Theoretical Foundation:** The paper lacks a rigorous discussion of the underlying theory of causation.
*   **Ambiguous Explanations:** Key examples and technical details are poorly explained and lack justification.
*   **Misleading Figure & Inconsistent Reporting:** The model diagram is inaccurate, and hyperparameter reporting is inconsistent.
*   **Limited Evaluation:** The claim of improved performance on “which then” is based on insufficient evidence.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you explicitly state the theory of causation underpinning your work and address its known limitations?
2.  Please clarify the purpose of padding and its potential impact on the model's ability to encode causal meaning.
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation for why independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones, considering the potential benefits of shared sub-sentential information?
4.  Will you release a systematically constructed test set with ambiguous examples like “which then” to validate your claims of improved disambiguation?
