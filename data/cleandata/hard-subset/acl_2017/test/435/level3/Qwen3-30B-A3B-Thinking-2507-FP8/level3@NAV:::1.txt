

**Review**

This paper proposes LSTM-based models for disambiguating causal lexical markers, claiming neural representations better capture causal expressions than syntactic methods. The authors evaluate on the AltLex corpus, showing modest but consistent improvements over baselines.

**Main Contributions:** The paper's strongest contribution is the freely available AltLex corpus, which addresses limited coverage of causal expressions. The LSTM architecture for causal disambiguation is secondary, and the claim about neural representations capturing causal meaning is not sufficiently supported.

**Strengths:** (1) The AltLex corpus is a valuable resource for causality research, addressing a clear gap in existing datasets. (2) The authors release TensorFlow code and models, enhancing reproducibility. (3) The empirical results on AltLex show consistent improvements over baselines, demonstrating the approach's viability.

**Weaknesses:** (1) The introduction reads like a literature review, redundantly repeating the Related Work section and failing to clearly state the paper's novel contribution. (2) The theoretical foundation is unclear—authors invoke Lewis-style counterfactuals without addressing known problems or committing to a specific causal theory. (3) The analysis of "which then" (line 256) is confusing, with unexplained judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition. (4) Methodological issues include a misleading Figure 1 caption, unclear padding explanation (line 480), and inconsistent hyperparameter reporting (lines 578 vs 636). (5) The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones (line 587) contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information and lacks justification.

**Questions to Authors:** How do you reconcile your interpretation of LSTM performance (line 587) with the expectation that shared sub-sentential information should improve results? Can you provide systematic testing of "which then" disambiguation beyond the single example in Table 8?
