- Paper addresses detecting causal vs. non-causal clause relations without explicit markers using the AltLex dataset, claiming three contributions: an LSTM-based neural architecture, evidence that context encoding is necessary, and qualitative generalization analysis.  
- Main claimed contribution is the neural (LSTM) architecture that reportedly outperforms prior work.  
- Clear problem motivation: shows limitations of syntactic/lexical approaches and argues causality requires context.  
- Significant empirical improvement: +2.13% F1 over prior SOTA (Hidey & McKeown) on the non-bootstrapped corpus, with consistent metric gains.  
- Strong qualitative examples demonstrating disambiguation of ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then”) where prior systems fail.  
- Weakness: task definition is unclear and not distinguished from implicit connective recognition, undermining novelty.  
- Weakness: methodological flaw—best configuration chosen by test-set performance rather than dev set, risking inflated results.  
- Weakness: no statistical significance testing reported for improvements.  
- Weakness: insufficient detail on bootstrapping procedure that added ~15% of data, harming reproducibility.  
- Weakness: unclear 0-1-2 coding in TSV files and unspecified dataset reposting/rights.  
- Question: how does this task differ from implicit connective recognition and why can’t existing methods be applied?  
- Question: provide exact bootstrapping algorithm and criteria used to extend the AltLex corpus.  
- Question: clarify whether a dev set was used for model selection and how test-based selection affects reported gains.  
- Question: explain the 0-1-2 coding scheme used in the dataset files.  
- Question: what is the paper’s specific contribution beyond showing neural methods outperform engineered features; could simpler baselines (e.g., 1-layer LSTM) suffice and why was the chosen architecture used?  
- Overall: paper makes a meaningful contribution showing the necessity of context encoding, but task clarity, experimental design, and dataset/method details must be addressed before acceptance.
