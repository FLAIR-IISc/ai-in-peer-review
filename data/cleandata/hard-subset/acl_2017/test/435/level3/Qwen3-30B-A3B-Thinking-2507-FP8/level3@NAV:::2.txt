

**Review**

This paper proposes a neural network approach to disambiguate causal lexical markers when not explicitly marked, using the AltLex dataset. The main contributions, as assessed, are: (1) a novel LSTM-based architecture for causal relation disambiguation that outperforms prior feature-based methods; (2) comprehensive qualitative and quantitative analysis demonstrating the model's ability to generalize to ambiguous causal expressions; and (3) a clear demonstration that context encoding is essential for accurate causal disambiguation.

Strengths include the well-written presentation with a clear problem statement, thorough analysis of model behavior across different lexical markers, and convincing examples where the model correctly identifies causal relations despite ambiguous markers (e.g., "which then"). The paper also provides a strong empirical comparison showing the proposed system outperforms the state-of-the-art by approximately 2.13% F1 on the non-bootstrapped corpus version.

However, several critical weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution. First, the task definition remains unclear compared to implicit connective recognition, and the paper fails to explain why prior methods cannot be directly applied. Second, the experimental design contains significant issues: the best configuration appears to be selected on the test set rather than the dev set, potentially inflating reported gains. Third, the bootstrapping procedure used to extend the corpus by ~15% is not explained, making the corpus construction opaque. Fourth, the paper lacks statistical significance testing and provides no analysis of dataset annotation quality, which could affect score reliability given the Wikipedia-based data. Finally, the novelty claim is weak—the paper's main contribution (neural methods outperforming engineered features) is largely expected in the field, and simpler baselines (e.g., single-layer LSTMs) were not included for proper comparison.

The authors should clarify the task distinction from implicit connective recognition, explain the bootstrapping procedure, report statistical significance, and address the test set selection bias. They should also provide a more nuanced analysis of why the model favors precision over recall. Without addressing these issues, the incremental contribution of this work is questionable.
