

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary
This paper proposes LSTM-based neural networks for classifying whether causal connectives express intended causal relations, arguing that neural representations better capture diverse causal expressions than syntactic methods. The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, claiming modest but consistent improvements over baselines. They present two model variants: "Stated Pair LSTM" (with inter-LSTM connections) and "Pair LSTM" (independent LSTMs).

## Main Contributions
1. **Empirical demonstration of neural approaches for causal disambiguation**: The paper shows that neural networks can outperform syntactic methods on causal relation disambiguation, though the gains are modest.
2. **Systematic evaluation on AltLex corpus**: The authors provide a thorough empirical assessment of their model variants, with detailed analysis of performance across different lexical markers.
3. **Code and model release**: The authors provide TensorFlow code and pre-trained models, enhancing reproducibility.

## Strengths
1. **Well-defined task and corpus**: The paper clearly defines causal meaning disambiguation as a binary classification task (causal vs. non-causal) and uses the AltLex corpus, which is a valuable resource for causal relation studies.
2. **Consistent empirical results**: The experiments show consistent improvements over baselines (B1 and B2) across different model configurations and corpus versions, with the "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" configuration achieving the best balance between precision and recall.
3. **Practical implementation**: The release of TensorFlow code and models enables further research and replication, which is a significant contribution to the community.

## Weaknesses
1. **Weak theoretical foundation**: The paper lacks a clear theoretical stance on causation. The authors reference Lewis-style counterfactuals without addressing known problems or committing to an explicit theory, making the core premise of their work unclear. The discussion of examples (line 256) remains confusing without explanation of event decomposition or truth judgments.
2. **Misleading presentation**: The introduction duplicates content from the related work section and fails to articulate a clear positive contribution. The figure 1 caption is misleading (only shows Pair LSTM), and the diagram is imprecise with unclear symbols and ambiguous LSTM connections.
3. **Unjustified claims and inconsistent methodology**: The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones (line 587) contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information and lacks proper justification. Hyperparameter choices are inconsistent (lines 578 vs. 636), making it difficult to interpret model behavior. The claim about "which then" disambiguation is based on a single example, not systematic testing.
4. **Technical ambiguities**: The discussion of padding (line 480) is unclear about its relevance to encoding causal meaning, and the paper fails to explain why padding strategies matter for causal disambiguation.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could the authors clarify their theoretical stance on causation? Specifically, how does their approach align with or differ from Lewis's counterfactual theory, and how do they address known challenges with this approach?
2. The claim about "which then" disambiguation is based on a single example. Could the authors provide systematic evaluation with multiple constructed ambiguous examples to support this claim?
3. The hyperparameter choices appear inconsistent across the paper. Could the authors clarify the rationale for the different settings reported in lines 578 and 636?

## Overall Assessment
The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of causal relation disambiguation by demonstrating that neural approaches can outperform syntactic methods on the AltLex corpus. However, the weak theoretical foundation, misleading presentation, and methodological inconsistencies significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The empirical results are promising but not sufficiently novel or compelling to warrant acceptance in ACL, especially given the paper's conceptual shortcomings. The release of code and models is a positive aspect but does not compensate for the fundamental theoretical and methodological weaknesses.
