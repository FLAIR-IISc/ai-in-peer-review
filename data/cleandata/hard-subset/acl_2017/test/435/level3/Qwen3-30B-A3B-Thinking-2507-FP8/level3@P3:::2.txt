

# Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of detecting causal vs. non-causal clause relations when not explicitly marked, using the AltLex dataset. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a neural network architecture that outperforms previous methods for causal meaning disambiguation; (2) empirical evidence that context encoding is necessary for causal disambiguation; and (3) qualitative analysis demonstrating the model's ability to generalize to ambiguous cases. The primary contribution is the neural architecture (LSTM-based) that significantly outperforms prior work, with the most important evidence being the clear demonstration that context encoding is essential for accurate causal disambiguation.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and justification**: The paper effectively establishes the need for context-based causal disambiguation by demonstrating the limitations of syntactic approaches and lexical resources. The authors convincingly argue that causality is not restricted to specific lexical constructions or syntactic patterns, which is a significant contribution to the field. The problem definition is clear and well-motivated, making it easy to understand the task's importance.

2. **Significant performance improvement**: The proposed model achieves a 2.13% F1 improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (Hidey & McKeown) in the "non bootstrapping" corpus, with consistent improvements across multiple metrics. This represents a meaningful advancement in the field, particularly since the authors demonstrate that their model generalizes to ambiguous cases where prior work fails (as shown in Table 8).

3. **Strong qualitative analysis**: The paper provides compelling examples where the model correctly disambiguates causal meaning in cases where the lexical marker is ambiguous (e.g., "which then"), while prior systems fail. This qualitative evidence strongly supports the authors' claim that context encoding is necessary for accurate causal disambiguation, making the contribution more tangible than just numerical improvements.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clarity on task definition**: The paper fails to adequately distinguish this task from implicit connective recognition, which is a significant concern. The AltLex dataset appears to be a collection of implicit causal relations, but the authors don't explain why prior implicit connective recognition methods cannot be directly applied. This lack of differentiation undermines the novelty claim and makes it difficult to assess the paper's contribution relative to existing work.

2. **Experimental design concerns**: The paper reports that the best configuration was selected based on test set performance rather than dev set performance, which is a serious methodological flaw. The reported 2.13% F1 improvement may be inflated due to this selection bias. Additionally, the paper does not include statistical significance testing for the reported improvements, which weakens the empirical claims.

3. **Insufficient methodological details**: The paper mentions bootstrapping that extends the corpus by ~15% but provides no details about the bootstrapping procedure, making it impossible to reproduce or understand the corpus construction. The unclear 0-1-2 coding in TSV files and questions about dataset reposting rights further undermine the paper's reproducibility.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the task of causal lexical marker disambiguation differ from implicit connective recognition, and why cannot existing implicit connective recognition methods be directly applied to this task?

2. Please clarify the bootstrapping procedure used to extend the AltLex corpus by 15%, including the exact algorithm and criteria for adding new instances.

3. The paper reports the best configuration was selected based on test set performance. Could you clarify whether the authors used a dev set for model selection, and if not, how this might affect the reported performance gains?

4. The paper mentions the use of "0-1-2 coding" in TSV files but doesn't explain the meaning of these codes. Please clarify the coding scheme.

5. The authors claim neural methods outperform engineered features, but this is expected in the current NLP landscape. What specific contribution does this paper make beyond demonstrating that neural methods work well for this task? Would simpler baselines (e.g., 1-layer LSTM) have been sufficient, and why did the authors choose their specific architecture?

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to causal relation detection by demonstrating the necessity of context encoding, and the empirical results are compelling. However, the lack of clarity on the task definition, methodological concerns regarding experimental design, and insufficient detail on the corpus construction are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before the paper can be accepted. The authors should clarify these points to better establish the novelty and significance of their contribution.
