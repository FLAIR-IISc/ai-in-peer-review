- Primary contribution: The freely available AltLex corpus is the paper's strongest contribution; the LSTM architecture is secondary and the claim that neural representations capture causal meaning is insufficiently supported.

- Main result: LSTM-based models show modest but consistent improvements over baselines on the AltLex corpus.

- Strength: AltLex corpus fills a clear gap in datasets for causality research.

- Strength: Authors release TensorFlow code and models, improving reproducibility.

- Strength: Empirical experiments demonstrate the approach's viability via consistent gains.

- Weakness: Introduction redundantly repeats Related Work and fails to clearly state the novel contribution.

- Weakness: Theoretical foundation is unclear—Lewis-style counterfactuals are invoked without addressing known issues or committing to a causal theory.

- Weakness: The analysis of the phrase "which then" is confusing, with unexplained judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition.

- Weakness: Methodological problems include a misleading Figure 1 caption, unclear explanation of padding, and inconsistent hyperparameter reporting.

- Weakness: The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential/shared models contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information and lacks justification.

- Question: How do the authors reconcile the interpretation of LSTM performance with the expectation that shared sub-sentential information should improve results?

- Question: Can the authors provide systematic testing of "which then" disambiguation beyond the single example in Table 8?
