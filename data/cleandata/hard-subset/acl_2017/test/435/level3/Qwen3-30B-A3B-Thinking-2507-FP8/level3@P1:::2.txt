

This paper tackles causal relation disambiguation, specifically identifying causal vs. non-causal clause relations when not explicitly marked, using the AltLex dataset. The authors argue that neural networks, particularly LSTMs, better encode contextual meaning for causal disambiguation than previous approaches based on syntactic constructions or lexical resources. They propose a neural architecture with two inputs (first event and lexical marker + second event), processed by LSTMs followed by dense layers with tanh activation and a softmax output.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) clear identification of the need for contextual encoding rather than syntactic constructions for causal disambiguation, (2) a well-constructed neural architecture that outperforms the state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus, and (3) qualitative and quantitative analyses demonstrating the model's ability to handle ambiguous causal markers.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case that causality isn't tied to specific syntactic constructions, as evidenced by the Neeleman and van de Koot (2012) critique. The authors provide a thorough evaluation on the AltLex corpus, including both "non bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" versions, with consistent improvements over Hidey & McKeown (2016) by 2.13% F1 in the best configuration. The qualitative examples (Table 8) effectively demonstrate the model's ability to correctly disambiguate causal meaning in cases where the lexical markers are ambiguous, such as "break" and "which then" - where the system correctly identifies causal meaning while the baseline fails.

Weaknesses: The most significant weakness is the experimental design flaw: the best configuration appears to have been selected on the test set rather than a development set, potentially inflating reported gains. The paper lacks statistical significance testing for the reported improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the gains are meaningful. The novelty claim is questionable, as neural methods outperforming engineered features is expected rather than novel in NLP. The bootstrapping procedure for expanding the corpus is not explained, which is critical for understanding the dataset's reliability. Additionally, the paper doesn't clearly distinguish its task from implicit connective recognition, weakening the motivation.

The paper is well-written with clear problem description, but several concerns remain. The authors claim the system "outperforms the state-of-the-art," but the improvements are modest (0.5-1.5% F1) and not statistically significant. The paper mentions bootstrapping that extends the corpus by ~15%, but doesn't explain the procedure. The code and data were uploaded, but the 0-1-2 coding in TSV files is unclear, and the reposting rights for the dataset are questionable.

The authors should clarify how this task differs from implicit connective recognition, as this is a critical distinction for the paper's contribution. They should also provide a proper development set for model selection and include statistical significance testing. The paper would benefit from including simpler baselines (e.g., 1-layer LSTM) to better demonstrate the value of their architecture.

Questions to authors: (1) How does your causal disambiguation task differ from implicit connective recognition tasks in existing literature? (2) Please explain the bootstrapping procedure used to expand the AltLex corpus. (3) How was the best configuration selected (development set vs. test set)? (4) Why does your model consistently favor precision over recall, and what types of examples are most challenging? (5) What is the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files, and what are the reposting rights for the dataset?

Overall, the paper makes a reasonable contribution to causal relation disambiguation by demonstrating that contextual encoding with LSTMs can handle ambiguous causal markers better than previous approaches. However, the modest improvements, lack of statistical significance, and experimental design flaws limit the paper's impact. The work would be stronger with clearer task distinction, proper development set usage, and more substantial novelty claims.
