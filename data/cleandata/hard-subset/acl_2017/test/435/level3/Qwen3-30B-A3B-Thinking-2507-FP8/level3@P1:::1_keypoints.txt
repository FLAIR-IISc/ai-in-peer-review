- Summary: Proposes an LSTM-based neural approach to disambiguate whether lexical markers signal causal relations, evaluated on the AltLex corpus with claimed improvements.

- Contribution 1: Formalizes causal meaning disambiguation as a binary classification task.

- Contribution 2: Shows neural context encoding can outperform prior methods on the AltLex corpus.

- Contribution 3: Releases code and models for reproducibility.

- Strength 1: Tackles a meaningful, context-dependent causality problem and naturally extends prior work by encoding event pairs with LSTMs.

- Strength 2: Empirical gains are consistent; best model ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") achieves 81.96% F1, ±1.06% over prior SOTA.

- Strength 3: Public release of TensorFlow code and models aids reproducibility and future work.

- Weakness 1: Theoretical foundation is unclear; paper implicitly leans on a Lewis-style counterfactual view without addressing its problems or committing to a causal theory.

- Weakness 2: Introduction and related work overlap and are poorly structured, obscuring the paper’s unique contribution and stance on causation.

- Weakness 3: Some claims lack support (e.g., improved disambiguation of "which then" is based on a single example).

- Weakness 4: Experimental details are inconsistent and unclear (conflicting hyperparameter reports; unclear padding strategy and its relevance to causality).

- Weakness 5: Interpretation that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones is unjustified and contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information.

- Question 1: Clarify the paper’s theoretical stance on causation and how it informs the method.

- Question 2: Provide systematic evidence beyond the single "which then" example to support disambiguation claims.

- Question 3: Explain why independent LSTMs outperform sequential models and how this aligns with expected shared information between event pairs.

- Question 4: Justify the choice of the "mean" padding strategy and its relation to encoding causal meaning versus being a training artifact.

- Overall: Important problem and modest empirical gains, but weak theory, inconsistent experiments, and insufficient evidence for key claims mean substantial revision is needed; technical contribution is incremental.
