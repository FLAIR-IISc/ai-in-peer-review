

# Review for ACL 2017

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a neural network architecture using LSTMs to classify whether lexical markers indicate causal relations between events. The authors claim that neural networks can better encode contextual meaning for causal disambiguation compared to previous syntactic or lexical feature-based approaches. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A demonstration that neural approaches can outperform feature-based methods on the AltLex corpus, suggesting that context encoding may be valuable for causal relation classification.
2. A systematic evaluation of neural architectures for causal disambiguation, addressing a gap in the literature where prior work has relied on hand-crafted features.
3. The release of code and models, which is a valuable contribution to the community for reproducibility and future research.

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature by applying neural methods to causal relation disambiguation, which has previously been dominated by feature-based approaches. The authors' empirical demonstration that neural methods can outperform these baselines on the AltLex corpus is a valuable contribution to the field.

2. The paper makes its code and models publicly available, which is a significant contribution to the community and will facilitate future work in this area. This is particularly valuable given the limited resources for causal relation studies.

3. The paper demonstrates that neural approaches can handle ambiguous causal markers (like "which then") better than previous methods, as shown in Table 8. This suggests the potential for more robust causal relation classifiers that don't rely on fixed lexical patterns.

## Weaknesses

1. The theoretical foundation is unclear and problematic. The authors claim to study causal relations but fail to specify what they mean by "causality" or how their approach relates to existing theories. The discussion of counterfactuals (line 256) is confusing and unexplained, leaving the reader uncertain about the paper's theoretical stance. This is a significant weakness for a paper claiming to study a fundamental linguistic phenomenon.

2. The paper's introduction and related work sections are repetitive, with the introduction essentially duplicating the content of the related work. This makes it difficult to identify the specific contribution of the paper, which is a major issue for a conference submission.

3. The experimental design contains several inconsistencies. The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones (line 587) is based on a flawed interpretation that contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information. Additionally, hyperparameter choices are inconsistent throughout the paper (lines 578 vs 636), making it difficult to interpret the results and conclusions.

4. The claim about disambiguating "which then" is based on a single example (Table 8), which is insufficient evidence. The authors should provide systematic testing with many constructed ambiguous examples to support this claim.

5. The paper's Figure 1 and caption are misleading and imprecise. The caption states it shows "Pair LSTM" but the diagram appears to show a different architecture, and the diagram itself lacks clear layer semantics and has ambiguous connections. This undermines the paper's technical presentation.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify your theoretical stance on causation? Specifically, how does your approach relate to Lewis-style counterfactual theories, and how do you address the problems with this approach that are well-known in the literature?

2. The paper claims "which then" is disambiguated correctly by your model but not by the baseline. Can you provide more systematic evidence for this claim beyond the single example in Table 8?

3. The explanation of zero-padding (line 480) is unclear. Is this a necessary aspect of your model, or is it simply a technical detail of the implementation? If the latter, how does it relate to the causal meaning encoding you claim to be studying?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of causal relation classification by demonstrating that neural approaches can outperform feature-based baselines on the AltLex corpus. However, the theoretical foundation is weak, the paper is repetitive, and the experimental design contains significant inconsistencies. While the code release is a positive aspect, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its main claims, particularly regarding the theoretical framework and the handling of ambiguous causal markers. I recommend rejection.
