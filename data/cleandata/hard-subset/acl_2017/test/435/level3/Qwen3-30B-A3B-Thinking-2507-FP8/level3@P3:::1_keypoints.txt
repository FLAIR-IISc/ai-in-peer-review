- Proposes LSTM-based models (Pair LSTM and Stated Pair LSTM) to classify whether causal connectives express causal relations; evaluated on the AltLex corpus with modest improvements over baselines.

- Contribution: empirical demonstration that neural approaches can outperform syntactic methods for causal disambiguation (modest gains).

- Contribution: systematic evaluation on the AltLex corpus with analysis across different lexical markers and model variants.

- Contribution: release of TensorFlow code and pre-trained models for reproducibility.

- Strength: clear task definition (binary causal vs. non-causal) and use of the AltLex corpus as an appropriate dataset.

- Strength: consistent empirical improvements across baselines and configurations; "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" shows best precision/recall balance.

- Strength: practical implementation and public release of code/models enabling replication.

- Weakness: weak theoretical foundation—references Lewis-style counterfactuals without addressing known problems or committing to a clear theory; example analysis remains confusing.

- Weakness: misleading presentation—intro duplicates related work, Figure 1 caption is inaccurate, and the diagram uses unclear symbols and ambiguous LSTM connections.

- Weakness: unjustified claims and inconsistent methodology—contradictory claim about independent vs. sequential LSTMs, inconsistent hyperparameter reporting, and anecdotal evidence for "which then" disambiguation.

- Weakness: technical ambiguities—unclear discussion of padding and no explanation why padding strategies matter for causal disambiguation.

- Question: clarify the paper's theoretical stance on causation and how it aligns with or differs from Lewis's counterfactual theory, addressing known challenges.

- Question: provide systematic evaluation of "which then" and similar cases rather than relying on a single example.

- Question: clarify and reconcile inconsistent hyperparameter choices reported in different parts of the paper.

- Overall: modest empirical contribution undermined by weak theory, misleading presentation, and methodological inconsistencies; code release is positive but insufficient to justify acceptance.
