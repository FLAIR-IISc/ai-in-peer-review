- Proposes an LSTM-based neural architecture to classify whether lexical markers indicate causal relations and shows neural methods can outperform feature-based baselines on the AltLex corpus.
- Provides a systematic evaluation of neural architectures for causal disambiguation, addressing reliance on hand-crafted features in prior work.
- Releases code and models to support reproducibility and future research.
- Addresses an important gap by applying neural methods and demonstrating empirical improvement on AltLex.
- Public code and models are a valuable resource given limited datasets for causal relation studies.
- Shows the neural approach handles ambiguous causal markers (e.g., “which then”) better than previous methods (illustrated in results).
- Theoretical foundation is unclear: fails to define “causality,” poorly relates to existing theories, and presents a confusing counterfactual discussion.
- Introduction and related work are repetitive, making the paper’s unique contribution hard to identify.
- Experimental design inconsistencies: surprising claims about independent vs sequential LSTMs and inconsistent hyperparameter reporting undermine result interpretation.
- Claim of disambiguating “which then” rests on a single example and lacks systematic validation.
- Figure 1 and its caption are misleading and imprecise, with unclear layer semantics and ambiguous connections.
- Question: Clarify theoretical stance on causation and relation to Lewis-style counterfactual theories and their known issues.
- Question: Provide systematic evidence for correct disambiguation of “which then” beyond the single example.
- Question: Explain the role of zero-padding—necessary model aspect or implementation detail—and how it relates to encoding causal meaning.
- Overall: Modest empirical contribution with useful code release, but weak theory, repetition, and experimental flaws lead to a recommendation to reject.
