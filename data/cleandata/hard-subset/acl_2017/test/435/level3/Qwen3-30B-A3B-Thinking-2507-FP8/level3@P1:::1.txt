

Summary: This paper proposes a neural network approach for causality disambiguation, focusing on classifying whether lexical markers (like "because" or "so") signal causal relations between events. The authors claim that encoding context through neural networks better captures the diverse ways causality is expressed than previous syntactic or lexical feature-based methods. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, claiming improvements over prior work.

Main contributions: The paper makes three key contributions. First, it provides a clear task definition for causal meaning disambiguation as a binary classification task. Second, it demonstrates that neural encoding of context can outperform previous methods on the AltLex corpus. Third, it releases code and models to support reproducibility.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful gap in causality classification by moving beyond syntactic constructions and lexical resources, which aligns with the authors' stated hypothesis that causality is context-dependent. Their approach of using LSTM to encode the two events of a potential causal relation represents a natural progression from previous work.
2. The empirical results show consistent improvements over baselines, particularly with the "Pair LSTM" architecture. The best configuration ("Pair LSTM Mean Adam") achieves 81.96% F1 on the bootstrapping version of the corpus, outperforming the state-of-the-art by 1.06% F1.
3. The authors release TensorFlow code and models, which is valuable for reproducibility and future work in this area.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's theoretical foundation is unclear and underdeveloped. The authors seem to implicitly invoke a Lewis-style counterfactual view of causation without addressing known problems with this approach (e.g., the example at line 256 shows confusing judgments about event decomposition and truth/falsity). They fail to commit to an explicit theory of causation, which is essential for their task definition.
2. The introduction and related work sections are poorly structured and contain significant overlap. The introduction reads like a literature review rather than presenting a clear contribution, and the authors fail to state a clear positive contribution or take a clear stance on causation theory.
3. Several claims are not properly supported. The claim that their system disambiguates "which then" better than prior work is based on a single example (Table 8), which is insufficient evidence. The authors should conduct systematic testing with many ambiguous examples to support this claim.
4. The experimental details are inconsistent and unclear. The hyperparameter choices reported in lines 578 and 636 differ, making it hard to understand the best configuration. The padding strategy explanation (line 480) is unclear and fails to connect padding to causal meaning encoding.
5. The interpretation of the "independent LSTMs" result (line 587) is unjustified. The authors claim that "the assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold," but this contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information that should be captured by sequential processing.

Questions to Authors:
1. Can you clarify your theoretical stance on causation and how it specifically informs your approach? The paper seems to implicitly rely on counterfactual causation without addressing known limitations.
2. How did you handle the ambiguous "which then" example in your analysis beyond the single instance shown in Table 8? Can you provide more systematic evidence for this claim?
3. Why do independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones, and how does this align with expectations about shared information between the two events in a causal relation?
4. What is the specific rationale for using the "mean" padding strategy, and how does this choice relate to encoding causal meaning rather than being merely a training artifact?

Overall, while the paper addresses an important problem and demonstrates modest improvements over baselines, the theoretical foundation is weak, the experimental details are inconsistent, and key claims lack sufficient evidence. The authors should substantially revise the paper to clarify their theoretical stance, provide more systematic evidence for their claims, and resolve inconsistencies in their experimental setup before the work can be considered for publication. The technical contribution is incremental, and the paper would benefit from a more focused presentation that clearly distinguishes its contributions from prior work.
