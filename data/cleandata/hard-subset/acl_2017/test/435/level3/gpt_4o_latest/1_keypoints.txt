- Introduces an LSTM-based model to classify connective uses for detecting causal relations.  
- Primary contribution: neural encoding of causality with LSTMs, showing modest but consistent improvements on the AltLex corpus and outperforming Hidey & McKeown (2016); TensorFlow code provided.  
- Secondary contribution: comparison of independent vs sequential LSTM architectures, finding independent LSTMs perform better and arguing for contextual encoding over traditional syntactic/lexical approaches.  
- Novelty: uses LSTMs to encode causal meaning, moving beyond feature-based methods.  
- Empirical validation: well-structured experiments that consistently outperform baselines, using both bootstrapped and non-bootstrapped AltLex variants.  
- Reproducibility: authors provide TensorFlow implementations to facilitate replication.  
- Insightful observation: independent LSTM architectures outperform sequential ones, suggesting new architectural considerations.  
- Weak theoretical grounding: introduction reads like a literature review and lacks a clear, consistent causation framework.  
- Model description unclear: Figure 1 (“Pair_LSTM”) is vague and missing critical layer and representation details.  
- Example interpretation issues: specific example (line 256) is ambiguously analyzed, leaving event division and causal links unresolved.  
- Hyperparameters unexplained: no clear rationale or description of selection/search procedure, hurting interpretability and reproducibility.  
- Padding discussion confusing: padding strategies are poorly explained and their relevance to performance is unclear.  
- Insufficient validation: claim about disambiguating the causal meaning of “which then” is interesting but not robustly validated across diverse examples.  
- Questions for authors: clarify causation framework; provide full architecture details (layers, activations, input processing); explain hyperparameter selection methodology; elaborate on padding strategies and their impact.  
- Overall: valuable and promising LSTM-based approach with solid results, but requires revisions addressing theoretical clarity, model detail, and result interpretability before endorsement.
