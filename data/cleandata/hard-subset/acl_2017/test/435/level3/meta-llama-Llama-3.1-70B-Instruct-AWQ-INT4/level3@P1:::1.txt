**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically for disambiguating the causal meaning of lexical markers in natural language text. The authors argue that traditional syntactic methods are limited in their ability to capture the diverse ways in which causality is expressed in language, and that neural representations can better learn the contextual relationships between words. The paper presents experimental results on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating modest but consistent improvements over a baseline system.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural Architecture for Causality Classification**: The authors propose a novel neural network architecture for causality classification, which uses two inputs (the first event and the lexical marker and second event) and a combination of LSTM and dense layers to encode the contextual relationships between words.
2. **Improved Performance on AltLex Corpus**: The authors demonstrate that their proposed system outperforms a baseline system on the AltLex corpus, achieving higher precision, recall, and F1 scores.
3. **Release of TensorFlow Code and Models**: The authors release their TensorFlow code and models, making it possible for others to replicate and build upon their work.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel Approach to Causality Classification**: The authors' use of neural representations to capture the contextual relationships between words is a novel approach to causality classification, and their experimental results demonstrate its potential.
2. **Improved Performance on Challenging Corpus**: The AltLex corpus is a challenging dataset for causality classification, and the authors' system achieves higher performance than a baseline system.
3. **Release of Code and Models**: The authors' release of their code and models is a significant contribution to the research community, making it possible for others to build upon their work.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Unclear Theory of Causation**: The authors' theory of causation is unclear, and they do not address known problems with counterfactual views of causation.
2. **Confusing Example and Discussion**: The authors' discussion of the example at line 256 is confusing, and their judgments about truth and event decomposition are unexplained.
3. **Misleading Figure Caption and Diagram**: The figure caption is misleading, and the diagram is imprecise, with unclear symbols, missing layer semantics, and ambiguous LSTM connections.
4. **Unclear Hyperparameter Choices**: The authors' hyperparameter choices and reported best settings are inconsistent or hard to interpret across the paper, making conclusions about model behavior unclear.
5. **Limited Evaluation of "which then" Disambiguation**: The authors' claim that their system disambiguates "which then" better than prior work is based on a single example, and they do not provide systematic testing with many constructed ambiguous examples.

**Questions to Authors**

2. Can you provide more details about your example at line 256, including your judgments about truth and event decomposition?
3. Can you revise your figure caption and diagram to make them more accurate and clear?
4. Can you provide more details about your hyperparameter choices and how you selected the best settings for your model?
5. Can you provide more systematic testing of your system's ability to disambiguate "which then" and other ambiguous examples?
