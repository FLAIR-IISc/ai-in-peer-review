**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically for detecting causal vs. non-causal clause relations when not explicitly marked. The authors argue that Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are better suited for clause representation than Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). They use the AltLex dataset, which contains a larger amount of causal expressions than the Penn Discourse Tree Bank (PDTB). The proposed system uses GloVe embeddings, LSTM, and three dense layers with a tanh activation function, followed by a softmax layer. The results show that the proposed system outperforms the state-of-the-art by 0.5-1.5% F1 score.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel neural network architecture**: The authors propose a neural network architecture specifically designed for causality classification, which outperforms the state-of-the-art.
2. **Use of LSTM for clause representation**: The authors argue that LSTM is better suited for clause representation than CNN, and demonstrate its effectiveness in the proposed system.
3. **Evaluation on the AltLex dataset**: The authors evaluate their system on the AltLex dataset, which is a more comprehensive dataset for causality classification than the PDTB.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved performance**: The proposed system outperforms the state-of-the-art by 0.5-1.5% F1 score, demonstrating its effectiveness in causality classification.
2. **Novel approach**: The authors propose a novel neural network architecture specifically designed for causality classification, which is a significant contribution to the field.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their system on a comprehensive dataset, which provides a more accurate assessment of its performance.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited novelty**: The use of LSTM and neural networks for natural language processing tasks is not new, and the authors' contribution may be seen as incremental.
2. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The authors do not report statistical significance testing, which makes it difficult to determine the reliability of their results.
3. **Potential overfitting**: The authors select the best configuration on the test set rather than the development set, which may lead to overfitting.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the bootstrapping procedure used to enhance the AltLex corpus?
2. How do you address the issue of overfitting, given that the best configuration is selected on the test set?
3. Can you provide more information on the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files, and clarify the reposting rights for the dataset?
