**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically for disambiguating the causal meaning of relations between two events. The authors argue that neural representations can better capture diverse causal expressions than syntactic methods. The proposed architecture uses Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent neural networks to encode the sequential information of the input text. The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus and compare it to two baselines, including a state-of-the-art system. The results show that the proposed approach outperforms the baselines in terms of precision, recall, and F1 score.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural Network Architecture**: The authors propose a novel neural network architecture for causality classification, which uses LSTM to encode the sequential information of the input text.
2. **Evaluation on AltLex Corpus**: The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, which is a challenging dataset for causality classification.
3. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The authors compare their approach to a state-of-the-art system and show that it outperforms it in terms of precision, recall, and F1 score.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved Performance**: The proposed approach outperforms the baselines in terms of precision, recall, and F1 score, demonstrating its effectiveness for causality classification.
2. **Robustness to Ambiguity**: The authors show that their approach can handle ambiguous causal expressions, such as "which then", which is a challenging case for causality classification.
3. **Generalizability**: The proposed approach is based on neural networks, which can generalize well to unseen data, making it a promising approach for causality classification.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Foundation**: The authors do not provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, which makes it difficult to understand the underlying mechanisms.
2. **Unclear Hyperparameter Choices**: The authors do not provide a clear explanation for their hyperparameter choices, which makes it difficult to reproduce their results.
3. **Limited Evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on a single dataset, which may not be representative of all causality classification tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the theoretical foundation of your approach and how it relates to existing theories of causality?
2. How did you choose the hyperparameters for your neural network architecture, and what is the rationale behind these choices?
3. Have you evaluated your approach on other datasets or tasks, and if so, what were the results?
