This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, which involves identifying whether there is a causal relation between two events. The authors argue that the encoding of the meaning of the events is required for the disambiguation of their causal meaning, and that neural representations can better capture diverse causal expressions than syntactic methods.

The paper's main contributions are the proposal of a neural network architecture for causality classification, and the evaluation of this architecture on the AltLex corpus. The authors also release their TensorFlow code and models, which is a valuable contribution to the research community.

The experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support for the approach. The authors' system outperforms the state-of-the-art system of Hidey and McKeown (2016) on the "non bootstrapping" version of the corpus, and achieves comparable results on the "bootstrapping" version.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The introduction reads like a literature review, and duplicates the related work section. It fails to state a clear positive contribution or take a clear stance on causation. The assumed theory of causation is unclear, and the authors seem to invoke a Lewis-style counterfactual view without addressing known problems or committing to an explicit theory.

The discussion around the example at line 256 is confusing, and the authors' judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition are unexplained, leaving the causal claim unclear. The figure 1 caption is misleading, and the diagram is imprecise, with unclear symbols, missing layer semantics, and ambiguous LSTM connections.

The sentence about padding at line 480 is unclear, and it is not stated whether padding is a TensorFlow/training artifact or not. The relevance of padding to encoding causal meaning needs clarification. The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones is interesting, but the authors' interpretation is unjustified and contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information.

The hyperparameter choices and reported best settings are inconsistent or hard to interpret across the paper. For example, the lines 578 and 636 report different hyperparameters for the same experiment. This makes conclusions about model behavior unclear.

The claim that the system disambiguates "which then" better than prior work is based on a single example, and the reviewer recommends systematic testing with many constructed ambiguous examples.

Strengths:

1. The paper proposes a neural network architecture for causality classification, which is a valuable contribution to the research community.
2. The authors evaluate their architecture on the AltLex corpus, which is a challenging task.
3. The paper releases the TensorFlow code and models, which is a valuable contribution to the research community.

Weaknesses:

1. The introduction is unclear and fails to state a clear positive contribution or take a clear stance on causation.
2. The assumed theory of causation is unclear, and the authors seem to invoke a Lewis-style counterfactual view without addressing known problems or committing to an explicit theory.
3. The discussion around the example at line 256 is confusing, and the authors' judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition are unexplained, leaving the causal claim unclear.
4. The hyperparameter choices and reported best settings are inconsistent or hard to interpret across the paper.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you clarify the assumed theory of causation and how it relates to the proposed neural network architecture?
2. Can you explain the discussion around the example at line 256 and provide more context about the authors' judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition?
3. Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter choices and reported best settings, and explain why they are inconsistent or hard to interpret across the paper?
4. Can you provide more examples of how the system disambiguates ambiguous expressions, such as "which then", and explain why the system is better than prior work in this regard?
