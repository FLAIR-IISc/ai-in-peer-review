- Proposes a neural network architecture for causality classification.  
- Evaluates the architecture on the challenging AltLex corpus.  
- Releases TensorFlow code and trained models (valuable reproducibility contribution).  
- Experiments show modest but consistent empirical support for the approach.  
- System outperforms Hidey & McKeown (2016) on the non-bootstrapped AltLex split and is comparable on the bootstrapped split.  
- Introduction duplicates related work and fails to state a clear positive contribution or stance on causation.  
- The paper’s assumed theory of causation is unclear; it appears to invoke a Lewis-style counterfactual view without addressing known issues or committing to a theory.  
- Discussion of the example at line 256 is confusing; authors’ judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition are unexplained, leaving the causal claim unclear.  
- Figure 1 caption is misleading and the diagram is imprecise: unclear symbols, missing layer semantics, and ambiguous LSTM connections.  
- The sentence about padding (line 480) is unclear and it’s not stated whether padding is a TensorFlow/training artifact or relevant to causal encoding.  
- Claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones is interesting but the authors’ interpretation is unjustified and contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information.  
- Hyperparameter reporting is inconsistent and hard to interpret (e.g., conflicting settings at lines 578 and 636), undermining conclusions about model behavior.  
- Claim that the system disambiguates “which then” better than prior work is based on a single example; systematic testing with many constructed ambiguous examples is recommended.  
- Reviewer questions: clarify the assumed causation theory and its relation to the model.  
- Reviewer questions: explain the example at line 256 and the authors’ truth/falsity and event decomposition judgments.  
- Reviewer questions: provide full, consistent details on hyperparameter choices and reported best settings.  
- Reviewer questions: provide more systematic examples demonstrating disambiguation (e.g., multiple “which then” cases) and justification for superiority over prior work.
