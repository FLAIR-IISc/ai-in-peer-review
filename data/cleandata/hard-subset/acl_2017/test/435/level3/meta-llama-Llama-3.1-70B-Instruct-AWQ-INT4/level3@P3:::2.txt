This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, which is the disambiguation of the causal meaning of the relation between two events. The authors argue that the encoding of the meaning of a sentence is required for the disambiguation of its causal meaning. They use the AltLex dataset, which contains a larger amount of causal expressions than the Penn Discourse Tree Bank (PDTB).

The proposed system uses a combination of GloVe embeddings, Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, and three tanh dense layers with a softmax output. The authors claim that LSTMs are better suited for clause representation than Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) due to the lack of a particular syntactic structure in causation. The system is evaluated on the AltLex corpus and compared to two baselines: a simple baseline that classifies based on the most common class of each causal connective, and the system proposed by Hidey and McKeown (2016), which uses a Support Vector Machine (SVM) with a large set of features generated from the combination of features from the original parallel corpus and some lexical resources.

The results show that the proposed system outperforms the state-of-the-art system by 2.13% in terms of F1 score on the Causal class. The authors also provide a qualitative analysis of the system's performance, showing that it is able to correctly disambiguate the causal meaning of sentences with commonly causative verbs, but not always with a causal meaning.

The paper is well-written, and the problem description is clear. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the related work and the limitations of previous approaches. However, there are some concerns regarding the experimental setup and the evaluation of the system. Specifically, the best configuration appears to be selected on the test set rather than the development set, which may inflate the reported gains. Additionally, there is no statistical significance testing reported, and the dataset annotation quality may limit the score reliability.

Furthermore, the contribution of the paper may be considered incremental, as neural methods outperforming engineered features is expected. The authors could have included simpler baselines, such as a 1-layer LSTM, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their system. Moreover, it would be interesting to see an analysis explaining why the model favors precision over recall.

In terms of novelty, the paper's contribution is limited to the application of a neural network architecture to the task of causality classification. The idea of using LSTMs for clause representation is not new, and the use of GloVe embeddings and tanh dense layers is a common practice in natural language processing.

Despite these limitations, the paper provides a thorough analysis of the task of causality classification and the challenges associated with it. The authors' claim that the encoding of the meaning of a sentence is required for the disambiguation of its causal meaning is well-supported by the results. However, further work is needed to address the concerns regarding the experimental setup and the evaluation of the system.

**Summary of the paper:**

The paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, which is the disambiguation of the causal meaning of the relation between two events. The authors use the AltLex dataset and compare their system to two baselines. The results show that the proposed system outperforms the state-of-the-art system by 2.13% in terms of F1 score on the Causal class.

**Main contributions:**

1. Proposal of a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification.
2. Use of LSTMs for clause representation, which is better suited for the task than CNNs.
3. Evaluation of the system on the AltLex corpus and comparison to two baselines.

**Strengths:**

1. Well-written paper with a clear problem description.
2. Thorough analysis of the related work and the limitations of previous approaches.
3. Good performance of the proposed system on the Causal class.

**Weaknesses:**

1. Experimental setup concerns, such as the selection of the best configuration on the test set rather than the development set.
2. Lack of statistical significance testing and dataset annotation quality concerns.
3. Incremental contribution, as neural methods outperforming engineered features is expected.
4. Limited novelty, as the idea of using LSTMs for clause representation is not new.

**Questions to authors:**

1. How did you select the best configuration of your system, and why did you not use the development set for this purpose?
2. Can you provide more details on the dataset annotation quality and how it may affect the score reliability?
3. How do you plan to address the concerns regarding the experimental setup and the evaluation of the system in future work?
