- Proposes a neural network architecture for causality classification, arguing context encoding is required and claiming state-of-the-art results on the AltLex corpus.
- Main contributions: a new neural architecture, LSTM-based models to capture diverse causal expressions, and evaluation on AltLex showing modest consistent gains.
- Strength: tackles the challenging task of causality classification with neural methods.
- Strength: provides TensorFlow code and models for reproducibility.
- Strength: evaluates on a relevant corpus (AltLex).
- Weakness: Introduction reads like a literature review and fails to state a clear positive contribution or explicit stance on causation.
- Weakness: Assumed theory of causation is unclear; appears to invoke a Lewis-style counterfactual view without acknowledging its problems or committing to a theory.
- Weakness: Discussion of the example at line 256 is confusing; truth/falsity judgments and event decomposition are unexplained.
- Weakness: Figure 1 caption and diagram are misleading and imprecise, with unclear symbols, missing layer semantics, and ambiguous LSTM connections.
- Weakness: Claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones is interesting but interpretation is unjustified and contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information.
- Weakness: Hyperparameter choices and reported best settings are inconsistent or hard to interpret across the paper.
- Weakness: Claim that the system disambiguates "which then" better than prior work relies on a single example and lacks systematic evidence.
- Requested clarification: specify the assumed theory of causation and how it informs the approach.
- Requested clarification: address known problems with the Lewis-style counterfactual view if that is the assumed theory.
- Requested clarification: provide detailed explanation for the example at line 256, including truth/falsity and event decomposition judgments.
- Requested revision: make Figure 1 precise and clear with explicit layer semantics and connections.
- Requested information: provide details on hyperparameter choices, reported best settings, and how they were determined.
- Requested evidence: conduct systematic testing with many constructed ambiguous examples to support the "which then" disambiguation claim.
