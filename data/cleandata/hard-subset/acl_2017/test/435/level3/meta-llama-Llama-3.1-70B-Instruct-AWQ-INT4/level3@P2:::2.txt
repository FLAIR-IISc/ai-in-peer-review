**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically for disambiguating causal vs. non-causal clause relations when not explicitly marked. The authors argue that encoding the meaning of the input sentences is required for this task and propose using a neural network with two inputs, one for the first event and the other for the lexical marker and the second event. The system uses GloVe embeddings, LSTM, and three tanh dense layers with a softmax output. The authors evaluate their system on the AltLex dataset and report improvements over the state-of-the-art system by Hidey and McKeown.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural Network Architecture**: The authors propose a novel neural network architecture for causality classification, which uses two inputs to encode the meaning of the input sentences.
2. **Evaluation on AltLex Dataset**: The authors evaluate their system on the AltLex dataset, which is a challenging dataset for causality classification.
3. **Qualitative and Quantitative Analyses**: The authors provide both qualitative and quantitative analyses of their system's performance, including examples where the model generalizes correctly.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-written Paper**: The paper is well-written, and the problem description is clear.
2. **Clear Evaluation**: The authors provide a clear evaluation of their system, including comparisons with the state-of-the-art system.
3. **Qualitative Analyses**: The authors provide qualitative analyses of their system's performance, including examples where the model generalizes correctly.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Clarity on Task Definition**: The paper could benefit from a clearer definition of the task and how it differs from implicit connective recognition.
2. **Unclear Bootstrapping Procedure**: The paper mentions bootstrapping that extends the corpus by ~15% but does not explain the bootstrapping procedure.
3. **Experimental Concerns**: The best configuration appears to be selected on the test set rather than the dev set, potentially inflating reported gains.
4. **No Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper does not report statistical significance testing, which may limit the reliability of the scores.
5. **Novelty Concerns**: The contribution of the paper may be incremental, as neural methods outperforming engineered features is expected.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify how this task differs from implicit connective recognition and why prior methods cannot be applied?
2. Can you explain the bootstrapping procedure used to extend the corpus?
3. Can you provide statistical significance testing to support the reported gains?
4. Can you include simpler baselines (e.g., 1-layer LSTM) and analyses explaining why the model favors precision over recall?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written, and the problem description is clear. However, there are some concerns regarding the clarity of the task definition, the bootstrapping procedure, and the experimental setup. The authors should address these concerns to strengthen the paper. Additionally, the authors may want to consider including simpler baselines and analyses to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their system.
