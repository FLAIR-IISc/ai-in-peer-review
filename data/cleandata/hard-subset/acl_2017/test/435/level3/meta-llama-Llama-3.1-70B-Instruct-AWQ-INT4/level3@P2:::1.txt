This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically focusing on the disambiguation of causal meaning in sentences. The authors argue that the encoding of the context is required for the disambiguation of causality and that their proposed system outperforms the state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) the proposal of a neural network architecture for causality classification, (2) the use of LSTM-based models to capture diverse causal expressions, and (3) the evaluation of the system on the AltLex corpus, showing modest but consistent support for the approach.

The strengths of this submission are: (1) the authors' attempt to tackle the challenging task of causality classification using neural networks, (2) the provision of TensorFlow code and models, allowing for reproducibility and further research, and (3) the evaluation of the system on a corpus that is relevant to the task.

However, there are several weaknesses in this submission. Firstly, the introduction reads like a literature review and fails to state a clear positive contribution or take a clear stance on causation. The assumed theory of causation is unclear, and the authors seem to invoke a Lewis-style counterfactual view without addressing known problems or committing to an explicit theory. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to understand the authors' approach and evaluate its contributions.

Furthermore, the discussion around the example at line 256 is confusing, and the authors' judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition are unexplained, leaving the causal claim unclear. Additionally, the figure 1 caption is misleading, and the diagram is imprecise, with unclear symbols, missing layer semantics, and ambiguous LSTM connections.

The authors' claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones is interesting, but their interpretation is unjustified and contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information. Moreover, the hyperparameter choices and reported best settings are inconsistent or hard to interpret across the paper, making conclusions about model behavior unclear.

Finally, the claim that the system disambiguates "which then" better than prior work is based on a single example, and the reviewer recommends systematic testing with many constructed ambiguous examples to support this claim.

Questions to Authors:

1. Can you clarify your assumed theory of causation and how it relates to your approach?
2. How do you address the known problems with the Lewis-style counterfactual view of causation?
3. Can you provide more details about the example at line 256 and explain your judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition?
4. Can you revise figure 1 to make it more precise and clear?
5. Can you provide more information about the hyperparameter choices and reported best settings, and how they were determined?
6. Can you provide systematic testing with many constructed ambiguous examples to support the claim that the system disambiguates "which then" better than prior work?
