- Paper develops LSTM-based models to classify whether connectives indicate intended causal relations, arguing neural representations better capture diverse causal expressions than syntactic methods.
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support for the approach; authors release TensorFlow code/models.
- Introduction reads like a literature review, duplicates Related Work, and fails to state a clear positive contribution or take a clear stance on causation.
- The assumed theory of causation is unclear: authors seem to invoke a Lewis-style counterfactual view without addressing known problems or committing to an explicit theory.
- The discussion around the example at line 256 is confusing—authors’ judgments about truth/falsity and event decomposition are unexplained, leaving the causal claim unclear.
- Figure 1 caption is misleading (it shows only Pair_LSTM) and the diagram is imprecise—unclear symbols, missing layer semantics, and ambiguous LSTM connections.
- Line 480’s sentence about padding is unclear; if padding is a TensorFlow/training artifact that should be stated, and its relevance to encoding causal meaning needs clarification.
- Claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential ones is interesting but the authors’ interpretation (line 587) is unjustified and contradicts expectations about shared sub-sentential information.
- Hyperparameter choices and reported best settings are inconsistent or hard to interpret across the paper (e.g., lines 578 vs 636), making conclusions about model behavior unclear.
- The claim that the system disambiguates “which then” better than prior work is based on a single example; the reviewer recommends systematic testing with many constructed ambiguous examples.
