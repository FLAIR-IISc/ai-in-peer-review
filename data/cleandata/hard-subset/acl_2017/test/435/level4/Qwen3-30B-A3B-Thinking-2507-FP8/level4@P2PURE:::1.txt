

This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether an intended causal relation exists. The core premise is that causal expression exhibits extreme diversity, rendering syntactic approaches inadequate, and that neural models' abstract representations offer a more suitable foundation for such classification decisions.

Experiments utilize the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. Results provide modest but consistent support for the core approach, alongside initial insights into effective model implementation. The paper includes TensorFlow-based model code for reproducibility.

Key concerns and questions:

*   The introduction functions more as a literature review than a concise overview of the paper's contributions, creating redundancy with the subsequent related work section. While non-standard introductions are permissible, this one fails to function effectively: despite reviewing numerous ideas, it avoids taking a clear stance on the nature of causation or its expression, instead focusing solely on the negative claim that causation is not reducible to syntax. The positive contribution remains vague until the final paragraph of the introduction.
*   The paper lacks clarity on the underlying theory of causation. The authors appear to default to a counterfactual view akin to David Lewis's, where causation is a modal sufficiency claim with additional counterfactual conditions (see lines 238+). That specific arrow requires a highly specialized implication type, and Lewis's theory faces well-documented challenges (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). While other parts of the paper state the authors do not endorse the counterfactual view, the alternative theory is unspecified. It cannot merely be the temporal constraint mentioned on page 3.
*   The commentary on line 256 is unclear. The authors appear to claim the sentence is false. If true, a causal link between the argument and breakage should exist. Unresolved issues regarding event decomposition and their impact on causal theories are not addressed here, leaving the critique confusing.
*   Figure 1's caption is misleading, as the diagram depicts only the "Pair_LSTM" model variant. More critically, the diagram lacks precision. While omitting standard model details from prose is acceptable, diagrams must have clear, consistent semantics. What do the unlabeled circles between input and "LSTM" boxes represent? The prose mentions a lookup layer, GloVe layer, and then... what? How many representation layers exist? The diagram precisely shows pooling tanh layers pre-softmax but omits critical structural details. It is also unclear what the "LSTM" boxes represent; the prose suggests the leftmost/final representation connects directly to higher layers. This connection should be explicitly depicted.
*   The line 480 sentence is confusing. The discussed models do not inherently require padding; this appears to be a TensorFlow/efficiency constraint. If so, explicitly state this. The final clause's connection to "the most convenient way to encode causal meaning" is unclear—convenience seems unrelated to causal meaning encoding, and the relevance to the stated question is not evident.
*   The finding that two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") outperform a single LSTM with sequential input is unexpected. This parallels unresolved debates in natural language entailment (independent vs. sequential representation of premise/hypothesis), suggesting further investigation is needed for causal relations. Therefore, the line 587 statement—"This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers"—cannot be endorsed. It is counterintuitive for sentence subparts potentially sharing significant information to benefit from independent encoding.
*   Hyperparameter choices yielding optimal performance across tasks are difficult to interpret (e.g., compare lines 578 and 636). Should this be analyzed, or attributed to model-data interaction unpredictability?
*   Section 4.3 claims the system "correctly disambiguates" the causal meaning of 'which then' (unlike Hidley and McKeown's system). A single example (Table 8) does not suffice. To substantiate this claim, construct a diverse set of ambiguous examples and evaluate the system's accuracy across them. This would address whether the result was coincidental.
