1. Proposes a neural architecture with two independent LSTMs for causal meaning disambiguation, outperforming prior methods on the AltLex corpus.  
2. Provides empirical evidence that contextual encoding is necessary to disambiguate ambiguous causal lexical markers.  
3. Presents a detailed analysis showing the model succeeds on cases where previous methods fail, e.g., non-causal uses of causative verbs.  
4. Addresses a notable gap in causality research by showing syntactic/lexical approaches are insufficient and AltLex is a more comprehensive resource.  
5. Delivers a well-executed experimental evaluation comparing against strong baselines and demonstrating the Pair LSTM's superiority.  
6. Releases code and model details to enhance reproducibility and demonstrates handling of ambiguous lexical cases.  
7. Shows a neural approach can generalize beyond the coverage limits of lexical-resource-based methods.  
8. The paper’s theoretical foundation is underdeveloped and contradictory regarding the adoption/rejection of the counterfactual view of causation.  
9. The introduction is poorly structured and reads like a literature review rather than clearly stating the paper’s contributions early.  
10. The analysis of ambiguous cases is anecdotal (single example) and needs a more systematic evaluation across the dataset.  
11. The interpretation that independent LSTMs outperforming connected LSTMs implies no relation between event meanings is inconsistent and unjustified.  
12. Figure 1 is misleading and imprecise, containing unexplained elements and unclear depiction of the LSTM components.  
13. Request clarification of which theoretical framework of causation the authors adopt and how it informs the approach.  
14. Ask for a detailed explanation of why independent encoding (Pair LSTM) works better than connected encoding despite intuition of shared semantics.  
15. Request a systematic analysis (e.g., precision/recall) of how the model handles ambiguous markers across the dataset, not just single examples.  
16. Ask how class-distribution differences in the bootstrapped corpus affected performance and what this implies about robustness to distribution shift.
