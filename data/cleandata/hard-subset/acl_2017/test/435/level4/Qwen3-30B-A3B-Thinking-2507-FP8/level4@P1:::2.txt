

**Summary:**  
This paper proposes a neural network approach for causal relation disambiguation, focusing on cases where causal relations are not explicitly marked by lexical markers (e.g., "because"). The authors argue that context encoding is necessary for proper causal meaning disambiguation and propose an LSTM-based architecture that outperforms the previous state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016).

**Main Contributions:**  
1. A neural network architecture for causal meaning disambiguation that encodes contextual information rather than relying on syntactic or lexical features.  
2. Analysis of the AltLex corpus showing that causal expressions are not limited to fixed lexical constructions, supporting the need for context-aware methods.  
3. Demonstration that neural approaches can outperform feature-based systems for this task, with improvements of up to 2.13% F1 on the Causal class.

**Strengths:**  
1. The paper provides a clear and well-motivated problem statement, correctly identifying limitations of previous approaches that rely on fixed syntactic constructions or lexical resources. The argument against syntax-based causality models is particularly strong and well-supported by references to Neeleman & van de Koot (2012).  
2. The authors provide a thorough qualitative analysis of the system's performance, demonstrating how their model correctly handles ambiguous cases (e.g., "break" being used in non-causal contexts). This analysis is more valuable than simply reporting numerical improvements.  
3. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a particularly strong description of the task definition. The authors also make their code and data publicly available, significantly enhancing reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. The paper claims a 2.13% F1 improvement over Hidey & McKeown (2016), but it's unclear how the best configuration was selected. If the authors selected the best configuration on the test set (rather than a development set), this constitutes a data leakage issue that undermines the reported performance.  
2. The paper lacks statistical significance testing for the performance improvements. Given the size of the AltLex corpus, it would be straightforward to compute p-values for the reported gains, which would strengthen the claims.  
3. The "bootstrapping" process for expanding the AltLex corpus is not clearly explained. The paper mentions a "non bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" version of the corpus but doesn't clarify what bootstrapping entails or how it differs from the original corpus construction.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. How does this task differ from implicit connective recognition, and why can't existing implicit connective recognition methods be directly applied to this problem?  
2. What is the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files provided with the dataset? The paper mentions binary classification, so the purpose of this coding is unclear.  
3. What exactly is the "bootstrapping" process that expands the corpus by about 15%? The paper describes corpus construction in Section 3.2 but doesn't clarify the bootstrapping step.

**Additional Comments:**  
The paper does not address whether the authors have permission to repost the AltLex corpus. The authors should clarify this in the paper.  

The claim that neural methods "outperform the state-of-the-art" is somewhat misleading, as the comparison is only with one specific baseline (Hidey & McKeown's SVM system), and it's not clear if this represents the current state of the art. The authors should clarify whether other approaches might be more competitive.  

The modest 2.13% F1 improvement should be contextualized. The authors should discuss whether this level of improvement is practically significant for real-world applications, as the paper currently presents it as a major advancement without sufficient justification for its importance.
