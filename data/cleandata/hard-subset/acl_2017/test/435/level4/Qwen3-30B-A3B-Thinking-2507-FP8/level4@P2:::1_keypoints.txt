1. Proposes an LSTM-based neural approach to disambiguate causal meaning of lexical markers using contextual encoding.  
2. Main contribution: demonstrates that neural context encoding is necessary over syntactic constructions or lexical resources for causal disambiguation.  
3. Secondary contribution: evaluation on the AltLex corpus showing consistent improvements over previous methods.  
4. Third contribution: detailed empirical validation across architectures and optimization strategies.  
5. Strength: moves beyond syntax-based approaches and shows superior LSTM performance for causality classification.  
6. Strength: thorough AltLex evaluation with careful analysis of architectures, padding strategies, optimizers, and clear baseline comparisons and metrics.  
7. Strength: analysis of ambiguous connectives (e.g., "which then") offers illustrative examples where the model outperforms prior methods.  
8. Weakness: unclear and inconsistent theoretical foundation regarding the counterfactual view of causality.  
9. Weakness: poorly structured introduction that repeats related work and fails to clearly state the paper’s main contributions.  
10. Weakness: insufficient justification for preferring independent encoding of events over a connected architecture.  
11. Weakness: evaluation of ambiguous connectives relies on a single example, offering insufficient evidence for general claims.  
12. Question: clarify which theory of causation the paper assumes and how it aligns with the task definition.  
13. Question: explain how this work advances beyond prior neural/contextual approaches (e.g., Do et al., Riaz and Girju).  
14. Question: address the generalizability of the claim that context encoding is required beyond the AltLex corpus.  
15. Question: provide a more comprehensive analysis of the "which then" connective across multiple training and test examples.
