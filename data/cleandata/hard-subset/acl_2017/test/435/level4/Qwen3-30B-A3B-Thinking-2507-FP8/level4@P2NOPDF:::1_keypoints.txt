- Proposes an LSTM-based model to classify whether connectives express intended causal relations; experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent results and code is provided.
- Introduction reads like a literature review, fails to state a positive theory of causation, and gives an ambiguous/unstated stance on Lewis-style counterfactuals.
- The example "The argument broke the screen" is confusing and the paper does not resolve event-division issues affecting causal interpretation.
- Figure 1 is miscaptioned and lacks clear architectural detail (unexplained nodes, embedding/lookup layers, number of representation layers, and LSTM-to-layer connections).
- The claim that models “require padding” is misleading—padding seems an implementation detail, not an inherent model property, and its link to encoding causal meaning is unexplained.
- The assertion that two independent LSTMs outperform a single sequential LSTM is overstated and inadequately analyzed; the conclusion that arguments should be encoded independently is not justified.
- Optimal hyperparameters vary inconsistently across tasks, but the paper does not analyze whether this reflects model-data interactions or random variation.
- The claim that the system correctly disambiguates "which then" is based on a single example and requires broader evaluation to be substantiated.
- Overall: promising neural approach but weakened by theoretical ambiguity, unclear model description, unsupported design claims, and insufficient evidence for key assertions.
