

This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying whether connectives express intended causal relations, arguing that causal expression is too diverse for syntactic approaches and that neural models' abstract representations offer a more suitable foundation. Experiments use the AltLex corpus (Hidley & McKeown), yielding modest but consistent support for the core approach and initial insights into model design. The authors provide TensorFlow implementations.

Several concerns require clarification:

1.  **Introduction Structure:** The introduction reads more like a literature review than a concise overview of the paper's contributions. This creates redundancy with the subsequent related work section. Crucially, it fails to establish a positive theoretical stance on causation or its expression, instead only stating what causation *is not* (reducible to syntax). The final paragraph vaguely hints at the contribution without specifying it.

2.  **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper lacks clarity on the assumed theory of causation. While line 238 references a counterfactual framework (e.g., Lewis), the specific modal implication ("arrow") required for this to function is problematic, and Lewis's theory faces well-documented issues (Copley & Wolff, 2014). The paper later states it does not endorse this view, yet offers no alternative. The temporal constraint mentioned on page 3 is insufficient; a clear theoretical basis is essential.

3.  **Example Interpretation (Line 256):** The authors claim the sentence is false, implying no causal link between the argument and breakage. However, if the sentence is true, a causal link *should* exist. The paper doesn't address the unresolved challenges in event segmentation for causal theories, leaving this interpretation confusing.

4.  **Figure 1 Ambiguity:** The caption misrepresents the diagram as depicting the full model, when it shows only the "Pair_LSTM" variant. More critically, the diagram is imprecise. Unlabeled circles between input and "LSTM" boxes lack semantic meaning. The prose describes a look-up layer, GloVe layer, and "..."—it's unclear how many representation layers exist. While some model details can be omitted from text, the diagram must have clear semantics. The "LSTM" boxes also lack clarity: is the final representation the only one connected to higher layers? This connection should be explicitly shown.

5.  **TensorFlow Padding Comment (Line 480):** The claim that models "require padding" is misleading. Padding is a TensorFlow/efficiency requirement, not an inherent model need. Please clarify this. The final clause ("how this relates to 'the most convenient way to encode causal meaning'") is unclear; convenience seems unrelated to causal meaning encoding.

6.  **Independent LSTMs vs. Sequential (Line 587):** The finding that two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") outperform a sequential model (first feeding into second) is surprising. This mirrors unresolved debates in natural language entailment (independent vs. sequential representation). The authors' conclusion—that the assumption about input event meaning relations "does not hold," necessitating independent encoding—lacks justification. Given the likely information overlap between subparts of a sentence, this claim requires stronger support than the current phrasing provides.

7.  **Hyperparameter Choices:** The best-performing hyperparameters vary significantly across tasks (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636). This discrepancy needs explanation rather than being dismissed as "unpredictable." The paper should address whether this reflects model sensitivity or data-specific quirks.

8.  **'Which Then' Claim (Section 4.3):** The claim that the system "correctly disambiguates the causal meaning of 'which then' where Hidey & McKeown fail" (Table 8) is overstated. One example is insufficient evidence. To substantiate this, the authors should test the system on a broader set of ambiguous examples to demonstrate consistent, reliable performance beyond chance.

Overall, the core idea is promising, and the experiments provide useful initial validation. However, the paper requires significant clarification on its theoretical grounding, model details, and interpretation of results to strengthen its contribution. Addressing these points is crucial before publication.
