

This paper introduces a neural network approach for detecting causal relations between clauses, leveraging deep learning techniques (though the networks are not deeply structured, as is common in such studies). The work addresses the challenge of identifying causal versus non-causal relations when no explicit discourse connective is present, utilizing the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). The authors argue that LSTMs better capture clause features than convolutional networks and propose a classification model using GloVe embeddings as input to an LSTM layer, followed by three tanh-activated dense layers and a softmax decision layer.  

The optimal model configuration achieves a 0.5–1.5% F1 improvement over Hidey & McKeown’s (2016) SVM baseline. The paper demonstrates strong generalization through examples where indicator words consistently causal in training are correctly classified as non-causal in testing, and provides both qualitative and quantitative analysis—both commendable aspects. The paper is well-written with a particularly clear problem description.  

However, a clarification is needed on the distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition, including why prior implicit connective recognition methods are unsuitable here. The authors’ code and dataset (including older data) are publicly available, which is highly appreciated. The meaning of the 0–1–2 coding in the TSV files requires explanation, as the paper describes a binary classification task. Additionally, the authors’ rights to repost the AltLex dataset (originally from Hidey & McKeown) should be addressed. A key clarification needed is the definition and purpose of "bootstrapping," which expands the corpus by ~15% but is not detailed in the paper despite the corpus construction being explained.  

While neural networks are a natural fit for this task, the claimed improvements are not fully substantiated. The best configuration (among 4–7 options) appears to be selected using the test data, rather than the devset, which undermines the comparison. Statistical significance testing for the F1 gain should be included given the dataset size, and the reliability of the gold-standard annotation (from English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia) should be acknowledged, as imperfections may necessitate cautious interpretation of results. Furthermore, neural networks routinely outperform hand-engineered features in binary classification, so the results primarily confirm a known trend. Including simpler baselines (e.g., a 1-layer LSTM) and analyzing why the model favors precision over recall would strengthen the contribution.
