

Summary and main contributions: This paper proposes an LSTM-based neural network architecture for disambiguaging causal lexical markers in context, addressing the limitation that causal relations cannot be adequately captured by syntactic constructions alone. The primary contribution is a novel neural model that encodes the context of causal relations through two independent LSTM networks, which outperforms previous approaches on the AltLex corpus. The secondary contribution is demonstrating that contextual encoding is necessary for accurate causal meaning disambiguation, particularly for ambiguous lexical markers like "since" and "after." The tertiary contribution is providing the TensorFlow-based implementation of the model for reproducibility.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling argument that causal relations cannot be captured by syntactic constructions alone, which is well-supported by the analysis of linguistic examples and prior work. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art baseline (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) across multiple metrics (F1 by 2.13% and Accuracy by 7.72% on the non-bootstrapping version), providing strong empirical evidence for the proposed approach. The paper's implementation is fully reproducible, including the TensorFlow code, which is a significant practical contribution to the field.

Weaknesses: The introduction fails to establish a clear theoretical foundation for causation, instead presenting a literature review without articulating the authors' specific theoretical stance. This leaves readers confused about the paper's underlying assumptions regarding causal relations, particularly given the authors' rejection of the counterfactual constraint while not clearly proposing an alternative. The paper makes a strong claim about the superiority of independent LSTM encoding over connected LSTM encoding (Stated Pair LSTM) based on a single observation without sufficient exploration of why this might be the case, which contradicts intuition about how causal relations might share information between events. The analysis of ambiguous connectives like "which then" is based on a single example (Table 8), which is insufficient to support the claim of successful disambiguation, especially when the baseline system also failed on that example.

Questions to Authors: The paper claims that the model successfully disambiguates "which then" as causal (Table 8), but this is based on a single example. Please provide a broader analysis of how the model handles this ambiguous connective across multiple examples to substantiate this claim. The paper states that the counterfactual constraint is not used in the definition of causation, but it's unclear what alternative theoretical framework is assumed. Please clarify the authors' theoretical position on causation and how it differs from Lewis's counterfactual theory. The diagram in Figure 1 depicts both "Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM" variants but is labeled only for "Pair LSTM," creating confusion about the model architecture. Please clarify and correct the figure caption and diagram to accurately represent the model variants.
