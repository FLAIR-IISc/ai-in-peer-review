- Proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying causal connectives and reports modest but consistent gains on the AltLex corpus; TensorFlow code provided.
- Introduction reads like a literature review and fails to state a clear theoretical stance on what causation is.
- Paper rejects a Lewis-style counterfactual account but the model’s assumptions implicitly rely on counterfactual reasoning, creating a theoretical contradiction.
- The temporal constraint offered is insufficient to ground the model’s causal classification.
- Example analysis (e.g., "The argument broke the phone") is confusing and ignores event segmentation issues that affect causal judgments.
- Figure 1 misrepresents the full architecture, omits the embedding/lookup layer, contains unexplained diagram elements, and lacks clear connections from LSTM to pooling.
- The padding discussion wrongly frames padding as encoding causal meaning instead of a technical implementation detail.
- Choosing two independent LSTMs is presented as theoretical evidence without justification or literature support; the claim conflates model design with causal theory.
- Hyperparameter settings are inconsistently reported across sections and tasks, with no explanation of sensitivity or search procedure.
- The claim that the model disambiguates "which then" causality is supported by a single example and lacks broader empirical validation.
- Core idea—that neural models may outperform syntactic approaches—is plausible but underdeveloped due to shaky theory, imprecise model description, and limited evidence.
- Recommended revisions: clarify and reconcile the causal theory, fix and detail Figure 1, justify the independent-LSTM design, expand evaluation on ambiguous cases, and clarify technical details (padding, hyperparameters).
- Overall verdict: promising but currently lacks theoretical rigor and sufficient empirical support for acceptance at ACL.
