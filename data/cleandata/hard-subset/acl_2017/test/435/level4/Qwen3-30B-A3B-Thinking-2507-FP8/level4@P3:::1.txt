

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically focusing on disambiguating causal lexical markers based on contextual information. The authors argue that causality cannot be adequately captured through syntactic constructions or lexical resources alone, and that neural models capable of encoding contextual meaning are better suited for this task. Their main contributions are:

1. A neural architecture using two independent LSTMs for causal meaning disambiguation, which outperforms previous methods on the AltLex corpus.
2. Empirical evidence demonstrating that contextual encoding is necessary for accurate causal disambiguation, particularly for ambiguous lexical markers.
3. A thorough analysis showing the model's ability to correctly handle cases where previous methods fail, especially with non-causal uses of causative verbs.

**Strengths**

1. The paper addresses a significant gap in causality research by demonstrating that causality classification cannot be adequately handled through syntactic or lexical approaches alone. The AltLex corpus provides a more comprehensive resource for causal expressions than previous datasets, and the authors' neural approach shows consistent improvements over baselines on this challenging dataset.

2. The experimental evaluation is well-executed and thoroughly documented. The authors compare their model against two strong baselines (including the previous state-of-the-art method by Hidey and McKeown) and provide comprehensive results across multiple configurations. Their finding that the "Pair LSTM" architecture (with two independent LSTMs) outperforms the "State Pair LSTM" architecture (with connected LSTMs) is a meaningful contribution to the field.

3. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing code and model details, enhancing reproducibility. The authors also clearly demonstrate their model's ability to handle ambiguous cases (e.g., "break" as a causative verb in non-causal contexts), which previous methods failed to capture.

4. The paper effectively addresses the limitation of previous work that relied on lexical resources (which restrict the system to the coverage of those resources), showing that a neural approach can generalize better across diverse causal expressions.

**Weaknesses**

1. The theoretical foundation of the paper is underdeveloped and contradictory. The paper vacillates between endorsing a counterfactual view of causation (lines 238-240) and rejecting it (lines 251-255), without clearly articulating what theory of causation the authors are adopting. This ambiguity undermines the paper's central claim about the nature of causal relations.

2. The introduction is poorly structured and reads more like a literature review than a concise overview of the paper's contributions. The authors fail to clearly state their positive contribution early in the paper, instead focusing on what they reject (syntactic approaches) without adequately explaining what they propose in its place.

3. The analysis of the model's performance on ambiguous cases (e.g., "which then" in Table 8) is based on a single example, which is insufficient to support the claim that their model "correctly disambiguates its causal meaning." The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more systematic analysis of ambiguous cases across the dataset.

4. The model description contains technical inconsistencies. The paper claims that the "Pair LSTM" architecture (with two independent LSTMs) outperforms the "State Pair LSTM" (with connected LSTMs), but then incorrectly states that this "means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold." This is a surprising conclusion that lacks sufficient justification and contradicts the intuition that causal events share semantic information.

5. Figure 1 is misleading and imprecise, failing to clearly depict the model architecture. The diagram includes unexplained elements (empty circles between input and LSTM boxes) and does not clarify what the "LSTM" boxes represent, making it difficult to understand the proposed architecture.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Could you clarify the theoretical framework of causation you're adopting? The paper appears to reject the counterfactual view (lines 251-255) but then uses counterfactual reasoning in the definition of causation (lines 238-240). Please explain which theory of causation you're using and how it informs your approach.

2. The paper claims that the "Pair LSTM" architecture outperforms the "State Pair LSTM" because "our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold." However, this seems counterintuitive given that causal events should share semantic information. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why independent encoding works better than connected encoding?

3. The analysis of ambiguous cases (e.g., "which then") is based on a single example. Could you provide a more systematic analysis of how your model handles ambiguous cases across the dataset, including precision and recall for these specific cases?

4. The paper mentions that the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus has instances where the class distribution differs between training and test sets. How did this impact the model's performance, and what does this suggest about the robustness of the model to distribution shifts?
