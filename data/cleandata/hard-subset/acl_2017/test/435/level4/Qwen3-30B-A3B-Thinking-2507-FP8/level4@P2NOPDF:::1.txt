

This paper proposes an LSTM-based model to classify whether connectives express intended causal relations, arguing that the diversity of causal expression necessitates abstract neural representations over syntactic approaches. Experiments on the AltLex corpus (Hidley & McKeown) yield modest but consistent results supporting the core idea, with the authors providing TensorFlow code for replication.  

While the technical approach is sound, several critical issues undermine the paper's contribution and clarity:  

1.  **Introduction and Theoretical Foundation**: The introduction reads more like a literature review than a concise overview of the paper's contribution, creating redundancy with the subsequent related work section. Crucially, it fails to articulate a positive theoretical stance on causation, instead only stating what causation *is not* (not syntactically reducible). The paper ambiguously references a counterfactual view (Lewis-style, line 238), yet later disavows it. Without clarifying the assumed theory of causation (beyond the temporal constraint on p.3), the work lacks a coherent theoretical grounding. The Lewis counterfactual framework faces well-documented challenges (Copley & Wolff, 2014), and the paper does not address why this framework is adopted or rejected.  

2.  **Example Analysis (Line 256)**: The authors claim the sentence "The argument broke the screen" is false due to lack of causal link. This is unclear; if the sentence is true, a causal link *should* exist. The paper fails to resolve the underlying event-division issues impacting causal analysis, leaving the example's interpretation confusing.  

3.  **Figure 1 and Model Architecture**: The caption misrepresents the diagram as depicting the full model, when it only shows "Pair_LSTM." More critically, the diagram lacks semantic precision: the empty circles between input and LSTM boxes are unexplained, and it’s ambiguous whether the model includes a lookup layer, GloVe layer, and *how many* representation layers exist. The prose mentions "pooling tanh layers pre-softmax" but omits key architectural details. The connection between the LSTM output and subsequent layers is also unclear; this should be explicitly shown.  

4.  **Padding Requirement (Line 480)**: The claim that models "require padding" is misleading. Padding is likely a TensorFlow/implementation necessity, not a model property. The paper should clarify this and explain why this requirement relates to "the most convenient way to encode causal meaning," as the connection is not evident.  

5.  **Independent vs. Sequential LSTMs (Lines 587+)**: The paper asserts that using two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") outperforms a single LSTM with sequential input, concluding this invalidates their assumption about input event meaning relations. This is surprising, as subparts of a sentence often share information. The claim that "it is better to encode each argument independently" is overstated; this remains an open question (as in natural language entailment literature), not a settled finding requiring such a definitive conclusion.  

6.  **Hyperparameter Inconsistency**: Performance metrics (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636) show inconsistent optimal hyperparameters across tasks. The paper does not discuss whether this reflects model-data interactions or random variation, weakening the interpretation of results.  

7.  **'Which Then' Claim (Section 4.3)**: The claim that the system "correctly disambiguates the causal meaning of 'which then'" (while Hidey & McKeown’s system does not) is based on a single example (Table 8). This is insufficient evidence. To substantiate this claim, the authors should test the system on a broader set of ambiguous examples to demonstrate consistent performance, rather than relying on one instance.  

**Summary**: The paper presents a reasonable neural approach to a challenging problem, with promising initial results. However, significant theoretical ambiguities, unclear explanations of the model architecture, unsupported claims about model design choices, and insufficient evidence for key assertions prevent it from making a compelling contribution. Addressing these points—particularly clarifying the causal theory, refining the model description, justifying the independent LSTM choice, and validating the 'which then' claim with broader testing—would substantially strengthen the work. As presented, the theoretical foundation is underdeveloped, and several key claims lack adequate support.
