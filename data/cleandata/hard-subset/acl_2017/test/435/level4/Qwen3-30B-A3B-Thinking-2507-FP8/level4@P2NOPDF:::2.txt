

This paper proposes a neural network approach for detecting causal relations between clauses, particularly when the relation is not explicitly marked by discourse connectives (e.g., distinguishing 'because' from ambiguous indicators like 'and'). Leveraging the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), the authors argue that LSTMs better capture clause features than convolutional networks. Their architecture uses GloVe embeddings fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three tanh-connected dense layers and a softmax decision layer. The best configuration achieves a 0.5–1.5% F1 improvement over Hidey & McKeown’s (2016) SVM baseline. The authors provide qualitative analysis showing the model correctly generalizes to cases where training data indicated causal indicators (e.g., 'since') but test data requires non-causal labeling, which is commendable.

The paper is well-written with a clear problem statement. However, a critical clarification is needed: the distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition (ICR). Previous ICR methods (e.g., using syntactic patterns) are not directly applicable here, as this work focuses solely on causal relations without connective markers, not on identifying *which* connective (e.g., 'because' vs. 'thus') is implied. The authors should explicitly address why ICR techniques cannot be adapted to this specific causal binary classification.

The authors deserve credit for sharing code and the AltLex dataset, which aids reproducibility. However, two issues require resolution. First, the TSV files use 0-1-2 coding, contradicting the paper’s binary classification claim (causal vs. non-causal). The meaning of this coding must be clarified. Second, while the authors cite Hidey & McKeown (2016), it is unclear whether they have permission to redistribute the dataset, which should be explicitly stated.

The claimed improvement (2.13% F1) is problematic. The best-performing configuration (among 4–7 options) appears to be selected using the test set, not the development set—a standard practice for fair comparison. This undermines the reported results. Furthermore, the paper lacks statistical significance testing (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals), which is essential given the dataset size. The gold-standard annotations from English/Simple English Wikipedia also show inconsistencies, suggesting scores should be interpreted cautiously.

While neural networks are increasingly standard for such tasks, the novelty is limited. Previous work (e.g., in binary classification) has already demonstrated that neural methods outperform handcrafted features. Thus, the results largely confirm an established trend rather than introducing new insights. The authors should include simpler baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM) to isolate the value of their architecture. Additionally, the analysis should explore why their model favors precision over recall—this could reveal meaningful patterns about the data or model behavior.

In summary, the paper presents a reasonable neural approach to an underexplored task with solid qualitative analysis and good reproducibility practices. However, methodological flaws (test-set leakage, lack of significance testing), unclear technical details (0-1-2 coding, bootstrapping), and insufficient novelty weaken the contribution. The core distinction from implicit connective recognition must be clarified to position the work accurately. The authors should address these points before acceptance.
