

This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for disambiguating causal meaning in lexical markers, arguing that causal relations cannot be captured by syntactic constructions alone and require context-aware neural representations. The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus introduced by Hidey and McKeown (2016), demonstrating modest but consistent improvements over prior work.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) a neural architecture for causal meaning disambiguation that encodes contextual information rather than relying on syntactic patterns or lexical resources; (2) an analysis of how different neural configurations perform on causal disambiguation, particularly regarding the use of two independent LSTMs versus connected ones; and (3) empirical validation showing that the proposed model outperforms the state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case that causality cannot be reduced to fixed syntactic constructions, which aligns with recent linguistic work (Neeleman and van de Koot, 2012). The authors effectively demonstrate that neural models can capture contextual nuances in causal expressions better than rule-based or feature-heavy approaches. Their experiments provide clear evidence that the proposed architecture outperforms the baseline system of Hidey and McKeown (2016), particularly in balancing precision and recall for causal disambiguation. The paper also provides detailed implementation details and code, which is valuable for reproducibility.

Weaknesses: The introduction is overly focused on critiquing previous work rather than clearly articulating the paper's specific contributions. The theoretical foundation for the causal definition remains unclear, with the paper vacillating between a counterfactual view (line 238) and rejecting it (line 256), without establishing a coherent theoretical framework. The example regarding "The argument between John's parents broke the window" (line 256) is confusingly presented as being false, when the issue is rather about whether the human mental model of causality would typically associate these events.

The diagram in Figure 1 is misleading and imprecise. It purports to depict only the "Pair_LSTM" variant but lacks clear semantics for the empty circles between inputs and LSTM boxes, creating ambiguity about the model architecture. The description of padding strategies (line 480) is unnecessarily vague, failing to clarify that padding requirements stem from implementation constraints rather than theoretical considerations.

The claim that "Stated Pair LSTM" performs worse than "Pair LSTM" (line 587) seems counterintuitive given that the authors' assumption was that the two events share meaningful relationships. This interpretation requires stronger justification, particularly since the two event fragments in causal relations often share substantial contextual overlap.

The analysis of the 'which then' connective (Section 4.3) relies on a single example to claim the model outperforms the baseline. This is insufficient evidence to support such a strong claim; a more thorough analysis across multiple ambiguous examples would be necessary to validate this assertion.

The paper's handling of hyperparameters is inconsistent and poorly explained. The optimal configuration changes between the "non bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" corpus versions (lines 578 vs. 636), yet the authors don't adequately explain why these differences exist or what they imply about the model's behavior.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you clarify the theoretical framework for causation assumed in this work, particularly regarding the counterfactual constraint? The paper seems to reject it in some places but appears to rely on it in others.
2. Why does the "Pair LSTM" architecture outperform the "Stated Pair LSTM" despite the authors' initial assumption about the relationship between the two events? What theoretical insights might explain this surprising result?
3. The analysis of the 'which then' connective relies on a single example. Could you provide additional examples from the test set to demonstrate the model's robustness in handling ambiguous causal markers?
4. The paper claims that the model outperforms the state-of-the-art by 2.13% F1 in the Causal class. Could you provide statistical significance tests for these improvements, particularly given the relatively small corpus sizes?

Additional Comments: The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of the specific task definition at the beginning, rather than relying on the introduction to build up to it. The theoretical discussion of causation could be significantly strengthened by referencing established frameworks in philosophy of language and cognitive science. The use of "Hidley" instead of "Hidey" in the review is a minor error that should be corrected.
