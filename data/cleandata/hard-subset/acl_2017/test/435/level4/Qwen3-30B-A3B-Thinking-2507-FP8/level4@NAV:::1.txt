

This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying connectives to determine whether an intended causal relation exists. The core premise is that causal expression exhibits significant diversity, rendering syntactic approaches inadequate, and thus abstract representations from neural models are better suited for such classification tasks.

Experiments utilize the AltLex corpus (Hidley & McKeown). Results provide modest but consistent empirical support for the approach, offering preliminary insights into effective model implementation. The paper includes TensorFlow-based code for reproducibility.

Critical comments and questions:
*   The introduction functions more as a literature review than a concise overview of the paper's contributions, creating redundancy with the subsequent related work section. It fails to establish a clear positive theoretical stance on causation or its expression, instead focusing solely on the negative claim that causation cannot be reduced to syntax. The final paragraph of the introduction hints at the contribution but lacks specificity.
*   The paper remains unclear about the underlying theory of causation. While line 238 references a counterfactual view aligned with Lewis (modal sufficiency with additional conditions), this requires a highly specific implication type, which faces well-documented theoretical challenges (see Copley & Wolff, 2014). Despite claims elsewhere that the authors do not endorse this view, the paper does not specify an alternative theory beyond the temporal constraint mentioned on page 3.
*   The comment on line 256 regarding the sentence's falsity is confusing. If the sentence is true (e.g., "The argument caused the breakage"), a causal link should exist. The paper does not address how event segmentation issues impact causal interpretation, leaving this point unclear.
*   Figure 1's caption is misleading, as the diagram depicts only the "Pair_LSTM" variant. More critically, the diagram lacks precision: the meaning of empty circles between input and "LSTM" boxes is ambiguous, and the prose description of layers (lookup, GloVe, etc.) conflicts with the visual representation. The diagram should clarify the number of representation layers and the exact role of the "LSTM" boxes (e.g., whether only the final representation connects to higher layers).
*   The line 480 statement about padding requirements is unclear. The models themselves do not inherently require padding; this likely stems from TensorFlow or training efficiency. If so, this should be explicitly stated. The final clause's connection to "the most convenient way to encode causal meaning" is also unclear, as convenience does not directly relate to causal semantics.
*   The finding that two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") outperform a single cascaded LSTM contradicts intuition, given that subparts of a sentence likely share information. This parallels unresolved questions in natural language entailment (independent vs. cascaded representation). The assertion on line 587 that "our assumption about the relation between the meanings... does not hold" is unsupported; the result is surprising and warrants further investigation, not a definitive conclusion.
*   Hyperparameter choices across tasks (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636) appear inconsistent. It is unclear whether these differences reflect meaningful model behavior or inherent unpredictability in neural model performance.
*   Section 4.3 claims the model "correctly disambiguates the causal meaning" of 'which then' while Hidley & McKeown's system does not. One example (Table 8) is insufficient evidence. To substantiate this claim, a broader set of ambiguous examples should be tested to demonstrate consistent performance beyond a single instance.
