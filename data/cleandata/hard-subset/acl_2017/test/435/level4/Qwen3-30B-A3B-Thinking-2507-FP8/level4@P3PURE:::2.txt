

This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks (noting that, as in many studies, the networks are not deeply structured). The authors leverage the recent AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to address the task of identifying causal versus non-causal relations when no explicit discourse connective is present. Arguing that convolutional networks are less effective than LSTMs at capturing relevant clause features, the authors propose a classification architecture using GloVe-based clause representations fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three dense layers (with tanh activation) and a softmax decision layer.  

The best system configuration achieves a 0.5–1.5% F1 improvement over Hidey and McKeown’s (2016) SVM baseline. The paper includes qualitative analysis showing successful generalizations (e.g., indicator words consistently causal in training but correctly classified as non-causal in testing), which is appreciated. The paper is well-written with a particularly clear problem description. However, a clarification of the distinctions between this task and implicit connective recognition would be valuable, including why prior implicit connective recognition methods are unsuitable here.  

The authors’ decision to upload code (briefly inspected but not executed) and the associated dataset (with older data) is commendable, providing useful examples. It remains unclear, though, what the 0–1–2 coding in the TSV files signifies given the paper’s focus on binary classification. Additionally, as this dataset originates from Hidey and McKeown, it is necessary to confirm whether the authors have permission to redistribute it. The term "bootstrapping" also requires clarification: while the corpus construction is clearly described, the 15% expansion via bootstrapping is not.  

Despite the interest in applying neural networks to this task, the claimed merits are not fully convincing. The best-performing configuration (among 4–7 options) appears to have been selected using the test set, and this configuration is reported as yielding the 2.13% F1 improvement. A fair comparison would require selecting the best configuration on a development set. Furthermore, the significance of the improvement is unclear: the dataset size should permit statistical significance testing, and the gold-standard annotation (from English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia) is imperfect, necessitating caution in interpreting results. Finally, neural methods have previously demonstrated superior performance over hand-crafted features for binary classification tasks; thus, these results largely confirm a known trend. It would be insightful to compare against simpler neural baselines (e.g., a 1-layer LSTM) and analyze why the proposed model favors precision over recall.
