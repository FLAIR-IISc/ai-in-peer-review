

Summary: This paper proposes a neural network approach for detecting causal relations between clauses using the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to address cases where causal relations are not explicitly marked. The authors argue that LSTMs are more suitable than CNNs for capturing contextual features of causal relations and propose an architecture using GloVe embeddings, LSTM layers, and dense layers with tanh activation. Their best configuration improves upon the previous state-of-the-art (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) by 0.5-1.5% F1.

Main contributions: The primary contribution is a neural network architecture that demonstrates improved performance over the previous state-of-the-art for causal meaning disambiguation. The secondary contribution is the qualitative analysis showing the system's ability to correctly identify causal relations in ambiguous cases, particularly with lexical markers that are ambiguous in context.

Strengths: The paper is well-written with a particularly clear description of the causal meaning disambiguation task. The authors provide thorough qualitative analysis demonstrating the system's ability to correctly identify causal relations in ambiguous contexts, especially for lexical markers that are ambiguous in context. The authors make their code and dataset publicly available, which significantly enhances reproducibility and utility for the research community.

Weaknesses: The paper does not clearly distinguish this causal meaning disambiguation task from implicit connective recognition, which would better contextualize the contribution. The best model configuration appears to be selected on the test set rather than a dev set, which raises concerns about evaluation methodology and potential test set leakage. The statistical significance of the performance improvement is not established, and the quality of the gold-standard annotation (from the AltLex dataset) is questionable given the dataset's construction method. The marginal improvement over the baseline is somewhat expected given previous work showing neural methods can outperform hand-engineered features.

Questions to Authors: Please clarify the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files (the paper describes a binary classification task). Address copyright concerns regarding the reuse of the AltLex dataset. Provide a clear definition of "bootstrapping" in the context of the corpus extension (the paper mentions extending the corpus by about 15% but doesn't explain the bootstrapping process).
