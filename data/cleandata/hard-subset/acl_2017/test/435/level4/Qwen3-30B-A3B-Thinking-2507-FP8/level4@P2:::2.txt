

This paper addresses causal relation disambiguation, focusing on identifying causal versus non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly signaled by standard lexical markers (e.g., "because" or "since"). The authors argue that context encoding is necessary for disambiguating causal meaning, proposing an LSTM-based architecture that processes two events (cause and effect) along with any potential lexical markers. Their system outperforms the state-of-the-art SVM-based approach from Hidey and McKeown (2016) by 2.13% in F1 score on the Causal class.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. A clear demonstration that context encoding is necessary for causal meaning disambiguation, particularly for ambiguous lexical markers where the same expression can have both causal and non-causal interpretations (e.g., "break" or "since").
2. A thorough analysis of the AltLex corpus's challenges, especially regarding ambiguous causal markers and the importance of context for disambiguation.
3. The public release of the code and corpus, significantly enhancing reproducibility and enabling future research in this area.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a compelling argument for why context encoding is essential for causal disambiguation, supported by strong empirical results. The qualitative examples showing the model correctly identifying non-causal uses of commonly causal verbs (e.g., "break" in "The factory was not well equipped to handle the gas") are particularly convincing and address a key limitation of previous approaches.

2. The authors make a significant contribution to the community by publicly releasing both their code and the AltLex corpus. This transparency facilitates reproducibility and future research, which is increasingly important in NLP.

3. The analysis of the AltLex corpus is particularly strong, with clear discussion of the challenges posed by ambiguous causal markers and their distribution across the training and test sets. The paper effectively demonstrates the limitations of previous approaches that rely on lexical resources with fixed coverage.

4. The paper is well-written, with a clear and focused problem statement. The distinction between causal meaning disambiguation and causal argument identification is well-motivated and adds clarity to the task definition.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper fails to clearly differentiate its task from the standard implicit connective recognition task, which is a significant oversight. Previous work in implicit connective recognition (e.g., from the PDTB) addresses similar challenges with ambiguous markers, yet the paper doesn't explain why those approaches cannot be applied to this specific problem or how this work differs from them.

2. The experimental setup appears to have selected the best configuration on the test set rather than a validation set, which is a serious methodological flaw. The reported improvement of 2.13% F1 is based on the best configuration found on the test data, not a development set, which undermines the validity of the comparison with Hidey and McKeown's approach.

3. The significance of the reported improvement is not properly established. The paper lacks statistical significance testing, and it doesn't address the potential noise in the gold-standard annotations (which the authors themselves acknowledge as imperfect). Given the size of the dataset, a McNemar's test or similar would strengthen the claims.

4. The paper doesn't adequately justify the need for a neural approach when previous work has shown neural methods generally outperform hand-engineered features for binary classification tasks. Simpler baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM) should have been included to demonstrate that the complexity of the proposed architecture is necessary.

5. The explanation of "bootstrapping" in the corpus is insufficient. While the paper describes the corpus construction, it fails to clarify how the bootstrapping process works or why it's necessary. The authors should also address whether they have the right to redistribute the AltLex corpus as they do, given it was originally created by Hidey and McKeown.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does your task specifically differ from implicit connective recognition, and why can't previous implicit connective recognition methods be applied to your task?
2. Can you clarify the bootstrapping process used to expand the AltLex corpus? What specific improvements does it provide, and why is it necessary?
3. The paper mentions "non bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" versions of the corpus. Please explain how the bootstrapping process was applied and how it affects the annotation consistency between training and test sets.

Additional Comments:
The paper's claim that neural networks are needed for causal meaning disambiguation seems partially validated by the results, but the significance of the improvement is not convincingly established. The paper should address why the neural approach is specifically better suited for this task rather than generalizing the known advantage of neural methods over feature-based approaches for classification tasks. The analysis of the model's bias toward precision over recall is also underdeveloped, which would be valuable for understanding the model's limitations.
