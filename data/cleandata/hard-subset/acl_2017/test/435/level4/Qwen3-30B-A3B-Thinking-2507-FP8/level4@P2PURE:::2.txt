

This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks (though, as in many studies, the networks are not deeply layered). While certain discourse connectives unambiguously signal causal relations (e.g., 'because'), the authors leverage the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to identify causal versus non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. Arguing that convolutional networks are less effective than LSTMs at capturing relevant clause features, the authors propose a classification architecture using GloVe-based clause representations fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected tanh layers and a softmax decision layer.  

The best system configuration achieves a 0.5–1.5% F1 improvement over Hidey and McKeown’s (2016) SVM baseline. The authors demonstrate qualitative generalizations, including cases where indicator words consistently causal in training data are correctly identified as non-causal in test data. I commend the thorough qualitative and quantitative analysis. The paper is well-written, with a particularly clear problem description. However, a clarification of the distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition would be valuable, including why prior implicit connective recognition methods are unsuitable here.  

The authors’ public code upload (which I briefly inspected but did not execute) and provision of the dataset (with code) are highly appreciated, as they provide numerous examples. It remains unclear, though, what the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files signifies given the paper’s binary classification claim. Additionally, as the dataset originates from Hidey and McKeown, it is unclear whether the authors have permission to redistribute it. The paper should also clarify the meaning of "bootstrapping," which expands the corpus by ~15%—a process briefly mentioned but not explained, despite the clear corpus construction description.  

While neural network experimentation on this task is interesting, the proposed system’s merits are not fully convincing. The best configuration (among 4–7 options) was selected using the test set, and this configuration is reported to improve F1 by 2.13% over Hidey and McKeown. A fair comparison requires selecting the best configuration on the dev set. Furthermore, the statistical significance of the improvement is unclear; given the dataset size, significance indicators should be reported. Additionally, the gold-standard annotation (from English/Simple English Wikipedia) is imperfect, warranting cautious interpretation of results.  

Finally, neural methods have previously outperformed hand-engineered features for binary classification tasks, so these results largely confirm a known trend. It would be valuable to include simpler baselines (e.g., a 1-layer LSTM) and analyze why the neural approach favors precision over recall.
