

Summary: This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality disambiguation, specifically addressing cases where causal relations are not explicitly marked by lexical markers. The authors argue that contextual encoding of events is necessary for accurate causal disambiguation, and they introduce an LSTM-based model that processes cause and effect events as separate inputs. The system is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, which contains causal relations expressed through diverse linguistic constructions. The authors claim their model outperforms the state-of-the-art (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) by 2.13% F1 in the Causal class on the "non bootstrapping" corpus version.

Main Contributions: 
1. The paper makes a clear contribution by demonstrating that neural networks can effectively disambiguate causal meaning in cases where the causal relation is not explicitly marked by lexical markers, addressing a significant gap in existing work.
2. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the AltLex corpus and its challenges, particularly regarding ambiguous lexical markers whose causal meaning depends on context.
3. The paper offers strong qualitative evidence showing that the neural model can learn to disambiguate causal meaning beyond simple heuristics (e.g., baselines that use the most frequent class of each lexical marker).

Strengths:
1. The problem statement is exceptionally clear and well-motivated, with compelling examples illustrating the challenges of causal disambiguation when lexical markers are ambiguous. The paper effectively demonstrates that existing approaches (e.g., syntax-based or lexical resource-dependent methods) fail to capture the contextual nature of causality.
2. The authors provide a strong qualitative analysis of the system's performance, showing how it correctly classifies examples where the lexical marker is ambiguous but context determines the causal meaning (e.g., "break" in different contexts). This analysis effectively supports their claim that contextual encoding is necessary.
3. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by using and analyzing the AltLex corpus, which addresses a key limitation in existing causal relation resources (PDTB's restricted coverage of causal expressions).

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation methodology contains a critical flaw: the best configuration (Pair LSTM Mean Adam) appears to have been selected on the test set rather than a dev set. The paper reports that this configuration improved the state-of-the-art by 2.13% F1, but without proper model selection on a dev set, this comparison is invalid and undermines the reported results. A fair comparison should involve selecting the best configuration on a dev set.
2. The paper fails to establish statistical significance for the improvements over the baseline. Given the corpus size, the authors should have performed McNemar's test (as they do for the balanced dataset) to demonstrate that the improvements are not due to chance.
3. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why neural networks are necessary for this task. Simpler models (e.g., a single-layer LSTM or linear classifier) might achieve comparable performance, and the authors should have included such baselines to establish the necessity of their approach. The claim that "neural networks are able to encode the meaning of those events" is not sufficiently supported by the experiments.
4. The "bootstrapping" process used to expand the AltLex corpus is inadequately explained. The paper mentions that the bootstrapping version has instances where training and test sets have different class distributions, but it doesn't clarify how the bootstrapping was performed or why it was necessary for the corpus expansion.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify the bootstrapping process used to expand the AltLex corpus. The paper states that the bootstrapping version increases corpus size by about 15%, but the methodology is not explained. How was this process implemented, and why was it necessary?
2. How was the best configuration (Pair LSTM Mean Adam) selected? The paper reports this configuration as outperforming the baseline, but it's unclear whether this selection was made on the test set or a dev set.
3. Could you provide statistical significance tests for the improvements over the baseline? Given the corpus size, McNemar's test would be appropriate to establish whether the improvements are statistically meaningful.

Overall, while the paper addresses a meaningful problem with a clear motivation and provides valuable qualitative analysis, the evaluation methodology contains a critical flaw that undermines the reported results. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be accepted. The contribution to understanding contextual encoding for causal disambiguation is promising, but the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claimed improvements.
