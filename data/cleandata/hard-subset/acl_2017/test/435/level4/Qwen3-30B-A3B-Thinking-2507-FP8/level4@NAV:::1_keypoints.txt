- Proposes an LSTM-based model to classify connectives for detecting intended causal relations, arguing neural representations handle causal expression diversity better than syntactic methods.
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent empirical support and include TensorFlow code for reproducibility.
- Introduction reads like a literature review, is redundant with related work, and fails to clearly state contributions or a positive theoretical stance on causation.
- The paper’s theory of causation is unclear: it references a Lewis-style counterfactual view (problematic per Copley & Wolff) but offers no concrete alternative beyond a temporal constraint.
- The discussion about sentence falsity and event segmentation is confusing and does not explain how segmentation affects causal interpretation.
- Figure 1 is misleading and imprecise (appears to show only Pair_LSTM, ambiguous symbols, conflicts with prose); it should clarify layer structure and the LSTM roles.
- The statement about padding requirements is unclear—padding likely stems from TensorFlow/training choices and should be explicitly explained; its link to encoding causal meaning is unjustified.
- The surprising result that two independent LSTMs beat a cascaded LSTM contradicts intuition; the authors’ conclusion that their assumption is wrong is unsupported and needs further analysis.
- Hyperparameter choices differ across tasks without justification, leaving it unclear whether differences reflect meaningful behavior or training variability.
- Claim that the model “correctly disambiguates” examples like ‘which then’ is based on a single instance and requires broader evaluation with more ambiguous examples.
