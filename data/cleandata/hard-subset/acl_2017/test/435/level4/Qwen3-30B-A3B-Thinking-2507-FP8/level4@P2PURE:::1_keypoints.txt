- Paper proposes an LSTM-based model to classify connective uses for detecting intended causal relations, arguing neural representations handle causal-expression diversity better than syntactic approaches.
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support for the approach and provide initial implementation insights; TensorFlow code is included for reproducibility.
- Introduction reads like an extended literature review, duplicates related work, and fails to state the paper’s positive contributions clearly until the end.
- The paper lacks a clear theoretical account of causation: it implicitly leans toward a counterfactual/Lewis-style view yet neither defends that view nor specifies an alternative.
- Commentary around line 256 is unclear—authors seem to claim a sentence is false but do not resolve issues of event decomposition or their implications for causal theories.
- Figure 1’s caption is misleading (shows only Pair_LSTM) and the diagram lacks precise, consistent semantics and labels for components (lookup/GloVe layers, unlabeled circles, exact LSTM connections).
- The sentence at line 480 is confusing about padding: if padding is a TensorFlow/efficiency artifact, that should be stated, and its asserted link to “convenience of encoding causal meaning” is unclear.
- The result that two independent LSTMs outperform a single sequential LSTM is surprising and parallels debates in entailment; the paper’s interpretation (that independent encoding is preferable) is not convincingly justified.
- Hyperparameter choices that yield optimal performance vary across tasks and are hard to interpret—unclear whether this is due to analysis oversight or inherent model-data interactions.
- The claim in Section 4.3 that the system “correctly disambiguates” the causal meaning of “which then” is unsupported by a single example; a broader, controlled evaluation of ambiguous examples is needed.
