

This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying connectives to determine whether they express intended causal relations. The core premise is that causal expression is highly diverse and thus cannot be reduced to syntactic analysis, making abstract representations from neural models more suitable for this task.

Experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus (Hidley and McKeown). Results provide modest but consistent support for the approach, offering initial insights into effective model implementation. The paper includes TensorFlow-based code for replication.

Critical comments and questions:

* The introduction reads more like a literature review than a standard introduction, creating redundancy with the subsequent Related Work section. While non-standard intros are acceptable, this one fails to establish a clear theoretical stance on causation or its expression. It merely states that causation cannot be reduced to syntax (a negative claim) without articulating the positive contribution beyond the vague concluding paragraph of the section.

* The paper lacks clarity on the assumed theory of causation. The text (lines 238+) implicitly adopts a counterfactual view akin to David Lewis, where causation is a modal sufficiency claim with additional counterfactual conditions. However, the arrow in the cited formalism requires a highly specific implication type, and Lewis's theory faces well-documented challenges (see Copley and Wolff, 2014). While the paper states it does not endorse the counterfactual view elsewhere, it does not specify the alternative theory. The temporal constraint mentioned on page 3 is insufficient as a foundation.

* The comment on line 256 is confusing. The authors appear to claim the sentence is false. If the sentence is true, a causal link between the argument and the breakage should exist. The paper does not address unresolved issues in event segmentation (which impact causal theories), leaving the reasoning unclear.

* Figure 1's caption is misleading, as the diagram depicts only the "Pair_LSTM" variant. More significantly, the diagram is imprecise. Unlabeled circles between input and "LSTM" boxes lack explanation. The prose mentions a lookup layer, GloVe layer, and subsequent processing, but the diagram fails to clarify the number of representation layers or the exact meaning of the "LSTM" boxes. It seems only the leftmost/final representation connects to higher layers; this connection should be explicitly depicted.

* The sentence beginning line 480 is unclear. The models do not inherently require padding; this appears to be a TensorFlow/efficiency constraint. If so, state this explicitly. The final clause also seems disconnected from the question of "the most convenient way to encode causal meaning," as convenience does not directly relate to causal semantics.

* The finding that two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") outperform a single LSTM with sequential feeding echoes unresolved debates in natural language entailment (independent vs. sequential representation of premise/hypothesis). This is an open question for entailment and likely for causal relations too. Thus, the conclusion on line 587—"This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently..."—is surprising, as subparts of a sentence likely share significant information.

* Hyperparameters yielding optimal performance across tasks are inconsistent (e.g., compare lines 578 and 636). This suggests either an unexplained data-model interaction or unpredictability; the paper should clarify whether this is meaningful or incidental.

* Section 4.3 claims the system "correctly disambiguates the causal meaning" of the connective 'which then' (where Hidley and McKeown's system fails). One example (Table 8) is insufficient. To substantiate this claim, generate a diverse set of ambiguous examples and evaluate the system's accuracy across them, rather than relying on a single instance to determine if the result was due to chance.
