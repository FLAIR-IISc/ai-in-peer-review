

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a neural network approach for causality classification, specifically focusing on disambiguating the causal meaning of lexical markers based on contextual information. The main contribution is the demonstration that neural encoding of context is necessary for accurate causal meaning disambiguation, rather than relying on syntactic constructions or lexical resources. A secondary contribution is the evaluation of this approach on the AltLex corpus, showing consistent performance improvements over previous methods. The third contribution is the clear empirical validation of the claim that context encoding is essential, with detailed analysis of model behavior across different architectures and optimization strategies.

**Strengths**  
1. The paper addresses a significant gap in causality classification by moving beyond syntax-based approaches and demonstrating that neural context encoding is necessary for disambiguating causal meaning. This is well-supported by the experimental results showing that the proposed LSTM-based model outperforms previous methods that rely on lexical resources or syntactic features.

2. The evaluation on the AltLex corpus is well-structured and thorough, with careful analysis of different model architectures, padding strategies, and optimizers. The paper provides clear comparisons against relevant baselines (B1 and B2), with detailed performance metrics (Precision, Recall, F1) that convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

3. The paper's empirical analysis of ambiguous connectives (e.g., "which then") provides valuable insights into how the model handles linguistic ambiguity. The examples in Table 8 effectively illustrate the model's ability to correctly disambiguate causal meaning where previous methods fail, supporting the main claim of the paper.

**Weaknesses**  
1. The theoretical foundation of the causality model is unclear and inconsistent. The paper begins by rejecting the counterfactual view of causality (line 238), yet later appears to adopt it without justification. The paper also fails to clearly articulate which theory of causation it assumes, leaving readers confused about the theoretical underpinnings of the task definition. This lack of clarity undermines the paper's contribution to the field.

2. The introduction is poorly structured and reads more like a literature review than a paper overview. It unnecessarily repeats content from the related work section and fails to clearly present the paper's main contributions. The final paragraph of the introduction vaguely states the paper's contribution without providing a clear, concise statement of what the paper actually does.

3. The analysis of model behavior (lines 578-587) is insufficiently justified. The claim that "the assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold" is surprising given that the two events are part of the same causal relation. The paper provides no theoretical or empirical justification for why independent encoding of events is preferable to a connected architecture, making this conclusion seem premature.

4. The evaluation of ambiguous connectives (Section 4.3) relies on a single example ("which then") to claim superior performance over the baseline. This is insufficient evidence to support the claim that the model "correctly disambiguates its causal meaning." A more comprehensive analysis across multiple ambiguous examples would be necessary to substantiate this claim.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The paper rejects the counterfactual view of causality (line 238), but then appears to adopt it in subsequent sections. Which theory of causation does the paper assume, and how does this align with the task definition? Please clarify this theoretical foundation.

2. The introduction states the paper's contribution as "neural encoding of context," but the related work section already discusses similar approaches (e.g., Do et al., 2011; Riaz and Girju, 2013-2014). How does this work advance beyond these prior approaches in terms of theoretical or methodological contributions?

3. The paper claims that "the encoding of the context is required for the disambiguation of the causal meaning," but the experiments only evaluate this on the AltLex corpus. How generalizable is this claim to other corpora and causal expressions? Please discuss the limitations of this claim.

4. The analysis of the "which then" connective (Table 8) relies on a single example. To support the claim that the model correctly disambiguates this ambiguous connective, please provide a more comprehensive analysis across multiple examples of this connective in both the training and test sets.
