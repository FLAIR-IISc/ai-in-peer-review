

This paper addresses the task of detecting causal relations between clauses, particularly focusing on implicit causal relations not marked by explicit discourse connectives. The authors leverage the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) and propose a neural architecture using GloVe embeddings, an LSTM layer to capture clause structure, followed by three dense tanh layers and a softmax output. The approach argues that LSTMs better represent clause features than convolutional networks for this task. The best configuration achieves a marginal 0.5–1.5% F1 improvement over the prior SVM-based system (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). The paper is well-written with a clear problem statement, and the authors provide valuable qualitative analysis of system generalizations (e.g., correctly identifying non-causal uses of words like "because" in test data). Their decision to share code and dataset is commendable, though the meaning of the 0–1–2 coding in TSV files (despite the paper describing binary classification) and the reuse of the AltLex dataset without explicit clarification of permissions require resolution.

The primary concern lies in the experimental methodology. The reported 2.13% F1 improvement appears to stem from selecting the best model configuration directly on the test set, rather than using a development set for hyperparameter tuning. This practice risks test set leakage and undermines the validity of the comparison. A fair evaluation would require selecting the optimal configuration on the development set, then reporting results on the held-out test set. Without this, the claimed improvement is not statistically robust.

Furthermore, the significance of the performance gain is unclear. Given the dataset size, the authors should report statistical significance tests (e.g., McNemar’s test) to confirm whether the F1 improvement is meaningful. Additionally, the gold-standard annotations derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia are noted to contain inconsistencies. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and discuss how annotation noise might affect the reported scores, as even small errors in the gold standard could inflate the perceived performance of the system.

The paper also lacks a critical distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition (ICR), a closely related field. While the authors briefly mention ICR, they do not explain why established ICR methods (e.g., those using lexical features or syntactic parsing) cannot be directly applied here. Clarifying this—perhaps by contrasting the scope (e.g., this task focuses *only* on causal relations, whereas ICR typically handles multiple discourse relations)—would strengthen the contribution claim. 

The mention of "bootstrapping" extending the corpus by 15% is insufficiently explained. The paper describes the corpus construction but omits how bootstrapping was implemented (e.g., which heuristics or rules were used to generate new examples). This omission hinders reproducibility and raises questions about the corpus’s reliability. The authors should provide a clear, concise description of this step.

While neural networks are increasingly standard for such tasks, the paper’s claim of novelty is weakened by the lack of simpler neural baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM). Comparing against such a minimal architecture would better demonstrate whether the added complexity of three dense layers is justified. The authors also note a precision-recall trade-off in their results but fail to analyze *why* the model favors precision—e.g., due to class imbalance or feature representation—leaving this insight unexplored.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound approach with clear writing and useful supplementary materials. However, the marginal performance gains, methodological inconsistencies (test-set configuration selection), and insufficient task clarification limit its impact. The authors should address the following before publication: (1) clarify the distinction from implicit connective recognition; (2) revise the experimental protocol to use a development set for model selection; (3) report statistical significance; (4) explain the 0–1–2 coding and bootstrapping process; and (5) add a simple neural baseline to validate their architecture’s necessity. With these revisions, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field.
