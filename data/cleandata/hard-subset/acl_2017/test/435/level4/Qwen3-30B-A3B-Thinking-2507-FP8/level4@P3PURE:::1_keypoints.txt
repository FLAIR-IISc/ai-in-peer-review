- Paper proposes an LSTM-based model to classify connectives for intended causal relations, arguing neural abstractions suit the task better than syntax.  
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support; TensorFlow code is provided for replication.  
- Introduction reads like a literature review, duplicates Related Work, and fails to state a clear theoretical stance or positive contribution on causation.  
- The assumed theory of causation is unclear and implicitly counterfactual (Lewis-like); the paper neither defends this nor offers a specified alternative.  
- Line 256’s claim about sentence truth and causal linking is confusing and ignores unresolved event segmentation issues that affect causal reasoning.  
- Figure 1 is misleading and imprecise: it only shows the Pair_LSTM variant, has unlabeled elements, and fails to clarify representation layers and LSTM box meanings.  
- The sentence about padding (line 480) is unclear—padding appears to be an engineering/TensorFlow constraint and should be stated explicitly rather than linked to causal encoding.  
- The result that two independent LSTMs outperform a sequential single LSTM echoes open debates in entailment and the paper’s conclusion favoring independent encoding is surprising and under-argued.  
- Optimal hyperparameters vary inconsistently across tasks, suggesting unexplained data–model interactions or instability that the paper should address.  
- The claim that the system disambiguates the connective “which then” is based on a single example; stronger evaluation with diverse ambiguous examples is needed.
