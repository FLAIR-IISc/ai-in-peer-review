

This paper introduces a neural network approach for detecting causal relations between clauses, leveraging recent advances in deep learning (though the networks themselves are relatively shallow, as is common in such studies). While explicit discourse connectives like "because" unambiguously signal causal relations, the authors address the more challenging task of distinguishing causal from non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. They utilize the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) for this purpose. Arguing that convolutional networks are less effective than LSTMs at capturing relevant clause features, the authors propose a classification architecture using GloVe embeddings for clauses, processed through an LSTM layer followed by three densely connected tanh layers and a softmax decision layer.

The best-performing configuration achieves a 0.5–1.5% absolute F1 improvement over Hidey and McKeown's (2016) SVM baseline. The authors provide compelling qualitative examples demonstrating the model's ability to generalize—correctly identifying non-causal instances for words that were always causal in the training data. This thorough qualitative and quantitative analysis is commendable.

The paper is well-written, with a particularly clear problem description. However, a clarification of how this task differs from implicit connective recognition would be valuable. Specifically, the authors should discuss why existing implicit connective recognition methods are unsuitable for their specific problem.

It is commendable that the authors have shared their code and the dataset (including the older version) on the submission site. While I briefly inspected the code without executing it, the dataset provides useful examples. That said, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files remains unclear, given the paper's focus on binary classification. Additionally, as this data originates from Hidey and McKeown, it would be helpful to clarify the authors' right to redistribute it. The paper also requires a definition of "bootstrapping," which expands the corpus by approximately 15%; the construction of the original corpus is well-explained, but the bootstrapping procedure is not.

While exploring neural networks for this task is interesting, the claimed improvements lack full conviction. The best configuration (selected from 4–7 options) appears to be tuned on the test set, with the resulting 2.13% F1 improvement reported. A fair comparison would involve selecting the best configuration on the development set. Furthermore, the statistical significance of the improvement is not established, despite the dataset's size making such analysis feasible. The reliability of the gold-standard annotations (derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia) also warrants consideration, as they are not entirely consistent, potentially affecting score interpretation.

Finally, neural methods have consistently outperformed engineered features for binary classification tasks, so these results largely confirm a known trend. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should include experiments with simpler baseline architectures (e.g., a single-layer LSTM) and analyze why their neural approach appears to prioritize precision over recall.
