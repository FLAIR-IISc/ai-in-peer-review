

This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying whether connectives express intended causal relations, arguing that the diversity of causal expression necessitates abstract neural representations over syntactic approaches. The experiments use the AltLex corpus (Hidley & McKeown), yielding modest but consistent performance gains. The authors provide TensorFlow code, which is commendable. However, significant theoretical and methodological concerns undermine the contribution’s clarity and validity.

The introduction is problematic as it reads more like a literature review than a concise roadmap of the paper’s contribution. It fails to establish a clear theoretical stance on causation—repeatedly emphasizing what causation *is not* (e.g., syntactically reducible) without articulating *what it is*. The final paragraph vaguely hints at a "more abstract representation" as the solution, but this lacks specificity. Crucially, the paper’s theoretical foundation remains inconsistent. While the authors reject a Lewis-style counterfactual view (line 242), their model implicitly relies on it (line 238: "C → E" requires a highly specific modal implication that Lewis’s framework struggles to support, as noted in Copley & Wolff 2014). If not counterfactual, what theory underpins the model? The temporal constraint mentioned on page 3 is insufficient to ground causal classification. This theoretical ambiguity weakens the core premise.

The example analysis (line 256) is confusing. The authors claim the sentence "The argument broke the phone" is false, implying no causal link. Yet if true, the argument *would* causally explain the breakage. The paper neglects to address how event segmentation (a key issue in causal theory) affects this judgment, leaving the example’s interpretation unresolved.

Figure 1’s caption misrepresents the model as depicting the full architecture, when it only shows "Pair_LSTM." More critically, the diagram lacks technical precision: the empty circles between input and LSTM boxes are unexplained, the embedding layer’s role is omitted, and the LSTM boxes’ semantics are ambiguous (e.g., are all boxes representing intermediate layers?). The prose claims a "look-up layer" and "Glove layer," but the diagram does not clarify how these integrate with the LSTM. The connection from the LSTM output to the pooling layers should be explicitly drawn to avoid confusion.

The padding comment (line 480) is unclear. The authors state padding is "the most convenient way to encode causal meaning," but padding is a TensorFlow implementation artifact, not a causal encoding strategy. If padding is merely a technical necessity, this should be stated plainly. The claimed link to causal meaning representation is unfounded and distracts from the core methodological question.

The choice of two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") over a chained variant is presented as evidence that "the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold" (line 587). This conclusion is surprising and unsupported. In natural language entailment, independent encoding *is* debated precisely because premise-hypothesis pairs often share information. The paper provides no justification for why causal relations would behave differently, nor does it cite relevant literature. This claim appears to conflate model design with theoretical insight.

Hyperparameter choices (lines 578 vs. 636) are inconsistently reported. The best parameters vary significantly across tasks without explanation, suggesting either data-dependent idiosyncrasies or insufficient hyperparameter search. The authors should clarify whether these variations reflect model sensitivity or random fluctuations, rather than attributing them to "unpredictability."

Section 4.3 claims the model "correctly disambiguates 'which then' causal meaning" (unlike Hidley & McKeown), citing only one example (Table 8). A single instance cannot validate generalizability. To substantiate this, the authors should test the model on a diverse set of ambiguous examples (e.g., varying syntactic structures, event types) and report accuracy across a broader test set. Without this, the claim remains anecdotal.

In summary, the paper’s core idea—that neural models suit causal classification better than syntax—is plausible but underdeveloped. The theoretical foundation is shaky, the model description lacks precision, and key claims rest on insufficient evidence. The modest results are promising but do not yet justify the paper’s broader assertions. To strengthen the work, the authors should: (1) clarify the causal theory assumed (and reconcile contradictions), (2) refine Figure 1 to match the prose, (3) justify the independent LSTM design with evidence, (4) provide a larger test set for 'which then' disambiguation, and (5) clarify technical details (padding, hyperparameters). With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution. As is, it lacks the theoretical rigor and empirical support expected for ACL.
