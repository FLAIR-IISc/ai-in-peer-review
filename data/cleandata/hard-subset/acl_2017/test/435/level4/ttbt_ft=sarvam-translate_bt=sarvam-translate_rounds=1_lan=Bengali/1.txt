This paper creates a model based on LLTM that categorizes the use of connective words. It can determine whether these words indicate a causal relationship. Its main idea is that causal relationships are expressed very differently and therefore cannot be analyzed syntactically. Abstract representations from neural models are more suitable for making these conclusions.

These experiments were conducted on the Altlex corpus, invented by Hadley and McKeown. The results provide slight but consistent support for the general idea and offer some initial insights into the best way to translate this idea into a model. This paper includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Some important comments and questions:

* The introduction is unusual because it is like a literature review, where there is no complete summary of what is in the paper. This results in some repetition with related parts of the work. I think an unconventional type of introduction is acceptable, but it is not very effective: even after reviewing many concepts, it does not take a position on what or how causal relationships are expressed, but only highlights a negative point (which cannot be explained through syntax). We are not told what the positive contribution would be, except for the very general last paragraph of the section.
* Adding to the above points, I am disappointed that the paper is not clear about causal theory. The authors lean towards a speculative approach like David Lewis's idea, where causal relationships demand a model sufficiency, with some additional speculative conditions added. See lines 238 and onwards; this arrow must be a special type of implication to work, and Lewis's theory has some well-known problems (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere in the paper, it is commented that the authors do not support a speculative approach, but then what theory is being speculated? It cannot be just the time limit mentioned on page 3!
* I don't understand the comments related to the example in line 256. The authors are probably saying that they consider the sentence to be false. If it is true, then there should be some causal relationship between the argument and the break. There are some problems with dividing the events into sub-events, and these affect causal theory, but they are not being discussed here, so I am confused.
* The caption of figure 1 is misleading because the diagram only wants to show the "pair_LLTM" variant of the model. My main complaint is that this diagram is unnecessarily ambiguous. I think it is okay to omit some parts of the standard model definition, but these diagrams should have a clear and consistent meaning. What are all the empty circles between the input and the "LLTM" boxes? The prose says the model has a look-up layer, a glove layer, and then... what? How many layers are there in the representation? The diagram is clear about the pooling THNNH layer for pre-softmap, but not about this. I am also not clear about what the "LLTM" boxes represent. It seems that only the left/last representation is directly connected to the above layers. I suggest showing that connection clearly.
* I don't understand the sentence starting from line 480. The models under discussion inherently do not need any padding. I am guessing it is a requirement of TensorFlow and/or efficient training. That's fine. If that's right, please tell me. Although I don't understand the last clause. How is this problem related to the question of what is the "most convenient way to encode causal meaning"? I don't understand how convenience is a problem or how it is directly related to causal meaning.
* The authors have seen that having two distinct LLTMs ("stated_LLTM") is somewhat better than having one, where the first is connected to the second. This problem reminds me of the discussions in literature on natural language entailment, where the question is whether premises and hypotheses should be presented independently or whether the first should be connected to the second. I consider this an open question for entailment, and I can bet that it needs further investigation for causal relationships as well. So I cannot support the sentence starting from line 587: "The meaning of this behavior is that our assumption about the relationship between the meaning of two input events is not correct, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then measure the relationship between the arguments using the dense layer." This is very surprising, because we are talking about a component of a sentence that can share a lot of information.
* It is difficult to understand the meaning of the hyperparameters that lead to the best performance in the tasks. For example, compare line 636 with line 578. Should we explain this or blame the uncertainty of how these models interact with the data?
* Section 4.3 ended with the connective word 'which দেন', where the system can "correctly disambiguate its causal meaning", where that is not possible in the case of Hadley and McKeown. That may be right, but it is not enough to show it. To prove this point, I suggest creating various examples that express ambiguity and see how many times the system gives the correct answer. This will help to know whether the example from table 8 was lucky or not.
