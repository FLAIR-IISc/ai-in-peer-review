### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying whether a lexical marker signals a causal relation. The central hypothesis is that the expression of causality is too diverse for purely syntactic approaches and is better captured by the abstract representations learned by neural networks. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), where it outperforms previous work. The results provide modest but consistent support for the authors' hypothesis and offer initial insights into how to best structure a neural model for this task.

### **Strengths**

1.  The paper's primary hypothesis—that the diverse linguistic expression of causality is better handled by abstract neural representations than by fixed syntactic patterns—is well-motivated and compelling. This provides a strong foundation for the work.
2.  The experimental results, while showing modest improvements, are consistent and support the overall direction of the research. The paper provides a solid first step in exploring neural architectures for this problem.
3.  The authors' inclusion of their TensorFlow-based models is a valuable contribution that enhances reproducibility and will facilitate future research in this area.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of a clear conceptual framework:** The paper's introduction functions more as a literature review and, like the rest of the paper, does not commit to a clear theory of causation. The authors critique syntactic approaches and seem to reject a counterfactual view of causality, but they do not articulate what definition they are assuming beyond a simple temporal constraint. This theoretical ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the task definition and the model's success. For instance, the authors appear to endorse a counterfactual view when defining causation formally (e1 → e2), yet later dismiss it without offering a replacement.
2.  **Unclear model description:** The model's architecture is not described with sufficient precision. Figure 1 is misleading, as it only depicts the "Pair LSTM" variant, and contains unexplained elements (e.g., the empty circles between the input and LSTM boxes). The prose does not clarify key details, such as the number of layers or precisely how the LSTM outputs are utilized. This lack of clarity hinders full comprehension and reproducibility.
3.  **Overstated conclusions from model comparisons:** The paper concludes that because the independent "Pair LSTM" architecture slightly outperforms the connected "Stated Pair LSTM," it is definitively "better to encode each argument independently." This is a strong claim based on limited evidence. This architectural choice is an open research question in related tasks like natural language inference, where context between the two inputs can be crucial. The observed small difference in performance does not warrant such a definitive conclusion.
4.  **Anecdotal error analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.3, which claims the model correctly disambiguates the ambiguous connective 'which then', is based on a single example. This is insufficient evidence to conclude that the model has genuinely learned to handle this ambiguity, as the correct classification could be coincidental. A more systematic analysis with a range of constructed examples would be needed to substantiate this claim.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the example, `(The argument between John’s parents)e1 (broke the window)e2`, could you clarify your reasoning for asserting that the "human mental model of causality does not usually relate an argument with the breaking of a window"? To many readers, this would be a plausible causal chain, making your dismissal of it confusing.
2.  The justification for evaluating different padding strategies is framed as finding "the most convenient to encode the causal meaning." However, padding is typically an implementation detail required for efficient batch processing in frameworks like TensorFlow, rather than a component that encodes meaning. Could you clarify if there is a deeper linguistic or modeling motivation for this framing?
3.  The best-performing hyperparameters and zero-padding strategies differ between the 'non-bootstrapping' and 'bootstrapping' versions of the corpus (e.g., Adadelta vs. Adam optimizer). Do you have an interpretation for why the optimal configuration changes, or should this be attributed to the unpredictability of the training dynamics on different datasets?
