- Proposes an LSTM-based model to classify connective use for detecting implied causal relations, arguing neural representations outperform syntactic methods.  
- Experiments run on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown); results are consistent but modest; TensorFlow models are released.  
- Introduction is poorly structured—reads like a literature survey, overlaps related work, and delays stating the paper’s positive contribution and definition of causation.  
- The assumed theory of causation is unclear and inconsistent (appears to lean on Lewis-style counterfactuals in places) and is insufficiently justified.  
- Analysis of the example at line 256 is unclear and omits discussion of event sub-division, leaving the interpretation unresolved.  
- Figure 1’s caption is incorrect (only shows Pair_LSTM) and the diagram is imprecise/under-explained (unexplained empty circles, unclear layer counts, ambiguous LSTM outputs).  
- The discussion around padding (line 480) is confusing; practical TensorFlow batching constraints should be stated explicitly and not conflated with semantic encoding choices.  
- The finding that two independent LSTMs (Stated_LSTM) outperform a sequential architecture is interesting but the strong conclusion drawn is premature given shared sentence context.  
- Optimal hyperparameters vary considerably across tasks without explanation, making the reported settings hard to interpret.  
- The claim that the model correctly disambiguates "which then" is supported by only a single example and needs broader testing to be validated.
