This paper proposes a neural network-based method for detecting causal relations between clauses, particularly when the relation is not explicitly marked by a connective like 'because'. The authors leverage the recently introduced AltLex dataset for this binary classification task (causal vs. non-causal). Arguing that LSTMs are better suited than CNNs for representing clausal features, they propose an architecture that uses GloVe embeddings to represent clauses, which are then encoded by an LSTM layer. The LSTM output is passed through three dense layers with tanh activation, followed by a final softmax layer for classification.

The paper is generally well-written, with a clear and concise problem description. I particularly appreciate the qualitative analysis, which provides concrete examples where the model successfully generalizes beyond simple lexical cues learned from the training data. The authors are also to be commended for submitting their code and data, which greatly aids reproducibility and transparency.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed.

**Major Concerns:**

The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The authors report results for their best system configuration, which appears to have been selected based on its performance on the test set. This is a methodologically unsound practice, as model selection and hyperparameter tuning must be performed on a development set to avoid overfitting to the test data and to ensure a fair evaluation. The performance of the single model configuration selected via the development set should be reported on the test set for a valid comparison with the baseline. As it stands, the reported improvement of 2.13% F1 over the Hidey and McKeown (2016) baseline is not reliable.

Furthermore, the reported improvements are modest. Given the size of the dataset, the authors should perform statistical significance tests to validate whether their gains over the baseline are statistically meaningful. Without such tests, it is difficult to assess the actual contribution of the proposed model.

**Minor Concerns and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Task Definition:** The paper would be strengthened by a clearer distinction between the proposed task and the well-established task of implicit discourse relation recognition (e.g., as in the Penn Discourse Treebank). A discussion on why existing methods for the latter are not directly applicable here, or how this task differs, would provide important context for the reader.

2.  **Data and Reproducibility:** While the provision of code and data is welcome, a few points require clarification:
    *   The "bootstrapping" procedure used to extend the corpus by 15% is mentioned but not sufficiently explained. This process should be detailed to ensure the work is fully reproducible.
    *   The provided TSV data files use a 0-1-2 coding scheme, but the paper describes a binary classification task. This discrepancy should be explained.
    *   The authors should clarify if they have the rights to redistribute the AltLex dataset, as it was created by other researchers.

3.  **Gold Standard Quality:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of the gold-standard data quality. The AltLex dataset, derived from aligning English and Simple English Wikipedia, may contain annotation noise. An acknowledgment of this, perhaps by citing the original dataset's inter-annotator agreement scores, would provide a more nuanced interpretation of the results and the task's inherent difficulty. This context is important when evaluating small performance gains.

4.  **Baselines and Analysis:** The result that a neural model outperforms a feature-engineered SVM is expected in the current research climate. The contribution could be strengthened by including simpler neural baselines. For instance, comparing the proposed multi-layer architecture to a single-layer LSTM would help isolate the contribution of the additional dense layers and provide a better understanding of the complexity required for this task. Additionally, the observation that the model favors precision over recall is interesting and warrants further analysis. Exploring why the architecture exhibits this bias could lead to valuable insights.

In summary, this paper tackles an interesting problem and presents a reasonable neural approach. However, the claims of improvement are undermined by a flawed evaluation methodology. I would be willing to reconsider my rating if the authors provide results based on a proper experimental setup (model selection on the dev set) and include significance testing.
