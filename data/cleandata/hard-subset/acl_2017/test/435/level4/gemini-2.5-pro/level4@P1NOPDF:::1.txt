This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying whether the use of a connective indicates a causal relation. The authors' central hypothesis is that the expression of causality is too diverse for purely syntactic treatments. They argue that the more abstract representations learned by neural models are better suited for this classification task.

The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus. The results, while modest, consistently support the authors' central hypothesis and offer initial insights into how best to implement this approach. The TensorFlow models used for the experiments are included with the submission.

Some critical comments and questions:

*   The introduction is structured more like a literature review than an overview of the paper's contributions. This creates redundancy with the subsequent related work section. While non-standard introductions can be effective, this one falls short: despite surveying numerous ideas, it does not commit to a specific theory of causation or its expression. Instead, it focuses on the negative claim that causality is not reducible to syntax. The paper's positive contributions are not clearly articulated until the final, very general paragraph of the section.

*   Building on the previous point, it is disappointing that the paper lacks a clear theoretical commitment to a definition of causation. The authors appear to default to a counterfactual view broadly similar to that of David Lewis, where causation is a modal sufficiency claim with additional counterfactual conditions. The implication arrow used in the notation on line 238 (and following) must represent a very specific kind of implication for this to work, and Lewis's theory has well-known problems. The authors state elsewhere that they do not endorse the counterfactual view, which leaves the reader wondering what theory is being assumed. The temporal constraint mentioned on page 3 is insufficient on its own.

*   I did not understand the comments regarding the example on line 256. The authors seem to imply that they consider the sentence false. If the sentence is indeed true, it implies a causal link between the argument and the breakage. This raises complex issues about event decomposition, which are central to causal theories but are not discussed here, leaving the authors' reasoning unclear.

*   The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram appears to depict only the "Pair_LSTM" variant of the model. More importantly, the diagram is needlessly imprecise. While it is acceptable to omit some standard model details from the text, the accompanying diagrams should have a clear and consistent semantics. For instance, the purpose of the empty circles between the input and the "LSTM" boxes is unclear. The text mentions a look-up layer and a GloVe layer, but it is not clear what follows, or how many layers of representation there are in total. The diagram is precise about the pooling tanh layers before the softmax layer, but lacks this clarity for earlier components. It is also unclear what the "LSTM" boxes represent. It appears that only the final hidden state is connected to the subsequent layers; this connection should be depicted explicitly.

*   The sentence beginning on line 480 is confusing. The models under discussion do not intrinsically require padding. Presumably, this is a practical requirement for efficient batch processing in TensorFlow. If so, this should be stated explicitly. The final clause of the sentence is particularly confusing. It is not clear how this implementation detail relates to "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning." The relevance of "convenience" here, and the connection to the semantics of causality, is obscure.

*   The authors find that a model with two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") performs somewhat better than a sequential model where the representation of the first argument is fed into the LSTM for the second. This finding is reminiscent of debates in the natural language inference literature regarding whether to encode the premise and hypothesis independently or sequentially. This remains an open question for NLI, and likely requires further investigation for causal relation detection as well. Therefore, the strong conclusion drawn in the sentence beginning on line 587 seems premature: "This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers." This conclusion is surprising, given that the two arguments are often sub-clauses of the same sentence and can be expected to share significant contextual information.

*   It is difficult to interpret the optimal hyperparameter settings across the different tasks (e.g., compare lines 578 and 636). The authors should comment on whether there is a meaningful interpretation of these differences, or if they should be attributed to the inherent stochasticity of training on this dataset.

*   Section 4.3 concludes with the claim that the proposed system, unlike that of Hidey and McKeown, can "correctly disambiguate its causal meaning" for the connective 'which then'. While this may be true, a single example is insufficient to substantiate this claim. To strengthen this point, the authors should test their model on a wider range of examples that exhibit this ambiguity and report on its performance. This would provide more robust evidence and address the possibility that the system was simply fortunate with the specific example in Table 8.
