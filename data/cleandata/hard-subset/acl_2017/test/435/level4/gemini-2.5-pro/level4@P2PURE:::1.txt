This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying whether the use of a connective indicates a causal relation. The central premise is that the linguistic expression of causality is too diverse for purely syntactic approaches. The authors argue that the abstract representations learned by neural models are better suited for this classification task.

Experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus. The results, while modest, consistently support the authors' hypothesis and offer initial insights into optimal model design for this task. The authors have also made their TensorFlow-based models publicly available.

Some critical comments and questions:

*   The introduction is structured more like a literature review than a standard overview of the paper's contributions, which creates redundancy with the subsequent related work section. While a non-standard introduction can be effective, this one fails to properly frame the paper. Despite reviewing numerous ideas, it does not commit to a specific theory of causation or its linguistic expression. Instead, it primarily makes a negative claim (that causation is not reducible to syntax) without clearly articulating the paper's positive contribution, which is only gestured at in the final paragraph.

*   A significant weakness is the paper's lack of a clear theoretical commitment regarding causation. The paper appears to default to a counterfactual view similar to that of David Lewis (e.g., line 238), which defines causation via modal sufficiency and other conditions. However, this framework has well-documented problems, and the implication denoted by the arrow on line 238 would need to be of a very specific kind. The authors' claims elsewhere that they do not endorse this view are confusing, as they fail to provide a clear alternative. The temporal constraint mentioned on page 3 is, by itself, insufficient to ground the work.

*   The authors' analysis of the example on line 256 is unclear. They seem to imply the sentence is false, yet a causal link between the argument and the breakage appears plausible. The discussion does not address underlying complexities, such as the decomposition of events, which are relevant to causal theories and leaves the reader confused about the authors' reasoning.

*   The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram depicts only the "Pair_LSTM" model variant. More importantly, the diagram lacks precision. For instance, the semantics of the empty circles between the input and the LSTM boxes are not explained. The prose mentions a look-up layer and a GloVe layer, but the total number of representational layers is ambiguous. Similarly, it is unclear what the "LSTM" boxes represent, as only the final hidden state appears to connect to subsequent layers. A more detailed diagram with a clear and consistent semantics, explicitly showing these connections, would significantly improve clarity.

*   The justification for padding provided in the sentence beginning on line 480 is confusing. The models themselves do not intrinsically require padding; this is likely a practical requirement for efficient batch processing in a framework like TensorFlow. If so, this should be stated directly. The final clause, which links this technical requirement to "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning," is particularly puzzling. It is not clear how a practical implementation detail like padding relates to conceptual questions of convenience or the semantic encoding of causality.

*   The authors find that a model with two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") outperforms a model where the representation of the first argument is fed into the LSTM for the second. This finding relates to an ongoing debate in the natural language inference (NLI) literature about whether to encode premise and hypothesis independently or sequentially. Given that this remains an open question in NLI, the strong conclusion drawn in the sentence beginning on line 587 seems premature for causal relations as well. The claim that this result "means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold" is surprising, especially since the two arguments are sub-clauses of a single sentence and are likely to share significant contextual information.

*   The optimal hyperparameter settings appear to vary significantly across different tasks (e.g., compare line 578 with line 636). The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of whether this variation has a meaningful interpretation or if it should be attributed to the inherent stochasticity of model training and data interaction.

*   In Section 4.3, the authors claim their system can correctly disambiguate the causal meaning of the connective 'which then' in an instance where the Hidey and McKeown (2016) system fails. However, this claim is substantiated by only a single example. To provide more robust evidence, the authors should test the model on a wider range of examples that exhibit this ambiguity. This would help determine if the model has genuinely learned to handle this construction or if the reported success on the example in Table 8 was fortuitous.
