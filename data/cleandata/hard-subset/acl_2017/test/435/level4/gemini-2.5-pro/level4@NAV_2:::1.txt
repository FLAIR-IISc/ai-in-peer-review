### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a neural network approach, based on Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units, for the task of causal meaning disambiguation. The central hypothesis is that the expression of causality in language is too varied for traditional syntactic or rule-based methods, and that the distributed representations learned by neural models are better suited for this classification task. The authors evaluate their model on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating modest but consistent improvements over existing baselines.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The application and evaluation of an LSTM-based architecture for classifying whether a lexical marker signals a causal relation. The results demonstrate the viability of this approach, outperforming the state-of-the-art on the chosen dataset.
2.  An empirical comparison of different architectural variants, such as using two independent LSTMs versus a single sequentially-connected one, providing initial insights into effective model design for this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated approach for a challenging problem:** The paper correctly identifies that causal relations are expressed through a diverse and open-ended set of linguistic constructions, making them difficult to capture with purely syntactic or feature-engineered systems. The proposal to use neural networks to learn more abstract representations of the context surrounding a potential causal marker is therefore well-founded and appropriate for the problem.
2.  **Positive Empirical Results:** The experiments, conducted on the AltLex corpus, show consistent if modest improvements over the previous state-of-the-art system. The proposed model achieves a better balance between precision and recall, suggesting it is more effective at correctly identifying true causal instances without introducing as many false positives.
3.  **Reproducibility:** The authors have included their TensorFlow-based models, which is a commendable practice that facilitates the verification of their results and enables future work to build directly upon their implementation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of a clear theoretical foundation for "causation":** The paper spends considerable time arguing against syntax-based theories of causation but fails to articulate a clear positive theory of what it assumes causation to be. While a counterfactual view is mentioned (and seemingly dismissed in some cases), the work does not commit to a specific definition. Without a clear semantic or pragmatic grounding, the task becomes a pattern-matching exercise on a specific dataset, making it difficult to assess the generality of the model's learned knowledge.
2.  **Unclear model description and diagram:** The model architecture is not described with sufficient precision. Figure 1, which is meant to illustrate the "Pair LSTM" architecture, is ambiguous. It is unclear what the empty circles between the input embeddings and the LSTM boxes represent, how many layers of representation exist, or how exactly the LSTM outputs are utilized (e.g., is it only the final hidden state that is passed on?). This lack of clarity hinders a full understanding of the proposed model.
3.  **Overstated conclusions about model architecture:** The paper concludes that encoding the two events independently ("Pair LSTM") is superior to a sequential encoding ("Stated Pair LSTM") based on small performance differences. This claim seems premature. In related tasks like Natural Language Inference, the optimal way to combine premise and hypothesis representations is still an open question. Given that the two events are part of the same sentence and may share significant context, it is surprising that a fully independent encoding would be definitively better. This result may be an artifact of the dataset or training setup rather than a generalizable finding.
4.  **Flawed introduction and paper structure:** The introduction functions more as a literature review, making it redundant with the "Related Work" section. More importantly, it focuses almost exclusively on a negative argument (what causation is not) and fails to adequately frame the paper's specific goals and positive contributions, which are only briefly mentioned in the final paragraph.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the specific theory or definition of causation your work is based on? The paper effectively critiques other theories (syntactic, counterfactual) but does not seem to adopt a concrete alternative, aside from a temporal constraint.
2.  Could you provide a more precise description of the model architecture, perhaps by clarifying Figure 1? Specifically, what layers exist between the word embedding lookup and the LSTM? And is it only the final hidden state of each LSTM that is used by the subsequent dense layers?
3.  The discussion of padding strategies seems motivated by implementation details (e.g., requirements for efficient batching in TensorFlow). Is there a deeper theoretical or linguistic reason to believe that the choice of padding length (max, mean, mode) relates to "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning"?
4.  The analysis in Section 4.3 makes a strong claim about the model's ability to disambiguate the connective 'which then' based on a single example. To substantiate this, could you provide a more systematic analysis, perhaps by testing the model on a curated set of ambiguous examples, to show that this is a robust capability of the model and not an anecdotal success?
