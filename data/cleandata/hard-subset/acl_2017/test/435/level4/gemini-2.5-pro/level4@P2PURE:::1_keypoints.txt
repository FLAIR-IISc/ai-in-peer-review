- Presents an LSTM-based model to classify whether a connective signals a causal relation, arguing neural representations outperform purely syntactic approaches.  
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus yield modest but consistent support for the authors’ hypothesis; TensorFlow code is released.  
- Introduction reads like a literature review and fails to clearly frame the paper’s contributions or commit to a specific theoretical stance.  
- Paper lacks a clear theoretical commitment on causation: it ambiguously invokes a Lewis-style counterfactual view while disclaiming it, leaving the grounding of the work unclear.  
- Analysis of the example around line 256 is confusing and ignores event decomposition and other causal-theory complexities.  
- Figure 1 caption is misleading and the diagram is imprecise: unclear semantics for nodes, layers, and LSTM connections.  
- Justification for padding is confusing and appears to conflate a practical batching requirement with a claim about encoding causal meaning.  
- The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential encoding is interesting but the strong conclusion about event-meaning relations is premature given parallels to open NLI debates.  
- Significant variation in optimal hyperparameters across tasks is reported but not discussed—unclear if this reflects meaningful differences or training stochasticity.  
- The claim of correctly disambiguating the connective “which then” is based on a single example and needs broader evaluation to be convincing.
