This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves classifying the relationship between two events connected by a potential lexical marker as either causal or non-causal. The authors propose a neural network architecture based on Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units to encode the context of the two events. The core hypothesis is that a deep representation of the sentential context is necessary to resolve the ambiguity of many lexical markers. The proposed system, which independently encodes the two event clauses before classification, is evaluated on the AltLex corpus. The results show a modest improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, a feature-based SVM model.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that a standard LSTM-based neural architecture can outperform a feature-engineered system for the task of causal meaning disambiguation on the AltLex dataset. The model effectively leverages pre-trained word embeddings to encode the context surrounding the potential causal relation.
2.  A qualitative analysis that provides concrete examples where the proposed model correctly handles ambiguous markers that were misclassified by the prior state-of-the-art system. This analysis supports the authors' claim regarding the model's ability to learn from contextual cues beyond simple lexical triggers.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on the AltLex corpus, surpassing the performance of the system introduced by the corpus creators (Hidey and McKeown, 2016). The evaluation is thorough, covering both the original and the "bootstrapped" versions of the dataset, and the reported improvements, while modest, are consistent across different configurations.
2.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, with a clear and well-motivated introduction to the problem of causality in language. The authors effectively situate their work within the relevant linguistic and computational literature, making the paper easy to follow for readers familiar with the domain.
3.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The error analysis presented in Table 8 is a significant strength. By showing specific examples where the model succeeds despite misleading cues (e.g., a causative verb like "break" used in a non-causal context), the authors provide compelling evidence that their model is learning deeper contextual patterns rather than relying on surface-level features.
4.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors are to be commended for including their code and data with the submission. This practice is crucial for verifying the results and enabling future work to build directly upon this research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Evaluation Methodology:** The paper's central claim of outperforming the baseline seems to be based on selecting the best model configuration after observing performance on the test set. A fair comparison requires that model selection (i.e., choosing the architecture, optimizer, and padding strategy) be performed on a dedicated development set, with the final reported score being from a single run on the held-out test set. Without this, the results may be overly optimistic.
2.  **Significance and Novelty:** The reported F1-score improvements are relatively small. The paper lacks statistical significance tests, making it difficult to ascertain whether the gains are meaningful or simply due to chance. Furthermore, the finding that a standard LSTM-based model can outperform a traditional feature-engineered SVM is a well-established pattern in NLP. The architectural novelty is limited, and the paper would be stronger if it included simpler neural baselines (e.g., a feed-forward network over averaged embeddings) to better justify the chosen complexity.
3.  **Task Framing and Positioning:** The paper could do a better job of positioning this work relative to the established task of implicit discourse relation recognition. An explicit discussion of the similarities and differences, and an explanation of why methods from that field are not directly compared against, would strengthen the paper's contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify the protocol for model selection? Was a development set used to determine the best-performing configuration (e.g., "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta") before evaluating on the test set? If the best configuration was chosen based on test set performance, the reported improvements may not be robust.
2.  The paper mentions that the "bootstrapping" process extends the corpus but does not detail what this process entails. Could you briefly explain this process and how it might impact the data distribution, particularly regarding the lexical markers that appear in the test set but not in the training set?
3.  Your analysis notes that the proposed neural models tend to achieve higher precision but lower recall than the SVM baseline. Do you have any hypothesis as to why the LSTM architecture exhibits this particular precision/recall trade-off on this task?
4.  The provided data files appear to use a 0-1-2 labeling scheme, whereas the paper describes a binary classification task. Could you please clarify this discrepancy?
