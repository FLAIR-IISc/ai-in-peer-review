This paper presents a neural network approach for detecting causal relations between clauses, particularly in cases where the relation is not explicitly marked by a discourse connective. The authors leverage the recently introduced AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) for this task. The proposed model employs an LSTM to encode clauses, which are initialized with GloVe embeddings. The LSTM output is then passed through a multi-layer perceptron with a final softmax layer for classification. The system reports a modest improvement in F1-score over the previous state-of-the-art, an SVM-based method from the creators of the dataset.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-written and provides a particularly clear motivation and problem description. A key strength is the inclusion of both quantitative and qualitative analyses. The error analysis, which includes examples where the system correctly generalizes beyond patterns seen in the training data (e.g., handling indicator words that appear in non-causal contexts in the test set), is insightful and demonstrates a good understanding of the model's behavior.

Furthermore, the authors are to be commended for submitting their code and data, which significantly aids reproducibility and allows for a deeper inspection of their work.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

Despite its clarity and solid presentation, the paper has several weaknesses that temper the significance of its contribution.

My primary concern revolves around the experimental methodology. The paper appears to report results for the best-performing model configuration selected on the *test set*. This practice is methodologically unsound, as it risks overfitting to the test data and invalidates the comparison to the baseline. The proper approach is to select the best hyperparameters based on performance on the development set and then report the score of that single, pre-selected configuration on the test set. Without results from this standard procedure, it is difficult to assess the true performance of the proposed model.

Secondly, the reported improvements in F1-score (around 0.5-1.5% over the baseline, depending on the configuration) are marginal. The paper would be substantially strengthened by the inclusion of statistical significance tests to determine if these gains are meaningful. Furthermore, the discussion of results should account for the potential noise in the gold-standard annotations. As the AltLex dataset is derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia alignments, it may contain imperfect labels. This annotation noise means that small performance differences between systems might not be practically significant, a point that warrants discussion.

Finally, while the application of neural networks to this task is a logical next step, the results largely confirm the existing trend of neural models outperforming traditional feature-engineered systems on such classification tasks. The contribution would be more compelling with a more thorough ablation study or comparison to simpler neural baselines. For instance, including results from a 1-layer LSTM or even a simpler bag-of-words model would help isolate the performance contribution of the specific architectural choices.

**Questions and Points for Clarification**

1.  **Relation to Implicit Discourse Relation Classification:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this task differs from the related task of implicit discourse relation classification (e.g., in the Penn Discourse TreeBank). Clarifying why methods from that domain are not directly applicable or were not used as baselines would provide valuable context.

2.  **Data Bootstrapping:** The description of the data processing is mostly clear, but the "bootstrapping" step, which reportedly extends the corpus by 15%, is not explained. A brief description of this process should be added to the paper.

3.  **Experimental Details:** As mentioned in the weaknesses, please clarify how the final model configuration was selected. If it was based on the test set, the experiments should be re-run by selecting the best model on the development set.

4.  **Result Analysis:** The analysis of results could be deepened. For instance, the authors could investigate why their neural method appears to favor precision over recall compared to the baseline, which could reveal interesting properties of the model.

5.  **Data Details:** Two minor points on the provided supplementary materials:
    *   Could you clarify the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV data files, given that the paper describes a binary classification task?
    *   Please confirm that you have the rights to redistribute the AltLex dataset.
