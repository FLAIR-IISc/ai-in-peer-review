This paper proposes an LSTM-based model to classify whether discourse connectives signal a causal relation. The central hypothesis is that the expression of causality is too diverse for traditional syntactic approaches, making the abstract representations learned by neural models more suitable for this task. The authors evaluate their model on the AltLex corpus, and the results, while modest, offer consistent support for this hypothesis. The paper also provides initial insights into model architecture for this task and includes the TensorFlow code used for the experiments.

While the core idea is well-motivated and the work is a valuable contribution, the paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing the following points:

**Comments and Questions:**

1.  **Introduction and Framing:** The introduction is structured more like a literature review than an overview of the paper's specific contributions, which leads to redundancy with the subsequent Related Work section. More importantly, while the introduction effectively argues against purely syntactic approaches to causality, it fails to establish a clear theoretical stance on what causation *is* or how the authors propose to model it. The paper's positive contribution is not clearly articulated until the final paragraph, leaving the reader without a clear roadmap from the outset.

2.  **Theory of Causation:** A significant weakness is the lack of a clearly articulated theory of causation. The paper seems to implicitly adopt a counterfactual view similar to that of David Lewis (e.g., line 238), where causation is framed as a special kind of modal implication. This is problematic, as Lewis's theory has well-documented issues, and the paper does not address how the proposed model navigates these. At other points, the authors seem to distance themselves from the counterfactual view without offering a coherent alternative. A theory of causality cannot be reduced to a simple temporal constraint (as mentioned on page 3), and the paper would be much stronger if it explicitly defined the theoretical framework it operates within.

3.  **Clarity of Example (line 256):** The discussion of the example on line 256 is confusing. The authors appear to argue that the sentence is false, but their reasoning is unclear. If the sentence is indeed true, a causal link between the argument and the breakage would be expected. The discussion does not address the relevant complexities, such as the division of events into sub-events, which are central to such causal judgments. Clarification on this point is needed.

4.  **Model Diagram (Figure 1):** The model diagram in Figure 1 needs significant improvement.
    *   The caption is misleading, as it fails to mention that the diagram depicts only the "Pair\_LSTM" variant.
    *   More critically, the diagram lacks precision. It is unclear what the empty circles between the input and the LSTM boxes represent, or how many layers of representation exist between the initial lookup/Glove layer and the LSTMs.
    *   The "LSTM" boxes themselves are ambiguous; it appears only the final hidden state is used by the subsequent layers, but this connection should be depicted explicitly. While prose can omit some standard model details, a diagram should have a clear and consistent semantics.

5.  **Justification for Padding (line 480):** The sentence beginning on line 480 regarding padding is confusing. The need for padding is typically an implementation detail for efficient batch processing in frameworks like TensorFlow, rather than an intrinsic property of the model architecture. If this is the case, it should be stated as such. Furthermore, the connection drawn in the final clause between this implementation detail and "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning" is obscure. It is not clear how padding relates to the semantics of causality or why convenience is a relevant theoretical criterion here.

6.  **Conclusion about Independent LSTMs:** The finding that the "Stated\_LSTM" model (with two independent LSTMs) outperforms the sequential model is interesting and echoes similar architectural debates in the Natural Language Inference (NLI) literature. However, the conclusion drawn from this result (line 587) is far too strong. The authors claim this "means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold." This single experimental result is insufficient to prove such a general claim. The question of how to best model the interaction between two related text spans remains an open research question, and it is surprising to suggest complete independence is optimal, especially for clauses within the same sentence that likely share context.

7.  **Hyperparameter Consistency:** The hyperparameters reported for the best-performing models appear inconsistent across different tasks (e.g., comparing lines 578 and 636). Is there a meaningful interpretation for these differences, or should this variation be attributed to the inherent stochasticity of the training process and model-data interactions? Some discussion on this point would be beneficial.

8.  **Unsupported Claim of Disambiguation:** The claim at the end of Section 4.3 that the model can "correctly disambiguate the causal meaning" of connectives like 'which then', where a previous model fails, is based on a single example. To substantiate such a claim, a more rigorous analysis is required. The authors should consider creating a small test suite of ambiguous examples to evaluate the model's performance systematically and demonstrate that the success shown in Table 8 is not an outlier.
