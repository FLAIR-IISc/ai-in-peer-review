This paper proposes a neural network model for detecting causal relations between clauses when no explicit connective is present. The authors leverage the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) for this binary classification task (causal vs. non-causal). Arguing that LSTMs are better suited than CNNs for representing clausal features, they propose an architecture consisting of a GloVe-based word representation layer, an LSTM layer, three densely-connected tanh layers, and a final softmax classification layer. Although the authors refer to this as "deep learning," the network itself is not particularly deep.

The system's best configuration achieves a 0.5-1.5% F1 improvement over the SVM-based classifier of Hidey and McKeown (2016). The quantitative results are supported by a welcome qualitative analysis, which includes compelling examples of the system's ability to generalize. For instance, the model correctly identifies non-causal uses of indicator words that appeared exclusively in causal contexts within the training data. The combination of quantitative and qualitative analysis is appreciated.

The paper is well-written, with a particularly clear problem description. However, its contribution would be better contextualized by a clarification of the differences between this task and the more established task of implicit connective recognition. A discussion of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable here would be particularly valuable.

It is commendable that the authors included their code and data with the submission. A few points regarding the data and its preparation require clarification:
*   The meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the provided TSV files is unclear, as the paper describes a binary classification task.
*   Since the data is from Hidey and McKeown (2016), the authors should confirm they have the right to redistribute it.
*   The paper should explain the "bootstrapping" process, which reportedly extends the corpus by 15%, as this procedure is mentioned but not described.

While it is interesting to see neural networks applied to this task, the merits of the proposed system and the reported improvements are not entirely convincing due to several issues.

First, and most critically, the experimental methodology appears flawed. The paper seems to report results for the best-performing configuration on the *test set* (among 4-7 options), which is then used to claim an improvement over the baseline. A fair comparison requires that model selection and hyperparameter tuning be performed exclusively on the development set.

Second, the significance of the small F1-score improvement is difficult to assess. The authors should report statistical significance tests to validate their claims. Furthermore, given that the gold-standard annotations are derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia alignments, the reliability of the labels themselves should be considered. Upon inspection, this annotation process is imperfect, and any noise in the gold standard means that small performance differences should be interpreted with caution.

Finally, the paper's central finding—that a neural model can outperform a feature-engineered SVM—confirms a well-established trend in NLP and is not surprising in itself. To better isolate the contributions of the specific architecture, the experiments would be strengthened by including simpler neural baselines, such as a single-layer LSTM. A more in-depth analysis of the results would also be beneficial; for example, the authors could investigate and discuss why their neural approach appears to favor precision over recall compared to the baseline.
