This paper proposes a neural network model for detecting causal relations between clauses, particularly when such relations are not explicitly marked by a connective. The authors leverage the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) for this task. Arguing that LSTMs are better suited than CNNs for representing the relevant clausal features, the authors develop an architecture that processes GloVe embeddings with an LSTM layer, followed by three fully-connected layers with tanh activations and a final softmax classification layer. While the authors refer to this as "deep learning," the network, as in many similar studies, is not particularly deep.

The best-performing configuration of the proposed system achieves an F1 score improvement of 0.5-1.5% over the SVM-based classifier of Hidey and McKeown (2016). The paper's inclusion of both quantitative results and a qualitative analysis is a strength. For instance, the authors provide several examples where the system successfully generalizes, correctly identifying indicator words as non-causal in the test set even when they were always causal in the training data.

The paper is well-written, with a particularly clear description of the problem. However, the paper would benefit from a clearer distinction between the proposed task and the related task of implicit connective recognition. This discussion could include an explanation of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition may not be suitable for this problem.

The authors are to be commended for submitting their source code. The inclusion of the dataset with the code is also helpful for understanding the task through examples. However, several points regarding the supplementary materials require clarification:
*   The meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the provided TSV data files is unclear, given that the paper describes a binary classification task.
*   The paper should clarify the meaning of "bootstrapping," a process which apparently extended the corpus by about 15% but is not explained.
*   Given that the data originates from Hidey and McKeown (2016), the authors should confirm that they have the right to redistribute it.

While experimenting with neural networks on this task is a worthwhile endeavor, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. A major concern is that the best system configuration appears to have been selected based on performance on the test set. This configuration is then reported to achieve a "2.13% F1" improvement over the baseline. A fair comparison requires model selection to be performed on the development set.

Furthermore, the significance of the reported improvement is not clearly established. First, given the size of the dataset, statistical significance tests should be performed to validate the results. Second, the reliability of the gold-standard annotation itself, which is derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia, should be considered. Imperfections in the annotation suggest that small differences in scores should be interpreted with caution.

Finally, the finding that neural methods can outperform models based on hand-crafted features on binary classification tasks is largely a confirmation of an established trend. The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including simpler neural baselines, such as a single-layer LSTM, to better isolate the contributions of the proposed architecture. The analysis could also be improved by investigating why the neural method appears to favor precision over recall.
