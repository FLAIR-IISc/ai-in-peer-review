### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the task of classifying causal vs. non-causal relations between two events, particularly when signaled by ambiguous lexical markers. The authors argue that encoding the semantic context of the events is crucial for this disambiguation task. They propose a neural network architecture that uses LSTMs to create representations of the two events and the connecting lexical marker. The text segments are first represented using pre-trained GloVe embeddings, then processed by parallel LSTMs. The resulting vectors are concatenated and passed through several dense layers before a final softmax classification layer. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform the previous state-of-the-art system, which was based on a feature-engineered SVM.

### Contributions

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The primary contribution is an empirical one: the paper demonstrates that a standard LSTM-based architecture can achieve a new state-of-the-art result on the AltLex dataset for causal relation disambiguation. This result confirms that neural context encoding is an effective strategy for this task, surpassing a strong feature-engineered baseline.
2.  **Qualitative Analysis:** A secondary contribution is the analysis showing the model's ability to generalize beyond simple cues. The paper provides concrete examples where the model correctly classifies instances containing lexical markers that are highly biased toward one class in the training data, demonstrating that it learns from the wider sentential context rather than just memorizing marker distributions.

### Strengths

1.  **Improved Performance on a Challenging Task:** The proposed model establishes a new state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus. While the F1 score improvements are modest (around 0.5-2%), any gain on this task is valuable. The analysis shows the system achieves a better balance between precision and recall than the previous system, which is a desirable property.
2.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, with a clear introduction that effectively motivates the problem of causal disambiguation by discussing the limitations of purely syntactic or lexical approaches. The description of the task and the proposed method is easy to follow.
3.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The inclusion of a qualitative analysis (Table 8) is a significant strength. By showing specific examples where the proposed model succeeds and the baseline fails, the authors provide compelling evidence for their central claim that context encoding is key. This analysis offers valuable insight into the model's capabilities beyond what aggregate metrics can show.
4.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code available with the submission. This is a commendable practice that facilitates verification of the results and encourages future work to build upon this method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Methodological Concerns Regarding Model Selection:** The most significant weakness concerns the experimental methodology. The paper evaluates a number of architectural variations (e.g., "Pair LSTM" vs. "Stated Pair LSTM"), padding strategies, and optimizers. However, it appears that the best-performing configuration was selected based on its performance on the *test set*. This is a serious methodological issue, as it risks overfitting to the test data and invalidates the central claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art. A proper experimental setup would involve selecting the best model configuration on a separate development set before a single, final evaluation on the test set.
2.  **Limited Novelty of the Approach:** The architectural novelty of the proposed model is limited. The use of LSTMs to encode text for a downstream classification task is a well-established approach. The main finding—that a neural model without handcrafted features can outperform a system based on them—confirms a general and widely accepted trend in NLP. While a valuable empirical result for this specific task, the work does not introduce a novel neural architecture or technique.
3.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The reported improvements in F1-score over the baseline are relatively small. Given the size of the dataset and the potential for noise in the automatic annotation process used to create it, it is essential to demonstrate that these improvements are statistically significant. Without such tests, it is difficult to confidently conclude that the proposed model is genuinely superior to the baseline.
4.  **Insufficient Positioning Relative to Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of how this task relates to the well-studied problem of *implicit discourse relation recognition* (e.g., in the PDTB framework and CoNLL shared tasks). While the AltLex corpus focuses on a wider variety of lexical markers, the core challenge of using context to classify a relation is very similar. The paper does not explain why methods from that line of research were not considered as baselines or points of comparison.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify your model selection procedure? Was a development set used to choose the best architecture, padding strategy, and optimizer (e.g., "Pair LSTM Mean Adam") before evaluating on the final test set? If the test set was used for model selection, the reported results may not be a reliable measure of generalization performance.
2.  Could you elaborate on the relationship between your task and the task of implicit discourse relation recognition? Why were prominent neural methods from that research area not considered as baselines for comparison?
3.  The "bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus is used in your experiments, but the paper only briefly mentions its existence without detailing the process. Could you provide a brief explanation of how this bootstrapping was performed to extend the original corpus?
