- LSTM-based approach for causal meaning disambiguation evaluated on the AltLex corpus, showing modest but consistent improvements.  
- Contribution: applies an LSTM architecture to classify whether a lexical marker signals a causal relation, outperforming prior state-of-the-art on the dataset.  
- Contribution: empirically compares architectural variants (two independent LSTMs vs sequentially-connected) to provide initial model-design insights.  
- Strength: Well-motivated—neural models can learn abstract representations for the diverse linguistic expressions of causality where syntactic/rule-based methods struggle.  
- Strength: Positive empirical results—better balance of precision and recall and consistent improvements over previous systems.  
- Strength: Reproducibility—TensorFlow models released to facilitate verification and future work.  
- Weakness: No clear theoretical definition of "causation" adopted; paper critiques other theories but lacks a positive semantic/pragmatic grounding.  
- Weakness: Model description and Figure 1 are ambiguous—unclear what certain diagram elements mean, number of layers, and which LSTM outputs are used.  
- Weakness: Overstated architectural claims—asserting independent encoding superiority based on small performance differences may be premature or dataset-specific.  
- Weakness: Flawed introduction and structure—introduction reads like related work, focuses on negatives, and fails to clearly frame goals and contributions.  
- Question: Which specific theory or definition of causation does the work adopt beyond critiquing others and mentioning a temporal constraint?  
- Question: Please clarify the model architecture (Figure 1), layers between embeddings and LSTM, and whether only final hidden states feed the dense layers.  
- Question: Is there a linguistic/theoretical rationale for the chosen padding strategies (max/mean/mode) beyond batching/implementation convenience?  
- Question: Can the authors provide a systematic analysis (e.g., curated ambiguous examples) to substantiate the model’s claimed ability to disambiguate cases like "which then" rather than relying on a single example?
