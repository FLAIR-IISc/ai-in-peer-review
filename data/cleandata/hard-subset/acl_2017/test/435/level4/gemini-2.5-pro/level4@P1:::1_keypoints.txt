- Paper proposes an LSTM-based model to classify whether lexical markers signal causality, arguing neural representations better capture diverse causal expressions; evaluated on AltLex and shows modest improvements over prior work.
- Strength: Hypothesis that neural, abstract representations handle diverse linguistic causality better than fixed syntactic patterns is well-motivated.
- Strength: Experimental results are modest but consistent, providing a solid first step for neural approaches to this task.
- Strength: Authors release TensorFlow code/models, enhancing reproducibility.
- Weakness: Lacks a clear conceptual framework or explicit definition of causation; contradictory treatment of counterfactual notions creates theoretical ambiguity.
- Weakness: Model architecture is insufficiently specified (Figure 1 misleading, missing layer counts and LSTM output usage), hindering comprehension and reproducibility.
- Weakness: Claims that independently encoding arguments is superior are overstated given only a small performance advantage and limited evidence.
- Weakness: Error analysis is anecdotal (single example for ambiguous connective) and insufficient to support claims about the model learning disambiguation.
- Question: Please clarify the claim about the human mental model not relating an argument to a broken window in the provided example—readers may find that causal chain plausible.
- Question: Clarify the motivation for framing padding strategies as encoding causal meaning versus an implementation detail for batching.
- Question: Explain why optimal hyperparameters and zero-padding strategies differ between non-bootstrapped and bootstrapped corpora—systematic reason or training variability?
