This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying whether the use of a connective implies a causal relation. The central hypothesis is that causal relations are expressed in diverse ways that are difficult to capture with syntactic approaches. The authors argue that the abstract representations learned by neural models are better suited for this classification task.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus. The results provide modest but consistent support for the proposed approach and offer initial insights into how to best architect a model for this task. The authors have also made their TensorFlow-based models available, which is a welcome contribution.

While the paper addresses an interesting problem with a modern approach, several aspects require clarification and improvement.

**Major Comments:**

*   **Introduction and Framing:** The introduction's structure is unconventional, functioning more as a literature review than a traditional overview of the paper's contributions. This structure results in redundancy with the subsequent Related Work section. More importantly, while the introduction effectively argues against purely syntactic approaches to causation, it fails to establish a clear theoretical stance on what causation *is* or how it is expressed. The paper's positive contribution is only vaguely gestured at in the final paragraph, leaving the reader without a clear roadmap of the proposed method and its theoretical grounding.

*   **Theory of Causation:** A significant weakness of the paper is its lack of a clearly articulated theory of causation. The authors appear to default to a counterfactual view, reminiscent of Lewis, where causation is framed as a modal sufficiency claim (line 238 and following). For this framework to be viable, the implication operator used would require a very specific interpretation, and this counterfactual view is known to have limitations. The paper is inconsistent on this point, as other sections suggest a rejection of the counterfactual view. If that is the case, the alternative theoretical foundation is not specified, beyond a brief mention of a temporal constraint, which is insufficient on its own.

*   **Model Architecture and Diagram:** The model architecture, as depicted in Figure 1, requires clarification. Firstly, the caption is misleading as it seems to describe only the "Pair_LSTM" variant. More critically, the diagram lacks precision. For instance, the function of the empty circles between the input and the LSTM boxes is not explained. The text mentions a look-up layer and a GloVe layer, but the subsequent number and type of layers before the LSTM are not specified in either the text or the diagram. Furthermore, the representation of the "LSTM" boxes is ambiguous. It appears that only the final hidden state is passed to the subsequent layers, but this connection should be depicted explicitly for clarity.

*   **Architectural Conclusions:** The finding that two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") outperform a sequential model is interesting. This architectural choice echoes debates in the natural language inference (NLI) literature about whether to encode premise and hypothesis independently or sequentially. Given this context, the strong conclusion drawn in the sentence beginning on line 587 is premature. The claim that this result "means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold" seems overstated. The question of independent vs. sequential encoding is still an open research question in related tasks, and it is surprising that complete independence would be optimal for related clauses within a single sentence that often share context.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**

*   **Example on Line 256:** The discussion of the example on line 256 is unclear. The authors seem to imply the sentence is false, but if it were true, a causal link would be expected between the argument and the breakage. The commentary does not address underlying issues like event decomposition, which could be relevant, making the authors' point difficult to grasp.

*   **Justification for Padding (Line 480):** The sentence beginning on line 480 regarding padding is confusing. LSTMs do not intrinsically require padding; this is typically a practical requirement for efficient batch processing in frameworks like TensorFlow. The paper should state this explicitly if it is the case. The final clause of the sentence, which links this implementation detail to "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning," is particularly puzzling. The connection between batching convenience and the semantic encoding of causality is not apparent and requires justification.

*   **Hyperparameter Analysis:** The reported hyperparameters for the best-performing models appear inconsistent across different experimental settings (e.g., comparing lines 578 and 636). The paper would benefit from a discussion on whether there is a meaningful interpretation of these differences or if they should be attributed to the inherent instability of training on this dataset.

*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative analysis in Section 4.3 makes a strong claim about the model's ability to disambiguate the causal meaning of "which then" based on a single example. To substantiate this claim, the authors should perform a more thorough analysis. This could involve creating a small challenge set of examples that highlight the ambiguity of this connective and reporting the model's performance on this set. This would provide more robust evidence and clarify whether the success on the example in Table 8 was an isolated case.
