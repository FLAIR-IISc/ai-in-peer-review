This paper presents a method for identifying causal relationships between clauses using a neural network architecture. While termed "deep learning," the network's depth is modest, which is common in related studies. The work focuses on instances where a relation is not explicitly marked by an unambiguous connective (such as 'because'). To address this, the authors employ the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to classify relations as either causal or non-causal. The proposed model is justified by the argument that LSTMs are better suited than CNNs for capturing relevant clausal features. The architecture processes GloVe embeddings with an LSTM layer, followed by three tanh-activated dense layers and a final softmax classification layer.

The proposed system, in its best configuration, achieves a 0.5-1.5% F1 improvement over the SVM-based classifier from Hidey and McKeown (2016). The paper provides a commendable quantitative and qualitative analysis, including examples where the system correctly generalizes. For instance, it correctly identifies indicator words as non-causal in the test set, even when those same words were exclusively causal in the training data.

The manuscript is well-written, with a particularly lucid explanation of the problem domain. However, the submission would benefit from a more explicit clarification of how this task differs from implicit connective recognition. A discussion on the inapplicability of prior methods from that area to the current problem would be particularly valuable.

The authors are to be commended for providing their code and the associated data with the submission, which is a valuable practice for reproducibility. A few points regarding these materials require clarification. The meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the provided TSV files is unclear, as the paper describes a binary classification task. It is also worth confirming whether the authors have the appropriate rights to redistribute the Hidey and McKeown dataset. Additionally, the paper should elaborate on the "bootstrapping" procedure, which is mentioned as extending the corpus by approximately 15% but is not explained in detail.

Although the application of neural networks to this task is interesting, the claimed advantages of the proposed system are not fully convincing due to concerns about the experimental methodology. Specifically, it appears that the optimal system configuration was selected based on its performance on the test set. This configuration is then reported as achieving the "2.13% F1" improvement over the baseline. A standard and fair evaluation, however, would require model selection and hyperparameter tuning to be performed exclusively on the development set.

Furthermore, the significance of the reported performance gains is questionable. Given the dataset size, statistical significance tests should be conducted to validate the improvement. Additionally, the reliability of the gold-standard annotations warrants discussion. A brief inspection suggests that the annotations sourced from Wikipedia are imperfect, which implies that small performance differences may not be meaningful and should be interpreted with caution.

Finally, the finding that a neural approach surpasses a method based on engineered features for this type of classification task is, to some extent, an expected outcome that confirms existing trends in the field. To better isolate the contributions of the proposed architecture, it would be beneficial to include simpler neural baselines, such as a single-layer LSTM. The results analysis could also be deepened by investigating why the neural model appears to favor precision over recall.
