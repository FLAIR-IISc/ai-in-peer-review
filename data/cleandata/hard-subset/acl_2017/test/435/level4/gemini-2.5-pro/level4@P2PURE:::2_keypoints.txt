- Proposes an LSTM-based neural model (GloVe → LSTM → 3 fully-connected tanh layers → softmax) for detecting causal relations between clauses without explicit connectives using the AltLex dataset.  
- Authors argue LSTMs are better than CNNs for this task, but the network is not particularly deep despite being called "deep learning."  
- Best system reports a modest F1 improvement (0.5–1.5%, cited also as 2.13%) over Hidey & McKeown (2016)'s SVM baseline.  
- Strength: paper includes quantitative results and useful qualitative analysis showing generalization (e.g., correctly treating training-only indicator words as non-causal in test).  
- Writing and problem description are clear, but the paper needs a clearer distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition, and why prior methods may not apply.  
- Authors provide source code and dataset, which aids reproducibility and example inspection.  
- Supplementary materials need clarification: unclear 0-1-2 coding in TSV files despite a binary task.  
- Supplementary materials need clarification: the “bootstrapping” process that increased the corpus by ~15% is not explained.  
- Legal/ethical clarification requested: confirm right to redistribute data originating from Hidey & McKeown (2016).  
- Major methodological concern: best model configuration appears chosen using the test set instead of the development set, biasing reported comparisons.  
- Statistical significance of reported improvements is not established; given dataset size and noisy annotations from Wikipedia/Simple English, small score differences should be treated cautiously.  
- Contribution is incremental: neural methods outperforming feature-based models is expected; paper should include simpler neural baselines (e.g., single-layer LSTM) to isolate architecture benefits.  
- Analysis could be strengthened by investigating why the neural model favors precision over recall.
