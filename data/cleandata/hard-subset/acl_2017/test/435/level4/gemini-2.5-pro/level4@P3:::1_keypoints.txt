1. Task & hypothesis: causal meaning disambiguation; authors argue LSTM-based context encoding better captures causation than fixed syntactic/lexical patterns.  
2. Proposed model: LSTM architecture using pre-trained embeddings, evaluated on AltLex and outperforming a feature-engineered baseline.  
3. Contribution: shows an effective neural model achieving state-of-the-art without complex feature engineering.  
4. Contribution: compares two architectures—Pair LSTM (independent) vs Stated Pair LSTM (state passed)—to explore event interaction modeling.  
5. Contribution: code is included to support reproducibility.  
6. Strength: empirical improvements over Hidey & McKeown (2016), e.g., +1.64% F1 and +6.04% accuracy on the non-bootstrapping set.  
7. Strength: methodological simplicity and better generalizability by avoiding reliance on lexical resources.  
8. Strength: thoughtful experimental design with reasonable baselines and hyperparameter/architecture exploration.  
9. Weakness: lacks a clear theoretical definition of "causation" and rejects counterfactuals without offering an alternative.  
10. Weakness: model description and Figure 1 are imprecise and omit the Stated Pair LSTM variant, hindering understanding and reproduction.  
11. Weakness: some conclusions are overstated—claims about encoder independence and successful disambiguation of 'which then' are weakly supported.  
12. Question: clarify the paper's theoretical definition/criteria for labeling a relation as "Causal."  
13. Question: provide a precise description/diagram of model layers and transformations between embeddings and LSTM inputs.  
14. Question: report statistical significance for Pair vs Stated Pair results and discuss whether findings generalize beyond AltLex.
