- Proposes an LSTM-based model to classify whether connective use signals causation, arguing neural representations better capture causality’s linguistic diversity than syntactic/rule-based methods.  
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support for the approach; public release of TensorFlow models is a valuable contribution.  
- Core idea is promising and initial results encouraging, but the paper needs stronger conceptual framing, experimental analysis, and clearer presentation.  
- Major weakness: lacks a clear theoretical/conceptual framework for causation—unclear what semantic/pragmatic properties the model is intended to learn and ambiguous stance on counterfactual theory.  
- Major weakness: overstates conclusions from architecture comparison—claim that independent encoding of arguments invalidates assumed relations is not sufficiently supported.  
- Figure 1 is unclear and potentially misleading; diagram and caption should explicitly show LSTM hidden states and connections to pooling/softmax.  
- Justification for padding is confusing and presented as semantic motivation when it is likely an implementation/detail convenience.  
- Discussion of the example on line 256 is hard to follow; reasoning about sentence falsity and event granularity is not clearly articulated.  
- Claim that the model disambiguates causal meaning of “which then” is supported by only a single example; needs broader analysis of ambiguous cases.  
- Hyperparameters vary considerably across settings; authors should explain whether this reflects meaningful differences or stochasticity from small data.
