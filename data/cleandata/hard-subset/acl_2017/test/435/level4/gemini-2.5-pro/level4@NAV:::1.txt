This work presents an LSTM-based model for classifying the use of connectives to determine if a causal relation is implied. The central premise is that the expression of causality is highly varied and cannot be adequately captured by syntactic methods; therefore, the more abstract representations produced by neural models are better suited for this classification task.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus from Hidley and McKeown. The results provide consistent, albeit modest, evidence for the paper's main hypothesis and offer preliminary insights into how to best implement this concept in a functional model. The paper also includes the TensorFlow-based models used in the experiments.

Critical comments and questions are as follows:

*   The introduction's structure is unconventional, functioning more as a literature survey than a direct overview of the paper's contents. This creates overlap with the subsequent related work section. While a non-standard introduction could be acceptable, this one is ineffective. Despite reviewing numerous concepts, it fails to commit to a specific definition of causation or its expression, focusing instead on a negative argument (i.e., that causality is not reducible to syntax). The paper’s positive contribution is not clearly articulated until the section's final paragraph.

*   Extending the previous point, a notable weakness is the lack of clarity regarding the assumed theory of causation. The authors appear to adopt a counterfactual perspective similar to that of David Lewis, where causation is treated as a modal sufficiency claim with additional counterfactual conditions (see line 238 ff.). For this to be viable, the arrow notation used must represent a very specific type of implication, and Lewis's theory is known to have established issues. The paper also contains statements suggesting the authors do not endorse the counterfactual view, which is contradictory. This leaves the reader wondering what theoretical framework is actually being employed. The temporal constraint mentioned on page 3 is insufficient on its own.

*   The analysis of the example on line 256 is unclear. It appears the authors consider the sentence false. However, if the sentence is true, it implies the existence of a causal connection between the argument and the breakage. While related complexities exist regarding the division of events into sub-events, these are not discussed, which left me perplexed.

*   The caption for Figure 1 is inaccurate, as the diagram illustrates only the "Pair_LSTM" model variant. More significantly, the diagram lacks precision. Architectural diagrams require a clear and consistent interpretation. For instance, the purpose of the empty circles between the input and LSTM blocks is not explained. The prose indicates a look-up layer and a GloVe layer, but the number of subsequent representation layers is unclear. The diagram is specific about the pooling tanh layers before the softmax but not about the preceding layers. The representation of the "LSTM" blocks themselves is also ambiguous; it seems only the final hidden state is passed to the subsequent layers, and this connection should be explicitly visualized.

*   The reasoning in the sentence starting on line 480 is difficult to follow. The models described do not inherently require padding. I surmise that this is a practical constraint imposed by TensorFlow for efficient batch training. If so, this should be stated explicitly. The final clause, which links this implementation detail to "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning," is particularly confusing. The connection between technical convenience and the semantic representation of causality is not apparent.

*   The finding that two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") outperform a sequential architecture is interesting. This echoes ongoing debates in the natural language entailment literature regarding whether to process the premise and hypothesis independently or sequentially. Consequently, the strong conclusion drawn in the sentence starting on line 587 seems premature. This conclusion is especially surprising given that the inputs are components of a single sentence and are likely to share significant contextual information.

*   The optimal hyperparameters reported vary considerably across tasks (e.g., compare lines 578 and 636), making them difficult to interpret. It is unclear whether this variation has a systematic explanation or if it is simply an artifact of the complex interactions between the models and the data.

*   Section 4.3 claims that the system can "correctly disambiguate its causal meaning" for the connective 'which then' in a case where the Hidey and McKeown model fails. This claim is substantiated by only a single example, which is insufficient evidence. To validate this claim, the authors should test the model on a broader set of ambiguous examples involving this connective to determine if the reported success was incidental or represents a genuine capability of the model.
