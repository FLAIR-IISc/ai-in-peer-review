- Task & claim: Proposes an LSTM-based model to disambiguate causal vs. non-causal relations between two event clauses, claiming deep sentential context is needed and showing modest improvement on the AltLex corpus.  
- Contribution 1: Empirically shows a standard LSTM with pre-trained embeddings can outperform a feature-engineered SVM on AltLex.  
- Contribution 2: Provides qualitative examples where the model correctly disambiguates ambiguous markers that the prior SVM misclassified.  
- Strength: Strong empirical performance—state-of-the-art results on original and bootstrapped AltLex with consistent, if modest, improvements.  
- Strength: Clear motivation and presentation, well-situated in linguistic and computational literature.  
- Strength: Insightful qualitative error analysis (Table 8) demonstrating the model learns contextual patterns beyond lexical triggers.  
- Strength: Commits to reproducibility by including code and data.  
- Weakness: Evaluation methodology concerns—model selection appears to have been done using test-set performance rather than a held-out development set.  
- Weakness: Limited significance and novelty—small F1 gains without statistical tests, architectural novelty is minimal, and no simpler neural baselines are compared.  
- Weakness: Task framing needs improvement—insufficient positioning relative to implicit discourse relation recognition and lack of comparison to methods from that field.  
- Question: Clarify the model selection protocol—was a development set used or were configurations chosen based on test performance?  
- Question: Explain the bootstrapping process used to extend the corpus and how it affects data distribution and markers seen in test vs. training.  
- Question: Why do the neural models show higher precision but lower recall than the SVM—any hypotheses?  
- Question: Clarify the labeling scheme discrepancy—data files use 0-1-2 while the paper describes a binary task.
