This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying whether a connective use indicates a causal relation. The central motivation is that the expression of causality is linguistically diverse and semantically nuanced, making it difficult to capture with purely syntactic or rule-based methods. The authors argue that the more abstract representations learned by neural models are better suited for this task. Experiments conducted on the AltLex corpus offer modest but consistent support for this hypothesis and provide initial insights into effective model architectures. The public release of the TensorFlow models used in the experiments is a valuable contribution to the community.

While the core idea is promising and the initial results are encouraging, the paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing several issues related to its conceptual framing, experimental analysis, and presentation clarity.

**Major Comments**

1.  **Lack of a Clear Theoretical Framework for Causation:** The paper's primary weakness is its lack of a clear conceptual and theoretical grounding for its central concept: causation. The introduction functions more as a literature review than a motivated overview of the paper's specific contributions. It effectively argues against a purely syntactic approach but fails to articulate a positive theory of causation that the models are intended to capture. The authors seem to implicitly adopt a counterfactual view broadly similar to that of David Lewis (e.g., line 238), yet this is a complex and contested theory with known issues that the paper does not engage with. Elsewhere, the authors state they do not fully endorse this view, but they do not provide a clear alternative beyond a simple temporal constraint. This ambiguity leaves the reader unsure about what specific semantic or pragmatic properties the model is actually learning to identify. A clearer definition of the target phenomenon is essential for both motivating the modeling choices and interpreting the results.

2.  **Overstated Conclusions from Architectural Comparisons:** The paper draws a strong conclusion from the finding that a model with two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") slightly outperforms one where the LSTMs are sequential. The authors state: "This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold..." (line 587). This claim seems overstated. The question of whether to process two related text spans independently or sequentially is a well-known open research question in related areas like Natural Language Inference (NLI), where definitive conclusions remain elusive. Given that the two arguments being modeled are often sub-clauses within a single sentence and can share significant context and co-reference, a definitive conclusion that they should be encoded independently requires more substantial evidence and discussion than is provided.

**Minor Comments and Questions for Clarification**

1.  **Clarity of Figure 1:** Figure 1, which depicts the model architecture, is difficult to interpret and could be improved. Firstly, the caption is potentially misleading if it is meant to represent the general architecture and not just the "Pair_LSTM" variant. More critically, the diagram is imprecise. It is unclear what the empty circles between the input layer and the "LSTM" boxes represent. The prose mentions a look-up layer and a GloVe layer, but the number and nature of any subsequent layers are ambiguous. Furthermore, it is not clear what the "LSTM" boxes themselves represent—is it the entire recurrent network or a single time step? I suggest revising the diagram to explicitly show how the final hidden state(s) of the LSTM(s) connect to the subsequent pooling and softmax layers.

2.  **Unclear Justification for Padding:** The justification provided for padding (line 480) is confusing. The text links the decision to "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning." However, padding is typically a practical requirement for efficient batch processing in deep learning frameworks like TensorFlow, rather than a decision motivated by semantic representation. If this is simply an implementation detail, the justification should be stated as such. The connection to "causal meaning" and "convenience" is not apparent.

3.  **Confusing Example Discussion:** The discussion of the example on line 256 is difficult to follow. The authors' commentary seems to imply that they consider the sentence false, but their reasoning is not clearly articulated. The subtleties of event structure and granularity are relevant to causal theories, but without further explanation, the point the authors are trying to make with this example is lost.

4.  **Insufficient Evidence for Disambiguation Claim:** In Section 4.3, the claim that the model can "correctly disambiguate [the] causal meaning" of the connective 'which then' is supported by only a single example from Table 8. To make this point more convincingly, the authors should analyze the model's performance on a wider and more varied set of ambiguous cases involving this and other connectives. This would help determine if the correct prediction on the provided example is representative of a genuinely learned capability or is potentially coincidental.

5.  **Hyperparameter Variation:** The hyperparameters for the best-performing models appear to vary significantly across different experimental settings (e.g., compare lines 578 and 636). It would be helpful if the authors could offer any intuition or interpretation for these differences. Is this variation meaningful, or should it be attributed to the inherent stochasticity of training on a relatively small dataset?

In summary, this paper tackles an important and challenging problem with a well-motivated approach. However, its contribution would be much stronger if the authors clarified their theoretical commitments, tempered their conclusions, and improved the clarity of their technical descriptions.
