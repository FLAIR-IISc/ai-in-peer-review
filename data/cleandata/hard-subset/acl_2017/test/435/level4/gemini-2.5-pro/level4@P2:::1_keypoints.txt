- Paper presents a dual-input LSTM that encodes event1 and marker+event2 separately, concatenates representations, and classifies whether a lexical marker signals causality; evaluated on AltLex and outperforms an SVM baseline.
- Contribution: introduces a novel Pair LSTM architecture showing learned dense embeddings can beat heavily feature-engineered systems for causal marker disambiguation.
- Contribution: provides empirical comparisons of architectures (Pair LSTM vs Stated Pair LSTM) and implementation choices (padding strategies, optimizers), offering initial guidance on model design.
- Strength: achieves state-of-the-art results on the AltLex corpus with improved F1 and a better precision–recall balance on the bootstrapped dataset.
- Strength: well-motivated shift from syntactic/feature-based methods to context-aware neural representations for capturing nuanced expressions of causality.
- Strength: authors release TensorFlow code, supporting reproducibility and follow-up work.
- Weakness: lacks a clear theoretical definition of causation or a precise statement of the semantic phenomenon the model targets; “human mental model of causality” remains vague.
- Weakness: model description and Figure 1 are imprecise (unlabeled nodes, unclear what “LSTM” boxes denote), and the introduction overlaps the related work, reducing clarity.
- Weakness: analysis relies on anecdotal evidence (single example “which then”) and makes potentially overstated claims about model capabilities and the reasons for architecture differences.
- Question: clarify whether the example “(The argument …) (broke the window)” is judged factually improbable or considered out of scope, and provide an explicit definition of the targeted causal relations.
- Question: explain the claimed link between zero-padding strategy and semantic encoding of causality, since padding is primarily a batching technicality.
- Question: offer further intuition or analysis for why decoupling event encoders (Pair LSTM) slightly outperforms initializing the second LSTM with the first’s state (Stated Pair LSTM) despite shared sentential context.
