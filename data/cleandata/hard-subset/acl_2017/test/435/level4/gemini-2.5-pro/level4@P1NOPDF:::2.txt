This paper proposes a neural network-based method for detecting causal relations between clauses. While some discourse connectives (e.g., "because") explicitly signal causality, this work focuses on the more challenging task of identifying causal vs. non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. To this end, the authors leverage the recently introduced AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016). Arguing that LSTMs are better suited than CNNs for representing the relevant clausal features for this task, the authors propose a classification architecture consisting of a GloVe-based clause representation fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three dense layers (with tanh activation) and a final softmax output layer.

The best configuration of the proposed system is reported to improve F1-score by 0.5-1.5% over the SVM-based classifier from Hidey and McKeown (2016). The paper supports this quantitative result with a welcome qualitative analysis, providing several examples where the system generalizes well (e.g., correctly identifying indicator words as non-causal in the test set, even when they were exclusively causal in the training set). I appreciate the inclusion of both quantitative and qualitative analyses.

The paper is well-written, and the problem description is particularly clear. However, the presentation would benefit from a clarification of the differences between this task and the more established task of implicit discourse relation classification. This could include a discussion of why previous methods for implicit relation recognition may not be suitable for this specific problem.

I also have several questions and points that require clarification:

*   **Reproducibility:** The authors are commended for submitting their code and data. This greatly aids in understanding the work. However, some details are unclear. The provided TSV data files use a 0-1-2 coding scheme, which is confusing given that the paper describes a binary classification task. An explanation of this scheme is needed.
*   **Data Rights:** Since the provided data is from Hidey and McKeown (2016), the paper should clarify if the authors have the right to redistribute it.
*   **Bootstrapping:** The paper states that "bootstrapping" was used to extend the corpus by approximately 15%. While the initial construction of the corpus is explained, this subsequent bootstrapping step is not described and should be detailed.

While experimenting with neural networks on this task is valuable, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing due to issues with the evaluation. My primary concern is that the best model configuration appears to have been selected based on performance on the test set. This is the configuration that is reported as achieving the "2.13% F1" improvement. A fair comparison would require selecting the best configuration based on the development set and then reporting the performance of that single configuration on the test set.

Furthermore, the significance of the reported improvement is unclear. First, given the size of the dataset, statistical significance tests should be performed to validate the claims of improvement. Second, the reliability of the gold-standard annotations themselves should be taken into account. The original AltLex paper could be cited for inter-annotator agreement scores, for instance. Upon brief inspection, the annotation, which is derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia, appears to contain some noise. This suggests that the reported scores should be interpreted with some caution.

Finally, the result that a neural method outperforms a system based on hand-crafted features for a binary text classification task largely confirms an established trend, rather than offering a surprising new insight. To better situate the contribution, it would be interesting to see experiments with simpler neural baselines, such as a single-layer LSTM, to understand the source of the performance gains. The analysis could also be extended to explore why the proposed neural method appears to favor precision over recall, which would provide a deeper understanding of the model's behavior.
