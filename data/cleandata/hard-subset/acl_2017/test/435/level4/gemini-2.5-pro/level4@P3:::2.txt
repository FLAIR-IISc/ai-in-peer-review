### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves classifying whether a relation between two events, connected by a potentially ambiguous lexical marker, is causal or not. The authors argue that encoding the semantic context of the events is crucial for this task and that LSTMs are better suited for this than CNNs. They propose a dual-input LSTM-based neural network architecture that encodes the two events separately. The model is trained and evaluated on the AltLex corpus, a dataset specifically created for this type of ambiguous causal expression. The proposed model achieves a modest improvement in F1-score over the previous state-of-the-art system by Hidey and McKeown (2016), which is a feature-based SVM classifier. The paper also includes a qualitative analysis to demonstrate the model's ability to generalize beyond simple lexical cues found in the training data.

**Contributions**

1.  **Application of an LSTM architecture for causal disambiguation:** The primary contribution is the successful application of an LSTM-based neural network to the task of disambiguating causal lexical markers. The proposed architecture demonstrates a small but consistent improvement over a strong, feature-engineered SVM baseline on the AltLex dataset.
2.  **Qualitative analysis of model performance:** The paper provides an insightful analysis of specific cases where the model correctly identifies non-causal relations, even when the lexical markers are almost exclusively causal in the training data. This highlights the model's capacity to leverage sentential context effectively.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and provides a particularly clear motivation and description of the problem. The introduction effectively situates the work within linguistic and computational theories of causality, making the paper accessible and easy to follow.
2.  **Reproducibility:** The authors are to be commended for providing their code and the specific version of the data used for the experiments. This commitment to reproducibility is a significant strength, as it allows other researchers to verify the results and build upon this work.
3.  **Detailed Analysis:** The paper presents a solid quantitative and qualitative analysis. The results on two versions of the corpus (bootstrapped and non-bootstrapped) are reported, and the inclusion of examples where the system outperforms the baseline (Table 8) is particularly valuable for understanding the model's strengths.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Evaluation Methodology and Model Selection:** A major concern is that the best system configuration appears to have been selected based on test set performance. Table 5 presents results for multiple architectural and hyperparameter choices, and the best-performing configuration is then compared to the baseline. A fair comparison requires model selection and hyperparameter tuning to be performed on a separate development set. Without this, the reported improvements over the baseline may be optimistic and the generalizability of the results is questionable.
2.  **Significance of the Improvement:** The reported F1-score improvement is relatively small (e.g., 2.13% on the non-bootstrapped corpus, and approximately 1% on the bootstrapped version). Given the size of the dataset, it is important to establish whether this improvement is statistically significant. Furthermore, the AltLex dataset was created via a semi-automatic process, which can introduce noise. The small margin of improvement could potentially be within the range of this annotation noise, which might temper the significance of the results.
3.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The finding that a standard LSTM-based model outperforms a traditional feature-engineered system is a common pattern in recent NLP research. The proposed architecture itself is not particularly novel. The paper would be stronger if it included experiments with simpler neural baselines (e.g., a bag-of-words model with a dense layer, or a single-layer LSTM) to better justify the complexity of the proposed architecture and to provide a more comprehensive picture of what is required for this task.
4.  **Lack of Contextualization with Related Tasks:** The paper could benefit from a clearer discussion of how this task relates to the well-established task of implicit discourse relation recognition in the PDTB framework. While the AltLex corpus focuses on explicit markers that are ambiguous, the underlying problem is similar. An explanation of why methods from implicit relation classification were not considered as baselines would strengthen the paper's positioning.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify your methodology for model selection and hyperparameter tuning? Was a dedicated development set used to select the best-performing architecture ("Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" or "Pair LSTM Mean Adam") before evaluating it on the test set, or was this selection based on the test set results reported in Table 5?
2.  Have you performed statistical significance tests to confirm that your model's improvements over the B2 baseline (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) are statistically significant?
3.  The paper states that the AltLex corpus was enhanced using a bootstrapping method. Could you provide a brief explanation of this process? Understanding this would help clarify the differences between the "non bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" versions of the dataset and the results obtained on them.
