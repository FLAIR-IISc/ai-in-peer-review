### Summary

This paper proposes a neural network-based method for causal relation classification. The work focuses on disambiguating causal from non-causal relations between two text spans, particularly in cases not marked by unambiguous connectives (e.g., "because"). The authors utilize the recently released AltLex dataset for this task. The proposed architecture represents the text spans using pre-trained GloVe embeddings, processes them with an LSTM layer, and then passes the representation through three dense layers before a final softmax classification layer. The best-performing configuration reports a modest F1-score improvement of 0.5-2% over the prior state-of-the-art SVM-based system by Hidey and McKeown (2016). The paper includes both quantitative and qualitative analyses, showcasing examples where the model correctly handles ambiguous cases.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and provides a very clear description of the problem domain. The introduction situates the task well within the broader context of causality in linguistics and computational linguistics.

2.  **Reproducibility:** The authors are to be commended for submitting their code and data, which greatly aids reproducibility. While I did not execute the code, its availability is a significant strength and facilitates future work in this area.

3.  **Qualitative and Quantitative Analysis:** The inclusion of both quantitative results and a qualitative error analysis is valuable. The examples provided in Table 8, which show the model successfully generalizing beyond simple lexical patterns seen in the training data, are particularly insightful and support the authors' claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Evaluation Methodology:** The main weakness of the paper lies in its evaluation methodology. It appears that the best-performing model configuration (e.g., padding strategy, optimizer) was selected based on its performance on the *test set*. This is not a sound practice, as it can lead to overstating performance. A fair comparison requires selecting the optimal hyperparameters and model configuration on a separate development set. The claimed F1 improvement of 2.13% is therefore not fully convincing, as it reflects the performance of a model tuned to the test data.

2.  **Significance of Results:** The reported performance gains over the state-of-the-art are modest. Given the size of the dataset, the authors should report statistical significance tests to confirm whether these improvements are meaningful. Furthermore, the reliability of the gold-standard annotation itself, which is derived from aligning English and Simple English Wikipedia, may be a confounding factor. An acknowledgment of potential label noise and its impact on evaluation would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Limited Novelty and Baselines:** While the application of neural networks to this task is interesting, the finding that a neural model outperforms a feature-engineered SVM is not, in itself, a surprising result in the current NLP landscape. The contribution would be stronger if the authors included simpler neural baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM, or a feed-forward network over averaged embeddings) to better isolate the benefits of their specific architecture.

4.  **Insufficient Analysis:** The analysis could be extended to explain *why* the proposed neural method tends to favor precision over recall. Is this an artifact of the architecture, the class imbalance, or the nature of the learned representations? A deeper investigation into this behavior would be welcome.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the relationship between this task and the more established task of implicit discourse relation recognition? Specifically, why are methods from that literature not directly applicable or used as baselines here?

2.  Could you elaborate on the "bootstrapping" process used to extend the AltLex corpus? The paper mentions that it increases the corpus size but does not explain the method.

3.  In the submitted data files, what is the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files, given that the paper describes a binary (Causal/Non-Causal) classification task?

4.  A minor point regarding the submitted data: do the licensing terms of the original AltLex corpus permit its redistribution?
