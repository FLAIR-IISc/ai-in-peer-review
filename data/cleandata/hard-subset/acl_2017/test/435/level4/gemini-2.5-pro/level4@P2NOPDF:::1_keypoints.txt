- Proposes an LSTM-based model to classify whether a connective indicates a causal relation, arguing neural representations capture diverse causal expressions better than syntactic methods.  
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support and the authors release their TensorFlow models.  

Major concerns:
- Introduction reads like a literature review, is redundant with Related Work, and fails to provide a clear roadmap or theoretical grounding of the contribution.  
- Lacks a clear theory of causation; inconsistently invokes a counterfactual view without properly defining the implication operator or offering a coherent alternative.  
- Model architecture and Figure 1 are unclear and imprecise (misleading caption, unexplained diagram elements, unspecified layers, ambiguous LSTM outputs).  
- The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential encoding is interesting but the paper overstates conclusions about the semantic independence of input events.

Minor concerns:
- Example discussion (line 256) is confusing and neglects issues like event decomposition, making the authors' point hard to follow.  
- Justification for padding (line 480) is unclear and conflates an implementation/batching detail with semantic encoding choices.  
- Reported hyperparameters appear inconsistent across experiments; the paper needs to discuss whether differences are meaningful or due to training instability.  
- Qualitative analysis is weak: the single example cited for disambiguating "which then" is insufficient and should be supplemented by a controlled challenge set and performance evaluation.
