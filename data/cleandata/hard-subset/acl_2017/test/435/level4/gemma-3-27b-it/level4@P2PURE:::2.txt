This paper introduces a neural network approach to detect causal relations between clauses. While employing networks that are not particularly deep—a common characteristic of many such studies—the method leverages the recent AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) to address the challenging task of identifying causal relationships when they are not explicitly signaled by discourse connectives. The authors argue that LSTMs are better suited than convolutional networks for representing the relevant clause features, and propose a classification architecture consisting of GloVe-based clause embeddings, an LSTM layer, three tanh-activated dense layers, and a softmax output layer.

The system’s best-performing configuration achieves an F1 score improvement of 0.5-1.5% over the 2016 Hidey & McKeown SVM baseline. The paper presents illustrative examples of successful generalizations, demonstrating the system’s ability to correctly identify non-causal relations in test data despite encountering indicator words frequently associated with causality in the training set. I commend the authors for their thorough qualitative and quantitative analysis.

The paper is well-written, and the problem description is particularly clear. However, a clarification regarding the distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition would be beneficial, potentially including a discussion of why existing methods for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable. 

The authors’ provision of code and the AltLex dataset is highly appreciated, facilitating reproducibility and further research. However, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding scheme in the TSV files is unclear given the paper’s framing of the task as binary classification. Furthermore, it warrants clarification whether the authors possess the necessary rights to redistribute the Hidey & McKeown data. The term "bootstrapping," which expands the corpus by approximately 15%, also requires more detailed explanation; while the initial corpus construction is clear, the bootstrapping process is not.

While applying neural networks to this task is interesting, the system’s advantages are not fully demonstrated. The reported 2.13% F1 improvement over Hidey & McKeown is based on a configuration optimized on the test data, rather than a more principled selection based on performance on a development set. 

Moreover, the significance of this improvement is questionable. Given the dataset size, statistical significance testing is warranted. Additionally, the reliability of the gold-standard annotations—derived from English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia—should be considered, as they are not perfect, and may affect the interpretation of the results. 

Finally, the findings largely confirm the known effectiveness of neural networks over feature engineering for binary classification, and thus lack substantial novelty. Exploring simpler network architectures as baselines, such as a single-layer LSTM, would be valuable. The result analysis could also investigate the observed trade-off between precision and recall.
