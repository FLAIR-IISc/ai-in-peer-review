This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks—though, as in many studies, the networks are not particularly deep. While certain discourse connectives unambiguously signal a causal relation (e.g., ‘because’), this paper leverages a recent dataset (AltLex, by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to tackle the task of identifying causal versus non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. The authors argue that convolutional networks are less adept at representing the relevant features of clauses than LSTMs, and propose a classification architecture that employs a GloVe-based representation of clauses, fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

The best configuration of the system improves upon Hidey and McKeown’s 2016 baseline (an SVM classifier) by 0.5–1.5% F1. The paper presents several examples of generalizations where the system performs well, correctly identifying instances where indicator words typically associated with causality in the training data are actually non-causal in the test data. I appreciate this qualitative and quantitative analysis.

The paper is well written, and the problem description is particularly clear. However, a clarification of the differences between this task and implicit connective recognition would be valuable. This could include a discussion of why existing methods for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable here.

It’s commendable that the authors have uploaded their code to the submission site (I briefly inspected it, but did not execute it). Providing the data (along with the code) is also useful, as it offers numerous examples. However, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files was unclear, given that the paper describes a binary classification task. Furthermore, given that this is data from Hidey and McKeown, I wonder if the authors have the rights to redistribute it; this point should be clarified. The meaning of “bootstrapping,” which apparently extends the corpus by approximately 15%, also requires clarification. While the corpus construction is explained briefly but clearly, the bootstrapping process itself is not.

While experimenting with neural networks on this task is certainly interesting, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. The best configuration—selected from among four to seven options—was found on the test data, and it is this configuration that is reported to improve upon Hidey’s results by “2.13% F1.” A fairer comparison would involve selecting the best configuration on a development set.

Moreover, the significance of the improvement is not entirely clear. On one hand, given the dataset size, it should be possible to compute statistical significance indicators. On the other hand, the reliability of the gold-standard annotation itself should be considered – potentially with input from the dataset creators. Upon inspection, the annotation derived from English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia is not perfect, and therefore the reported scores should be interpreted with caution.

Finally, neural methods have previously demonstrated superior performance to human-engineered features for binary classification tasks, so the results largely confirm a known property. It would be interesting to see experiments with simpler networks used as baselines, such as a 1-layer LSTM. The analysis of results could also attempt to explain why the neural method appears to prioritize precision over recall.
