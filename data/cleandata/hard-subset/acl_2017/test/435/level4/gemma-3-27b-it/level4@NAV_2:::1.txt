This paper develops an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether a causal relation is intended. The central idea is that expressing causal relations is highly diverse and thus not easily addressed through syntactic analysis, and that the more abstract representations generated by neural models are therefore better suited for making these decisions. The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results offer modest but consistent support for the general idea and provide initial insights into how best to implement this idea in a model. The paper includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Several critical comments and questions arise:

* The introduction is atypical in that it functions more like a literature review than a comprehensive overview of the paper’s content. This results in some redundancy with the subsequent related work section. While a non-standard introduction is acceptable, this one is ineffective; despite reviewing numerous concepts, it doesn't articulate a clear stance on what causation is or how it’s expressed. It only presents a negative argument—that causation is not reducible to syntax—without defining the positive contribution beyond the general final paragraph of the section.
* I found the discussion of the underlying theory of causation lacking. The authors appear to adopt a counterfactual view akin to that of David Lewis, framing causation as a modal sufficiency claim with associated counterfactual conditions (see line 238 and following). The arrow in this context represents a specialized form of implication, with well-documented challenges (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). While the paper acknowledges reservations regarding the counterfactual view, it does not clearly articulate the assumed theoretical framework. Simply referencing the temporal constraint (page 3) is insufficient.
* The interpretation of the example on line 256 is unclear. The authors seem to suggest the sentence is false. If true, a causal link between the argument and the breakage should exist. Discussions about how to decompose events into sub-events and their impact on causal theories are absent, leading to confusion.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading as it’s intended to represent only the "Pair_LSTM" model variant. Furthermore, the diagram is unnecessarily imprecise. While omitting certain standard model details is acceptable, the diagram’s semantics should be clear and consistent. The empty circles between the input and the "LSTM" boxes are ambiguous. The prose suggests a look-up layer and a Glove layer, but what follows remains unclear. How many layers of representation are there? The diagram precisely depicts the pooling tanh layers preceding the softmax, but not the earlier layers. The representation within the "LSTM" boxes is also unclear; it seems only the leftmost/final representation connects directly to the subsequent layers. A clearer depiction of this connection is recommended.
* The sentence beginning on line 480 is puzzling. The models under consideration do not inherently require padding, likely due to TensorFlow requirements or efficient training. If so, that should be stated. The final clause is unclear, though. How does this issue relate to determining the “most convenient way to encode causal meaning”? The relevance of convenience or its direct relationship to causal meaning is not apparent.
* The finding that independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) perform slightly better than a sequential arrangement, where the first LSTM feeds into the second, echoes discussions in the natural language entailment literature concerning independent versus sequential representation of premises and hypotheses. This remains an open question for entailment and warrants further investigation for causal relations. Therefore, the assertion beginning on line 587 – that the behavior indicates the initial assumption about the relationship between input event meanings is invalid, and that independent encoding followed by relation measurement via dense layers is preferable – is surprising, given the potential for information sharing between sentence subparts.
* The hyperparameters yielding the best performance across tasks are difficult to interpret. Compare line 578 with line 636, for instance. Should this be viewed as an inherent unpredictability of model interaction with data, or is there a more discernible pattern?
* Section 4.3 concludes that the system correctly disambiguates the causal meaning of “which then,” while the Hidey and McKeown system fails. While this may be accurate, a single example is insufficient. To substantiate this claim, I recommend creating a diverse range of examples exhibiting the ambiguity and evaluating how frequently the system delivers the correct verdict. This will address the possibility of a lucky outcome with the example in table 8.
