- Proposes an LSTM-based neural method to detect causal vs. non-causal clause relations without explicit connectives using GloVe embeddings, an LSTM layer, three tanh dense layers, and a softmax decision layer.

- Uses the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown 2016) and reports both qualitative and quantitative evaluations with illustrative examples.

- Best system gains about 0.5–1.5% F1 over Hidey & McKeown’s SVM baseline; a reported 2.13% F1 improvement is based on the best test configuration.

- Paper is well-written with a particularly clear problem description.

- Needs clarification on how this task differs from implicit connective recognition and why existing implicit-connective methods are not directly applicable.

- Authors provide code and data, which is commendable for reproducibility.

- The meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files is unclear given the paper frames the problem as binary.

- Clarification required on the authors’ rights to redistribute the AltLex dataset.

- The “bootstrapping” that expands the corpus by ~15% is insufficiently explained.

- Evaluation methodology is problematic: optimal configuration was selected on test data rather than a development set, risking overfitting.

- Statistical significance of the reported improvements is not reported; significance testing and standard indicators are needed.

- Reliability concerns about the gold-standard annotations (derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia) suggest reported scores should be interpreted cautiously.

- Results largely confirm known trends that neural methods outperform hand-engineered features in binary classification, so the novelty/significance of the improvement is limited.

- Recommend adding simpler baselines (e.g., 1-layer LSTM) and analysis of model behavior, such as why the method favors precision over recall.
