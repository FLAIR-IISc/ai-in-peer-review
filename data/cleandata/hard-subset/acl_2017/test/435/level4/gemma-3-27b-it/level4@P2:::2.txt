This paper addresses the task of identifying causal relations between clauses, leveraging a recent dataset (AltLex, Hidey and McKeown, 2016) designed for scenarios where causality isn’t explicitly signaled by common discourse connectives. The authors propose a neural network architecture employing LSTMs and dense layers for classifying these relations as causal or non-causal, arguing that LSTMs are better suited than convolutional networks for capturing the relevant contextual information. The proposed system achieves a modest improvement (0.5-1.5% F1) over the state-of-the-art SVM classifier from Hidey and McKeown (2016), with qualitative analysis showcasing successful generalizations to cases where indicator words exhibit differing causal meanings in training and test data. The paper is generally well-written and provides a clear explanation of the problem.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Application of Neural Networks to Causal Relation Disambiguation:** The primary contribution is the exploration of LSTM-based neural networks for the task of identifying causal relations in contexts lacking explicit causal markers, utilizing the AltLex dataset. While neural methods are increasingly common, applying them to this specific task with this dataset is a valuable extension.
2. **Empirical Evaluation on the AltLex Dataset:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed model on the AltLex corpus. This evaluation, including a comparison to a prior state-of-the-art system, offers insights into the effectiveness of the neural approach on this particular dataset.
3. **Qualitative Analysis of Model Performance:** The inclusion of qualitative examples demonstrating the model’s ability to generalize beyond simple connective-based cues enhances understanding of its strengths and limitations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper articulates the challenge of causal relation identification in the absence of explicit markers, and provides a solid justification for exploring neural network approaches. The context provided regarding limitations of prior approaches and the importance of nuanced understanding of causality is compelling.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experimental design is robust, including a baseline comparison to a published state-of-the-art system and exploration of different network configurations and optimization techniques.  The inclusion of code and data (with necessary caveats – see Weaknesses) facilitates reproducibility.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Results:** The authors not only present quantitative results but also delve into a qualitative analysis of the model’s successes and failures, demonstrating an understanding of the nuances of the task. The examination of specific examples (like those involving ‘break’) is particularly insightful.
4. **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is logically organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors’ approach and findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Significance of Improvement:** The performance improvement over the baseline (0.5-1.5% F1) is relatively small, and its statistical significance is not established. A rigorous statistical test is necessary to determine if the observed improvement is genuinely significant.
2. **Selection Bias in Best Configuration:** The reported improvement is based on the best configuration *selected on the test data*. This introduces a risk of overfitting and an overly optimistic assessment of performance. A proper evaluation methodology would involve selecting the best configuration on a development set *before* evaluating on the test set.
3. **Data Usage Concerns:**  The authors re-distribute the AltLex dataset created by Hidey and McKeown (2016). While the data is accessible, the paper should explicitly address whether permission has been obtained to re-distribute it and provide proper attribution.
4. **Ambiguity of ‘Bootstrapping’:** The process of “bootstrapping” the corpus is mentioned but not clearly explained.  The paper should provide more details on how the corpus was extended and the potential implications of this process for the evaluation results.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While a prior SVM baseline is used, the paper lacks a comparison to simpler neural network architectures (e.g., a single-layer LSTM). Including such baselines would help contextualize the performance gains achieved by the proposed model and assess whether the complexity of the architecture is truly necessary.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Statistical Significance:** Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-test) to demonstrate the significance of the performance improvement over the baseline.
2. **Development Set Evaluation:**  Was a development set used to select the best configuration of the proposed model? If so, please report the performance on the development set. If not, please re-evaluate the model using a development set.
3. **Data Redistribution Permissions:** Did you obtain explicit permission from Hidey and McKeown (2016) to redistribute the AltLex dataset?
4. **Bootstrapping Details:** Please provide a detailed explanation of the “bootstrapping” process used to extend the AltLex corpus.
5. **Annotation Reliability:** What measures were taken to assess the reliability of the gold-standard annotations in the AltLex corpus?



**Additional Comments:**

The connection to prior work on implicit connective recognition could be strengthened. A discussion of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable to this task would be beneficial. The 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files should be clarified.
