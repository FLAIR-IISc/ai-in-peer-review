- Proposes an LSTM-based model to classify connective uses and detect intended causal relations, arguing neural abstract representations beat syntactic analysis.
- Experiments use the AltLex corpus; results give modest but consistent support and the authors release TensorFlow code for reproducibility.
- Introduction reads like a literature review, duplicates related work, and fails to state the paper’s central contribution clearly until late.
- The paper’s treatment of causation theory is unclear; it appears to lean on a counterfactual/Lewis-style view without addressing alternatives or known theoretical problems.
- The interpretation of the example around line 256 is confusing about truth and causal linkage; event decomposition issues are raised but not developed.
- Figure 1’s caption is misleading (it only depicts the Pair LSTM) and the diagram lacks precision about lookup/Glove layers, layer counts, the meaning of empty circles, and LSTM connections.
- The sentence near line 480 conflates TensorFlow padding (an implementation/efficiency detail) with encoding causal meaning, which is unclear and should be stated explicitly if intended.
- The finding that independent LSTMs slightly outperform a chained LSTM is interesting but the paper’s claim to prefer independent encoding is premature without deeper analysis.
- Reported best hyperparameters are hard to interpret; discrepancies (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636) leave unclear whether effects are random or systematic.
- Section 4.3’s claim that the system disambiguates “which then” better than the baseline relies on a single example and needs broader evaluation to be substantiated.
