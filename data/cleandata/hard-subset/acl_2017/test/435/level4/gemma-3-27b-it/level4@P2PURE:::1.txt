This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses, specifically determining whether they signal an intended causal relation. The core motivation is that causal relations are expressed in a highly diverse manner, making syntactic approaches insufficient, and that the more abstract representations learned by neural models are better suited to this task.

The experiments utilize the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results provide modest, yet consistent, support for this central idea and offer preliminary insights into effectively implementing it within a model. The authors have generously made the TensorFlow-based models used in their experiments publicly available.

However, several points require further attention:

* The introduction currently functions more as a literature review than a concise overview of the paper's contributions. This leads to overlap with the subsequent related work section. While unconventional introductions can be acceptable, this one lacks a clear thesis; it predominantly presents a negative argument—that causation is not reducible to syntax—without establishing a corresponding positive contribution until the final paragraph.
* The paper lacks clarity regarding the underlying theory of causation it assumes. The authors appear to lean towards a broadly Lewisian counterfactual account, treating causation as a modal sufficiency claim with added counterfactual conditions (see line 238 and following). The implication represented by the arrow in this context requires careful consideration, as Lewis’s theory is known to face challenges (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Although the paper occasionally suggests a departure from the counterfactual view, the assumed theory remains undefined, and simply invoking a temporal constraint (page 3) is insufficient.
* I am unclear about the authors’ interpretation of the example on line 256. If the sentence is considered true, a causal connection between the argument and the breakage must exist. Further discussion is needed regarding the division of events into sub-events, as these divisions fundamentally impact causal theories, but are not addressed here, leading to confusion.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram is intended to represent only the "Pair_LSTM" model variant. More significantly, the diagram lacks precision. While omitting details from the full model definition in the prose is acceptable, diagrams should maintain a clear and consistent semantics. The empty circles between the input and "LSTM" boxes are unexplained. The prose indicates a look-up layer and a GloVe layer, but the subsequent layers of representation remain ambiguous. While the pooling tanh layers pre-softmax are clearly depicted, the preceding layers are not. Clarification regarding what the "LSTM" boxes represent—specifically, which representation is directly connected to the subsequent layers—would be beneficial.
* The sentence beginning on line 480 is unclear. The models do not inherently require padding, likely due to TensorFlow requirements or efficient training considerations. If this is the case, it should be explicitly stated. The connection between this implementation detail and the question of “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning” is also unclear; the relevance of convenience to causal meaning is not demonstrated.
* The finding that two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") perform slightly better than a sequential configuration (first LSTM feeding into the second) echoes debates in natural language entailment research, specifically regarding independent versus chained premise/hypothesis representations. This remains an open question in entailment and likely warrants further investigation in the context of causal relations. Therefore, I question the conclusion starting on line 587 – “This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers.” This conclusion is surprising given the potential for shared information within sentence subparts.
* The hyperparameters leading to optimal performance across tasks are difficult to reconcile (e.g., lines 578 and 636). Is this attributable to the inherent unpredictability of these models' interactions with the data, or does it warrant further analysis?
* Section 4.3 claims the system "correctly disambiguate[s]” the causal meaning of the connective ‘which then’ in a way that Hidey and McKeown's system does not. However, a single example is insufficient to support this claim. To substantiate this assertion, I recommend evaluating the system on a broader range of ambiguous examples and assessing its accuracy to determine whether the successful outcome in Table 8 was simply a matter of chance.
