This paper introduces a neural network approach to identify causal relationships between clauses, tackling the challenging problem of detecting causality when not explicitly signaled by discourse connectives. Leveraging the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), the authors posit that LSTMs are better suited than convolutional networks for capturing the necessary clause-level features. Their proposed architecture employs GloVe embeddings fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three dense (tanh) layers and a softmax output layer for classification. 

The system achieves a 0.5-1.5% F1 score improvement over the original Hidey & McKeown (2016) SVM baseline, with compelling qualitative examples demonstrating the model’s ability to generalize beyond indicator words. I commend the authors for their thorough quantitative and qualitative analysis.

The paper is well-written, particularly its clear problem definition. However, a clarification distinguishing this task from implicit connective recognition would be beneficial, potentially including a discussion of why existing methods for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable here. 

The inclusion of code and data on the submission site is greatly appreciated, facilitating reproducibility and further research. However, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the data’s TSV files, given the described binary classification task, requires clarification. Furthermore, the authors should address the rights associated with redistributing the Hidey & McKeown dataset. The bootstrapping process, which expands the corpus by approximately 15%, also needs further explanation beyond the brief overview provided.

While exploring neural networks for this task is worthwhile, the system’s advantages are not fully demonstrated. The reported 2.13% F1 improvement relies on selecting the best configuration *on the test data*, rather than a more principled selection using a development set. 

Moreover, the statistical significance of the improvement requires assessment, considering the dataset size. A discussion of the gold-standard annotation's reliability, given observed inconsistencies in the English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia source, is also warranted. The observed performance aligns with the known strengths of neural networks for binary classification, and the results could be viewed as a confirmation of this. Exploring simpler network architectures as baselines – such as a single-layer LSTM – would be valuable. Finally, an analysis attempting to explain the model’s tendency towards higher precision than recall would be insightful.
