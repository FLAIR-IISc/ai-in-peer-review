- Presents an LSTM-based model to classify connective usages in the AltLex corpus for intended causality; experiments show modest but consistent support and the authors release TensorFlow code.  
- Introduction reads like a broad literature review, overlaps with related work, and lacks a clear thesis statement about the paper’s contribution.  
- The paper’s assumed theory of causation is unclear (appears to lean toward Lewis-style counterfactuals but is inconsistently endorsed); the causal framework and known criticisms (e.g., Copley & Wolff) must be stated.  
- The discussion of the example at line 256 is confusing—claims that the sentence is false are unexplained, and event decomposition implications for causal theory are not addressed.  
- Figure 1 (Pair_LSTM) is misleading and imprecise: semantics of diagram elements (empty circles, look-up vs. GloVe layers), number of representational layers, and how LSTM outputs are used are unclear.  
- The sentence around line 480 is unclear; padding appears to be an implementation detail (TensorFlow) and its asserted link to “encoding causal meaning” is unexplained.  
- The finding that two independent LSTMs (Stated_LSTM) outperform a sequential setup echoes the premise–hypothesis debate; concluding independence of argument meanings is premature given possible intra-sentence shared information.  
- Optimal hyperparameters differ across tasks (lines 578 vs. 636) with no explanation—unclear if this reflects model-data interactions or requires further analysis.  
- The claim that the model successfully disambiguates “which then” is based on a single example and lacks a robust evaluation over diverse ambiguous instances.
