- Paper develops an LSTM-based model to classify connective uses for intended causal relations, arguing neural abstractions beat syntactic analysis.  
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support and provide implementation insights; authors release TensorFlow code.  
- Introduction reads like a literature review and is redundant with related work; it fails to clearly state the paper’s positive contributions until late.  
- Paper lacks a clearly defined theory of causation; it appears to lean on a Lewis-style counterfactual view without stating or defending the theoretical assumptions.  
- The discussion around the example at line 256 is confusing—unclear whether the sentence is judged true or false and how sub-event decomposition affects the causal judgment.  
- Figure 1’s caption is misleading (it illustrates only the Pair_LSTM variant) and the diagram is imprecise and ambiguous about input layers, empty circles, layer counts, pooling/tanh placement, and LSTM connections.  
- The sentence around line 480 is unclear: padding is not inherent to the models, and the claimed link between padding/convenience and encoding “causal meaning” is unexplained.  
- The observation that two independent LSTMs outperform a sequential architecture is interesting, but the authors’ conclusion that arguments should always be encoded independently is surprising and not convincingly justified.  
- Hyperparameter choices that yielded best performance are inconsistent across tasks (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636) and are not adequately interpreted or explained.  
- Claim that the system “correctly disambiguate[s]” the connective “which then” relies on a single example; broader testing on ambiguous instances is needed to substantiate this claim.
