This paper presents an LSTM-based model designed to classify whether connective phrases indicate a causal relationship. The core motivation is the recognized diversity in expressing causality, suggesting that traditional syntactic approaches are insufficient and that the more abstract representations afforded by neural models are better suited for this task. The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus, and the results demonstrate modest, consistent improvements over a baseline, offering initial insights into effective model design. The authors also provide the TensorFlow implementation of their models.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Neural Network Effectiveness:** The primary contribution is demonstrating that an LSTM-based architecture can effectively classify causal relations within the AltLex corpus, outperforming a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016). This provides empirical support for the hypothesis that neural networks are capable of capturing the nuances of causal language.
2. **Application to a Challenging Dataset:** The work tackles a complex task—causal relation classification—using a relatively new and challenging corpus (AltLex) specifically designed to address the limitations of existing resources regarding diverse causal expressions.
3. **Initial Exploration of Model Architectures:** The paper explores different LSTM configurations (connected vs. independent) for encoding event relationships, offering preliminary insights into which approaches are more promising for this task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of identifying causal relations in natural language, highlighting the limitations of syntax-based approaches.
2. **Empirically Grounded:** The findings are supported by experimental results on a dedicated dataset, providing evidence for the proposed approach. The inclusion of model code facilitates reproducibility.
3. **Relevant Baseline Comparison:** Comparing against the state-of-the-art (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) provides a meaningful benchmark for evaluating the proposed model.
4. **Discussion of Architectural Choices:** The paper provides a rationale for the chosen LSTM architecture and explores the impact of different configurations (connected vs. independent LSTMs).
5. **Analysis of Difficult Cases:** The analysis in Section 4.3, focusing on the example with “which then”, demonstrates an effort to understand model behavior on ambiguous cases.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Unfocused Introduction:** The introduction functions more as a literature review than a focused overview of the paper's contribution. It lacks a strong thesis statement outlining the key innovation. The negative framing ("it's not reducible to syntax") is insufficient without a clear articulation of the proposed solution.
2. **Unclear Theoretical Grounding:** The paper lacks a precise definition of the underlying theory of causation. The allusions to counterfactual accounts are vague and potentially misleading. A more explicit statement of the theoretical framework is needed.
3. **Ambiguous Example Discussion:** The discussion surrounding the example on line 256 is confusing. The authors’ interpretation of the example is unclear and potentially impacts the reader’s understanding of the task.
4. **Imprecise Diagram:** The diagram in Figure 1 lacks clarity and essential details about the model architecture. The representation of layers and connections is not sufficiently precise, hindering understanding.
5. **Confounding Padding Discussion:** The explanation of padding (line 480 onwards) is unclear and lacks justification for its relevance to encoding causal meaning. The connection between padding strategy and causal understanding is not established.
6. **Overstated Architectural Interpretation:** The conclusion regarding the superiority of independent LSTMs (line 587) is not adequately supported by the experimental results. The observed difference might be due to other factors, and the claim requires further investigation.
7. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The lack of a clear explanation for the optimal hyperparameter settings (e.g., comparing lines 578 and 636) hinders the generalizability of the findings.
8. **Insufficient Evidence for Specific Successes:** The claim of successfully disambiguating “which then” (Section 4.3) is based on a single example. More extensive testing is needed to validate this claim.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Theoretical Framework:** Can you explicitly state the theory of causation underlying your work? If you are not adopting a specific counterfactual account, what assumptions are you making about what constitutes a causal relationship?
2. **Diagram Clarification:** Can you provide a more detailed and precise diagram of the model architecture (Figure 1), clearly labeling all layers and connections?
3. **Padding Relevance:** Please explain the connection between the padding strategy used and the task of causal meaning disambiguation. Why is the choice of padding method relevant to the model’s performance?
4. **LSTM Configuration:** Can you provide a more nuanced explanation for why independent LSTMs outperformed connected LSTMs? What specific properties of the data or task might explain this difference?
5. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Could you elaborate on the process used to select the optimal hyperparameters? Were any specific search strategies employed, and if so, what were they?
6. **"Which Then" Generalization:** Can you present results on a larger set of examples involving “which then” to demonstrate the generalizability of the model's performance on this ambiguous connective?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more concise and focused introduction.
* A more rigorous discussion of the theoretical assumptions would strengthen the work.
* Improved clarity in the diagram and explanations would enhance understanding.
* Further investigation into the architectural choices and hyperparameter sensitivity is warranted.
