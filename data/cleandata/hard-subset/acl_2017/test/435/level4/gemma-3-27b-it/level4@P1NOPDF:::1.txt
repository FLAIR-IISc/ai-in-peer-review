This paper develops an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses, specifically to determine whether they indicate an intended causal relation. The core idea is that expressing causal relations is highly diverse and therefore not easily addressed through syntactic analysis. The authors propose that the more abstract representations learned by neural models are better suited for this task.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results provide modest, yet consistent, support for this general approach, and offer initial insights into how to effectively implement it. The authors have generously included the TensorFlow-based models used in their experiments.

However, I have several critical comments and questions:

* The introduction is unconventional, reading more like a literature review than a concise overview of the paper’s contributions. This creates redundancy with the subsequent related work section. While I am open to non-standard introductions, this one doesn’t quite succeed. Despite reviewing numerous ideas, it doesn’t articulate a clear position on the nature of causation or its expression; it primarily makes a negative claim (that it’s not reducible to syntax). The positive contribution isn’t clearly stated until the final paragraph of the section.
* Expanding on this, I found the paper lacking a clearly defined theory of causation. The authors seem to lean towards a counterfactual view similar to that of David Lewis, treating causation as a form of modal sufficiency with added counterfactual conditions (see line 238 and following). The implication represented by the arrow in this context requires a specific interpretation, and Lewis’s theory is known to have limitations (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). While the paper occasionally suggests they don't fully endorse the counterfactual view, it remains unclear what theoretical framework *is* being assumed. The temporal constraint mentioned on page 3 is insufficient on its own.
* I’m unclear about the comment regarding the example on line 256. The authors appear to consider the sentence false, implying a lack of a causal link between the argument and the breakage. However, if the sentence is true, such a link should exist. The paper touches on issues related to dividing events into sub-events, which are relevant to causal theories, but doesn’t explore these connections, leaving me confused.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram is intended to illustrate only the “Pair_LSTM” model variant. More significantly, the diagram is unnecessarily imprecise. While omitting details from the model definition in the prose may be acceptable, diagrams should possess a clear and consistent semantics. What do the empty circles between the input and the “LSTM” boxes represent? The text suggests a look-up layer and a GloVe layer, but then what? How many layers of representation are there? The diagram clearly depicts the pooling tanh layers before the softmax layer but lacks detail about these initial layers. I’m also unclear about what the “LSTM” boxes signify. It appears only the leftmost/final representation is directly connected to the subsequent layers, and this connection should be visualized more clearly.
* I don't understand the sentence starting on line 480. The models being discussed do not inherently require padding. I assume this is a requirement of TensorFlow and/or efficient training, which is fine. However, the final clause is unclear. How is this even related to finding “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning”? I don’t see how convenience is an issue, or how it directly impacts causal meaning.
* The authors find that using two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) yields slightly better results than a sequential architecture where the first LSTM feeds into the second. This observation echoes discussions in natural language entailment literature, where researchers debate whether to represent the premise and hypothesis independently or sequentially. This remains an open question for entailment, and I suspect it also warrants further investigation for causal relations. Therefore, I disagree with the assertion beginning on line 587: “This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers.” This conclusion is surprising, given that the sentence components being analyzed might share considerable information.
* The hyperparameters that resulted in the best performance across tasks are difficult to interpret. For example, compare line 578 and line 636. Should we attribute these discrepancies to the inherent unpredictability of how these models interact with data, or is there a deeper underlying reason?
* Section 4.3 concludes that the system “correctly disambiguate[s]” the causal meaning of the connective ‘which then,’ unlike the results reported by Hidley and McKeown. While this may be true, a single example is insufficient evidence. To strengthen this claim, I suggest testing the system on a broader range of ambiguous examples and evaluating how often it delivers the correct interpretation. This would help address the possibility that the system simply succeeded with the specific example presented in Table 8.
