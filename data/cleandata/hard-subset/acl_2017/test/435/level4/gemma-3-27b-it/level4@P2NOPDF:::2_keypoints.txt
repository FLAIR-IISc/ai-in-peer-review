- Proposes an LSTM-based neural model (GloVe → LSTM → three tanh dense layers → softmax) to detect causal relations between clauses without explicit connectives.  
- Builds on the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) to distinguish causal vs. non-causal relations when not explicitly signaled.  
- Argues LSTMs are better than CNNs for capturing clause-level features.  
- Reports a 0.5–1.5% F1 improvement over the original SVM baseline (authors cite 2.13% F1 gain).  
- Provides strong quantitative and qualitative analysis, including examples where the model correctly generalizes non-causal instances despite causal indicator words.  
- Paper is clearly written and frames the problem well but should better differentiate this task from implicit connective recognition and explain why prior methods aren’t directly applicable.  
- Releases code and the AltLex dataset, aiding reproducibility, but lacks clarity on the TSV 0-1-2 coding scheme and redistribution rights for the Hidey & McKeown data.  
- The term “bootstrapping” and the ~15% corpus expansion are insufficiently explained.  
- Evaluation practice of selecting the best configuration on the test set risks overfitting; model selection should use a development set.  
- Statistical significance of the reported gains is not reported and should be computed given dataset size.  
- Reliability of gold-standard annotations is unclear; using English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia may introduce inconsistencies that affect results.  
- Findings largely confirm known results that neural nets outperform feature-engineered methods, limiting novelty of the improvement.  
- Recommend adding simpler baseline experiments (e.g., single-layer LSTM) and analyzing the precision–recall trade-off, since the model seems to favor precision.
