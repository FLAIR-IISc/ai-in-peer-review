This paper proposes a neural network approach to detect causal relations between clauses, tackling the challenging problem of identifying causality when not explicitly signaled by discourse connectives. The authors leverage the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) – a valuable resource for this task – and argue that LSTMs are better suited than convolutional networks for capturing the relevant contextual features. Their proposed architecture employs GloVe embeddings as input to an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected tanh layers and a softmax output layer for classification.

The system achieves an F1 score improvement of 0.5-1.5% over the original Hidey & McKeown (2016) SVM baseline, with the best configuration reaching a 2.13% F1 improvement. A particularly positive aspect of this work is the inclusion of qualitative analysis, illustrating instances where the system successfully generalizes beyond indicator words present in the training data. The clear writing and problem description are also commendable.

However, several points warrant further consideration. While the task is well-defined, a clearer distinction between it and the related task of implicit connective recognition would be beneficial. Specifically, the authors should discuss why existing methods for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable to this causal relation detection task. 

The authors’ decision to release their code and the AltLex dataset alongside it is highly appreciated, fostering reproducibility and further research. However, the 0-1-2 coding scheme in the TSV files requires clarification, given the stated binary classification objective. Furthermore, the authors should verify and explicitly acknowledge the rights associated with redistributing the AltLex dataset, originally created by Hidey and McKeown. The description of the “bootstrapping” process, which expands the corpus by approximately 15%, is also currently insufficient; more detail on its implementation is needed.

The significance of the reported improvement is somewhat unclear. While a 2.13% F1 gain is reported, it appears to be based on selecting the best configuration *on the test set*. A more rigorous evaluation would involve selecting the optimal configuration using a development set and then reporting performance on the held-out test set. Additionally, assessing the statistical significance of the improvement would strengthen the findings. Given the potential for annotation noise – a cursory inspection reveals imperfections in the English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia-derived annotations – the reported scores should be interpreted with caution. A discussion of inter-annotator agreement or potential sources of annotation error would be valuable.

While the application of neural networks to this task is a logical step, the results, as presented, feel somewhat confirmatory rather than groundbreaking. Previous work has demonstrated the efficacy of neural networks over hand-engineered features for binary classification. To better establish the value of the proposed architecture, including experiments with simpler network baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM) would be informative. Finally, the analysis of results could benefit from exploring why the system appears to prioritize precision over recall.
