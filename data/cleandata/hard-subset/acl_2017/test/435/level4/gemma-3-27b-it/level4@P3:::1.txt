This paper develops an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine if a causal relation is intended. The core idea is that the expression of causal relations is highly diverse and therefore less amenable to syntactic approaches, suggesting that more abstract neural representations are better suited for this task. The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidey and McKeown. The results provide modest but consistent support for this idea and offer preliminary insights into translating it into a model. The authors provide the TensorFlow-based models used in the experiments.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Empirical Investigation of Neural Networks for Causal Discourse Parsing:** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation of LSTM networks for the task of causal relation classification, demonstrating their effectiveness on a challenging corpus (AltLex) compared to a strong baseline. This provides valuable evidence supporting the use of neural models for this complex NLP problem.
2.  **Focus on Contextual Encoding for Causal Disambiguation:** The work emphasizes the importance of encoding the entire contextual sentence for disambiguating causal meaning, rather than relying on syntactic or lexical features alone. This focus aligns with recent trends in NLP towards more holistic contextual understanding.
3.  **Detailed Analysis of Model Configurations and Hyperparameters:** The paper provides a detailed comparison of different LSTM architectures (connected vs. independent LSTMs) and hyperparameter settings, offering valuable insights into the practical considerations for building effective causal relation classifiers.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed system consistently outperforms the baseline (Hidey and McKeown's SVM-based system) on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating its effectiveness in identifying causal relations. The performance gains are statistically significant and reported clearly.
2.  **Well-Defined Task and Corpus:** The paper focuses on a well-defined and challenging task (causal relation classification) and leverages a valuable resource (the AltLex corpus) that specifically targets the issue of non-explicit causal markers.
3.  **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, systematically evaluating different model architectures, padding strategies, optimizers, and regularization techniques. This rigorous approach strengthens the validity of their findings.
4.  **Availability of Code:** Providing the TensorFlow code enables reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.
5.  **Clear Presentation of Results:** The results are presented in a clear and organized manner, with tables summarizing the performance of different configurations.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Unclear Theoretical Foundation:** The introduction lacks a clear statement of the theoretical assumptions regarding causation. The paper touches upon counterfactual accounts but doesn't commit to a specific theory, leaving the reader uncertain about the underlying conceptual framework.
2.  **Redundant Introduction:** The introduction reads more like a literature review than a focused overview of the paper's contributions. This leads to redundancy with the subsequent related work section and obscures the core argument.
3.  **Imprecise Diagram (Figure 1):** The diagram illustrating the model architecture is overly simplistic and lacks precision. Key details, such as the layers between the embedding and LSTM layers, are omitted, hindering understanding.
4.  **Unclear Justification for Padding Strategy:** The explanation for the different padding strategies and their connection to causal meaning is unclear. The discussion lacks a clear rationale for why one padding strategy might be more suitable than others.
5.  **Overstated Conclusion Regarding Independent LSTMs:** The conclusion that independent LSTMs are superior because the "assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold" is not convincingly supported by the experimental results. The slight performance improvement could be due to other factors.
6.  **Limited Generalizability of Example-Based Claims:** The claim that the system can “correctly disambiguate” the meaning of "which then" based on a single example is insufficient to demonstrate its robustness. A more comprehensive evaluation with a diverse set of examples is needed.
7.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The discrepancies in optimal hyperparameters across tasks (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636) raise concerns about the stability and generalizability of the model.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you explicitly state the theoretical assumptions about causation underlying your work?
2.  Could you clarify the layers between the embedding layer and the LSTM layer in your model architecture? A more detailed diagram would be helpful.
3.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation for the different padding strategies and their impact on performance?
4.  What are the potential reasons for the slight performance improvement observed with independent LSTMs, beyond simply rejecting the assumption about the relationship between the meanings of the two input events?
5.  Could you provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the system’s ability to handle ambiguous connectives like "which then" with a wider range of examples?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to the Copley and Wolff paper regarding Lewis's counterfactual theory is helpful but could be integrated more directly into the discussion of the theoretical framework.
*   Consider clarifying the connection between the AltLex corpus and the broader challenges of causal relation extraction in NLP.
