- Presents an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses (whether they signal intended causal relations).
- Experiments use the AltLex corpus and show modest but consistent support for neural models; TensorFlow code is publicly released.
- Introduction reads like a literature review, lacks a clear thesis and positive statement of contributions, and overlaps with related work.
- The paper fails to state or justify a clear underlying theory of causation; it appears Lewisian/counterfactual but the implications and known objections are not addressed.
- The interpretation of the example around line 256 is unclear; event/sub-event segmentation and its impact on causal claims are not discussed.
- Figure 1’s caption is misleading (it depicts only the Pair_LSTM variant) and the diagram is imprecise—unexplained symbols, unclear layer semantics, and ambiguity about which LSTM representations feed later layers.
- The sentence around line 480 is unclear about padding (implementation vs. model necessity) and does not justify why this bears on encoding causal meaning.
- The result that separate LSTMs outperform chained LSTMs echoes open entailment debates; the paper’s strong conclusion that independent encoding is better is premature given possible shared information across arguments.
- Optimal hyperparameters differ across tasks in ways that are hard to reconcile; this deserves further analysis to determine if it’s data/model instability or meaningful.
- Claim that the system “correctly disambiguates” ‘which then’ over prior work is based on a single example and needs evaluation on a broader set of ambiguous cases to rule out chance.
