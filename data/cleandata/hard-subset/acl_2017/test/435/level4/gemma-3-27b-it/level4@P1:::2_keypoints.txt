- Proposes LSTM-based neural method (GloVe → LSTM → three tanh dense layers → softmax) to detect causal vs. non-causal clause relations, arguing LSTMs beat CNNs.  
- Uses AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown 2016) and reports best config improves ~0.5–1.5% F1 over the SVM baseline.  
- Provides qualitative and quantitative analyses, with examples where the model correctly rejects spurious causal links.  
- Paper is well-written but should clarify distinction from implicit connective recognition and why existing implicit-connective methods are insufficient.  
- Code and data release is commendable, but the 0-1-2 coding in TSV files is unexplained and redistribution permission for AltLex is unclear.  
- The bootstrapping step (corpus extended by ~15%) is insufficiently described and needs more detail.  
- Reported “2.13% F1” improvement appears to be chosen by selecting best test configuration, which is an unfair evaluation procedure; selection should be based on a development set.  
- Statistical significance of improvements is not reported; significance testing is needed given dataset size.  
- Reliability of gold annotations is questionable (data from English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia), potentially affecting result validity.  
- Contribution is incremental and mainly confirms established trend that neural methods outperform hand-engineered features.  
- Should compare against simpler network baselines (e.g., single-layer LSTM) and analyze why the model emphasizes precision over recall.
