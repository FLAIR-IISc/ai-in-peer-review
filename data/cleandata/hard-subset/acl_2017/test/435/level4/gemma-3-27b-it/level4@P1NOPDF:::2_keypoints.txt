- Paper proposes LSTM-based neural method (GloVe inputs → LSTM → 3 tanh dense layers → softmax) to detect causal vs non-causal clause relations on AltLex data.
- Uses AltLex (Hidey & McKeown 2016) to identify causal relations when not explicitly marked by discourse connectives.
- Authors claim LSTMs outperform convolutional networks for this task.
- Best system improves over Hidey & McKeown’s SVM baseline by ~0.5–1.5% F1 (reported 2.13% F1 in one configuration).
- Paper includes useful qualitative examples showing correct generalizations where cue words are non-causal in test data.
- Writing and problem description are clear, but distinction from implicit connective recognition is not sufficiently discussed.
- Code and data were uploaded, which is good, but the TSV 0-1-2 coding is unexplained and redistribution rights for Hidey & McKeown’s data are unclear.
- “Bootstrapping” that expands the corpus by ~15% is mentioned but not adequately described.
- Experimental methodology is flawed: best configuration was chosen on the test set instead of a development set.
- Statistical significance of the reported improvement is not provided; gold-standard annotation quality (from English/Simple English Wikipedia) may be unreliable.
- Contribution is incremental—confirms known advantage of neural methods over hand-engineered features—so stronger baselines (e.g., 1-layer LSTM) should be included.
- Analysis should address why the neural method favors precision over recall and provide deeper error analysis.
