This paper presents an LSTM-based model designed to classify the connective usages in the AltLex corpus, specifically focusing on whether they signal an intended causal relationship. The core motivation is that causal relations exhibit considerable variability and are therefore ill-suited to purely syntactic analysis, making the more abstract representations learned by neural networks a potentially superior approach. The experiments demonstrate modest, yet consistent, support for this idea and offer initial guidance on model implementation. The authors generously provide the TensorFlow code used in their experiments.

However, several aspects of the paper require clarification and further development. The introduction currently functions more as a broad literature review than a focused overview of the paper’s contribution. This leads to overlap with the subsequent related work section. While unconventional introductions can be effective, this one lacks a clear thesis statement; it primarily establishes what causation *isn’t* (reducible to syntax) without articulating a positive contribution until the final paragraph. 

This issue extends to a broader concern: the paper remains unclear about its underlying theory of causation. The authors appear to lean towards a counterfactual account similar to David Lewis’s, based on modal sufficiency, (see lines 238+), but statements elsewhere suggest they do not fully endorse this view. Identifying the assumed causal framework is crucial, as the implied conditional relation requires specific interpretation and is subject to known criticisms (e.g., Copley & Wolff, 2014). Simply referencing a temporal constraint is insufficient.

I also encountered difficulty understanding the discussion surrounding the example on line 256. The authors’ assertion that the sentence is false is unclear; if true, a causal link between the argument and breakage should be present. Questions regarding event decomposition and their impact on causal theories remain unaddressed, contributing to my confusion. 

Figure 1, intended to illustrate the “Pair_LSTM” model variant, is misleading and imprecise. The diagram lacks a consistent semantics, leaving unclear what the empty circles between the input and LSTM layers represent. The description refers to a look-up layer and a GloVe layer, but the overall model architecture—the number of representational layers—remains ambiguous. Furthermore, the function of the “LSTM” boxes themselves is not readily apparent; it’s unclear if only the final hidden state is used in subsequent layers, and this connection should be visualized explicitly.

The sentence beginning on line 480 is also unclear. The assertion about padding requirements seems to relate to TensorFlow implementation details, which should be stated directly. The connection between padding and “the most convenient way to encode causal meaning” is not established and should be clarified. 

Regarding model architecture, the finding that two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) perform slightly better than a sequential configuration is reminiscent of the premise-hypothesis debate in natural language entailment. This remains an open question in that field, and likely warrants further investigation within the context of causal relation modeling. Consequently, the conclusion beginning on line 587, suggesting the independence of input argument meanings, is premature given the potential for shared information within a sentence.

The optimal hyperparameters across tasks (lines 578 vs. 636) are difficult to reconcile. It’s unclear whether this reflects inherent unpredictability in the interaction between these models and the data, or if further analysis is required.

Finally, the conclusion in section 4.3 regarding the successful disambiguation of “which then” (line 667) is based on a single example and therefore lacks sufficient support. A more robust evaluation, employing a wider range of ambiguous examples, is needed to substantiate this claim and demonstrate the system’s reliability beyond a potentially lucky outcome.
