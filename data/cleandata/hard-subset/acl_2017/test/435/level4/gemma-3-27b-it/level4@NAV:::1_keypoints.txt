- Introduces an LSTM-based model to classify whether connectives signal intended causal relations, arguing neural representations suit this variability better than syntactic approaches.

- Evaluated on the AltLex corpus with modest but consistent support; authors release TensorFlow implementation.

- Introduction reads like a literature review and lacks a focused argumentative thread; core contribution only becomes clear at the very end.

- Paper needs a clearer underlying theory of causation; it seems to assume a Lewisian/counterfactual stance without acknowledging known challenges or specifying alternatives.

- Discussion of the example on line 256 is confusing—claiming the sentence is false is perplexing and ignores issues of event/sub-event decomposition important for causal claims.

- Figure 1 caption and diagram are misleading and imprecise: it depicts only Pair_LSTM, contains unclear elements (empty circles), and omits details about post-GloVe layers and exact LSTM connections.

- The sentence around line 480 about padding is unclear; padding likely reflects TensorFlow/training constraints and should not be presented as tied to encoding causal meaning.

- The claim that independent LSTMs outperform sequential encoding (Stated_LSTM vs. chained LSTM) is premature; parallels open questions in entailment and doesn’t prove independent encoding of event meaning.

- Optimal hyperparameters differ across tasks without explanation; unclear whether this reflects inherent neural unpredictability or other factors.

- The assertion that the system “correctly disambiguates” ‘which then’ is based on a single example and needs a broader, diverse evaluation to rule out chance.
