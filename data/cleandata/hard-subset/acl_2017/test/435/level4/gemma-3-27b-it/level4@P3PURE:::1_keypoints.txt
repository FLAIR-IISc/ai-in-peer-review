- Proposes an LSTM-based model to classify connective uses as indicating intended causal relations, arguing neural representations suit the diversity of causal expression better than syntactic approaches.  
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus yield modest but consistent support for the idea; TensorFlow models used in experiments are included with the paper.  
- Introduction reads like a literature review, duplicates related work, and fails to clearly state the paper’s contribution until late.  
- The paper does not clearly specify its theory of causation; it appears to default to a counterfactual/Lewis-style view without committing or offering an alternative.  
- The discussion around the example on line 256 and the role of event sub-events is confusing and underexplored.  
- Figure 1’s caption is misleading and the diagram lacks precise semantics and architectural detail (empty circles, LSTM internals, layer counts unclear).  
- The sentence around line 480 linking padding/TensorFlow requirements to “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning” is unclear and unjustified.  
- The claim that independent LSTMs outperform a sequential structure and thus arguments should be encoded independently is premature and not convincingly supported.  
- Optimal hyperparameters differ across tasks in ways that are hard to interpret; it’s unclear if this reflects model/data interaction or deeper issues.  
- The claim that the system correctly disambiguates the connective “which then” is based on a single example and needs a systematic evaluation on a comprehensive ambiguity set.
