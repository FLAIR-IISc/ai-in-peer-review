This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses, using neural networks. The work leverages a recent dataset (AltLex, by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to tackle the task of identifying causal vs. non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked by a connective. The authors argue that LSTMs are better suited to represent the relevant features of clauses than convolutional networks, and propose a classification architecture which utilizes a Glove-based representation of clauses, an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

The best configuration of the system improves by 0.5-1.5% F1 over Hidey and McKeown's 2016 system (an SVM classifier). The analysis includes qualitative and quantitative evaluation, with several examples illustrating the system's ability to generalize. 

The paper is well-written and the problem description is particularly clear. However, a clarification of the distinction between this task and the task of implicit connective recognition would be beneficial, potentially including a discussion of why existing methods for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable here. 

The authors’ provision of their code alongside the submission is highly appreciated. The inclusion of the data, facilitating reproducibility and further research, is also commendable. However, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files is unclear, given the paper describes a binary classification task. Furthermore, clarification is needed regarding the authors' rights to redistribute the AltLex dataset, originally created by Hidey and McKeown. The term "bootstrapping," which apparently expands the corpus by approximately 15%, requires further explanation; while the corpus construction is briefly described, the bootstrapping process itself is not.

While the use of neural networks for this task is interesting, the significance of the proposed system’s improvements isn’t fully convincing. The reported improvement of "2.13% F1" is based on the best configuration identified on the test data. A more rigorous evaluation would involve selecting the optimal configuration on a development set.

Additionally, the statistical significance of the improvement is unclear. Computing standard significance indicators would strengthen the findings. Considering the reliability of the gold-standard annotation is also important, as the annotation derived from English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia is not perfect, and the reported scores should be interpreted cautiously. 

Finally, the observed improvement aligns with the known performance benefits of neural methods over hand-engineered features in binary classification tasks. The results, therefore, largely confirm existing trends. Exploring simpler network architectures as baselines, such as a 1-layer LSTM, would provide valuable context. Analyzing the results to understand the neural method’s tendency to prioritize precision over recall could also offer insights.
