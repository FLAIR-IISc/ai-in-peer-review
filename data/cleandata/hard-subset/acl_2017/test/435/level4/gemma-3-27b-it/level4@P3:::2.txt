This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses, using neural networks. The core contribution lies in applying LSTMs to the task of causal relation classification, specifically leveraging the AltLex dataset introduced by Hidey and McKeown (2016) which focuses on identifying causal relations not explicitly marked by common connectives. The authors argue that LSTMs are better suited than convolutional networks for capturing the nuanced context necessary for this task, and propose a classification architecture utilizing Glove embeddings, LSTM layers, and subsequent dense layers with a softmax output. The proposed system achieves a modest improvement (0.5-1.5% F1) over the previous state-of-the-art (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), showcasing generalization capabilities with examples where the system correctly identifies non-causal relationships despite training data suggesting otherwise. The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the problem and proposed solution.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Application of LSTMs to Causal Relation Classification with AltLex:** The primary contribution is the successful application of LSTM networks to the challenging task of identifying implicit causal relations using the AltLex corpus. This demonstrates the potential of deep learning methods for capturing contextual information crucial for causality disambiguation.
2.  **Empirical Evaluation on the AltLex Dataset:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of the proposed method on a relatively new and valuable dataset, AltLex. This helps establish a baseline for future research in this area and contributes to a better understanding of the challenges involved in implicit causal relation detection.
3.  **Qualitative and Quantitative Analysis:** The authors complement the quantitative results with a qualitative analysis of specific examples, highlighting the system’s ability to generalize beyond simple lexical cues. This strengthens the claim that the network is learning meaningful representations of causal relationships.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of identifying causal relations beyond explicit connectives and motivates the need for more sophisticated approaches. The explanation of the AltLex dataset’s relevance is also well-done.
2.  **Sound Methodology:** The choice of LSTMs is justified, and the architecture is clearly described. The use of pre-trained word embeddings (Glove) is a sensible approach, and the exploration of different optimization techniques and padding strategies demonstrates a good experimental setup.
3.  **Reproducibility:** Providing the code and data (with appropriate acknowledgement) significantly enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work. This allows other researchers to build upon the presented findings.
4.  **Thoughtful Analysis:** The authors demonstrate a critical understanding of their results, acknowledging potential limitations and areas for improvement. The discussion of precision versus recall is particularly insightful.
5.  **Demonstrated Generalization:** The examples provided showcasing correct classification of instances differing from the training data demonstrate the system’s ability to generalize and learn underlying causal semantics, rather than relying solely on surface-level features.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Modest Improvement:** While the improvement over the state-of-the-art is statistically significant, it is relatively small. This raises questions about the practical impact of the proposed method and whether the complexity of the LSTM architecture is fully justified.
2.  **Evaluation Protocol Concerns:** The selection of the "best" configuration based on test set performance raises concerns about overfitting and potentially inflated results. A more rigorous evaluation would involve hyperparameter tuning and validation on a separate development set.
3.  **Statistical Significance and Annotation Reliability:** The paper lacks a discussion of statistical significance testing to determine the robustness of the observed improvements. Moreover, the potential impact of annotation errors within the AltLex dataset, which relies on automatic alignment and bootstrapping, is not adequately addressed.
4.  **Lack of Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to Hidey & McKeown (2016) is valuable, but the inclusion of simpler neural network baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM) could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the benefits of the proposed architecture.
5.  **Clarification of Task Relationship:** The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of how this task differs from, and relates to, the broader field of implicit discourse connective recognition.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to demonstrate the robustness of the reported performance improvements?
2.  Regarding the “bootstrapping” process for creating the AltLex corpus, could you elaborate on the criteria used to determine whether a new instance was correctly labeled, and what measures were taken to mitigate the risk of propagating errors?
3.  Was a development set used for hyperparameter tuning, or was the test set used for model selection? If the latter, can you provide results on a held-out development set?
4.  Could you discuss the potential impact of annotation noise in the AltLex dataset on the reported results, and how this might affect the generalizability of your findings?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Clarify the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files.
*   Confirm the rights regarding re-distribution of the AltLex dataset.
*   A brief discussion of error analysis (e.g., common error cases) would provide further insight into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.
