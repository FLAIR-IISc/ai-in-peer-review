- Paper studies an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to detect intended causal relations, using the AltLex corpus.  
- Results show modest but consistent support for neural representations over syntactic approaches, and the authors release their TensorFlow models.  
- Introduction reads like a literature review, is redundant with related work, and lacks a clear statement of the paper’s central thesis and contributions.  
- The theoretical account of causation is underdefined and appears to rely on a problematic counterfactual/Lewis-style reading without addressing known criticisms.  
- The temporal constraint and other theoretical remarks are insufficient; event decomposition issues are mentioned but not resolved, creating conceptual ambiguity.  
- The example around line 256 is confusing: the authors’ claim that the sentence is “false” is unclear and inconsistently treated with respect to causality.  
- Figure 1 and its caption are misleading (should denote the Pair_LSTM variant); the diagram is imprecise with unexplained symbols and unclear layer semantics.  
- The role of the LSTM outputs (e.g., whether final hidden states are used) and the number of representation layers between embeddings and pooling need to be clarified.  
- The discussion of padding is unclear; if padding is used for TensorFlow efficiency that should be stated, and the link to encoding causal meaning is tenuous.  
- The finding that independent LSTMs outperform sequential architectures is interesting, but the authors’ conclusion that this implies no meaningful relationship between inputs is premature.  
- Inconsistent optimal hyperparameters across tasks are reported; the paper should clarify whether these differences are meaningful or due to unstable interactions with the data.  
- The claim that the system “correctly disambiguates” ‘which then’ is unsupported by a single example and requires broader, systematic evaluation with diverse ambiguous instances.
