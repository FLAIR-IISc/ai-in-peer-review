This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks (though, as in many studies, the networks are not particularly deep). While certain discourse connectives unambiguously signal causal relations (e.g., ‘because’), the paper leverages a recent dataset (AltLex, by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to address the task of identifying causal versus non-causal relations when they are not explicitly marked. The authors argue that convolutional networks are less adept at representing the relevant features of clauses than LSTMs, and propose a classification architecture utilizing Glove-based representations of clauses input into an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh activation) and a final decision layer with a softmax. 

The best configuration of the system improves upon Hidey and McKeown’s 2016 results (an SVM classifier) by 0.5-1.5% F1. Several examples of successful generalizations are presented, demonstrating the system’s ability to correctly identify non-causal relationships in test data despite indicator words being consistently causal in the training data. I appreciate this qualitative and quantitative analysis.

The paper is well written, and the problem description is particularly clear. However, clarification of the differences between this task and implicit connective recognition would be beneficial, potentially including a discussion of why existing methods for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable here.

The authors’ provision of code on the submission site is commendable (I briefly inspected it without execution). Uploading the dataset (along with the code) is also helpful, providing numerous examples. However, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files is unclear given the paper’s description of a binary classification task. Furthermore, given that the data originates from Hidey and McKeown, it is worth clarifying whether the authors have the right to repost it. A point requiring clarification in the paper is the meaning of "bootstrapping," which apparently extends the corpus by approximately 15%; while the initial corpus construction is explained clearly, the bootstrapping process itself is not.

While experimenting with neural networks on this task is interesting, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. The reported improvement over Hidey’s results ("2.13% F1") stems from selecting the best configuration on the test data. A more rigorous comparison would involve selecting the best configuration on a development set. 

Moreover, the significance of the improvement is not fully established. Given the dataset size, it would be valuable to include statistical significance indicators. Consideration should also be given to the reliability of the gold-standard annotation – obtained from English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia – which is not perfect. Consequently, the reported scores should be interpreted with caution.

Finally, neural methods have previously demonstrated superior performance to human-engineered features for binary classification tasks; thus, these results largely confirm a known property. Experiments with simpler network architectures as baselines, such as a single-layer LSTM, would be informative. The result analysis could also investigate why the neural method appears to prioritize precision over recall.
