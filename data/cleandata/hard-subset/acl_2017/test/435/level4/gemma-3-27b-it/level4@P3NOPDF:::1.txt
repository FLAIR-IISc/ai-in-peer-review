This paper investigates an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses, specifically focusing on whether they signal an intended causal relationship. The core motivation is that the diverse and nuanced expression of causality is poorly captured by syntactic approaches, and that the abstract representations learned by neural networks offer a more promising path forward. The experiments utilize the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown, and the results demonstrate modest, yet consistent, support for this hypothesis, alongside initial insights into effective model design. The authors generously provide the TensorFlow-based models used in their experiments.

However, several aspects of the paper require further clarification and refinement. The introduction currently functions more as a literature review than a focused overview of the paper’s contribution. While acknowledging the breadth of prior work is valuable, this approach leads to redundancy with the subsequent related work section. More importantly, the introduction lacks a clear statement of the paper’s central thesis and anticipated contribution beyond a general rejection of purely syntactic treatments of causality.

Related to this, the paper’s underlying theory of causation remains insufficiently defined. The authors appear to lean towards a counterfactual account, reminiscent of Lewis’s analysis, relying on a specific interpretation of implication (lines 238ff). This is problematic, as Lewis’s theory is well-known to be subject to various criticisms (e.g., Copley & Wolff, 2014 – http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). While the authors occasionally express reservations about a strict counterfactual reading, the core theoretical framework remains unclear. Simply invoking a temporal constraint (page 3) is inadequate. 

Further conceptual ambiguities arise with the example discussed around line 256. The authors’ assertion that the sentence is “false” is unclear. If a causal link between the argument and the breakage exists, the sentence should be considered true. The broader issue of event decomposition and its impact on causal theories is alluded to, but not addressed, contributing to confusion.

The presentation of the model itself could be significantly improved. The caption for Figure 1 is misleading as it should specifically denote the "Pair_LSTM" variant. More critically, the diagram is imprecise. While simplifying complex models is understandable, the diagram should maintain a clear and consistent semantics. The empty circles connecting the input to the “LSTM” boxes are unexplained, and the overall layer structure is unclear. Specifically, the number of representation layers between the GloVe embedding and the pooling layers is not readily apparent. The function of the "LSTM" boxes themselves—is it the final hidden state that’s used for subsequent processing?—needs to be made explicit.

The discussion surrounding padding (line 480) is also unclear. If padding is necessitated by TensorFlow for training efficiency, this should be stated directly. The connection between padding and “the most convenient way to encode causal meaning” is tenuous and requires further explanation. Why is convenience relevant, and how does it relate to the core task?

The observation that two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) outperform a sequential architecture resonates with findings in natural language entailment, where independent versus chained representations of premise and hypothesis are actively debated. Consequently, the authors’ strong conclusion (line 587) – that the independent encoding reflects a lack of meaningful relationship between the two input events – is premature and perhaps surprising, given the potential for shared information within a sentence. Further investigation is warranted.

The reported hyperparameters achieving optimal performance appear inconsistent across tasks (e.g., lines 578 and 636). While model sensitivity to hyperparameters is common, clarifying whether these discrepancies represent meaningful insights or simply reflect the unpredictable interaction of the model with the data would strengthen the analysis.

Finally, the claim in Section 4.3 that the system “correctly disambiguates” the causal meaning of ‘which then’ (compared to Hidley & McKeown’s analysis) is insufficiently supported by a single example. A more robust evaluation would involve constructing a diverse set of ambiguous instances and assessing the system's performance across a wider range of cases to determine if the success is genuine or a matter of chance.
