This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses, using neural networks. While many studies employ relatively shallow networks, this work leverages deep learning to tackle the task of identifying causal versus non-causal relations, particularly when the relation isn't explicitly signaled by a discourse connective. The paper builds upon a recent dataset (AltLex, by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) and argues that LSTMs are better suited than convolutional networks for representing the relevant features of clauses. The proposed architecture uses a Glove-based representation of clauses, input to an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

The best configuration of the system improves by 0.5-1.5% F1 over Hidey and McKeown’s 2016 method (an SVM classifier). The authors provide a qualitative and quantitative analysis, including examples of successful generalizations—cases where the system correctly identifies non-causal relationships despite the training data suggesting a causal link.

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem. However, clarifying the distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition would be beneficial. This could include a discussion of why existing methods for implicit connective recognition are insufficient in this context.

The authors’ provision of code and data is commendable, facilitating reproducibility and further research. However, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files is unclear, given the paper's description of a binary classification task. It’s also worth clarifying whether the authors have permission to redistribute the AltLex dataset. The term “bootstrapping,” which apparently extends the corpus by approximately 15%, could also be explained more thoroughly, as the paper briefly describes corpus construction but doesn't detail the bootstrapping process.

While experimenting with neural networks on this task is valuable, the significance of the proposed system’s improvements isn’t entirely convincing. The reported improvement of “2.13% F1” relies on selecting the best configuration on the test data. A fairer comparison would involve selecting the optimal configuration based on performance on a development set.

Furthermore, the statistical significance of the improvement remains unclear. Given the dataset size, it would be beneficial to include statistical significance indicators. The reliability of the gold-standard annotation should also be considered, as the data, derived from English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia, is not flawless, potentially impacting the validity of the scores.

Neural methods have previously demonstrated superior performance over human-engineered features in binary classification tasks; therefore, these results largely confirm an established trend. It would be valuable to compare the proposed system against simpler network architectures, such as a single-layer LSTM. Analyzing the results to understand why the neural method prioritizes precision over recall would also be insightful.
