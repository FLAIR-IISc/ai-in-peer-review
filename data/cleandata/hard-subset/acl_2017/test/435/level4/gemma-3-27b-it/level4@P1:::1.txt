This paper develops an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether a causal relation is intended. The central idea is that expressing causal relations is highly diverse and therefore not easily addressed through syntactic analysis. The authors posit that the more abstract representations afforded by neural models are better suited for this task.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results provide modest but consistent support for this idea, offering initial insights into how best to implement it in a model. The paper includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments, facilitating reproducibility.

Some critical comments and questions:

* The introduction is unconventional in its structure, resembling a literature review more than a concise overview of the paper’s contents. This leads to redundancy with the subsequent related work section. While a non-standard introduction is acceptable, this one is not particularly effective; despite a thorough review of existing ideas, it lacks a clear statement of the paper’s central argument and contribution beyond the negative claim that causality is not reducible to syntax. The reader isn’t clearly informed of the positive contribution until the very end of the section.
* I found the discussion of the underlying theory of causation somewhat unclear. The authors appear to lean towards a counterfactual view, similar to that of David Lewis, where causation is defined as a modal sufficiency claim with added counterfactual conditions. See line 238 and following; the implication represented by the arrow requires specific counterfactual conditions to be valid, raising well-known theoretical challenges (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Although the paper acknowledges potential disagreement with this view, it doesn’t explicitly state the alternative theory being assumed. Relying solely on the temporal constraint mentioned on page 3 is insufficient.
* The interpretation of the example on line 256 is unclear. The authors seem to suggest the sentence is false, but if true, a causal link between the argument and the breakage should exist. The paper touches upon issues of event decomposition and their relevance to causal theories, but doesn’t explore them fully, leaving this point confusing.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram only depicts the “Pair LSTM” model variant. Furthermore, the diagram lacks precision. While omitting some details of the standard model definition is acceptable, the diagram should have a clear and consistent semantics. The empty circles between the input and the “LSTM” boxes are unclear. The prose indicates a lookup layer and a Glove layer, but then what? How many layers of representation are used? The diagram clearly shows the pooling tanh layers before the softmax layer, but not the preceding layers. It’s also unclear what the “LSTM” boxes represent; it seems only the leftmost/final representation is directly connected to the layers above. A clearer depiction of this connection is needed.
* The sentence beginning on line 480 is difficult to understand. The models do not inherently require padding; this appears to be a technical requirement of TensorFlow for efficient training. If this is correct, please state it explicitly. The connection between padding and “the most convenient way to encode causal meaning” is unclear. I don’t understand how convenience relates to causal meaning here.
* The finding that two independent LSTMs (“Stated LSTM”) perform slightly better than a model where the first LSTM feeds into the second is reminiscent of debates in natural language entailment regarding independent versus chained premise/hypothesis representations. This remains an open question in entailment and likely warrants further investigation in the context of causal relations. Therefore, the claim starting on line 587 – "This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers" – is premature. This finding is surprising given the potential for shared information between parts of a single sentence.
* The hyperparameters that yielded the best performance are difficult to interpret. Comparing lines 578 and 636, for example, it’s unclear whether to attribute the differences to inherent unpredictability in model-data interactions or to other factors.
* Section 4.3 claims that the system "correctly disambiguates the causal meaning" of ‘which then,’ while the baseline does not. This conclusion is based on a single example and is therefore insufficient. To substantiate this claim, I suggest evaluating the system on a wider range of examples that demonstrate the ambiguity of this connective, assessing how often the system reaches the correct verdict. This would help determine whether the observed success is genuine or due to chance.
