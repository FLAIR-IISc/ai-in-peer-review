- Paper proposes an LSTM-based model to classify whether connectives signal causal relations, arguing neural representations outperform syntactic analysis; experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support and TensorFlow code is provided.

- Introduction reads like a literature review, is redundant with related work, and fails to state a clear positive contribution or a clear definition of causation.

- The paper’s theoretical stance on causation is unclear; it appears to adopt a counterfactual view without explicitly committing to or defending that framework or addressing known challenges.

- Interpretation of the example around line 256 is confusing (authors seem to imply the sentence is false) and lacks discussion of event decomposition and its implications for causal analysis.

- Figure 1’s caption is misleading (represents only the Pair_LSTM variant) and the diagram is imprecise/ambiguous about lookup/Glove layers, number of representation layers, and exact LSTM outputs used.

- The comment around line 480 about padding is puzzling; if padding is used for implementation efficiency (e.g., TensorFlow), that should be stated and its relevance to encoding causal meaning clarified.

- The observation that independent LSTMs outperform sequentially stacked LSTMs is surprising, echoes NLI debates, and requires deeper analysis about information sharing versus independent encoding.

- Best-performing hyperparameters vary across tasks (e.g., discrepancies between lines 578 and 636), making it hard to interpret whether this reflects data-model idiosyncrasy or a pattern worth reporting.

- The claim that the system correctly disambiguates “which then” compared to Hidey & McKeown is based on a single example; more diverse examples and frequency-based evaluation are needed to rule out a lucky outcome.

- Overall results provide initial implementation insights but the paper needs clearer theoretical framing, better figure clarity, more rigorous analysis of model behaviors, and stronger empirical validation.
