This paper introduces an LSTM-based model designed to classify connective uses based on whether they signal an intended causal relationship. The core motivation rests on the observation that expressing causal relations is highly variable and thus ill-suited to purely syntactic approaches; the authors posit that the more abstract representations learned by neural networks are better equipped for this task.

The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results provide modest, yet consistent, support for this central idea, and offer initial guidance on translating it into a practical model. The authors have generously made the TensorFlow implementation of their models publicly available.

However, several points require further clarification:

* The introduction functions more as a literature review than a focused overview of the paper’s specific contributions, leading to some overlap with the subsequent related work section. While non-traditional introductions can be acceptable, this one lacks a clear argumentative thread. It extensively reviews existing work without articulating a distinct position on the nature of causation or its linguistic expression, instead primarily focusing on the limitations of syntactic approaches. The paper’s core contribution remains largely undefined until the final paragraph of the introduction.

* The paper would benefit from a more explicit articulation of the underlying theory of causation. The authors appear to lean toward a broadly Lewisian counterfactual account, relying on a specific interpretation of implication (as highlighted from line 238 onward). The well-documented challenges associated with Lewis’s theory should be acknowledged. While the authors occasionally disavow a strict counterfactual reading, the assumed alternative remains unclear; simply referencing a temporal constraint (page 3) is insufficient.

* The discussion surrounding the example on line 256 is unclear. The authors' assertion that the sentence is false is perplexing. If the sentence is accurate, a causal connection between the argument and the breakage should exist. Furthermore, unresolved issues regarding the division of events into sub-events – a crucial consideration for causal theories – are not addressed, contributing to confusion. 

* The caption for Figure 1 is potentially misleading, as the diagram is intended to represent only the “Pair_LSTM” model variant. More significantly, the diagram itself lacks precision. While omitting details from the full model definition in the prose is understandable, the diagram should maintain a consistent and unambiguous semantics. The meaning of the empty circles between the input and the “LSTM” boxes is unclear. The model architecture – specifically, the layers following the GloVe embedding – is not sufficiently detailed. While the pooling tanh layers pre-softmax are clearly depicted, the number of representational layers remains ambiguous. The function of the “LSTM” boxes themselves also requires clarification; it appears only the leftmost/final representation connects to subsequent layers, a detail that should be visually emphasized.

* The sentence beginning on line 480 is difficult to interpret. The models do not inherently require padding, which likely stems from TensorFlow implementation requirements for efficient training. If this is the case, it should be stated directly. The connection between padding and "the most convenient way to encode causal meaning" is unclear; the relevance of convenience to causal representation is not established.

* The finding that two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) outperform a sequential architecture (first LSTM feeding into the second) echoes debates in natural language entailment regarding independent versus concatenated premise and hypothesis representations. This remains an open question in entailment research and warrants further investigation within the context of causal relations. Consequently, the claim beginning on line 587 – that the results disprove an assumption about the relationship between input event meanings and therefore justify independent encoding – is premature, particularly given the potential for shared information within sentence subparts.

* The optimal hyperparameters across tasks are difficult to reconcile (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636). Whether this is attributable to the inherent unpredictability of neural network behavior with respect to data remains an open question.

* The conclusion in Section 4.3, stating the system can “correctly disambiguate” the causal meaning of ‘which then’ where Hidley and McKeown’s system fails, is based on a single example. This claim requires stronger substantiation through a more comprehensive evaluation involving a diverse range of ambiguous instances to demonstrate consistent and reliable disambiguation, and to rule out the possibility of success due to chance.
