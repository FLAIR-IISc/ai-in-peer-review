This paper presents a neural network approach to identifying causal relations between clauses, a task complicated by the frequent absence of explicit causal connectives. Building on the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), the authors address the challenge of distinguishing causal and non-causal relationships when not explicitly signaled. They argue that LSTMs are better suited than convolutional networks for capturing the relevant features within clauses and propose an architecture consisting of GloVe embeddings, an LSTM layer, three tanh-activated dense layers, and a final softmax decision layer.

The proposed system achieves a 0.5-1.5% F1 score improvement over the original SVM baseline from Hidey and McKeown (2016). The authors support their findings with both quantitative results and qualitative analysis, including insightful examples of successful generalization—cases where the system correctly identifies non-causal relationships despite indicator words being frequently causal in the training data. This thorough analysis is a strength of the paper.

The paper is clearly written and effectively frames the problem. However, a more explicit discussion differentiating this task from implicit connective recognition would be beneficial. Specifically, addressing why existing methods developed for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable here would strengthen the work.

The authors are commended for publicly releasing their code, facilitating reproducibility and further research. The inclusion of the AltLex dataset alongside the code is also valuable. However, the paper lacks clarity regarding the 0-1-2 coding scheme used in the TSV files, particularly given the described binary classification task. Furthermore, the authors should clarify their rights to redistribute the Hidey and McKeown dataset. The meaning of “bootstrapping,” which expands the corpus by approximately 15%, also requires further explanation beyond the brief description of the corpus construction.

While exploring neural networks for this task is worthwhile, the significance of the improvements is not entirely convincing. The reported 2.13% F1 gain is based on selecting the best-performing configuration *on the test set*—a practice that risks overfitting. A more rigorous evaluation would involve selecting the optimal configuration using a development set. 

Moreover, the statistical significance of the improvement remains unclear. Given the dataset size, computing statistical significance measures would be appropriate. A discussion of the reliability of the gold-standard annotations is also warranted. The source of the annotations (English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia) suggests potential inconsistencies that could impact the reported scores.

The finding that neural networks outperform traditional feature engineering in this binary classification task aligns with established results. Consequently, the results, while positive, largely confirm existing knowledge. Future work could benefit from including experiments with simpler network architectures, such as a single-layer LSTM, to provide a stronger baseline comparison. Finally, analyzing the precision-recall trade-off—specifically why the model appears to favor precision—could offer valuable insights into the model's behavior.
