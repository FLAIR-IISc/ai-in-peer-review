This paper develops an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether they indicate an intended causal relation. The guiding idea is that the expression of causal relations is highly diverse and thus not readily amenable to syntactic treatment, and that the more abstract representations delivered by neural models are therefore more suitable for this task.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results offer modest but consistent support for the core idea, and provide initial insights into translating this idea into a concrete model. The paper distribution includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

However, I have several critical comments and questions:

* The introduction is unconventional, functioning more as a literature review than a clear overview of the paper’s contribution. This leads to redundancy with the subsequent related work section. While I am open to non-standard introductory approaches, this one is not particularly effective; despite reviewing a range of ideas, it does not establish a position on the nature of causation or its expression, focusing instead solely on a negative assertion (its non-reducibility to syntax). The positive contribution of the paper remains largely undefined until the final paragraph of the introduction.
* Expanding on this point, I found it disappointing that the paper lacks clarity regarding the underlying theory of causation it assumes. The authors appear to default to a counterfactual view, broadly aligned with that of David Lewis, where causation is framed as a modal sufficiency claim supplemented by other counterfactual conditions. (See line 238 and following; the implied relationship requires a specific type of implication to hold, a point addressed by well-known criticisms of Lewis’s theory – see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Although the paper contains statements suggesting the authors do not fully endorse the counterfactual view, it remains unclear what alternative theory is being adopted. Simply citing the temporal constraint on page 3 is insufficient.
* I am unclear regarding the comment about the example on line 256. The authors seem to suggest the sentence is false; if true, a causal link between the argument and breakage should exist. The paper also touches upon issues related to dividing events into sub-events, which are relevant to causal theories, but these are not further explored, leaving me confused.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram is intended to represent only the “Pair_LSTM” variant. My primary concern is the diagram’s lack of precision. While omitting details from the standard model definition in the prose might be acceptable, the diagrams should possess a clear and consistent semantics. What do the empty circles between the input and “LSTM” boxes represent? The text suggests the model comprises a look-up layer, a GloVe layer, and then…what? How many layers of representation are there? The diagram accurately depicts the pooling tanh layers pre-softmax but lacks detail regarding the earlier stages. The representation within the “LSTM” boxes is also unclear; it appears only the leftmost/final representation is directly connected to the subsequent layers, a connection which should be visually clarified.
* I do not understand the sentence beginning on line 480. The models discussed do not inherently require padding. This appears to be a consequence of TensorFlow’s requirements and/or efficient training, which is reasonable. However, the final clause is perplexing. How is this issue related to determining “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning”? I fail to see how convenience is a relevant consideration, or its direct link to causal meaning.
* The authors found that employing two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) yielded slightly better results than a sequential structure where the first LSTM feeds into the second. This issue mirrors debates in natural language entailment literature concerning whether to represent the premise and hypothesis independently or sequentially. I consider this an open question for entailment, and it likely requires further investigation in the context of causal relations. Therefore, I cannot endorse the assertion beginning on line 587: “This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers.” This is surprising, given the potential for substantial information sharing between subparts of a sentence.
* The hyperparameters that produced optimal performance across tasks are difficult to interpret. For example, compare line 578 with line 636. Should this be attributed to the inherent unpredictability of how these models interact with data, or is a more systematic explanation possible?
* Section 4.3 concludes that the system “correctly disambiguate[s]” the causal meaning of the connective ‘which then’, unlike the analysis presented by Hidey and McKeown. While this may be true, a single example is insufficient evidence. To strengthen this claim, I suggest creating a comprehensive set of examples showcasing the ambiguity and assessing the system’s accuracy on these cases. This will help address the possibility that the system simply achieved a fortunate outcome with the example from table 8.
