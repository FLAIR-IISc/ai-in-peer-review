Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles causal relation disambiguation between two events/clauses, focusing on cases where the relation is not explicitly marked by an unambiguous connective. Using the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), the authors propose an LSTM-based architecture that encodes the two arguments (plus the lexical marker, when present) with GloVe embeddings, followed by three tanh-activated dense layers and a final softmax layer.
- The main contribution, as I see it, is an empirical study showing that a relatively simple LSTM-based model can slightly outperform a feature-rich SVM baseline on AltLex, including some qualitative analyses where the model generalizes beyond the most frequent class of specific markers. The novelty is modest (the architecture is relatively shallow and standard), but the dataset application and analysis are useful.
- A secondary contribution is the release of code and data accompanying the submission, which aids reproducibility and inspection of examples.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and motivation. The paper explains well why context-sensitive causal disambiguation is needed, and why methods limited to fixed lexical/syntactic patterns are insufficient. The argument that LSTMs can better encode contextual semantics than CNNs (which require fixed kernels) is reasonable.
- Quantitative and qualitative evaluation. The reported improvements over Hidey and McKeown (2016) are small but consistent (best configuration improves by roughly 0.5–1.5 F1; elsewhere the paper cites a 2.13 F1 gain). The qualitative examples are informative, e.g., markers that are predominantly causal in training but correctly classified as non-causal in test, and ambiguous expressions such as “which then.”
- Reproducibility. Sharing code (and older data) is appreciated and allowed me to inspect the implementation details.
- Use of a recent, challenging dataset. Applying neural methods to AltLex is timely and helps illuminate the limits of feature-engineered approaches on ambiguous lexicalizations.

Weaknesses
- Model selection appears to use the test set. The paper reports results for multiple configurations (4–7 options) and highlights the best on the test data, including the “2.13% F1” improvement. A fair comparison requires selecting hyperparameters on a development set and reporting the corresponding test performance. Without this, the reported gains may be optimistic.
- Lack of significance testing and uncertainty estimates. Given the dataset size, statistical tests (e.g., approximate randomization or McNemar’s test) should be feasible to assess whether improvements over the SVM baseline are significant. Relatedly, the AltLex gold labels—constructed via English/Simple English alignments and bootstrapping—are known to be noisy; a discussion of annotation reliability and how it might affect reported scores would help contextualize the gains.
- Limited novelty and baselines. The architecture is standard for sentence-pair classification; the main result confirms a broad trend that neural models often outperform feature-based SVMs on binary classification. Stronger baselines would make the case more convincing, e.g., a single-layer LSTM with a simple pooling head, or even an average-of-embeddings model, to quantify how much the added dense layers help.
- Precision–recall imbalance. The neural models often favor precision over recall (and vice versa for some settings). The paper notes this but does not analyze why the model behaves this way (class imbalance? loss/thresholding? overfitting to unambiguous markers?). A deeper error analysis would strengthen the claims about contextual encoding.
- Task positioning. The paper would benefit from a clearer distinction between this task and implicit discourse relation (or implicit connective) recognition, and why prior methods for implicit relation classification (including neural models) cannot be directly applied or adapted here. This includes clarifying what additional information (e.g., an observed lexical marker “l”) is assumed available to the classifier.

Questions to authors
- Hyperparameter selection: Did you tune configurations on a separate development set? If not, please re-run with model selection on dev and report the corresponding test results for a fair comparison with Hidey and McKeown (2016).
- Significance: Are the improvements over the SVM baseline statistically significant? Please include significance testing (e.g., McNemar’s test) and confidence intervals where appropriate.
- Bootstrapping: The paper mentions that bootstrapping expands the corpus by about 15%. Please detail the bootstrapping procedure (seed alignments, expansion steps, noise filtering) and how it changes class and marker distributions across train/dev/test.
- Labeling and files: The TSV files use a 0–1–2 coding, yet the task is described as binary. What do the three values represent? How do they map to the Causal vs. Non-Causal labels used in evaluation?
- Data redistribution: Since the data originate from Hidey and McKeown (2016), do you have permission to repost it in your supplementary material? If redistribution is not permitted, consider providing scripts to reconstruct the dataset.
- Baselines: Could you add simpler neural baselines (e.g., a 1-layer LSTM or average-of-embeddings + MLP) and report their performance? This would help isolate the contribution of the proposed architecture.
- Precision vs. recall: What drives the precision–recall trade-off in your best settings (e.g., padding strategy, optimizer choice, class imbalance)? Have you explored class weighting, threshold tuning, or calibration to improve recall without sacrificing precision?
- Task relation: How does your task differ from implicit connective recognition in PDTB-style frameworks? Can existing implicit relation models be applied directly by treating the input pair as the two arguments, and if not, why?

Overall, the paper is clearly written and the analysis is thoughtful. With a stricter evaluation protocol (dev-based model selection, significance testing), clearer task positioning, and stronger baselines, the contribution would be more compelling.
