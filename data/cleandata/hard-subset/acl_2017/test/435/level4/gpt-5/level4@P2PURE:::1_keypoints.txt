- Paper proposes an LSTM-based model to classify whether connectives signal intended causal relations, arguing neural abstractions suit causal diversity better than syntactic heuristics.
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support for the approach; TensorFlow model code is included with the submission.
- Introduction reads like a literature review and fails to clearly state the paper’s positive contributions or take a clear position on what causation is.
- The paper is not explicit about the underlying theory of causation; it appears to lean on a Lewisian counterfactual view at points but is inconsistent and does not defend that choice.
- The discussion of the example around line 256 is confusing—authors seem to treat a sentence as false without addressing event segmentation or explaining the causal link.
- Figure 1’s caption and diagram are misleading and imprecise: unclear layer semantics, unexplained empty circles, ambiguous LSTM box meanings, and unclear connections to later layers.
- The sentence beginning on line 480 is unclear about padding requirements and how “convenience” relates to encoding causal meaning—likely a TensorFlow/training detail that needs clarification.
- The conclusion that independent LSTMs (Stated_LSTM) being better implies arguments should always be encoded independently is unjustified and surprising given potential shared information between subparts.
- Reported best hyperparameters are hard to interpret and inconsistent across sections (e.g., lines 578 vs 636), leaving unclear whether findings are robust or dataset/model idiosyncrasies.
- The claim that the system correctly disambiguates “which then” compared to Hidey and McKeown is based on a single example and needs a broader, systematic evaluation to be substantiated.
