- Paper proposes LSTM-based models to disambiguate whether a connective instance signals a causal relation between two events, arguing causality benefits from contextual encoding.

- Evaluates two architectures—Pair LSTM (separate encoders) and Stated Pair LSTM (second LSTM initialized with first)—on the AltLex corpus against an SVM baseline and majority-per-connective.

- Models use GloVe embeddings, tanh dense layers, and Adadelta/Adam optimizers; results show improved precision and overall accuracy with some optimizer/padding analysis and a small error analysis.

- Empirical contribution: a simple LSTM encoder over event contexts can match or surpass a strong feature-rich SVM on AltLex, especially improving precision and accuracy.

- Architectural/optimization contribution: comparative analysis of independent vs. stateful encoders, padding strategies, and optimizers gives practical modeling guidance.

- Reproducibility contribution: authors release TensorFlow models/code.

- Strength: Empirical gains over a strong baseline (notably ~2-point F1 increase for causal class and ~7–8 point accuracy gain on non-bootstrapped set), supported by statistical test in downsampled setting.

- Strength: Reduces feature engineering and reliance on lexical resources by using only pre-trained embeddings, aiding coverage for varied causal expressions.

- Strength: Systematic exploration of modeling choices (stateful vs independent encoders, padding strategies, optimizers) and discussion of precision/recall tradeoffs.

- Strength: Careful attention to data properties, including class/connective distributions and bootstrapping vs non-bootstrapping splits, plus downsampling experiments.

- Strength: Code availability should facilitate replication and extension.

- Weakness: Theoretical framing of causation is unclear and inconsistent; no concrete operationalization aligned with the annotations.

- Weakness: Model specification and figure clarity are lacking—unclear layers, pooling/aggregation, and exact use of LSTM states, which hampers reproducibility.

- Weakness: Overinterpretation of architecture comparisons—concluding a general principle from independent vs stateful encoder performance is too strong without controlled ablations.

- Weakness: Padding discussion conflates batching/tooling with modeling; padding effects should be framed as sequence aggregation/pooling issues with masking, not intrinsic meaning encodings.

- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope—results confined to AltLex; claims sometimes based on single examples and lack per-connective breakdowns or external corpus validation.

- Question: What operational theory of causation do you assume and how does it align with AltLex annotations, especially for temporal/counterfactual edge cases?

- Question: Why form the second input as "lexical marker + e2"? Did you try symmetric inputs or other marker placements and how sensitive are results to this choice?

- Question: Are max/mean/mode operations applied over token-level hidden states or embeddings, and are sequences masked to avoid padding influencing pooling?

- Question: Did you try bidirectional LSTMs, attention/cross-attention, or a full-context encoder, and how does a simple averaged-embeddings logistic baseline compare?

- Question: Can you provide per-connective performance, minimal-pair tests for ambiguous markers (e.g., "which then"), and broader robustness analyses?

- Question: Please specify all hyperparameters, early stopping, random seeds, and report stability across multiple runs given optimizer/pooling sensitivity.
