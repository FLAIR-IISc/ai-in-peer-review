This paper tackles the detection of causal relations between clauses using neural models (labeled “deep learning,” though the networks used are relatively shallow). While certain discourse connectives are unambiguous (e.g., “because” invariably signals causality), the work leverages the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to distinguish causal from non-causal relations when the signal is not a canonical explicit connective. The authors argue that LSTMs better capture the relevant clause-level features than CNNs, and propose a classifier that encodes GloVe embeddings with an LSTM, followed by three tanh-activated dense layers and a final softmax decision layer. The best configuration reports a ~0.5–1.5% F1 gain over the SVM-based approach of Hidey and McKeown (2016). The paper includes qualitative examples where the model correctly generalizes (e.g., markers predominantly causal in training that are correctly labeled non-causal at test), alongside quantitative evaluation. The writing is clear, and the problem framing is especially well presented. A clearer delineation from the task of implicit connective recognition would be helpful, potentially discussing why prior methods for that task are not directly applicable here. It is commendable that the authors shared code with the submission (briefly inspected, not executed), and that older data were included with the code to illustrate instances. However, the meaning of 0–1–2 labels in the TSV files is unclear given the binary classification setup, and it is uncertain whether the authors are permitted to redistribute Hidey and McKeown’s data. The paper should also clarify what “bootstrapping” entails—this appears to enlarge the corpus by roughly 15%, yet the specifics are not described beyond the corpus construction summary.

Despite the interest of applying neural models to this task, the strength of the contribution is not entirely compelling. It appears the best-performing configuration (among 4–7 variants) was selected based on test performance, and that is the setting reported as improving over Hidey and McKeown by “2.13% F1.” A fair comparison should choose hyperparameters on a development set. Furthermore, the significance of the gains is uncertain: given the dataset size, statistical significance testing should be feasible; in addition, the reliability of the gold annotations (constructed from English/Simple English alignment) is imperfect, so reported scores deserve caution. Finally, prior work has often shown neural methods outperform feature-engineered baselines on binary classification, so the findings largely confirm expectations. It would strengthen the paper to include simpler neural baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM). The error analysis could also probe why the neural approach seems to prioritize precision over recall.
