This paper introduces a technique for identifying causal relations between clauses using neural models (“deep learning,” though, as in many papers, the networks are not especially deep). While some discourse markers are unequivocal in signaling the relation (e.g., “because” indicates causality), the work leverages a recent dataset (AltLex, Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to tackle the problem of classifying causal vs. non-causal relations when no explicit marker is present. Arguing that convolutional networks are less suited than LSTMs to capture clause-level features, the authors present a classifier that uses GloVe-based clause representations fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three tanh-activated dense layers and a final softmax decision layer.

The best system configuration yields a 0.5–1.5% F1 improvement over Hidey and McKeown’s 2016 SVM baseline. The paper highlights several successful generalizations (e.g., words that are always causal in training but correctly identified as non-causal at test time). I therefore value the combination of quantitative and qualitative analysis.

The manuscript is clearly written, with an especially lucid problem formulation. That said, it would help to clarify how this task differs from implicit connective recognition, possibly including why prior methods for implicit connective recognition do not directly apply here.

It is commendable that the authors uploaded their code to the submission site (I examined it briefly but did not run it). Providing the older data alongside the code is also useful as it offers many examples. However, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files is unclear given that the paper frames the task as binary classification. I also wonder, since this is Hidey and McKeown’s data, whether the authors have the right to repost it. Additionally, the term “bootstrapping,” which appears to expand the corpus by roughly 15%, needs clarification: while the corpus construction is briefly yet clearly described, the bootstrapping process is not.

Although exploring neural networks for this task is interesting, the strength of the proposed approach is not entirely convincing. It appears that the best configuration (among 4–7 options) is selected based on test performance, and this is the configuration reported as improving over Hidey by “2.13% F1.” A fair comparison should choose the best configuration on the development set.

Furthermore, the significance of the reported gains is uncertain. Given the dataset’s size, it should be feasible to report statistical significance measures. One should also consider the reliability of the gold annotations (ideally from the dataset creators). Upon inspection, the annotations derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia are imperfect, so the reported scores should be interpreted cautiously.

Finally, prior work has shown neural methods outperform hand-engineered features on binary classification, so the results largely confirm a known trend. It would be valuable to include simpler neural baselines, such as a single-layer LSTM. The error analysis could also shed light on why the neural method appears to favor precision over recall.
