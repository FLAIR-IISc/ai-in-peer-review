This paper addresses the detection of causal relations between clauses without explicit discourse connectives, leveraging the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016). The authors argue that LSTMs better capture clause-level features than CNNs, and propose a classifier with GloVe embeddings, an LSTM layer, three tanh feed-forward layers, and a softmax output. On AltLex, the best reported configuration yields modest F1 improvements over Hidey and McKeown’s SVM baseline. The paper includes both quantitative results and illustrative examples showing that the model can correctly override spurious lexical cues (e.g., indicator words that are causal in training but not in test), which is valuable.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and generally well-written exposition.
- Sensible modeling choice for clause-level semantics; the architecture is standard and appropriate.
- Qualitative analysis demonstrates meaningful generalization beyond memorizing lexical indicators.
- Code availability is commendable and facilitates reproducibility; providing data examples is helpful.

Main concerns
- Evaluation protocol and model selection: It appears the best-performing configuration among several variants (4–7 options) was selected based on test-set performance and then reported as the main result (“2.13% F1” improvement). This constitutes tuning on the test set and likely inflates the reported gains. A fair comparison requires choosing hyperparameters on a development set and reporting the single resulting test score.
- Magnitude and significance of improvements: The reported improvements are small (0.5–1.5 F1 in places; elsewhere 2.13 F1). Given the dataset size, the authors should assess statistical significance (e.g., paired bootstrap or approximate randomization over per-instance decisions; McNemar’s test on accuracy if appropriate). Also report variance across multiple random seeds to ensure stability.
- Dataset and annotation reliability: The English/Simple English Wikipedia-based annotations are known to be noisy. The paper should discuss the reliability of the gold standard (ideally citing inter-annotator agreement from the dataset creators) and temper claims accordingly. If noise is substantial, confidence intervals or robustness checks would help.
- Task positioning: The relationship to implicit connective recognition is not fully clarified. Please make the distinction explicit and explain why methods for implicit connectives cannot be directly applied here (or include such baselines if they can).
- Bootstrapping procedure: The paper notes a ~15% corpus expansion via “bootstrapping,” but the process is under-specified. Please detail seeds/patterns used, iteration criteria, noise filtering, stopping rules, and whether bootstrapped data is used for training only. Report its impact via ablations.
- Label encoding and data redistribution: The provided TSV files use 0–1–2 labels while the paper frames the task as binary. What do these labels denote (e.g., non-causal/uncertain/causal)? How were they mapped during training/evaluation? Also, clarify whether you have permission to redistribute the AltLex data or consider providing scripts to obtain it instead.
- Baselines and ablations: While neural methods often outperform feature-engineered SVMs on binary classification, stronger and simpler neural baselines should be included to isolate where the gains come from. In particular, a 1-layer LSTM with and without pre-trained embeddings, and a shallow MLP over averaged embeddings, would clarify the contribution of architectural depth and pre-training. An ablation removing the extra dense layers would test their necessity on this dataset size.
- Precision–recall trade-off: The reported results seem to favor precision over recall. Please analyze calibration and thresholding, class balance, and whether class weighting or focal loss changes the trade-off. A PR curve or per-class confusion analysis would make this concrete.
- Claims about CNN vs LSTM: The paper asserts that CNNs are less adept at representing clause features. Provide empirical evidence via a tuned CNN baseline (e.g., multi-filter widths, max-pooling) and/or a hybrid CNN+RNN, or soften the claim.

Suggestions for improvement
- Use a clean model-selection protocol: Choose hyperparameters on a dev set or via nested cross-validation. Report the single test score, plus mean ± std over multiple seeds.
- Unify performance reporting: Reconcile the differing improvement numbers (0.5–1.5 vs 2.13 F1). Include confidence intervals or significance tests, and provide per-class precision/recall/F1 with macro/micro aggregates as appropriate.
- Expand ablations: Report results for:
  - 1-layer LSTM vs deeper or stacked LSTMs (if applicable).
  - With/without pre-trained GloVe vs random initialization.
  - With/without the three dense tanh layers.
  - CNN baseline and SVM baseline re-implemented under identical splits.
- Regularization and capacity: Justify the depth (three dense layers) and parameter count relative to dataset size. Report dropout rates, early stopping criteria, and any weight decay. Provide learning curves to show under/overfitting.
- Error analysis: Beyond indicator-word generalization, categorize common false positives/negatives (e.g., lexical ambiguity, world knowledge, discourse structure) and discuss whether syntactic cues or dependency paths could help.
- Task clarity: More explicitly contrast causal relation detection without explicit markers with implicit connective recognition. If they overlap, consider evaluating on a standard implicit discourse dataset or adapting those models as baselines.

Minor comments
- Standardize citations and terminology (GloVe; Hidey and McKeown, 2016; AltLex). Avoid calling the model “deep” unless architectural depth is central to the contribution.
- Ensure the code package documents preprocessing, label mapping, bootstrapping details, and exact train/dev/test splits to enable faithful replication.
- If redistribution rights are uncertain, provide download scripts and document licensing.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a relevant problem and presents a reasonable neural approach with some qualitative insights. However, the current evaluation protocol and the small, potentially non-significant gains limit the strength of the empirical claims. With a stricter model-selection process, clearer task positioning, better-documented data handling (especially bootstrapping and labels), and stronger baselines/ablations, the work would be substantially more convincing.
