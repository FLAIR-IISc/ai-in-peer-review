This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying, for a given connective occurrence, whether it signals a causal relation. The motivation is that causal expressions are highly diverse and not easily captured by syntactic rules, so distributed, context-sensitive representations learned by neural models may be better suited to the task.

Experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown), and the results offer modest but consistent support for the general idea, with some useful observations about model design (e.g., input pairing and optimization choices). The submission also includes the TensorFlow code used in the experiments.

Some critical comments and questions:
- The introduction reads more like a related-work survey than an overview of the paper’s goals and contributions. This leads to redundancy with the subsequent related-work section. I’m open to non-standard introductions, but this one doesn’t work well: it mainly advances a negative thesis (causality isn’t reducible to syntax) without clearly stating the positive view of causation and its linguistic expression that the paper adopts. As a result, the intended contribution remains vague until the final paragraph of the section.

- Relatedly, the paper is not clear about its assumed theory of causation. It seems to default to a broadly Lewisian counterfactual view, where causation is a kind of modal sufficiency (see line 238 and following). For that to work, the arrow needs to be a very special implication, and Lewis’s account has well-known issues (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere the paper suggests it does not endorse a counterfactual view; if so, what is the operative theory? It cannot be just the temporal constraint on page 3.

- I do not understand the comments around the example on line 256. The paper seems to suggest the sentence is false. If it is true, then there should be some causal link between the argument and the breakage. There are hard questions about event granularity and sub-events that bear on causation, but the paper does not engage them here, leaving the status of the example unclear.

- The caption for Figure 1 is misleading: the diagram depicts only the Pair_LSTM variant. More broadly, the figure is unnecessarily imprecise. It is acceptable to omit standard components from the prose, but then the diagrams should have a consistent semantics. What are the empty circles between the input and the LSTM boxes? The prose mentions a lookup layer and GloVe embeddings, but the figure does not clearly indicate how many representational layers there are before the LSTM. It also appears that only the final LSTM state connects to the upper layers; if so, please depict that explicitly.

- The sentence starting at line 480 is confusing. The models themselves do not intrinsically require padding; this is presumably a TensorFlow and/or batching efficiency requirement. If that is correct, please say so. I also do not see how the choice among padding strategies relates to finding “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning.” Convenience seems orthogonal to the semantics of causal encoding.

- The finding that two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) outperform the variant in which the first feeds into the second is interesting and reminiscent of debates in textual entailment: should premise and hypothesis be encoded independently or sequentially? I see this as an open question there and likely here as well. Thus, I do not agree with the conclusion at line 587 that “our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold,” and that independent encodings are therefore better. Given that the two spans are parts of the same sentence and can share information, this conclusion seems too strong based on the current evidence.

- The hyperparameter story is hard to interpret. For instance, compare line 578 with line 636. Should we read anything into these patterns (e.g., optimizer–padding interactions), or are these idiosyncrasies of the data/model fit? A brief discussion would help readers understand whether any design choices are robust.

- Section 4.3 ends by claiming that the system “correctly disambiguate[s]” the causal meaning of which then, whereas Hidey and McKeown’s system does not. One example does not suffice to establish this. I suggest constructing a small controlled set of sentences illustrating the ambiguity of which then and reporting accuracy on that set. This will help determine whether the system got lucky with the example in Table 8 or has learned generalizable cues.

Overall, the paper tackles a worthwhile problem with a reasonable neural approach and provides empirical evidence that context-sensitive encoding helps with causal disambiguation. The work would be substantially strengthened by a clearer statement of the underlying theory of causation being assumed, a sharper and more consistent model description, and more cautious interpretation of comparative results.
