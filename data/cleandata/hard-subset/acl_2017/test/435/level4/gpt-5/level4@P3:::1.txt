Summary
This paper proposes LSTM-based models to disambiguate whether a candidate connective instance signals a causal relation between two events. The authors argue that causality in language is not tied to fixed syntactic patterns and thus benefits from models that encode contextual meaning. They evaluate two architectures—a “Pair LSTM” (separate encoders for each input) and a “Stated Pair LSTM” (the second LSTM initialized with the first)—on the AltLex corpus of Hidey and McKeown, comparing against a majority-per-connective baseline and the original SVM-based system. The models use GloVe embeddings, tanh-activated dense layers, and Adadelta/Adam optimizers. Results show competitive to superior performance, particularly improved precision and overall accuracy, with some analysis of optimizer and padding strategies and a small error analysis.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A simple LSTM encoder over event contexts can match or surpass a strong feature-rich SVM baseline on AltLex, especially in precision and accuracy, supporting the claim that contextual encoding helps causal disambiguation.

2) Architectural/optimization insights: Comparative analysis of independent vs. stateful LSTM encoders, padding strategies, and optimizers (Adadelta vs. Adam) provides useful guidance for modeling choices in this task.

3) Reproducibility: Release of TensorFlow models/code used in the experiments.

Strengths
- Empirical gains over a strong baseline: On the non-bootstrapping AltLex set, the best LSTM configuration improves F1 for the causal class by about 2 points and accuracy by roughly 7–8 points over the SVM baseline; on the bootstrapping set, several LSTM configurations are competitive or better. The improvements are supported by a McNemar test in the downsampled setting.

- Reduced feature engineering and reliance on lexical resources: Using only pre-trained word embeddings, the models avoid dependence on limited-coverage lexicons (e.g., WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank), which is desirable for handling varied or novel causal expressions.

- Analysis across modeling choices: The paper systematically explores stateful vs. independent encoders, zero-padding strategies (max/mean/mode), and optimizers, and discusses their impact on precision/recall balance—useful for the community.

- Attention to data properties: The authors carefully describe class/connective distributions, discuss the “bootstrapping” vs. “non-bootstrapping” splits, and perform additional experiments with class downsampling to probe robustness.

- Code availability: The inclusion of code should facilitate replication and extension.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical framing of causation is unclear and sometimes inconsistent: The paper gestures toward counterfactual and temporal constraints but does not adopt a clear stance or cite a concrete operationalization aligned with the annotation. The argument that causation is “not reducible to syntax” is reasonable, but the positive account of what is being modeled remains vague. This weakens the motivation and interpretation of results.

- Model specification and figure clarity: Figure 1’s caption and contents are confusing (e.g., empty circles, what exactly the “LSTM” boxes denote, whether the final states or pooled representations are used). The text mentions a look-up layer, GloVe embeddings, and then “LSTM,” but the number and nature of layers between embeddings and LSTM, and the exact pooling/aggregation, are not crisply described. This hampers reproducibility despite code availability.

- Overinterpretation of architecture comparisons: The finding that independent encoders outperform the stateful variant is interesting, but the conclusion that “the assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two events does not hold” is too strong. As in natural language inference, whether to condition one sequence on the other vs. encode independently can be data- and architecture-dependent. More controlled ablations (e.g., attention-based interaction) would be needed to claim a general principle.

- Padding rationale and its connection to meaning: The discussion of zero-padding strategies suggests an intrinsic link to “the most convenient way to encode causal meaning,” but padding is primarily a batching/tooling issue. If pooling strategies (max/mean/mode) over time are being compared, this should be framed explicitly as alternative sequence aggregation functions, with a clearer rationale and preferably masking to avoid padding artifacts.

- Limited evaluation scope and some claims based on single instances: The main results are confined to AltLex. While this is a reasonable starting point, generalization to other corpora (e.g., PDTB explicit causal relations, Causal-TimeBank) would strengthen the contribution. The qualitative analysis occasionally overclaims—e.g., disambiguation of “which then” is illustrated with a single example. Per-connective breakdowns and targeted tests for ambiguous connectives would substantiate these claims.

Questions to the Authors
- Theory and annotation alignment: What theory of causation do you assume operationally for this task? How does it align with the AltLex annotations, especially for cases where temporal precedence is violated or counterfactual dependence is unclear?

- Input representation: Why is the second input formed as “lexical marker + e2”? Did you try symmetric inputs (e1 and e2 without concatenating the marker) or including the marker with both? How sensitive are results to this choice?

- Sequence aggregation and padding: Are max/mean/mode operations applied over token-level hidden states (i.e., pooling) or over embeddings? Are sequences properly masked to avoid padding influencing pooling? Please clarify the need for fixed-length batching and how padding interacts with the pooling choice.

- Architectural variants: Did you try bidirectional LSTMs, attention over the other sequence (e.g., cross-attention), or a sentence-level encoder over the full context without pre-segmenting into e1/e2? A comparison with a simple averaged-embeddings logistic regression baseline would also contextualize gains.

- Robustness analyses: Can you provide per-connective performance, especially for ambiguous markers (e.g., since, after, which then)? For “which then,” could you construct a set of minimal pairs to quantify disambiguation beyond a single instance?

- Reproducibility details: Please specify all model hyperparameters (hidden sizes, number of LSTM layers, dropout rates, regularization coefficients), early stopping criteria, and random seed handling. Some results suggest optimizer/pooling sensitivity; a stability analysis over multiple runs would help.

Overall, this is a timely and relevant paper with solid empirical results and useful modeling insights. Clarifying the theoretical framing, tightening the model description, tempering some interpretations, and expanding the evaluation/analysis—particularly for ambiguous connectives—would substantially strengthen the submission.
