This paper proposes an LSTM-based classifier for connective usage, determining whether a connective signals an intended causal relation. The core motivation is that causal expression is highly varied and thus ill-suited to purely syntactic analysis; accordingly, the more abstract representations learned by neural models are a better foundation for these judgments.

The experiments use the AltLex corpus of Hidley and McKeown. The results give modest but steady support for the overall idea and offer preliminary guidance on how to implement it in a model. The submission includes the TensorFlow implementations used in the experiments.

Some critical comments and questions:
* The introduction reads more like an extended literature review than a proper overview of the paper, creating overlap with the subsequent related work section. I am open to unconventional introductions, but this one falls short: it surveys many ideas without taking a position on what causation is or how it is expressed, beyond the negative claim that it is not reducible to syntax. The section does not clearly articulate the positive contribution until its very general final paragraph.
* Relatedly, the paper is not explicit about the assumed theory of causation. The authors seem to lean toward a broadly Lewisian counterfactual account, where causation resembles a form of modal sufficiency with additional counterfactual conditions (see line 238 and following). For that to work, the arrow must denote a very specific kind of implication, and there are well-known issues with Lewis’s view (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere the authors suggest they do not endorse a counterfactual view, but then what is the operative theory? It cannot be just the temporal constraint mentioned on page 3.
* I do not follow the discussion around the example on line 256. The authors appear to treat the sentence as false. If it is true, there should be some causal connection between the argument and the breakage. While there are open questions about event granularity and sub-events that bear on causal theories, those are not addressed here, which leaves the analysis unclear.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the graphic appears intended to represent only the “Pair_LSTM” variant. More importantly, the diagram is unnecessarily vague. If some standard components are omitted from the prose, then the diagrams should have precise and consistent semantics. What do the empty circles between the inputs and the “LSTM” blocks denote? The text describes a lookup layer, a GloVe layer, and then … what exactly? How many representational layers are there? The figure is precise about the pooling and tanh layers before the softmax, but not about the earlier parts. It is also unclear what the “LSTM” boxes stand for; it seems only the leftmost/final state feeds into the upper layers. Please make that connection explicit in the figure.
* I do not understand the sentence beginning on line 480. These models do not inherently require padding. If padding is introduced for TensorFlow compatibility and/or training efficiency, please say so. The final clause is also unclear: how does this relate to identifying “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning”? Why is convenience relevant, and how does this connect to causal semantics?
* The finding that two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) outperform a setup where the first feeds the second echoes debates in natural language entailment about independent versus conditional encoding of premise and hypothesis. I view this as unresolved for entailment, and it likely warrants further study for causal relations as well. Thus I cannot endorse the statement on line 587 that this behavior shows the assumed relationship between the two input meanings does not hold and that it is therefore better to encode each argument separately and compare them with dense layers. This is surprising given that these are subspans of a single sentence and may share substantial information.
* The hyperparameters associated with the best results are difficult to reconcile across tasks. See line 578 versus line 636, for instance. Should we infer a pattern here, or chalk this up to the often unpredictable ways these models interact with data?
* Section 4.3 concludes that the system can “correctly disambiguate” the causal sense of the connective “which then,” in contrast to Hidey and McKeown. That may be right, but a single example is insufficient to establish the claim. To support it, please construct a diverse set of instances exhibiting the ambiguity and evaluate how often the system makes the correct call. This would help determine whether the Table 8 instance was merely fortuitous.
