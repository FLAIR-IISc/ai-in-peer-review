- Paper tackles clause-level causal relation detection without explicit discourse connectives using the AltLex dataset.
- Model: GloVe embeddings → LSTM → three tanh feed-forward layers → softmax, claiming LSTMs better than CNNs for clause semantics.
- Reports modest F1 improvements over Hidey & McKeown’s SVM baseline, with best config showing ~2.13% F1 gain.
- Qualitative analysis shows the model can override spurious lexical cues, demonstrating meaningful generalization.
- Code and data examples are provided, aiding reproducibility.

- Major concern: model selection appears to be tuned on the test set (selecting best config among variants), inflating reported gains.
- Improvements are small; statistical significance and variance across random seeds are not reported.
- AltLex annotations are noisy; paper omits discussion/citation of annotation reliability or inter-annotator agreement.
- Relation to implicit connective recognition is unclear; need to clarify differences or include those baselines.
- Bootstrapping procedure (15% corpus expansion) is under-specified: seeds, iterations, filtering, stopping rules, and usage not detailed.
- Label encoding in redistributed TSVs (0–1–2) is unexplained and task framed as binary; mapping and redistribution permissions are unclear.
- Baselines and ablations are insufficient—stronger/simple neural baselines and ablations (e.g., 1-layer LSTM, MLP over averaged embeddings) are missing.
- Precision-focused results need calibration analysis, class-balance discussion, PR curves, and exploration of loss weighting or thresholding.
- Claim that CNNs are inferior lacks empirical support via tuned CNN or hybrid baselines.

- Use a clean model-selection protocol (dev set or nested CV) and report single test score plus mean±std over seeds.
- Reconcile differing reported improvement numbers and include confidence intervals/significance tests and per-class precision/recall/F1.
- Expand ablations: 1 vs stacked LSTM, pretrained vs random embeddings, with/without dense layers, tuned CNN and SVM under identical splits.
- Justify model capacity and regularization choices; report dropout, weight decay, early stopping, and provide learning curves to show fit.
- Broaden error analysis to categorize false positives/negatives and discuss whether syntactic or dependency features could help.
- Clarify task positioning vs implicit connective recognition and, if relevant, evaluate or adapt implicit-connective models.

- Minor: standardize citations/terminology and avoid calling the model “deep” unless justified.
- Ensure code documents preprocessing, label mapping, bootstrapping details, and exact train/dev/test splits for replication.
- If redistribution rights are uncertain, provide download scripts and license documentation.

- Overall: relevant problem and reasonable neural approach with useful qualitative insights, but weak evaluation protocol, small/possibly non-significant gains, and insufficient documentation/baselines limit the paper’s empirical claims.
