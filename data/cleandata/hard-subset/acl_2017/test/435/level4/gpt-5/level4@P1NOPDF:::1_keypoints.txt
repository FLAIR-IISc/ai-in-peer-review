- Presents an LSTM-based model to classify connective uses as signaling intended causal relations, arguing neural abstractions handle causal diversity better than syntax.  
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support and the authors release TensorFlow code.  
- Introduction reads like a literature review and is redundant with related work, leaving contributions vague until the end.  
- The paper’s theoretical stance on causation is unclear and inconsistent (appears Lewisian/counterfactual in places but not endorsed); this must be clarified.  
- The analysis of the example at line 256 is confusing regarding truth and event segmentation, and lacks a clear causal interpretation.  
- Figure 1’s caption and diagram are misleading and imprecise: unclear lookup/GloVe/representation layers, LSTM semantics, and explicit connections to upper layers.  
- The comment on padding (line 480) is unclear—likely an implementation/training-efficiency detail—but the paper should state that and explain relevance to encoding meaning.  
- The finding that independent LSTMs outperform conditional encoding echoes entailment debates; the authors’ conclusion that their assumption fails is premature and needs further investigation.  
- Hyperparameter choices appear inconsistent (e.g., lines 578 vs 636) and are hard to interpret versus being artifacts of deep model-data interactions.  
- The claim that the system disambiguates “which then” is based on a single example and requires broader, targeted evaluation to establish robustness.  
- Overall positive: useful empirical contribution and valuable released code; recommended fixes include clarifying causal theory, tightening intro/related work, making model figures precise, explaining implementation-driven choices, and probing generalization on connective ambiguities.
