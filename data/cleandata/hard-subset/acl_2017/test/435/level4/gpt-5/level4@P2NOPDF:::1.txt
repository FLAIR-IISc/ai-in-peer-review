This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses according to whether they signal an intended causal relation. The central motivation is that causal expression is highly heterogeneous and not well captured by syntax alone, so more abstract neural representations are a better basis for this decision. Experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus of Hidey and McKeown. The results show modest but consistent gains supporting the overall approach, and the paper offers initial guidance on effective modeling choices. The authors also release TensorFlow implementations used in the experiments.

Comments and questions:
- The introduction reads more like a literature review than an overview of the paper’s contributions, creating redundancy with the related work section. A less conventional introduction could be fine, but this one neither articulates a clear stance on what causation is nor how it is expressed; instead it mainly asserts a negative claim (that causation is not reducible to syntax). Apart from a brief final paragraph, the positive contribution is not clearly foreshadowed. I recommend restructuring to (i) state the paper’s concrete research questions and contributions, (ii) define the assumed view of causal meaning, and (iii) briefly preview the modeling and experimental design.
- More broadly, the paper is not explicit about its underlying theory of causation. It appears to lean on a counterfactual view akin to David Lewis’s, where causation is a kind of modal sufficiency under additional counterfactual conditions (see line 238ff). For that to work, the “arrow” must be a highly constrained implication, and Lewis-style accounts face well-known challenges (e.g., Copley & Wolff 2014: http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere you suggest you do not endorse a counterfactual theory; if so, what theory do you assume instead? It cannot be just the temporal precedence constraint noted on page 3. Please state and justify the theoretical commitments that guide your data annotations and modeling choices.
- I do not follow the discussion around the example on line 256. You seem to treat the sentence as false. If the sentence is true, there should be some causal link between the argument and the breakage. While event granularity and sub-event structure complicate causal analysis, those issues are not addressed here, leaving the rationale unclear. Please clarify the truth-conditional and causal judgments you are making, and how they interact with your event segmentation assumptions.
- The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram depicts only the Pair_LSTM variant. More importantly, the figure is needlessly imprecise. The prose mentions a lookup layer, a GloVe layer, and then leaves the remaining architecture ambiguous. What do the empty circles between the input and the “LSTM” boxes denote? How many representation layers are there? What exactly do the “LSTM” boxes represent—full sequences or just the final hidden state passed upward? The pooling and tanh layers before the softmax are shown precisely; the lower stack should be equally clear. I suggest redrawing the figure with explicit layer names, shapes, and connections (e.g., indicate whether you use last hidden state vs. max/mean pooling over time).
- The sentence beginning on line 480 is confusing. The models themselves do not intrinsically require padding; that is typically for batching/efficiency (or framework constraints in TensorFlow). If that is the reason, please state it directly. The final clause about “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning” seems orthogonal to padding decisions; clarify how convenience or causal encoding is implicated here, if at all.
- You find that using two independent LSTMs (Stated_LSTM) outperforms a setup where one feeds into the other. This mirrors debates in natural language inference about independent versus conditioned premise/hypothesis encoders. I view this as unresolved in NLI, and it likely merits deeper study for causal relations too. Thus I cannot endorse the claim on line 587 that this behavior falsifies your assumption about the relation between the two arguments and therefore implies it is “better” to encode them independently. Given that the two spans are subparts of a sentence and may share substantial information, stronger evidence and analysis are needed before drawing that conclusion.
- The hyperparameters yielding the best performance are hard to interpret across tasks (cf. line 578 vs. line 636). Should we view these differences as meaningful, or as artifacts of data/model stochasticity and interactions? A brief discussion of sensitivity analyses or variance across random seeds would help.
- Section 4.3 concludes that the system can “correctly disambiguate” the causal meaning of the connective “which then,” whereas Hidey and McKeown’s system does not. That may be true, but one example is insufficient to support the claim. To substantiate it, please construct a broader set of minimal pairs and diverse contexts illustrating the ambiguity and report accuracy across them. This would also help determine whether the success on Table 8 is robust or incidental.

Overall assessment:
- Strengths: Clear motivation for moving beyond syntax in modeling causal meaning; consistent, if modest, empirical gains; helpful release of TensorFlow code; and interesting preliminary findings about encoder configurations.
- Weaknesses: Lack of a clearly stated theoretical stance on causation; imprecise architectural description (especially in Figure 1); several unclear statements (padding rationale, specific example judgments); and overinterpretation of limited empirical evidence (encoder conclusion; “which then” claim).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the theoretical framework for causation guiding your annotations and modeling, and ensure consistency with the examples discussed.
- Tighten the introduction to foreground contributions and reduce overlap with related work.
- Make the architecture specification precise in both prose and figures (layers, pooling choice, what states are passed forward, and where).
- Explain implementation choices (e.g., padding) as engineering constraints rather than semantic ones, unless you can show the latter.
- Temper claims about encoder superiority and connective disambiguation, or back them with broader, controlled evaluations and stability analyses across seeds/hyperparameters.
