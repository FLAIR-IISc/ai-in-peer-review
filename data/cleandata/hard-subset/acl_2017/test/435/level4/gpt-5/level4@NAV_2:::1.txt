This paper presents an LSTM-based approach to determine, for occurrences of potentially causal connectives, whether the intended relation is causal. The central premise is that causal expression in language is highly varied and not easily captured by fixed syntactic templates; thus, neural representations that encode context are better suited for this decision. Experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus of Hidey and McKeown. The findings offer modest but consistent support for the premise and provide some initial guidance on effective architectural and optimization choices. The paper distribution includes the TensorFlow implementations used.

Some critical comments and questions:
- The introduction reads more like an extended literature survey than a roadmap for the paper, which leads to redundancy with the related work section. More importantly, it advances mainly a negative claim (causation is not reducible to syntax) without clearly articulating a positive account of what causation is or how it is linguistically encoded. The concrete contribution is only briefly stated at the end, leaving the framing underspecified.
- The paper is not clear about the underlying theory of causation. It appears to assume a broadly Lewisian counterfactual view (e.g., line 238 and the e1 → e2 notation), which requires a very specific interpretation of the arrow and faces well-known challenges (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere, the paper suggests it does not endorse the counterfactual view. If so, what is the operative theory? It cannot be just the temporal constraint noted on page 3.
- The discussion around the example at line 256 is confusing. The text seems to treat the sentence as false. If the sentence is true, then some causal connection between the argument and the breakage must be in play. Issues about event granularity and sub-event structure are highly relevant but not addressed, which makes the stance hard to interpret.
- Figure 1’s caption is misleading, as the figure depicts only the Pair_LSTM variant. More generally, the diagram lacks precision. What do the empty circles between the inputs and the LSTM blocks represent? The prose mentions a lookup and GloVe layer, but the figure does not make clear how many representational layers are used or how they connect. Do the “LSTM” boxes feed only final states upward? If so, depict that explicitly. The pooling/tanh layers pre-softmax are spelled out; the lower parts should be equally explicit.
- The sentence starting at line 480 is unclear. These models do not inherently require padding; this seems to be for TensorFlow compatibility and/or efficient batching. If that is the reason, please state it. Also, the claim about “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning” seems orthogonal to padding strategy—please clarify the conceptual link.
- The finding that two independent LSTMs (Stated_LSTM) outperform the version where the first feeds the second echoes architectural debates in textual entailment (premise/hypothesis encoded independently vs. sequentially). I would not endorse the strong conclusion at line 587 that the assumed relation between inputs “does not hold” and that encoding them independently is therefore “better.” Given possible shared information within a sentence, this deserves a more cautious interpretation and further study.
- The hyperparameter story is hard to interpret. Lines 578 and 636 point to different choices as “best.” Is there a generalizable pattern here, or should we chalk this up to data/model idiosyncrasies?
- The claim in Section 4.3 about ‘which then’ would benefit from broader evidence. One illustrative example is not enough to establish systematic disambiguation. Consider constructing a diverse set of ‘which then’ instances to quantify how often the model chooses correctly, to rule out a lucky case.
