This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses according to whether they signal an intended causal relation. The motivation is that causal expressions are highly diverse and thus not easily captured by syntactic heuristics, whereas neural models provide more abstract representations that may be better suited to this decision.

The experiments use the AltLex corpus developed by Hidey and McKeown. The results provide modest but consistent support for the core idea and offer initial guidance on how best to operationalize it in a model. The paper distribution includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Comments and questions:
- The introduction reads more like a literature review than an overview of the paper’s contributions, leading to redundancy with the related work section. I am open to a non-standard introduction, but this one does not work: it surveys many ideas without taking a clear position on what causation is or how it is expressed, beyond the negative claim that it is not reducible to syntax. The positive contribution remains vague until the general concluding paragraph of the section.
- Relatedly, the paper is not clear about the theory of causation it assumes. The text seems to default to a broadly Lewisian counterfactual view, where causation is a modal sufficiency claim supplemented with further counterfactual conditions. See line 238 and following; the arrow there must be a very special kind of implication for this to be tenable, and there are well-known problems with Lewis’s theory (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere the authors suggest they do not endorse the counterfactual view; if so, what is the operative theory? It cannot be just the temporal constraint noted on page 3.
- I do not understand the discussion of the example on line 256. The authors seem to be treating the sentence as false. If it is true, then there should be some causal link between the argument and the breakage. There are open issues about event decomposition into subevents that bear on causal theories, but those are not discussed here, which leaves the status of the example unclear.
- The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, since the diagram depicts only the Pair_LSTM variant of the model. More importantly, the diagram is needlessly imprecise. It is fine to omit standard model details from the prose, but then the figures should have clear and consistent semantics. What are the empty circles between the input and the LSTM boxes? The prose mentions a lookup layer and a GloVe layer; what comes next? How many representational layers are there? The diagram is precise about the pooling and tanh layers before the softmax, but not about earlier components. It is also unclear what the “LSTM” boxes represent; it appears to be only the final representation that connects to the upper layers. I suggest making those connections explicit in the diagram.
- I do not understand the sentence beginning on line 480. The models do not intrinsically require any padding. I assume this is a TensorFlow and/or training efficiency requirement; if so, please say so. I also do not understand the final clause: how is this related to identifying “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning”? It is not clear why convenience is relevant here or how it bears on causal meaning.
- The finding that two independent LSTMs (Stated_LSTM) perform somewhat better than a configuration where the first feeds into the second echoes debates in natural language entailment about whether to encode the premise and hypothesis independently or sequentially. I consider this an open question for entailment, and it likely warrants further investigation for causal relations as well. For that reason, I cannot endorse the sentence beginning on line 587: “This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers.” This is especially surprising given that subparts of a sentence may share substantial information.
- It is difficult to make sense of the hyperparameters that led to the best performance across tasks. Compare line 578 with line 636. Should we interpret these differences, or attribute them to idiosyncrasies in how the models interact with the data?
- Section 4.3 concludes that for the connective “which then,” the system can “correctly disambiguate its causal meaning,” whereas the system of Hidey and McKeown does not. That may be correct, but a single example does not suffice to support the claim. To substantiate it, I suggest constructing a diverse set of examples that instantiate the ambiguity and evaluating how often the system selects the correct interpretation. This would help determine whether the success in Table 8 is representative or merely a lucky case.
