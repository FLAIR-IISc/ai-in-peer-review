Summary
This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying whether particular connective uses indicate an intended causal relation. The core motivation is that causal meaning is expressed in highly diverse ways that are not easily captured by syntactic methods; neural models may therefore be better suited to the task by learning abstract representations. Experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus of Hidey and McKeown. Results show modest but consistent gains over baselines and offer preliminary insights into modeling choices. The paper distribution includes TensorFlow implementations used in the experiments.

Strengths
- Clear, timely problem: causal interpretation of connectives is important and challenging.
- Empirical contribution: consistent improvements on AltLex suggest the approach has promise.
- Modeling exploration: comparison of architectural variants provides useful initial guidance.
- Reproducibility: code release is a strong plus.

Major comments and questions
- Introduction and positioning: The introduction reads more like a literature review than a focused overview, and it overlaps with the related work section. It advances primarily a negative thesis (causal meaning is not reducible to syntax) without stating a clear positive account of causal meaning or a crisp statement of the paper’s contributions. Please reframe the introduction to: (i) define causal meaning as used in the paper; (ii) state concrete research questions/hypotheses; (iii) preview the model, dataset, and key findings.

- Theoretical commitments about causation: The paper is unclear about the assumed theory of causation. Several passages (e.g., around line 238) suggest a broadly Lewisian counterfactual view where causation is a kind of modal sufficiency, but other comments imply this is not endorsed. If the arrow used in the formalization is not material implication, what is its intended semantics? Given well-known issues for strict counterfactual treatments (e.g., Copley & Wolff 2014), the paper should articulate either (a) an explicit theoretical stance, or (b) a clear operationalization that decouples the empirical task from contentious metaphysics (e.g., a dataset-driven definition of “causal” labels). Relying only on a temporal precedence constraint (as mentioned on p.3) seems insufficient.

- Example on line 256: The discussion is confusing about truth conditions. If the sentence is considered true, then some causal link between the argument and the breakage should follow on the paper’s view. If it is considered false, please make that explicit and explain why. Clarify how event segmentation and sub-event structure are treated, since these choices affect judgments about causation.

- Figure 1 caption and diagram: The caption suggests the figure depicts only the Pair_LSTM variant, but the diagram is ambiguous. The empty circles between the input and “LSTM” boxes are unlabeled and their function is unclear. The prose mentions a lookup layer and GloVe embeddings—are there additional embedding/projection layers? How many layers are used before the recurrent layer, and are embeddings fine-tuned? What exactly do the “LSTM” boxes denote (full sequence encoders vs. final states vs. pooled outputs)? Since the pre-softmax pooling and tanh layers are depicted precisely, the lower layers should be equally precise. Please revise the caption to specify the variant and redraw the diagram to match the textual specification (including explicit connections from final/pooled states to the upper layers).

- Padding and “convenience” (line 480): Standard LSTMs do not intrinsically require padding; padding is typically for batching/efficiency or framework constraints. If padding is a TensorFlow or minibatching requirement, please say so. The claim that this relates to “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning” is unclear—convenience is an engineering choice, not a semantic one. Please disentangle implementation constraints from claims about semantic encoding.

- Independent vs. pipelined encoders: The finding that two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) outperform a model where one feeds into the other is interesting and reminiscent of results in NLI on independent vs. conditional encoding. However, the conclusion at line 587 (“our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold; therefore independent encoding is better”) seems too strong. Subclausal arguments often share information; conditional or joint modeling might still help if interaction is modeled appropriately (e.g., cross-attention, shared encoders with late interaction, bi-affine relations). I would frame this as an empirical observation for this dataset/model family and encourage broader exploration before drawing theoretical conclusions.

- Hyperparameter stability: The optimal hyperparameters appear inconsistent across tasks (e.g., compare lines 578 and 636). Is this noise due to data size/splits, sensitivity to random seeds, or true task-specific preferences? Please report variance across multiple runs, include statistical significance testing, and consider ablations that isolate the effect of key hyperparameters (embedding fine-tuning, hidden size, dropout, pooling). Without this, it is hard to interpret the drivers of performance.

- Evaluation of specific connectives (“which then”): The claim that the system “correctly disambiguates” this connective relative to Hidey & McKeown is currently supported by a single example. To substantiate, please construct a small suite of controlled examples that span the ambiguity space and report accuracy and error types. Alternatively, annotate a targeted subset of AltLex instances for this connective and compare both systems systematically.

Clarity and presentation
- Please standardize terminology and model names in text, tables, and figures (e.g., Pair_LSTM, Stated_LSTM), and correct minor typos (e.g., “GloVe”).
- Provide complete architectural specifications: tokenization, vocabulary, OOV handling, embedding dimensions and whether they are updated, number of LSTM layers and directions, pooling operations, activation functions, and regularization (dropout/weight decay).
- Document training details for reproducibility: optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, gradient clipping, early stopping criteria, class balance and any reweighting, and the exact preprocessing used for padding/truncation.

Overall assessment
This is a promising paper addressing an important problem. The empirical results offer modest but consistent gains and the code release is commendable. However, the paper would benefit from (i) a clearer statement of its theoretical stance or operational definition of causal meaning; (ii) a more precise and self-consistent model specification and figure; (iii) stronger, more stable empirical evidence (multiple runs, significance tests, and targeted evaluations of specific connectives); and (iv) more cautious interpretation of architectural comparisons. With these revisions, the contribution would be much clearer and its empirical claims more convincing.
