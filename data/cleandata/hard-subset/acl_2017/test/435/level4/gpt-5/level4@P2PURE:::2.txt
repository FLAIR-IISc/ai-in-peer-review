This paper presents a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks—“deep learning,” though the networks are not particularly deep. While some discourse connectives unambiguously signal a relation (e.g., “because” is causal), the authors leverage the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to address the task of identifying causal vs. non-causal relations when no explicit marker is present. Arguing that convolutional networks are less adept than LSTMs at capturing relevant clause features, they propose a classifier that uses GloVe-based clause representations fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three tanh-activated dense layers and a final softmax decision layer.

The best configuration improves F1 by 0.5–1.5% over Hidey and McKeown’s 2016 SVM classifier. The paper also highlights several generalization cases where the system performs well (e.g., indicator words that are always causal in the training data but are correctly identified as non-causal in the test data). I therefore appreciate the combination of qualitative and quantitative analyses.

The paper is well written, and the problem description is particularly clear. However, it would be helpful to clarify how this task differs from implicit connective recognition, possibly including a discussion of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition are not applicable here.

I appreciate that the authors uploaded their code to the submission site (I inspected it briefly but did not execute it). Providing the older data with the code is also useful, as it offers many examples. However, the meaning of the 0–1–2 coding in the TSV files is unclear, given that the paper presents a binary classification task. I also wonder, since this is Hidey and McKeown’s data, whether the authors have the right to repost it as they do. In addition, the paper should clarify what is meant by “bootstrapping,” which apparently expands the corpus by about 15%; while the corpus construction is briefly but clearly explained, the additional bootstrapping is not.

While it is certainly interesting to experiment with neural networks on this task, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. It appears that the best configuration (among 4–7 options) was selected based on test results, and this configuration is reported to improve over Hidey by “2.13% F1.” A fair comparison would select the best configuration on the development set.

Moreover, the significance of the improvement is not entirely clear. Given the dataset size, it should be possible to compute statistical significance measures. One should also consider the reliability of the gold-standard annotations (ideally with input from the dataset creators). Upon inspection, the annotations derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia are not perfect, so the scores should be interpreted with caution.

Finally, neural methods have previously been shown to outperform hand-engineered features on binary classification, so these results largely confirm a known property. It would be valuable to include simpler neural baselines, such as a 1-layer LSTM. The analysis could also explore why the neural approach appears to favor precision over recall.
