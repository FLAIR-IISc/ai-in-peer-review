Summary and contributions
This paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation: given two events and a potential causal marker in context, determine whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that causality is not tied to fixed lexical/syntactic patterns and propose an LSTM-based architecture that encodes the two arguments (and marker) with pre-trained GloVe embeddings, followed by dense layers. They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), reporting small but consistent improvements over a feature-based SVM baseline (B2) and over a strong connective-prior baseline (B1), with both the original and the bootstrapped versions of the corpus. They also provide qualitative analyses and release code.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that contextual neural encoding (LSTM) can modestly improve causal relation disambiguation over feature-based methods on AltLex, including on ambiguous markers and distribution-shifted cases.
2) An analysis of precision/recall trade-offs across optimizers, padding strategies, and architecture variants, plus qualitative examples showing generalization beyond connective priors.
3) Practical resources: code release and a discussion of dataset characteristics (e.g., ambiguity, bootstrapping, class imbalance).

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and motivation. The paper situates causal disambiguation within broader linguistic debates (e.g., limits of causative verbs/syntax) and motivates context-sensitive modeling, which aligns with the data characteristics of AltLex (ambiguous/variant markers).
- Solid empirical setup on both AltLex variants. The authors evaluate on the original and bootstrapped splits, explicitly noting the increased difficulty from distributional differences in the latter. Performance is reported both as Causal-class F1 and overall accuracy; trends are analyzed across configurations.
- Consistent improvements over strong baselines. On the non-bootstrapped set, the best LSTM variant improves over B2 by +2.13 F1 (Causal) and +7.72 accuracy. On the bootstrapped set, multiple LSTM variants slightly outperform B2 in F1/accuracy and notably improve precision (e.g., +3.89 pp precision with Pair LSTM Mean Adam), indicating better control of false positives than the feature-based system.
- Qualitative analysis that goes beyond marker priors. The paper shows cases where the model overrides misleading connective priors from training (e.g., break without causation; “which then”), supporting the claim that contextual encoding helps disambiguation rather than memorizing connective frequencies.
- Reproducibility. Code is provided, and the paper documents key implementation choices (GloVe 840B 300d; LSTM variants; zero-padding strategies; Adam vs. Adadelta; dropout/regularization ranges).

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The core architecture (two-input LSTM + MLP) is standard. The paper’s main value is empirical; the methodological contribution is incremental. Claims about CNNs being less suitable are not substantiated by a direct CNN baseline on AltLex.
- Potential selection bias in model choice. It appears the “best configuration” is chosen based on test-set performance across 4–7 options. A fair comparison requires hyperparameter selection on a development set, followed by a single test evaluation. Without this, reported gains (e.g., +2.13 F1) may overestimate generalization.
- Statistical significance and robustness. The paper uses McNemar’s test only to compare against B1 on a downsampled variant. No significance is reported versus B2 on the original splits. Given the modest gains, statistical testing (e.g., McNemar, bootstrap resampling) versus B2 is needed, with multiple random seeds to account for initialization variance.
- Scope relative to implicit discourse relation classification (IDRC). The task overlaps with implicit connective recognition in PDTB-style discourse parsing. The paper briefly mentions this but does not clearly contrast the tasks or explain why IDRC methods (e.g., BiLSTM/attention models, context2vec-style features) are not directly applicable. This limits the positioning of the work within the broader literature.
- Dataset and reproducibility details need clarification. “Bootstrapping” is only briefly mentioned, yet it changes class distributions and difficulty; more detail is needed for replicability. Likewise, the TSV label coding (0–1–2) is unclear for a binary task. Finally, re-posting AltLex data may raise licensing concerns; this should be addressed.
- Baseline coverage and ablations. There is no comparison to simpler neural baselines (e.g., single-layer LSTM; average-of-embeddings + MLP), nor ablations to quantify the contribution of including the marker “l” vs. events alone, or the effect of encoding e1→e2 state transfer (Stated Pair) vs. independent encoders (Pair). Given the central claim about contextual encoding, such ablations would strengthen the conclusions.
- Precision–recall imbalance not fully explained. Many configurations favor precision over recall. It would help to analyze whether this stems from class imbalance, marker ambiguity, or optimizer effects, and whether threshold tuning or class weighting could improve recall without sacrificing precision.

Questions to authors
- Hyperparameter selection: Did you use a held-out development set for model selection and early stopping? If not, can you rerun with a dev/test split and report results for a single selected configuration?
- Significance: Can you report statistical significance versus B2 on the original AltLex splits (e.g., McNemar, approximate randomization), with multiple random seeds?
- Task positioning: How does your setup differ from implicit discourse relation classification? Can you compare against a strong IDRC baseline or explain why it does not apply here?
- Baselines/ablations: Please include results for (i) a 1-layer LSTM and (ii) avg-embeddings + MLP; (iii) a CNN baseline; and (iv) ablate the inclusion of the lexical marker in the second input.
- Dataset details: What do TSV labels 0–1–2 denote? Please clarify the bootstrapping process and provide licensing information/permissions for redistributing AltLex.
- Class imbalance and thresholds: Did you try class weighting, focal loss, or decision-threshold tuning to improve recall? Any calibration analysis?

Additional comments
- The two-step framing (meaning disambiguation, then argument directionality) is reasonable; a brief roadmap for the second step and how the current encoder could be reused would be useful.
- Minor: please proofread references for formatting inconsistencies and missing details (venues/pages/years).
