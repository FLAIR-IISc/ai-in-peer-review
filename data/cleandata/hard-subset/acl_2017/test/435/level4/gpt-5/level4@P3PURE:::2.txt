This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks (described as “deep learning,” though, as in many studies, the networks are not particularly deep). While certain discourse connectives are unambiguous with respect to the relation they signal (e.g., “because” is causal), the paper leverages a recent dataset (AltLex; Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to address the task of identifying causal vs. non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. Arguing that convolutional networks are less adept than LSTMs at representing the relevant features of clauses, the authors propose a classification architecture that uses a GloVe-based clause representation fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three tanh-activated dense layers and a final softmax decision layer.

The best configuration improves by 0.5–1.5% F1 over Hidey and McKeown’s 2016 SVM classifier. The paper also presents several examples of useful generalizations—e.g., indicator words that are always causal in the training data but are correctly identified as non-causal in the test data. I appreciate that the system is analyzed both qualitatively and quantitatively.

The paper is well written, and the description of the problem is particularly clear. However, a clarification of the differences between this task and implicit connective recognition would be welcome, including a discussion of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition cannot be applied here.

It is commendable that the authors uploaded their code to the submission site (I inspected it briefly but did not execute it). Uploading the (older) data with the code is also useful, as it provides many examples. That said, it was not clear to me what the 0–1–2 coding in the TSV files signifies, given that the paper presents a binary classification task. I also wonder, since this is the dataset from Hidey and McKeown, whether the authors have the right to repost it as they do. Additionally, the paper should clarify the meaning of “bootstrapping,” which apparently extends the corpus by about 15%. While the construction of the corpus is briefly but clearly explained, this additional bootstrapping step is not.

While it is certainly interesting to experiment with neural networks on this task, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. It appears that the best configuration (among 4–7 options) is selected on the test data, and this configuration is reported as improving over Hidey and McKeown by “2.13% F1.” A fair comparison would require selecting the best configuration on the development set.

Moreover, it is not entirely clear how significant the improvement is. Given the size of the dataset, it should be possible to compute statistical significance indicators. It would also be helpful to consider the reliability of the gold-standard annotation itself (possibly with guidance from the dataset creators). Upon inspection, the annotation obtained from the English/Simple English Wikipedia is not perfect; therefore, the reported scores should be interpreted with caution.

Finally, neural methods have previously been shown to outperform human-engineered features for binary classification tasks, so in a sense the results confirm a known property. It would be informative to include simpler neural baselines (e.g., a 1-layer LSTM). The analysis of results could also explore why the neural method appears to favor precision over recall.
