- Presents an LSTM-based neural model using GloVe for detecting causal relations without explicit connectives (AltLex dataset).
- Reports modest F1 improvements (~0.5–1.5 pts) over Hidey & McKeown (2016) SVM; best config claims a 2.13 F1 gain.
- Qualitative analysis shows generalization beyond lexical cues (correctly flags non-causal despite causal-leaning terms).
- Paper is clearly written and problem formulation is well presented.
- Needs clarification on how this task differs from implicit connective recognition and whether prior implicit discourse methods transfer.
- TSV files use 0–1–2 labels but the task is framed as binary—clarify the label scheme and mapping to binary decisions.
- Confirm whether redistribution of Hidey & McKeown data is permitted and adjust the released files to comply with licensing.
- Describe the “bootstrapping” that expanded the corpus by ~15%: procedure, selection criteria, quality control, and effects on splits.
- Concern that best configuration was chosen using the test set—model selection should be based on dev set and test used only once.
- Report results averaged over multiple runs with different random seeds to show variance and robustness.
- Conduct statistical significance testing (e.g., bootstrap or approximate randomization) to support claimed gains.
- Discuss annotation quality and inter-annotator agreement or known error rates for the dataset and temper claims accordingly.
- Contribution is incremental; neural models outperforming feature-engineered baselines on binary tasks is unsurprising without stronger evidence.
- Include simpler neural baselines (single-layer LSTM, shallow MLP over averaged embeddings) to quantify marginal benefits.
- Provide ablation studies (remove dense layers, vary embeddings, compare LSTM vs BiLSTM vs CNN) to isolate which components drive improvements.
- Analyze error types, decision thresholds, and precision–recall trade-offs, and discuss calibration or tuning for recall/precision.
- Overall implementation and qualitative examples are valuable, but addressing task positioning, reproducibility, model selection, significance, and stronger baselines/ablations is necessary.
