- Paper proposes an LSTM-based model to classify whether connectives signal intended causal relations, evaluated on the AltLex corpus with modest but consistent gains and released TensorFlow code.
- Introduction is too much like a literature review and fails to clearly state research questions, contributions, or the paper’s stance on causation; recommend restructuring to preview methods and claims.
- The paper does not state or justify a clear theoretical framework for causation (appears to lean on a Lewis-style counterfactual view); theoretical commitments guiding annotations and modeling must be explicit.
- The analysis around the example on line 256 is unclear about truth-conditional and causal judgments and how event segmentation/granularity affects those judgments.
- Figure 1 is misleading and imprecise (shows only Pair_LSTM); the architecture diagram and prose must explicitly name layers, shapes, pooling choices, and what LSTM outputs are used.
- The discussion about padding (line 480) is confusing; padding is typically an engineering/batching issue and should be explained as such unless causal-encoding implications are demonstrated.
- The claim that independent LSTMs outperform conditioned encoders does not by itself falsify assumptions about argument relations; more analysis is needed before concluding independence is superior.
- Hyperparameter differences across tasks are hard to interpret; include sensitivity analyses and report variance across random seeds to assess stability.
- The “which then” disambiguation claim is based on a single example and is insufficient; construct broader minimal-pair tests and report accuracy across diverse contexts.
- Strengths: clear motivation to go beyond syntax, consistent empirical gains, released code, and interesting preliminary findings on encoder configurations.
- Weaknesses: unclear theoretical stance on causation, imprecise architectural description, several unclear statements (padding, example judgments), and overinterpretation of limited empirical evidence.
- Suggested improvements: clarify theoretical framework and annotation rationale; tighten the introduction; precisely specify architecture in text and figures; explain implementation choices; and temper claims or support them with broader, controlled, and stability evaluations.
