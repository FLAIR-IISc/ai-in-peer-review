This paper proposes a neural approach to detecting causal relations between clauses, focusing on cases where the relation is not explicitly marked. While certain discourse connectives are unambiguous (e.g., “because” signals causality), the authors leverage the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to classify causal vs. non-causal relations without explicit markers. Arguing that convolutional networks are less effective than LSTMs at capturing clause-level features, the paper presents a model that uses GloVe-based clause embeddings fed into an LSTM, followed by three tanh-activated dense layers and a softmax output.

The best configuration reportedly improves over Hidey and McKeown’s 2016 SVM system by roughly 0.5–1.5 F1 points, and the paper provides qualitative examples of successful generalization (e.g., words that appear always causal in training but are correctly predicted as non-causal in test). I appreciate both the quantitative evaluation and the qualitative analysis.

The paper is clearly written, and the problem is well motivated. However, the distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition needs clarification. In particular, please discuss how this setting differs from implicit relation prediction in discourse parsing, and why prior methods for implicit connective recognition are not directly applicable here.

It is commendable that the authors uploaded their code (I briefly inspected it) and included the older data, which offers helpful examples. However, the meaning of the 0–1–2 coding in the TSV files is unclear given that the paper frames the task as binary classification; please explain these labels. Also, since the data originate from Hidey and McKeown, please clarify whether you have permission to redistribute them. Finally, the paper mentions “bootstrapping” that expands the corpus by about 15%, but this process is not described; given its impact on training data size and distribution, a clear account of the bootstrapping procedure is needed.

While applying neural models to this task is interesting, the empirical claims are not fully convincing. It appears that the best configuration (among 4–7 options) was selected based on test-set performance, and the reported “2.13% F1” improvement relies on this selection. A fair comparison should choose hyperparameters and architectures on a development set and report a single test result.

The magnitude and reliability of the gains also deserve closer scrutiny. Given the dataset size, statistical significance testing should be feasible. Moreover, the gold-standard quality warrants discussion: the English/Simple English Wikipedia–derived annotations are imperfect, so small differences in F1 should be interpreted cautiously. Comments from the dataset creators or an inter-annotator agreement summary would help contextualize results.

Finally, since neural methods often outperform feature-engineered models on binary classification, these results largely confirm an established trend. Stronger baselines would make the contribution clearer—for example, including a simpler one-layer LSTM and other lightweight architectures. It would also be valuable to analyze why the neural approach appears to favor precision over recall and whether this bias arises from class imbalance, thresholding, loss weighting, or architectural choices.
