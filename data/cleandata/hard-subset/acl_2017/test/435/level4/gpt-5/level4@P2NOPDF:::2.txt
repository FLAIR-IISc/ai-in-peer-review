This paper presents a neural approach to detecting causal relations between clauses, focusing on cases where the relation is not explicitly marked by a clear connective. While some connectives (e.g., “because”) unambiguously indicate causality, the authors leverage the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to distinguish causal from non-causal relations when no explicit marker is present. Arguing that CNNs are less effective at capturing clause-level features than LSTMs, they propose an architecture that uses GloVe embeddings fed into an LSTM layer, followed by three tanh-activated dense layers and a final softmax classifier.

The best reported configuration improves F1 by roughly 0.5–1.5 points over Hidey and McKeown’s 2016 SVM baseline. The paper also includes qualitative analysis, showing generalization beyond lexical indicators (e.g., terms that are always causal in training but correctly identified as non-causal in test), which I appreciate. Overall, the paper is clearly written, and the problem formulation is well presented.

However, the distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition needs clarification. Please explain how the objectives differ and why methods for implicit connective prediction do or do not transfer here. A brief comparison to prior approaches for implicit discourse relation classification would strengthen the positioning.

I appreciate that the authors uploaded code with examples and the older data; I skimmed the code but did not run it. A few issues need attention:
- The TSV files use 0–1–2 labels, yet the paper frames the task as binary. Please clarify the label scheme and how it maps to the binary decision.
- Since the data come from Hidey and McKeown, please confirm whether reposting/distribution is permitted and, if necessary, adjust the release to comply with licensing.
- The paper mentions “bootstrapping” that expands the corpus by about 15%, but this process is not described. Please detail the procedure, criteria for adding instances, quality control, and its impact on train/dev/test splits.

Regarding methodology, I am not fully convinced by the claims of improvement. The best-performing configuration (among four to seven options) appears to have been selected based on test-set performance, and the paper highlights a 2.13 F1 gain using that choice. For a fair comparison, model selection should be performed on the development set, with the test set used only once for final evaluation. Please report dev-based model selection results and, ideally, include variance over multiple runs with different random seeds.

It would also help to quantify the robustness and significance of the reported gains. Given the dataset size, statistical testing (e.g., bootstrap resampling or approximate randomization) should be feasible. In addition, the quality and reliability of the gold annotations—especially those derived from English/Simple English Wikipedia—appear imperfect upon inspection. Please discuss inter-annotator agreement or known error rates from the dataset creators, and temper claims accordingly.

From a broader perspective, it is not surprising that neural models can outperform feature-engineered baselines on binary classification, so the contribution is incremental. Stronger empirical grounding would come from:
- Including simpler neural baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM or a shallow MLP over averaged embeddings) to quantify the marginal benefit of the deeper architecture.
- Providing ablations (e.g., removing dense layers, varying embedding initializations, comparing LSTM vs. BiLSTM vs. CNN) to isolate which components drive gains.
- Analyzing error types and decision thresholds to explain the observed precision–recall trade-offs, and whether tuning for recall (or calibrating probabilities) changes the balance meaningfully.

Despite these concerns, the paper’s focus on unmarked causal relations is valuable, the implementation details are generally clear, and the qualitative examples are informative. With clearer task positioning relative to implicit connective recognition, a transparent and dev-driven model selection protocol, significance testing, and stronger baselines/ablations, the work would be notably more compelling. Clarifying the data labeling scheme, the legality of redistribution, and the bootstrapping procedure is also essential for reproducibility and for assessing the validity of the reported improvements.
