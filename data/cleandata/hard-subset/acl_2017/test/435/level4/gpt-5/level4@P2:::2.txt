Summary
The paper tackles causal relation disambiguation between two textual arguments using a neural approach. Building on the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), the authors aim to classify relations as causal vs. non-causal in cases where explicit connectives are absent or ambiguous. They argue LSTMs are better suited than CNNs for capturing clause-level meaning. Their model uses GloVe embeddings, an LSTM to encode the inputs, several tanh-activated dense layers, and a final softmax classifier. Empirically, the best configuration yields a small improvement over the feature-based SVM of Hidey and McKeown, and the paper includes qualitative analyses of successes on ambiguous markers.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
- A neural architecture for causal relation disambiguation that encodes the meanings of the textual arguments (primary).
- An empirical evaluation on AltLex with modest improvements over a strong feature-based baseline, plus qualitative error analysis (secondary).

Strengths
- Focus on context-driven disambiguation. The paper addresses causal markers that are ambiguous or absent, an area where pattern- or resource-driven systems struggle. The model’s reliance on contextual encoding aligns with prior observations that causality is not tied to fixed syntactic patterns.
- Competitive results with analysis. The best configuration improves over Hidey and McKeown’s SVM by roughly 1–2 F1 points (the paper cites 2.13% F1 on one setting) and increases accuracy. The qualitative examples are useful, showing cases where the model correctly overrides indicator priors learned from training (e.g., terms usually causal in training but non-causal at test).
- Clear problem framing and writing. The task is well motivated and explained, and the paper distinguishes explicit vs. implicit/AltLex signaling with concrete examples.
- Reproducibility effort. Providing code (and example data) is commendable and facilitates inspection and reuse.

Weaknesses
- Model selection appears to use test data. The paper reports the best configuration among several options (e.g., padding strategies, optimizers) based on test performance and highlights those gains as SOTA. A fair comparison requires selecting hyperparameters on a development set and reporting the single, dev-selected system on test. As presented, the reported improvement could reflect test-set tuning.
- Limited evidence on significance and annotation reliability. Given the dataset size, significance testing (e.g., McNemar or bootstrap) should be reported for the main comparisons. Moreover, the AltLex construction process can introduce noise (Wiki/SimpleWiki alignment, bootstrapping), so a discussion of label reliability and the expected variance in scores is warranted.
- Insufficient positioning vs. implicit connective recognition. The paper should clarify how this task differs from implicit discourse relation recognition and why prior methods from that literature are not directly applicable here (or provide a comparison). Currently, the relationship is only briefly touched upon.
- Reporting and data clarity. Several practical points need clarification: the meaning of the 0–1–2 codes in the shared TSV files despite framing the task as binary; what “bootstrapping” adds to the corpus and how those instances differ from the initial set; and whether the authors have permission to redistribute the AltLex-derived data.
- Narrow baseline set and limited ablations. The gains over feature-based SVM are small and consistent with the general advantage of neural encoders on sentence-level classification. Stronger or simpler neural baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM, BiLSTM with max/mean pooling, or even average-of-embeddings + MLP) and ablations (number of dense layers, with/without marker) would make the contribution more convincing. The observed precision–recall trade-off (model favoring precision) deserves analysis (e.g., which markers or contexts are missed).

Questions to authors (optional)
- Task scope: How does your task relate to implicit discourse relation classification in PDTB-style settings? Could established implicit DRR methods be adapted here, and if so, how do they compare?
- Data handling: What do the 0–1–2 labels mean in your TSV files? Are you authorized to redistribute the AltLex-derived data bundled with your code?
- Bootstrapping: Please define the “bootstrapping” process that expands the corpus, its quality controls, and how it affects class distributions across train/dev/test.
- Model selection and significance: Did you tune hyperparameters on a separate dev set? If not, please provide dev-based selection and report test results for that single setting. Can you report statistical significance for the main comparisons?
- Baselines and analysis: Can you include results for a single-layer LSTM (and/or average-of-embeddings + MLP) and analyze why your model emphasizes precision over recall (e.g., error categories, marker frequency effects, sentence length, or domain effects)?

Overall assessment
A clear, well-written paper on an important subproblem in causality detection, with a reasonable neural approach and careful qualitative examples. However, the empirical claims would be stronger with dev-set model selection, significance testing, clearer positioning against implicit relation work, and more comprehensive neural baselines and ablations. Addressing these points would solidify the contribution.
