This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses according to whether they signal an intended causal relation. The core premise is that causal expressions are highly diverse and not well captured by syntactic heuristics, making abstract representations from neural models a more suitable basis for such decisions.
The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results provide modest but consistent support for the overall idea and offer initial guidance on how best to operationalize it in a model. The paper distribution includes the TensorFlow-based models used in the experiments.

Some critical comments and questions:
* The introduction reads more like a literature review than an overview of the paper’s contributions, which creates redundancy with the subsequent related work section. I’m open to a nonstandard introduction, but this one is not effective: despite surveying many ideas, it takes no position on what causation is or how it is expressed, beyond the negative claim that it is not reducible to syntax. Other than a very general final paragraph, the introduction does not clearly state the paper’s positive contribution.
* Relatedly, the paper is not explicit about the theory of causation it assumes. The authors seem to default to a broadly Lewisian counterfactual view, where causation amounts to modal sufficiency with additional counterfactual constraints. See line 238 and following: the arrow would have to be a very particular kind of implication for this to work at all, and there are well-known problems with Lewis’s theory (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere the paper suggests the authors do not endorse the counterfactual view, but then what theory is being assumed? It cannot be just the temporal constraint mentioned on page 3.
* I do not understand the comments about the example on line 256. The authors seem to treat the sentence as false. If it is true, there should be some causal link between the argument and the breakage. There are open questions about how to segment events into sub-events and how that affects causal theories, but those issues are not addressed here, which leaves me confused.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram appears to depict only the Pair_LSTM variant. More broadly, the diagram is needlessly imprecise. It is fine to omit standard components from the prose, but then the diagrams should have a clear, consistent semantics. What are the empty circles between the inputs and the “LSTM” boxes? The prose suggests a lookup layer, a GloVe layer, and then … what? How many representational layers are there? The diagram is precise about the pooling/tanh layers before the softmax, but not about the earlier layers. It is also unclear what the “LSTM” boxes denote; it seems only the leftmost/final representation feeds into the upper layers. I recommend making that connection explicit in the figure.
* I do not understand the sentence beginning on line 480. The models themselves do not intrinsically require padding. I assume this is a TensorFlow and/or training-efficiency requirement; if so, please say that. I also do not see how the final clause relates to “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning.” Why is convenience relevant here, and how does it pertain directly to causal meaning?
* The authors report that two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) perform somewhat better than a model where the first feeds into the second. This echoes debates in the natural language entailment literature about whether to encode the premise and hypothesis independently or sequentially. I view that as an open question for entailment, and likely for causal relations as well. Thus I cannot endorse the sentence beginning on line 587: “This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers.” This is surprising, given that the two subparts of a sentence may share substantial information.
* The hyperparameters that yielded the best performance across tasks are hard to interpret. Compare line 578 with line 636, for instance. Should we draw conclusions from this, or attribute it to the often unpredictable ways these models interact with data?
* Section 4.3 concludes that for the connective “which then,” the system can “correctly disambiguate its causal meaning,” whereas Hidey and McKeown’s does not. That may be true, but a single example is not sufficient. To substantiate the claim, I suggest constructing a diverse set of sentences that exhibit the ambiguity and evaluating how often the system reaches the correct judgment. This would address whether the result on Table 8 is representative rather than a one-off.
