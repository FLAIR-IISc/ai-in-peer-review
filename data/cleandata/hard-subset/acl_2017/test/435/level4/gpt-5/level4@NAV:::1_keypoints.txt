- Proposes an LSTM-based classifier to decide whether a connective signals an intended causal relation, arguing neural representations suit varied causal expression better than syntax.  
- Experiments use the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown) and show modest but consistent support; TensorFlow code is provided.  
- Introduction reads like an extended literature review and fails to clearly state the paper’s positive contribution or take a clear stance on causation early.  
- Paper is not explicit about its theoretical stance on causation; it appears to lean toward a Lewisian/counterfactual account but is inconsistent about endorsing that view.  
- Analysis of the example on line 256 is unclear—authors treat the sentence as false without addressing event granularity or explaining the causal connection.  
- Figure 1 caption is misleading and the diagram is vague/underspecified (missing details on lookup/GloVe layers, empty circle semantics, LSTM state flow).  
- Sentence around line 480 is unclear about padding and why “convenience” of encoding relates to causal semantics; padding rationale should be stated.  
- The result that independent LSTMs outperform a stacked setup mirrors entailment debates and does not conclusively show the assumed relationship between subspans is absent.  
- Hyperparameter choices vary inconsistently across tasks (e.g., lines 578 vs. 636), making it hard to draw generalizable patterns.  
- Claim that the system “correctly disambiguates” “which then” is based on a single example and needs evaluation over a diverse set of ambiguous instances.  
- Overall contribution: provides preliminary empirical support and implementation guidance, but methodological, theoretical, and presentation issues need clarification and stronger evaluation.
