- Paper proposes LSTM-based models to decide whether occurrences of potentially causal connectives express causation, arguing neural context representations handle varied causal expression better than fixed syntactic templates; experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support and the authors release TensorFlow code.

- Introduction reads like an extended literature survey, is redundant with Related Work, and fails to clearly frame a positive account of what causation is or how it is encoded linguistically; the concrete contribution is only briefly stated.

- The paper is unclear about its underlying theory of causation: it sometimes appears Lewisian/counterfactual but elsewhere disavows that view, and no operative theory beyond a temporal constraint is established.

- The analysis of the example around line 256 is confusing about truth conditions and ignores important issues of event granularity and sub-event structure, making the authors’ stance hard to interpret.

- Figure 1’s caption and diagram are misleading and imprecise (it only shows the Pair_LSTM variant); components like the empty circles, representational layers, LSTM outputs, and lower-layer connections are not clearly depicted.

- The sentence around line 480 about padding is unclear—padding seems like an implementation/batching choice for TensorFlow, not an inherent model requirement, and the asserted link to “encoding causal meaning” needs clarification.

- The result that independently encoded LSTMs (Stated_LSTM) outperform sequential encoding echoes debates in textual entailment; the strong claim that the assumed relation “does not hold” is premature and requires more cautious interpretation and further study about shared intra-sentence information.

- The hyperparameter findings are inconsistent (different “best” choices cited at lines 578 and 636), leaving unclear whether there is a generalizable pattern or just data/model idiosyncrasy.

- The single illustrative example for the claim about ‘which then’ is insufficient; the authors should evaluate a diverse set of ‘which then’ instances and quantify model accuracy to rule out a lucky case.
