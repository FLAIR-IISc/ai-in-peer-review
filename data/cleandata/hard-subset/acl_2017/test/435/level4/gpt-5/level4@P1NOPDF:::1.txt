This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses according to whether they signal an intended causal relation. The guiding idea is that expressions of causation are highly diverse and thus not well captured by syntax alone; abstract representations learned by neural models are therefore a better basis for these decisions.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidey and McKeown. The results offer modest but consistent support for the overall approach and provide initial guidance on how to instantiate the idea in a model. The paper distribution includes the TensorFlow implementations used in the experiments.

Some critical comments and questions:
* The introduction reads more like a literature review than an overview of the paper’s contributions, creating redundancy with the subsequent related work section. I am open to a non-standard introduction, but this one is not effective: it reviews many ideas without taking a clear stance on what causation is or how it is expressed, beyond the negative claim that it is not reducible to syntax. The positive contribution remains vague until the final paragraph.
* Relatedly, the paper is not clear about the underlying theory of causation. The authors seem to default to a broadly Lewisian counterfactual view, where causation is a kind of modal sufficiency with additional counterfactual conditions (see line 238 and following). For this to work, the arrow must be a very specific kind of implication, and there are well-known problems with Lewis’s theory (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere the authors suggest they do not endorse a counterfactual account. If so, what theory is being assumed? It cannot be just the temporal constraint mentioned on page 3.
* I do not understand the discussion of the example on line 256. The authors seem to suggest that they regard the sentence as false. If it is true, then there should be some causal link between the argument and the breakage. There are indeed issues about event segmentation into sub-events that affect causal analysis, but those issues are not addressed here, leaving the discussion unclear.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, since the diagram appears to depict only the Pair_LSTM variant. More importantly, the diagram is needlessly imprecise. It is fine to omit standard model components from the prose, but then the figures should have a clear, consistent semantics. What are the empty circles between the input and the “LSTM” boxes? The text suggests there is a lookup layer, a GloVe layer, and then … what? How many representational layers are there? The diagram is precise about the pooling and tanh layers before the softmax, but not about earlier components. It is also unclear what the “LSTM” boxes denote; it seems only the final state is connected to the upper layers. Please depict that connection explicitly.
* I do not understand the sentence beginning on line 480. The models themselves do not intrinsically require padding; I assume this is a TensorFlow and/or training-efficiency requirement. If so, please say that explicitly. I also do not understand the final clause about “the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning.” How is convenience relevant here, and how does padding relate to causal meaning?
* The finding that two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) perform somewhat better than a configuration where the first feeds the second is reminiscent of debates in natural language entailment about independent versus conditional encoding of premise and hypothesis. I regard that as an open question for entailment, and it likely merits further investigation for causal relations as well. Therefore, I cannot endorse the sentence beginning on line 587: “This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers.” This is surprising given that the two arguments are subparts of the same sentence and may share considerable information.
* It is hard to make sense of the hyperparameters that yielded the best performance across tasks. Compare line 578 with line 636, for example. Should we try to interpret these choices, or should we attribute them to the often unpredictable ways in which deep models interact with data?
* Section 4.3 concludes that the system can “correctly disambiguate” the causal meaning of the connective “which then,” whereas Hidey and McKeown’s system cannot. This may be true, but a single example does not establish it. To substantiate the claim, I suggest constructing a broader set of sentences that exhibit the ambiguity and evaluating how often the system produces the correct judgment. This would address whether the success on Table 8’s example is robust or merely luck.

Overall, the paper makes a useful empirical contribution and the released code is valuable. To strengthen the work, I recommend (i) clarifying the theoretical stance on causation and how it informs the modeling choices, (ii) tightening and de-duplicating the introduction and related work, (iii) making the model description and figures precise and self-consistent, (iv) explaining implementation-driven choices (e.g., padding) and their relevance to meaning representation, and (v) probing the model’s generalization on targeted connective ambiguities.
