Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes an LSTM-based architecture for disambiguating whether a lexical marker (e.g., since, after) signals a causal relation between two textual spans. The central premise is that causal meaning is highly context-dependent and not well captured by syntactic templates alone; hence, modeling sentence meaning with neural encoders should help.
- The method is evaluated on the AltLex corpus of Hidey and McKeown. The results show modest but consistent gains over baselines and offer insights into design choices (e.g., independent vs. state-initialized LSTMs; optimizer and pooling strategies).
- The authors also provide the TensorFlow models used in their experiments.

Strengths
- Timely problem focus: The paper addresses a real gap in discourse parsing—disambiguating potentially causal markers whose interpretation depends heavily on context. The argument against over-reliance on fixed connectives or syntactic patterns is persuasive and well-situated in the literature.
- Empirical gains with simple modeling: On the AltLex benchmark, the proposed models consistently outperform a strong frequency baseline and are competitive or better than a feature-rich SVM that relies on external lexical resources. This supports the claim that neural encoders can capture useful context for causal disambiguation.
- Architectural insight: The comparison between “Pair LSTM” (independent encoders) and “Stated Pair LSTM” (second LSTM initialized from the first) is valuable. Even if the interpretation is debatable (see below), the experiments contribute practical guidance for modeling relations between spans.
- Reproducibility: Making the TensorFlow models available adds value for the community and facilitates replication and extension.

Weaknesses
- Introduction and framing: The introduction reads like an extended related work section and repeats content found later, but it does not clearly state what theory of causation the paper assumes. Beyond rejecting purely syntactic accounts, the positive stance is vague. This weakens the motivation for the specific modeling choices.
- Inconsistent theoretical grounding: The paper seems to gesture toward a counterfactual view (e1 → e2) around line 238, yet later disavows counterfactual commitments. If the approach is intended to be theory-agnostic and purely distributional, that should be stated explicitly; otherwise, the formalization should be clarified and aligned with a chosen theory (and acknowledge well-known issues with counterfactual accounts).
- Confusing example analysis: The discussion around the example near line 256 is unclear. If the sentence is true as stated, it entails some causal link (possibly via subevents), but the paper appears to treat it as a counterexample. Without engaging with event granularity or how causation is projected across subevents, the argument is hard to follow.
- Model diagram and description: Figure 1 is labeled as “Pair LSTM” but does not clearly specify the full stack (e.g., embedding lookup vs. GloVe initialization, number of layers, what the empty circles denote). Since the prose omits some details, the figure should be unambiguous—especially regarding what representation from the LSTMs feeds into the dense layers (final hidden state vs. pooled sequence states).
- Padding rationale: The discussion of padding (around line 480) conflates framework/training requirements with representational choices. RNNs do not intrinsically require fixed-length inputs; if padding is used for batch efficiency, please say so. The evaluation of different padding strategies should be motivated in terms of their effect on sequence pooling/aggregation, not “convenience.”
- Over-interpretation of architectural results: The claim (around line 587) that the superior performance of independent encoders means “the assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold” seems too strong. This mirrors open questions in NLI about whether to encode premise and hypothesis independently or conditionally; the present results are informative but not decisive about the nature of the underlying relation.
- Hyperparameter inconsistency: The best settings vary across conditions (e.g., optimizers and pooling choices appear to trade off precision and recall differently). It would help to characterize these interactions or caution readers not to overgeneralize from these settings.
- Anecdotal error analysis: The conclusion in §4.3 about the connective “which then” rests on a single example. Given its rarity and ambiguity in training data, a broader targeted evaluation on such connectives would strengthen the claim that the model truly leverages context rather than benefiting from chance.

Questions to authors
- What is the intended theoretical stance on causation? If you do not adopt a counterfactual view, how should readers interpret the e1 → e2 formalization and the temporal constraint? Is the approach deliberately theory-agnostic?
- For the example near line 256, do you consider the sentence false, or do you accept it as true but non-causal? How do you handle event decomposition (e.g., arguments leading to actions) when assessing causation?
- Please clarify the model architecture: number and type of embedding layers (lookup + pretrained GloVe initialization), whether embeddings are fine-tuned, what the “empty circles” denote, and exactly which LSTM outputs are passed to the dense layers (final hidden state vs. pooled outputs).
- On padding: Is padding solely for batching/implementation in TensorFlow? How do the max/mean/mode strategies map to sequence pooling over token representations, and why would one expect them to affect causal disambiguation?
- Could you temper the interpretation of the independent-vs.-conditional LSTM result, or provide further diagnostics (e.g., attention or similarity analyses) to elucidate what inter-span information is captured?
- Can you provide a focused evaluation on ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then,” “since,” “after”) with a richer set of constructed and natural examples to substantiate the claim that the model robustly disambiguates them?
- Please comment on the generality of the results: Do you expect similar gains on PDTB-style explicit relations or other corpora? What are the key failure modes you observed?

Overall, this is a useful empirical contribution with promising results and practical modeling insights, but it would benefit from clearer theoretical framing, sharper model exposition, and stronger targeted analyses of ambiguity.
