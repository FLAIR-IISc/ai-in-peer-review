This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks. Although the networks employed in this study are not particularly deep, the authors leverage a recent dataset (AltLex, by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to tackle the task of identifying causal versus non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. Certain discourse connectives, such as 'because,' are unambiguous regarding the relation they signal, but the authors focus on cases where the relation is implicit.

The authors argue that convolutional networks are less effective at representing the relevant features of clauses than LSTMs. Consequently, they propose a classification architecture that utilizes a Glove-based representation of clauses, input into an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

The best configuration of the system yields an improvement of 0.5-1.5% in F1 score over Hidey and McKeown's 2016 system (SVM classifier). The authors provide several examples of generalizations where the system performs well, including cases where indicator words that are always causal in the training data are correctly identified as non-causal in the test data. I appreciate that the system is analyzed both qualitatively and quantitatively.

The paper is well-written, and the description of the problem is particularly clear. However, a clarification of the differences between this task and the task of implicit connective recognition would be welcome. This could include a discussion of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition cannot be applied to this task.

I appreciate that the authors have uploaded their code to the submission site, which I briefly inspected but did not execute. Uploading the data (with the code) is also useful, as it provides numerous examples. However, it is unclear what the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files represents, given that the paper mentions binary classification. Additionally, I wonder whether the authors have the right to repost the data from Hidey and McKeown. One point to clarify in the paper is the meaning of "bootstrapping," which apparently extends the corpus by about 15%. While the construction of the corpus is briefly but clearly explained in the paper, the additional bootstrapping is not.

While it is interesting to experiment with neural networks on this task, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. It appears that the best configuration (among 4-7 options) is found on the test data, and it is this best configuration that is announced as improving over Hidey by "2.13% F1." However, a fair comparison would involve selecting the best configuration on the devset.

Moreover, it is not entirely clear how significant the improvement is. On the one hand, it should be possible to compute some statistical significance indicators given the size of the dataset. On the other hand, one should consider the reliability of the gold-standard annotation itself, possibly from the creators of the dataset. Upon inspection, the annotation obtained from the English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia is not perfect, and therefore the scores might need to be considered with a grain of salt.

Finally, neural methods have been previously shown to outperform human-engineered features for binary classification tasks, so the results are somewhat expected. It would be interesting to see experiments with simpler networks used as baselines, e.g., a 1-layer LSTM. The analysis of results could try to explain why the neural method seems to favor precision over recall.
