This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks. The authors take advantage of a recent dataset, AltLex (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), to identify causal vs. non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. They argue that convolutional networks are not as adept at representing the relevant features of clauses as LSTMs and propose a classification architecture that uses a Glove-based representation of clauses, input in an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

The best configuration of the system achieves a 0.5-1.5% F1 improvement over Hidey and McKeown's 2016 system (SVM classifier). The authors provide several examples of generalizations where the system performs well, such as correctly identifying indicator words that are always causal in the training data but are non-causal in the test data. I appreciate the qualitative and quantitative analysis of the system.

The paper is well-written, and the problem description is clear. However, a clarification of the differences between this task and implicit connective recognition would be welcome. This could include a discussion of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition cannot be applied in this case.

I appreciate that the authors uploaded their code to the submission site, which I briefly inspected but did not execute. Uploading the data (with the code) is also useful, as it provides many examples. However, I am unclear about the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files, given that the paper mentions binary classification. I also wonder if the authors have the right to repost the data from Hidey and McKeown. Additionally, the meaning of "bootstrapping" is unclear, which apparently extends the corpus by about 15%. While the construction of the corpus is briefly but clearly explained in the paper, the additional bootstrapping is not.

While experimenting with neural networks on this task is interesting, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. The best configuration (among 4-7 options) is found on the test data, and it is this best configuration that is announced as improving over Hidey by "2.13% F1". However, a fair comparison would involve selecting the best configuration on the devset. Moreover, it is unclear how significant the improvement is. It should be possible to compute statistical significance indicators given the size of the dataset. Additionally, the reliability of the gold-standard annotation itself should be considered, possibly from the creators of the dataset. Upon inspection, the annotation obtained from the English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia is not perfect, and therefore the scores might need to be considered with a grain of salt.

Finally, neural methods have been previously shown to outperform human-engineered features for binary classification tasks, so the results are not entirely surprising. It would be interesting to see experiments with simpler networks used as baselines, e.g., a 1-layer LSTM. The analysis of results could try to explain why the neural method seems to favor precision over recall.
