This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether they indicate a causal relation. The underlying idea is that the expression of causal relations is highly diverse and cannot be effectively captured through syntactic analysis, making the more abstract representations provided by neural models more suitable for this task.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidey and McKeown, and the results offer modest yet consistent support for this idea, providing initial insights into how to translate this concept into a model. The paper distribution includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Some critical comments and questions:

* The introduction is unconventional, reading more like a literature review than a comprehensive overview of the paper's content. This leads to redundancy with the related work section that follows. While I am open to non-standard introductions, this one falls short, as it fails to take a clear stance on causation and its expression, instead only making a negative point (i.e., that it cannot be reduced to syntax). The positive contribution is not clearly stated, except in the final paragraph of the section.

* Extending on this point, I found it disappointing that the paper does not clearly articulate the theory of causation being assumed. The authors appear to default to a counterfactual view, similar to that of David Lewis, where causation is a modal sufficiency claim with additional counterfactual conditions. However, this raises concerns, as the arrow in this context requires a special kind of implication, and Lewis's theory is known to have issues (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere in the paper, the authors seem to distance themselves from the counterfactual view, but it is unclear what theory they are assuming instead. The temporal constraint mentioned on page 3 is insufficient.

* I am unclear about the comments regarding the example on line 256. The authors seem to suggest that they regard the sentence as false, but if it is true, then there should be a causal link between the argument and the breakage. There are remaining issues regarding the division of events into sub-events, which impact causal theories, but these are not discussed, leaving me confused.

* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram is supposed to depict only the "Pair_LSTM" variant of the model. Furthermore, the diagram is needlessly imprecise. While it is acceptable to omit certain details from the prose, the diagrams should have clear and consistent semantics. The empty circles between the input and the "LSTM" boxes are unclear, and the prose does not provide sufficient information about the model's architecture, including the number of representation layers. The diagram is precise about the pooling tanh layers pre-softmax but not about other aspects. I suggest depicting the connection between the leftmost/final representation and the layers above clearly.

* I do not understand the sentence beginning on line 480. The models being discussed do not inherently require padding. I assume this is a requirement of TensorFlow and/or efficient training, but this should be explicitly stated. The final clause is also unclear, as I do not see how this issue relates to the question of what is "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning."

* The authors find that having two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") is somewhat better than having one LSTM feed into another. This issue is reminiscent of discussions in the literature on natural language entailment, where the question is whether to represent premise and hypothesis independently or have the first feed into the second. I consider this an open question for entailment, and I suspect it requires further investigation for causal relations as well. Therefore, I do not endorse the sentence beginning on line 587, as it suggests that the assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, and it is better to encode each argument independently and then measure the relation between the arguments using dense layers. This is surprising, given that we are dealing with subparts of a sentence that may share a lot of information.

* It is challenging to make sense of the hyperparameters that led to the best performance across tasks. For example, comparing line 578 with line 636 raises questions. Should we interpret this or attribute it to the unpredictability of how these models interact with data?

* Section 4.3 concludes by stating that the system can "correctly disambiguate the causal meaning" of the connective 'which then', whereas the system of Hidey and McKeown does not. While this might be correct, one example is insufficient to demonstrate this. To substantiate this point, I suggest creating a wide range of examples that manifest the ambiguity and evaluating how often the system delivers the correct verdict. This will help address the question of whether the system's performance was due to chance.
