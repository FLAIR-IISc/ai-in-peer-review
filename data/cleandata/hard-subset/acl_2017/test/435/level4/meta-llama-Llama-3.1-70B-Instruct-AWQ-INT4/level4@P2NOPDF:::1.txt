This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether they indicate a causal relation. The underlying idea is that causal relations are expressed in diverse ways, making them unsuitable for syntactic analysis, and that neural models can provide more abstract representations to facilitate decision-making.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown, and the results offer modest but consistent support for the proposed approach. The paper also provides some initial insights into how to translate this idea into a model. The distribution includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Some critical comments and questions:

* The introduction is unconventional, reading more like a literature review than a comprehensive overview of the paper's content. This leads to redundancy with the related work section that follows. While non-standard introductions can be acceptable, this one falls short by failing to take a clear stance on causation and its expression, instead only making a negative point (it's not reducible to syntax). The reader is not informed of the paper's positive contribution until the final paragraph of the section.

* The paper lacks clarity on the theory of causation being assumed. The authors seem to default to a counterfactual view, similar to David Lewis's, where causation is a modal sufficiency claim with additional counterfactual conditions. However, this is problematic, as the arrow in the implication needs to be a special kind, and Lewis's theory has well-known issues (see Copley and Wolff, 2014). The authors also make comments suggesting they do not endorse the counterfactual view, leaving the assumed theory unclear. It cannot be solely based on the temporal constraint mentioned on page 3.

* The comments on the example in line 256 are unclear. The authors appear to regard the sentence as false, but if it's true, there should be a causal link between the argument and the breakage. The paper does not discuss the remaining issues of dividing events into sub-events and their impact on causal theories, leaving the reader confused.

* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram only depicts the "Pair_LSTM" variant of the model. A more significant concern is the diagram's imprecision. While it's acceptable to omit parts of the standard model definition from the prose, the diagrams should have clear and consistent semantics. The empty circles between the input and "LSTM" boxes are unclear, and the model's layer structure is not well-defined. The diagram is precise about the pooling tanh layers pre-softmax but not about the representation layers. The connection between the "LSTM" boxes and the layers above is also unclear.

* The sentence starting on line 480 is unclear. The models do not intrinsically require padding, which seems to be a requirement of TensorFlow and/or efficient training. If this is correct, it should be stated explicitly. The final clause is also unclear, as it's not evident how this issue relates to the question of encoding causal meaning.

* The authors find that having two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") is somewhat better than one where the first feeds into the second. This issue is reminiscent of discussions in the literature on natural language entailment, where the question is whether to represent premise and hypothesis independently or have the first feed into the second. This is an open question for entailment and likely requires further investigation for causal relations as well. Therefore, the sentence starting on line 587 is not entirely convincing, as it concludes that the assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, and it's better to encode each argument independently.

* The hyperparameters that led to the best performance across tasks are difficult to interpret. Comparing line 578 with line 636, for example, raises questions about whether these should be interpreted or attributed to the unpredictability of how these models interact with data.

* Section 4.3 concludes that the system can "correctly disambiguate the causal meaning" of the connective 'which then', whereas Hidley and McKeown's system does not. However, one example is insufficient to demonstrate this point. To substantiate this claim, it would be beneficial to create a wide range of examples that manifest the ambiguity and evaluate how often the system delivers the correct verdict. This would help address the question of whether the system got lucky with the example from table 8.
