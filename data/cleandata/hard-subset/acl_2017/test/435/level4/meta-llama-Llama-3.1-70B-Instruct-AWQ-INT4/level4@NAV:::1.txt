This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for identifying connective uses that imply a causal relationship, leveraging the diverse expression of causal relations and the abstract representations provided by neural models. The authors utilize the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown for their experiments, which yield modest yet consistent support for the underlying idea and offer preliminary insights into model implementation. The paper also includes TensorFlow-based models used in the experiments.

However, several aspects of the paper warrant critical examination:

* The introduction is atypical, resembling a literature review rather than a comprehensive overview of the paper's content. This redundancy with the related work section detracts from the introduction's effectiveness. A clearer stance on causation and its expression would strengthen the introduction, particularly in light of the paper's focus on a non-syntactic approach.
* The paper's theory of causation remains unclear, with the authors seemingly defaulting to a counterfactual view akin to David Lewis's modal sufficiency claim. However, this perspective is not explicitly stated, and the accompanying counterfactual conditions are not clearly defined. The authors' comments on not endorsing the counterfactual view only add to the confusion, leaving the underlying theory ambiguous.
* The discussion surrounding the example on line 256 is perplexing, as the authors appear to consider the sentence false, despite the presence of a potential causal link between the argument and the breakage. The omission of sub-event division and its impact on causal theories further complicates the interpretation of this example.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram only represents the "Pair_LSTM" model variant. Moreover, the diagram's imprecision is problematic, particularly with regards to the empty circles between input and "LSTM" boxes. Clarifying the model's architecture, including the look-up layer, Glove layer, and representation layers, would enhance the diagram's accuracy and consistency.
* The sentence beginning on line 480 is unclear, as the models do not inherently require padding. The connection between this requirement and the question of encoding causal meaning is also unclear. A more explicit explanation of the relationship between padding, TensorFlow, and efficient training would be beneficial.
* The finding that two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") outperform a single LSTM with a feed-forward connection raises questions reminiscent of natural language entailment discussions. The conclusion that independently encoding each argument and measuring their relation using dense layers is not entirely convincing, given the potential for shared information between subparts of a sentence.
* The hyperparameters that yielded the best performance across tasks are difficult to interpret, with inconsistencies between lines 578 and 636. Further clarification or attribution of these discrepancies to the unpredictability of model-data interactions would be helpful.
* Section 4.3's conclusion regarding the system's ability to disambiguate the causal meaning of the connective 'which then' is based on a single example. To substantiate this claim, a more comprehensive evaluation using a diverse range of examples would be necessary to determine whether the system's performance is consistent or coincidental.
