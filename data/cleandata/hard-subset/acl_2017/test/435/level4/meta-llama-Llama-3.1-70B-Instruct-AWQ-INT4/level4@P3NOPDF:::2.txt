This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks. The authors take advantage of a recent dataset (AltLex, by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to solve the task of identifying causal vs. non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. While certain discourse connectives are unambiguous regarding the relation they signal (e.g., 'because' is causal), the proposed method aims to address cases where the relation is implicit.

The authors argue that convolutional networks are not as adept at representing the relevant features of clauses as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. They propose a classification architecture that uses a GloVe-based representation of clauses, input into an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

The best configuration of the system improves by 0.5-1.5% F1 over Hidey and McKeown's 2016 system (SVM classifier). The authors provide several examples of generalizations where the system performs well, including cases where indicator words that are always causal in the training data are correctly identified as non-causal in the test data. The qualitative and quantitative analysis of the system is appreciated.

The paper is well-written, and the description of the problem is clear. However, a clarification of the differences between this task and the task of implicit connective recognition would be welcome. This could include a discussion of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition cannot be used in this case.

The authors' decision to upload their code to the submission site is commendable, and the inclusion of the dataset (with code) provides a useful resource. However, it is unclear what the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files represents, given that the paper mentions binary classification. Additionally, it is unclear whether the authors have the right to repost the data, which is originally from Hidey and McKeown.

One point to clarify in the paper is the meaning of "bootstrapping," which apparently extends the corpus by about 15%. While the construction of the corpus is briefly but clearly explained in the paper, the additional bootstrapping is not.

The merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. The best configuration (among 4-7 options) is found on the test data, and it is this best configuration that is announced as improving over Hidey by "2.13% F1." However, a fair comparison would involve selecting the best configuration on the devset.

The significance of the improvement is also unclear. Given the size of the dataset, it should be possible to compute statistical significance indicators. Additionally, the reliability of the gold-standard annotation itself should be considered, possibly in consultation with the creators of the dataset. Upon inspection, the annotation obtained from the English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia is not perfect, and therefore the scores might need to be considered with caution.

Finally, the results are somewhat expected, as neural methods have been previously shown to outperform human-engineered features for binary classification tasks. It would be interesting to see experiments with simpler networks used as baselines, e.g., a 1-layer LSTM. The analysis of results could also attempt to explain why the neural method seems to favor precision over recall.
