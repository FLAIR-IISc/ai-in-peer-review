- Proposes an LSTM-based model to classify connective uses for causality, arguing neural representations better capture diverse causal expressions than syntax; experiments use the AltLex corpus and TensorFlow code is released.

- Experimental results provide modest but consistent support for the approach and offer initial modeling insights.

- Introduction reads like a literature review, is redundant with related work, and fails to clearly state the paper’s positive contribution or stance on causation.

- The paper does not articulate a clear theory of causation; it appears to default to a Lewis-style counterfactual view but is inconsistent and ignores well-known problems with that theory.

- Confusion about the example on line 256: unclear whether the sentence is judged true/false and how event vs. sub-event segmentation affects causal claims.

- Figure 1’s caption is misleading (it shows only the Pair_LSTM variant), the diagram is imprecise (unclear empty circles), and the prose omits crucial architectural details (e.g., number of representation layers).

- The sentence around line 480 about padding is unclear; if padding is required for TensorFlow/training efficiency this should be explicitly stated and linked to why it matters for encoding causal meaning.

- The claim that two independent LSTMs (Stated_LSTM) outperform a stacked LSTM is tentative; this echoes open questions in NLI about independent vs. sequential representation and shouldn’t be taken as settled.

- Hyperparameter choices and their impact on best performance are not well explained and appear unstable or unpredictable across reported settings.

- The claim that the system correctly disambiguates the connective “which then” is based on a single example and needs broader, systematic evaluation to rule out chance.
