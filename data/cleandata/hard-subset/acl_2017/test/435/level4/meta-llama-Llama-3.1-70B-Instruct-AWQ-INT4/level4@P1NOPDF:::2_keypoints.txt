- Proposes an LSTM-based neural method (GloVe → LSTM → 3 tanh dense layers → softmax) to detect causal vs. non-causal clause relations using the AltLex dataset.
- Argues LSTMs capture clause features better than convolutional networks for this task.
- Reports a modest F1 improvement (0.5–1.5%; up to 2.13% when picking best config on test) over Hidey & McKeown (2016) SVM baseline.
- Provides qualitative and quantitative analyses, including examples where the model correctly generalizes indicator words.
- Paper is well-written with a clear problem description.
- Requests clarification of how this task differs from implicit connective recognition and why prior methods for that task aren’t applicable.
- Authors uploaded code and data, but the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in TSV files is unclear.
- Questions whether the authors have rights to repost Hidey & McKeown’s data.
- Asks for clarification of the “bootstrapping” step that extends the corpus by ~15%.
- Criticizes model selection procedure: best configuration appears chosen on the test set rather than the dev set, biasing reported gains.
- Requests statistical significance testing to validate the reported improvement.
- Raises concerns about gold-standard annotation quality (Wikipedia-derived), suggesting scores may be unreliable.
- Notes the result largely confirms known advantage of neural methods over hand-engineered features, limiting novelty.
- Suggests adding simpler neural baselines (e.g., 1-layer LSTM) for comparison.
- Recommends analysis to explain why the neural method favors precision over recall.
