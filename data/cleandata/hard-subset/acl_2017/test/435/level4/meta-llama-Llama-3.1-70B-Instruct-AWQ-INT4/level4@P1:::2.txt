This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks. While certain discourse connectives are unambiguous regarding the relation they signal (e.g., 'because' is causal), the paper takes advantage of a recent dataset (called AltLex, by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to solve the task of identifying causal vs. non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. Arguing that convolutional networks are not as adept as representing the relevant features of clauses as LSTMs, the authors propose a classification architecture which uses a Glove-based representation of clauses, input in an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a neural network architecture for causality classification, specifically for disambiguating the causal meaning of relations between two events. The main contributions of this work are:

1. The development of a neural network architecture that can effectively disambiguate the causal meaning of relations between two events.
2. The use of a Glove-based representation of clauses as input to the LSTM layer, which allows the model to capture the semantic meaning of the clauses.
3. The evaluation of the proposed system on the AltLex corpus, which is a challenging dataset for causality classification.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. The paper is well-written, and the description of the problem is particularly clear.
2. The authors provide a qualitative and quantitative analysis of the system's performance, which is helpful in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.
3. The authors have uploaded their code to the submission site, which is useful for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. The paper does not provide a clear clarification of the differences between this task and the task of implicit connective recognition.
2. The paper does not discuss why previous methods for implicit connective recognition cannot be used in this case.
3. The paper does not provide a clear explanation of the "bootstrapping" process, which apparently extends the corpus by about 15%.
4. The paper does not provide a fair comparison between the proposed system and the baseline system, as the best configuration of the proposed system is selected based on the test data.
5. The paper does not provide a statistical significance analysis of the results, which makes it difficult to determine the significance of the improvement over the baseline system.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a clear clarification of the differences between this task and the task of implicit connective recognition?
2. Can you discuss why previous methods for implicit connective recognition cannot be used in this case?
3. Can you provide a clear explanation of the "bootstrapping" process and how it extends the corpus by about 15%?
4. Can you provide a fair comparison between the proposed system and the baseline system, by selecting the best configuration of the proposed system based on the devset?
5. Can you provide a statistical significance analysis of the results to determine the significance of the improvement over the baseline system?
