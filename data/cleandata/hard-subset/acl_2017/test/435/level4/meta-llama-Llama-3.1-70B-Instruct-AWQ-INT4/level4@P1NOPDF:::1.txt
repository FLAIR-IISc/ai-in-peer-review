This paper proposes an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether they indicate a causal relation. The underlying idea is that causal relations are expressed in a highly diverse manner, making them unsuitable for syntactic treatment. Instead, the authors argue that the more abstract representations provided by neural models are better suited for making these decisions.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results offer modest but consistent support for the general idea, providing some initial insights into how to translate this idea into a model. The paper includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Some critical comments and questions:

* The introduction is unconventional, resembling a literature review rather than a comprehensive overview of the paper's content. This leads to some redundancy with the related work section that follows. While I am open to non-standard introductions, this one falls short. Despite reviewing numerous ideas, it fails to take a stance on what causation is or how it is expressed, only making a negative point (it's not reducible to syntax). The reader is not informed about the positive contribution except for the very general final paragraph of the section.

* Building on the above, I found it disappointing that the paper lacks clarity regarding the theory of causation being assumed. The authors appear to default to a counterfactual view, similar to that of David Lewis, where causation is a modal sufficiency claim with additional counterfactual conditions. See line 238 and following; the arrow needs to be a special kind of implication for this to work, and there are well-known problems with Lewis's theory (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere in the paper, the authors mention not endorsing the counterfactual view, but then what is the assumed theory? It cannot be solely the temporal constraint mentioned on page 3.

* I am unclear about the comments regarding the example on line 256. The authors seem to be saying that they regard the sentence as false. If it's true, then there should be some causal link between the argument and the breakage. There are remaining issues about how to divide events into sub-events, impacting causal theories, but these are not being discussed, leaving me confused.

* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram is supposed to depict only the "Pair_LSTM" variant of the model. My bigger concern is that this diagram is needlessly imprecise. While it's acceptable to leave parts of the standard model definition out of the prose, the diagrams should have clear and consistent semantics. What are the empty circles between the input and the "LSTM" boxes? The prose suggests that the model has a look-up layer, a Glove layer, and then... what? How many layers of representation are there? The diagram is precise about the pooling tanh layers pre-softmax but not about this. I'm also unclear on what the "LSTM" boxes represent. It seems like it's just the leftmost/final representation that is directly connected to the layers above. I suggest depicting that connection clearly.

* I don't understand the sentence beginning on line 480. The models under discussion do not intrinsically require any padding. I'm guessing this is a requirement of TensorFlow and/or efficient training. If that's correct, please clarify. I'm also unclear about the final clause. How is this issue related to the question of what is "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning"? I don't see how convenience is an issue or how this relates directly to causal meaning.

* The authors find that having two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") is somewhat better than one where the first feeds into the second. This issue is reminiscent of discussions in the literature on natural language entailment, where the question is whether to represent premise and hypothesis independently or have the first feed into the second. I regard this as an open question for entailment, and I suspect it needs further investigation for causal relations too. Therefore, I cannot endorse the sentence beginning on line 587: "This behaviour means that our assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then to measure the relation between the arguments by using dense layers." This is surprising since we are talking about subparts of a sentence that might share a lot of information.

* It's challenging to make sense of the hyperparameters that led to the best performance across tasks. Compare line 578 with line 636, for example. Should we interpret this or attribute it to the unpredictability of how these models interact with data?

* Section 4.3 concludes by saying that the system can "correctly disambiguate the causal meaning" of the connective 'which then', whereas Hidley and McKeown's system does not. While this might be correct, one example is insufficient to demonstrate this. To substantiate this point, I suggest creating a wide range of examples that manifest the ambiguity and evaluating how often the system delivers the correct verdict. This will help address the question of whether it got lucky with the example from Table 8.
