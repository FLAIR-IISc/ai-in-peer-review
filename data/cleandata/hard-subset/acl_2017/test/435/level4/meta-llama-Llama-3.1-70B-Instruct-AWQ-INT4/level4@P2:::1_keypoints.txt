- Summary: Proposes an LSTM-based neural architecture for causality classification between two events and reports state-of-the-art results on the AltLex corpus.  
- Contribution 1: Introduces a model that encodes two input events with LSTMs and classifies causality using several dense tanh layers.  
- Contribution 2: Empirically evaluates the model on the AltLex corpus and outperforms prior work.  
- Contribution 3: Shows the model can disambiguate causal meaning for ambiguous connectives (e.g., "which then").  
- Strength 1: Clear and concise overview of the causality classification task and challenges.  
- Strength 2: Proposes a novel, task-appropriate neural architecture.  
- Strength 3: Provides a thorough empirical comparison with state-of-the-art on AltLex.  
- Strength 4: Paper is well-written with clear technical explanations.  
- Weakness 1: Introduction reads more like a literature review and doesn't clearly present the paper's contributions.  
- Weakness 2: Lacks a clear theory of causation, leaving assumptions and task framing unclear.  
- Weakness 3: Figure 1 is imprecise and semantically inconsistent.  
- Weakness 4: Hyperparameter choices and tuning leading to best performance are not clearly explained.  
- Question 1: Please clarify your theory of causation and its relation to the classification task.  
- Question 2: How will you address the lack of resources for causality classification mentioned in the paper?  
- Question 3: Can you provide more examples demonstrating disambiguation of ambiguous connectives like "which then"?  
- Question 4: How will you investigate representing premise and hypothesis independently versus sequentially (first feeding into second)?
