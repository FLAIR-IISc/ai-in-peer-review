**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, which involves identifying whether there is a causal relation between two events. The authors argue that the encoding of the meaning of the two events is required for the disambiguation of their causal meaning. They evaluate their proposed system on the AltLex corpus and show that it outperforms the state-of-the-art.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a neural network architecture for causality classification, which is based on the encoding of the two input events with an LSTM and the use of several dense layers with a tanh activation function.
2. They evaluate their proposed system on the AltLex corpus and show that it outperforms the state-of-the-art.
3. The authors demonstrate that their system can correctly disambiguate the causal meaning of relations involving ambiguous connectives, such as "which then".

**Strengths**

1. The authors provide a clear and concise overview of the task of causality classification and the challenges involved.
2. They propose a novel neural network architecture that is well-suited to the task of causality classification.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their proposed system on the AltLex corpus, including a comparison with the state-of-the-art.
4. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the technical details.

**Weaknesses**

1. The introduction is more like a literature review than a full overview of what the paper contains, which can make it difficult to understand the authors' contributions.
2. The paper does not provide a clear theory of causation, which can make it difficult to understand the authors' assumptions and how they relate to the task of causality classification.
3. The diagram in Figure 1 is needlessly imprecise and does not provide a clear and consistent semantics.
4. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of the hyperparameters that led to the best performance across tasks.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a clearer explanation of your theory of causation and how it relates to the task of causality classification?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of the lack of resources for the task of causality classification, which is mentioned in the paper?
3. Can you provide more examples of how your system can correctly disambiguate the causal meaning of relations involving ambiguous connectives, such as "which then"?
4. How do you plan to further investigate the issue of whether to represent premise and hypothesis independently or have the first feed into the second, which is mentioned in the paper?
