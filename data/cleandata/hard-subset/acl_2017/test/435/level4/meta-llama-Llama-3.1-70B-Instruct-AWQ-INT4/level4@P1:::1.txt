This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, focusing on the disambiguation of the causal meaning of a sentence. The authors claim that encoding the meaning of a sentence is required for this disambiguation, and their results show that this approach outperforms the state-of-the-art.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a neural network architecture for causality classification, which uses two inputs to encode the meaning of a sentence and disambiguate its causal meaning.
2. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach on the AltLex corpus, achieving better results than the state-of-the-art method.
3. The authors also provide insights into the importance of encoding the context of a sentence for causality classification, highlighting the limitations of traditional approaches that rely on syntactic patterns or semantic resources.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to causality classification, which addresses the limitations of traditional methods.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
3. The paper includes a detailed description of the neural network architecture, making it easy to follow and replicate.

**Weaknesses**

1. The introduction is more like a literature review than a full overview of the paper, leading to some redundancy with the related work section.
2. The paper does not clearly articulate the theory of causation being assumed, which may lead to confusion.
3. The diagram in Figure 1 is needlessly imprecise and could be improved for clarity.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more information on the theory of causation being assumed in this paper?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of dividing events into sub-events, which may impact causal theories?
3. Can you provide more examples to substantiate the claim that the system can correctly disambiguate the causal meaning of the connective 'which then'?
