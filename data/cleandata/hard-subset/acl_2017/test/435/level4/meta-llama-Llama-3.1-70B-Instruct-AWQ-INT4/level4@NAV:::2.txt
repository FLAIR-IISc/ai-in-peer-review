This paper presents a novel approach to identifying causal relationships between clauses using neural networks. The authors leverage the AltLex dataset (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to tackle the challenge of distinguishing causal and non-causal relations in the absence of explicit discourse connectives. They argue that convolutional networks are less effective in capturing clause features than Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and propose a classification architecture that utilizes Glove-based clause representations, an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final softmax decision layer.

The proposed system achieves an improvement of 0.5-1.5% in F1 score over the baseline established by Hidey and McKeown's 2016 SVM classifier. The authors provide qualitative and quantitative analysis of their system, including examples of successful generalizations. The paper is well-written, with a clear problem description. However, a discussion on the differences between this task and implicit connective recognition, as well as the limitations of previous methods in this context, would be beneficial.

The authors' decision to share their code and data is commendable, providing a useful resource for the research community. Nevertheless, clarification on the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files and the authors' rights to redistribute the data would be appreciated. Additionally, the concept of "bootstrapping" and its impact on the corpus size (approximately 15%) requires further explanation.

While the proposed system is intriguing, its merits are not entirely convincing. The best configuration was selected based on the test data, which may not be a fair comparison. A more robust evaluation would involve selecting the best configuration on the development set. Furthermore, the significance of the improvement is unclear, and statistical indicators of significance would be valuable. The reliability of the gold-standard annotation should also be considered, as the annotation obtained from the English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia is not perfect.

The results, although interesting, are somewhat expected, given the established superiority of neural methods over human-engineered features in binary classification tasks. Experiments with simpler networks, such as a 1-layer LSTM, would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach. An analysis of the results could also explore why the neural method appears to prioritize precision over recall.
