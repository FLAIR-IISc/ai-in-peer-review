This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether they indicate a causal relation. The underlying idea is that the expression of causal relations is highly diverse and thus not amenable to syntactic treatment, and that the more abstract representations delivered by neural models are more suitable for making these decisions.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results offer modest but consistent support for the general idea and provide some initial insights into how to translate this idea into a model. The paper distribution includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Some critical comments and questions:

* The introduction is unconventional, reading more like a literature review than a comprehensive overview of the paper's content. This leads to redundancy with the related work section that follows it. While open to non-standard introductions, this one falls short, as it primarily reviews existing ideas without taking a clear stance on causation or its expression, beyond a negative point (it's not reducible to syntax). The positive contribution of the paper is only briefly mentioned in the final paragraph of the section.

* Building on the above, it is disappointing that the paper does not clearly articulate the theory of causation being assumed. The authors appear to default to a counterfactual view, similar to that of David Lewis, where causation is a modal sufficiency claim with additional counterfactual conditions. However, this is not explicitly stated, and the implications of this assumption are not thoroughly explored. Furthermore, there are well-known problems with Lewis's theory (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). The authors' comments elsewhere in the paper suggest that they do not endorse the counterfactual view, but if so, what theory of causation is being assumed? It cannot be solely based on the temporal constraint mentioned on page 3.

* The comments regarding the example on line 256 are unclear. The authors seem to be saying that they regard the sentence as false, but if it is true, then there should be some causal link between the argument and the breakage. However, there are remaining issues about how to divide events into sub-events, and these impact causal theories, which are not being discussed here, leaving the reader confused.

* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram is supposed to depict only the "Pair_LSTM" variant of the model. Moreover, the diagram is needlessly imprecise. While it is acceptable to omit certain aspects of the standard model definition from the prose, the diagrams should have clear and consistent semantics. The empty circles between the input and the "LSTM" boxes are unclear, and the relationship between the look-up layer, the Glove layer, and the subsequent layers is not well-defined. The diagram is precise about the pooling tanh layers pre-softmax but not about these aspects. It is also unclear what the "LSTM" boxes represent, as it seems that only the leftmost/final representation is directly connected to the layers above. Depicting this connection clearly would be beneficial.

* The sentence beginning on line 480 is unclear. The models under discussion do not intrinsically require padding. It is likely that this is a requirement of TensorFlow and/or efficient training, but this should be explicitly stated. The final clause is also unclear, as it is not apparent how this issue relates to the question of what is "the most convenient way to encode the causal meaning." The relevance of convenience to causal meaning is not established.

* The authors find that having two independent LSTMs ("Stated_LSTM") is somewhat better than one where the first feeds into the second. This issue is reminiscent of discussions in the literature on natural language entailment, where the question is whether to represent premise and hypothesis independently or have the first feed into the second. This is an open question for entailment, and further investigation is likely needed for causal relations as well. Therefore, the conclusion drawn in the sentence beginning on line 587 is not fully convincing. The assumption that the meanings of the two input events do not hold, and that it is better to encode each argument independently and then measure the relation between the arguments using dense layers, is surprising, especially since we are dealing with subparts of a sentence that might share a lot of information.

* The hyperparameters that led to the best performance across tasks are difficult to interpret. For example, comparing line 578 with line 636 raises questions about the consistency of the results. It is unclear whether these discrepancies should be interpreted or attributed to the unpredictability of how these models interact with data.

* Section 4.3 concludes that the system can "correctly disambiguate the causal meaning" of the connective 'which then', whereas the system of Hidey and McKeown cannot. However, this claim is based on a single example and requires further substantiation. To address this, it would be beneficial to create a wide range of examples that manifest the ambiguity and evaluate how often the system delivers the correct verdict, thereby determining whether the result is due to chance or a genuine capability of the system.
