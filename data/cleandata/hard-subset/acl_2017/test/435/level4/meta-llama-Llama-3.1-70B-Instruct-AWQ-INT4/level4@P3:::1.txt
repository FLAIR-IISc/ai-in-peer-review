**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically for disambiguating the causal meaning of lexical markers in context. The authors argue that the encoding of the meaning of a sentence is required for the disambiguation of its causal meaning. They evaluate their proposal on the AltLex corpus and compare it to the state-of-the-art method, showing improved performance.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Neural Network Architecture**: The authors propose a neural network architecture with two inputs, one for the first event and the other for the lexical marker and the second event, to encode the context of the relation and disambiguate its causal meaning.
2. **Improved Performance**: The authors show that their proposal outperforms the state-of-the-art method on the AltLex corpus, achieving better precision, recall, and F1 scores.
3. **Analysis of Results**: The authors provide an analysis of the results, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of their proposal and discussing the implications of their findings.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved Performance**: The authors demonstrate that their proposal achieves better performance than the state-of-the-art method on the AltLex corpus.
2. **Robustness to Ambiguity**: The authors show that their proposal can correctly disambiguate the causal meaning of lexical markers, even in cases where the state-of-the-art method fails.
3. **Flexibility**: The authors' proposal is not limited to specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources, making it more flexible and generalizable.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Clarity**: The authors' discussion of the theory of causation is unclear, and they seem to default to a counterfactual view without fully endorsing it.
2. **Imprecision in Diagrams**: The diagram in Figure 1 is imprecise and lacks clear semantics, making it difficult to understand the model's architecture.
3. **Limited Analysis of Hyperparameters**: The authors do not provide a thorough analysis of the hyperparameters that led to the best performance, making it difficult to interpret the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify your theoretical stance on causation and how it relates to the counterfactual view?
2. How do you plan to address the imprecision in the diagrams and provide a clearer representation of the model's architecture?
3. Can you provide a more thorough analysis of the hyperparameters that led to the best performance and discuss their implications for the model's behavior?

**Additional Comments**

The authors' proposal shows promise in improving the performance of causality classification, but further work is needed to address the theoretical and methodological limitations of the paper.
