This paper presents an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether they indicate a causal relation. The authors propose that the expression of causal relations is highly diverse and cannot be reduced to syntactic treatment, making neural models more suitable for this task.

The experiments are conducted on the AltLex corpus developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results provide modest but consistent support for the general idea and offer initial insights into how to translate this idea into a model. The paper includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

However, there are several areas that require improvement. The introduction is unconventional, reading more like a literature review than a comprehensive overview of the paper's contents. This leads to redundancy with the related work section that follows. A more effective introduction would clearly state the paper's contribution and take a stance on the concept of causation.

Furthermore, the paper lacks clarity on the theory of causation being assumed. The authors seem to default to a counterfactual view, similar to that of David Lewis, but this is not explicitly stated. The counterfactual view has well-known problems, and the authors' comments elsewhere in the paper suggest that they do not endorse this view. It is essential to clearly define the theory of causation being assumed, as it cannot be reduced to a simple temporal constraint.

Additionally, some comments in the paper are unclear or misleading. For example, the discussion of the example on line 256 is confusing, as the authors seem to imply that the sentence is false, despite acknowledging a potential causal link. The caption for Figure 1 is also misleading, as the diagram only depicts the "Pair_LSTM" variant of the model. The diagram itself is imprecise, with unclear semantics and unnecessary complexity.

The paper's discussion of the models' requirements is also unclear. The sentence beginning on line 480 implies that the models intrinsically require padding, but this is not the case. The authors should clarify that this is a requirement of TensorFlow and/or efficient training.

The comparison between the "Stated_LSTM" and "Pair_LSTM" models is also problematic. The authors conclude that having two independent LSTMs is better than having one feed into the other, but this is an open question that requires further investigation.

The hyperparameters that led to the best performance across tasks are also difficult to interpret. The authors should provide more insight into how these models interact with the data.

Finally, the conclusion in Section 4.3 that the system can "correctly disambiguate" the causal meaning of the connective 'which then' is not sufficiently substantiated. The authors should provide more examples to demonstrate the system's ability to handle ambiguity.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting idea, it requires significant improvements in clarity, theory, and experimentation to effectively demonstrate its contribution.
