This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks. Although the networks employed in this study are not particularly deep, the authors take advantage of a recent dataset (AltLex, by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to tackle the task of identifying causal versus non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. The authors argue that convolutional networks are less adept at representing the relevant features of clauses than LSTMs, and propose a classification architecture that utilizes a Glove-based representation of clauses, input into an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

The best configuration of the system achieves a 0.5-1.5% improvement in F1 score over Hidey and McKeown's 2016 system (SVM classifier). The authors provide several examples of generalizations where the system performs well, including correctly identifying indicator words that are always causal in the training data but non-causal in the test data. The system is analyzed both qualitatively and quantitatively, which is appreciated.

The paper is well-written, and the problem description is particularly clear. However, a clarification of the differences between this task and the task of implicit connective recognition would be welcome, including a discussion of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition cannot be applied in this case.

It is commendable that the authors have uploaded their code to the submission site, which I briefly inspected but did not execute. Uploading the data (with the code) is also useful, as it provides many examples. However, the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files is unclear, given that the paper mentions binary classification. Furthermore, it is uncertain whether the authors have the right to repost the data from Hidey and McKeown. One point to clarify in the paper is the meaning of "bootstrapping," which apparently extends the corpus by about 15%. While the construction of the corpus is briefly but clearly explained in the paper, the additional bootstrapping is not.

While experimenting with neural networks on this task is certainly interesting, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. It appears that the best configuration (among 4-7 options) is found on the test data, and it is this best configuration that is announced as improving over Hidey by "2.13% F1." However, a fair comparison would involve selecting the best configuration on the development set.

Moreover, it is unclear how significant the improvement is. On one hand, it should be possible to compute statistical significance indicators given the size of the dataset. On the other hand, the reliability of the gold-standard annotation itself (possibly from the creators of the dataset) should also be considered. Upon inspection, the annotation obtained from the English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia is not perfect, and therefore the scores may need to be considered with caution.

Finally, neural methods have been previously shown to outperform human-engineered features for binary classification tasks, so the results are somewhat a confirmation of a known property. It would be interesting to see experiments with simpler networks used as baselines, such as a 1-layer LSTM. The analysis of results could attempt to explain why the neural method seems to favor precision over recall.
