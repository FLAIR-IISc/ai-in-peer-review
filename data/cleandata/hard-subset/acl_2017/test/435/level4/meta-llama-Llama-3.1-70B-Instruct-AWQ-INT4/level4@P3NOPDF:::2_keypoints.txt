- Paper proposes an LSTM-based neural method to detect causal vs. non-causal clause relations, focusing on implicit (unmarked) cases.  
- Uses the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) to train and evaluate the model.  
- Argues LSTMs represent clause features better than convolutional networks for this task.  
- Model architecture: GloVe clause embeddings → LSTM → three tanh dense layers → softmax decision layer.  
- Best system configuration yields a 0.5–1.5% F1 improvement over Hidey & McKeown’s SVM; one run claims +2.13% F1.  
- Provides qualitative examples showing useful generalizations and some correct handling of indicator words.  
- Paper is well-written and problem description is clear.  
- Needs clarification on how this task differs from implicit connective recognition and why prior methods aren’t applicable.  
- Authors shared code and dataset, which is commendable, but the meaning of 0-1-2 labels in the TSV is unclear.  
- Unclear whether authors have permission to repost the original AltLex data.  
- The term “bootstrapping” (corpus extended by ~15%) is mentioned but not adequately explained.  
- Experimental selection appears to pick the best configuration on test data, which is an unfair comparison; devset selection is needed.  
- No statistical significance testing reported; given dataset size, significance should be computed.  
- Gold-standard annotation quality (from English/Simple English Wikipedia) is imperfect and may affect score reliability.  
- Results are unsurprising because neural methods often outperform hand-engineered features for binary tasks.  
- Recommend adding simpler neural baselines (e.g., 1-layer LSTM) and analyzing why the model favors precision over recall.
