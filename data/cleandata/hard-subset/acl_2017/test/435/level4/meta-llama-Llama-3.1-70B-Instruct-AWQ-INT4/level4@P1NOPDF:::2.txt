This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relations between clauses using neural networks. While certain discourse connectives are unambiguous regarding the relation they signal (e.g., 'because' is causal), the paper leverages a recent dataset (AltLex, by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to tackle the task of identifying causal vs. non-causal relations when the relation is not explicitly marked. The authors argue that convolutional networks are not as adept at representing the relevant features of clauses as LSTMs and propose a classification architecture that uses a Glove-based representation of clauses, input into an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

The best configuration of the system improves by 0.5-1.5% F1 over Hidey and McKeown's 2016 system (SVM classifier). Several examples of generalizations where the system performs well are provided (indicator words that are always causal in the training data but are correctly identified as non-causal in the test data). I appreciate that the system is analyzed both qualitatively and quantitatively.

The paper is well-written, and the description of the problem is particularly clear. However, a clarification of the differences between this task and the task of implicit connective recognition would be welcome. This could include a discussion of why previous methods for implicit connective recognition cannot be applied in this case.

I appreciate that the authors have uploaded their code to the submission site (I inspected it briefly but did not execute it). Uploading the data (along with the code) is also useful, as it provides numerous examples. However, I was unclear about the meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files, given that the paper mentions binary classification. I also wonder if the authors have the right to re-post the data from Hidey and McKeown. One point to clarify in the paper is the meaning of "bootstrapping," which apparently extends the corpus by about 15%. While the construction of the corpus is briefly but clearly explained in the paper, the additional bootstrapping is not.

While experimenting with neural networks on this task is certainly interesting, the merits of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. It appears that the best configuration (among 4-7 options) is found on the test data, and it is this best configuration that is announced as improving over Hidey by "2.13% F1." However, a fair comparison would involve selecting the best configuration on the dev set.

Moreover, it is not entirely clear how significant the improvement is. On the one hand, it should be possible, given the size of the dataset, to compute some statistical significance indicators. On the other hand, one should consider the reliability of the gold-standard annotation itself (possibly from the creators of the dataset). Upon inspection, the annotation obtained from the English/Simple English Wikipedia is not perfect, and therefore the scores might need to be considered with caution.

Finally, neural methods have been previously shown to outperform human-engineered features for binary classification tasks, so in a sense, the results are rather a confirmation of a known property. It would be interesting to see experiments with simpler networks used as baselines, e.g., a 1-layer LSTM. The analysis of results could try to explain why the neural method seems to favor precision over recall.
