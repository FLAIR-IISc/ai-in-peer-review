- Introduces an LSTM-based neural method to detect causal relations between clauses, arguing LSTMs outperform convolutional nets for this task.  
- Uses the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) to tackle causal relation detection when explicit discourse connectives are absent.  
- System architecture: Glove-based clause embeddings → LSTM layer → three dense tanh layers → softmax output.  
- Reports modest F1 improvements (0.5–1.5% over Hidey & McKeown; one claim of 2.13% when best config chosen on test).  
- Provides qualitative examples showing beneficial generalizations (e.g., marking frequent training indicator words as non-causal in test).  
- Paper is well-written with a clear problem description but needs to clarify differences from implicit connective recognition.  
- Lacks discussion of why prior implicit-connective methods are inapplicable to this task.  
- Code release and inclusion of the older dataset are appreciated, but rights to repost the Hidey & McKeown dataset are questioned.  
- The meaning of 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files is unclear given the described binary task.  
- Mentions bootstrapping to extend the corpus by ~15% but provides insufficient details about the bootstrapping process.  
- Experimental protocol concern: best configuration appears selected on the test set rather than the development set, risking overfitting.  
- No statistical significance testing reported; improvement significance is unclear given dataset size.  
- Raises concerns about gold-standard annotation reliability (noisy Wikipedia-derived labels) and suggests consulting dataset creators.  
- Contributions are not fully convincing since results mainly reaffirm known neural superiority over hand-crafted features.  
- Recommends additional baselines (e.g., single-layer LSTM) and deeper analysis of why the model favors precision over recall.
