- Introduces an LSTM-based model to classify whether connectives signal intended causal relations, arguing neural representations suit causation's diversity better than syntactic rules.  
- Experiments on the AltLex corpus show modest but consistent support for the idea and provide preliminary implementation insights; TensorFlow code/models are included.  
- Introduction reads like a literature review, duplicates related work, and fails to clearly state the paper's main contribution or take a positive stance on what causation is.  
- The assumed theory of causation is unclear; the paper appears to lean toward a counterfactual (Lewis-like) view but does not acknowledge its problems or specify an alternative.  
- Discussion around the example near line 256 is confusing and omits important issues about splitting events into sub-events, leaving the causal analysis unclear.  
- Figure 1’s caption is misleading (it shows only the Pair_LSTM variant) and the diagram lacks precise, consistent semantics (unclear symbols, layer structure, and connections).  
- The sentence about padding (line ~480) is confusing; if padding is used for TensorFlow/efficiency this should be stated, and the link between padding convenience and encoding causal meaning is unexplained.  
- The result that two independent LSTMs (Stated_LSTM) slightly outperform a sequential LSTM is reported, but the paper’s conclusion favoring independent encoding is not fully justified given related debates in entailment literature.  
- The best-performing hyperparameters vary across tasks with no clear explanation, making those differences hard to interpret.  
- The claim that the system can correctly disambiguate “which then” (unlike Hidey & McKeown) is based on a single example and requires a broader, systematic evaluation to rule out chance.
