This paper develops an LSTM-based model for classifying connective uses to determine whether they indicate a causal relationship was intended. Its core idea is that the expression of causal relationships is extremely diverse and therefore not suitable for syntactic treatment, and the more abstract representations given by neural models are therefore more suitable as the basis for making these judgments.

The experiments were conducted on the Altlex corpus developed by Hidde and McKeown. The results provide modest but consistent support for the general idea, and they offer some initial insights into how best to translate this idea into a model. The paper distribution includes TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Some important comments and questions:

*   The introduction is unusual because it is more like a literature review than a complete overview of the material included in the paper. This leads to some redundancy with the related work section that follows. I think I am open to a non-standard type of introduction, but it doesn't really work: despite reviewing a lot of ideas, it doesn't take a stand on what the cause is or how it is expressed, but only makes a negative point (which is not reducible to syntax). We are not really told what the positive contribution will be, except for the very general final paragraph of the section.
*   Adding to the above, I found it disappointing that the paper is not really clear about the theory of cause that is being assumed. The authors seem to default to a counterfactual approach that is roughly similar to David Lewis's, where the causal is a modal sufficiency claim with some other counterfactual conditions. See lines 238 and onwards; the arrow to work this must have a very specific type of implication, and there are well-known problems with Lewis's theory (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). The paper has comments elsewhere that the authors do not support the counterfactual approach, but then what is the theory being assumed? It cannot be just the temporary constraint mentioned on page 3!
*   I do not understand the comments about the example given on line 256. The authors seem to think they are mistaking the sentence. If this is true, there must be some causal relationship between the argument and the breakdown. There are remaining issues about dividing events into sub-events, and these affect causal theories, but they are not being discussed here, which has confused me.
*   The caption for figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram should only represent the "pair_LSTM" type of model. My big complaint is that this diagram is unnecessarily ambiguous. I think it is okay to leave some parts of the standard model definition out of prose, but then these diagrams should have a clear and consistent semantics. What are all the empty circles between the input and "LSTM" boxes? The prose seems to suggest that the model has a look-up layer, a glove layer, and then... what? How many layers of representation are there? The diagram is accurate about the pooling TNH layers in Pre-Softmax, but not about this. I am also not clear what the "LSTM" boxes represent. It seems that this is just the leftmost/last representation that is directly connected to the layers above. I suggest clarifying that connection.
*   I do not understand the sentence starting from line 480. The model under discussion does not need any padding internally. I guess it needs TensorFlow and/or efficient training. That's fine. If this is true, please say so. However, I do not understand the final clause. How does this issue relate to the question of "what is the most convenient way to encode causal meaning"? I don't see how convenience is an issue or how it directly relates to causal meaning.
*   The authors seem to think that having two independent LSTMs ("stated_LSTMs") is somewhat better than having one feed into the other. This issue reminds me of discussions in the literature on natural language implication, where the question is whether the premise and hypothesis should be represented independently or whether the first feeds into the second. I consider this an open question for implication, and I bet it should be further investigated for causal relationships as well. So I cannot really support the sentence starting from line 587: "This behavior means that our assumption about the relationship between the meanings of two input events is not valid, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then measure the relationship between the arguments using dense layers." This is very surprising because we are talking about subparts of a sentence that can share a lot of information.
*   It is difficult to understand the hyperparameters that led to the best performance in the tasks. For example, compare line 578 with line 636. Should we understand this or just attribute it to the unpredictability of how these models interact with data?
*   Section 4.3 connective 'with देन' concludes by saying that the system "can accurately obscure its causal meaning", while Hidde and McKeown do not. This may be true, but an example is not enough to show it. To prove this point, I suggest creating a wide range of examples that reveal ambiguity and see how often the system makes the right decision. This will help address the question of whether it was lucky with the example from table 8.
