This paper introduces a neural network approach to identify causal relationships between clauses, tackling the challenging scenario where such relations aren’t explicitly signaled by discourse connectives. Leveraging the AltLex dataset (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), the authors address the task of distinguishing causal from non-causal clause pairings. They posit that LSTMs are better suited than convolutional networks for capturing the relevant features, and implement a classification architecture consisting of GloVe embeddings, an LSTM layer, three tanh-activated dense layers, and a softmax output layer.

The system achieves a 0.5-1.5% F1 score improvement over the original Hidey & McKeown (2016) SVM baseline. The inclusion of qualitative analysis, showcasing successful generalizations to indicator words, is commendable. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem. However, a discussion clarifying the distinction between this task and implicit connective recognition – including why existing methods for the latter aren’t directly applicable – would strengthen the work.

The authors’ provision of code and the AltLex dataset is greatly appreciated, facilitating reproducibility and further research. However, the 0-1-2 coding scheme in the TSV files requires clarification given the stated binary classification nature of the task. Furthermore, the authors should address the rights surrounding the redistribution of the Hidey & McKeown dataset. The “bootstrapping” process, which expands the corpus by approximately 15%, also warrants a more detailed explanation beyond the brief overview provided.

While the application of neural networks to this task is interesting, the reported improvements aren’t entirely persuasive. The reported 2.13% F1 gain is based on a configuration optimized on the test data, rather than a more rigorous selection process using a development set. Moreover, the statistical significance of the improvement isn’t assessed, and the reliability of the AltLex gold-standard annotations – derived from English/SimpleEnglish Wikipedia – should be considered, as the annotation quality isn’t perfect. 

Given the established success of neural networks over feature engineering in binary classification, the results largely confirm existing trends. Exploring simpler network architectures as baselines (e.g., a single-layer LSTM) would be valuable. Finally, an analysis attempting to explain the observed preference for precision over recall would provide further insight.
