This paper introduces an LSTM-based model designed to classify connective uses based on whether they signal an intended causal relationship. The core premise is that the nuanced and varied expression of causal relations makes syntactic approaches insufficient, and that the more abstract representations learned by neural models are better suited for this task.

The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, originally developed by Hidley and McKeown. The results demonstrate modest, yet consistent, support for the central idea, and offer preliminary insights into effective model implementation. The authors have generously included the TensorFlow-based models used in their experiments with the paper’s distribution.

However, several points require further consideration:

* The introduction functions more as a literature review than a focused overview of the paper’s contributions. This creates redundancy with the subsequent related work section. While unconventional introductions can be acceptable, this one lacks a clear thesis statement; it primarily establishes what causation *isn’t* (reducible to syntax) without articulating the positive contribution beyond a general statement in the final paragraph.
* The paper lacks explicit articulation of the underlying theory of causation. The authors appear to lean towards a counterfactual account, similar to that of David Lewis, relying on a specific interpretation of implication (as noted from line 238 onwards). This approach presents known theoretical challenges (as discussed in Copley & Wolff, 2014), and the paper’s occasional disavowals of the counterfactual view leave the assumed theoretical framework unclear. Simply invoking a temporal constraint (page 3) is insufficient.
* The discussion surrounding the example on line 256 is unclear. The authors’ assertion that the sentence is false is puzzling; if true, a causal link between the argument and breakage should exist. Broader issues concerning event segmentation and their impact on causal theories are not addressed, contributing to confusion.
* The caption for Figure 1 is misleading, as the diagram is intended to represent only the “Pair_LSTM” model variant. More significantly, the diagram itself lacks precision. While omitting details from the model description in the prose is acceptable, the diagram should maintain a clear and consistent semantics. The meaning of the empty circles between the input and “LSTM” boxes is unclear. The model appears to include a look-up layer and a GloVe layer, but the number of subsequent representation layers is not specified. While the pooling tanh layers pre-softmax are clearly depicted, the architecture leading up to them is not. Furthermore, the representation within the “LSTM” boxes requires clarification – specifically, which representation is directly connected to the subsequent layers. A clearer depiction of this connection is recommended.
* The sentence beginning on line 480 is difficult to follow. The models do not inherently require padding, which appears to be a consequence of TensorFlow and/or training efficiency. If this is the case, it should be stated explicitly. The connection between this technical detail and the question of “the most convenient way to encode causal meaning” is unclear. The relevance of “convenience” to causal meaning is not established.
* The finding that two independent LSTMs (“Stated_LSTM”) perform slightly better than a sequential architecture (first LSTM feeding into the second) echoes debates in natural language entailment regarding independent versus sequential premise/hypothesis representation. This remains an open question in entailment and likely warrants further investigation in the context of causal relations. Therefore, the conclusion beginning on line 587 – that the results invalidate the assumption about the relationship between the input event meanings and that independent encoding is preferable – is premature, particularly given the potential for shared information within sentence subparts.
* The hyperparameters yielding optimal performance across tasks are difficult to reconcile (e.g., lines 578 and 636). It is unclear whether this inconsistency should be interpreted or attributed to the inherent unpredictability of these models.
* Section 4.3 claims the system “correctly disambiguate[s]” the causal meaning of ‘which then’ in a way that Hidey and McKeown’s system does not. This claim is based on a single example and requires more robust substantiation. A comprehensive evaluation using a diverse set of ambiguous examples would strengthen this assertion and address the possibility of a lucky result with the example presented in table 8.
