This paper develops an LSTM-based model to classify connective uses to determine whether they intend a causal relationship. Its core idea is that the expression of causal relationships is extremely diverse and therefore not suitable for syntactic treatment, and the more abstract representations given by neural models are therefore more suitable as the basis for making these judgments.

The experiments were conducted on the Altlex corpus developed by HIde and McKeown. The results provide modest but consistent support for the general idea, and they provide some initial insights into the best way to translate this idea into a model. The paper distribution includes TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Some important comments and questions:

*   The introduction is unusual because it is more like a literature review than a complete overview of the material included in the paper. It goes towards some redundancy with the related work section that follows. I think I am open to a non-standard type of introduction, but it really doesn't work: despite reviewing a lot of ideas, it takes no stand on what the cause is or how it is expressed, but only makes a negative point (which is not reducible to syntax). We are not really told what the positive contribution will be, except for the very general final paragraph of the section.
*   Adding to the above, I was disappointed that the paper is not really clear about the theory of cause that is being assumed. The author seems to default to a counterfactual approach that is roughly similar to David Lewis's, where the cause is a model sufficiency with some other counterfactual conditions. See lines 238 and onwards; there should be a very specific type of implication in the arrow to work on this, and there are well-known problems with Lewis's theory (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). The paper has comments elsewhere that the author does not support the counterfactual approach, but then what theory is being assumed? It cannot be the temporary constraint mentioned on page 3!
*   I do not understand the comments about the example given at line 256. The author thinks they are misunderstanding the sentence. If this is true, then there should be some causal relationship between the argument and the breakdown. There are remaining issues about dividing events into sub-events, and these affect causal theories, but they are not being discussed here, which has confused me.
*   The caption for figure 1 is misleading, because the diagram should only represent a model of the type "pair_LSTM". My big complaint is that this diagram is unnecessarily ambiguous. I think it is okay to leave some parts of the standard model definition out of the prose, but then these diagrams should have a clear and consistent economics. What are all the empty circles between the input and the "LSTM" boxes? The prose suggests that the model has a lookup layer, a glove layer, and then... how many layers of representation are there? The diagram is accurate on pooling TNH in Pre-Softmax, but not about it. I am also not clear what the "LSTM" boxes represent. It seems that this is the leftmost/last representation that is directly connected to the above layers. I suggest clarifying that connection.
*   I do not understand the sentence starting from line 480. The model under discussion does not need any padding internally. I think it needs TensorFlow and/or efficient training. That's fine. If this is true, please say so. However, I do not understand the final clause. How does this issue relate to the question "what is the most convenient way to encode causal meaning"? I do not see how convenience is an issue or how it is directly related to causal meaning.
*   The author thinks that having two independent LSTMs ("stated_LSTM") is somewhat better than putting one feed into the other. This issue reminds me of the discussion in the literature on natural language implication, where the question is whether the premise and hypothesis should be represented independently or whether the first feeds into the second. I consider this an open question for implication, and I bet it should be further investigated for causal relationships as well. Therefore I cannot really support the sentence starting from line 587: "This behavior means that our assumption about the relationship between the meanings of two input events is not valid, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then measure the relationship between the arguments using dense layers." This is very surprising because we are talking about subparts of a sentence that can share a lot of information.
*   It is difficult to understand the hyperparameters that motivate the best performance in the tasks. For example, compare line 578 with line 636. Should we understand this or simply attribute it to the unpredictability of how these models interact with data?
*   Section 4.3 कनेक्टिव 'विद देन' concludes by saying that the system "can accurately obscure its causal meaning", while HIde and McKeown do not. This may be true, but an example is not enough to show it. To prove this point, I suggest creating a wide range of examples that reveal ambiguity and see how often the system makes the right decision. This will help to find out whether it was lucky with the example from table 8.
