This paper proposes a method for detecting causal relationships between clauses, using neural networks ("deep learning," although, like many studies, the networks are not particularly deep). In fact, while some discourses explicitly identify the connection they indicate (for example, 'because' is a causal relationship), this paper leverages a recently published dataset (called AltLex by Hidey and McKeown, 2016) to solve the task of identifying causal versus non-causal relationships when the connection is not explicitly marked. Arguing that convolutional networks are not as efficient as LSTMs in representing the relevant features of clauses, the authors propose a classification architecture that uses a glove-based representation of clauses, which is input to an LSTM layer, followed by three densely connected layers (tanh) and a final decision layer (softmax).

The system's best configuration has an F1 of 0.5-1.5% better than Hidey and McKeown's 2016 (SVM classifier). Several examples of generalization are shown where the system performs well (the indicative words that are always causal in the training data, but correctly non-causal in the test data). Therefore, I appreciate that the system has been analyzed qualitatively and quantitatively.

The paper is well-written, and the problem statement is particularly clear. However, explanations of the difference between this work and the underlying connective recognition task would be welcome. This could include a discussion of why previous methods for underlying connective recognition could not be used in this case.

It is very commendable that the authors uploaded their code to the submission site (I briefly looked at it but did not execute it). Uploading the (old) data (with the code) is also useful because it provides several examples. It was not clear to me what the 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files meant, given that the paper mentions binary classification. I was also surprised to see that this is Hidey and McKeown's data, the authors have the right to repost it as they do. -- A point to clarify in the paper would be the meaning of "bootstrapping," which clearly increases the corpus by about 15%: while corpus building is briefly but clearly explained in the paper, there is no additional bootstrapping.

While experimenting with neural networks on this task is certainly interesting, the properties of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. It seems that the best configuration (out of 4-7 options) is found on the test data, and this is the best configuration declared to be "2.13% better F1" than Hidey's. However, a proper comparison would involve selecting the best configuration on devset.

Furthermore, it is not entirely clear how significant the improvement is. On the one hand, given the size of the dataset, it should be possible to calculate some statistically significant indicators. On the other hand, the reliability of gold-standard annotations (possibly from the dataset's creators) should also be considered. Upon inspection, annotations obtained from the English/Simple English Wikipedia are not correct, and therefore the scores may need to be considered with a pinch of salt.

Finally, neural methods have previously been shown to outperform human-made features for binary classification tasks, so in a way, the results are confirmation of a known property. It would be interesting to see experiments with simple networks used as baselines, for example, a 1-layer LSTM. Analyzing the results may try to explain why the neural method supports accuracy on recall.
