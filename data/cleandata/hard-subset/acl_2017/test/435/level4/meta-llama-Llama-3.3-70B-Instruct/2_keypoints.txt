- Proposes a neural approach to detect causal relations between clauses using the AltLex dataset.  
- Architecture: GloVe clause embeddings → LSTM → three dense tanh layers → softmax; authors argue LSTMs outperform CNNs for this task.  
- Reports a 0.5–1.5% F1 improvement over Hidey & McKeown (2016) SVM; best configuration claimed to yield up to 2.13% F1 lift.  
- Provides qualitative examples showing the model generalizes (e.g., treats indicator words as non-causal in test despite causal training usage).  
- Paper is well-structured with a clear problem description but needs clarification on how this task differs from implicit connective recognition and why prior methods don’t apply.  
- Code and data were uploaded for reproducibility, which is appreciated.  
- The meaning of the 0-1-2 coding in TSV files is unclear given the paper frames the task as binary.  
- Unclear whether authors have permission to redistribute Hidey & McKeown data.  
- The “bootstrapping” procedure that increases the corpus by ~15% is insufficiently explained.  
- Model selection appears to use the test set (choosing best of 4–7 configs), which is inappropriate; selection should be on the development set.  
- Statistical significance of the reported gains is not provided; given dataset size, significance testing should be feasible.  
- Concerns about the reliability and noisiness of the gold-standard annotations (Wikipedia-derived) and their impact on scores.  
- Result novelty is limited because neural methods often outperform hand-crafted features; simpler baseline networks (e.g., 1-layer LSTM) should be evaluated.  
- Analysis should investigate why the neural model favors precision over recall.
