This paper proposes a method for identifying causal relationships between clauses using neural networks ("deep learning," although many studies have stated that the networks are not particularly deep). In fact, while some discourse connectives are unambiguous about the relationships they indicate (e.g., 'because' causal), this paper takes advantage of a recent dataset (AltLex by Hide and McKeown, 2016) to solve the task of identifying causal versus non-causal relationships when the relationship is not clearly identified. Neural networks are not as efficient as LSTMs in representing the relevant features of clauses, so the authors propose a classification architecture that uses a glove-based representation of clauses, which is input at the LSTM level, then three densely connected layers (tons) and a final decision layer with a softmax.

The system's best configuration improves F1 by 0.5-1.5% over Hide and McKeown's 2016 (SVM classifier). Several examples of generalizations are shown where the system performs well (the indicative words that are always causal in the training data, but correctly non-causal in the test data). Therefore, I appreciate that the system has been analyzed qualitatively and quantitatively.

The paper is well-written and the problem description is particularly clear. However, a clarification of the differences between this work and the underlying connective recognition task would be welcome. It might include a discussion of why previous methods for underlying connective recognition cannot be used in this case.

It is highly commendable that the authors have uploaded their code to the submission site (I briefly visited it but did not execute it). Uploading the (old) data (with the code) is also useful because it provides many examples. The meaning of 0-1-2 coding in the TSV files was not clear to me, as the paper mentions binary classification. I am also wondering, since this is Hide and McKeown's data, do the authors have the right to repost it? - One thing to clarify in the paper would be the meaning of "bootstrapping," which apparently expands the corpus by about 15%: although the corpus structure is concise but clearly explained, there is no over-bootstrapping.

The testing of neural networks in this work is certainly interesting, but the qualities of the proposed system are not entirely convincing. It really seems that the best configuration (among 4-7 options) is found in the test data, and it is this best configuration that is declared to be improved by "2.13% F1" from the Luke. However, a fair comparison involves selecting the best configuration in the dataset.

Furthermore, it is not entirely clear how significant the improvement is. On the one hand, it should be possible to calculate some statistical significance indicators in terms of the dataset size. On the other hand, the reliability of the gold-standard annotations (possibly from the dataset creators) should also be considered. Upon inspection, the annotations obtained from the English/Plain English Wikipedia are not perfect, and therefore the scores may be taken with some skepticism.

Finally, neural methods have previously shown to perform better than human-created features for binary classification tasks, so in a sense, the results are rather a confirmation of a known feature. It would be interesting to test with common networks used as baselines, such as a 1-layer LSTM. An analysis of the results may try to explain why neural methods prefer recall over accuracy.
