This paper builds a model based on LLTM that classifies the use of connective words. It can determine whether these words indicate a causal relationship. Its core idea is that causal relationships are expressed very differently and therefore cannot be analyzed syntactically. Abstract representations from neural models are more suitable for reaching this conclusion.

These experiments were conducted in the Altlex corpus, invented by Hadley and McKeown. The results provide slight but consistent support for the general idea and provide some initial insights into the best way to translate this idea into a model. This paper includes the TensorFlow-based models used for the experiments.

Some important comments and questions:

* The introduction is unusual because it is like a literature review, where there is no complete summary of what is in the paper. This results in some repetition with some parts related to the work. I think an unconventional type of introduction is acceptable, but it is not very effective: even after reviewing many concepts, it does not take a position on what or how causal relationships are expressed, but only highlights a negative point (which cannot be explained through syntax). We are not told what the positive contribution would be, except for a very general concluding paragraph.
* Adding to the above points, I am disappointed that the paper is not clear about causal theory. The authors lean towards a speculative approach like David Lewis's idea, where causal relationships claim a model adequacy, with some additional speculative conditions added. See lines 238 and onwards; this arrow must be a special type of implicit to work and there are some well-known problems in Lewis's theory (see http://bcopley.com/wp-content/uploads/CopleyWolff2014.pdf). Elsewhere in the paper, it is commented that the authors do not support a speculative approach, but then what theory is being speculated? It cannot be just the timeframe mentioned on page 3!
* I do not understand the comments related to the example in line 256. The authors are probably saying that they consider the sentence to be false. If it is true, then there should be some causal relationship between the argument and the interval. There are some problems with dividing the events into sub-events and these affect causal theory, but they are not discussed here, so I am confused.
* The caption of figure 1 is misleading because the diagram only wants to show the "pair_LLTM" transformation of the model. My main complaint is that this diagram is unnecessarily ambiguous. I think it is okay to omit some parts of the standard model definition, but these diagrams should have a clear and consistent meaning. What are all the empty circles between the input and the "LLTM" boxes? The prose says the model has a look-up layer, a gloves layer, and then... what? How many layers are there in the representation? The diagram is clear about the pooling THNNH layer for the pre-softmax, but not about it. I am also not clear about what the "LLTM" boxes represent. It seems that only the left/last representation is directly connected to the above layers. I suggest showing that connection clearly.
* I do not understand the sentence starting from line 480. The models under discussion inherently do not need any padding. I am guessing it is a requirement of TensorFlow and/or efficient training. Okay. If that is correct, please tell me. Although I do not understand the last clause. How does this problem relate to the question of "what is the most convenient way to encode causal meaning"? I do not understand how the convenience is a problem or how it is directly related to causal meaning.
* The authors have seen that having two distinct LLTMs ("stated_LLTM") is somewhat better than having one, where the first is connected to the second. This problem reminds me of the underlying literature discussions in natural language, where the question is whether premises and assumptions should be presented independently or whether the first should be connected to the second. I consider this an open question for the underlying and I can bet that it needs further investigation for causal relationships as well. So I cannot support the sentence starting from line 587: "The meaning of this behavior is that our assumption about the relationship between the meanings of two input events is not correct, so it is better to encode each argument independently and then measure the relationship between the arguments using dense layers." This is very surprising, because we are talking about an element of a sentence that can share a lot of information.
* It is difficult to understand the meaning of the hyperparameters that lead to optimal performance in the tasks. For example, compare line 578 with line 636. Should we explain this or blame the uncertainty of how these models interact with the data?
* Paragraph 4.3 ended with the connective 'which gives', where the system can "correctly disambiguate its causal meaning", where that is not possible in the case of Hadley and McKeown. It may be correct but it is not enough to show. To prove this point, I suggest creating various examples that express ambiguity and see how many times the system gives the correct answer. This will help to know whether the example from table 8 was lucky.
