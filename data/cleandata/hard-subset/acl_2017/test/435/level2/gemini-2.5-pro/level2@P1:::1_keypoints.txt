- Paper addresses causal meaning disambiguation by classifying whether a relation between two event spans is causal, using a dual-input LSTM that encodes events separately and is evaluated on the AltLex corpus with new SOTA results.

- Contribution: Achieves state-of-the-art empirical results on AltLex, showing representation learning is effective for causality classification.

- Contribution: Proposes a simple LSTM-based architecture that avoids hand-crafted features and external linguistic resources, improving generalizability.

- Contribution: Provides analysis of modeling choices (e.g., independent vs. state-initialized LSTMs) and qualitative examples illustrating model behavior.

- Strength: Strong empirical performance with substantial F1 and accuracy gains over prior baselines and Hidey & McKeown (2016).

- Strength: Clear and well-motivated problem formulation arguing for context encoding over pattern- or lexicon-based approaches.

- Strength: Simplicity and potential generalizability of the "Pair LSTM" method compared to feature-heavy prior systems.

- Strength: Insightful analysis including model variant comparisons and qualitative examples where the neural model succeeds where the baseline fails.

- Weakness: Input representation is unclear—paper does not specify exactly how e1, l, and e2 are extracted/concatenated from sentences, hindering reproducibility.

- Weakness: Architectural novelty is limited—the Pair LSTM is a standard sentence-pair encoding pattern, so the contribution is more an effective application than a novel architecture.

- Weakness: Lacks ablation studies to assess importance of components (pre-trained embeddings vs random, role of lexical marker l, and simpler baselines like averaged embeddings).

- Question: Clarify how the two LSTM input sequences are constructed from raw sentences with annotated spans (e.g., is the lexical marker included in the second input?).

- Question: Why does the independent "Pair LSTM" outperform the sequentially-initialized variant—could sequential encoding conflate surface order with logical relations?

- Question: Have you analyzed attention/activation patterns to identify which context words drive decisions in ambiguous cases (e.g., "which then") to support the context-encoding claim?
