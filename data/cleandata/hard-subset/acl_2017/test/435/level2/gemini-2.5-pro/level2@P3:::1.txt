**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which it defines as a binary classification problem: determining whether a causal relation exists between two events in a text. The central claim is that successfully performing this task requires encoding the semantic context of the events, rather than relying on predefined syntactic patterns or lexical resources which have limited coverage.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A dual-input LSTM-based neural network architecture designed to independently encode the text spans of two potentially related events. The encoded representations are then combined to classify the relationship as causal or non-causal. This approach moves beyond traditional feature engineering by learning representations directly from the text.
2.  A thorough empirical evaluation on the two versions of the AltLex corpus. The proposed model is shown to outperform strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art feature-based system on this dataset. The results support the paper's main hypothesis that a context-encoding neural model can better capture the nuances of causal language.
3.  A detailed analysis of the model's performance, including an exploration of different architectural variants (e.g., connected vs. independent LSTMs), optimizers, and padding strategies. Qualitative examples effectively demonstrate the model's ability to handle ambiguous cases that are challenging for previous methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and clear problem formulation.** The paper does an excellent job of surveying prior work and articulating its limitations, namely the reliance on specific syntactic constructions or lexical resources that fail to capture the diverse expression of causality in language. This provides a strong justification for the proposed neural approach, which aims to learn a more general representation of causal relations from context.
2.  **Robust experimental methodology.** The evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The work compares against appropriate and strong baselines, including the system that introduced the dataset. The use of both the "non bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" versions of the AltLex corpus, as well as an additional experiment on a balanced subset, demonstrates the robustness of the findings across different data conditions.
3.  **Strong empirical results and insightful analysis.** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance, showing clear improvements over a strong feature-based system in terms of both F1-score and accuracy. The discussion of the precision-recall trade-off is valuable, and the model's ability to achieve a better balance is a significant result. The qualitative error analysis in Section 4.3 provides compelling anecdotal evidence for the model's effectiveness on lexically and structurally ambiguous examples.
4.  **Clarity of presentation.** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation, proposed method, and experimental results are presented logically and are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited architectural exploration.** The proposed LSTM architecture is effective but relatively standard. Given the task of modeling a relationship between two text spans, it would be interesting to see an exploration of more sophisticated mechanisms, such as attention. An attention mechanism could allow the model to learn which parts of the first event are most relevant to the second event (and vice-versa), potentially capturing the relational nature of causality more explicitly than simple concatenation of the encoded vectors. This could be a valuable point of comparison and a way to further improve performance.
2.  **Lack of justification for model input structure.** The model takes two inputs: the first event (`e1`) and the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event (`l` + `e2`). The rationale for grouping the lexical marker with the second event is not explicitly discussed. It is unclear if alternative configurations were tested, such as treating the marker as a separate input, prepending it to the entire sequence, or simply encoding the full sentence context between the events. An ablation study on the treatment of the lexical marker could strengthen the architectural claims.
3.  **Ambiguity in the definition of "event" inputs.** Section 3.1 defines an event as a verb or noun phrase, but it is not specified precisely what text is fed into the LSTMs. For instance, is the input just the minimal text span of the event (e.g., "the leak"), or does it include the entire clause containing the event? Clarifying the process of extracting the input sequences from the source sentences would improve the reproducibility and clarity of the method.
4.  **Minor formatting issues.** The references section contains several formatting errors (e.g., extraneous characters, repeated author names). These should be corrected in the final version of the paper.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could the paper elaborate on the decision to concatenate the lexical marker `l` with the second event `e2`? Were other configurations, such as encoding the marker separately or encoding the full context between the event spans, considered during development?
2.  The results suggest that the "Pair LSTM" (independent encoders) consistently outperforms the "Stated Pair LSTM" (sequentially connected encoders). This is an interesting finding. Does this imply that the causal relationship is better modeled as a non-sequential, semantic comparison between two independently understood events, rather than a direct continuation of context? A brief discussion on the interpretation of this result would be insightful.
