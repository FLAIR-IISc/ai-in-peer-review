

Summary of the paper: The paper proposes a neural network architecture for disambiguating causal meaning in sentences, arguing that encoding sentence meaning is required for causal disambiguation. The authors claim their system outperforms the state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the AltLex corpus, particularly regarding ambiguous causal markers and their contextual dependence.
2. The authors propose an LSTM-based neural architecture for causal meaning disambiguation that outperforms a baseline system on the AltLex corpus.

Strengths:
1. The paper correctly identifies a key challenge in causality classification: the contextual dependence of causal markers (e.g., "since" can be temporal or causal). The AltLex corpus is well-suited for studying this problem, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of its characteristics, particularly regarding ambiguous lexical markers.

2. The authors properly critique previous approaches that rely on lexical resources (WordNet, VerbNet, etc.), correctly noting that such systems are limited by the coverage of these resources. Their neural approach using only word embeddings is more general and avoids this limitation, which is a valid point.

3. The experimental results are convincing in that their approach significantly outperforms the majority class baseline (B1) and the Hidey and McKeown system (B2) in terms of precision for the causal class, suggesting their model can better distinguish causal from non-causal relations.

Weaknesses:
1. The main claim that "encoding the meaning of a sentence is required for the disambiguation of its causal meaning" is not sufficiently substantiated. Previous work (Do et al. 2011, Riaz and Girju 2013, 2014) also used contextual information for causality classification, and the paper doesn't adequately differentiate their approach from these prior works. The paper's neural approach is similar to these previous works in using contextual information, but the authors don't make this distinction clear.

2. The paper's comparison to the state-of-the-art is limited to the Hidey and McKeown (2016) system, which is not the most recent approach. They don't compare to other neural approaches published around the same time (e.g., Kruengkrai et al. 2017), making the claim of "outperforming the state-of-the-art" questionable. The paper should compare to more recent neural methods to properly establish their contribution.

3. The paper doesn't adequately address the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus. The Causal class is significantly smaller than the Non-Causal class, which could affect the results. While they do show results on a balanced version of the corpus, they don't discuss how the imbalance affected their initial results or how their model handles it.

4. The proposed neural architecture ("Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM") is not novel. These are standard neural network architectures for sentence pair classification, and the paper doesn't provide a strong rationale for why these architectures were chosen over more common alternatives. The paper would benefit from a more detailed justification of their architecture choice.

5. The paper's evaluation is limited to the AltLex corpus. They don't demonstrate how their approach would perform on other corpora or in a more general causality classification task, which limits the generalizability of their results.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper claims to outperform the state-of-the-art, but the Hidey and McKeown (2016) system is not the most recent approach. Have you compared your system to other recent neural approaches for causality classification?
2. The paper mentions that the "bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus has instances with different class distributions in training and test sets. How does your system specifically handle these challenging cases, and what is the impact on performance?
3. The authors claim that neural networks are able to encode the meaning of events, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that their approach is fundamentally different from previous context-based approaches. Could you clarify how your approach differs from those that also use contextual information?
