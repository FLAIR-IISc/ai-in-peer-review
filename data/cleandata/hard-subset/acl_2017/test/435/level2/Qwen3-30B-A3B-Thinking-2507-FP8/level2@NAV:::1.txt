

This paper proposes a neural network architecture for causal meaning disambiguation, arguing that context encoding is essential for accurately determining causal relations between events. The main contributions are: 1) a novel LSTM-based architecture that processes cause and effect events along with the lexical marker between them; 2) empirical demonstration that context encoding outperforms previous lexical/syntactic feature-based approaches; and 3) successful application to the AltLex corpus, which contains ambiguous causal expressions.

Strengths: The paper correctly identifies a key limitation in prior work (reliance on specific syntactic constructions and lexical resources) and proposes a more generalizable approach. The experimental results are compelling, with the proposed "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" configuration outperforming the previous state-of-the-art by 2.13% in F1 score on the non-bootstrapping corpus. The analysis of ambiguous cases (e.g., "which then") demonstrates the model's ability to handle contextual disambiguation where previous approaches fail. The paper also thoughtfully addresses the corpus limitations and class imbalance issues.

Weaknesses: The paper does not sufficiently address the limited size of the AltLex corpus (only ~20k examples), which is inadequate for robust neural network training. The description of the neural architecture lacks critical hyperparameter details (e.g., exact LSTM cell size, embedding dimensions), hindering reproducibility. The novelty claim is overstated; the paper doesn't clearly differentiate its context-encoding approach from Melamud et al. (2016) who also used LSTMs for context encoding. The paper also fails to analyze why the "Pair LSTM" architecture (without event connection) outperforms the "State Pair LSTM" variant.

Questions: How does the performance scale with larger datasets? What specific contextual features does the model learn to distinguish causal from non-causal relations? Could the model be adapted to handle the more challenging causal argument identification subtask mentioned in the paper?
