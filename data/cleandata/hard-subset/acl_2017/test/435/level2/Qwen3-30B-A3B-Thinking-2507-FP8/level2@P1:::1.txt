

Summary: The paper proposes a neural network architecture for causality classification, specifically focusing on disambiguuation of causal meaning based on context. The authors argue that causality classification should not be limited to syntactic patterns or lexical resources, but rather should rely on neural encoding of contextual information. They evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus (Hidey and McKeown, 2016), which contains both explicit and implicit causal expressions. The authors compare two LSTM-based architectures ("Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM") with different zero-padding strategies and optimizers.

Main contributions:
1. The authors present a neural network approach for causality disambiguation that relies on contextual encoding rather than syntactic features or lexical resources.
2. They demonstrate that their model outperforms the state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus, which is the largest available resource for this task.
3. They provide detailed analysis of the model's performance on ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "since," "after"), showing that their approach correctly disambiguates cases where previous methods fail.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a compelling argument that causality is not defined by fixed linguistic constructions, addressing a key limitation in prior work. The authors effectively critique previous approaches that rely on syntactic constructions or lexical resources, showing how these fail to capture the full range of causal expressions.
2. The evaluation is well-designed and uses the most appropriate corpus (AltLex) for the task. The comparison with Hidey and McKeown (2016) is fair and meaningful, and the authors provide detailed analysis of how their model handles ambiguous cases (Table 8), which is a significant strength.
3. The paper provides a thorough analysis of model configurations, including different zero-padding strategies, optimizers, and architectures. The results show that their approach consistently outperforms baselines, particularly in balancing precision and recall for the causal class.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why they chose LSTM over more recent neural architectures (e.g., transformers, attention mechanisms) that have become standard since 2016. While they briefly mention CNNs, they don't discuss why they didn't explore more advanced architectures that might better capture long-range dependencies in causal relations.
2. The paper doesn't adequately address the class imbalance in the AltLex corpus. While they mention reducing the number of Non-Causal instances in one experiment, they don't provide a systematic analysis of how class imbalance affects their results or how their model might be improved to handle imbalanced data better.
3. The improvement over the state-of-the-art is relatively modest (around 2-7% on different metrics), and the paper doesn't strongly argue why this improvement is significant in the context of the field. The authors claim to "outperform the state-of-the-art" but don't provide sufficient context for how meaningful this improvement is.

Questions to Authors:
1. How would your model perform on a more balanced dataset, and what would be the impact of class imbalance on your results? The current corpus has a significant imbalance (see Table 3), which might affect the model's performance.
2. Why did you choose LSTM over other neural architectures (e.g., transformers, attention mechanisms) that might better capture long-range dependencies in causal relations? Given that the paper was submitted for ACL 2017, transformers were still emerging, but the authors could have discussed this trade-off more thoroughly.
3. How do you plan to extend your work to the second subtask of causality classification (causal argument identification) mentioned in the paper? The authors mention this as future work but don't elaborate on the challenges or potential approaches.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of causality classification, with a well-motivated approach and thorough evaluation. The paper makes a convincing case for the importance of contextual encoding in causality disambiguation, and the results demonstrate that neural approaches can outperform previous methods. The minor weaknesses noted above are not deal-breakers, but addressing them would strengthen the paper significantly. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the field, and with some additional analysis, this work could be a strong candidate for publication at ACL 2017.
