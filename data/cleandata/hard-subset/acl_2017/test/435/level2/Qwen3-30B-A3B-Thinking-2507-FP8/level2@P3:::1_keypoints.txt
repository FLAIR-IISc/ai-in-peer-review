- Summary: Proposes a neural architecture for disambiguating causal lexical markers using contextual encoding, outperforming prior methods on the AltLex corpus and handling ambiguous markers.

- Strength: Strong theoretical argument that causal meaning requires contextual encoding rather than fixed syntactic patterns or lexical resources, well-grounded in literature.

- Strength: Appropriate and necessary evaluation on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating strong performance on a challenging dataset of causal relations.

- Strength: Detailed analysis on ambiguous cases (e.g., "which then") with illustrative examples showing the model handles instances where prior systems fail.

- Weakness: Insufficient justification for choosing the specific neural architecture (Pair LSTM with mean padding and Adam), leaving optimality unclear.

- Weakness: Limited comparison to prior work—only two baselines—and lack of discussion relating the approach to other neural causality methods.

- Weakness: Missing details on training, hyperparameters, and computational requirements, hindering reproducibility and assessment of practicality.

- Weakness: No discussion of limitations such as language-specific biases, domain scalability, or performance across different grammatical structures.

- Question: Request for more details on hyperparameter tuning, including ranges tested for dropout and regularization.

- Question: How does the architecture compare to transformer-based models for handling long-range dependencies in causal relations?

- Question: Are there plans to extend the work to other languages or domains, and what challenges are anticipated?

- Conclusion: Valuable demonstration of the necessity of contextual encoding for causal disambiguation, but needs stronger architectural justification, broader comparisons, and greater training transparency.
