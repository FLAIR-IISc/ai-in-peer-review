

Summary: The paper proposes a neural network architecture for disambiguating causal lexical markers based on context, arguing that causal meaning requires contextual encoding rather than reliance on syntactic constructions or lexical resources. The primary contribution is a neural approach to causal meaning disambiguation that outperforms prior methods on the AltLex corpus. The secondary contribution is a thorough evaluation demonstrating the model's ability to handle ambiguous lexical markers.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a well-supported theoretical argument that causality is not captured by fixed syntactic constructions or lexical resources, which is well-grounded in the literature review. The critique of previous approaches that rely on specific syntactic patterns or lexical resources is compelling and addresses a meaningful gap in the field.

2. The evaluation on the AltLex corpus is appropriate and necessary, as it represents the most comprehensive resource for causal relations at the time. The authors properly justify their choice of corpus and demonstrate that their approach works well on this challenging dataset, particularly for ambiguous lexical markers.

3. The analysis of the model's performance on ambiguous cases like "which then" provides strong evidence for the claim that contextual encoding is necessary for causal disambiguation. The examples in Table 8 effectively illustrate the model's ability to handle cases where previous systems fail.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient justification for the specific neural architecture chosen. While different configurations are evaluated, the rationale for selecting Pair LSTM with mean padding and Adam optimizer is not clearly articulated, making it difficult to understand why this configuration is optimal.

2. The comparison to previous work is limited to two baselines, and the paper does not adequately address how the proposed approach relates to other neural methods for causality classification. A more comprehensive comparison to contemporary neural approaches would strengthen the paper's contribution claim.

3. The paper does not provide sufficient detail about the training process, hyperparameters, or computational requirements. This limits reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the practicality of the approach, particularly for larger-scale applications.

4. The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed approach, such as potential language-specific biases, scalability to different domains, or performance on causal relations with different grammatical structures. Addressing these limitations would provide a more balanced perspective on the work's contribution.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the specific ranges tested for dropout and regularization values?

2. How does the proposed architecture compare to transformer-based models that have become more prevalent since 2016, particularly in terms of handling long-range dependencies in causal relations?

3. Are there any plans to extend this work to other languages or domains beyond the AltLex corpus, and if so, what challenges might arise in those contexts?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating the necessity of contextual encoding for causal meaning disambiguation. While the evaluation is appropriate and the results are promising, the paper would benefit from more thorough justification of the architecture choice, a more comprehensive comparison to related work, and greater transparency about training details. The work shows potential for practical application in natural language understanding systems that require accurate causal reasoning.
