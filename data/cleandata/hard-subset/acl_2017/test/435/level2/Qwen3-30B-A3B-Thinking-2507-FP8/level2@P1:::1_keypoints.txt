- Proposes a neural network architecture that disambiguates causal meaning using contextual encoding rather than syntactic patterns or lexical resources.  
- Evaluates two LSTM-based architectures ("Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM") with different zero-padding strategies and optimizers on the AltLex corpus.  
- Demonstrates performance gains over previous state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus, the largest available resource for this task.  
- Provides detailed analysis showing the model successfully disambiguates ambiguous lexical markers (e.g., "since," "after") where prior methods fail.  
- Argues that causality cannot be captured by fixed linguistic constructions and critiques prior syntactic/lexical approaches.  
- Uses a well-chosen evaluation corpus (AltLex) and presents fair comparisons to Hidey and McKeown (2016), including granular analysis (e.g., Table 8).  
- Thoroughly analyzes model configurations (zero-padding, optimizers, architectures) and shows consistent improvements, especially for balancing precision and recall on the causal class.  
- Does not justify the choice of LSTM over newer architectures (transformers/attention) that could better capture long-range dependencies.  
- Fails to systematically address class imbalance in the AltLex corpus or analyze its impact on results and mitigation strategies.  
- Improvement over prior work is modest (≈2–7%); the paper lacks discussion on the practical/significant importance of this gain.  
- Asks how the model would perform on a more balanced dataset and what the impact of class imbalance would be.  
- Requests justification for choosing LSTM instead of more recent architectures and discussion of that trade-off.  
- Seeks clarification on plans and approaches for extending the work to causal argument identification (the second subtask).  
- Overall verdict: solid, well-motivated contribution showing contextual neural encoding helps causality disambiguation; addressing the noted weaknesses would strengthen the paper for publication.
