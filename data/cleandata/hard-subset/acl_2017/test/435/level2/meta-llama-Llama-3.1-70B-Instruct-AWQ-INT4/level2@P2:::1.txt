This paper proposes a neural network architecture for the task of causality classification, specifically for disambiguating the causal meaning of lexical markers in context. The authors claim that encoding the meaning of the sentence is required for this task and provide empirical results to support this claim.

**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

1. The authors propose a neural network architecture with two inputs, one for the first event and the other for the lexical marker and the second event, to disambiguate the causal meaning of lexical markers in context.
2. They evaluate their proposal on the AltLex corpus, which is a corpus of implicit causal relations, and show that their system outperforms the state-of-the-art.
3. They also provide an analysis of the results, highlighting the importance of encoding the context for disambiguating the causal meaning of lexical markers.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper provides a clear and well-motivated proposal for addressing the task of causality classification.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their proposal, including a comparison with the state-of-the-art and an analysis of the results.
3. The paper highlights the importance of encoding the context for disambiguating the causal meaning of lexical markers, which is a key contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper relies heavily on the AltLex corpus, which may not be representative of all types of causal relations.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by their system, which could provide further insights into the challenges of the task.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results and the potential applications of the proposed system.

**Questions to authors:**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting, given the relatively small size of the AltLex corpus?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the error analysis of their system, including the types of errors that are most common and the potential causes of these errors?
3. How do the authors see their proposed system being used in practice, and what potential applications do they envision for the system?
