## Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

This paper addresses the challenging task of causal relation classification, arguing that encoding sentence meaning via neural networks is crucial for disambiguating causal interpretations, particularly when explicit causal markers are absent or ambiguous. The authors propose a neural network architecture utilizing LSTMs to encode the context surrounding potential causal relations and demonstrate improved performance on the AltLex corpus compared to a strong baseline.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the empirical demonstration that a neural network approach, specifically LSTMs, can effectively capture contextual information for causal disambiguation, outperforming a feature-engineered SVM baseline. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimentation with different LSTM configurations and optimization strategies, providing insights into the best practices for this task. Finally, the paper highlights the limitations of relying solely on lexical resources for causality detection, advocating for a more context-aware approach.

The strengths of this paper are its clear motivation, well-defined task, and rigorous experimental evaluation. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature on causality and computational linguistics. The ablation studies with different LSTM architectures and optimizers are valuable, and the inclusion of results on the challenging “bootstrapping” version of the AltLex corpus adds credibility. The analysis of correctly classified examples, particularly those involving ambiguous connectives, provides further support for their claims.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The proposed architecture is relatively simple and lacks novelty; the use of LSTMs for sequence encoding is standard practice. The paper doesn’t explore more advanced architectures like Transformers, which have shown superior performance in similar tasks. The analysis of error cases is limited; a deeper dive into misclassified examples could reveal areas for improvement. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the AltLex corpus, they don’t discuss potential biases within the corpus itself that might influence the results. The writing could be more concise, and some sections (e.g., the extensive literature review) feel unnecessarily detailed for a conference paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you chose LSTMs over other sequence encoding architectures like Transformers?
2.  What specific types of errors does your system still make, and what linguistic phenomena contribute to these errors?
3.  Have you investigated potential biases within the AltLex corpus that might affect the generalizability of your results?
