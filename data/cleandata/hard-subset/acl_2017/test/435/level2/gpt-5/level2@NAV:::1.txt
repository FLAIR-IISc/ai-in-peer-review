Summary and claimed contributions:
The paper proposes an LSTM-based architecture for disambiguating whether a relation between two events is causal, arguing that contextual sentence meaning must be encoded to resolve ambiguity in causal markers. Using the AltLex corpus (both non-bootstrapping and bootstrapping versions), the model reportedly outperforms prior work that relies on feature engineering and lexical resources.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) An end-to-end neural approach that encodes event context (two-input LSTMs) for causal meaning disambiguation, reducing reliance on handcrafted features.
2) An empirical study on AltLex demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines (most-frequent-class per marker and an SVM-based system), including analysis under class imbalance.

Strengths:
- Empirical improvements over prior work: On the non-bootstrapping set, the best model improves Causal F1 from 73.76 to 75.40 and Accuracy from 71.86 to 77.90; on bootstrapping, Causal F1 improves from 80.90 to 81.96 with Accuracy from 79.58 to 80.52. These are consistent across several configurations.
- Solid framing and dataset analysis: The paper motivates why causality cannot be reduced to fixed lexical/syntactic patterns and analyzes marker ambiguity and distribution shifts introduced by bootstrapping.
- Practical modeling choices: A simple architecture with pretrained GloVe, careful comparisons of two-input variants (with/without state transfer), optimizers, and padding strategies; the finding that independent encodings (“Pair LSTM”) outperform state-transfer is informative.
- Preliminary significance testing: McNemar tests show significant gains over the strongest frequency baseline in the reduced, balanced setting.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: Two parallel LSTMs with dense layers is a standard sequence encoding approach; architectural innovation is minimal relative to existing LSTM baselines.
- Narrow evaluation: Results are confined to AltLex; no cross-corpus validation (e.g., PDTB explicit/implicit subsets, Causal-TimeBank) or transfer tests to assess generalization.
- Incomplete ablations: It is unclear how much signal comes from the lexical marker vs. event context. No tests removing the marker, swapping e1/e2, or varying context windows.
- Reproducibility details are sparse: Preprocessing (event extraction boundaries, truncation/padding lengths), training schedules, and hyperparameters are not fully specified; significance vs. the SVM system is not reported.

Questions to authors:
- Can you report significance versus the SVM baseline on both corpus versions?
- How does performance change if the lexical marker is masked or removed? If e1/e2 are swapped?
- What are the maximum sequence lengths and truncation policies for the two inputs?
- Have you evaluated on PDTB or Causal-TimeBank to test cross-dataset robustness?
