Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two events (e1, e2) and a lexical marker (l), determine whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that contextual encoding of the events is necessary for reliable disambiguation.
- Contribution 1: A simple two-input LSTM-based neural architecture that encodes e1 and (l + e2) and feeds their concatenated representations into dense layers for binary classification.
- Contribution 2: An empirical evaluation on the AltLex corpus (both “non-bootstrapping” and “bootstrapping” versions), showing improvements over (i) a strong connective-majority baseline and (ii) the feature-rich SVM of Hidey and McKeown (2016).
- Contribution 3: Preliminary analysis indicating the model can correctly classify some ambiguous or misleading markers and causative verbs when context suggests a non-causal reading.

Strengths
- Empirical gains on a relevant benchmark: The model improves over a strong per-marker majority baseline (B1) and the prior SVM system (B2). On the non-bootstrapping set, F1 on the Causal class improves by ~1.6–1.7 points and accuracy by ~6–7 points over B2; on the bootstrapping set, improvements are smaller but consistent for several configurations.
- Focus on contextual disambiguation: The method leverages context around ambiguous markers (e.g., “since,” “after,” “which then”) and demonstrates cases where lexical identity alone is insufficient. The example-based analysis is convincing and aligned with the motivation.
- Clear task framing: The paper distinguishes causal meaning classification from causal argument identification and motivates why the former should not be constrained by fixed syntactic patterns.
- Simplicity and portability: The model relies only on pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe) and standard LSTM components, without task-specific feature engineering or reliance on lexical resources, improving portability to new domains and markers.

Weaknesses
- Reproducibility and architectural clarity: Key modeling details are underspecified or internally inconsistent. The description of “zero-padding strategies” (max/mean/mode) reads more like pooling operations than padding; Equation (1) is unclear. Important details are missing (LSTM directionality, number of layers/hidden sizes, use of last hidden state vs sequence pooling, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, batching, early stopping, dev split, training epochs). The “Stated Pair LSTM” vs “Pair LSTM” initialization is described, but other hyperparameters and implementation choices are not sufficiently documented for replication.
- Evaluation methodology and statistical rigor: Many configurations are evaluated, but it is unclear how tuning was done (no explicit dev set mentioned), raising the risk of test-set overfitting. No variance across random seeds is reported. Statistical significance is only tested against B1 on a reduced dataset; there is no significance testing vs B2 where claims of state-of-the-art are made. Given the small margins on the bootstrapping set, this matters.
- Metrics and class imbalance: Reporting focuses on F1 for the Causal class and Accuracy; macro-averaged F1 or per-class F1/Precision/Recall would be more appropriate given the imbalance. The undersampling experiment is informative but compares only to B1; comparisons to B2 and other baselines on the balanced setting are missing.
- Baseline coverage and ablations: Stronger/simple baselines are missing, e.g., logistic regression or MLP on averaged embeddings of (e1, l, e2), BiLSTM over the full sentence with/without knowledge of l, or a model excluding the lexical marker to directly test the “context encodes causality” claim. An ablation removing l from the inputs would quantify reliance on marker identity vs event context. Reporting performance restricted to ambiguous markers would strengthen the claim.
- Data and span extraction: The paper assumes access to spans for e1, l, and e2; however, it is not fully clear how these are identified in AltLex and whether span extraction or clause segmentation introduces noise. The paper would benefit from a precise description of how spans are obtained from the corpus.
- Writing and references: Several tables/figures contain formatting issues or missing numbers (e.g., Table 6), and many references are incomplete. These hinder verification and clarity.

Questions to authors
- How are e1 and e2 spans obtained from AltLex? Are they gold spans in the corpus, or did you perform clause segmentation/heuristics? If the latter, what is the error rate and how might it affect results?
- Please clarify the “zero-padding” vs “pooling” operations. Is pad(x) in Eq. (1) a sequence-length decision or a temporal pooling operation (max/mean/mode) over LSTM outputs? What exactly is passed to the dense layers?
- Did you fine-tune GloVe embeddings during training? What were the LSTM hidden sizes, number of layers, dropout rates applied to which layers, batch size, epochs, and early stopping criteria? Was a development set used to select hyperparameters?
- How much does performance drop if the lexical marker tokens are excluded from the second input (i.e., use only e2)? Conversely, what if you encode only l (or l + local context) without e1/e2? This would quantify the contribution of contextual event encoding vs marker identity.
- Did you control for unseen/ambiguous markers at test time (e.g., results on the subset where marker-class distributions differ between train/test, or on markers unseen in training)? Providing per-marker breakdowns for ambiguous markers would be helpful.
- Are the improvements over B2 statistically significant on the main test sets? If so, please report significance testing and confidence intervals across multiple random seeds.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Report macro-F1 and per-class F1 to account for class imbalance, and provide confusion matrices. Consider calibration metrics or PR curves for the Causal class.
- Include stronger baselines (e.g., averaged-embedding classifiers, sentence-level BiLSTM/attention) and ablations (removing l; encoding full sentence vs segmented events).
- Provide results specifically on ambiguous markers to better support the central claim.
- Clarify dataset splits and ensure all model selection is done on a dev set; avoid selecting the best configuration on the test set.
- Clean up tables/figures and complete references. Consider releasing code and preprocessing scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and shows promising improvements with a simple neural approach, but the method description and evaluation need to be tightened to substantiate the claims and enable replication.
