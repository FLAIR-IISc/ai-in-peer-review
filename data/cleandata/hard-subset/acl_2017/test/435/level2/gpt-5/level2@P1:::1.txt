Summary and Contributions
This paper tackles causal meaning disambiguation: given two events and an expression that may signal causality, decide whether the relation is causal or not. The authors argue that context encoding is key and propose an LSTM-based architecture with two inputs: (e1) and (l concatenated with e2). They evaluate on the AltLex corpus (Hidey & McKeown, 2016), comparing against a per-connector majority baseline and a feature-rich SVM system, and report consistent gains in F1 (causal class) and accuracy on both the original and bootstrapped splits. They also explore optimizer and pooling choices and provide qualitative analysis.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple neural architecture for causal disambiguation that encodes the context around potentially causal lexical markers and reduces reliance on hand-engineered features and lexicons.
2) An empirical study on AltLex demonstrating improvements over prior work, with analysis of ambiguous markers and error cases.
3) A clear problem framing that separates causal meaning classification from argument identification.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines: On AltLex, the best configuration improves over the SVM baseline by +1.6 F1 on the bootstrapped split (81.96 vs. 80.90) and +1.6 F1 on the non-bootstrapped split (75.40 vs. 73.76), with larger accuracy gains in the non-bootstrapped case (+6.0). The improvements persist across several configurations and a reduced-imbalance setting (with McNemar tests reported there).
- Context-focused modeling: Moving away from lexicon-heavy feature engineering toward learned encodings is well-motivated for causal expressions that are highly context-dependent and not exhaustively covered by resources. The qualitative examples (e.g., “which then” and non-causative uses of “break”) illustrate cases where contextual cues matter and the model succeeds.
- Careful comparison to connector-majority baseline: The per-connector baseline is strong due to many unambiguous markers; consistently beating it indicates the model learns beyond connector identity.
- Analysis of optimizer/aggregation choices and precision/recall trade-offs: The paper surfaces useful practitioner insights (e.g., Adam tends to balance precision and recall better than Adadelta in this task).
- Problem decomposition: Framing causality classification as (i) causal meaning disambiguation and (ii) argument identification is sensible and clarifies scope.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and missing neural baselines: The architectures are standard LSTM encoders with dense layers. There is no comparison to simpler neural alternatives (e.g., average-of-embeddings logistic regression, BiLSTM over the full sentence with a marker indicator, CNN), nor to ablations (e1 only, l+e2 only, full sentence). Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to the proposed two-input design versus general neural modeling.
- Method description clarity/reproducibility: The discussion of sequence length handling is confusing. “Zero-padding strategies” described as max/mean/mode (Eq. 1) sound like pooling operations rather than padding. The role of LSTMs relative to these operations is unclear (are sequences pooled before/after LSTM? Is the LSTM used at all when pooling?). Critical training details are missing (epochs, batch size, early stopping, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, seed variance, dev split). Figure 1 is referenced but not fully specified. These issues impede reproducibility.
- Evaluation scope and controls: Results rely solely on AltLex; no tests on related resources (e.g., PDTB causal or Causal-TimeBank) to gauge generalization. Most metrics reported are causal-class F1 and overall accuracy; macro-averaged F1 over both classes and per-connector macro averaging would strengthen claims. Statistical significance is only reported for the reduced corpus, not for the main comparisons. It is also unclear to what extent performance is driven by connector identity; a control that masks or anonymizes the marker, or evaluates by connector type unseen in training, would be informative.
- Task scope vs. claims: Inputs require an expression “susceptible of signalling causality,” which excludes many truly implicit relations. The title and some narrative suggest broader causal disambiguation, but the experimental setting restricts to cases with an identifiable expression l. This should be made more explicit, and claims calibrated accordingly.
- Dataset presentation issues: Some tables appear inconsistent or contain typographical errors (e.g., class counts), and the train/test split protocol for both corpus variants is under-specified. It is not clear whether the original splits are followed, whether hyperparameters are tuned on a dev set, and how many runs are averaged.

Questions to Authors
- Architecture details: Please clarify the sequence processing pipeline. Where exactly are max/mean/mode applied—are they pooling operations over time? If so, how are LSTMs used? Do you pool LSTM hidden states (e.g., max-over-time), or pool raw embeddings and then apply dense layers? A precise schematic with tensor shapes would help.
- Hyperparameters and training: What are batch size, number of epochs, early stopping criteria, learning rates, and weight decay? Are GloVe embeddings fine-tuned? How are OOV tokens handled? How many random seeds were run, and what is the variance?
- Data splits and tuning: Do you use the original AltLex train/dev/test splits? If not, how are splits created? Which set is used for model selection? Please report significance for the main results and averages over multiple runs.
- Controls/ablations: Have you tried (i) full-sentence encoders with a marker indicator; (ii) removing l from the second input; (iii) using only e1 or only (l+e2); (iv) replacing LSTM with average or CNN; (v) masking the connector identity during training/testing? These would clarify where the gains come from and how much the model leverages context vs. connector identity.
- Generalization: Can you evaluate on PDTB causal relations (explicit and implicit) and/or Causal-TimeBank to assess cross-domain robustness? Alternatively, report per-connector performance and a setting with connectors unseen in training.
- Resource release: Will you release code, trained models, and preprocessing scripts (including event and marker extraction from AltLex) to enable replication?

Overall, the paper addresses a relevant problem and shows promising improvements with neural context encoding. Strengthening methodological clarity, adding ablations and broader evaluation, and calibrating claims to the current scope would significantly improve the work.
