Summary and contributions
- The paper targets causal meaning disambiguation: given two event spans (e1, e2) and a candidate lexical marker l, determine whether the relation is causal. The work proposes a two-input LSTM-based architecture that encodes (e1) and (l + e2), followed by dense layers for interaction and classification.
- Empirical evaluation on both the original (“non-bootstrapping”) and expanded (“bootstrapping”) AltLex datasets shows consistent improvements over a strong majority-by-marker baseline and over the feature-rich SVM system of Hidey and McKeown (2016).
- The study contrasts two model variants (Pair LSTM vs. Stated Pair LSTM), explores optimization choices (Adam vs. Adadelta) and sequence-length handling strategies, and includes qualitative analysis of ambiguous markers and a significance test under a more balanced setting.

Strengths
- Empirical gains over the published AltLex baseline. On the non-bootstrapping split, the best configuration (Pair LSTM Max Adadelta) exceeds the prior SVM by +2.13 F1 (Causal class) and +7.72 Accuracy. On the bootstrapping split, several configurations edge out the SVM by ~+1 F1 and ~+1 Accuracy. This supports the claim that contextual encoding aids disambiguation beyond lexicon-derived features.
- Resource-light approach. The architecture uses only pre-trained GloVe embeddings without external lexical resources (WordNet/VerbNet/PropBank), yet matches or surpasses a strong feature-rich system. This is attractive for scalability and domain transfer.
- Sensible experimental breadth. Results are reported across two official corpus versions, multiple model variants, different optimizers, and different sequence-length settings. The additional experiment on a downsampled non-causal class, along with McNemar’s test, strengthens conclusions under a less skewed label distribution.
- Informative analysis of ambiguity. The discussion of ambiguous markers (e.g., “which then”), causative verbs with non-causal usages (e.g., “break”), and the distributional pitfalls in AltLex offers useful insight into where contextual models help.
- Clear task framing. Separating causality classification from argument identification is a practical decomposition that aligns with how many pipelines are built.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguity and correctness of the model description. The “zero-padding strategies” are described via Equation (1) as pad(x) = {max, mean, mode}, which is not a standard padding operation and leaves unclear how lengths are chosen and how truncation is handled. Shapes and operations in Equations (1)-(2) are loosely specified (e.g., vectorization by a dense layer from IRn×d to IRnd), obscuring reproducibility.
- Missing ablations against simpler neural baselines. The central claim—encoding context with LSTMs is required—is not tested against straightforward alternatives such as: (a) averaging word embeddings with an MLP; (b) a BiLSTM over the full sentence; (c) a CNN baseline (especially given the discussion of Kruengkrai et al., 2017). Without these, the incremental value of the proposed architecture over simpler neural encoders remains uncertain.
- Limited external validation. All results are on AltLex. While other corpora are smaller (e.g., Causal-TimeBank), even partial or zero-shot evaluation (e.g., training on AltLex, testing on Causal-TimeBank explicit links) would better support generality. As framed, the task assumes gold event spans and a candidate marker; practical end-to-end utility remains unassessed.
- Reporting choices under class imbalance. Metrics focus on the Causal class and overall accuracy. Macro-averaged F1, per-class precision/recall, confusion matrices, and calibration/threshold analysis would more fully characterize performance under skew and facilitate comparison to the high-recall SVM baseline. Statistical significance is reported only for the downsampled experiment, not the main results.
- Interpretation of the Stated Pair LSTM finding. The conclusion that “the assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold” is too strong; the poorer performance of state initialization could stem from optimization dynamics, information bottlenecks, or insufficient capacity in the interaction layers, rather than absence of meaningful event-event dependencies.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Padding/truncation: How are sequence lengths determined in the “max/mean/mode” settings? Are longer sequences truncated (head/tail), or are shorter ones padded only? Please clarify Equations (1)-(2) with precise shapes and operations.
- LSTM specifics: What are the LSTM hidden sizes, number of layers, directionality (uni vs. bi), and whether layer normalization or recurrent dropout is used? How are OOV and casing handled?
- Inputs and context: Do inputs contain only the annotated spans (e1) and (l + e2), or any additional sentence context? For implicit relations without an explicit connective, what is provided in place of l?
- Baselines: What is the performance of a bag-of-embeddings + MLP baseline on the same inputs? Was a CNN baseline attempted? If not, could such results be added in a revision to substantiate the architectural choice?
- Generalization: Any preliminary results on Causal-TimeBank or other datasets (even if small), possibly via transfer or few-shot, to support claims of broader applicability?

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and correct the model specification, especially padding/truncation and the interaction layers; include full hyperparameters for reproducibility and, if possible, release code.
- Add simple neural ablations (avg-embeddings + MLP, CNN, BiLSTM over full sentence) and report macro-F1 with per-class metrics and confusion matrices. Include significance tests on the main test sets.
- Extend evaluation to at least one additional corpus (even with caveats) or provide a controlled synthetic/held-out subset targeting ambiguous markers to better isolate contextual gains.
- Provide a deeper error analysis, categorizing common failure modes (e.g., temporal vs. causal “since/after,” discourse scope, anaphora), and analyze sensitivity to the presence/absence of l.
- Minor: fix typos and formatting (e.g., “bootstrapping,” table numbering), and complete/standardize references.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important subtask with a reasonable neural approach and provides solid empirical evidence of gains on the AltLex benchmark, with thoughtful analysis of ambiguity. With clearer model specification, stronger ablations, and modest extensions to evaluation and reporting, this work would be a useful contribution to causality modeling.
