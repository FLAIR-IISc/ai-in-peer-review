This paper proposes a neural network architecture for causal disambiguation, arguing that encoding sentence meaning is crucial for identifying causal relations. The authors claim to outperform the state-of-the-art on the Hidey & McKeown (2016) corpus.

**Contributions:**

1.  **Neural Architecture for Causal Disambiguation:** The primary contribution is the application of LSTMs for the task of causal relation classification. While LSTMs are not novel *per se*, their application to this specific problem, framing it as a meaning encoding task, is a reasonable approach.
2.  **Empirical Evaluation on AltLex Corpus:** The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, which is a valuable resource for causal relation understanding due to its inclusion of non-standard causal expressions. This evaluation provides a concrete demonstration of the method’s performance.
3.  **Analysis of Limitations of Lexical Feature-Based Approaches:** The paper effectively critiques previous approaches reliant on lexical resources and syntactic structures, highlighting the limitations of these methods and motivating the need for a more context-aware approach.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of causal disambiguation and motivates the use of neural networks to overcome the limitations of previous methods. The introduction provides a good overview of the existing landscape and justifies the proposed approach.
2.  **Solid Experimental Setup:** The authors appropriately compare their method against a reasonable baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) and explore different LSTM configurations and optimizers. The use of two versions of the AltLex corpus allows for a more robust evaluation.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The paper provides some insightful analysis of the results, highlighting the limitations of the baseline method and discussing the performance of the proposed system on challenging cases. The discussion of the ambiguous connectives and the impact of the corpus's class distribution is valuable.
4.  **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While the application of LSTMs is reasonable, the architecture itself is relatively standard. The paper does not introduce any novel LSTM modifications or attention mechanisms that would significantly advance the field. The contribution regarding the architecture is therefore incremental.
2.  **Insufficient Ablation Study:** Although different configurations are tested, a more thorough ablation study would be beneficial. For example, it would be valuable to investigate the impact of different word embedding sizes or the contribution of the concatenation of the lexical marker with the second event.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the authors mention challenging cases, a more detailed error analysis would provide deeper insights into the model’s weaknesses. Specifically, analyzing examples where the model fails to correctly identify causal relations could help guide future improvements.
4.  **Overstated Claims of Outperforming SOTA:** The claim of "outperforming the state-of-the-art" feels slightly strong given the relatively modest improvements reported. The performance difference is not overwhelming, and the baseline method (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) is not extensively described, making a direct comparison difficult.
5.  **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** The authors compare against Hidey & McKeown (2016) and a simple majority class baseline. Adding a simple bag-of-words or TF-IDF based model as a baseline would provide further context for the LSTM’s performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the model? Specifically, what kinds of sentences or lexical markers does the model struggle with?
2.  The paper mentions that the "bootstrapping" version of the corpus has instances that do not follow the class distribution of the training set. Can you provide more details on how this affects the results and whether you attempted to address this issue (e.g., through data weighting or sampling)?
3.  Why was the concatenation of the lexical marker with the second event chosen, and were other approaches (e.g., separate embeddings for each) considered?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding types?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a visual representation of the network architecture (e.g., a diagram).
*   The notation in Equation 2 is slightly unclear and could be improved for readability.
*   Consider referencing more recent advances in neural networks for relation extraction, such as attention mechanisms or transformer-based models.
