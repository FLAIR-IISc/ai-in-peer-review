This paper addresses the task of causal discourse relation classification, arguing that neural networks can effectively encode sentence meaning to disambiguate causal interpretations. The authors propose a neural network architecture based on LSTMs and evaluate it on the AltLex corpus, reporting improved performance over a baseline SVM system.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the empirical demonstration that a relatively simple LSTM-based architecture can achieve strong performance on the challenging AltLex corpus for causal relation classification, outperforming the previously reported state-of-the-art. While the architecture itself is not groundbreaking, the results suggest that encoding contextual meaning via LSTMs is a valuable approach for this task. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental analysis, including ablation studies with different LSTM configurations and optimization methods, which provide insights into the optimal settings for this problem. Finally, the authors rightly point to the scarcity of resources for this task and advocate for the creation of new corpora, highlighting an important future direction for research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The core strength of the paper is the demonstrable improvement in performance over the existing state-of-the-art (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex corpus. The reported F1 score improvements are substantial, providing convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Well-Designed Experiments:** The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments, varying LSTM configurations (stated vs. pair), zero-padding strategies (max, mean, mode), and optimizers (Adadelta, Adam). This systematic evaluation helps to understand which components contribute most to performance. The analysis of results with the reduced corpus is also insightful.
3. **Relevant and Well-Motivated Task:** Causal relation classification is a core NLP problem with applications in various areas, including text understanding, reasoning, and question answering. The authors effectively motivate the task by highlighting the limitations of earlier approaches based on syntactic or lexical features. The discussion of AltLex and its importance for addressing the coverage issues of prior corpora is also valuable.
4. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The problem definition, proposed method, and experimental setup are clearly described. The inclusion of examples from the corpus further aids understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** The proposed LSTM-based architecture is relatively standard and lacks significant novelty. While it performs well, it doesn't introduce any fundamentally new neural network techniques. The authors acknowledge this, but it's still a weakness when considering the overall contribution.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the authors present quantitative results, a more detailed qualitative error analysis would be beneficial. Examining specific examples where the system fails to correctly classify causal relations could reveal valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for improvement. The mention of correctly classifying ambiguous cases with "which then" is a good start, but more detail is needed.
3. **Dependency on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** The system heavily relies on pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe). While this is common practice, the paper doesn't explore the impact of different embedding types or the potential for fine-tuning the embeddings during training. This might limit the system's ability to handle domain-specific language or rare words.
4. **Limited Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison with Hidey & McKeown (2016) is reasonable, it would be beneficial to compare with more recent and potentially stronger baseline models, such as those based on transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) that have achieved state-of-the-art results in various NLP tasks.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including examples of misclassified instances and the reasons for the errors?
2. Have you experimented with different pre-trained word embeddings or considered fine-tuning the embeddings during training? If so, what were the results?
3. How would the performance of your system compare to stronger baseline models based on transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa)?
4. The paper mentions the two-step task of "causal meaning classification and causal arguments identification." Is there a plan to address the latter task and potentially integrate the two into a more comprehensive system?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The sentence "Therefore, we propose the neural encoding of the context of the relation in order to disambiguate its causal meaning." appears repeatedly in the introduction. It feels redundant and could be rephrased.
*   Table 4 could be clarified by explaining what "Unambiguous in Causal class" and "Unambiguous in Non Causal class" refer to.
*   It would be helpful to briefly discuss the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to the baseline.
