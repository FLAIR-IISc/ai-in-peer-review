## Review of "Neural Disambiguation of Causal Lexical Markers based on Context"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of causal relation classification, arguing that encoding sentence meaning via neural networks is key to disambiguating causal meaning beyond relying on specific lexical markers or syntactic structures. The authors present a neural network architecture, specifically employing LSTMs, and demonstrate improved performance on the AltLex corpus compared to a baseline system.

The primary contribution of this work is the empirical demonstration that a neural network approach, focusing on contextual encoding, can effectively classify causal relations. The authors successfully outperform a strong baseline (Hidey & McKeown, 2016) on a difficult dataset, indicating the effectiveness of their approach. A secondary contribution lies in the acknowledgement of the limitations of relying solely on lexical resources for causality detection, and advocating for a more holistic, context-aware approach. Finally, the paper provides a valuable experimental setup and analysis using the AltLex corpus, a relatively new resource for this task.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear motivation, solid experimental validation, and insightful analysis. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches grounded in specific syntactic constructions or lexical features. The ablation study with different LSTM architectures (Stated Pair vs. Pair) and optimizers (Adadelta vs. Adam) provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of each component. The analysis of misclassified examples, particularly those involving ambiguous connectives like "which then," offers a compelling rationale for their approach.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The architectural choices, while reasonable, lack extensive justification. A deeper exploration of different neural architectures, such as transformers, could have strengthened the work. The paper also doesn't deeply analyze *why* the LSTM approach outperforms the baseline; more detailed error analysis, beyond highlighting specific examples, is needed. Additionally, the presentation could be more concise; some sections are repetitive and could be streamlined.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper presents a promising approach to a challenging NLP problem. The results are encouraging, and the authors' emphasis on contextual encoding is a valuable contribution. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing LSTMs over other sequence modeling architectures like Transformers?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, quantifying the types of errors made by your system and the baseline?
3.  The paper mentions the counterfactual constraint on causality. Could you discuss how incorporating such constraints might impact the performance of your model?

**Additional Comments:**

The formatting and writing quality are generally good. Consider adding a diagram illustrating the proposed neural network architecture for better clarity.
