### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, framing it as a binary classification problem: given two events and a potential lexical marker, determine if the relationship between them is causal. The authors argue that previous feature-based and rule-based methods are limited by their reliance on specific syntactic patterns or lexical resources. They propose that a neural network, specifically an LSTM-based architecture, can better encode the contextual information needed to disambiguate causality.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  The application of a standard LSTM-based neural architecture to the task of causal relation classification on the AltLex corpus.
2.  An empirical demonstration that this straightforward neural approach can outperform a more complex, feature-engineered SVM baseline from 2016 on this specific dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Relevance:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important problem in NLP. Causal relation extraction is a challenging task with significant downstream applications in question answering, summarization, and information extraction.
2.  **Simplicity of Approach:** The proposed method is a direct application of established neural techniques (LSTMs for sequence encoding followed by dense layers for classification). This avoids the brittle and labor-intensive feature engineering characteristic of earlier systems, which is a valid direction for research.
3.  **Positive Empirical Results:** The experiments show consistent improvements over the two baselines on the AltLex corpus. The gains over the previous state-of-the-art system (B2) are noteworthy, particularly the F1 score improvement of 2.13% on the non-bootstrapped corpus and the more balanced precision/recall trade-off on the bootstrapped corpus.

### Weaknesses

My primary concerns with this paper relate to its limited novelty, the adequacy of its experimental validation, and the depth of its analysis.

1.  **Lack of Methodological Novelty:** The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a standard Siamese-like model that has been widely used for various sentence-pair classification tasks for several years. It involves encoding two sequences with LSTMs, concatenating their representations, and passing them through a multi-layer perceptron. There is no aspect of this architecture that is novel or specifically tailored to the nuances of causality. While applying existing methods to new problems is acceptable, the paper presents this as a primary contribution without acknowledging its standard nature or providing a compelling argument for why this specific, common architecture is uniquely suited for causality over other standard architectures.
2.  **Insufficient Baselines and Evaluation:** The evaluation relies heavily on a comparison to a single, dated baseline: the feature-based SVM system from Hidey and McKeown (2016), which introduced the dataset. For a contemporary NLP paper, this is insufficient. A rigorous evaluation would require comparison against other established neural relation classification models. For instance:
    *   A single BiLSTM that encodes the entire sentence context containing `e1`, `l`, and `e2`.
    *   Attention-based mechanisms to highlight salient words in the events.
    *   Given the time of publication, a comparison against pre-trained transformer models (e.g., fine-tuning BERT) would be the expected standard for this type of classification task.
    Without these stronger and more relevant baselines, it is difficult to ascertain whether the reported performance gains are due to the specific merits of the proposed model or simply reflect the known general superiority of neural methods over traditional feature-based SVMs.
3.  **Superficial Analysis:** The analysis of the model's behavior is limited. The qualitative examples in Table 8 are interesting but feel cherry-picked. A systematic error analysis is missing. What are the common characteristics of the false positives and false negatives? On which types of lexical markers or syntactic constructions does the model succeed or fail? How does this compare to the failure modes of the SVM baseline? Furthermore, the paper lacks ablation studies. For example, what is the impact of using pre-trained GloVe embeddings versus training them from scratch? How do different architectural choices (e.g., number of dense layers, LSTM hidden size) affect the outcome? This lack of deep investigation leaves the claim that the model succeeds by "encoding the context" as an unproven assertion rather than a demonstrated conclusion.
4.  **Problem Formulation and Clarity:** The authors define the input to their model as `e1` and the concatenation of `l` and `e2`. The rationale behind this specific configuration is not provided or justified. It is unclear why this is preferable to other intuitive setups, such as encoding `e1` and `e2` separately and treating the marker `l` as a distinct feature, or simply encoding the entire sentence. Additionally, key hyperparameters of the model, such as the LSTM hidden state size, are not reported, hindering reproducibility.

In summary, while the paper achieves positive results on an interesting task, it essentially confirms that a standard neural network can outperform a 2016-era feature-based system. The work lacks the methodological novelty, rigorous evaluation against contemporary baselines, and in-depth analysis expected for a publication at a top-tier venue.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The "Pair LSTM" architecture is a very common design for sentence-pair tasks. Could you please clarify the novel aspects of your proposed method, or reframe the contribution if the novelty lies elsewhere, for example, in the analysis or problem formulation?
2.  Your main comparison is against a feature-based SVM from 2016. Why were more recent and powerful neural baselines for relation classification (e.g., attention-based LSTMs, transformer models) not included in your evaluation? Such comparisons are crucial for contextualizing your model's performance.
3.  Could you provide a justification for your specific input representation (`e1` and `[l; e2]`)? Have you experimented with alternative ways of feeding the events and the lexical marker into the model, and if so, how did they perform?
4.  The analysis in Section 4.3 relies on a few examples. Could you provide a more quantitative error analysis? For instance, what percentage of errors occur on ambiguous markers versus unambiguous ones? Are there particular semantic or syntactic patterns that systematically confuse your model?
