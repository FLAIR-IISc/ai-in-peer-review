### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which it defines as a binary classification problem: determining if a semantic relation between two events, potentially signaled by a lexical marker, is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors argue against traditional methods that rely on fixed syntactic patterns or lexical resources, hypothesizing instead that a neural network can learn to encode the context of the events to make this determination. They propose a dual-input LSTM-based neural network architecture and evaluate it on the AltLex corpus. Their results show that their best-performing configuration achieves a modest improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, a feature-rich SVM model, on this specific dataset.

### Contributions
1.  An empirical evaluation demonstrating that a standard LSTM-based architecture, without explicit linguistic features, can achieve performance superior to a strong, feature-engineered SVM baseline on the AltLex corpus for causal relation classification.
2.  An exploration of several configurations of this neural architecture, including different LSTM state-passing strategies, input padding methods, and optimizers for this task.

### Strengths
1.  **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles the important and challenging task of identifying causal relations, particularly those expressed through a diverse set of lexical markers ("AltLex"). Moving beyond a closed set of connectives is a key challenge in discourse analysis, and the paper’s focus is well-motivated.
2.  **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The authors rightly compare their method against the system introduced by Hidey and McKeown (2016), which is the established state-of-the-art on the AltLex corpus. This comparison against a strong, feature-based SVM provides a solid benchmark for evaluating the contribution of the proposed neural approach.
3.  **Ablation of Model Configurations:** The paper presents results for a reasonable set of model variations (e.g., "Pair LSTM" vs. "Stated Pair LSTM", different padding strategies and optimizers). This provides some insight into which architectural choices are more effective for this task and dataset.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a very standard model for sentence-pair classification tasks. It consists of two parallel LSTMs whose outputs are concatenated and fed into a classifier. This approach has been widely used in NLP for tasks like paraphrase identification and natural language inference for several years. The paper presents this as a new contribution for causality disambiguation, but it is essentially a straightforward application of an established neural method to a new task. The claim that this architecture is a key contribution of the paper is overstated.
2.  **Evaluation is Confined to a Single Dataset:** The paper's claims about the superiority of its context-encoding approach are based entirely on results from one dataset, AltLex. The generalizability of these findings is questionable without testing on other corpora that annotate causality, such as the Penn Discourse Treebank (PDTB) or Causal-TimeBank. The AltLex corpus was constructed using a specific bootstrapping methodology based on alignments between Wikipedia and Simple Wikipedia, which may introduce dataset-specific artifacts. A truly robust method for causality detection should demonstrate its effectiveness across different domains and annotation schemes. Without such evidence, the results feel preliminary and dataset-specific.
3.  **Modest Performance Gains and Lack of Significance Testing:** The improvements over the strong SVM baseline (B2) are marginal, especially on the larger "bootstrapping" version of the corpus. An F1-score increase from 80.90% to 81.96% (+1.06%) is a very small gain, particularly when replacing a well-understood, feature-based model with a less interpretable "black-box" neural network. The authors do not report any statistical significance tests for their main results in Table 5, making it impossible to determine if these minor improvements are statistically significant or simply due to random initialization or training variance.
4.  **Superficial Analysis:** The paper lacks a deep analysis of the model's behavior. For instance, the authors find that the independent "Pair LSTM" outperforms the "Stated Pair LSTM" (where the second LSTM is initialized with the state of the first). This is a potentially interesting finding, as it might suggest that the causal relationship is not captured sequentially. However, the paper only concludes that "it is better to encode each argument independently" without further investigation or intuition. The error analysis is limited to a few selected examples in Table 8, which is insufficient to provide a comprehensive understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses compared to the baseline. What kinds of causal phenomena does the model capture that the feature-based system cannot, and where does it still fail?

### Questions to Authors
1.  The "Pair LSTM" architecture is a standard model for sentence-pair classification. Could you please clarify the methodological novelty of your approach beyond the direct application of this existing architecture to the causality detection task?
2.  Your claims about the advantages of neural context encoding are validated on only one corpus (AltLex). Why was the evaluation not extended to other available causality resources like PDTB? Such an experiment would be crucial to support the claim that your method is generally effective and not just tuned to the specifics of the AltLex dataset.
3.  The performance gains over the strong SVM baseline are quite small on the bootstrapping dataset. Have you conducted statistical significance tests for the main results presented in Table 5? This is essential to confirm that the observed improvements are reliable.
4.  Could you offer a more detailed explanation or hypothesis for why the unconnected "Pair LSTM" works better than the "Stated Pair LSTM"? This seems counter-intuitive, as one might expect the context from the first event span to be highly relevant for processing the second.
