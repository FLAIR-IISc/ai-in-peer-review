**Reviewer #2**

**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves determining whether a lexical marker connecting two events signals a causal relationship. The authors argue that previous methods are overly reliant on specific syntactic patterns or feature engineering from lexical resources (e.g., WordNet, VerbNet), which limits their generalizability. To overcome this, they propose a neural network architecture based on LSTMs that encodes the textual context of the two events to make its classification. The model takes the two event phrases as input, encodes them with separate LSTMs, and then uses a series of dense layers to classify the relationship as Causal or Non-Causal. The authors evaluate their approach on the AltLex corpus, showing that their model outperforms a majority-class baseline and the previous feature-based state-of-the-art system on this dataset.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is an empirical demonstration that a standard LSTM-based sentence-pair classification architecture, which relies solely on pre-trained word embeddings to encode context, can significantly outperform a strong, feature-rich SVM baseline for causal lexical marker disambiguation. This provides strong evidence for the hypothesis that neural context encoding is more effective than relying on hand-crafted linguistic features for this task.
2.  The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation and analysis on the AltLex corpus. This includes an ablation study of different model configurations (e.g., padding strategies, optimizers, connected vs. separate LSTMs) and an analysis on a class-balanced version of the dataset, which strengthens the validity of the results.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Well-Defined Hypothesis:** The paper does an excellent job of situating the work within existing literature. It clearly articulates the limitations of prior approaches—namely their dependence on specific syntactic constructions and the limited coverage of lexical resources—and proposes a clean, compelling hypothesis: that the causal nature of a relation can be effectively determined by neurally encoding the surrounding context, without requiring explicit linguistic features.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the AltLex corpus, outperforming the previous best system (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) by a notable margin across multiple metrics (e.g., a 2.13% F1 and 7.72% Accuracy improvement on the non-bootstrapping dataset). The consistent improvement over both a majority-class baseline and a feature-engineered SVM demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3.  **Thoughtful Analysis:** The paper includes several valuable analyses. The comparison between different model variants (e.g., "Pair LSTM" vs. "Stated Pair LSTM", different optimizers) provides useful insights into the architectural choices. Furthermore, the qualitative analysis in Table 8, which showcases examples correctly classified by the proposed model but missed by the baseline, compellingly illustrates the model's ability to leverage subtle contextual cues over relying on the typical sense of a causative verb like "break". The additional experiment on a balanced dataset also adds confidence to the conclusions.
4.  **Model Simplicity and Generalizability:** The proposed approach is elegant in its simplicity. By avoiding complex feature engineering and reliance on external linguistic resources (beyond pre-trained embeddings), the model is more generalizable and potentially easier to adapt to other domains or languages compared to the feature-heavy baseline system.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The main weakness of the paper is that the proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a standard and well-established model for sentence-pair classification tasks. While its application to this specific problem is new and the results are strong, the architecture itself is not a novel contribution to the field of neural network design. The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly framed its contribution as the successful *application* of this architecture and the demonstration of its superiority over feature-based methods for this task, rather than implying the architecture itself is a primary contribution.
2.  **Lack of Implementation and Training Details:** The paper is missing several key details required for reproducibility. Important hyperparameters are not specified, such as the hidden state dimensions of the LSTMs, the number and size of the dense layers following concatenation, the batch size used for training, and the number of training epochs or criteria for early stopping. Including these details in an appendix would significantly strengthen the paper.
3.  **Superficial Analysis of Architectural Choices:** The analysis comparing the "Pair LSTM" and "Stated Pair LSTM" could be deeper. The conclusion that the connected "Stated Pair LSTM" performs worse because the "assumption about the relation between the meanings of the two input events does not hold" is vague. A more concrete hypothesis would be more insightful. For instance, does passing the state introduce a harmful sequential bias, given that the causal event does not always appear first in the text? Or does it cause the model to over-rely on the first event's representation, hindering its ability to model the interaction? A more thorough discussion here would be beneficial.
4.  **Clarity of Input Representation:** The description of the model's input is slightly ambiguous. Figure 1 shows two separate inputs, `Event 1` and `Lexical Marker + Event 2`. However, the text describes the second input as "the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event." It is unclear how these are combined. Are the tokens of the lexical marker simply prepended to the tokens of the second event? A clearer description or example of the exact input sequences would improve clarity.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the missing hyperparameter and training details for your best-performing model to ensure reproducibility? Specifically, the LSTM hidden size, the architecture of the subsequent dense layers, batch size, and the number of epochs trained.
2.  You found that the "Pair LSTM" (independent encoders) outperformed the "Stated Pair LSTM" (where the second LSTM is initialized with the first's final state). Your explanation for this is that the assumed semantic link "does not hold". Could you please elaborate on your hypothesis here? Why do you believe that explicitly passing the context from the first event via the hidden state hinders performance on this particular task?
3.  The "Pair LSTM" architecture is a fairly standard model for sentence-pair classification. Could you clarify how you position your contribution? Is it primarily the novel architecture itself, or is it the successful application of this architecture to demonstrate that a pure context-encoding approach outperforms complex feature engineering for causal disambiguation?
