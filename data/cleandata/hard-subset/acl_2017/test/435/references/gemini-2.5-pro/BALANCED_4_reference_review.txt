### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, framed as a binary classification problem to determine if a relationship between two events (e1, e2), signalled by a lexical marker (l), is causal. The authors argue that previous feature-based methods are limited by their reliance on specific syntactic patterns and lexical resources. To overcome this, they propose an LSTM-based neural network architecture designed to encode the context of the two events. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform the previous state-of-the-art feature-based system.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective application of a standard neural architecture to causal disambiguation:** The primary contribution is the empirical demonstration that a relatively simple dual-LSTM architecture, trained end-to-end using only pre-trained word embeddings, can surpass a strong, feature-engineered SVM baseline on this task. This provides a valuable data point on the effectiveness of neural context encoding for this specific semantic problem.
2.  **A solid experimental evaluation and analysis:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation on two versions of the AltLex corpus, including experiments with a class-balanced setup. The analysis of different model configurations (e.g., padding, optimizers) and the qualitative error analysis in Table 8 offer useful insights into the model's behavior and its advantages over the baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed models achieve a clear improvement over the established baseline (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) on both versions of the AltLex corpus. For instance, on the non-bootstrapping data, the "Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" model improves F1-score by 1.64 percentage points and accuracy by a substantial 6.04 percentage points. These gains are significant and effectively validate the authors' central hypothesis that a neural approach can better capture the contextual nuances required for this task than the previous feature-based method.

2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Generalizability:** The proposed "Pair LSTM" model is a straightforward and well-understood architecture. Its success is achieved without reliance on complex, hand-crafted features or external linguistic knowledge bases like WordNet or VerbNet (beyond pre-trained embeddings). This makes the approach more generalizable, less brittle, and easier to reproduce, establishing a strong and accessible baseline for future research on causal relation extraction.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation and Analysis:** The authors conduct a rigorous set of experiments. They compare against both a simple majority-class baseline and the more sophisticated prior state-of-the-art. The exploration of different model variants (Stated vs. Pair LSTM), optimizers, and padding strategies adds depth to the results. Furthermore, the qualitative analysis in Table 8 provides compelling anecdotal evidence, showing the model's ability to correctly classify instances with ambiguous markers (e.g., "which then") or potentially misleading causative verbs (e.g., "break") where the baseline fails. The additional experiment on a downsampled, more balanced dataset further strengthens the findings.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a standard Siamese-like network commonly used for sentence pair classification tasks. While its application to this specific problem is new, the architecture itself does not represent a significant methodological innovation. The paper presents this as a primary contribution ("we contribute a neural network architecture"), which overstates its novelty. The work would be stronger if it were framed more as a strong empirical study that establishes a new neural baseline, rather than as the proposal of a novel architecture.

2.  **Lack of Comparison with Modern Baselines:** The experiments compare against a strong baseline from 2016. However, in the intervening years, pre-trained transformer models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) have become the standard for virtually all text classification tasks, consistently outperforming LSTM-based models. A comparison against a fine-tuned BERT model would be a much more relevant and stringent baseline. Without this, it is difficult to assess the true performance of the proposed model in the context of the current state-of-the-art in NLP. The absence of such a baseline is a significant omission.

3.  **Unclear Input Representation:** The paper is not sufficiently clear about how the input sequences for the LSTMs are constructed. The text states the inputs are the two events (e1, e2) and the lexical marker (l), but the architecture description implies the inputs are `e1` and `l + e2`. It is unclear if the text surrounding the annotated spans is included, or only the text within the spans themselves. For example, for the sentence "e2 because e1", is the input to the first LSTM just the text of `e2` and the second `because` followed by the text of `e1`? This lack of precision hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to fully understand why certain architectural choices were made (e.g., why `l` is grouped with `e2`). A few concrete examples of sentence-to-input mappings would greatly improve clarity.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact procedure for constructing the two input sequences for the LSTMs from a given sentence and its annotations (e1, l, e2)? Specifically, is any context outside of these spans included? If not, have you considered the potential benefit of including the surrounding or intermediate text?

2.  The primary baseline is an SVM model from 2016. Have you considered comparing your approach to a more contemporary baseline, such as a fine-tuned BERT model, which is now standard for sentence-pair classification tasks? This would help to more accurately situate your model's performance within the current state-of-the-art.

3.  Your results show that the "Pair LSTM" (independent encoders) consistently performs better than the "Stated Pair LSTM" (where the second LSTM is initialized with the first's final state). This is an interesting finding. Do you have any further intuition as to why explicitly passing the state between the event encoders is detrimental for this task?
