### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves determining whether a lexical marker connecting two events signals a causal relation. The authors argue that previous methods are overly reliant on syntactic patterns, hand-crafted features, and lexical resources, which limits their coverage and generalizability. They propose a dual-input LSTM-based neural network that learns to classify relations as Causal or Non-Causal by encoding the contextual information of the two events involved. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective application of neural methods to causal disambiguation:** The primary contribution is the empirical demonstration that a standard recurrent neural network architecture, relying only on pre-trained word embeddings, can significantly outperform a strong, feature-engineered SVM baseline on this task. This validates the hypothesis that encoding the full context via LSTMs is a more powerful approach than relying on a curated set of linguistic features.
2.  **Thorough experimental analysis:** The authors conduct a solid set of experiments on the two versions of the AltLex corpus. They systematically evaluate different architectural choices (e.g., "Pair LSTM" vs. "Stated Pair LSTM"), optimizers, and padding strategies. Furthermore, the inclusion of a qualitative error analysis in Section 4.3, with concrete examples, strengthens their claims about the model's ability to handle context-dependent ambiguity.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed model achieves clear and significant improvements over the baselines. On the "non-bootstrapping" dataset, the best model improves F1-score by 1.64 percentage points and accuracy by 6.04 percentage points over the previous SOTA (B2). On the more challenging "bootstrapping" version, the model achieves a better balance between precision and recall, improving precision by 3.01 points while maintaining a competitive F1 score. This strong empirical performance is a compelling argument for acceptance.
2.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work in the Introduction and Related Work sections. It provides a concise but effective overview of linguistic theories of causation and prior computational work, clearly highlighting the limitations of syntax- and lexicon-heavy approaches. The framing of the problem as one that requires deep contextual understanding, for which neural networks are well-suited, is convincing.
3.  **Good Qualitative Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.3 provides valuable insight into the model's behavior. By showing specific examples where the proposed model succeeds and the baseline fails (Table 8), the authors give concrete evidence for their central claim. For instance, correctly classifying a non-causal usage of the typically causative verb "break" or disambiguating the ambiguous marker "which then" demonstrates the model's capacity to leverage context beyond simple lexical cues.
4.  **Reproducibility and Methodological Soundness:** The authors describe their model architecture, hyperparameters (dropout, regularization), and experimental setup in sufficient detail. The comparison against both a simple majority-class baseline (B1) and the original paper's SOTA system (B2) is appropriate and provides a clear measure of the contribution.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The proposed neural architecture ("Pair LSTM") is a fairly standard dual-encoder model, a common pattern for sentence-pair classification tasks. While its application to this problem is effective and well-executed, the model itself does not introduce a new technique or a significant architectural innovation. The paper's contribution lies more in the successful application and empirical results than in methodological novelty.
2.  **Lack of Justification for Input Representation:** The model takes two inputs: the first event (`e1`) and the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event (`l + e2`). This is a crucial design choice, but it is presented without justification or ablation. It is unclear why this grouping was chosen over other possibilities, such as a three-input model (`e1`, `l`, `e2`) or a single-input model encoding the full sentence with special tokens marking the spans. Concatenating the marker with the second event might conflate their representations in a suboptimal way.
3.  **Evaluation on a Single Dataset:** The experiments are confined to the AltLex corpus. While this is the appropriate dataset given that the baseline system was introduced with it, the claims about the general superiority of context-encoding neural models would be much stronger if tested on other causality-related datasets. For example, performance on implicit causal relations in the PDTB or on the smaller Causal-TimeBank could have provided evidence of the model's robustness and generalizability beyond the specific characteristics of AltLex's construction methodology.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind your choice of input representation, specifically concatenating the lexical marker `l` with the second event `e2`? Have you explored alternative representations, such as providing `e1`, `l`, and `e2` as three separate inputs to be encoded, or encoding the full sentence and using the hidden states corresponding to the spans?
2.  The "Stated Pair LSTM" (initializing the second LSTM with the final state of the first) consistently underperformed the independent "Pair LSTM". This seems counter-intuitive, as one might expect some form of sequential dependency to be helpful. Do you have any hypotheses as to why explicitly passing the state hurt performance in this task?
