### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which it defines as classifying the relationship between two events (e1, e2), potentially mediated by a lexical marker (l), as either 'Causal' or 'Non-Causal'. The authors propose a dual-input LSTM-based neural network architecture. One input encodes the first event (e1), while the second encodes the lexical marker concatenated with the second event (l + e2). The model is evaluated on the "non bootstrapping" and "bootstrapping" versions of the AltLex corpus, showing modest improvements over the previous feature-based state-of-the-art system from Hidey and McKeown (2016).

### Contributions
1.  **An empirical evaluation of a dual-encoder LSTM model for causal relation classification.** The authors apply a relatively standard neural architecture to the AltLex dataset and demonstrate that it can outperform a strong, feature-engineered SVM baseline on this task, particularly on the "non bootstrapping" version of the corpus.
2.  **An analysis of model design choices for this task.** The paper investigates several architectural and hyperparameter variations, such as connecting the LSTMs' states, different optimizers (Adadelta vs. Adam), and different sequence padding strategies. The results provide some insight into what works best for this specific problem and dataset (e.g., independent LSTMs outperform state-connected ones).

### Strengths
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper does a good job of motivating the problem, distinguishing it from related work that relies on fixed syntactic constructions or limited lexical resources. The decision to frame the task as "causal meaning classification" separate from "causal argument identification" brings clarity to the specific contribution.
2.  **Thoroughness of Evaluation on the AltLex Corpus:** The authors evaluate their model on two versions of the AltLex corpus and compare it against two sensible baselines, including the original work that introduced the dataset. They also experiment with a balanced version of the dataset to address class imbalance issues.
3.  **Qualitative Error Analysis:** The inclusion of Table 8, which shows specific examples where the proposed model succeeds and the baseline fails, is insightful. It provides concrete evidence for the model's ability to handle ambiguity, such as with the verb "break" or the connective "which then", strengthening the paper's central claim about the importance of contextual encoding.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Severe Lack of Methodological Clarity:** The description of the padding strategy in Section 3.3 is deeply concerning and appears technically flawed. The authors state they evaluated padding to the "maximum, the mean and mode of the length of the two inputs". Padding to a mean or mode length is non-standard and nonsensical; it would either truncate a large portion of the data (if longer than the mean/mode) or fail to create uniform tensor shapes for batching (if shorter). This fundamental methodological detail is so poorly explained that it calls into question the reproducibility and validity of the reported experiments. The notation in Equation 2 is also imprecise (`W · x` should likely be `W · vec(x)`).
2.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper is marred by a significant number of formatting errors, typos, and inconsistencies that detract from its scientific credibility. The References section is completely mangled, containing stray BibTeX markup (`\Q2017\E`), question marks, and repeated author blocks, rendering it almost unreadable. Other examples include the use of a comma as a decimal separator in Table 7 ("69,84%"), the typo "Empiric results" in the introduction, and the inconsistent capitalization of baselines ("B1" and "B2"). Table 3 is referenced in Section 3.2 but is missing from the paper. Such a lack of care in preparation is unacceptable for a top-tier conference.
3.  **Gaps in Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The performance gains over the state-of-the-art baseline (B2) on the more challenging "bootstrapping" corpus are marginal (e.g., 81.56% vs 80.90% F1 for the best "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" model). The authors provide no statistical significance tests to demonstrate that this improvement is not due to random chance.
    *   In the crucial experiment with the balanced dataset (Section 4.2, Table 7), the authors compare their model only against the weak majority-class baseline (B1). The comparison against the SoTA baseline (B2) is conspicuously absent. This omission prevents a fair assessment of the model's performance when the dataset's class distribution is controlled for.
    *   The footnote stating that worse-performing systems were omitted from Table 5 "for the sake of brevity" is poor scientific practice, as it can be interpreted as cherry-picking results.
4.  **Limited Novelty:** The proposed "Pair LSTM" architecture is a standard dual-encoder model, a well-established architecture in NLP for comparing or relating two pieces of text. While its application to this specific task is valid, the methodological contribution is incremental rather than novel. The paper's framing could be more modest about its architectural innovation.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide a precise and technically sound explanation of your padding strategy? How does padding to the "mean" or "mode" of sequence lengths work? What happens to sequences longer than this chosen length—are they truncated or handled differently?
2.  The state-of-the-art baseline (B2) was not included in your evaluation on the balanced dataset (Table 7). Could you explain this omission and, if possible, provide the results for this crucial comparison?
3.  Given the small margins of improvement over B2 on the "bootstrapping" corpus, have you conducted any statistical significance tests? If so, what were the results?
4.  The reference list is currently unreadable due to severe formatting errors. We assume this will be fully corrected. Can you confirm?
