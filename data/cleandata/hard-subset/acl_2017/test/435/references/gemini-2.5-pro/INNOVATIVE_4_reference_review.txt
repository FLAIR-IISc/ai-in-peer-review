### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which involves determining whether a relation between two events, often signaled by a lexical marker, is causal. The authors argue against traditional methods that rely on fixed syntactic patterns or limited lexical resources, hypothesizing instead that the causal nature of a relation is embedded in the semantic context of the events themselves.

The primary contributions of this work are, in my view:
1.  **A Conceptual Shift in Causality Detection:** The main contribution is the bold proposal to move away from feature-engineered, pattern-matching systems towards a purely context-based neural approach. By treating causality as a semantic phenomenon to be learned from raw text, the paper charts a new and promising direction for the field, freeing the task from the brittleness of handcrafted rules and the limited coverage of lexical resources.
2.  **An Effective and Simple Neural Architecture:** The paper introduces a dual-input LSTM model that effectively encodes the two events in a causal proposition. The success of this straightforward architecture in outperforming a complex, feature-rich baseline serves as powerful evidence for the paper's central hypothesis.
3.  **A Clear Problem Formulation:** The authors provide a useful decomposition of the broader causality extraction problem into two subtasks: causal meaning classification and causal argument identification. By focusing on and making significant progress on the first subtask, they provide a clear and well-scoped contribution.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Bold Approach:** The paper's greatest strength is its departure from the established paradigm. Instead of incrementally improving upon feature-based systems, the authors make a compelling case for a fundamental change in approach. Arguing that neural networks can learn to "understand" causality from context, without explicit syntactic or lexical priors, is a significant and forward-looking claim. This conceptual novelty is highly valuable and has the potential to influence future work in this and related areas of relation extraction.
2.  **Elegant and Powerful Methodology:** The proposed dual-LSTM architecture is refreshingly simple. In an era of ever-increasing model complexity, the paper demonstrates that a clean, well-motivated architecture can be highly effective. Its ability to outperform the state-of-the-art system of Hidey and McKeown (2016) is a testament to the power of the core idea—that learning representations of the event contexts is more potent than relying on an extensive, hand-designed feature set.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on the AltLex corpus, demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines across multiple settings. The analysis in Section 4.3 is particularly persuasive. The examples, such as the disambiguation of the typically causative verb "break" in a non-causal context, or the correct classification of the ambiguous marker "which then", provide concrete evidence that the model is indeed leveraging context in a nuanced way that goes beyond simple keyword matching.

### Weaknesses

While I am enthusiastic about the paper's core idea, there are some areas that could be improved. I see these as opportunities for future work rather than fatal flaws that undermine the current contribution.

1.  **Limited Scope of the Task:** The paper admirably scopes its contribution to the "causal meaning classification" subtask. However, this leaves the overall problem only half-solved. A complete system would also need to identify which event is the cause and which is the effect. The paper does not discuss how its approach might extend to or facilitate this second, crucial step. While this does not detract from the validity of the current results, it does limit the immediate practical utility of the proposed model.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Architectural Insight:** The empirical finding that independent LSTMs ("Pair LSTM") outperform a sequentially connected model ("Stated Pair LSTM") is quite interesting. It suggests that a causal relation is not best modeled as a simple sequential dependency between the event representations. This is a non-trivial insight, but the paper merely reports the result without exploring the implications. A deeper discussion on why this might be the case would have elevated the paper's contribution from a strong empirical result to a more profound linguistic and computational insight.
3.  **Potential Ambiguity in Input Representation:** The description of the model input in Section 3.3 is slightly ambiguous. It states that the inputs are the "first event" and the "lexical marker and the second event." It is unclear if this refers strictly to the annotated text spans of the events, or if the model processes the full sentence context where these events occur. If only the event spans are used, the model's access to "context" is limited, and this should be stated as a potential constraint. Clarifying this point is essential for accurately interpreting the model's capabilities.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact text fed into the LSTMs? Does the model receive only the annotated `e1` and `l+e2` spans, or is the surrounding sentence context included in the input sequences?
2.  The superior performance of the "Pair LSTM" over the "Stated Pair LSTM" is a fascinating result. Do you have any further hypotheses on why modeling the two events independently before combination is more effective than a sequential encoding? What might this imply about the linguistic expression of causality?
3.  You propose a two-step framework for causality classification. Do you have any thoughts on how the representations learned by your current model could be leveraged for the second subtask of identifying the causal arguments (i.e., distinguishing the cause from the effect)?
