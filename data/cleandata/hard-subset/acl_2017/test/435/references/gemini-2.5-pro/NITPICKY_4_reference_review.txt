### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning classification, which involves determining whether a lexical marker between two events signals a causal relation. The authors argue against traditional methods that rely on specific syntactic patterns or feature engineering from lexical resources, proposing instead a neural approach that encodes the context of the two events using LSTMs. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform a previous state-of-the-art feature-based system.

From my perspective, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that a straightforward LSTM-based model, which relies solely on pre-trained word embeddings without external linguistic resources (e.g., WordNet, VerbNet), can effectively disambiguate causal markers and outperform a strong, feature-rich SVM baseline on the AltLex dataset.
2.  A comparison of two simple LSTM architectural variants ("Pair LSTM" vs. "Stated Pair LSTM") for this task, offering some initial insights into how to model the interaction between the two event clauses.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Approach:** The paper effectively argues for moving beyond methods reliant on pre-defined syntactic patterns or lexical resources for causality detection. The motivation to use neural context encoding is sound and aligns with current trends in NLP, addressing the limited coverage and brittleness of prior feature-based approaches.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed model demonstrates a clear improvement over the previous state-of-the-art system (B2) on the "non bootstrapping" version of the AltLex corpus (75.40% vs. 73.76% F1) and achieves competitive, arguably better-balanced, performance on the more challenging "bootstrapping" version. The higher precision achieved by the authors' best models is a desirable characteristic compared to the baseline's high-recall, low-precision profile.
3.  **Simplicity of the Model:** The strength of the paper lies in showing that a relatively simple and standard neural architecture can be highly effective. This suggests that the rich contextual representations learned from LSTMs are sufficient for the task, obviating the need for complex and potentially costly feature engineering.

### Weaknesses

My recommendation is ultimately guided by a significant number of minor, but collectively substantial, flaws in the paper's methodology, presentation, and experimental rigor.

1.  **Serious Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper is marred by a lack of professional polish that severely hinders readability and credibility.
    *   The **References section is completely unformatted**, appearing as a single block of text with bizarre formatting artifacts (e.g., `Do et al\.,? 2011`, `\Q2017\E`). This is unacceptable for a conference submission and makes verifying sources nearly impossible.
    *   **Tables 3 and 6 are referenced in the text but are missing from the paper**, leaving the reader unable to verify corpus statistics that are central to the experimental setup.
    *   The caption for Table 5 contains a reference to a footnote ("1") that does not exist.
    *   The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings (e.g., "we call to this architecture", "the grade of coverage", "a bootsrapping method was applied to enhance the corpus").

2.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail and Unconventional Choices:** The description of the method lacks the detail required for reproducibility and contains questionable design choices.
    *   **Crucial Hyperparameters are Missing:** The paper fails to report key model details. What are the hidden state dimensions of the LSTMs? What are the sizes of the three dense layers? While ranges for dropout and regularization are provided, the exact values used for the best-performing models in Table 5 are not specified.
    *   **Unjustified Padding Strategy:** The authors evaluate padding sequences to the "mean" and "mode" length. This is a highly unconventional approach. Standard practice is to pad to the maximum length within a batch (or the entire dataset). This choice is not justified and its implications are not discussed (e.g., does it involve truncating longer sequences, potentially losing information?).
    *   **Ambiguous Input Representation:** The second input is described as "the concatenation of the lexical marker and the second event." The exact mechanism of this concatenation is unclear. Are the token sequences simply joined? This conflates the crucial lexical marker `l` with the event `e2`, potentially diminishing its signal. A more principled approach, such as feeding `l` through a separate encoder or embedding, is not considered.

3.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor and Superficial Analysis:**
    *   **Problem Framing Assumption:** The task is defined as classifying a relation given pre-identified events `e1`, `e2`, and marker `l`. The paper completely omits any discussion of how these elements are identified. This is a critical and non-trivial prerequisite, and assuming gold-standard segmentation frames the problem in an artificial setting without acknowledging this major simplification.
    *   **Selective Reporting:** The note in Table 5 stating that "those systems that performed worse than B1 and B2 are not in Table 5" is concerning. Omitting poor results constitutes selective reporting and prevents a full understanding of the model's sensitivity to hyperparameters.
    *   **Arbitrary Data Subsampling:** In Section 4.2, the non-causal class is downsampled by "a factor of ten" to create a more balanced dataset. This is an arbitrary and extreme reduction. The method of sampling is not specified, and results on a single, drastically altered dataset are not sufficient to draw robust conclusions.
    *   **Insufficient Significance Testing:** A McNemar’s test is reported only for the secondary experiment on the reduced dataset. Statistical significance tests are conspicuously absent for the main results in Table 5. It is unclear if the improvements over the B2 baseline are statistically significant.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the preprocessing pipeline? Specifically, how are the event spans `e1` and `e2` and the lexical marker `l` identified and extracted to create the inputs for your model? Does your experimental setup rely on the gold-standard annotations provided in the AltLex corpus?
2.  Can you provide a justification for using mean/mode length for padding? How are sequences that are longer than the mean or mode handled? How does this strategy compare to the standard approach of padding to a maximum sequence length?
3.  For reproducibility, please provide the complete set of hyperparameters for your best-performing models ("Pair LSTM Max Adadelta" on the non-bootstrapping corpus and "Pair LSTM Mean Adam" on the bootstrapping corpus), including LSTM hidden dimensions, all dense layer dimensions, and the specific dropout and regularization values used.
4.  Are the improvements over the B2 baseline in Table 5 statistically significant? Please provide results from appropriate significance tests.
