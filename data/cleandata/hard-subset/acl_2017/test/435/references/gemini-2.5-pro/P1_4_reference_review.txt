### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of causal meaning disambiguation, which it frames as a binary classification problem: determining if a relationship between two events, connected by a lexical marker, is Causal or Non-Causal. The authors argue that previous feature-based and syntax-grounded methods are limited by their reliance on handcrafted features and brittle syntactic assumptions. They propose a neural architecture based on dual LSTMs to encode the contextual meaning of the two events and their connecting marker, using only pre-trained word embeddings as input. The model is evaluated on the AltLex corpus, where it is shown to outperform the previous state-of-the-art system.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance with a Simpler Model:** The primary contribution is empirically demonstrating that a relatively standard dual-LSTM architecture, without any task-specific linguistic features, can achieve state-of-the-art results on the AltLex benchmark for causal disambiguation. This reinforces the trend in NLP that effective contextual encoding from neural models can surpass complex, feature-engineered systems.
2.  **Thorough Ablation and Error Analysis:** The authors conduct a solid evaluation, comparing different architectural variants (independent vs. state-passing LSTMs), padding strategies, and optimizers. More importantly, the qualitative analysis in Section 4.3 provides compelling examples (e.g., the verb 'break', the marker 'which then') that support their central claim that context, rather than lexical triggers alone, is key to disambiguation.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Clear SOTA Improvement:** The paper presents convincing results, outperforming both a strong majority-class baseline and the previous state-of-the-art method (Hidey and McKeown, 2016) on the AltLex dataset. On the "non-bootstrapping" set, the proposed "Pair LSTM" model achieves an F1-score of 75.40% and an accuracy of 77.90%, representing a significant improvement over the baseline B2's 73.76% F1 and 71.86% accuracy. The gains on the "bootstrapping" set are also clear, particularly in precision. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.
2.  **Methodological Simplicity and Generality:** A major strength is that the model does not rely on any handcrafted features, syntactic parsers, or external lexical resources like WordNet or VerbNet, which were required by the previous SOTA system. This makes the approach more general, less brittle, and easier to apply to new domains or languages where such resources may not be available. The model learns directly from the text and pre-trained embeddings, which is a significant advantage.
3.  **Good Analysis of Model Behavior:** The paper goes beyond just reporting metrics. The discussion of the Precision/Recall trade-off between Adadelta and Adam optimizers is insightful. The experiment on a class-balanced subset of the data (Table 7) is a good sanity check that confirms the model is not just exploiting dataset imbalance. The qualitative analysis in Table 8 is particularly effective, as it showcases specific, difficult examples where the model's contextual understanding allows it to succeed where the feature-based baseline fails. This analysis strongly supports the paper's core hypothesis.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The proposed "Pair LSTM" model is a fairly standard siamese-like architecture used for sentence-pair tasks. While its application to causal disambiguation is effective and well-executed, the architecture itself does not represent a significant methodological innovation. The contribution lies more in the successful application and demonstration of its effectiveness against feature-based methods, rather than in proposing a new neural network design.
2.  **Evaluation on a Single, Potentially Niche Dataset:** The paper's claims are entirely supported by experiments on the AltLex corpus. While this is the appropriate dataset given the prior work, the way AltLex was constructed (by aligning Wikipedia and Simple Wikipedia to find connectives) may introduce biases or artifacts. It is unclear if the model's strong performance is due to learning general principles of causality or learning patterns specific to the encyclopedic style and structure of Wikipedia text. Validation on a dataset from a different domain (e.g., news, literature) would have substantially strengthened the claims of the model's general applicability.
3.  **Unclear Justification for Input Representation:** The model takes event `e1` as one input and the concatenation of the lexical marker `l` and event `e2` (`l + e2`) as the second input. The paper does not provide a clear justification for this asymmetric design choice. Causal markers can appear in various positions relative to the cause and effect clauses. Grouping the marker with `e2` might be a reasonable heuristic, but it is not universally true, and this design decision could be a confounding factor. An exploration or justification of this choice is missing.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** While generally well-written, some aspects of the paper could be clearer. The distinction between "Stated Pair LSTM" and "Pair LSTM" is explained briefly but could be made more prominent, perhaps with a clearer diagram. The paper also contains minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., "The last set of features reduces the ability of the system... because they restrict the system..."). The reference list at the end is poorly formatted, which detracts from the overall polish of the submission.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to concatenate the lexical marker `l` with the second event `e2`? Have you experimented with alternative ways of incorporating the marker, for instance, by prepending it to the full sentence context, treating it as a separate feature, or concatenating it between the representations of `e1` and `e2`?
2.  The "Stated Pair LSTM" (initializing the second LSTM with the final state of the first) consistently underperformed the "Pair LSTM" (independent encoders). This is an interesting result, as one might assume that passing state would be beneficial for modeling relational phenomena. Do you have a hypothesis for why encoding the two events independently and combining them later works better for this task? Does this suggest the causal link is not a direct sequential dependency but a more abstract semantic relationship?
3.  Given that your approach is feature-free and relies only on word embeddings, have you considered how it might perform in a zero-shot or few-shot setting on unseen causal markers? The current evaluation relies on markers seen during training (Table 4 highlights the train/test overlap). How well does the model generalize to entirely new ways of expressing causality?
